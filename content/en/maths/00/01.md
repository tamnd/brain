## Chapter 1. Nature of Mathematical Objects

Mathematics studies objects through structure. A number, set, function, graph, group, space, or proof object is not understood mainly by its physical representation. It is understood by the relations it supports, the operations defined on it, and the rules it satisfies.

This chapter introduces the basic ontology of mathematics. It explains what mathematical objects are, how they are grouped into sets and types, how equality differs from equivalence, and why finite and infinite objects require different methods of reasoning. It also introduces the distinction between constructive and classical viewpoints.

The central idea is that mathematical objects are contextual. The symbol `3` may be a natural number, an integer, a rational number, a real number, or an element of a finite field. The notation alone is insufficient. Meaning depends on the ambient structure.

| Question                           | Purpose                             |
| ---------------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| What objects are being discussed?  | Determines the domain               |
| What structure do they carry?      | Determines valid operations         |
| What counts as sameness?           | Determines equality or equivalence  |
| Is the setting finite or infinite? | Determines proof methods            |
| What logic is being used?          | Determines what counts as existence |

A structure packages objects with operations, relations, and laws. Groups, rings, fields, graphs, topological spaces, and vector spaces all follow this pattern. Once the structure is identified, the theory studies maps that preserve it and properties that remain invariant under those maps.

The chapter proceeds in five sections. Section 1.1 introduces abstract objects and structures. Section 1.2 compares sets, types, and universes as ways of organizing mathematical domains. Section 1.3 separates equality, identity, and equivalence. Section 1.4 explains why finite and infinite objects behave differently. Section 1.5 compares constructive and classical interpretations of proof and existence.

The goal is to establish a working discipline: every symbol should have a home, every object should belong to a context, and every claim should state the structure in which it is meaningful. This discipline prevents common errors and prepares the reader for the more specialized branches that follow.
