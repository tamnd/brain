## Chapter 2. Mathematical Truth

This chapter studies what it means for a mathematical statement to be true. The question has two layers. One concerns formal systems and proofs. The other concerns interpretation and meaning. The distinction appears throughout logic, model theory, and foundations.

Mathematical practice relies on proofs. A statement is accepted when it can be derived from axioms using valid inference rules. This is provability. At the same time, statements are interpreted inside structures such as numbers, spaces, or algebraic systems. Truth depends on whether the statement holds in that interpretation.

The tension between truth and provability drives much of modern logic. A formal system may fail to prove a statement that is true in a given structure. A system may also prove statements whose meaning depends on how symbols are interpreted.

This chapter develops these ideas in stages. It begins with the relation between truth and provability, then introduces formal systems and semantics, followed by consistency, completeness, and independence phenomena. Examples from different areas show how these concepts appear in practice.

A consistent system avoids contradiction. A complete system proves every statement or its negation within its language. These properties are desirable but not always compatible. Results in logic show limits on what formal systems can achieve.

The goal is operational clarity. When reading or writing a statement, you should be able to identify:

| Aspect          | Question                                                      |
| --------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Language        | What symbols and syntax are used?                             |
| Axioms          | What assumptions are fixed?                                   |
| Inference rules | How are new statements derived?                               |
| Interpretation  | In which structure is the statement evaluated?                |
| Status          | Is the statement provable, true, independent, or undecidable? |

The following sections make these distinctions precise and show how they interact across mathematics.
