## Chapter 1. Prehistoric and Early Counting

Mathematics begins in practical activity. Early humans needed to track quantity, compare sizes, and organize their environment. These needs produced the first forms of counting and measurement. This chapter examines how these basic ideas appeared and how they shaped later mathematics.

At this stage, mathematical thinking stays close to daily life. People count animals, measure land, track time, and divide resources. Each task requires some notion of number. The methods remain concrete. Marks, gestures, and physical groupings carry meaning directly.

One of the earliest methods is the tally system. A tally records quantity by making marks, often lines or cuts on a surface. A shepherd counting sheep may carve one mark for each animal. Ten sheep produce ten marks. This approach represents quantity without number words or symbols.

From this practice, one-to-one correspondence becomes clear. Each object matches one mark. This idea forms the foundation of counting. Modern counting follows the same structure, though expressed with words or digits.

Grouping appears as a refinement. Instead of many separate marks, people organize them into sets such as five or ten. This reduces effort and introduces structure. These groupings later influence positional number systems.

Number concepts gradually become more abstract. Early counting ties numbers to specific objects, such as three stones or five animals. Over time, the idea of “three” applies to any collection. This shift allows reasoning beyond immediate situations.

Measurement develops alongside counting. People compare lengths, areas, and volumes in everyday tasks. Land division, storage, and construction require consistent references. Early units often come from the human body, such as a foot or a hand span. These units vary between individuals, which limits precision.

Geometric ideas emerge from practice. Fields, shelters, and tools involve shapes and spatial relations. Straight lines, angles, and simple forms become recognizable patterns. These observations later support formal geometry.

Knowledge passes through oral transmission and demonstration. Skills move from one person to another through repetition. Memory places a constraint on complexity. Methods remain simple and closely tied to use.

Early mathematical thinking faces clear limits. Large quantities become difficult to manage. Complex relationships remain hard to express. General rules stay implicit rather than formalized. These constraints shape the pace of development.

Despite these limits, essential foundations appear early. Counting, grouping, measurement, and pattern recognition form the core structure of mathematics. Later developments extend and refine these ideas rather than replace them.

This chapter presents mathematics as a gradual construction. Practical needs drive early methods. Repeated use stabilizes them into shared practices. These practices become the starting point for more formal systems in later periods.
