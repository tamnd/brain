## 5.1 Compactness Theorem

The compactness theorem is one of the most important results in first-order logic. It says that a large set of sentences has a model exactly when every finite part of it has a model.

Let $T$ be a set of first-order sentences. The compactness theorem says:

$$
T \text{ has a model}
\quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad
\text{every finite } T_0 \subseteq T \text{ has a model}
$$

The forward direction is easy. If a structure $\mathcal M$ satisfies all sentences in $T$, then it certainly satisfies any finite subset $T_0 \subseteq T$.

The reverse direction is the deep part. It says that if no finite part of $T$ causes a contradiction, then the whole infinite set $T$ can be satisfied at once.

### The Basic Idea

Compactness is a bridge from finite consistency to infinite consistency.

Suppose $T$ contains infinitely many requirements:

$$
T = {\varphi_1, \varphi_2, \varphi_3, \dots}
$$

Checking all of them at once may seem difficult. Compactness says that, for existence of a model, it is enough to check every finite subcollection:

$$
{\varphi_1,\dots,\varphi_n}
$$

for each finite $n$.

If each finite subcollection has some model, then there is one model satisfying all formulas in $T$ together.

This does not mean the same finite model works for every finite subcollection. The models may change as the finite subset grows. Compactness only says that a global model exists somewhere.

### Finite Satisfiability

A theory $T$ is **finitely satisfiable** if every finite subset of $T$ has a model.

So compactness can be stated more briefly:

$$
T \text{ is satisfiable}
\quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad
T \text{ is finitely satisfiable}
$$

Here, satisfiable means “has at least one model”.

The important point is that first-order logic cannot create a purely infinite contradiction. If a theory is inconsistent, some finite part of it is already inconsistent.

In symbols:

$$
T \text{ has no model}
\quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad
\text{some finite } T_0 \subseteq T \text{ has no model}
$$

### Connection with Completeness

Compactness follows naturally from the completeness theorem.

Assume every finite subset of $T$ has a model. We want to show that $T$ has a model.

If $T$ had no model, then $T$ would semantically imply a contradiction:

$$
T \models \bot
$$

By completeness:

$$
T \vdash \bot
$$

But formal proofs are finite objects. A proof of $\bot$ can use only finitely many assumptions from $T$.

So there is some finite $T_0 \subseteq T$ such that:

$$
T_0 \vdash \bot
$$

By soundness:

$$
T_0 \models \bot
$$

Thus $T_0$ has no model, contradicting finite satisfiability.

Therefore $T$ must have a model.

This proof is short, but it contains the core insight: first-order proofs are finite, so first-order inconsistency is finitely witnessed.

### Example: Forcing an Infinite Model

Compactness can show that infinite models exist using only finite approximations.

Consider the theory:

$$
T = {\varphi_n : n \geq 1}
$$

where $\varphi_n$ says “there are at least $n$ distinct elements”:

$$
\varphi_n :=
\exists x_1,\dots,x_n
\bigwedge_{1 \leq i < j \leq n} x_i \neq x_j
$$

Every finite subset of $T$ mentions only finitely many requirements:

$$
\varphi_1,\dots,\varphi_N
$$

A structure with $N$ elements satisfies all of them.

So every finite subset has a model.

By compactness, the whole theory $T$ has a model.

Any model of $T$ must have at least $n$ elements for every $n$, so it is infinite.

This example shows how compactness turns an infinite collection of finite lower bounds into an infinite model.

### Example: A Nonstandard Element

Compactness also creates objects that are larger than all standard natural numbers.

Work in the language of arithmetic:

$$
\mathcal L = {0,1,+,\cdot,<}
$$

Add a new constant symbol $c$.

Now consider the theory:

$$
T = \operatorname{Th}(\mathbb N) \cup {c > \overline n : n \in \mathbb N}
$$

Here $\operatorname{Th}(\mathbb N)$ is the set of all first-order sentences true in the standard natural numbers, and $\overline n$ denotes the numeral for $n$:

$$
\overline n = \underbrace{1+\cdots+1}_{n \text{ times}}
$$

The new sentences say:

$$
c > 0,\quad c > 1,\quad c > 2,\quad \dots
$$

Every finite subset can be satisfied in $\mathbb N$ by interpreting $c$ as a sufficiently large natural number.

For example, if the largest condition is:

$$
c > 100
$$

choose:

$$
c = 101
$$

So every finite subset has a model.

By compactness, the whole theory has a model. In that model, $c$ is larger than every standard natural number.

This model cannot be the ordinary $\mathbb N$. It is a nonstandard model of arithmetic.

### Example: No First-Order Definition of Finiteness

Compactness shows that “being finite” cannot be expressed by a single first-order sentence.

Suppose there were a sentence $\mathrm{Finite}$ such that a structure satisfies it exactly when its domain is finite.

Now take the theory:

$$
T =
{\mathrm{Finite}}
\cup
{\varphi_n : n \geq 1}
$$

where $\varphi_n$ says “there are at least $n$ elements”.

Every finite subset of $T$ has a finite model. If the largest lower-bound sentence is $\varphi_N$, choose a structure with $N$ elements.

So every finite subset of $T$ is satisfiable.

By compactness, $T$ has a model.

But any model of all $\varphi_n$ is infinite, while $\mathrm{Finite}$ says it must be finite. Contradiction.

Therefore no first-order sentence can define finiteness.

### Compactness for Consequence

Compactness also has a consequence form.

If $T \models \varphi$, then some finite part of $T$ already implies $\varphi$:

$$
T \models \varphi
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
\exists T_0 \subseteq T
\text{ finite such that }
T_0 \models \varphi
$$

This means that first-order semantic consequence is finite in support. Even if $T$ is infinite, any single first-order conclusion depends on only finitely many axioms of $T$.

To see why, observe that:

$$
T \models \varphi
$$

is equivalent to saying:

$$
T \cup {¬\varphi}
$$

has no model.

By compactness, some finite subset has no model. That finite subset uses only finitely many sentences from $T$, so some finite $T_0 \subseteq T$ satisfies:

$$
T_0 \models \varphi
$$

### Why Compactness Matters

Compactness is useful because it lets us build models indirectly. Instead of constructing a full model immediately, we show that every finite fragment can be modeled.

It also explains why first-order logic cannot express many global properties. First-order logic can say “there are at least $n$ elements” for each fixed $n$, but no single first-order sentence can say “the structure is finite”.

The theorem is powerful because it converts a local condition into a global existence result. In first-order logic, if every finite test passes, then there is a model passing all tests at once.
