## Chapter 2. First-Order Logic

First-order logic extends propositional logic by looking inside statements.

In propositional logic, a statement such as:

$$
p
$$

is treated as a single atomic unit. We do not analyze its internal structure. It is simply true or false.

First-order logic allows statements to talk about **objects**, **properties**, and **relations**.

For example, instead of writing one variable:

$$
p
$$

we may write:

$$
\mathrm{Prime}(7)
$$

This says that $7$ has the property of being prime.

We may also write:

$$
\mathrm{Less}(2,5)
$$

This says that $2$ is less than $5$.

The language now has more structure. It includes symbols for objects, functions, predicates, equality, logical connectives, and quantifiers.

The two main quantifiers are:

$$
\forall
$$

and

$$
\exists
$$

The universal quantifier $\forall$ means “for all”. The existential quantifier $\exists$ means “there exists”.

For example:

$$
\forall x, \mathrm{Human}(x) \to \mathrm{Mortal}(x)
$$

says that every human is mortal.

A better fully parenthesized version is:

$$
\forall x,(\mathrm{Human}(x) \to \mathrm{Mortal}(x))
$$

The parentheses matter because quantifiers have scope. They tell us how much of the formula the variable controls.

Another example:

$$
\exists x, \mathrm{Prime}(x)
$$

says that there exists at least one prime object.

First-order logic is powerful because it separates **form** from **interpretation**. The same formula can be interpreted in different structures.

For example:

$$
\forall x, \exists y, (x < y)
$$

has different truth values depending on the domain.

In the natural numbers:

$$
\mathbb{N}
$$

it is true, because every natural number has a larger natural number.

In a finite ordered set such as:

$$
{1,2,3}
$$

it is false, because $3$ has no larger element inside the set.

This is the central idea of first-order semantics. A formula does not have an absolute meaning until we specify a structure.

First-order logic introduces several key notions.

A **term** names an object. A term may be a variable, a constant, or the result of applying a function symbol.

Examples:

$$
x
$$

$$
0
$$

$$
S(x)
$$

$$
x + y
$$

A **predicate** expresses a property or relation.

Examples:

$$
\mathrm{Even}(x)
$$

$$
x < y
$$

$$
\mathrm{Between}(x,y,z)
$$

A **formula** is built from predicates using connectives and quantifiers.

Examples:

$$
\mathrm{Even}(x) \land x > 0
$$

$$
\forall x,(\mathrm{Even}(x) \to \exists y, x = 2y)
$$

A **structure** gives meaning to the non-logical symbols. It specifies a domain of objects and interpretations for constants, functions, and predicates.

For arithmetic, a typical structure is:

$$
(\mathbb{N}; 0, S, +, \times, <)
$$

Here the domain is $\mathbb{N}$. The symbols $0$, $S$, $+$, $\times$, and $<$ receive their usual meanings.

A **model** is a structure in which a given sentence is true.

For example, the sentence:

$$
\forall x, (x + 0 = x)
$$

is true in the usual natural numbers. So the natural numbers form a model of that sentence.

A **sentence** is a formula with no free variables. Sentences can be true or false in a structure.

For example:

$$
\forall x, (x = x)
$$

is a sentence.

But:

$$
x = x
$$

has a free variable $x$, so its truth depends on an assignment of a value to $x$.

This chapter develops first-order logic in five stages. First, we define terms, predicates, and formulas. Second, we study quantifiers and scope. Third, we define structures and interpretations. Fourth, we define satisfaction and models. Fifth, we define validity and entailment.

First-order logic is the standard formal language for much of mathematics. It can express group axioms, order axioms, graph properties, arithmetic statements, and many geometric relations. At the same time, it has strict limits. Some mathematical ideas cannot be fully captured in first-order form.

That balance is what makes first-order logic central. It is expressive enough to formalize large parts of mathematics, yet controlled enough to have deep theorems such as completeness, compactness, and Löwenheim-Skolem.
