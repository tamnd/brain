## Chapter 8. Axiomatic Systems

Set theory becomes especially powerful when it is treated axiomatically. Instead of relying only on intuitive ideas about collections, we specify formal rules that govern what sets exist and how they behave. These rules are called **axioms**.

An axiom is a statement accepted as a starting point inside a formal theory. In set theory, axioms describe basic operations such as forming pairs, taking unions, collecting subsets, and building infinite sets. Once the axioms are fixed, theorems are proved from them.

Chapter 7 introduced the basic language of sets, relations, functions, cardinality, ordinals, and the Zermelo-Fraenkel axioms. Chapter 8 continues from there by studying several deeper features of axiomatic set theory.

The first major topic is the **Axiom of Choice**. Informally, it says that if we have a collection of nonempty sets, then we may choose one element from each set.

A common formulation is:

$$
\forall \mathcal{A},
\left[
\left(\forall A \in \mathcal{A},\ A \neq \varnothing\right)
\to
\exists f, \forall A \in \mathcal{A},\ f(A) \in A
\right]
$$

Here $\mathcal{A}$ is a family of nonempty sets, and $f$ is a choice function. The function $f$ selects one element from each set $A$ in the family.

At first, this may seem obvious. If there are only finitely many sets, we can simply choose one element from each. The subtlety appears for infinite families, especially when there is no explicit rule for making the choices.

The Axiom of Choice is important because many standard mathematical results depend on it. These include the well-ordering theorem, Zorn's lemma, and many existence theorems in algebra, topology, and analysis.

Several statements turn out to be equivalent to the Axiom of Choice over the usual Zermelo-Fraenkel axioms. Two of the most important are:

$$
\text{Every set can be well-ordered.}
$$

and

$$
\text{Every partially ordered set satisfying suitable chain conditions has a maximal element.}
$$

The second statement is known as **Zorn's lemma**. It is used throughout abstract algebra and functional analysis. For example, it helps prove that every vector space has a basis.

This chapter then introduces the **constructible universe**, usually denoted:

$$
L
$$

The universe $L$ is built in stages. At each stage, we include only sets that are definable from earlier stages. This gives a more controlled universe of sets than the full set-theoretic universe $V$.

The constructible universe is important because it provides a model in which many statements can be shown to hold. In particular, Gödel proved that if ZF is consistent, then ZFC is consistent, and the continuum hypothesis also holds inside $L$.

The chapter then turns to **consistency results**. A consistency result usually has the form:

$$
\mathrm{Con}(T) \to \mathrm{Con}(T + \varphi)
$$

This means: if the theory $T$ is consistent, then adding the statement $\varphi$ to $T$ remains consistent.

For example, one may prove relative consistency statements such as:

$$
\mathrm{Con}(\mathrm{ZF}) \to \mathrm{Con}(\mathrm{ZFC})
$$

or:

$$
\mathrm{Con}(\mathrm{ZFC}) \to \mathrm{Con}(\mathrm{ZFC} + \mathrm{CH})
$$

These statements do not prove absolute consistency. Instead, they compare theories. They say that one theory is no more contradictory than another.

The final topic is **independence**. A statement $\varphi$ is independent of a theory $T$ if neither $\varphi$ nor its negation can be proved from $T$:

$$
T \nvdash \varphi
$$

and

$$
T \nvdash ¬\varphi
$$

assuming $T$ is consistent.

Independence shows that some mathematical questions cannot be settled from a given axiom system alone. The most famous example is the continuum hypothesis, which concerns the size of the real numbers.

The continuum hypothesis says that there is no set whose cardinality lies strictly between the natural numbers and the real numbers:

$$
\nexists A,
\left(
|\mathbb{N}| < |A| < |\mathbb{R}|
\right)
$$

Gödel showed that CH cannot be disproved from ZFC, assuming ZFC is consistent. Cohen later showed that CH cannot be proved from ZFC, again assuming consistency. Together, these results show that CH is independent of ZFC.

This changes how we understand foundations. Some questions are decided by the usual axioms. Other questions require stronger axioms, alternative axioms, or a broader view of mathematical universes.

Chapter 8 develops this shift carefully. It begins with the Axiom of Choice and its equivalent forms, then introduces $L$, relative consistency, and independence. The goal is to understand set theory not only as a theory of sets, but also as a theory of possible mathematical universes.
