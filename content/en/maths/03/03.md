## Chapter 3. Proof Systems

A proof system is a formal method for deriving conclusions from assumptions. In propositional and first-order logic, proof systems make reasoning explicit. Instead of saying that an argument “looks valid,” we specify rules that determine exactly which steps are allowed.

The central relation is syntactic consequence:

$$
\Gamma \vdash A
$$

This means that $A$ can be proved from the assumptions in $\Gamma$.

Here, $\Gamma$ is a set of formulas, and $A$ is the conclusion. The symbol $\vdash$ belongs to proof theory. It refers to derivability by rules, not truth under interpretations.

This contrasts with semantic consequence:

$$
\Gamma \models A
$$

which means that every interpretation making all formulas in $\Gamma$ true also makes $A$ true.

So the two main notions are:

| Notion                | Symbol             | Meaning                                |
| --------------------- | ------------------ | -------------------------------------- |
| Syntactic consequence | $\Gamma \vdash A$  | $A$ has a formal proof from $\Gamma$   |
| Semantic consequence  | $\Gamma \models A$ | $A$ is true in every model of $\Gamma$ |

A good proof system should connect these two notions. Soundness says that proofs preserve truth:

$$
\Gamma \vdash A \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \Gamma \models A
$$

Completeness says that every semantic consequence can be proved:

$$
\Gamma \models A \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \Gamma \vdash A
$$

Together, these results say that formal derivability and semantic consequence match.

This chapter studies three major styles of proof systems.

### Natural Deduction

Natural deduction is designed to resemble ordinary mathematical reasoning. It uses introduction and elimination rules for each connective and quantifier.

For example, conjunction has an introduction rule:

$$
\frac{A \qquad B}{A \land B}
$$

If you have proved $A$ and proved $B$, then you may conclude $A \land B$.

It also has elimination rules:

$$
\frac{A \land B}{A}
\qquad
\frac{A \land B}{B}
$$

If you have proved $A \land B$, then you may use either part.

Natural deduction is friendly for learning because each connective has rules explaining how to create it and how to use it.

### Sequent Calculus

Sequent calculus represents reasoning with sequents of the form:

$$
\Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta
$$

The left side contains assumptions. The right side contains possible conclusions.

A sequent can be read as:

$$
\text{if all formulas in } \Gamma \text{ hold, then at least one formula in } \Delta \text{ holds}
$$

Sequent calculus is more structural than natural deduction. It makes assumptions, contexts, weakening, contraction, and exchange explicit. This makes it powerful for proving meta-theorems about logic.

A typical rule for conjunction on the right is:

$$
\frac{\Gamma \Rightarrow A \qquad \Gamma \Rightarrow B}{\Gamma \Rightarrow A \land B}
$$

This says that to prove $A \land B$ from $\Gamma$, prove $A$ from $\Gamma$ and prove $B$ from $\Gamma$.

### Hilbert Systems

Hilbert systems use many axioms and very few inference rules. A typical Hilbert system may have only one main rule, such as modus ponens:

$$
\frac{A \qquad A \to B}{B}
$$

Most of the logical work is encoded in axiom schemas.

For example, one axiom schema for implication is:

$$
A \to (B \to A)
$$

This represents infinitely many formulas, one for each choice of $A$ and $B$.

Hilbert systems are compact and elegant, but they are less convenient for ordinary proof writing. Their main value is theoretical. They make it easy to define proof systems with a small number of primitive rules.

### Proof Transformations

Different proof systems can prove the same logical truths, but their proofs may look very different.

A natural deduction proof may use temporary assumptions. A sequent calculus proof may manipulate contexts. A Hilbert proof may proceed through axiom schemas and repeated applications of modus ponens.

Proof transformations study how one proof can be converted into another. They also study how unnecessary detours can be removed.

For example, in natural deduction, it may happen that a proof introduces a connective and immediately eliminates it. Such a proof contains a local redundancy. Normalization removes these redundant steps.

### Cut-Elimination

Cut-elimination is one of the central results in proof theory.

The cut rule has the general form:

$$
\frac{\Gamma \Rightarrow A \qquad A, \Pi \Rightarrow \Delta}{\Gamma, \Pi \Rightarrow \Delta}
$$

It says that if $\Gamma$ proves $A$, and $A$ helps prove $\Delta$, then we may skip the intermediate formula $A$ and conclude $\Delta$ directly from $\Gamma$ and $\Pi$.

The formula $A$ is called the cut formula.

Cut is useful because it matches how mathematicians often reason. We prove an intermediate lemma, then use it later. But from a structural point of view, cut can hide the direct shape of a proof.

The cut-elimination theorem says that, under suitable conditions, every proof using cut can be transformed into a proof without cut.

This matters because cut-free proofs have strong structural properties. They expose the logical shape of the conclusion and often imply consistency, decidability, and interpolation results.

### Role of This Chapter

Proof systems give logic its symbolic machinery. They let us define proof as a finite object, compare different calculi, and prove that formal reasoning matches semantic truth.
