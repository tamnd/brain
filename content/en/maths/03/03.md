Proof systems provide a formal method for deriving conclusions from assumptions using explicit rules, and they complement the semantic viewpoint by giving a syntactic notion of reasoning that can be carried out step by step without referring to truth values in a structure.

In propositional and first order logic, a proof system specifies a collection of inference rules together with a notion of derivation, and these rules describe how new formulas may be obtained from previously established ones in a way that preserves logical correctness.

The chapter begins with natural deduction, which models reasoning in a way that closely follows informal mathematical arguments, where assumptions are introduced and later discharged, and where each logical connective is governed by introduction and elimination rules.

The sequent calculus is then introduced as a system that represents derivations as transformations of sequents, and this framework makes the structure of proofs more explicit while supporting powerful meta results such as cut elimination.

Hilbert systems are also studied, where proofs are constructed from a small number of axiom schemas together with a few inference rules, and although these systems are less intuitive, they are technically simple and well suited for formal analysis.

The chapter then examines proof transformations, where proofs are converted into different forms without changing their conclusions, and this allows one to compare different proof systems and to analyze the complexity and structure of derivations.

A central result is cut elimination, which shows that certain intermediate steps in proofs can be removed, leading to proofs that have a more canonical form and revealing deep connections between syntax and semantics.

These systems provide the formal machinery for reasoning in logic, and they establish the foundation for proof theory, where the structure and properties of proofs themselves become objects of mathematical study.
