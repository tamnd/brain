## 2.1 Terms, Predicates, Formulas

First-order logic has more internal structure than propositional logic. Instead of treating each statement as an indivisible symbol, it lets us build statements from objects, functions, and relations.

A first-order language usually contains:

| Component         | Role                                       | Example               |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------ | --------------------- |
| Variables         | stand for objects                          | $x, y, z$             |
| Constants         | name specific objects                      | $0, 1, e$             |
| Function symbols  | build new terms from old terms             | $S(x), x+y$           |
| Predicate symbols | express properties or relations            | $P(x), x<y$           |
| Equality          | states that two terms name the same object | $x=y$                 |
| Connectives       | combine formulas                           | $¬, \land, \lor, \to$ |
| Quantifiers       | bind variables                             | $\forall, \exists$    |

### Terms

A **term** is an expression that names an object.

Terms are built recursively.

1. Every variable is a term.
2. Every constant symbol is a term.
3. If $f$ is an $n$-ary function symbol and $t_1,\dots,t_n$ are terms, then
   $$
   f(t_1,\dots,t_n)
   $$
   is a term.
4. Nothing else is a term.

Examples:

$$
x
$$

$$
0
$$

$$
S(x)
$$

$$
x+y
$$

$$
(x+y)\times z
$$

A term does not state a claim. It refers to an object, once an interpretation is fixed.

For example:

$$
x+1
$$

is a term. It names an object depending on the value assigned to $x$.

### Predicate Symbols

A **predicate symbol** represents a property or relation.

A unary predicate applies to one term:

$$
\mathrm{Prime}(x)
$$

This says that $x$ has the property of being prime.

A binary predicate applies to two terms:

$$
x < y
$$

This says that $x$ is related to $y$ by the less-than relation.

A ternary predicate applies to three terms:

$$
\mathrm{Between}(x,y,z)
$$

This may be read as “$y$ lies between $x$ and $z$”.

The number of inputs a predicate takes is its **arity**.

| Predicate                 | Arity | Reading                    |
| ------------------------- | ----- | -------------------------- |
| $\mathrm{Prime}(x)$       | 1     | $x$ is prime               |
| $x<y$                     | 2     | $x$ is less than $y$       |
| $\mathrm{Between}(x,y,z)$ | 3     | $y$ is between $x$ and $z$ |

### Atomic Formulas

An **atomic formula** is the simplest kind of formula.

If $R$ is an $n$-ary predicate symbol and $t_1,\dots,t_n$ are terms, then:

$$
R(t_1,\dots,t_n)
$$

is an atomic formula.

Equality also forms atomic formulas:

$$
t_1 = t_2
$$

Examples:

$$
\mathrm{Even}(x)
$$

$$
x < y
$$

$$
x + 0 = x
$$

$$
\mathrm{Divides}(d,n)
$$

Atomic formulas are the first expressions that can be true or false after we choose a structure and assign values to variables.

### Formulas

Formulas are built from atomic formulas using logical connectives and quantifiers.

1. Every atomic formula is a formula.
2. If $A$ is a formula, then $¬A$ is a formula.
3. If $A$ and $B$ are formulas, then:
   $$
   (A \land B),\quad (A \lor B),\quad (A \to B),\quad (A \leftrightarrow B)
   $$
   are formulas.
4. If $A$ is a formula and $x$ is a variable, then:
   $$
   \forall x,A
   $$
   and
   $$
   \exists x,A
   $$
   are formulas.
5. Nothing else is a formula.

Examples:

$$
x < y
$$

$$
\mathrm{Prime}(x) \land x>2
$$

$$
\forall x,(x=x)
$$

$$
\exists y,(y>x)
$$

$$
\forall x,(\mathrm{Even}(x) \to \exists y,(x=2y))
$$

The last formula says: for every $x$, if $x$ is even, then there exists a $y$ such that $x=2y$.

### Free and Bound Variables

Quantifiers bind variables.

In:

$$
\forall x,(x>0)
$$

the variable $x$ is bound by $\forall x$.

In:

$$
x>0
$$

the variable $x$ is free.

A variable is **free** if it is not controlled by a quantifier. A variable is **bound** if it occurs within the scope of a quantifier for that variable.

Example:

$$
\forall x,(x<y)
$$

Here $x$ is bound, but $y$ is free.

The formula makes a claim about all $x$, but it still depends on the chosen value of $y$.

### Sentences

A **sentence** is a formula with no free variables.

Examples:

$$
\forall x,(x=x)
$$

$$
\exists x,\mathrm{Prime}(x)
$$

$$
\forall x,\exists y,(x<y)
$$

These can be true or false in a structure.

By contrast:

$$
x<y
$$

and

$$
\exists y,(x<y)
$$

are not sentences, because $x$ remains free.

A formula with free variables behaves like an open condition. A sentence behaves like a complete statement.
