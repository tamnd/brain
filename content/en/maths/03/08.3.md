The constructible universe is a class of sets built in stages by allowing only those sets that can be explicitly defined from earlier stages. It is denoted by:
$$
L
$$

The idea is to form a universe of sets that is as small as possible while still satisfying the usual axioms of set theory. At each stage, instead of taking all subsets of the previous stage, one takes only the subsets that are definable over the previous stage by formulas of set theory.

This construction is important because it gives a precise inner model of set theory. It also provides a setting in which both the axiom of choice and the continuum hypothesis hold.

### Definable Subsets

To define the constructible hierarchy, we first need the notion of a definable subset.

### Definition 8.39 (Definable Subset)

Let $M$ be a set. A subset $X \subseteq M$ is definable over $M$ if there is a formula:
$$
\varphi(x,a_1,\dots,a_n)
$$
in the language of set theory, with parameters:
$$
a_1,\dots,a_n \in M
$$
such that:
$$
X = \{x \in M : (M,\in) \models \varphi(x,a_1,\dots,a_n)\}
$$

Thus a definable subset of $M$ is a subset of $M$ that can be described by a first order formula using parameters from $M$.

The parameters are important. Without parameters, only very few subsets would be definable. With parameters, the definition allows the construction to remember information already present in $M$.

### Definition 8.40 (The Definable Power Set)

Let $M$ be a set. The definable power set of $M$ is:
$$
\operatorname{Def}(M)
$$
where:
$$
\operatorname{Def}(M)
=
\{X \subseteq M : X \text{ is definable over } M \text{ with parameters from } M\}
$$

This should be compared with the ordinary power set:
$$
\mathcal{P}(M)
$$

In general:
$$
\operatorname{Def}(M) \subseteq \mathcal{P}(M)
$$

The inclusion may be proper, because not every subset of $M$ has to be definable over $M$.

### Example 8.41

Let:
$$
M=\{0,1,2\}
$$

If the elements of $M$ are available as parameters, then each singleton:
$$
\{0\}, \quad \{1\}, \quad \{2\}
$$
is definable over $M$ by a formula of the form:
$$
x=a
$$

Finite unions of definable sets are also definable, so every subset of this finite set is definable over $M$ using parameters.

Thus in this finite example:
$$
\operatorname{Def}(M)=\mathcal{P}(M)
$$

For infinite sets, the situation is more subtle, because there may be many more subsets than there are formulas and parameters available to define them.

### The Constructible Hierarchy

The class $L$ is built by transfinite recursion along the ordinals.

### Definition 8.42 (The Constructible Hierarchy)

The constructible hierarchy is the sequence:
$$
(L_\alpha)_{\alpha \in \mathrm{Ord}}
$$
defined by transfinite recursion as follows.

At stage $0$:
$$
L_0=\varnothing
$$

At a successor stage:
$$
L_{\alpha+1}=\operatorname{Def}(L_\alpha)
$$

At a limit ordinal $\lambda$:
$$
L_\lambda=\bigcup_{\alpha<\lambda} L_\alpha
$$

The constructible universe is the class:
$$
L=\bigcup_{\alpha \in \mathrm{Ord}} L_\alpha
$$

Each stage $L_\alpha$ contains the sets that have been constructed before stage $\alpha$.

The successor step is the key point. In the cumulative hierarchy $V$, one forms:
$$
V_{\alpha+1}=\mathcal{P}(V_\alpha)
$$

In the constructible hierarchy, one forms only:
$$
L_{\alpha+1}=\operatorname{Def}(L_\alpha)
$$

Thus $L$ grows more slowly than $V$ because it includes only definable subsets at each stage.

### Example 8.43 (First Stages)

The first stage is:
$$
L_0=\varnothing
$$

Since the only subset of $\varnothing$ is $\varnothing$, and it is definable, we have:
$$
L_1=\{\varnothing\}
$$

The next stage is:
$$
L_2=\operatorname{Def}(L_1)
$$

Since:
$$
L_1=\{\varnothing\}
$$
its subsets are:
$$
\varnothing
\quad \text{and} \quad
\{\varnothing\}
$$

Both are definable over $L_1$, so:
$$
L_2=\{\varnothing,\{\varnothing\}\}
$$

The early levels resemble the finite stages of the usual cumulative hierarchy. The real difference becomes important at infinite stages, where the ordinary power set is usually much larger than the definable power set.

### Lemma 8.44 (Monotonicity)

If $\alpha \le \beta$, then:
$$
L_\alpha \subseteq L_\beta
$$

Proof

We prove the statement by transfinite induction on $\beta$.

If $\beta=0$, then $\alpha=0$, and:
$$
L_\alpha=L_\beta
$$
so the result is immediate.

Suppose $\beta=\gamma+1$. If $\alpha=\beta$, there is nothing to prove. If $\alpha<\beta$, then $\alpha \le \gamma$. By the induction hypothesis:
$$
L_\alpha \subseteq L_\gamma
$$

Every element of $L_\gamma$ is definable over $L_\gamma$ using itself as a parameter. More explicitly, if $x \in L_\gamma$, then the singleton condition:
$$
y=x
$$
defines $\{x\}$ over $L_\gamma$, and hence $x$ appears as an element of a definable subset of $L_\gamma$. Therefore elements already present at stage $\gamma$ remain present at the next stage, so:
$$
L_\gamma \subseteq L_{\gamma+1}
$$

Hence:
$$
L_\alpha \subseteq L_{\gamma+1}=L_\beta
$$

Now suppose $\beta$ is a limit ordinal. Then:
$$
L_\beta=\bigcup_{\gamma<\beta}L_\gamma
$$

If $\alpha<\beta$, then $L_\alpha$ is one of the terms in this union, and therefore:
$$
L_\alpha \subseteq L_\beta
$$

If $\alpha=\beta$, the result is immediate.

Thus $L_\alpha \subseteq L_\beta$ whenever $\alpha \le \beta$.

### Rank in the Constructible Hierarchy

Each constructible set first appears at some level of the hierarchy.

### Definition 8.45 (Constructible Rank)

If $x \in L$, the constructible rank of $x$ is the least ordinal $\alpha$ such that:
$$
x \in L_{\alpha+1}
$$

Equivalently, it is the first stage at which $x$ is constructed as an element of the next level.

The use of $L_{\alpha+1}$ rather than $L_\alpha$ reflects the fact that objects at level $L_{\alpha+1}$ are definable subsets of $L_\alpha$.

### Lemma 8.46

For every ordinal $\alpha$:
$$
L_\alpha \subseteq V_\alpha
$$

where $(V_\alpha)$ is the usual cumulative hierarchy.

Proof

We prove this by transfinite induction on $\alpha$.

For $\alpha=0$, we have:
$$
L_0=\varnothing=V_0
$$
so the result is immediate.

Suppose the result holds for $\alpha$. We show:
$$
L_{\alpha+1}\subseteq V_{\alpha+1}
$$

By definition:
$$
L_{\alpha+1}=\operatorname{Def}(L_\alpha)
$$

Every element of $L_{\alpha+1}$ is a subset of $L_\alpha$. By the induction hypothesis:
$$
L_\alpha \subseteq V_\alpha
$$

Hence every subset of $L_\alpha$ is also a subset of $V_\alpha$, so:
$$
L_{\alpha+1}\subseteq \mathcal{P}(V_\alpha)
$$

But:
$$
V_{\alpha+1}=\mathcal{P}(V_\alpha)
$$

Therefore:
$$
L_{\alpha+1}\subseteq V_{\alpha+1}
$$

Now suppose $\lambda$ is a limit ordinal and that:
$$
L_\alpha \subseteq V_\alpha
$$
for all $\alpha<\lambda$.

Then:
$$
L_\lambda=\bigcup_{\alpha<\lambda}L_\alpha
$$

and:
$$
V_\lambda=\bigcup_{\alpha<\lambda}V_\alpha
$$

Since each $L_\alpha$ is contained in $V_\alpha$, it follows that:
$$
L_\lambda\subseteq V_\lambda
$$

This completes the induction.

### Transitivity

The levels $L_\alpha$ are transitive sets.

### Definition 8.47 (Transitive Set)

A set $M$ is transitive if:
$$
x \in y \in M
$$
implies:
$$
x \in M
$$

Equivalently:
$$
\bigcup M \subseteq M
$$

A transitive set contains the elements of its elements.

### Lemma 8.48

For every ordinal $\alpha$, the set $L_\alpha$ is transitive.

Proof

We prove the result by transfinite induction on $\alpha$.

For $\alpha=0$, we have:
$$
L_0=\varnothing
$$
which is transitive.

Suppose $L_\alpha$ is transitive. We show that $L_{\alpha+1}$ is transitive.

Let:
$$
x \in y \in L_{\alpha+1}
$$

Since:
$$
L_{\alpha+1}=\operatorname{Def}(L_\alpha)
$$
each element of $L_{\alpha+1}$ is a subset of $L_\alpha$. Therefore:
$$
y \subseteq L_\alpha
$$

Since $x \in y$, it follows that:
$$
x \in L_\alpha
$$

By monotonicity:
$$
L_\alpha \subseteq L_{\alpha+1}
$$

Thus:
$$
x \in L_{\alpha+1}
$$

Therefore $L_{\alpha+1}$ is transitive.

Now suppose $\lambda$ is a limit ordinal and each $L_\alpha$ is transitive for $\alpha<\lambda$. Let:
$$
x \in y \in L_\lambda
$$

Since:
$$
L_\lambda=\bigcup_{\alpha<\lambda}L_\alpha
$$
there exists $\alpha<\lambda$ such that:
$$
y \in L_\alpha
$$

Because $L_\alpha$ is transitive:
$$
x \in L_\alpha
$$

Hence:
$$
x \in L_\lambda
$$

Thus $L_\lambda$ is transitive.

By transfinite induction, every $L_\alpha$ is transitive.

### The Class L

The constructible universe is:
$$
L=\bigcup_{\alpha \in \mathrm{Ord}}L_\alpha
$$

A set $x$ is called constructible if:
$$
x \in L
$$

This means that $x$ appears at some stage of the constructible hierarchy.

The statement that every set is constructible is written:
$$
V=L
$$

This is called the axiom of constructibility.

### Definition 8.49 (Axiom of Constructibility)

The axiom of constructibility is the statement:
$$
V=L
$$

It says that every set belongs to the constructible universe.

In words, every set can be obtained by iterating definability along the ordinals.

The axiom $V=L$ is stronger than the axiom of choice. It implies that the universe of sets has a very rigid and well ordered structure.

### Theorem 8.50

If $V=L$, then the axiom of choice holds.

Proof

Assume:
$$
V=L
$$

We will show that every set can be well ordered. Since the well ordering theorem is equivalent to the axiom of choice, this will imply the axiom of choice.

Each constructible set appears at some first level of the constructible hierarchy. For each set $x$, since $V=L$, there exists an ordinal $\alpha$ such that:
$$
x \in L_{\alpha+1}
$$

Let $\rho(x)$ be the least such ordinal. This gives a rank-like measure of how early $x$ appears in $L$.

At each stage $L_{\alpha+1}$, elements are definable subsets of $L_\alpha$. The formulas of set theory can be coded by natural numbers, and parameters from $L_\alpha$ can be ordered by the construction already made at earlier stages. Therefore one can define a canonical ordering of the new sets appearing at each stage by ordering their defining formulas and parameters.

Combining these stagewise orders gives a global well ordering of $L$. Since:
$$
V=L
$$
this is a global well ordering of the universe of sets.

Hence every set can be well ordered by restricting this global well order to that set.

By the well ordering theorem, the axiom of choice follows.

### Remark 8.51

The proof above uses the fact that formulas can be coded and that definitions can be ordered in a canonical way. The full formal proof requires coding syntax inside set theory, but the guiding idea is straightforward: constructible sets are built in a controlled order, so they can be globally well ordered by the first stage and first definition at which they appear.

### The Constructible Universe as an Inner Model

The class $L$ is not merely a collection of sets. It behaves like a universe of set theory inside the full universe $V$.

### Definition 8.52 (Inner Model)

An inner model is a transitive class $M$ such that:

1. $M$ contains all ordinals.

2. $M$ satisfies the axioms of ZF.

3. Membership in $M$ is the ordinary membership relation inherited from $V$.

The requirement that all ordinals belong to $M$ ensures that the model has the same ordinal scale as the surrounding universe.

### Theorem 8.53

The class $L$ is an inner model of ZF.

Proof

We give the main proof idea, since a fully detailed verification requires checking each axiom of ZF separately.

First, $L$ is transitive because each level $L_\alpha$ is transitive and:
$$
L=\bigcup_{\alpha \in \mathrm{Ord}}L_\alpha
$$

Second, every ordinal belongs to $L$. This follows by induction on ordinals. The empty set belongs to $L_1$, and if all earlier ordinals have been constructed, then an ordinal is the set of its predecessors and appears at a later constructible stage.

Third, the axioms of ZF hold inside $L$ because the construction is closed under the operations required by the axioms. Pairing, union, separation, replacement, infinity, foundation, and extensionality can all be verified by showing that the relevant sets appear at sufficiently high constructible levels.

For example, if $a,b \in L$, then both appear in some $L_\alpha$. Since $L_\alpha$ is transitive and the set:
$$
\{a,b\}
$$
is definable over a sufficiently large level using parameters $a$ and $b$, it follows that:
$$
\{a,b\} \in L
$$

Similarly, if $x \in L$, then:
$$
\bigcup x
$$
is definable over a sufficiently large level containing $x$, so:
$$
\bigcup x \in L
$$

The separation axiom holds because definable subsets of constructible sets are exactly the kind of objects added at successor stages.

Thus $L$ satisfies the axioms of ZF and is an inner model.

### Constructibility and Consistency

The main use of $L$ is in relative consistency results.

If ZF is consistent, then ZF together with $V=L$ is also consistent. Since $V=L$ implies the axiom of choice and the continuum hypothesis, this gives relative consistency results for those principles.

### Theorem 8.54 (Godel's Constructibility Theorem)

If ZF is consistent, then:
$$
\mathrm{ZF} + V=L
$$
is consistent.

Consequently, if ZF is consistent, then:
$$
\mathrm{ZFC}
$$
is consistent, and:
$$
\mathrm{ZFC} + \mathrm{CH}
$$
is consistent.

Proof

The idea is to interpret set theory inside the constructible universe $L$.

Assume there is a model of ZF. Inside that model, construct the class $L$ by the definability hierarchy. The preceding theorem says that $L$ satisfies ZF. Moreover, by its very construction, every set of $L$ is constructible inside $L$, so:
$$
L \models V=L
$$

Therefore:
$$
L \models \mathrm{ZF} + V=L
$$

This shows that if ZF has a model, then ZF plus $V=L$ has a model. Hence the consistency of ZF implies the consistency of:
$$
\mathrm{ZF} + V=L
$$

Since $V=L$ implies the axiom of choice, this also gives the relative consistency of ZFC. Since $V=L$ also implies the continuum hypothesis, it gives the relative consistency of:
$$
\mathrm{ZFC} + \mathrm{CH}
$$

### Remark 8.55

The theorem does not prove that $V=L$ is true. It proves that $V=L$ cannot be disproved from ZF if ZF itself is consistent. Later independence results show that the negation of the continuum hypothesis also cannot be disproved from ZFC, assuming suitable consistency hypotheses.

### The Role of Definability

The central feature of $L$ is definability. At each stage:
$$
L_{\alpha+1}=\operatorname{Def}(L_\alpha)
$$

This rule makes $L$ sparse compared with $V$, because:
$$
\operatorname{Def}(L_\alpha)
$$
may be much smaller than:
$$
\mathcal{P}(L_\alpha)
$$

Nevertheless, $L$ is rich enough to satisfy all axioms of ZF. This tension is what makes the constructible universe powerful: it is small enough to have a canonical well ordering, but large enough to be a full inner model of set theory.
