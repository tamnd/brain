## 8.3 Constructible Universe $(L)$

The **constructible universe** is a special inner universe of set theory. It is usually denoted by:

$$
L
$$

The idea is to build sets in stages, but only include sets that can be explicitly defined from earlier stages. This gives a very disciplined universe of sets.

The full set-theoretic universe is usually denoted:

$$
V
$$

The constructible universe $L$ sits inside $V$:

$$
L \subseteq V
$$

Every constructible set is a set, but not every set must be constructible.

### Why Constructibility Matters

The universe $L$ was introduced by Kurt Gödel. It is important because it gives a model of set theory where many difficult statements become decidable.

In particular, Gödel showed that if ZF is consistent, then:

$$
\mathrm{ZF} + \mathrm{AC} + \mathrm{CH}
$$

is also consistent.

Here $\mathrm{CH}$ is the continuum hypothesis.

The strategy is to show that inside $L$, both the Axiom of Choice and the continuum hypothesis hold.

So $L$ gives relative consistency results such as:

$$
\mathrm{Con}(\mathrm{ZF})
\to
\mathrm{Con}(\mathrm{ZFC})
$$

and:

$$
\mathrm{Con}(\mathrm{ZF})
\to
\mathrm{Con}(\mathrm{ZFC} + \mathrm{CH})
$$

The symbol $\mathrm{Con}(T)$ means that the theory $T$ is consistent.

### The Stage Construction

The constructible universe is built by transfinite recursion.

We define levels:

$$
L_\alpha
$$

for ordinals $\alpha$.

The first level is empty:

$$
L_0 = \varnothing
$$

At a successor stage, we take the definable subsets of the previous stage:

$$
L_{\alpha+1} = \mathrm{Def}(L_\alpha)
$$

Here $\mathrm{Def}(L_\alpha)$ means the collection of subsets of $L_\alpha$ that are first-order definable over the structure:

$$
(L_\alpha,\in)
$$

using parameters from $L_\alpha$.

At a limit ordinal $\lambda$, we take the union of all previous stages:

$$
L_\lambda = \bigcup_{\alpha < \lambda} L_\alpha
$$

Finally, the constructible universe is:

$$
L = \bigcup_{\alpha \in \mathrm{Ord}} L_\alpha
$$

where $\mathrm{Ord}$ is the class of all ordinals.

### What Definable Means

A subset $X \subseteq L_\alpha$ belongs to $L_{\alpha+1}$ if there is a first-order formula $\varphi(x,\vec{a})$ and parameters $\vec{a}$ from $L_\alpha$ such that:

$$
X = {x \in L_\alpha : (L_\alpha,\in) \models \varphi(x,\vec{a})}
$$

So $X$ is included in the next level if it can be described using the language of set theory over the previous level.

This is the key restriction. In the usual cumulative hierarchy, the next stage is:

$$
V_{\alpha+1} = \mathcal{P}(V_\alpha)
$$

which includes all subsets of $V_\alpha$.

In the constructible hierarchy, the next stage is:

$$
L_{\alpha+1} = \mathrm{Def}(L_\alpha)
$$

which includes only definable subsets of $L_\alpha$.

Since not every subset is necessarily definable, $L_{\alpha+1}$ may be much smaller than $V_{\alpha+1}$.

### Comparing $V$ and $L$

The cumulative hierarchy $V$ is built as follows:

$$
V_0 = \varnothing
$$

$$
V_{\alpha+1} = \mathcal{P}(V_\alpha)
$$

$$
V_\lambda = \bigcup_{\alpha < \lambda} V_\alpha
$$

for limit ordinals $\lambda$.

The constructible hierarchy replaces power sets with definable subsets:

$$
L_{\alpha+1} = \mathrm{Def}(L_\alpha)
$$

This gives the comparison:

| Universe | Successor stage                       | Meaning           |
| -------- | ------------------------------------- | ----------------- |
| $V$      | $V_{\alpha+1}=\mathcal{P}(V_\alpha)$  | all subsets       |
| $L$      | $L_{\alpha+1}=\mathrm{Def}(L_\alpha)$ | definable subsets |

So $L$ is like a minimal, carefully generated version of the universe.

### Early Stages

The early stages help make the construction more concrete.

Start with:

$$
L_0=\varnothing
$$

The only subset of $\varnothing$ is $\varnothing$ itself, and it is definable, so:

$$
L_1={\varnothing}
$$

The next levels contain sets built from what came before:

$$
L_2 = \mathrm{Def}(L_1)
$$

Since $L_1$ has one element, its subsets are:

$$
\varnothing,\quad {\varnothing}
$$

Both are definable, so:

$$
L_2 = {\varnothing,{\varnothing}}
$$

As the hierarchy continues, it builds finite sets, then infinite sets, and then more complex sets through ordinal stages.

### Constructible Sets

A set $x$ is **constructible** if it appears at some level $L_\alpha$:

$$
x \in L
\quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad
\exists \alpha,(x \in L_\alpha)
$$

The **constructible rank** of $x$ is the least ordinal $\alpha$ such that:

$$
x \in L_{\alpha+1}
$$

This rank tells us when $x$ first appears in the constructible hierarchy.

### The Axiom $V=L$

The statement:

$$
V=L
$$

says that every set is constructible.

Equivalently:

$$
\forall x, \exists \alpha,(x \in L_\alpha)
$$

This is a strong additional axiom. It says that the set-theoretic universe contains no sets beyond those generated by definability through the constructible hierarchy.

If $V=L$, then both the Axiom of Choice and the continuum hypothesis hold.

In fact, $V=L$ implies a stronger statement called the generalized continuum hypothesis:

$$
\mathrm{GCH}
$$

which says that for every infinite cardinal $\kappa$:

$$
2^\kappa = \kappa^+
$$

Here $\kappa^+$ is the next cardinal after $\kappa$.

### Why $L$ Satisfies Choice

Inside $L$, sets appear in a very ordered way. Each constructible set appears at a first stage, and definability gives a way to compare how sets are generated.

This allows one to define a global well-ordering of $L$:

$$
<_L
$$

This order arranges all constructible sets in a single well-order.

Once we have a global well-order, choice follows. Given any family of nonempty sets, choose the $<_L$-least element from each set.

So inside $L$:

$$
\mathrm{AC}
$$

holds.

### Why $L$ Satisfies CH

The continuum hypothesis says:

$$
2^{\aleph_0} = \aleph_1
$$

Equivalently, every set of real numbers is either countable or has the same size as $\mathbb{R}$, and there is no intermediate cardinality between:

$$
\aleph_0
$$

and:

$$
2^{\aleph_0}
$$

In $L$, the real numbers appear gradually through countable stages. The definability restriction controls how many reals can appear before $\omega_1$.

This allows Gödel to show:

$$
L \models \mathrm{CH}
$$

More generally:

$$
L \models \mathrm{GCH}
$$

So $L$ is a universe where the continuum has the smallest possible uncountable size.

### Inner Models

The constructible universe is an example of an **inner model**.

An inner model is a class $M$ such that:

$$
M \subseteq V
$$

and $M$ satisfies enough set-theoretic axioms internally.

More precisely, $M$ is usually required to be transitive and to contain all ordinals:

$$
\mathrm{Ord} \subseteq M
$$

The universe $L$ has these properties.

It is transitive:

$$
x \in y \in L \Longrightarrow x \in L
$$

and it contains every ordinal.

So $L$ is not just a random subclass of $V$. It is a structured universe where set theory can be interpreted.

### Absoluteness

One reason $L$ is useful is that many statements are absolute between $L$ and $V$.

A statement is **absolute** between two structures if it has the same truth value in both.

For example, basic facts about ordinals are absolute between $L$ and $V$.

If $L$ says that $\alpha$ is an ordinal, then $V$ agrees. If $V$ says that $\alpha$ is an ordinal and $\alpha \in L$, then $L$ agrees.

Absoluteness lets mathematicians compare the constructible universe with the larger universe.

### Philosophical Meaning

The statement $V=L$ gives a very lean picture of sets. It says that every set is generated by definability from earlier sets.

Some mathematicians like this because it gives a precise and orderly universe.

Others reject it as too restrictive, because it excludes many set-theoretic possibilities. For example, large cardinal axioms often conflict with $V=L$ or become trivialized inside $L$.

So $L$ is both a technical tool and a foundational viewpoint.

Technically, it proves consistency results.

Foundationally, it shows that the usual axioms do not uniquely determine the universe of sets.
