## Chapter 11. Turing Machines

Turing machines give a precise and minimal model of computation. They answer a basic question: what does it mean for a problem to be solvable by an algorithm? The model strips computation down to a few primitive operations, yet it can express any effective procedure.

A Turing machine operates on a tape that serves as unbounded memory. It reads and writes symbols and moves step by step according to a fixed transition function. Every computation becomes a sequence of configurations, each determined by the current state, the tape contents, and the head position. This stepwise view makes algorithms fully explicit and analyzable.

The chapter develops the formal definition of machines and shows how computations unfold as traces. It then introduces universal machines, which simulate any other machine given an encoding. This idea establishes a separation between hardware and program and explains how a single device can execute arbitrary procedures.

A central theme is the existence of limits. The halting problem shows that some well-posed questions cannot be decided by any Turing machine. The proof uses self-reference and diagonalization to construct a contradiction. From this starting point, many other undecidable problems are obtained by reduction, showing that undecidability is widespread rather than exceptional.

The chapter positions Turing machines as a reference model. They define the boundary between what can be computed and what cannot, independent of implementation details. This boundary supports later work in computability, complexity, and the theory of programming languages.
