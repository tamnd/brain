## 4.3 Elementary Equivalence

Two structures may be different as mathematical objects but still satisfy exactly the same first-order sentences. This idea is called **elementary equivalence**.

Let $\mathcal M$ and $\mathcal N$ be structures for the same language $\mathcal L$. We say that $\mathcal M$ and $\mathcal N$ are elementarily equivalent if, for every sentence $\varphi$ of $\mathcal L$,

$$
\mathcal M \models \varphi
\quad \text{if and only if} \quad
\mathcal N \models \varphi .
$$

We write:

$$
\mathcal M \equiv \mathcal N .
$$

The symbol $\equiv$ means that the two structures cannot be distinguished by first-order sentences in the chosen language.

### Sentences Matter

Elementary equivalence compares **sentences**, not formulas with free variables.

A sentence has no free variables. For example:

$$
\forall x, \exists y, (x < y)
$$

is a sentence.

But:

$$
x < y
$$

is not a sentence, because $x$ and $y$ are free.

This distinction matters because a formula with free variables needs an assignment of values to those variables. A sentence has a truth value in a structure by itself.

### Example: Dense Linear Orders

Consider the language:

$$
\mathcal L = {<}.
$$

The structure:

$$
(\mathbb Q; <)
$$

is the rational numbers with their usual order.

The structure:

$$
(\mathbb R; <)
$$

is the real numbers with their usual order.

These two structures are not isomorphic, because $\mathbb Q$ is countable and $\mathbb R$ is uncountable.

However, as pure dense linear orders without endpoints, they satisfy the same first-order sentences:

$$
(\mathbb Q; <) \equiv (\mathbb R; <).
$$

First-order logic in the language ${<}$ can express properties such as:

$$
\forall x, \exists y, (x < y)
$$

which says there is no largest element.

It can also express density:

$$
\forall x, \forall y, \bigl(x < y \to \exists z, (x < z \land z < y)\bigr).
$$

But it cannot express that the domain is countable or uncountable. So it cannot separate $\mathbb Q$ from $\mathbb R$ in this language.

### Elementary Equivalence vs Isomorphism

Isomorphism is stronger than elementary equivalence.

If two structures are isomorphic, then they satisfy exactly the same first-order sentences:

$$
\mathcal M \cong \mathcal N
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
\mathcal M \equiv \mathcal N .
$$

This is because an isomorphism preserves all structure named by the language.

The converse does not always hold:

$$
\mathcal M \equiv \mathcal N
\quad \not\Longrightarrow \quad
\mathcal M \cong \mathcal N .
$$

Elementary equivalence says the structures agree on all first-order sentences. Isomorphism says they are structurally identical up to renaming elements.

So we have a useful hierarchy:

$$
\text{isomorphism} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \text{elementary equivalence}.
$$

But elementary equivalence allows more freedom.

### Complete Theories

The **theory** of a structure $\mathcal M$ is the set of all sentences true in $\mathcal M$:

$$
\operatorname{Th}(\mathcal M)
=============================

{\varphi : \mathcal M \models \varphi}.
$$

Then elementary equivalence can be restated as equality of theories:

$$
\mathcal M \equiv \mathcal N
\quad \text{if and only if} \quad
\operatorname{Th}(\mathcal M)=\operatorname{Th}(\mathcal N).
$$

This is often the cleanest way to think about it.

A theory $T$ is **complete** if for every sentence $\varphi$ in the language, exactly one of the following holds:

$$
T \models \varphi
$$

or

$$
T \models ¬\varphi .
$$

A complete theory decides every sentence. If $\mathcal M$ is a structure, then $\operatorname{Th}(\mathcal M)$ is always complete, because every sentence is either true or false in $\mathcal M$.

### First-Order Indistinguishability

Elementary equivalence means first-order indistinguishability.

If:

$$
\mathcal M \equiv \mathcal N,
$$

then no first-order sentence can say, “$\mathcal M$ has this property, but $\mathcal N$ does not.”

This does not mean the structures are the same in every mathematical sense. It means the chosen language and first-order logic do not have enough expressive power to separate them.

For example, in the language ${<}$, the structures $(\mathbb Q;<)$ and $(\mathbb R;<)$ differ in cardinality and completeness. But cardinality and Dedekind completeness are not first-order properties of pure order in a way that separates them here.

### Dependence on Language

Elementary equivalence depends strongly on the language.

Consider again:

$$
(\mathbb Q; <)
\quad \text{and} \quad
(\mathbb R; <).
$$

In the language ${<}$, they are elementarily equivalent.

But if we enrich the language by adding more symbols, we may gain expressive power.

For example, in an ordered field language:

$$
\mathcal L = {0,1,+,\cdot,<},
$$

the structures:

$$
(\mathbb Q;0,1,+,\cdot,<)
$$

and

$$
(\mathbb R;0,1,+,\cdot,<)
$$

are not elementarily equivalent.

The sentence:

$$
\exists x, (x \cdot x = 2)
$$

is false in $\mathbb Q$ but true in $\mathbb R$.

So the same underlying sets can become easier or harder to distinguish depending on the available vocabulary.

### Elementary Substructures

A stronger relation than substructure is elementary substructure.

Suppose:

$$
\mathcal A \subseteq \mathcal B.
$$

We say that $\mathcal A$ is an **elementary substructure** of $\mathcal B$ if every formula has the same truth value in $\mathcal A$ as in $\mathcal B$, for parameters from $A$.

We write:

$$
\mathcal A \preccurlyeq \mathcal B.
$$

Formally, for every formula $\varphi(x_1,\dots,x_n)$ and all $a_1,\dots,a_n \in A$,

$$
\mathcal A \models \varphi(a_1,\dots,a_n)
\quad \text{if and only if} \quad
\mathcal B \models \varphi(a_1,\dots,a_n).
$$

This says that $\mathcal A$ sits inside $\mathcal B$ without changing first-order truth about elements of $\mathcal A$.

Every elementary substructure is a substructure, but not every substructure is elementary.

### Example: A Non-Elementary Substructure

Consider:

$$
(\mathbb Z; <)
\subseteq
(\mathbb Q; <).
$$

This is a substructure in the language ${<}$.

But it is not an elementary substructure.

In $\mathbb Q$, between $0$ and $1$ there exists an element:

$$
\exists z, (0 < z \land z < 1).
$$

This is true in $(\mathbb Q;<)$.

But in $(\mathbb Z;<)$, no integer lies strictly between $0$ and $1$. So the same formula with parameters $0,1$ is false in $(\mathbb Z;<)$.

Therefore:

$$
(\mathbb Z;<) \not\preccurlyeq (\mathbb Q;<).
$$

### Why Elementary Equivalence Matters

Elementary equivalence lets model theory study structures by their first-order theories.

Instead of asking whether two structures are identical, we ask what first-order logic can observe about them.

This gives a precise way to separate:

| Relation                             | Meaning                                                       | Strength  |
| ------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------- | --------- |
| $\mathcal M \cong \mathcal N$        | same structure up to renaming                                 | strongest |
| $\mathcal M \preccurlyeq \mathcal N$ | substructure preserving all first-order truth with parameters | strong    |
| $\mathcal M \equiv \mathcal N$       | same first-order sentences                                    | weaker    |

This distinction is central in model theory. It explains how different mathematical objects can share the same logical theory, and why first-order logic sometimes sees less than ordinary mathematical intuition does.
