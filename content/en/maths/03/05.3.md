## 5.3 Applications to Algebra

Compactness and the Löwenheim–Skolem theorems are abstract results, but they have concrete consequences in algebra. They allow you to construct algebraic objects indirectly, by showing that every finite part of a desired property can be realized.

The general pattern is:

* describe a structure using first-order sentences
* check that every finite subset of those sentences is satisfiable
* apply compactness to obtain a global model

This method avoids explicit construction.

### Algebraic Structures as First-Order Theories

Many algebraic systems can be described in a first-order language.

A group can be expressed using the language:

$$
\mathcal L = {\cdot, {}^{-1}, e}
$$

with axioms such as:

$$
(x \cdot y) \cdot z = x \cdot (y \cdot z)
$$

$$
x \cdot e = x \quad \land \quad e \cdot x = x
$$

$$
x \cdot x^{-1} = e \quad \land \quad x^{-1} \cdot x = e
$$

A field can be expressed using:

$$
\mathcal L = {+, \cdot, 0, 1}
$$

with axioms for addition, multiplication, distributivity, and inverses.

Once algebraic structures are written as first-order theories, compactness can be applied.

### Infinite Structures from Finite Conditions

Compactness lets you turn finite consistency into infinite existence.

Suppose you want a group with infinitely many distinct elements. You can add sentences saying:

$$
x_1 \neq x_2,\quad x_1 \neq x_3,\quad x_2 \neq x_3,\quad \dots
$$

More systematically, for each $n$:

$$
\exists x_1,\dots,x_n \bigwedge_{i<j} x_i \neq x_j
$$

Each finite subset of these conditions can be satisfied by a finite group large enough to contain the required number of distinct elements.

By compactness, there is a model satisfying all these sentences. That model must be infinite.

This gives an infinite group without explicitly constructing one.

### Fields with Special Properties

Compactness can also produce fields with prescribed algebraic behavior.

For example, suppose you want a field in which every polynomial equation of a certain type has a solution.

You can add axioms saying:

$$
\forall x_1,\dots,x_n \ \exists y \ \varphi(x_1,\dots,x_n,y)
$$

for each polynomial condition you want to enforce.

If every finite subset of these axioms can be satisfied in some field, then compactness gives a field satisfying all of them at once.

This idea is used in the construction of algebraically closed fields and other enriched structures.

### Algebraically Closed Fields

A field is algebraically closed if every non-constant polynomial has a root.

This property can be expressed by an infinite set of sentences. For each degree $n \geq 1$, include a sentence:

$$
\forall a_0,\dots,a_n \
\bigl(a_n \neq 0 \to \exists x \ (a_n x^n + \cdots + a_0 = 0)\bigr)
$$

Each sentence handles polynomials of a fixed degree.

Compactness helps explain why algebraically closed fields exist in many sizes. If a theory describing such fields has one infinite model, then by Löwenheim–Skolem it has models of many cardinalities.

This is why there are algebraically closed fields of every infinite size.

### Nonstandard Algebraic Structures

Compactness also produces nonstandard algebraic objects.

Consider a field that contains elements behaving like “infinitely large numbers”.

Introduce a constant $c$ and add sentences:

$$
c > n \quad \text{for all } n \in \mathbb N
$$

Each finite subset can be satisfied in a sufficiently large ordered field.

By compactness, there is a model where $c$ is larger than every standard natural number.

This produces a nonstandard extension of the field. Such constructions are used in nonstandard analysis.

### Limits of First-Order Algebra

Compactness also explains why certain algebraic properties cannot be expressed in first-order logic.

For example, consider the property:

* “the field has characteristic $0$”

This can be expressed as an infinite set of sentences:

$$
1 + 1 \neq 0,\quad
1 + 1 + 1 \neq 0,\quad
\dots
$$

Each sentence excludes one finite characteristic.

But there is no single first-order sentence expressing “characteristic $0$”. If there were, compactness would allow a model satisfying all finite characteristic exclusions while still having finite characteristic, which is impossible.

So the property is inherently infinite.

### Uniform Construction Method

Compactness gives a general method for building algebraic structures:

1. Write down the desired properties as first-order sentences
2. Check that every finite subset is satisfiable
3. Conclude that a global model exists

This method is especially useful when direct construction is difficult.

### Interaction with Löwenheim–Skolem

Once a structure exists, Löwenheim–Skolem tells you that it exists in many sizes.

For example, if there is one infinite algebraically closed field satisfying certain axioms, then there are countable such fields and uncountable ones.

So model theory separates two questions:

* existence of a structure
* size and classification of structures

Compactness answers the first. Löwenheim–Skolem addresses the second.

### Perspective

In algebra, one often builds objects explicitly. Model theory offers a different approach. You specify what you want through logical conditions and let compactness guarantee existence.

This shifts the focus from construction to consistency. If no finite obstruction exists, then a structure exists globally.

This viewpoint becomes more powerful in later topics, where definability and types refine how algebraic structures are described.
