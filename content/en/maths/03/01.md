## Chapter 1. Propositional Logic 

Propositional logic provides the simplest formal setting for studying reasoning. It works with whole statements, called propositions, and combines them using a small set of logical connectives. The central objective is to separate three layers that are often conflated in informal reasoning: the **form of expressions (syntax)**, their **meaning (semantics)**, and the **methods used to derive conclusions (proof systems)**.

A propositional language begins with variables such as $p, q, r$. These are combined into formulas using connectives like $¬$, $\land$, $\lor$, $\to$, and $\leftrightarrow$. The construction rules are purely syntactic. At this stage, a formula is simply a well-formed string.

Semantics assigns meaning by interpreting each variable as either true or false. This extends compositionally to all formulas. The standard mechanism is the truth table. For example, the implication $A \to B$ is false exactly when $A$ is true and $B$ is false. Every formula therefore determines a Boolean function:

$$
\{0,1\}^n \to \{0,1\}
$$

where $n$ is the number of variables involved. This perspective connects logic directly to algebra and computation.

Logical equivalence identifies formulas that have the same truth behavior under all interpretations. If two formulas $A$ and $B$ satisfy

$$
A \equiv B
$$

then they represent the same Boolean function. This allows systematic rewriting. Many transformations follow algebraic patterns, such as distributivity and De Morgan laws, which enable normalization and simplification.

Normal forms provide canonical representations. Every formula can be converted into conjunctive normal form (CNF) or disjunctive normal form (DNF). For example, a CNF formula has the shape:

$$
\bigwedge_{i=1}^k \left( \bigvee_{j=1}^{m_i} \ell_{ij} \right)
$$

where each $\ell_{ij}$ is a literal. These forms are central in automated reasoning and satisfiability problems, where logical questions are reduced to structured combinatorial tasks.

Proof theory introduces formal systems that derive conclusions from premises. A derivation establishes that a formula follows from others using fixed rules. The key meta-theoretic properties are:

* **Soundness**: every provable formula is semantically valid.
* **Completeness**: every semantically valid formula is provable.

Together, these ensure that syntactic reasoning fully captures semantic truth in propositional logic.

From a computational viewpoint, propositional logic forms the basis of Boolean circuits, SAT solvers, and verification tools. The satisfiability problem asks whether a formula evaluates to true under some assignment. This problem sits at the core of complexity theory and practical constraint solving.

This chapter establishes the formal language, defines truth precisely, develops equivalence and normal forms, and introduces proof systems. These components form the foundation for first-order logic and the broader theory developed in later chapters.
