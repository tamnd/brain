The axiom of choice appears in many different forms, and these forms often look quite unlike one another at first sight. Some formulations speak about choosing elements from sets, some speak about ordering arbitrary sets, and some speak about maximal objects inside partially ordered sets. The main result of this section is that these principles are equivalent over the usual axioms of ZF set theory.

### Definition 8.21 (Partial Order)

A partial order on a set $P$ is a relation:
$$
\le
$$
on $P$ satisfying the following three conditions.

For every $x \in P$:
$$
x \le x
$$

For all $x,y \in P$, if:
$$
x \le y
$$
and:
$$
y \le x
$$
then:
$$
x=y
$$

For all $x,y,z \in P$, if:
$$
x \le y
$$
and:
$$
y \le z
$$
then:
$$
x \le z
$$

These conditions are called reflexivity, antisymmetry, and transitivity. A set equipped with a partial order is called a partially ordered set, or poset.

### Definition 8.22 (Chain)

Let $(P,\le)$ be a partially ordered set. A chain in $P$ is a subset $C \subseteq P$ such that any two elements of $C$ are comparable.

This means that for all $x,y \in C$, either:
$$
x \le y
$$
or:
$$
y \le x
$$

A chain is therefore a part of the poset where the partial order behaves like a linear order.

### Definition 8.23 (Upper Bound)

Let $(P,\le)$ be a partially ordered set, and let $C \subseteq P$. An element $u \in P$ is an upper bound for $C$ if:
$$
x \le u
$$
for every $x \in C$.

The upper bound does not need to belong to $C$. It only needs to belong to $P$ and lie above every element of $C$.

### Definition 8.24 (Maximal Element)

Let $(P,\le)$ be a partially ordered set. An element $m \in P$ is maximal if there is no element $x \in P$ such that:
$$
m < x
$$

Equivalently, $m$ is maximal if:
$$
m \le x
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
x=m
$$
for every $x \in P$.

A maximal element is not necessarily greater than every element of $P$. It only means that the element cannot be enlarged inside the partial order.

### Lemma 8.25 (Maximal Is Not the Same as Greatest)

A greatest element of a poset is maximal, but a maximal element need not be greatest.

Proof

Suppose $g$ is greatest in $P$. This means:
$$
x \le g
$$
for every $x \in P$.

If $g \le y$, then since $g$ is greatest we also have:
$$
y \le g
$$

By antisymmetry:
$$
y=g
$$

Thus $g$ is maximal.

For the converse, consider a poset with two distinct elements $a$ and $b$ and no order relations except reflexivity. Then $a$ is maximal, because no element is strictly above it. Similarly, $b$ is maximal. But there is no greatest element, because neither $a \le b$ nor $b \le a$ holds.

### Theorem 8.26 (Zorn's Lemma)

Let $(P,\le)$ be a nonempty partially ordered set. Suppose that every chain in $P$ has an upper bound in $P$. Then $P$ has a maximal element.

This statement is called Zorn's lemma.

Zorn's lemma is one of the most commonly used forms of the axiom of choice. It is especially useful when one wants to prove the existence of a maximal object, such as a maximal ideal, a basis of a vector space, or a maximal linearly independent set.

### Theorem 8.27 (Axiom of Choice Implies Zorn's Lemma)

Assume the axiom of choice. Then Zorn's lemma holds.

Proof

Let $(P,\le)$ be a nonempty partially ordered set in which every chain has an upper bound. We must show that $P$ has a maximal element.

Assume, for contradiction, that $P$ has no maximal element. Then for every $x \in P$, there exists $y \in P$ such that:
$$
x < y
$$

For each $x \in P$, define:
$$
U_x = \{y \in P : x < y\}
$$

By the assumption that there is no maximal element, each $U_x$ is nonempty.

By the axiom of choice, there exists a function:
$$
c : P \to P
$$
such that:
$$
c(x) \in U_x
$$
for every $x \in P$.

Thus:
$$
x < c(x)
$$
for every $x \in P$.

Starting from any element $p_0 \in P$, this choice function gives a way to move upward:
$$
p_0 < c(p_0) < c(c(p_0)) < \cdots
$$

The idea is to continue this construction not merely through the natural numbers, but through all ordinal stages, always choosing a strictly larger element when possible and taking upper bounds at limit stages.

More precisely, one defines by transfinite recursion an increasing sequence in $P$. At successor stages, one applies the choice function $c$. At limit stages, the previous elements form a chain, so by hypothesis they have an upper bound in $P$, and one chooses such an upper bound.

This produces a strictly increasing sequence indexed by ordinals for as long as the construction continues.

However, the construction cannot continue through all ordinals while remaining inside the set $P$. The reason is that a set cannot contain a distinct element for every ordinal, since the collection of all ordinals is not a set.

Thus the assumption that $P$ has no maximal element leads to an impossible construction. Therefore $P$ must have a maximal element.

This proves Zorn's lemma.

### Remark 8.28

The proof above uses transfinite recursion and the fact that the class of all ordinals is too large to inject into a set. In many applications, one does not repeat this proof. Instead, one verifies the hypotheses of Zorn's lemma directly and then concludes that a maximal object exists.

### Definition 8.29 (Well Order)

A well order on a set $X$ is a total order $\le$ such that every nonempty subset of $X$ has a least element.

Thus a well order has two parts. First, any two elements of $X$ must be comparable. Second, every nonempty subset must contain an element that comes first in the order.

The usual order on $\mathbb{N}$ is a well order. The usual order on $\mathbb{Z}$ is not a well order, because the subset:
$$
\mathbb{Z}
$$
itself has no least element.

The usual order on $\mathbb{R}$ is not a well order, because the open interval:
$$
(0,1)
$$
has no least element.

### Theorem 8.30 (Well Ordering Theorem)

Every set can be well ordered.

This means that for every set $X$, there exists some relation $\le$ on $X$ such that $(X,\le)$ is a well ordered set.

The theorem does not say that the well order is natural or easy to describe. For many sets, such as $\mathbb{R}$, the well order whose existence is guaranteed by the theorem may be highly nonconstructive.

### Theorem 8.31 (Axiom of Choice Implies the Well Ordering Theorem)

Assume the axiom of choice. Then every set can be well ordered.

Proof

Let $X$ be a set. If $X=\varnothing$, then the empty order is a well order on $X$, so assume $X$ is nonempty.

By the axiom of choice, every nonempty subset of $X$ can be assigned one of its elements. More precisely, there exists a function:
$$
c : \mathcal{P}(X) \setminus \{\varnothing\} \to X
$$
such that:
$$
c(A) \in A
$$
for every nonempty subset $A \subseteq X$.

We now construct a sequence of elements of $X$ by transfinite recursion. At each stage, we choose an element not chosen earlier.

Let:
$$
x_\alpha = c\left(X \setminus \{x_\beta : \beta < \alpha\}\right)
$$
whenever:
$$
X \setminus \{x_\beta : \beta < \alpha\} \ne \varnothing
$$

This says that at stage $\alpha$, we choose an element from the part of $X$ that has not yet been chosen.

This process cannot continue forever through all ordinals without exhausting $X$, because otherwise it would define an injection from the class of all ordinals into the set $X$, which is impossible.

Therefore there is some ordinal $\gamma$ such that:
$$
X = \{x_\beta : \beta < \gamma\}
$$

Now define an order on $X$ by:
$$
x_\alpha < x_\beta
\quad \text{if and only if} \quad
\alpha < \beta
$$

This order is a well order because it is transported from the well order on the ordinal $\gamma$.

Indeed, if $Y \subseteq X$ is nonempty, then the set:
$$
\{\alpha < \gamma : x_\alpha \in Y\}
$$
is a nonempty set of ordinals and therefore has a least element. The corresponding element of $Y$ is the least element of $Y$ under the order just defined.

Thus $X$ can be well ordered.

### Theorem 8.32 (Well Ordering Theorem Implies the Axiom of Choice)

Assume every set can be well ordered. Then the axiom of choice holds.

Proof

Let:
$$
(A_i)_{i \in I}
$$
be a family of nonempty sets. We must construct a choice function.

Let:
$$
U = \bigcup_{i \in I} A_i
$$

By the well ordering theorem, there exists a well order $\le$ on $U$.

For each $i \in I$, the set $A_i$ is a nonempty subset of $U$. Since $\le$ is a well order, $A_i$ has a least element.

Define:
$$
f(i)=\text{the least element of } A_i \text{ under } \le
$$

Then:
$$
f(i) \in A_i
$$
for every $i \in I$.

Hence $f$ is a choice function for the family. Therefore the axiom of choice holds.

### Corollary 8.33

The axiom of choice is equivalent to the well ordering theorem.

Proof

Theorem 8.31 proves that the axiom of choice implies the well ordering theorem. Theorem 8.32 proves that the well ordering theorem implies the axiom of choice. Therefore the two principles are equivalent.

### Maximal Principles

Zorn's lemma is one example of a maximal principle. Many equivalent forms of the axiom of choice state that under suitable conditions, some partially ordered collection contains a maximal object.

### Theorem 8.34 (Hausdorff Maximal Principle)

Every partially ordered set has a maximal chain.

This means that if $(P,\le)$ is a partially ordered set, then there exists a chain $C \subseteq P$ such that no strictly larger chain contains it.

### Proposition 8.35

Zorn's lemma implies the Hausdorff maximal principle.

Proof

Let $(P,\le)$ be a partially ordered set. Let $\mathcal{C}$ be the set of all chains in $P$, ordered by inclusion.

Thus for chains $C,D \in \mathcal{C}$, we write:
$$
C \le D
$$
if:
$$
C \subseteq D
$$

We apply Zorn's lemma to $\mathcal{C}$.

Let:
$$
\mathcal{D}
$$
be a chain in $\mathcal{C}$ with respect to inclusion. This means that $\mathcal{D}$ is a collection of chains in $P$, and any two members of $\mathcal{D}$ are comparable by inclusion.

Define:
$$
U = \bigcup_{C \in \mathcal{D}} C
$$

We claim that $U$ is a chain in $P$.

Let $x,y \in U$. Then there exist $C,D \in \mathcal{D}$ such that:
$$
x \in C
\quad \text{and} \quad
y \in D
$$

Since $\mathcal{D}$ is linearly ordered by inclusion, either:
$$
C \subseteq D
$$
or:
$$
D \subseteq C
$$

If $C \subseteq D$, then both $x$ and $y$ belong to $D$, and since $D$ is a chain, $x$ and $y$ are comparable.

If $D \subseteq C$, the same argument applies using $C$.

Thus $U$ is a chain in $P$.

Also, every $C \in \mathcal{D}$ satisfies:
$$
C \subseteq U
$$

Therefore $U$ is an upper bound for $\mathcal{D}$ in $\mathcal{C}$.

By Zorn's lemma, $\mathcal{C}$ has a maximal element. Such a maximal element is exactly a maximal chain in $P$.

### Proposition 8.36

The Hausdorff maximal principle implies Zorn's lemma.

Proof

Assume the Hausdorff maximal principle.

Let $(P,\le)$ be a nonempty partially ordered set such that every chain in $P$ has an upper bound in $P$.

By the Hausdorff maximal principle, there exists a maximal chain:
$$
C \subseteq P
$$

By hypothesis, $C$ has an upper bound $u \in P$. Thus:
$$
x \le u
$$
for every $x \in C$.

We claim that $u$ is maximal in $P$.

Suppose not. Then there exists $v \in P$ such that:
$$
u < v
$$

Since every element $x \in C$ satisfies $x \le u$, we also have:
$$
x \le v
$$
for every $x \in C$.

Therefore:
$$
C \cup \{v\}
$$
is still a chain in $P$.

Moreover, $v \notin C$. If $v \in C$, then since $u$ is an upper bound for $C$, we would have:
$$
v \le u
$$
which contradicts:
$$
u < v
$$

Thus $C \cup \{v\}$ is a strictly larger chain than $C$, contradicting the maximality of $C$.

Therefore no such $v$ exists, and $u$ is a maximal element of $P$.

Hence Zorn's lemma holds.

### Corollary 8.37

Zorn's lemma and the Hausdorff maximal principle are equivalent.

Proof

Proposition 8.35 proves that Zorn's lemma implies the Hausdorff maximal principle. Proposition 8.36 proves that the Hausdorff maximal principle implies Zorn's lemma. Therefore the two principles are equivalent.

### Right Inverses of Surjections

Another equivalent formulation of choice says that every surjective function has a right inverse.

### Theorem 8.38

The axiom of choice is equivalent to the statement that every surjective function has a right inverse.

Proof

Assume the axiom of choice. Let:
$$
f : X \to Y
$$
be a surjective function.

For each $y \in Y$, define the fiber:
$$
X_y = \{x \in X : f(x)=y\}
$$

Because $f$ is surjective, each fiber $X_y$ is nonempty. By the axiom of choice, there exists a function:
$$
g : Y \to X
$$
such that:
$$
g(y) \in X_y
$$
for every $y \in Y$.

Then:
$$
f(g(y))=y
$$
for every $y \in Y$, so:
$$
f \circ g = \mathrm{id}_Y
$$

Thus $g$ is a right inverse of $f$.

Conversely, assume that every surjective function has a right inverse. Let:
$$
(A_i)_{i \in I}
$$
be a family of nonempty sets.

Define:
$$
X = \{(i,a) : i \in I \text{ and } a \in A_i\}
$$

Define:
$$
\pi : X \to I
$$
by:
$$
\pi(i,a)=i
$$

The function $\pi$ is surjective because each $A_i$ is nonempty. Hence $\pi$ has a right inverse:
$$
s : I \to X
$$
such that:
$$
\pi(s(i))=i
$$
for every $i \in I$.

For each $i$, since $s(i) \in X$, there exists $a_i \in A_i$ such that:
$$
s(i)=(i,a_i)
$$

Define:
$$
c(i)=a_i
$$

Then:
$$
c(i) \in A_i
$$
for every $i \in I$, so $c$ is a choice function. Hence the axiom of choice holds.

### Summary of Equivalent Forms

Over ZF, the following statements are equivalent.

1. The axiom of choice.

2. Every product of nonempty sets is nonempty:
$$
\prod_{i \in I} A_i \ne \varnothing
$$
whenever $A_i \ne \varnothing$ for all $i \in I$.

3. Every surjective function has a right inverse.

4. Every set can be well ordered.

5. Zorn's lemma.

6. The Hausdorff maximal principle.

These equivalences explain why the axiom of choice appears in many areas of mathematics under different names. In practice, one usually chooses the formulation that best fits the problem at hand: choice functions for selection problems, well orders for comparison and transfinite recursion, and Zorn's lemma for existence of maximal objects.
