## 8.2 Equivalent Formulations

The Axiom of Choice has many equivalent forms. They look different, but over the usual Zermelo-Fraenkel axioms, they have the same strength.

This means that if we assume ZF, then each of the following principles implies the others.

### The Axiom of Choice

The direct form says:

$$
\text{Every family of nonempty sets has a choice function.}
$$

Equivalently, if ${A_i}_{i \in I}$ is a family of nonempty sets, then:

$$
\prod_{i \in I} A_i \neq \varnothing
$$

This says that the Cartesian product of nonempty sets is nonempty.

For finite families, this is easy. For arbitrary infinite families, it requires the Axiom of Choice.

### The Well-Ordering Theorem

The **well-ordering theorem** says:

$$
\text{Every set can be well-ordered.}
$$

A well-order is a linear order in which every nonempty subset has a least element.

The natural numbers with their usual order are well-ordered:

$$
0 < 1 < 2 < 3 < \cdots
$$

Every nonempty subset of $\mathbb{N}$ has a least element.

The real numbers with their usual order are not well-ordered. For example:

$$
(0,1)
$$

has no least element.

The well-ordering theorem says that there exists some order on $\mathbb{R}$ that makes it well-ordered, although this order is highly nonconstructive.

The connection with choice is natural. If every set can be well-ordered, then we can choose the least element from each set in a family. That gives a choice function.

Conversely, the Axiom of Choice can be used to build a well-order on any set.

### Zorn's Lemma

**Zorn's lemma** is another equivalent form of the Axiom of Choice.

It says:

$$
\text{If every chain in a partially ordered set has an upper bound, then the poset has a maximal element.}
$$

To unpack this, let $(P,\leq)$ be a partially ordered set.

A **chain** is a subset $C \subseteq P$ such that any two elements of $C$ are comparable. That means for all $a,b \in C$:

$$
a \leq b
\quad \text{or} \quad
b \leq a
$$

An **upper bound** for $C$ is an element $u \in P$ such that:

$$
c \leq u
$$

for every $c \in C$.

A **maximal element** is an element $m \in P$ such that there is no strictly larger element above it. Formally:

$$
m \leq x \Longrightarrow x=m
$$

Notice that “maximal” does not mean “greatest”. A greatest element is above every element of $P$. A maximal element only has no proper extension above it.

### Example: Bases of Vector Spaces

Zorn's lemma is useful because many mathematical objects are built by extending partial objects until no further extension is possible.

For example, to prove that every vector space has a basis, let $V$ be a vector space. Consider the set:

$$
P = {S \subseteq V : S \text{ is linearly independent}}
$$

Order $P$ by inclusion:

$$
S \leq T \quad \text{means} \quad S \subseteq T
$$

Every chain of linearly independent sets has an upper bound, namely its union:

$$
\bigcup C
$$

This union is still linearly independent. Therefore, by Zorn's lemma, $P$ has a maximal linearly independent set.

A maximal linearly independent set is a basis. If it did not span $V$, we could add a vector outside its span and still remain linearly independent. That would contradict maximality.

So Zorn's lemma gives:

$$
\text{Every vector space has a basis.}
$$

This proof is typical. We form a poset of partial constructions, show chains have upper bounds, and conclude that a maximal construction exists.

### Hausdorff Maximal Principle

Another equivalent formulation is the **Hausdorff maximal principle**.

It says:

$$
\text{Every partially ordered set has a maximal chain.}
$$

A maximal chain is a chain that cannot be enlarged by adding another element while remaining a chain.

This principle is close in spirit to Zorn's lemma. Zorn's lemma gives maximal elements under chain upper-bound assumptions. The Hausdorff maximal principle gives maximal chains directly.

### Tukey's Lemma

**Tukey's lemma** is another common equivalent form.

It says that every nonempty finite-character family has a maximal member.

A family $\mathcal{F}$ of sets has **finite character** if membership in $\mathcal{F}$ is determined by finite subsets:

$$
A \in \mathcal{F}
\quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad
\text{every finite } F \subseteq A \text{ belongs to } \mathcal{F}
$$

This is useful in algebra and topology because many properties are finite in nature.

For example, linear independence has finite character. A set of vectors is linearly independent exactly when every finite subset is linearly independent.

Thus Tukey's lemma can also prove the existence of vector space bases.

### Maximal Ideals

Another standard consequence equivalent in strength to choice-related principles is the existence of maximal ideals in many algebraic settings.

For a nonzero commutative ring $R$ with identity, one often proves:

$$
\text{Every proper ideal of } R \text{ is contained in a maximal ideal.}
$$

The proof uses Zorn's lemma.

Let $I$ be a proper ideal. Consider all proper ideals containing $I$, ordered by inclusion. The union of every chain of proper ideals is again a proper ideal. Therefore, Zorn's lemma gives a maximal proper ideal containing $I$.

This is a core tool in commutative algebra. It is used to construct fields, study spectra of rings, and prove existence results.

### Comparing the Forms

| Principle                   | Informal meaning                               | Typical use                                |
| --------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Axiom of Choice             | choose one element from each nonempty set      | products, selections, representatives      |
| Well-ordering theorem       | every set can be put into a well-order         | transfinite recursion, cardinal comparison |
| Zorn's lemma                | extend partial objects to maximal objects      | bases, ideals, algebraic closures          |
| Hausdorff maximal principle | every poset has a maximal chain                | order theory                               |
| Tukey's lemma               | finite-character families have maximal members | algebra, topology                          |

These principles are equivalent over ZF, but they are convenient in different contexts.

### Why Equivalence Matters

The equivalence shows that the Axiom of Choice is not merely about choosing elements. It is also about ordering arbitrary sets, building maximal objects, and extending partial constructions.

This explains why choice appears in many areas of mathematics.

In algebra, it gives bases, maximal ideals, and algebraic closures.

In topology, it gives compactness theorems and product constructions.

In analysis, it appears in existence arguments involving functionals, representatives, and infinite products.

Different fields often use different forms. Algebra usually uses Zorn's lemma. Set theory often uses well-ordering. Product constructions use the direct choice-function form.

The theorem behind all of this is:

$$
\mathrm{AC}
\Longleftrightarrow
\text{Well-Ordering Theorem}
\Longleftrightarrow
\text{Zorn's Lemma}
\Longleftrightarrow
\text{Hausdorff Maximal Principle}
$$

over ZF.
