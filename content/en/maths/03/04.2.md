## 4.2 Substructures and Embeddings

Once a language has been fixed, we can compare structures written in that language. The most basic comparison is inclusion: one structure may sit inside another.

Suppose $\mathcal A$ and $\mathcal B$ are structures for the same language $\mathcal L$. We say that $\mathcal A$ is a **substructure** of $\mathcal B$ if the domain of $\mathcal A$ is contained in the domain of $\mathcal B$ and the symbols of $\mathcal L$ behave the same way on the smaller domain.

We write:

$$
\mathcal A \subseteq \mathcal B
$$

This notation means more than ordinary set inclusion. It means that the smaller structure inherits constants, functions, and relations from the larger one.

### Domains

Let $A$ be the domain of $\mathcal A$, and let $B$ be the domain of $\mathcal B$.

For $\mathcal A$ to be a substructure of $\mathcal B$, we need:

$$
A \subseteq B
$$

But this alone is not enough. The operations and relations must also agree.

For example, the set of natural numbers is a subset of the integers:

$$
\mathbb N \subseteq \mathbb Z
$$

In the language ${+, \cdot, 0, 1}$, the natural numbers form a substructure of the integers because addition and multiplication of natural numbers remain natural numbers, and the constants $0$ and $1$ are included.

### Constants

If $c$ is a constant symbol in $\mathcal L$, then the interpretation of $c$ in the smaller structure must be the same as in the larger structure.

Formally:

$$
c^{\mathcal A} = c^{\mathcal B}
$$

Since $c^{\mathcal A}$ must belong to $A$, this also requires:

$$
c^{\mathcal B} \in A
$$

So a substructure must contain all distinguished elements named by constants.

For example, in the language of groups:

$$
{\cdot, {}^{-1}, e}
$$

any substructure must contain the identity element $e$.

### Functions

Function symbols require closure.

If $f$ is an $n$-ary function symbol and $a_1,\dots,a_n \in A$, then:

$$
f^{\mathcal B}(a_1,\dots,a_n) \in A
$$

The interpretation in $\mathcal A$ is then inherited from $\mathcal B$:

$$
f^{\mathcal A}(a_1,\dots,a_n)
=============================

f^{\mathcal B}(a_1,\dots,a_n)
$$

This closure condition is essential.

For example, $\mathbb N$ is closed under addition and multiplication:

$$
a,b \in \mathbb N
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
a+b \in \mathbb N
$$

$$
a,b \in \mathbb N
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
a \cdot b \in \mathbb N
$$

So $\mathbb N$ can be a substructure of $\mathbb Z$ in the language ${+, \cdot, 0, 1}$.

But $\mathbb N$ is not a substructure of $\mathbb Z$ in the language ${+, -, 0}$ if $-$ is treated as a binary function, because:

$$
2 - 5 = -3
$$

and $-3 \notin \mathbb N$.

### Relations

Relations are inherited by restriction.

If $R$ is an $n$-ary relation symbol, then for all $a_1,\dots,a_n \in A$:

$$
R^{\mathcal A}(a_1,\dots,a_n)
\quad \text{holds if and only if} \quad
R^{\mathcal B}(a_1,\dots,a_n)
\quad \text{holds}
$$

Equivalently:

$$
R^{\mathcal A} = R^{\mathcal B} \cap A^n
$$

For example, consider ordered structures in the language ${<}$. The structure:

$$
(\mathbb N; <)
$$

is a substructure of:

$$
(\mathbb Z; <)
$$

because the order relation on $\mathbb N$ is just the restriction of the order relation on $\mathbb Z$.

### Example: Groups

In the group language:

$$
\mathcal L_{\mathrm{grp}} = {\cdot, {}^{-1}, e}
$$

a substructure of a group is exactly a subgroup.

If $H \subseteq G$, then $H$ is a substructure of $G$ when:

$$
e \in H
$$

$$
a,b \in H \Longrightarrow a \cdot b \in H
$$

$$
a \in H \Longrightarrow a^{-1} \in H
$$

These are the usual subgroup conditions.

So model-theoretic substructure generalizes familiar algebraic subobjects.

### Example: Graphs

In the graph language:

$$
\mathcal L_{\mathrm{graph}} = {E}
$$

where $E$ is a binary relation, a substructure is an induced subgraph.

If $\mathcal G = (V; E)$ and $W \subseteq V$, then the substructure on $W$ has edge relation:

$$
E^{\mathcal W} = E^{\mathcal G} \cap W^2
$$

This means that two vertices in $W$ are adjacent in the smaller graph exactly when they were adjacent in the larger graph.

So model-theoretic substructures of graphs are induced subgraphs, not arbitrary subgraphs. You cannot remove edges between retained vertices.

### Embeddings

A substructure is an inclusion-preserving case. An **embedding** allows the smaller structure to be copied into a larger one.

Let $\mathcal A$ and $\mathcal B$ be $\mathcal L$-structures. An embedding is an injective map:

$$
h : A \to B
$$

that preserves the meanings of all symbols in $\mathcal L$.

For constants:

$$
h(c^{\mathcal A}) = c^{\mathcal B}
$$

For function symbols:

$$
h(f^{\mathcal A}(a_1,\dots,a_n))
================================

f^{\mathcal B}(h(a_1),\dots,h(a_n))
$$

For relation symbols:

$$
R^{\mathcal A}(a_1,\dots,a_n)
\quad \text{if and only if} \quad
R^{\mathcal B}(h(a_1),\dots,h(a_n))
$$

for all $a_1,\dots,a_n \in A$.

The relation condition is two-sided. It preserves both truth and falsity of atomic relations.

### Embeddings as Copies

An embedding identifies $\mathcal A$ with a substructure of $\mathcal B$.

If:

$$
h : \mathcal A \to \mathcal B
$$

is an embedding, then the image:

$$
h[A] = {h(a) : a \in A}
$$

forms a substructure of $\mathcal B$.

This means that embeddings are structure-preserving copies. The structure $\mathcal A$ may not literally be a subset of $\mathcal B$, but it appears inside $\mathcal B$ through $h$.

### Example: Ordered Sets

Let:

$$
(\mathbb N; <)
$$

and

$$
(\mathbb Z; <)
$$

The inclusion map:

$$
i : \mathbb N \to \mathbb Z
$$

defined by:

$$
i(n)=n
$$

is an embedding of ordered sets.

It is injective, and for all $m,n \in \mathbb N$:

$$
m < n
\quad \text{if and only if} \quad
i(m) < i(n)
$$

So $\mathbb N$ embeds into $\mathbb Z$ as an ordered substructure.

### Example: Rings

Consider the map:

$$
h : \mathbb Z \to \mathbb Q
$$

given by:

$$
h(n)=n
$$

In the ring language:

$$
{+, \cdot, 0, 1}
$$

this is an embedding.

It preserves constants:

$$
h(0)=0
\qquad
h(1)=1
$$

It preserves addition:

$$
h(a+b)=h(a)+h(b)
$$

It preserves multiplication:

$$
h(a \cdot b)=h(a)\cdot h(b)
$$

It is injective, so $\mathbb Z$ appears inside $\mathbb Q$ as a ring substructure.

### Homomorphisms vs Embeddings

A homomorphism preserves functions and constants, but it may identify distinct elements. An embedding must be injective and must preserve relations both ways.

For example, a group homomorphism:

$$
h : G \to H
$$

preserves multiplication:

$$
h(ab)=h(a)h(b)
$$

But it may have a nontrivial kernel, so it may collapse elements.

An embedding has no collapse. It represents the source structure faithfully inside the target structure.

| Map type     |          Injective | Preserves functions | Preserves relations both ways |
| ------------ | -----------------: | ------------------: | ----------------------------: |
| Homomorphism |       not required |                 yes |          usually only forward |
| Embedding    |                yes |                 yes |                           yes |
| Isomorphism  | yes and surjective |                 yes |                           yes |

### Inclusion Maps

Every substructure gives an embedding.

If:

$$
\mathcal A \subseteq \mathcal B
$$

then the inclusion map:

$$
i : A \to B
$$

defined by:

$$
i(a)=a
$$

is an embedding.

So substructure can be seen as the special case where the embedding map is simply inclusion.
