## 3.4 Proof Transformations

Proof transformations study how proofs can be changed while preserving their conclusion. The goal is to understand when two proofs express the same argument, when a proof contains unnecessary steps, and how proofs in one system can be converted into proofs in another system.

A proof is a formal object. Because of that, we can analyze it structurally, just as we analyze formulas structurally.

For example, a proof may contain a detour:

$$
A
$$

$$
B
$$

$$
A \land B
$$

$$
A
$$

Here we first introduce $A \land B$, then immediately eliminate it to recover $A$. The conjunction was unnecessary. A transformation can remove this detour.

### Local Reductions

A **local reduction** removes a small redundant pattern inside a proof.

The most common pattern is:

1. introduce a connective
2. immediately eliminate the same connective

For conjunction:

$$
\frac{
\frac{A \qquad B}{A \land B}
}{A}
$$

reduces to:

$$
A
$$

Similarly:

$$
\frac{
\frac{A \qquad B}{A \land B}
}{B}
$$

reduces to:

$$
B
$$

The proof introduced $A \land B$, but the next step used only one component. The shorter proof goes directly to that component.

### Implication Reduction

For implication, the detour looks like this.

First, prove $A \to B$ by assuming $A$ and deriving $B$:

$$
\frac{
\begin{array}{c}
[A] \
\vdots \
B
\end{array}
}{A \to B}
$$

Then apply it to a proof of $A$:

$$
\frac{
A
\qquad
A \to B
}{B}
$$

The reduction substitutes the proof of $A$ into the place where $A$ was assumed.

Schematically:

$$
\frac{
D_A
\qquad
\frac{
\begin{array}{c}
[A] \
D_B \
B
\end{array}
}{A \to B}
}{B}
$$

reduces to:

$$
D_B[D_A/A]
$$

This means: replace the temporary assumption $A$ inside the proof of $B$ by the actual proof $D_A$.

This is the proof-theoretic form of function application. It is also the logical ancestor of substitution in lambda calculus.

### Disjunction Reduction

For disjunction, the introduction rule chooses one side:

$$
\frac{A}{A \lor B}
$$

The elimination rule performs proof by cases:

$$
\frac{
A \lor B
\qquad
\begin{array}{c}
[A] \
\vdots \
C
\end{array}
\qquad
\begin{array}{c}
[B] \
\vdots \
C
\end{array}
}{C}
$$

If $A \lor B$ was obtained from $A$, then only the $A$-case is needed.

So:

$$
\frac{
\frac{A}{A \lor B}
\qquad
\begin{array}{c}
[A] \
\vdots \
C
\end{array}
\qquad
\begin{array}{c}
[B] \
\vdots \
C
\end{array}
}{C}
$$

reduces to the proof of $C$ from the $A$-case, with the actual proof of $A$ substituted into the temporary assumption.

The $B$-case was present only because the proof had to handle both possibilities. Once we know the disjunction came from the left side, the right case becomes unnecessary.

### Normal Forms of Proofs

A proof is in **normal form** if it contains no immediate introduction-elimination detours.

Normal proofs are usually more direct. They avoid proving a compound formula only to immediately take it apart.

For natural deduction, the normalization theorem says that many proofs can be transformed into normal proofs.

Informally:

$$
\text{every proof can be simplified to a proof with no local detours}
$$

This result is important because it shows that formal proofs have internal structure. They can be simplified by rules, much like algebraic expressions can be simplified.

### Proof Equivalence

Two proofs may have the same conclusion but different forms.

For example, suppose we want to prove:

$$
A \land B \vdash B \land A
$$

One proof extracts $A$, extracts $B$, then introduces $B \land A$:

$$
\frac{
\frac{A \land B}{B}
\qquad
\frac{A \land B}{A}
}{B \land A}
$$

Another proof may first copy assumptions differently or pass through intermediate formulas. Both prove the same judgment.

Proof transformations let us define when two proofs are essentially the same. This becomes important in type theory, where proofs correspond to programs.

Under Curry-Howard, a proof of:

$$
A \to B
$$

behaves like a function from evidence for $A$ to evidence for $B$.

Then proof normalization corresponds to program evaluation.

### Translation Between Systems

Proof transformations also compare different proof systems.

A derivation in natural deduction can often be translated into a derivation in sequent calculus.

For example, the natural deduction rule:

$$
\frac{A \qquad B}{A \land B}
$$

corresponds to the sequent rule:

$$
\frac{\Gamma \Rightarrow A \qquad \Gamma \Rightarrow B}
{\Gamma \Rightarrow A \land B}
$$

Both express the same idea: to prove a conjunction, prove both components.

Similarly, implication introduction in natural deduction:

$$
\frac{
\begin{array}{c}
[A] \
\vdots \
B
\end{array}
}{A \to B}
$$

corresponds to implication right in sequent calculus:

$$
\frac{\Gamma, A \Rightarrow B}{\Gamma \Rightarrow A \to B}
$$

The systems present proofs differently, but their logical content is closely related.

### Conservativity

A transformation should preserve what is provable.

If one proof system can be translated into another, we often want:

$$
\Gamma \vdash_1 A
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
\Gamma \vdash_2 A
$$

If translation works both ways, then the systems prove the same formulas:

$$
\Gamma \vdash_1 A
\quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad
\Gamma \vdash_2 A
$$

This means the systems differ in presentation, not in logical strength.

For example, standard Hilbert systems, natural deduction systems, and sequent calculi for classical propositional logic prove the same valid formulas, even though their proofs look very different.

### Admissible Rules

A rule is **admissible** if adding it does not increase what can be proved.

Suppose a system does not include a rule $R$. If every use of $R$ can be replaced by a derivation using existing rules, then $R$ is admissible.

For example, in many systems, the following derived rule is admissible:

$$
\frac{A \land B}{B \land A}
$$

This rule may not be primitive. But it can be simulated:

$$
\frac{
\frac{A \land B}{B}
\qquad
\frac{A \land B}{A}
}{B \land A}
$$

So adding the rule would make proofs shorter, but it would not allow any new conclusions.

### Derived Rules

A **derived rule** is a rule justified by an existing derivation pattern.

For example:

$$
\frac{A \qquad B}{B \land A}
$$

is derived from conjunction introduction:

$$
\frac{B \qquad A}{B \land A}
$$

Derived rules are useful because they let us write proofs more naturally while keeping the underlying proof system small.

Another common derived rule is hypothetical syllogism:

$$
\frac{A \to B \qquad B \to C}{A \to C}
$$

A natural deduction derivation is:

Assume $A$.

From $A \to B$ and $A$, infer $B$.

From $B \to C$ and $B$, infer $C$.

Therefore:

$$
A \to C
$$

So:

$$
A \to B,\ B \to C \vdash A \to C
$$

### Proof Size

Proof transformations can change proof size.

Some transformations shorten proofs by removing detours. Others may make proofs larger. Translating a Hilbert proof into natural deduction, or a sequent proof into another calculus, may introduce many intermediate steps.

This matters in proof complexity. There, we ask how long the shortest proof of a formula must be in a given proof system.

For a family of formulas:

$$
A_1, A_2, A_3, \dots
$$

we may study the size of the shortest proof of $A_n$ as a function of $n$.

A proof system is stronger, in this sense, if it admits much shorter proofs for important families of formulas.

### Normalization and Computation

Proof normalization has a computational reading.

Under Curry-Howard:

| Logic               | Computation        |
| ------------------- | ------------------ |
| formula             | type               |
| proof               | program            |
| proof normalization | program evaluation |
| implication         | function type      |
| conjunction         | product type       |
| disjunction         | sum type           |

For example, the implication reduction described earlier corresponds to beta-reduction in lambda calculus:

$$
(\lambda x.,t),u \longrightarrow t[u/x]
$$

The proof of $A \to B$ acts like a function. The proof of $A$ acts like an argument. Eliminating the implication applies the function to the argument.

This connection becomes central in constructive logic and type theory.
