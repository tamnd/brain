## 8.1 Axiom of Choice

The **Axiom of Choice** is one of the most important axioms in modern set theory. It says that we can choose one element from each set in a family of nonempty sets, even when the family is infinite and no explicit rule for choosing is given.

A simple finite example is easy. Suppose we have three nonempty sets:

$$
A_1 = {1,2}
$$

$$
A_2 = {a,b,c}
$$

$$
A_3 = {\pi}
$$

We can choose one element from each:

$$
f(A_1)=1,\qquad f(A_2)=a,\qquad f(A_3)=\pi
$$

This gives a **choice function**.

In general, if $\mathcal{A}$ is a family of nonempty sets, a choice function for $\mathcal{A}$ is a function $f$ such that:

$$
f(A) \in A
$$

for every $A \in \mathcal{A}$.

So the Axiom of Choice says:

$$
\text{Every family of nonempty sets has a choice function.}
$$

More formally:

$$
\forall \mathcal{A},
\left[
\left(\forall A \in \mathcal{A},\ A \neq \varnothing\right)
\to
\exists f, \forall A \in \mathcal{A},\ f(A) \in A
\right]
$$

The point is subtle. For a finite family, we can make choices one by one. For an infinite family, especially an arbitrary family, there may be no visible formula or procedure that tells us which element to pick from each set. The Axiom of Choice allows the function to exist anyway.

### Why Choice Feels Natural

Many uses of choice look harmless.

For example, if every set in a family has a least element, then we do not need the Axiom of Choice. We can choose the least element from each set.

Suppose every set is a nonempty subset of $\mathbb{N}$. Then for each $A \subseteq \mathbb{N}$, we may define:

$$
f(A)=\min(A)
$$

This is an explicit rule.

The Axiom of Choice becomes important when no such canonical choice exists.

For example, suppose $\mathcal{A}$ is a family of arbitrary nonempty subsets of $\mathbb{R}$. Many subsets of $\mathbb{R}$ have no least element in the usual order. A set like:

$$
(0,1)
$$

has no smallest real number. So the “choose the least element” rule fails.

Choice says that even for such families, some choice function exists.

### Finite Choice and Infinite Choice

Finite choice can be proved using ordinary set-theoretic reasoning. If we have finitely many nonempty sets:

$$
A_1,A_2,\dots,A_n
$$

we can choose:

$$
a_i \in A_i
$$

for each $i$.

Then define:

$$
f(A_i)=a_i
$$

The infinite case is different. If the family is indexed by an infinite set $I$:

$$
{A_i}_{i \in I}
$$

and each $A_i$ is nonempty, the Axiom of Choice asserts:

$$
\exists f : I \to \bigcup_{i \in I} A_i
$$

such that:

$$
f(i) \in A_i
$$

for every $i \in I$.

This is often the most convenient indexed form:

$$
\prod_{i \in I} A_i \neq \varnothing
$$

whenever every $A_i$ is nonempty.

Here the product means the set of all functions $f$ with $f(i) \in A_i$ for each $i$.

### A Basic Example

Let:

$$
A_n = {n, -n}
$$

for each positive integer $n$.

Then the family is:

$$
\mathcal{A} = {A_n : n \in \mathbb{N},\ n > 0}
$$

A choice function chooses one element from each pair:

$$
f(n) \in {n,-n}
$$

For example:

$$
f(n)=n
$$

is a choice function.

Another possible choice function is:

$$
f(n)=
\begin{cases}
n, & n \text{ is even},\
-n, & n \text{ is odd}.
\end{cases}
$$

In this example, we have explicit rules. So the Axiom of Choice is not really needed. The axiom matters when the sets are arbitrary enough that no uniform rule is available.

### Choice and Cartesian Products

The Axiom of Choice can be stated using Cartesian products.

For a family of sets ${A_i}_{i \in I}$, the Cartesian product is:

$$
\prod_{i \in I} A_i
===================

\left{
f : I \to \bigcup_{i \in I} A_i
\mid
\forall i \in I,\ f(i) \in A_i
\right}
$$

So an element of the product is exactly a choice function.

The Axiom of Choice says:

$$
\left(\forall i \in I,\ A_i \neq \varnothing\right)
\to
\prod_{i \in I} A_i \neq \varnothing
$$

This means: a product of nonempty sets is nonempty.

For finite products, this is familiar. If $A$ and $B$ are nonempty, then:

$$
A \times B \neq \varnothing
$$

For arbitrary infinite products, this statement requires choice.

### Why Choice Is Powerful

The Axiom of Choice proves many important theorems.

One famous consequence is the **well-ordering theorem**:

$$
\text{Every set can be well-ordered.}
$$

A well-order is an order in which every nonempty subset has a least element.

This is easy to believe for $\mathbb{N}$, because every nonempty subset of natural numbers has a least element. It is much less intuitive for $\mathbb{R}$. The usual order on $\mathbb{R}$ is not a well-order, because:

$$
(0,1)
$$

has no least element.

The well-ordering theorem says that there exists some order on $\mathbb{R}$ that makes it well-ordered, although this order cannot usually be written down explicitly.

Another famous consequence is **Zorn's lemma**. It says, roughly, that under suitable conditions, a partially ordered set has a maximal element.

Zorn's lemma is widely used in algebra. For example, it proves:

$$
\text{Every vector space has a basis.}
$$

For finite-dimensional vector spaces, this is familiar from linear algebra. For arbitrary vector spaces, especially very large ones, the proof uses choice.

### Some Surprising Consequences

The Axiom of Choice also has consequences that feel less intuitive.

One famous example is the Banach-Tarski paradox. It says, using choice, that a solid ball in three-dimensional space can be decomposed into finitely many pieces and reassembled into two balls of the same size as the original.

This does not contradict physical geometry, because the pieces are highly nonmeasurable. They are not ordinary physical pieces that can be cut with a knife.

Choice also implies the existence of nonmeasurable subsets of the real line. These are sets to which no reasonable length can be assigned while preserving the usual rules of measure.

Because of examples like these, the Axiom of Choice is mathematically powerful but philosophically delicate.

### Choice in Ordinary Mathematics

Many standard mathematical results use some form of choice, sometimes silently.

Examples include:

$$
\text{Every surjective function has a right inverse.}
$$

If:

$$
g : X \to Y
$$

is surjective, then for each $y \in Y$, the fiber:

$$
g^{-1}({y})
$$

is nonempty.

A right inverse chooses one element from each fiber:

$$
h(y) \in g^{-1}({y})
$$

so that:

$$
g(h(y))=y
$$

This is exactly a choice function on the family of fibers.

Another example:

$$
\text{Every vector space has a basis.}
$$

This is proved using Zorn's lemma, hence it depends on choice in its general form.

### Weaker Forms of Choice

Mathematicians often study weaker versions of choice.

The **Axiom of Countable Choice** says that every countable family of nonempty sets has a choice function.

In indexed form:

$$
\left(\forall n \in \mathbb{N},\ A_n \neq \varnothing\right)
\to
\prod_{n \in \mathbb{N}} A_n \neq \varnothing
$$

This is weaker than the full Axiom of Choice.

Another related principle is **Dependent Choice**. It is often enough for analysis and says that we can build infinite sequences by making choices step by step, where each choice depends on the previous one.

These weaker forms are useful because they support much ordinary mathematics while avoiding some stronger consequences of full choice.

### Choice as an Existence Principle

The Axiom of Choice is best understood as an existence principle. It does not tell us how to choose. It says that a choice function exists.

This distinction matters.

An explicit construction gives a rule:

$$
f(A)=\min(A)
$$

when such a minimum exists.

The Axiom of Choice gives existence without a rule:

$$
\exists f, \forall A \in \mathcal{A},\ f(A) \in A
$$

This is why the axiom appears so often in existence proofs. It lets us build objects when local choices are possible, even if no global construction is explicitly available.
