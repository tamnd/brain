## Chapter 12. Degrees of Unsolvability

Computability theory shows that some problems cannot be solved by any algorithm. The halting problem is the standard example, but it is only the beginning. Once we know that some sets and problems are undecidable, we can ask a finer question: are all undecidable problems equally hard?

The answer is no. Degrees of unsolvability measure different levels of noncomputability.

The main tool is **reducibility**. A problem $A$ is reducible to a problem $B$ if a solution to $B$ would give us a solution to $A$. Informally, this means $A$ is no harder than $B$.

We write one common form as:

$$
A \leq_T B
$$

This means $A$ is **Turing reducible** to $B$. A machine deciding $A$ may ask questions to an oracle for $B$. If access to $B$ lets us decide $A$, then $A$ has no more computational difficulty than $B$.

Two problems have the same **Turing degree** if each can compute the other:

$$
A \equiv_T B
$$

That is:

$$
A \leq_T B
\quad \text{and} \quad
B \leq_T A
$$

A Turing degree is therefore an equivalence class of problems with the same computational strength.

The computable sets all form the lowest degree. If $A$ is computable, then it carries no oracle power beyond ordinary computation. Above that degree, we find noncomputable sets. Some are as hard as the halting problem. Others are noncomputable but strictly weaker.

A central class is the class of **recursively enumerable sets**, also called **computably enumerable sets**. A set $A$ is recursively enumerable if there is a machine that lists its elements, possibly forever:

$$
A = {a_0, a_1, a_2, \dots}
$$

Equivalently, membership in $A$ can be semi-decided. If $x \in A$, the machine eventually accepts. If $x \notin A$, the machine may run forever.

The halting set is recursively enumerable but not computable. We can enumerate halting computations by simulating machines step by step. But we cannot decide in general whether an arbitrary computation will halt.

This chapter also introduces **Post’s problem**. Post asked whether there exists a recursively enumerable degree strictly between the computable degree and the degree of the halting problem. In symbols, the question asks whether there is an r.e. set $A$ such that:

$$
\mathbf{0} <_T A <_T \mathbf{0'}
$$

Here $\mathbf{0}$ is the degree of computable sets, and $\mathbf{0'}$ is the degree of the halting problem.

The answer is yes. There are recursively enumerable sets whose degree lies strictly between computable sets and the halting problem. This result showed that undecidability has a rich internal structure.

The structure of degrees is far from linear. Some degrees are incomparable: neither can compute the other. So the world of unsolvable problems forms a partial order, not a simple ladder.

This chapter develops that structure in five stages. First, reducibility gives a way to compare decision problems. Second, Turing degrees group problems by equivalent computational power. Third, recursively enumerable sets provide the main arena for studying partial computability. Fourth, Post’s problem shows that undecidability has intermediate levels. Fifth, the structure of degrees reveals a complex ordering of noncomputable problems.

The main lesson is that undecidability has depth. A problem can be unsolvable, yet still easier or harder than another unsolvable problem. Degrees of unsolvability give a precise language for comparing these levels.
