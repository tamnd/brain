## 9.4 Determinacy Principles

Determinacy studies infinite games and the existence of winning strategies. It provides an alternative way to understand sets of real numbers, especially those that are highly definable.

### Infinite Games

A typical game involves two players, often called Player I and Player II. They take turns choosing natural numbers:

$$
a_0, b_0, a_1, b_1, a_2, b_2, \dots
$$

This produces an infinite sequence:

$$
x = (a_0, b_0, a_1, b_1, \dots) \in \mathbb{N}^{\mathbb{N}}
$$

A fixed set:

$$
A \subseteq \mathbb{N}^{\mathbb{N}}
$$

determines the outcome of the game.

* Player I wins if $x \in A$
* Player II wins if $x \notin A$

This defines a game denoted by:

$$
G(A)
$$

### Strategies

A **strategy** for a player is a rule that tells the player what move to make based on the previous moves.

For Player I, a strategy is a function:

$$
\sigma : (\mathbb{N}^{<\mathbb{N}})_{\text{even}} \to \mathbb{N}
$$

It takes a finite sequence of past moves and returns the next move.

A strategy is **winning** if, no matter how the opponent plays, following the strategy guarantees victory.

### Determined Games

A game is **determined** if one of the players has a winning strategy.

This means:

$$
\text{either Player I has a winning strategy, or Player II has one}
$$

There are no draws and no situations where both players fail to have a winning strategy.

### Axiom of Determinacy

The **axiom of determinacy** (AD) states:

$$
\text{every game } G(A) \text{ is determined}
$$

for all sets:

$$
A \subseteq \mathbb{N}^{\mathbb{N}}
$$

This is a strong principle. It contradicts the full axiom of choice, so it cannot hold in ZFC.

However, determinacy can hold for restricted classes of sets.

### Determinacy for Definable Sets

A more refined approach is to assume determinacy only for certain classes.

For example:

* all Borel sets are determined
* all analytic sets are determined
* more generally, all projective sets may be determined under strong assumptions

These restricted determinacy principles are consistent with large cardinal axioms.

### Consequences for Sets of Reals

Determinacy has strong consequences for subsets of $\mathbb{R}$.

Under determinacy assumptions, definable sets satisfy regularity properties such as:

* every set is Lebesgue measurable
* every set has the Baire property
* every uncountable set has a perfect subset

For example, under suitable determinacy:

$$
A \subseteq \mathbb{R}, \quad A \text{ uncountable} \quad \Rightarrow \quad A \text{ contains a perfect set}
$$

This prevents pathological constructions that arise under the axiom of choice.

### Connection to Descriptive Set Theory

Determinacy is closely tied to definability.

Games are defined using sets:

$$
A \subseteq \mathbb{N}^{\mathbb{N}}
$$

The complexity of $A$ determines how difficult it is to prove that the game is determined.

* Borel sets lead to well-understood games
* analytic sets require deeper arguments
* projective sets connect to large cardinal assumptions

So determinacy provides a dynamic view of definable sets.

### Large Cardinals and Determinacy

Strong forms of determinacy are connected to large cardinals.

For example, the determinacy of projective sets often follows from the existence of sufficiently large cardinals.

This creates a bridge:

$$
\text{large cardinals} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \text{determinacy} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \text{regularity of sets}
$$

This chain explains why large cardinal axioms have consequences for real analysis.

### Intuition

A useful way to think about determinacy is:

* a set $A$ defines a game
* a strategy is a method for navigating the game
* determinacy says that the game is always “decidable” in advance

So instead of analyzing a set directly, we analyze the game it defines.

If one player can always force a win, the set has a certain structure.

If determinacy holds widely, then definable sets behave in a controlled and regular way.
