## 4.4 Isomorphism and Invariants

An **isomorphism** is the strongest ordinary notion of sameness between structures. It says that two structures have exactly the same form, possibly with different names for their elements.

Let $\mathcal M$ and $\mathcal N$ be structures for the same language $\mathcal L$. An isomorphism is a bijection:

$$
h : M \to N
$$

that preserves all symbols of the language.

If such a map exists, we write:

$$
\mathcal M \cong \mathcal N
$$

This means that $\mathcal M$ and $\mathcal N$ are the same as $\mathcal L$-structures.

### Preserving Constants

If $c$ is a constant symbol, then an isomorphism must send the interpretation of $c$ in $\mathcal M$ to the interpretation of $c$ in $\mathcal N$:

$$
h(c^{\mathcal M}) = c^{\mathcal N}.
$$

For example, in the language of rings:

$$
{0,1,+,\cdot},
$$

an isomorphism must send $0$ to $0$ and $1$ to $1$.

### Preserving Functions

If $f$ is an $n$-ary function symbol, then:

$$
h(f^{\mathcal M}(a_1,\dots,a_n))
================================

f^{\mathcal N}(h(a_1),\dots,h(a_n))
$$

for all $a_1,\dots,a_n \in M$.

This says that applying the function first and then mapping gives the same result as mapping first and then applying the function.

For example, in a group:

$$
h(a \cdot b)=h(a)\cdot h(b).
$$

So a group isomorphism preserves multiplication.

### Preserving Relations

If $R$ is an $n$-ary relation symbol, then:

$$
R^{\mathcal M}(a_1,\dots,a_n)
\quad \text{if and only if} \quad
R^{\mathcal N}(h(a_1),\dots,h(a_n)).
$$

This condition says that relations are preserved exactly, both truth and falsity.

For an ordered structure:

$$
(M;<),
$$

an isomorphism must satisfy:

$$
a < b
\quad \text{if and only if} \quad
h(a) < h(b).
$$

It preserves the whole order pattern.

### Example: Finite Linear Orders

Consider two structures:

$$
\mathcal M = ({a,b,c}; <^{\mathcal M})
$$

with:

$$
a <^{\mathcal M} b <^{\mathcal M} c,
$$

and:

$$
\mathcal N = ({1,2,3}; <^{\mathcal N})
$$

with:

$$
1 <^{\mathcal N} 2 <^{\mathcal N} 3.
$$

The map:

$$
h(a)=1,\qquad h(b)=2,\qquad h(c)=3
$$

is an isomorphism.

The elements have different names, but the order structure is identical.

### Example: Graph Isomorphism

A graph in model theory can be written as:

$$
\mathcal G = (V; E),
$$

where $E$ is a binary relation.

Two graphs:

$$
(V;E)
\quad \text{and} \quad
(W;F)
$$

are isomorphic if there is a bijection:

$$
h : V \to W
$$

such that:

$$
E(u,v)
\quad \text{if and only if} \quad
F(h(u),h(v)).
$$

This means adjacency is preserved exactly.

So graph isomorphism is just model-theoretic isomorphism in the language with one binary relation symbol.

### Invariants

An **invariant** is a property preserved by isomorphism.

If $\mathcal M \cong \mathcal N$, then every invariant true of $\mathcal M$ is also true of $\mathcal N$.

For example:

| Structure type | Invariants                                              |
| -------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| Finite sets    | number of elements                                      |
| Groups         | order, abelian property, subgroup lattice               |
| Rings          | characteristic, units, ideals                           |
| Fields         | characteristic, algebraic closure, transcendence degree |
| Graphs         | number of vertices, degree sequence, connectedness      |
| Orders         | endpoints, density, order type                          |

The basic idea is simple: an invariant records something structural, not something dependent on the names of elements.

### Isomorphism-Invariant Properties

A property $P$ is **isomorphism-invariant** if:

$$
\mathcal M \cong \mathcal N
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
(P(\mathcal M) \iff P(\mathcal N)).
$$

For example, commutativity of a group is isomorphism-invariant.

If $\mathcal G$ is an abelian group, then:

$$
\forall x, \forall y, (x \cdot y = y \cdot x)
$$

holds in $\mathcal G$.

If $\mathcal G \cong \mathcal H$, then the same sentence holds in $\mathcal H$.

The property does not depend on the names of the elements. It depends only on the multiplication structure.

### First-Order Definable Invariants

Some invariants are expressible by first-order sentences.

For example, in the language of groups, being abelian is first-order expressible:

$$
\forall x, \forall y, (x \cdot y = y \cdot x).
$$

Having exponent $2$ is also first-order expressible:

$$
\forall x, (x \cdot x = e).
$$

In graph theory, being triangle-free is first-order expressible:

$$
\forall x, \forall y, \forall z,
\bigl(
(E(x,y) \land E(y,z) \land E(z,x))
\to
(x=y \lor y=z \lor x=z)
\bigr).
$$

This sentence says there are no three distinct vertices forming a triangle.

### Invariants Not Captured by First-Order Logic

Not every invariant is first-order definable.

For example, **finiteness** cannot be expressed by one first-order sentence.

There is no single first-order sentence $\varphi$ such that:

$$
\mathcal M \models \varphi
$$

exactly when $\mathcal M$ is finite.

Similarly, in graph theory, connectedness is not first-order expressible over arbitrary graphs.

Intuitively, first-order formulas can quantify over elements, but they cannot quantify over arbitrary finite paths of unbounded length as a single object.

This is an important lesson of model theory: first-order logic preserves isomorphism, but it sees only some isomorphism-invariant properties.

### Isomorphism Implies Elementary Equivalence

If two structures are isomorphic, then they satisfy the same first-order sentences.

Formally:

$$
\mathcal M \cong \mathcal N
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
\mathcal M \equiv \mathcal N.
$$

The reason is that an isomorphism preserves the truth of every formula.

More generally, if $h : \mathcal M \to \mathcal N$ is an isomorphism, then for every formula $\varphi(x_1,\dots,x_n)$ and every tuple $a_1,\dots,a_n \in M$,

$$
\mathcal M \models \varphi(a_1,\dots,a_n)
$$

if and only if:

$$
\mathcal N \models \varphi(h(a_1),\dots,h(a_n)).
$$

This is proved by induction on the complexity of formulas.

Atomic formulas are preserved by the definition of isomorphism. Logical connectives are handled by the induction hypothesis. Quantifiers are handled using the fact that $h$ is bijective.

### Elementary Equivalence May Be Weaker

The converse may fail:

$$
\mathcal M \equiv \mathcal N
\quad \not\Longrightarrow \quad
\mathcal M \cong \mathcal N.
$$

For example:

$$
(\mathbb Q;<)
\equiv
(\mathbb R;<)
$$

as dense linear orders without endpoints, but:

$$
(\mathbb Q;<)
\not\cong
(\mathbb R;<),
$$

because one domain is countable and the other is uncountable.

So isomorphism sees the whole structure. Elementary equivalence sees only what first-order sentences can express.

### Automorphisms

An **automorphism** is an isomorphism from a structure to itself:

$$
h : \mathcal M \to \mathcal M.
$$

Automorphisms describe internal symmetries.

For example, consider the ordered set:

$$
(\mathbb Z;<).
$$

For each integer $k$, the map:

$$
h(n)=n+k
$$

is an automorphism of the pure order structure.

It preserves order:

$$
m<n
\quad \text{if and only if} \quad
m+k<n+k.
$$

But in the richer structure:

$$
(\mathbb Z;0,<),
$$

this map is an automorphism only when $k=0$, because it must preserve the constant $0$.

This shows again that language matters. Adding symbols can reduce the number of symmetries.

### Summary Table

| Concept                | Meaning                                         |
| ---------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| Isomorphism            | bijective structure-preserving map              |
| Automorphism           | isomorphism from a structure to itself          |
| Invariant              | property preserved under isomorphism            |
| First-order invariant  | invariant expressible by a first-order sentence |
| Elementary equivalence | agreement on all first-order sentences          |
