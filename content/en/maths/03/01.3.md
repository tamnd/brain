## 1.3 Logical Equivalence

Two formulas can look different but behave the same under every valuation. Logical equivalence captures this idea precisely.

### Definition

Formulas $A$ and $B$ are **logically equivalent** if they have the same truth value under every valuation:

$$
A \equiv B
$$

Formally:

$$
\forall v,\quad v(A) = v(B)
$$

You can read $A \equiv B$ as “$A$ and $B$ always agree”.

### Checking Equivalence with Truth Tables

To verify equivalence, construct a truth table and compare the final columns.

Example:

$$
A \to B \quad \text{and} \quad ¬A \lor B
$$

| $A$          | $B$          | $A \to B$    | $¬A \lor B$  |
| ------------ | ------------ | ------------ | ------------ |
| $\mathrm{T}$ | $\mathrm{T}$ | $\mathrm{T}$ | $\mathrm{T}$ |
| $\mathrm{T}$ | $\mathrm{F}$ | $\mathrm{F}$ | $\mathrm{F}$ |
| $\mathrm{F}$ | $\mathrm{T}$ | $\mathrm{T}$ | $\mathrm{T}$ |
| $\mathrm{F}$ | $\mathrm{F}$ | $\mathrm{T}$ | $\mathrm{T}$ |

The columns match, so:

$$
A \to B \equiv ¬A \lor B
$$

### Common Equivalences

These identities are used frequently. You should recognize and apply them directly.

**Double negation**

$$
¬(¬A) \equiv A
$$

**Commutativity**

$$
A \land B \equiv B \land A
$$

$$
A \lor B \equiv B \lor A
$$

**Associativity**

$$
(A \land B) \land C \equiv A \land (B \land C)
$$

$$
(A \lor B) \lor C \equiv A \lor (B \lor C)
$$

**Distributivity**

$$
A \land (B \lor C) \equiv (A \land B) \lor (A \land C)
$$

$$
A \lor (B \land C) \equiv (A \lor B) \land (A \lor C)
$$

**De Morgan laws**

$$
¬(A \land B) \equiv ¬A \lor ¬B
$$

$$
¬(A \lor B) \equiv ¬A \land ¬B
$$

**Implication**

$$
A \to B \equiv ¬A \lor B
$$

**Biconditional**

$$
A \leftrightarrow B \equiv (A \to B) \land (B \to A)
$$

### Using Equivalence for Rewriting

Equivalence allows you to transform formulas step by step without changing their meaning.

Example:

$$
¬(A \to B)
$$

First rewrite implication:

$$
¬(¬A \lor B)
$$

Apply De Morgan:

$$
A \land ¬B
$$

So:

$$
¬(A \to B) \equiv A \land ¬B
$$

Each step preserves truth under all valuations.

### Tautologies and Contradictions

A formula is a **tautology** if it is always true:

$$
\models A
$$

Example:

$$
p \lor ¬p
$$

A formula is a **contradiction** if it is always false:

$$
\models ¬A
$$

Example:

$$
p \land ¬p
$$

Equivalence interacts with these notions:

* $A \equiv \mathrm{T}$ means $A$ is a tautology
* $A \equiv \mathrm{F}$ means $A$ is a contradiction

### Equivalence as Algebra

Logical equivalence behaves like equality in algebra. You can substitute equivalent formulas inside larger expressions.

If:

$$
A \equiv B
$$

then for any context $C[\cdot]$:

$$
C[A] \equiv C[B]
$$

This substitution principle is what makes equivalence useful in simplification, normalization, and automated reasoning.

Logical equivalence gives you a toolkit for transforming formulas into clearer, simpler, or more structured forms without changing their meaning.
