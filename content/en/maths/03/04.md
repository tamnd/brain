## Chapter 4. Structures and Models

Model theory studies logic from the semantic side. Instead of focusing first on proofs, it asks how formal languages are interpreted inside mathematical objects.

A **structure** is a mathematical universe equipped with specified operations, relations, and constants. A **model** is a structure in which a given set of sentences is true.

For example, group theory can be studied using a first-order language containing:

$$
\cdot, \quad {}^{-1}, \quad e
$$

A structure for this language consists of a set together with interpretations of multiplication, inverse, and identity. If that structure satisfies the group axioms, then it is a model of group theory.

More generally, if $T$ is a theory and $\mathcal M$ is a structure, then:

$$
\mathcal M \models T
$$

means that every sentence in $T$ is true in $\mathcal M$.

### Languages and Signatures

A first-order language is determined by its **signature**. The signature tells us which non-logical symbols are available.

Typical symbols include:

| Symbol type      | Example       | Meaning                 |
| ---------------- | ------------- | ----------------------- |
| Constant symbols | $0, 1, e$     | distinguished elements  |
| Function symbols | $+, \cdot, f$ | operations              |
| Relation symbols | $<, =, R$     | predicates or relations |

The signature fixes the vocabulary. The structure gives that vocabulary meaning.

For arithmetic, a common signature is:

$$
\mathcal L_{\mathrm{arith}} = {0, 1, +, \cdot, <}
$$

A structure for this language must specify a domain and interpretations of these symbols.

For example:

$$
(\mathbb N; 0, 1, +, \cdot, <)
$$

is the standard structure of natural number arithmetic.

### Structures

An $\mathcal L$-structure $\mathcal M$ consists of:

1. A nonempty domain $M$.
2. An interpretation of each constant symbol as an element of $M$.
3. An interpretation of each $n$-ary function symbol as a function $M^n \to M$.
4. An interpretation of each $n$-ary relation symbol as a subset of $M^n$.

If $c$ is a constant symbol, its interpretation in $\mathcal M$ is written:

$$
c^{\mathcal M} \in M
$$

If $f$ is an $n$-ary function symbol:

$$
f^{\mathcal M} : M^n \to M
$$

If $R$ is an $n$-ary relation symbol:

$$
R^{\mathcal M} \subseteq M^n
$$

The superscript $\mathcal M$ reminds us that the same formal symbol may have different meanings in different structures.

### Substructures and Embeddings

A **substructure** is a smaller structure sitting inside a larger one while preserving the interpretations of the language.

Suppose $\mathcal A$ and $\mathcal B$ are $\mathcal L$-structures. We write:

$$
\mathcal A \subseteq \mathcal B
$$

when the domain of $\mathcal A$ is a subset of the domain of $\mathcal B$, and all functions, relations, and constants are inherited correctly.

For example:

$$
(\mathbb Z; +, \cdot, 0, 1)
$$

sits inside:

$$
(\mathbb Q; +, \cdot, 0, 1)
$$

as a substructure in the language of rings.

An **embedding** is a map between structures that preserves the structure described by the language. It is the model-theoretic version of a structure-preserving map.

If:

$$
h : \mathcal A \to \mathcal B
$$

is an embedding, then constants, functions, and relations are preserved. For example, if the language has a binary function symbol $+$, then:

$$
h(a+b)=h(a)+h(b)
$$

for all $a,b \in A$.

### Elementary Equivalence

Two structures may look different but satisfy exactly the same first-order sentences.

Structures $\mathcal M$ and $\mathcal N$ are **elementarily equivalent** if:

$$
\mathcal M \equiv \mathcal N
$$

meaning:

$$
\mathcal M \models \varphi
\quad \text{if and only if} \quad
\mathcal N \models \varphi
$$

for every sentence $\varphi$ in the language.

This is weaker than isomorphism. Isomorphic structures are elementarily equivalent, but elementarily equivalent structures may have different sizes or internal forms.

For example, the compactness and Löwenheim-Skolem theorems imply that first-order theories can have nonstandard models. These models may satisfy the same first-order sentences as a familiar structure while containing additional or differently arranged elements.

### Isomorphism and Invariants

An **isomorphism** is a bijective embedding whose inverse also preserves structure.

If $\mathcal M$ and $\mathcal N$ are isomorphic, we write:

$$
\mathcal M \cong \mathcal N
$$

Isomorphic structures are structurally identical from the viewpoint of the language. They may use different underlying sets, but their operations and relations have the same pattern.

An invariant is a property preserved by isomorphism.

For example, in graph theory, the number of vertices is an invariant of finite graphs. In group theory, commutativity is an invariant of groups. In ordered structures, density is an invariant of order type.

Model theory studies which invariants can be expressed by first-order sentences and which cannot.

### Examples Across Algebra and Geometry

Many familiar mathematical objects are naturally treated as structures.

A group is a structure:

$$
(G; \cdot, {}^{-1}, e)
$$

A ring is a structure:

$$
(R; +, \cdot, 0, 1)
$$

An ordered field is a structure:

$$
(F; +, \cdot, 0, 1, <)
$$

A graph is a structure:

$$
(V; E)
$$

where $E$ is a binary relation on vertices.

An affine plane over a field may be studied as a geometric structure with points, lines, and incidence relations.

This uniform viewpoint is powerful. It lets us compare algebra, order, graph theory, arithmetic, and geometry using the same logical framework.
