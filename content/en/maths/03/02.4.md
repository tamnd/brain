## 2.4 Satisfaction and Models

A structure gives meaning to the symbols of a language. Satisfaction tells us when a formula is true in that structure.

The basic notation is:

$$
\mathcal M \models A
$$

This means that the formula $A$ is true in the structure $\mathcal M$.

For example, let:

$$
\mathcal N = (\mathbb{N};0,+,\times,<)
$$

Then:

$$
\mathcal N \models \forall x,(x+0=x)
$$

because every natural number satisfies $x+0=x$.

### Variable Assignments

Formulas may contain free variables. To evaluate them, we need to know which objects those variables name.

A **variable assignment** is a function:

$$
s : \mathrm{Var} \to M
$$

where $M$ is the domain of the structure $\mathcal M$.

If $M=\mathbb{N}$, an assignment may be:

$$
s(x)=3,\qquad s(y)=5
$$

Then the formula:

$$
x<y
$$

is true under this assignment.

We write:

$$
\mathcal M \models A[s]
$$

to mean that $A$ is true in $\mathcal M$ under the assignment $s$.

### Evaluating Terms

Before evaluating formulas, we first evaluate terms.

Suppose:

$$
\mathcal N = (\mathbb{N};0,S,+)
$$

and:

$$
s(x)=4
$$

Then:

$$
x+S(0)
$$

denotes:

$$
4+1
$$

so its value is:

$$
5
$$

In general, each term receives a value from the domain.

Variables are evaluated using the assignment.

Constants are evaluated using the structure.

Function symbols are evaluated by applying their interpreted functions.

### Satisfaction for Atomic Formulas

Atomic formulas are checked directly.

For equality:

$$
t_1=t_2
$$

we have:

$$
\mathcal M \models (t_1=t_2)[s]
$$

exactly when $t_1$ and $t_2$ denote the same object under $s$.

For a predicate:

$$
R(t_1,\dots,t_n)
$$

we have:

$$
\mathcal M \models R(t_1,\dots,t_n)[s]
$$

exactly when the tuple of term values belongs to the interpreted relation:

$$
R^{\mathcal M}
$$

Example: in the usual natural numbers,

$$
\mathcal N \models (2<5)
$$

because:

$$
(2,5)\in <^{\mathcal N}
$$

### Satisfaction for Connectives

The connectives are interpreted as in propositional logic.

Negation:

$$
\mathcal M \models ¬A[s]
$$

exactly when:

$$
\mathcal M \not\models A[s]
$$

Conjunction:

$$
\mathcal M \models (A\land B)[s]
$$

exactly when:

$$
\mathcal M \models A[s]
\quad \text{and} \quad
\mathcal M \models B[s]
$$

Disjunction:

$$
\mathcal M \models (A\lor B)[s]
$$

exactly when:

$$
\mathcal M \models A[s]
\quad \text{or} \quad
\mathcal M \models B[s]
$$

Implication:

$$
\mathcal M \models (A\to B)[s]
$$

exactly when either:

$$
\mathcal M \not\models A[s]
$$

or:

$$
\mathcal M \models B[s]
$$

### Satisfaction for Quantifiers

Quantifiers change the assignment.

For the universal quantifier:

$$
\mathcal M \models \forall x,A[s]
$$

means that $A$ is true no matter which object of $M$ we assign to $x$.

Formally:

$$
\mathcal M \models \forall x,A[s]
$$

exactly when, for every $a\in M$:

$$
\mathcal M \models A[s[x\mapsto a]]
$$

Here $s[x\mapsto a]$ means the assignment that sends $x$ to $a$ and agrees with $s$ on all other variables.

For the existential quantifier:

$$
\mathcal M \models \exists x,A[s]
$$

means that $A$ is true for at least one object of $M$ assigned to $x$.

Formally:

$$
\mathcal M \models \exists x,A[s]
$$

exactly when, for some $a\in M$:

$$
\mathcal M \models A[s[x\mapsto a]]
$$

### Example

Let:

$$
\mathcal N = (\mathbb{N};0,+,<)
$$

Consider:

$$
\forall x,\exists y,(x<y)
$$

To check this sentence, choose any natural number for $x$.

If:

$$
x=0
$$

we may choose:

$$
y=1
$$

If:

$$
x=10
$$

we may choose:

$$
y=11
$$

In general, for any $x\in\mathbb{N}$, choose:

$$
y=x+1
$$

Therefore:

$$
\mathcal N \models \forall x,\exists y,(x<y)
$$

Now consider the finite structure:

$$
\mathcal A = ({1,2,3};<)
$$

with the usual order restricted to ${1,2,3}$.

The sentence:

$$
\forall x,\exists y,(x<y)
$$

is false in $\mathcal A$.

When:

$$
x=3
$$

there is no $y\in{1,2,3}$ such that:

$$
3<y
$$

So:

$$
\mathcal A \not\models \forall x,\exists y,(x<y)
$$

### Models

A **model** of a sentence is a structure in which that sentence is true.

If:

$$
\mathcal M \models A
$$

then $\mathcal M$ is a model of $A$.

For a set of sentences $\Gamma$, we write:

$$
\mathcal M \models \Gamma
$$

when:

$$
\mathcal M \models A
$$

for every $A\in\Gamma$.

Example: the group axioms form a set of first-order sentences. Any structure satisfying all group axioms is a model of those axioms.

The same idea applies to rings, fields, graphs, orders, and many other mathematical structures.
