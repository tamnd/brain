## 9.3 Descriptive Set Theory

Descriptive set theory studies subsets of spaces such as the real line, with an emphasis on sets that can be described in a controlled and explicit way. The goal is to classify sets by how complex their definitions are, and to understand which regular properties they must satisfy.

### Polish Spaces

The basic setting is a **Polish space**, a topological space that is:

* separable
* completely metrizable

Important examples include:

$$
\mathbb{R}, \quad \mathbb{N}^{\mathbb{N}}, \quad 2^{\mathbb{N}}
$$

These spaces are rich enough to encode most objects used in analysis and combinatorics.

### Open and Closed Sets

The simplest definable sets are open and closed sets.

* Open sets are unions of basic neighborhoods
* Closed sets are complements of open sets

These sets are well behaved. They have strong structural and topological properties.

### Borel Sets

Borel sets are generated from open sets using countable operations.

Starting with open sets, we allow:

* countable unions
* countable intersections
* complements

The collection of all sets obtained this way is the **Borel hierarchy**.

We denote it by:

$$
\mathcal{B}(X)
$$

where $X$ is the space.

Borel sets form a large class, but still remain well structured. Many classical objects in analysis are Borel.

### Analytic Sets

Analytic sets extend the Borel sets.

A set $A \subseteq X$ is **analytic** if there exists a continuous function:

$$
f : \mathbb{N}^{\mathbb{N}} \to X
$$

such that:

$$
A = f(B)
$$

for some Borel set $B \subseteq \mathbb{N}^{\mathbb{N}}$.

Equivalently, analytic sets are projections of Borel sets.

If:

$$
B \subseteq X \times Y
$$

is Borel, then its projection:

$$
{ x \in X : \exists y \in Y,\ (x,y) \in B }
$$

is analytic.

Analytic sets can be more complex than Borel sets, but they still retain many regular properties.

### Projective Hierarchy

Beyond analytic sets, one builds the **projective hierarchy**.

Starting with analytic sets, we alternate projection and complementation.

This produces levels such as:

$$
\Sigma^1_1, \quad \Pi^1_1, \quad \Sigma^1_2, \quad \Pi^1_2, \dots
$$

Each level increases complexity.

As complexity grows, the behavior of sets becomes more sensitive to set-theoretic assumptions.

### Regularity Properties

A central theme is that definable sets often satisfy strong regularity properties.

Three important ones are:

* **Lebesgue measurability**
* **Baire property**
* **perfect set property**

The perfect set property says:

$$
\text{every uncountable set contains a perfect subset}
$$

This implies that uncountable sets must be large in a strong sense.

For Borel and analytic sets, these properties hold in ZFC.

For more complex sets, additional assumptions such as large cardinals or determinacy may be required.

### Definability and Complexity

Descriptive set theory measures how complicated a set is by how it is defined.

* Borel sets have explicit constructions
* analytic sets involve projections
* higher projective sets involve alternating operations

This creates a hierarchy where each level corresponds to a deeper form of definability.

Understanding where a set lies in this hierarchy helps determine what properties it must have.

### Interaction with Logic

Descriptive set theory connects closely with logic.

Formulas in first-order or higher-order logic can define sets of real numbers. The complexity of the formula often matches the complexity class in the hierarchy.

For example:

* existential quantifiers correspond to projections
* universal quantifiers correspond to complements of projections

So the projective hierarchy mirrors logical complexity.

### Applications

Descriptive set theory appears in many areas:

* classification problems in analysis
* structure of function spaces
* equivalence relations and invariants
* dynamical systems

It also plays a role in understanding which mathematical objects can be explicitly described and which require stronger assumptions.

The subject shows that even subsets of $\mathbb{R}$ can have deep and intricate structure, depending on how they are defined.
