## 5.2 Löwenheim–Skolem Theorems

The Löwenheim–Skolem theorems describe how the size of models behaves in first-order logic. They show that once a theory has one infinite model, it usually has many others of different sizes.

These results reveal a limitation of first-order logic. It cannot control the exact cardinality of infinite structures.

### Downward Löwenheim–Skolem

The downward theorem says that large models can be reduced to smaller ones while preserving all first-order truths.

Let $\mathcal L$ be a language, and let $\mathcal M$ be an $\mathcal L$-structure with infinite domain.

Then there exists a substructure $\mathcal N \preccurlyeq \mathcal M$ such that:

$$
|N| \leq \max(|\mathcal L|, \aleph_0)
$$

and:

$$
\mathcal N \preccurlyeq \mathcal M
$$

means that $\mathcal N$ is an **elementary substructure** of $\mathcal M$. That is, for every formula $\varphi(x_1,\dots,x_n)$ and every tuple $a_1,\dots,a_n \in N$:

$$
\mathcal N \models \varphi(a_1,\dots,a_n)
\quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad
\mathcal M \models \varphi(a_1,\dots,a_n)
$$

So $\mathcal N$ satisfies exactly the same first-order properties as $\mathcal M$, at least when talking about elements inside $N$.

In particular, if the language $\mathcal L$ is countable, then every infinite structure has a **countable elementary substructure**.

### Idea of the Construction

The construction works by building a smaller domain step by step.

Start with a set $A \subseteq M$. Then repeatedly add elements required to satisfy formulas.

For example, if a formula says:

$$
\exists x, \varphi(x, a)
$$

for some parameter $a \in A$, then we ensure that there is some witness $b$ in the smaller structure such that:

$$
\varphi(b, a)
$$

holds.

By systematically adding such witnesses, we build a set closed under the requirements of the language. This produces a substructure that is elementary.

This process is often called the **Skolem hull** construction.

### Upward Löwenheim–Skolem

The upward theorem goes in the opposite direction. It says that if a theory has an infinite model, then it has arbitrarily large models.

Let $T$ be a first-order theory with an infinite model. Then for every infinite cardinal $\kappa$ with:

$$
\kappa \geq |\mathcal L|
$$

there exists a model $\mathcal M$ of $T$ such that:

$$
|\mathcal M| = \kappa
$$

So once a theory has one infinite model, it has models of many different sizes.

### Combining Both Directions

Together, the two theorems show:

* Infinite models can be made smaller
* Infinite models can be made larger

So first-order theories typically do not determine a unique infinite size.

If a theory has one infinite model, then it has models in a wide range of cardinalities.

### Example: Dense Linear Orders

Consider the theory of dense linear orders without endpoints.

This theory says:

* The order is linear
* Between any two elements there is another element
* There is no smallest or largest element

The rational numbers:

$$
(\mathbb Q, <)
$$

form a model.

By the upward Löwenheim–Skolem theorem, there are also uncountable models of this theory.

By the downward Löwenheim–Skolem theorem, every uncountable model has a countable elementary submodel.

So the theory cannot distinguish between countable and uncountable dense orders using first-order sentences alone.

### Skolem Paradox

A striking consequence appears when applying these ideas to set theory.

Zermelo–Fraenkel set theory proves the existence of uncountable sets. For example, it proves that the real numbers are uncountable.

However, by the downward Löwenheim–Skolem theorem, if ZF has any infinite model, then it has a **countable model**.

So there is a structure $\mathcal M$ such that:

* $\mathcal M \models \text{ZF}$
* the domain of $\mathcal M$ is countable (from the outside)

Inside $\mathcal M$, there is a set that $\mathcal M$ believes is “uncountable”.

This is not a contradiction. The key point is that “countable” is defined inside the structure. A set may be countable externally but appear uncountable internally because no bijection exists *within the model*.

This phenomenon is called the **Skolem paradox**.

### Why This Happens

First-order logic cannot quantify over all subsets or all functions. It can only quantify over elements already present in the structure.

So a statement like “there is no bijection between $A$ and $\mathbb N$” means:

$$
\text{no such bijection exists inside the model}
$$

There may still be a bijection outside the model.

This gap between internal and external viewpoints is a central theme in model theory.

### Consequences

The Löwenheim–Skolem theorems have several important consequences:

| Observation                    | Explanation                                                        |
| ------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| No control over infinite size  | First-order theories cannot fix the cardinality of infinite models |
| Multiple non-isomorphic models | A theory may have many different models                            |
| Existence of countable models  | Even theories about large structures can have countable models     |
| Internal vs external viewpoint | Properties like countability depend on the model                   |

### Role in Model Theory

These theorems show that first-order logic is flexible but limited.

It can describe structures abstractly and capture many properties, but it cannot pin down a single infinite structure uniquely.

This leads naturally to deeper questions:

* When are two models essentially the same?
* Which properties can be expressed in first-order logic?
* How do models differ even when they satisfy the same theory?

These questions are developed in later sections through elementary equivalence, saturation, and classification theory.
