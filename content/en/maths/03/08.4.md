## 8.4 Consistency Results

Axiomatic set theory studies not only which statements are true, but also which statements can be added to a theory without creating contradictions. This leads to the notion of **consistency**.

A theory $T$ is **consistent** if it does not prove a contradiction. Formally:

$$
T \nvdash \bot
$$

where $\bot$ represents a contradiction.

Equivalently, there is no formula $A$ such that both:

$$
T \vdash A
\quad \text{and} \quad
T \vdash ¬A
$$

Consistency is a minimal requirement. If a theory is inconsistent, then every statement becomes provable, and the theory loses all meaning.

### Relative Consistency

In practice, we rarely prove that a theory is absolutely consistent. Instead, we prove **relative consistency**.

A relative consistency statement has the form:

$$
\mathrm{Con}(T) \to \mathrm{Con}(T + \varphi)
$$

This means that if $T$ is consistent, then adding the statement $\varphi$ does not introduce contradictions.

For example:

$$
\mathrm{Con}(\mathrm{ZF}) \to \mathrm{Con}(\mathrm{ZFC})
$$

This says that if Zermelo-Fraenkel set theory is consistent, then adding the Axiom of Choice keeps it consistent.

Similarly:

$$
\mathrm{Con}(\mathrm{ZFC}) \to \mathrm{Con}(\mathrm{ZFC} + \mathrm{CH})
$$

This says that if ZFC is consistent, then adding the continuum hypothesis does not create contradictions.

Relative consistency does not prove that any of these theories are consistent in an absolute sense. It only compares their strength.

### Models and Consistency

The main tool for proving consistency is the notion of a **model**.

If a theory $T$ has a model, then $T$ is consistent.

The idea is simple. A model is a structure in which all axioms of $T$ are true. If a contradiction were provable in $T$, it would be true in every model, which is impossible.

So:

$$
\text{If } T \text{ has a model, then } \mathrm{Con}(T)
$$

Relative consistency is often shown by building a model of one theory inside another.

For example, Gödel showed that the constructible universe $L$ is a model of:

$$
\mathrm{ZFC} + \mathrm{CH}
$$

So if ZF has a model $M$, then inside $M$ we can construct $L^M$, and this inner model satisfies ZFC and CH.

This gives:

$$
\mathrm{Con}(\mathrm{ZF}) \to \mathrm{Con}(\mathrm{ZFC} + \mathrm{CH})
$$

### Gödel’s Method

Gödel’s approach uses the constructible universe.

The key steps are:

* Start with a model of ZF
* Build the inner model $L$
* Show that $L$ satisfies all axioms of ZFC
* Show that $L$ satisfies additional statements such as CH

So $L$ acts as a controlled universe where many statements can be verified.

This method is often called an **inner model construction**.

### Cohen’s Method

Gödel’s method shows that certain statements cannot be disproved.

Paul Cohen introduced a different method, called **forcing**, which shows that certain statements cannot be proved.

For example, Cohen showed:

$$
\mathrm{Con}(\mathrm{ZFC}) \to \mathrm{Con}(\mathrm{ZFC} + ¬\mathrm{CH})
$$

This complements Gödel’s result.

Together, they show that:

$$
\mathrm{CH}
$$

is independent of ZFC.

The idea of forcing is to extend a given model $M$ to a larger model $M[G]$ in which new sets exist. These new sets are carefully added so that certain properties hold or fail.

For example, forcing can add new real numbers in a controlled way, changing the size of the continuum.

### Independence via Consistency

A statement $\varphi$ is independent of a theory $T$ if:

$$
\mathrm{Con}(T) \to \mathrm{Con}(T + \varphi)
$$

and:

$$
\mathrm{Con}(T) \to \mathrm{Con}(T + ¬\varphi)
$$

This means that both $\varphi$ and its negation are compatible with $T$.

In this situation, $T$ cannot decide $\varphi$.

The continuum hypothesis is the standard example:

$$
\mathrm{Con}(\mathrm{ZFC}) \to \mathrm{Con}(\mathrm{ZFC} + \mathrm{CH})
$$

and:

$$
\mathrm{Con}(\mathrm{ZFC}) \to \mathrm{Con}(\mathrm{ZFC} + ¬\mathrm{CH})
$$

So CH is independent of ZFC.

### Strength of Theories

Relative consistency also compares the strength of theories.

If:

$$
\mathrm{Con}(T_1) \to \mathrm{Con}(T_2)
$$

then $T_2$ is no stronger than $T_1$ in terms of consistency.

If both directions hold:

$$
\mathrm{Con}(T_1) \leftrightarrow \mathrm{Con}(T_2)
$$

then the theories have the same consistency strength.

Large cardinal axioms are often studied in this way. Stronger axioms imply the consistency of weaker ones.

For example, certain large cardinal assumptions imply:

$$
\mathrm{Con}(\mathrm{ZFC})
$$

but ZFC alone cannot prove the consistency of those large cardinal axioms.

This hierarchy organizes set theory by increasing strength.

### Limits of Consistency Proofs

Gödel’s second incompleteness theorem places limits on consistency proofs.

It says that a sufficiently strong theory $T$ cannot prove its own consistency:

$$
T \nvdash \mathrm{Con}(T)
$$

assuming $T$ is consistent.

This means that absolute consistency proofs must come from outside the theory.

So when we prove:

$$
\mathrm{Con}(T) \to \mathrm{Con}(T + \varphi)
$$

we are not proving consistency outright. We are shifting the question to a different theory.

### Typical Pattern

Most consistency results follow a similar structure.

* Start with a model $M$ of a base theory $T$
* Build a new structure (such as $L$ or a forcing extension $M[G]$)
* Show that the new structure satisfies additional axioms
* Conclude relative consistency

This pattern appears across set theory and logic.

### Why Consistency Results Matter

Consistency results show that axioms do not determine a single universe.

Different extensions of a theory can lead to different mathematical worlds.

Some statements are true in one model and false in another.

This changes the role of axioms. Instead of describing a single reality, they describe a space of possible structures.

Mathematics then studies which axioms to adopt, depending on the properties one wants the universe to have.
