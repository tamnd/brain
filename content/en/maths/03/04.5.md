## 4.5 Examples Across Algebra and Geometry

Model theory is useful because many different mathematical objects can be treated in the same formal way. Once we choose a language, each object becomes a structure, and its usual axioms become first-order sentences or schemas.

This section gives examples from algebra, order, graph theory, and geometry. The point is to see one common pattern:

$$
\text{language} \quad \longrightarrow \quad \text{structure} \quad \longrightarrow \quad \text{theory}.
$$

### Groups

A group can be studied in the language:

$$
\mathcal L_{\mathrm{grp}} = {\cdot, {}^{-1}, e}.
$$

A structure for this language has the form:

$$
(G; \cdot^{G}, {}^{-1G}, e^G).
$$

Usually we omit the superscripts and write:

$$
(G; \cdot, {}^{-1}, e).
$$

The group axioms can be written as first-order sentences.

Associativity:

$$
\forall x, \forall y, \forall z,
((x \cdot y)\cdot z = x \cdot (y \cdot z)).
$$

Identity:

$$
\forall x, (e \cdot x = x \land x \cdot e = x).
$$

Inverse:

$$
\forall x, (x \cdot x^{-1}=e \land x^{-1}\cdot x=e).
$$

A model of these sentences is exactly a group.

The abelian group axiom is also first-order:

$$
\forall x, \forall y, (x \cdot y = y \cdot x).
$$

So the class of abelian groups is elementary: it is described by first-order axioms.

### Rings

A ring can be studied in the language:

$$
\mathcal L_{\mathrm{ring}} = {0,1,+,-,\cdot}.
$$

A ring structure has the form:

$$
(R;0,1,+,-,\cdot).
$$

The ring axioms say that addition forms an abelian group, multiplication is associative, and multiplication distributes over addition.

One distributive law is:

$$
\forall x, \forall y, \forall z,
(x \cdot (y+z) = x\cdot y + x\cdot z).
$$

The other is:

$$
\forall x, \forall y, \forall z,
((x+y)\cdot z = x\cdot z + y\cdot z).
$$

Commutative rings add:

$$
\forall x, \forall y, (x\cdot y = y\cdot x).
$$

Integral domains add the condition that there are no zero divisors:

$$
\forall x, \forall y,
(x\cdot y = 0 \to (x=0 \lor y=0)).
$$

Fields are also first-order axiomatizable in this language by requiring every nonzero element to have a multiplicative inverse:

$$
\forall x, (x \neq 0 \to \exists y, (x\cdot y = 1)).
$$

### Ordered Fields

An ordered field uses the language:

$$
\mathcal L_{\mathrm{of}} = {0,1,+,-,\cdot,<}.
$$

A structure has the form:

$$
(F;0,1,+,-,\cdot,<).
$$

It is a field together with a linear order compatible with addition and multiplication.

Order compatibility with addition:

$$
\forall x, \forall y, \forall z,
(x < y \to x+z < y+z).
$$

Order compatibility with multiplication by positive elements:

$$
\forall x, \forall y, \forall z,
((0 < z \land x < y) \to x\cdot z < y\cdot z).
$$

The rational numbers form an ordered field:

$$
(\mathbb Q;0,1,+,-,\cdot,<).
$$

The real numbers also form an ordered field:

$$
(\mathbb R;0,1,+,-,\cdot,<).
$$

But these two ordered fields are not elementarily equivalent, because:

$$
\exists x, (x\cdot x = 2)
$$

is false in $\mathbb Q$ and true in $\mathbb R$.

### Graphs

A graph can be represented in a language with one binary relation symbol:

$$
\mathcal L_{\mathrm{graph}} = {E}.
$$

A graph structure is:

$$
(V;E),
$$

where $V$ is the set of vertices and $E \subseteq V^2$ is the edge relation.

For undirected simple graphs, we usually impose:

Irreflexivity:

$$
\forall x, ¬E(x,x).
$$

Symmetry:

$$
\forall x, \forall y, (E(x,y) \to E(y,x)).
$$

A triangle exists if:

$$
\exists x, \exists y, \exists z,
(x \neq y \land y \neq z \land x \neq z
\land E(x,y) \land E(y,z) \land E(z,x)).
$$

Being triangle-free is the negation of that sentence.

Graphs are a useful model-theoretic example because many global graph properties are not first-order definable. For instance, connectedness cannot be expressed by a single first-order sentence over arbitrary graphs.

### Linear Orders

A linear order uses the language:

$$
\mathcal L_{<} = {<}.
$$

A structure is:

$$
(M;<).
$$

The order axioms can be written first-order.

Transitivity:

$$
\forall x, \forall y, \forall z,
((x<y \land y<z) \to x<z).
$$

Irreflexivity:

$$
\forall x, ¬(x<x).
$$

Totality:

$$
\forall x, \forall y,
(x=y \lor x<y \lor y<x).
$$

Density is also first-order:

$$
\forall x, \forall y,
(x<y \to \exists z, (x<z \land z<y)).
$$

No endpoints can be expressed by two sentences:

$$
\forall x, \exists y, (y<x),
$$

$$
\forall x, \exists y, (x<y).
$$

The rational order:

$$
(\mathbb Q;<)
$$

and the real order:

$$
(\mathbb R;<)
$$

satisfy the same first-order sentences in the pure order language. Both are dense linear orders without endpoints.

### Vector Spaces

Vector spaces can be treated as first-order structures once the field is fixed.

For vector spaces over a fixed field $K$, we may use a language with:

* addition $+$
* zero vector $0$
* unary function symbols $\lambda_a$ for scalar multiplication by each $a \in K$

So the language is:

$$
\mathcal L_K = {0,+} \cup {\lambda_a : a \in K}.
$$

Here:

$$
\lambda_a(x)
$$

means scalar multiplication:

$$
a x.
$$

A vector space over $K$ becomes:

$$
(V;0,+,(\lambda_a)_{a\in K}).
$$

The vector space axioms are first-order in this language. For example:

$$
\forall x, \forall y, (\lambda_a(x+y)=\lambda_a(x)+\lambda_a(y)).
$$

For a fixed field $K$, model theory can study when two vector spaces are elementarily equivalent. Infinite-dimensional vector spaces over the same fixed field often have the same first-order theory in this language, even when their dimensions differ as cardinal numbers.

### Affine Geometry

Affine geometry can be described using points and lines.

One possible language has two sorts:

$$
\text{Point}, \qquad \text{Line},
$$

and one incidence relation:

$$
I(p,\ell),
$$

meaning point $p$ lies on line $\ell$.

A basic incidence axiom says that any two distinct points determine a unique line:

$$
\forall p, \forall q,
(p \neq q \to \exists \ell, (I(p,\ell) \land I(q,\ell)
\land
\forall m, ((I(p,m)\land I(q,m)) \to m=\ell))).
$$

This is a first-order sentence in a two-sorted language.

The model-theoretic advantage is that geometry can be studied through incidence relations rather than coordinates. Different coordinate systems may describe the same geometric structure.

### Algebraically Closed Fields

A central example in model theory is the theory of algebraically closed fields.

In the ring language:

$$
\mathcal L_{\mathrm{ring}} = {0,1,+,-,\cdot},
$$

a field is algebraically closed if every nonconstant polynomial has a root.

For each degree $n \ge 1$, this can be expressed by a first-order sentence:

$$
\forall a_0, \forall a_1 \cdots \forall a_n,
(a_n \neq 0 \to
\exists x, (a_nx^n + a_{n-1}x^{n-1}+\cdots+a_0=0)).
$$

Because this requires one sentence for each $n$, algebraic closure is expressed by an infinite axiom schema.

The theory of algebraically closed fields of fixed characteristic is complete. This means that, after fixing the characteristic, all algebraically closed fields satisfy the same first-order sentences in the ring language.

For example:

$$
\mathbb C
$$

and an algebraic closure of $\mathbb Q(t)$ of characteristic $0$ satisfy the same first-order sentences as fields, even though they are very different mathematical objects.
