## Chapter 5. Compactness and Completeness

Compactness and completeness are two central theorems of first-order logic. They explain why first-order logic is powerful, and also why it has strict expressive limits.

Completeness connects proof and truth. It says that if a sentence is true in every model of a theory, then there is a formal proof of it from that theory.

Compactness says that if every finite part of a theory has a model, then the whole theory has a model.

Together, these theorems show that first-order logic is locally controlled. Infinite theories can often be understood through their finite fragments.

### Completeness Theorem

Let $T$ be a first-order theory, and let $\varphi$ be a sentence.

The completeness theorem says:

$$
T \models \varphi
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
T \vdash \varphi
$$

The converse direction is soundness:

$$
T \vdash \varphi
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
T \models \varphi
$$

Together:

$$
T \vdash \varphi
\quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad
T \models \varphi
$$

This means that semantic consequence and formal derivability agree.

The symbol $T \models \varphi$ means that every model of $T$ also satisfies $\varphi$. The symbol $T \vdash \varphi$ means that there is a formal proof of $\varphi$ from the axioms of $T$.

So completeness tells us that proof systems are strong enough to capture all first-order semantic consequences.

### Compactness Theorem

The compactness theorem says:

$$
T \text{ has a model}
\quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad
\text{every finite } T_0 \subseteq T \text{ has a model}
$$

The forward direction is immediate. If $T$ has a model, then every finite subset $T_0$ also has a model.

The important direction is the reverse one. If every finite part of $T$ can be satisfied, then all of $T$ can be satisfied at once.

This is surprising because $T$ may contain infinitely many sentences. Compactness says that first-order inconsistency is always witnessed finitely.

Equivalently:

$$
T \models \varphi
$$

if and only if there is some finite subset $T_0 \subseteq T$ such that:

$$
T_0 \models \varphi
$$

So any first-order consequence of an infinite theory depends on only finitely many assumptions.

### Löwenheim-Skolem Theorems

The Löwenheim-Skolem theorems show that first-order theories often have models of many different sizes.

The downward Löwenheim-Skolem theorem says that if a first-order theory has an infinite model, then it has a smaller elementary submodel, often of countable size when the language is countable.

In one common form:

$$
\text{If } \mathcal M \text{ is infinite and } A \subseteq M,
$$

then there is an elementary substructure $\mathcal N \preccurlyeq \mathcal M$ such that:

$$
A \subseteq N
$$

and

$$
|N| \leq \max(|A|, |\mathcal L|, \aleph_0)
$$

The upward Löwenheim-Skolem theorem says that if a theory has an infinite model, then it has larger models too.

For a theory $T$ with an infinite model, there are models of $T$ in many larger cardinalities.

These theorems reveal an important limitation: first-order logic usually cannot control the exact size of infinite models.

### Applications to Algebra

Compactness is useful in algebra because it lets us pass from finite satisfiability to infinite existence.

For example, suppose every finite subsystem of a set of polynomial equations has a solution in some algebraic structure. Compactness can sometimes be used to obtain a model in which all equations have a simultaneous solution.

Compactness also explains why certain “finite approximations” cannot be distinguished from infinite behavior by first-order sentences.

For instance, no first-order sentence can say that a structure is finite. If first-order logic could express finiteness, then compactness would fail.

To see the idea, add sentences saying that there are at least $n$ distinct elements for every natural number $n$:

$$
\exists x_1,\dots,x_n \bigwedge_{i<j} x_i \neq x_j
$$

Every finite subset of these sentences has a finite model large enough to satisfy it. By compactness, the whole set has a model. That model must be infinite.

So first-order logic can express “there are at least $n$ elements” for each fixed $n$, but it cannot combine these into one sentence meaning “the structure is finite.”

### Nonstandard Models

Compactness also gives nonstandard models.

Consider arithmetic in the language:

$$
{0,1,+,\cdot,<}
$$

The standard model is:

$$
(\mathbb N;0,1,+,\cdot,<)
$$

Now add a new constant symbol $c$, and add sentences saying:

$$
c > 0,\quad c > 1,\quad c > 2,\quad c > 3,\quad \dots
$$

Every finite subset can be satisfied in the standard natural numbers by choosing $c$ larger than all numbers mentioned in that finite subset.

By compactness, the whole theory has a model. In that model, $c$ is greater than every standard natural number.

Such a model is nonstandard. It satisfies the same first-order arithmetic theory, but it contains elements beyond the usual natural numbers.

This shows that first-order axioms can describe arithmetic very strongly, but they cannot isolate the standard natural numbers up to isomorphism.

### Limitations of First-Order Logic

Compactness and Löwenheim-Skolem are powerful theorems, but they also expose limitations.

First-order logic cannot express many global properties of infinite structures.

Examples include:

| Property                          | Why first-order logic cannot fully capture it          |
| --------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------ |
| Finiteness                        | Contradicts compactness                                |
| Countability                      | Contradicts Löwenheim-Skolem behavior                  |
| Standard natural numbers          | Nonstandard models exist                               |
| Well-ordering in full generality  | Requires second-order strength                         |
| Connectedness of arbitrary graphs | Requires control over paths of unbounded finite length |

The issue is that first-order formulas quantify only over elements, not over arbitrary subsets, functions, or relations. This keeps the logic manageable, but it limits expressive power.
