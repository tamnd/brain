## 7.1 Sets, Relations, Functions

A **set** is a collection of objects. The objects inside a set are called **elements**.

For example:

$$
A = {1,2,3}
$$

The statement:

$$
2 \in A
$$

means that $2$ is an element of $A$.

The statement:

$$
4 \notin A
$$

means that $4$ is not an element of $A$.

A set is determined only by its elements. Order does not matter:

$$
{1,2,3} = {3,2,1}
$$

Repetition also does not matter:

$$
{1,1,2,3} = {1,2,3}
$$

### Subsets

A set $A$ is a **subset** of a set $B$ if every element of $A$ is also an element of $B$.

We write:

$$
A \subseteq B
$$

Formally:

$$
A \subseteq B
\quad \text{means} \quad
\forall x,(x \in A \to x \in B)
$$

Example:

$$
{1,2} \subseteq {1,2,3}
$$

The empty set is the set with no elements:

$$
\varnothing
$$

It is a subset of every set:

$$
\varnothing \subseteq A
$$

This is true because there is no element of $\varnothing$ that could violate the subset condition.

### Equality of Sets

Two sets are equal if they have exactly the same elements.

$$
A = B
\quad \text{means} \quad
\forall x,(x \in A \leftrightarrow x \in B)
$$

This principle is called **extensionality**. It says that a set is completely determined by its members.

For example:

$$
{x \in \mathbb{N} : x < 4}
==========================

{0,1,2,3}
$$

assuming:

$$
\mathbb{N} = {0,1,2,3,\dots}
$$

### Basic Set Operations

Given two sets $A$ and $B$, we can form new sets from them.

The **union** contains elements that belong to $A$ or $B$:

$$
A \cup B = {x : x \in A \lor x \in B}
$$

The **intersection** contains elements that belong to both:

$$
A \cap B = {x : x \in A \land x \in B}
$$

The **difference** contains elements that belong to $A$ but not to $B$:

$$
A \setminus B = {x : x \in A \land x \notin B}
$$

Example:

$$
A = {1,2,3}, \qquad B = {3,4,5}
$$

Then:

$$
A \cup B = {1,2,3,4,5}
$$

$$
A \cap B = {3}
$$

$$
A \setminus B = {1,2}
$$

### Power Sets

The **power set** of $A$ is the set of all subsets of $A$.

We write:

$$
\mathcal{P}(A)
$$

Example:

$$
A = {1,2}
$$

Then:

$$
\mathcal{P}(A)
==============

{\varnothing,{1},{2},{1,2}}
$$

If $A$ has $n$ elements, then $\mathcal{P}(A)$ has:

$$
2^n
$$

elements.

This happens because each element of $A$ has two choices: it is either included in a subset or excluded from it.

### Ordered Pairs and Cartesian Products

Sets do not care about order, but many mathematical objects do. To handle order, we use ordered pairs.

An **ordered pair** is written:

$$
(a,b)
$$

Here:

$$
(a,b) = (c,d)
$$

exactly when:

$$
a=c \quad \text{and} \quad b=d
$$

The **Cartesian product** of $A$ and $B$ is:

$$
A \times B = {(a,b) : a \in A,\ b \in B}
$$

Example:

$$
A={1,2}, \qquad B={x,y}
$$

Then:

$$
A \times B
==========

{(1,x),(1,y),(2,x),(2,y)}
$$

The Cartesian product lets us describe relations and functions.

### Relations

A **relation** from $A$ to $B$ is a subset of $A \times B$.

$$
R \subseteq A \times B
$$

If $(a,b) \in R$, we say that $a$ is related to $b$.

Example:

$$
A={1,2,3}, \qquad B={2,3,4}
$$

Define:

$$
R = {(a,b) \in A \times B : a < b}
$$

Then:

$$
R = {(1,2),(1,3),(1,4),(2,3),(2,4),(3,4)}
$$

A relation on a set $A$ is a subset of:

$$
A \times A
$$

For example, the usual order relation on natural numbers is:

$$
< \ \subseteq \mathbb{N} \times \mathbb{N}
$$

### Properties of Relations

A relation $R$ on a set $A$ may have special properties.

It is **reflexive** if every element is related to itself:

$$
\forall a \in A,\quad (a,a) \in R
$$

It is **symmetric** if relation works both ways:

$$
\forall a,b \in A,\quad (a,b) \in R \to (b,a) \in R
$$

It is **transitive** if relation can be chained:

$$
\forall a,b,c \in A,\quad ((a,b) \in R \land (b,c) \in R) \to (a,c) \in R
$$

For example, equality is reflexive, symmetric, and transitive.

The relation $\leq$ on $\mathbb{N}$ is reflexive and transitive, but not symmetric.

### Functions

A **function** from $A$ to $B$ is a relation that assigns each element of $A$ exactly one element of $B$.

We write:

$$
f : A \to B
$$

This means that for every $a \in A$, there is a unique $b \in B$ such that:

$$
f(a)=b
$$

Formally:

$$
\forall a \in A,\ \exists! b \in B,\ f(a)=b
$$

The symbol $\exists!$ means “there exists exactly one”.

The set $A$ is called the **domain** of $f$.

The set $B$ is called the **codomain** of $f$.

The actual set of outputs is called the **image** of $f$:

$$
\operatorname{im}(f)={f(a):a\in A}
$$

Always:

$$
\operatorname{im}(f)\subseteq B
$$

### Injective, Surjective, Bijective

A function $f:A\to B$ is **injective** if different inputs give different outputs.

$$
a_1 \neq a_2 \to f(a_1) \neq f(a_2)
$$

Equivalently:

$$
f(a_1)=f(a_2) \to a_1=a_2
$$

A function is **surjective** if every element of $B$ is hit by some element of $A$.

$$
\forall b \in B,\ \exists a \in A,\ f(a)=b
$$

A function is **bijective** if it is both injective and surjective.

Bijective functions are especially important because they show that two sets have the same size.

For example, the function:

$$
f:{1,2,3}\to{a,b,c}
$$

defined by:

$$
f(1)=a,\qquad f(2)=b,\qquad f(3)=c
$$

is bijective.
