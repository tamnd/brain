## Chapter 9. Advanced Set Theory

Advanced set theory studies methods and principles that go beyond the basic ZF and ZFC axioms. The central questions are no longer only about how sets are formed. They are also about what can be proved, what can be independent, and what stronger assumptions reveal about the universe of sets.

The main themes are:

$$
\text{forcing}, \quad \text{large cardinals}, \quad \text{definability}, \quad \text{determinacy}
$$

These topics show that set theory is both a foundation for mathematics and a mathematical subject with its own internal structure.

### Forcing

Forcing is a method for building new models of set theory from old ones. It was introduced to prove independence results, especially the independence of the continuum hypothesis.

The continuum hypothesis says:

$$
2^{\aleph_0} = \aleph_1
$$

Informally, it claims that there is no cardinal strictly between the size of the natural numbers and the size of the real numbers.

Forcing allows us to construct models where this statement is true and models where it is false, assuming the background axioms are consistent.

So forcing helps establish results of the form:

$$
\mathrm{ZFC} \nvdash \varphi
\quad \text{and} \quad
\mathrm{ZFC} \nvdash ¬\varphi
$$

This means that $\varphi$ cannot be decided from ZFC alone.

### Large Cardinals

Large cardinals are very large infinite cardinals with strong structural properties. They are stronger than the cardinals whose existence follows directly from ZFC.

Examples include:

$$
\text{inaccessible cardinals}
$$

$$
\text{measurable cardinals}
$$

$$
\text{supercompact cardinals}
$$

Large cardinal axioms are not usually proved from ZFC. Instead, they are added as stronger assumptions.

A large cardinal axiom often has the form:

$$
\exists \kappa , P(\kappa)
$$

where $\kappa$ is a cardinal and $P(\kappa)$ is a strong closure, reflection, embedding, or measure property.

These axioms create a hierarchy of consistency strength. Some theories imply the consistency of weaker theories, giving set theorists a way to compare foundational assumptions.

### Descriptive Set Theory

Descriptive set theory studies definable subsets of Polish spaces, especially subsets of the real line.

A **Polish space** is a separable completely metrizable topological space. Important examples include:

$$
\mathbb{R}, \quad \mathbb{N}^{\mathbb{N}}, \quad 2^{\mathbb{N}}
$$

Instead of studying arbitrary sets of reals, descriptive set theory studies sets that can be described in controlled ways.

The first major classes are:

$$
\text{open sets}, \quad \text{closed sets}, \quad \text{Borel sets}, \quad \text{analytic sets}
$$

Borel sets are generated from open sets using countable unions, countable intersections, and complements.

Analytic sets are continuous images of Borel sets. They are more general than Borel sets but still definable enough to have strong regularity properties.

### Determinacy Principles

Determinacy concerns infinite games.

A typical game has two players who alternately choose natural numbers:

$$
a_0, b_0, a_1, b_1, a_2, b_2, \dots
$$

The infinite sequence determines whether Player I or Player II wins.

A game is **determined** if one of the players has a winning strategy.

The axiom of determinacy says, roughly:

$$
\text{every suitable infinite game is determined}
$$

This principle conflicts with the full axiom of choice, but it has strong consequences for sets of real numbers. Under determinacy assumptions, many pathological sets disappear, and definable sets of reals behave more regularly.

For example, determinacy often implies that definable sets of reals have properties such as:

$$
\text{measurability}, \quad \text{the Baire property}, \quad \text{perfect set property}
$$

### Applications in Analysis and Topology

Advanced set theory affects ordinary mathematics more than one might expect. Many questions in analysis, topology, measure theory, and functional analysis depend on set-theoretic assumptions.

Some statements can be independent of ZFC. This means that standard axioms do not settle them.

For example, questions about:

$$
\text{cardinal characteristics of the continuum}
$$

$$
\text{special subsets of } \mathbb{R}
$$

$$
\text{bases in Banach spaces}
$$

$$
\text{compactness properties in topology}
$$

may change when we move between different models of set theory.

This chapter gives an overview of the main advanced tools. Forcing explains how to vary the universe. Large cardinals explain stronger possible universes. Descriptive set theory studies definable parts of the continuum. Determinacy gives an alternative regularity principle. Together, these ideas show that set theory is not only about membership and cardinality. It is also about the range of possible mathematical worlds.
