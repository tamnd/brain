## Chapter 13. Formal Proof Systems

Formal proof systems study proofs as mathematical objects. Instead of asking only whether a statement is true, proof theory asks how a statement can be derived, what rules are used, how long the proof is, and how proofs can be transformed.

A formal proof system has three main ingredients:

| Ingredient            | Role                                          |
| --------------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| Formal language       | tells us which expressions are valid formulas |
| Axioms or assumptions | give the starting points of proofs            |
| Inference rules       | tell us how new formulas may be derived       |

A proof is a finite object. Usually, it is a finite sequence or tree of formulas. Each step must be justified by an axiom, an assumption, or an inference rule.

For example, a basic inference rule is modus ponens:

$$
\frac{A \qquad A \to B}{B}
$$

This rule says that from $A$ and $A \to B$, we may infer $B$.

Formal proof systems make reasoning checkable. A proof does not depend on intuition, style, or persuasion. Once the rules are fixed, each line can be verified mechanically.

This chapter studies the syntax of proofs first. We define what counts as a derivation, how assumptions enter a proof, and how a conclusion follows from earlier formulas. This gives us the relation:

$$
\Gamma \vdash A
$$

meaning that $A$ is derivable from the assumptions in $\Gamma$.

Then we study derivability itself. The goal is to understand what can be proved inside a system and how derivations behave. Some proofs are direct. Others use temporary assumptions, contradiction, induction, or derived rules. A major task is to distinguish primitive rules from rules that are admissible because they can be simulated.

Normal forms for proofs are another central topic. Just as formulas can be simplified into normal forms, proofs can often be simplified too. A proof may contain unnecessary detours, such as proving an intermediate formula and immediately dismantling it. Normalization removes such detours and reveals the computational structure of the proof.

Consistency proofs ask whether a formal system avoids contradiction. A system is consistent if it does not prove both a formula and its negation:

$$
\not\vdash A \land ¬A
$$

Equivalently, in many classical systems, consistency means that not every formula is provable. If a system became inconsistent, it would lose its ability to distinguish valid conclusions from invalid ones.

Finally, proof length and proof complexity study the size of derivations. Two systems may prove the same theorems, but one may require much longer proofs than another. This turns proof theory into a quantitative subject. It connects logic with computation, complexity theory, automated theorem proving, and SAT solving.

The guiding idea of this chapter is that proofs have structure. They can be built, compared, compressed, normalized, and analyzed. Formal proof systems therefore give us a mathematical theory of reasoning itself.
