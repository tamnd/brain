## Chapter 10. Computable Functions

Computability theory studies what can be computed by a finite, mechanical procedure. It turns the informal idea of an “algorithm” into a precise mathematical object.

You already work with functions such as:

$$
f(n) = n+1, \quad g(x,y)=x+y
$$

These are easy to compute. Given an input, there is a clear step-by-step method that produces the output. Computability theory asks a deeper question:

$$
\text{Which functions admit such a method, and which do not?}
$$

This question forces a distinction between **description** and **computation**. A function may be described in a few words, yet there may be no algorithm that computes it for all inputs. The theory focuses on the boundary between what is computable and what is not.

To study this boundary, we need a precise definition of computation. Several definitions have been proposed, each from a different viewpoint:

* computation as symbolic manipulation by a machine
* computation as function evaluation
* computation as program execution
* computation as algebraic construction

These viewpoints lead to formal models such as Turing machines, recursive functions, lambda calculus, and simple programming languages. A central result of the subject is that all these models define the same class of functions. This agreement gives a stable notion of computability.

A key feature of computation is that it may **fail to terminate**. When a program runs, it may eventually return a value, or it may continue indefinitely. This leads to two kinds of functions:

$$
f : \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{N}
\quad \text{(total)}
$$

$$
f : \mathbb{N} \rightharpoonup \mathbb{N}
\quad \text{(partial)}
$$

Total functions produce an output for every input. Partial functions may be undefined on some inputs, reflecting non-terminating computations.

This distinction is not a technical detail. It is central to the theory. Many important computational processes cannot be expressed as total functions because there is no guarantee that they halt.

The chapter develops computability in stages.

First, recursive functions provide an algebraic way to define computable functions. You start with simple functions and build more complex ones using composition and recursion. This approach avoids machines and focuses on definability.

Second, the distinction between partial and total functions is made precise. This connects computation with termination and clarifies how algorithms behave when they do not produce an answer.

Third, the Church-Turing thesis relates formal models to the intuitive idea of an algorithm. It states that all effectively computable functions are captured by these formal definitions. This thesis is not proved mathematically, but it is supported by strong evidence from many independent models.

Fourth, several formal models of computation are introduced. Each model gives a different perspective, but they all aim to capture the same concept of effective procedure.

Fifth, the equivalence of these models is established. This shows that computability is not tied to a particular machine or programming language. It is a robust mathematical notion.

Computability theory has a distinctive character. Many results are negative. Instead of showing how to compute a function, the theory often proves that no algorithm can exist. These impossibility results are as important as constructive ones.

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

* distinguish between computable and non-computable functions
* understand how algorithms are modeled mathematically
* recognize the role of non-termination
* move between different models of computation
* understand why these models agree

These ideas form the foundation for later topics such as Turing machines, undecidability, and complexity theory.
