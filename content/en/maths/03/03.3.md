## 3.3 Hilbert Systems

A Hilbert system is a proof system with many axiom schemas and very few inference rules. In the simplest propositional versions, there is usually one main rule:

$$
\frac{A \qquad A \to B}{B}
$$

This rule is **modus ponens**.

The idea is different from natural deduction and sequent calculus. Natural deduction gives many local rules for connectives. Sequent calculus gives left and right rules for formulas inside sequents. A Hilbert system instead encodes most logical principles as axiom schemas.

### Axiom Schemas

An **axiom schema** is a pattern that represents infinitely many axioms.

For example:

$$
A \to (B \to A)
$$

is not one axiom. It is a template. We may replace $A$ and $B$ by any formulas.

If we choose:

$$
A = p
\qquad
B = q
$$

we get:

$$
p \to (q \to p)
$$

If we choose:

$$
A = r \land s
\qquad
B = ¬p
$$

we get:

$$
(r \land s) \to (¬p \to (r \land s))
$$

Both are instances of the same schema.

### A Basic Propositional Hilbert System

One common Hilbert system for implication and negation uses these axiom schemas:

$$
\mathrm{H1}: \quad A \to (B \to A)
$$

$$
\mathrm{H2}: \quad (A \to (B \to C)) \to ((A \to B) \to (A \to C))
$$

$$
\mathrm{H3}: \quad (¬B \to ¬A) \to (A \to B)
$$

Together with modus ponens, these schemas are enough for classical propositional logic with connectives $\to$ and $¬$.

Other connectives can be defined from these. For example:

$$
A \lor B \equiv ¬A \to B
$$

and:

$$
A \land B \equiv ¬(A \to ¬B)
$$

This shows why Hilbert systems can use a very small primitive language.

### Proofs

A proof in a Hilbert system is a finite sequence of formulas:

$$
A_1, A_2, \dots, A_n
$$

Each formula $A_i$ must be one of the following:

1. an instance of an axiom schema
2. an assumption
3. obtained from earlier formulas by modus ponens

The final formula $A_n$ is the conclusion.

If there is a proof of $A$ from assumptions $\Gamma$, we write:

$$
\Gamma \vdash A
$$

### Example: Proving $p \to (q \to p)$

The formula:

$$
p \to (q \to p)
$$

is an instance of H1:

$$
A \to (B \to A)
$$

using:

$$
A = p
\qquad
B = q
$$

So it has a one-line proof:

$$
p \to (q \to p)
$$

This is typical in Hilbert systems. Some formulas that require a visible argument in natural deduction are immediate because they are axiom instances.

### Example: Modus Ponens in a Proof

Suppose our assumptions are:

$$
p
$$

and:

$$
p \to q
$$

Then we can prove:

$$
q
$$

The Hilbert proof is:

| Line | Formula   | Reason                    |
| ---- | --------- | ------------------------- |
| 1    | $p$       | assumption                |
| 2    | $p \to q$ | assumption                |
| 3    | $q$       | modus ponens from 1 and 2 |

So:

$$
p,\ p \to q \vdash q
$$

### Why Hilbert Proofs Feel Sparse

Hilbert systems are compact, but they are often hard to read.

For example, natural deduction lets you write:

$$
\text{assume } p,\ \text{then prove } q,\ \text{therefore } p \to q
$$

A Hilbert system does not usually have a direct rule that says this. Instead, the same reasoning is encoded by a meta-theorem called the deduction theorem.

### Deduction Theorem

The deduction theorem says:

$$
\Gamma, A \vdash B
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
\Gamma \vdash A \to B
$$

This means that if $B$ can be proved using $A$ as an extra assumption, then $A \to B$ can be proved without keeping $A$ as an assumption.

This theorem lets Hilbert systems recover conditional reasoning.

For example, if:

$$
p, q \vdash p
$$

then the deduction theorem gives:

$$
p \vdash q \to p
$$

Applying it again gives:

$$
\vdash p \to (q \to p)
$$

In practice, we often use the deduction theorem to avoid writing long Hilbert derivations by hand.

### First-Order Hilbert Systems

For first-order logic, Hilbert systems add axiom schemas and rules for quantifiers.

A typical universal instantiation axiom is:

$$
\forall x,A(x) \to A(t)
$$

This says that if $A$ holds for every $x$, then it holds for a particular term $t$.

A common quantifier rule is generalization:

$$
\frac{A}{\forall x,A}
$$

with restrictions on free variables. The rule is valid only when $x$ is not free in any undischarged assumption. This prevents invalid reasoning from a special object to all objects.

For example, from:

$$
P(a)
$$

we may not conclude:

$$
\forall x,P(x)
$$

unless $a$ was arbitrary rather than special.

### Strengths of Hilbert Systems

Hilbert systems are useful because they are small.

A whole proof system can often be described by:

* a short list of axiom schemas
* one or two inference rules
* a precise definition of formula substitution

This makes them convenient for metatheory. They are easy to specify, easy to encode, and easy to study abstractly.

They are also useful in completeness proofs. Since derivations are finite sequences and the rule set is small, it is often easier to reason about the set of provable formulas.

### Weaknesses of Hilbert Systems

Hilbert proofs are usually long and unnatural.

They do not directly mirror ordinary mathematical reasoning. Simple arguments may require many applications of axiom schemas and modus ponens.

For learning and writing proofs, natural deduction is usually easier. For structural analysis, sequent calculus is usually better. For compact formal specification, Hilbert systems are useful.

### Comparison

| System            | Main style                     | Good for           |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------ | ------------------ |
| Natural deduction | assumptions and local rules    | readable proofs    |
| Sequent calculus  | sequents and structural rules  | proof analysis     |
| Hilbert system    | axiom schemas and modus ponens | compact metatheory |

A Hilbert system gives a minimal symbolic engine for logic. It shows that a small number of schemas and rules can generate all valid formulas of the logic.
