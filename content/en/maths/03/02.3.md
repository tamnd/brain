## 2.3 Structures and Interpretations

First-order formulas need a setting in which their symbols receive meaning. That setting is called a **structure**.

A formula such as:

$$
\forall x,\exists y,(x<y)
$$

does not become true or false by syntax alone. We must know what objects $x$ and $y$ range over, and what the symbol $<$ means.

### Languages and Non-Logical Symbols

A first-order language contains logical symbols and non-logical symbols.

Logical symbols have fixed meanings:

$$
¬,\ \land,\ \lor,\ \to,\ \leftrightarrow,\ \forall,\ \exists,\ =
$$

Non-logical symbols receive meaning from a structure. These include:

| Symbol type       | Example        | Needs interpretation       |
| ----------------- | -------------- | -------------------------- |
| Constant symbols  | $0, 1, e$      | which object they name     |
| Function symbols  | $S, +, \times$ | which function they denote |
| Predicate symbols | $<, P, R$      | which relation they denote |

For example, the symbol $+$ has no built-in meaning until a structure says what operation it denotes.

### Domains

A structure begins with a **domain**, also called its universe.

The domain is the collection of objects that variables range over.

Examples:

$$
\mathbb{N}
$$

$$
\mathbb{Z}
$$

$$
\mathbb{R}
$$

$$
{1,2,3}
$$

If the domain is $\mathbb{N}$, then variables range over natural numbers. If the domain is a graph’s vertex set, then variables range over vertices.

A structure usually has a nonempty domain.

### Interpreting Constants

A constant symbol is interpreted as one object in the domain.

Suppose the language has a constant symbol $c$ and the domain is:

$$
{1,2,3}
$$

A structure might interpret $c$ as:

$$
c^{\mathcal M}=2
$$

The superscript notation $c^{\mathcal M}$ means “the interpretation of $c$ in the structure $\mathcal M$”.

### Interpreting Functions

An $n$-ary function symbol is interpreted as a function from $n$ inputs in the domain to one output in the domain.

If $f$ is binary, then:

$$
f^{\mathcal M}: M^2 \to M
$$

where $M$ is the domain of $\mathcal M$.

Example:

$$
+^{\mathcal N}: \mathbb{N}^2 \to \mathbb{N}
$$

may be ordinary addition on natural numbers.

So the term:

$$
x+y
$$

gets its value by applying the interpreted function $+^{\mathcal N}$ to the values assigned to $x$ and $y$.

### Interpreting Predicates

An $n$-ary predicate symbol is interpreted as an $n$-ary relation on the domain.

If $R$ is binary, then:

$$
R^{\mathcal M} \subseteq M^2
$$

Example:

$$
<^{\mathcal N} \subseteq \mathbb{N}^2
$$

may be the usual less-than relation.

Then:

$$
x<y
$$

is true exactly when the pair of values assigned to $x$ and $y$ belongs to $<^{\mathcal N}$.

### Example: Natural Numbers

A common structure for arithmetic is:

$$
\mathcal N = (\mathbb{N}; 0, S, +, \times, <)
$$

Here:

| Symbol   | Interpretation                 |
| -------- | ------------------------------ |
| Domain   | $\mathbb{N}$                   |
| $0$      | the number $0$                 |
| $S$      | successor function, $S(n)=n+1$ |
| $+$      | ordinary addition              |
| $\times$ | ordinary multiplication        |
| $<$      | ordinary ordering              |

In this structure, the sentence:

$$
\forall x, (x+0=x)
$$

is true.

The sentence:

$$
\exists x, (x+x=1)
$$

is false in $\mathbb{N}$.

### Same Language, Different Structures

The same language can be interpreted by different structures.

Take the language:

$$
{0,+,<}
$$

One structure may use the natural numbers:

$$
(\mathbb{N};0,+,<)
$$

Another may use the integers:

$$
(\mathbb{Z};0,+,<)
$$

Another may use the real numbers:

$$
(\mathbb{R};0,+,<)
$$

The same formula can behave differently in these structures.

For example:

$$
\forall x,\exists y,(y+y=x)
$$

This is false in $\mathbb{N}$, because not every natural number is twice another natural number.

It is also false in $\mathbb{Z}$.

It is true in $\mathbb{R}$, because every real number has a half.

### Example: Graphs as Structures

First-order logic can also describe graphs.

Use a language with one binary predicate symbol:

$$
E(x,y)
$$

A graph structure is:

$$
\mathcal G = (V; E^{\mathcal G})
$$

where $V$ is the set of vertices and $E^{\mathcal G}$ is the edge relation.

The formula:

$$
E(x,y)
$$

says there is an edge from $x$ to $y$.

The sentence:

$$
\forall x,¬E(x,x)
$$

says there are no self-loops.

The sentence:

$$
\forall x,\forall y,(E(x,y)\to E(y,x))
$$

says the graph is undirected, assuming $E$ is represented as a directed relation.

### Interpretations Depend on the Structure

A symbol does not carry its own mathematical meaning.

The same predicate symbol $R$ could mean less-than in one structure, divisibility in another, or adjacency in a graph.

For example:

$$
R(x,y)
$$

could be interpreted as:

$$
x<y
$$

or:

$$
x \mid y
$$

or:

$$
x \text{ is connected to } y
$$

The formal language only gives the shape of the expression. The structure supplies the mathematical content.
