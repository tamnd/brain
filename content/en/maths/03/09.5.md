## 9.5 Applications in Analysis and Topology

Advanced set theory has concrete effects on analysis and topology. Many natural questions about subsets of $\mathbb{R}$, functions, and spaces cannot be resolved within ZFC alone. Their answers may vary across different models.

### Cardinal Characteristics of the Continuum

Between the size of the natural numbers and the size of the real numbers, there are many intermediate invariants. These are called **cardinal characteristics of the continuum**.

They measure combinatorial properties of subsets of $\mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{N}$.

Examples include:

* the bounding number $\mathfrak{b}$
* the dominating number $\mathfrak{d}$
* the splitting number $\mathfrak{s}$

These satisfy inequalities such as:

$$
\aleph_0 < \mathfrak{b} \leq \mathfrak{d} \leq 2^{\aleph_0}
$$

The exact values of these cardinals are not fixed by ZFC. Forcing can produce models where these quantities differ.

### Special Subsets of $\mathbb{R}$

Set theory influences the existence of unusual subsets of real numbers.

Examples include:

* non-measurable sets
* sets without the Baire property
* sets without the perfect set property

Under the axiom of choice, such sets exist.

Under determinacy assumptions, these pathologies disappear. Every set of reals may satisfy strong regularity properties.

So the behavior of subsets of $\mathbb{R}$ depends on the axioms we adopt.

### Measure and Category

Two central notions in analysis are:

* Lebesgue measure
* Baire category

Set theory determines how broadly these notions apply.

For example, under strong determinacy assumptions:

$$
\text{every set of reals is Lebesgue measurable}
$$

and:

$$
\text{every set has the Baire property}
$$

In contrast, under full choice, there exist sets that violate these properties.

This shows that basic analytical questions can depend on foundational assumptions.

### Functional Analysis

In functional analysis, set theory affects the structure of spaces and bases.

Questions include:

* does every Banach space have a basis
* do certain compactness properties hold
* how large can certain operator families be

Some of these questions are independent of ZFC.

Different models can yield different answers about the existence of bases or special subspaces.

### Topology

In topology, set theory influences compactness, separability, and covering properties.

Examples include:

* existence of special compact spaces
* behavior of product spaces
* properties of function spaces

Statements about these objects may depend on additional axioms such as:

$$
\text{CH}, \quad \text{Martin's Axiom}, \quad \text{large cardinal assumptions}
$$

For instance, the structure of certain topological spaces can change depending on whether:

$$
2^{\aleph_0} = \aleph_1
$$

or not.

### Forcing in Applications

Forcing is often used to construct models where specific analytical or topological statements hold or fail.

A typical pattern is:

* define a property $P$
* use forcing to build a model where $P$ holds
* use another forcing to build a model where $P$ fails

This shows that $P$ is independent of ZFC.

### Stability and Regularity

Advanced set theory often separates two kinds of behavior.

* **regular behavior**, where sets and spaces have predictable structure
* **pathological behavior**, where counterintuitive examples exist

Determinacy and large cardinals tend to enforce regularity.

The axiom of choice allows more pathological constructions.

Understanding this balance is important when interpreting results in analysis and topology.

### Perspective

Set theory does not only provide a foundation. It also acts as a parameter that shapes mathematical reality.

When working in analysis or topology, you may implicitly assume certain set-theoretic principles. Changing those assumptions can change which objects exist and which theorems hold.

This explains why advanced set theory remains relevant beyond logic. It determines the limits and possibilities of structures used throughout mathematics.
