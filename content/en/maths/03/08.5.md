## 8.5 Independence Phenomena

Independence is a central feature of modern set theory. It shows that some statements cannot be decided from a given set of axioms.

Let $T$ be a theory and $\varphi$ a statement. We say that $\varphi$ is **independent** of $T$ if:

$$
T \nvdash \varphi
\quad \text{and} \quad
T \nvdash ¬\varphi
$$

assuming $T$ is consistent.

This means that neither $\varphi$ nor its negation can be proved from the axioms of $T$.

### Independence via Models

A standard way to prove independence is to build two models:

* one model where $\varphi$ is true
* one model where $¬\varphi$ is true

If both models satisfy the axioms of $T$, then $T$ cannot decide $\varphi$.

In symbols, we show:

$$
\mathrm{Con}(T) \to \mathrm{Con}(T + \varphi)
$$

and:

$$
\mathrm{Con}(T) \to \mathrm{Con}(T + ¬\varphi)
$$

This establishes independence.

### The Continuum Hypothesis

The most famous example is the **continuum hypothesis (CH)**.

It states:

$$
\nexists A,
\left(
|\mathbb{N}| < |A| < |\mathbb{R}|
\right)
$$

Equivalently:

$$
2^{\aleph_0} = \aleph_1
$$

This says that there is no set whose size lies strictly between the natural numbers and the real numbers.

Gödel showed that CH cannot be disproved from ZFC:

$$
\mathrm{Con}(\mathrm{ZFC}) \to \mathrm{Con}(\mathrm{ZFC} + \mathrm{CH})
$$

Cohen showed that CH cannot be proved from ZFC:

$$
\mathrm{Con}(\mathrm{ZFC}) \to \mathrm{Con}(\mathrm{ZFC} + ¬\mathrm{CH})
$$

Together:

$$
\mathrm{CH} \text{ is independent of ZFC}
$$

This result changed the understanding of set theory. It showed that even basic questions about sizes of sets cannot be settled by the standard axioms.

### Other Independence Results

Independence appears in many areas of set theory.

Examples include:

* Suslin’s hypothesis
* the existence of measurable cardinals
* combinatorial principles such as $\Diamond$ and $\Box$
* various statements about stationary sets and trees

Many of these statements can be forced to hold or fail by choosing appropriate models.

### Forcing and Independence

The main tool for proving independence is **forcing**.

Forcing starts with a model $M$ of a theory such as ZFC. It then builds an extension:

$$
M[G]
$$

where $G$ is a generic object.

The extension adds new sets while preserving the axioms of the theory.

By carefully choosing the forcing construction, we can control which statements become true in $M[G]$.

For example, forcing can:

* add new real numbers
* change the size of the continuum
* create or destroy combinatorial structures

This allows us to produce models where a given statement holds, and others where it fails.

### Absoluteness and Non-Absoluteness

Some statements are **absolute** between models. They have the same truth value in different universes.

Other statements are **non-absolute**. Their truth depends on the model.

Independence results show that many important set-theoretic statements are non-absolute.

For example, the statement:

$$
2^{\aleph_0} = \aleph_1
$$

may be true in one model and false in another.

This means that the size of the continuum is not fixed by ZFC alone.

### Hierarchies of Strength

Independence leads to a hierarchy of stronger and weaker axioms.

If a statement $\varphi$ is independent of ZFC, mathematicians may consider adding $\varphi$ as a new axiom.

Different choices lead to different extended theories:

$$
\mathrm{ZFC} + \varphi
\quad \text{or} \quad
\mathrm{ZFC} + ¬\varphi
$$

Each extension has its own models and consequences.

Large cardinal axioms are examples of stronger axioms that extend ZFC. They assert the existence of very large infinite sets with special properties.

These axioms often settle questions that are independent of ZFC alone, but they also increase the strength of the theory.

### Independence and Foundations

Independence changes how we think about mathematical truth.

In classical mathematics, one may expect that every well-formed statement is either provable or refutable.

Independence shows that this expectation fails in strong formal systems.

Instead, a theory defines a range of possible universes. A statement may be true in some of these universes and false in others.

So truth becomes relative to a model:

$$
M \models \varphi
\quad \text{or} \quad
M \models ¬\varphi
$$

depending on the structure $M$.

### Choosing Axioms

Because of independence, mathematicians sometimes adopt additional axioms to settle undecidable questions.

The choice of axioms may be guided by:

* mathematical usefulness
* coherence with existing theory
* explanatory power
* structural principles such as maximality or minimality

For example, some prefer $V=L$ because it gives a minimal and well-ordered universe.

Others prefer large cardinal axioms because they lead to richer structures and stronger results.

There is no single universally accepted extension of ZFC that resolves all independent statements.

### Summary of the Phenomenon

Independence shows that:

* axioms do not determine a unique mathematical universe
* different models can satisfy the same axioms but differ on key statements
* additional axioms can extend the theory in different directions

This makes set theory both technically rich and philosophically open.
