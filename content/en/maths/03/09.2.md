## 9.2 Large Cardinals

Large cardinals are infinite cardinals with strong structural properties that go far beyond what can be proved in ZFC alone. They are introduced as additional axioms and form a hierarchy ordered by strength.

### Motivation

Basic set theory already distinguishes sizes of infinity:

$$
\aleph_0 < \aleph_1 < \aleph_2 < \cdots
$$

These cardinals arise within ZFC. Large cardinals go further. They assert the existence of cardinals with special closure, reflection, or embedding properties that shape the entire universe of sets.

A typical large cardinal statement has the form:

$$
\exists \kappa , P(\kappa)
$$

where $\kappa$ is a cardinal and $P(\kappa)$ expresses a strong property.

### Inaccessible Cardinals

A cardinal $\kappa$ is **inaccessible** if:

* it is uncountable
* it is regular
* it is a strong limit

Regular means:

$$
\mathrm{cf}(\kappa) = \kappa
$$

Strong limit means:

$$
\forall \lambda < \kappa,\quad 2^\lambda < \kappa
$$

Such a $\kappa$ cannot be reached by the usual set-theoretic operations starting from smaller cardinals.

An inaccessible cardinal marks a boundary where the structure of the universe “repeats” in a smaller form. The set:

$$
V_\kappa
$$

is then a model of a large fragment of set theory.

### Measurable Cardinals

A cardinal $\kappa$ is **measurable** if there exists a nontrivial measure on $\kappa$.

More precisely, there is a function:

$$
\mu : \mathcal{P}(\kappa) \to {0,1}
$$

such that:

* $\mu(\kappa)=1$
* $\mu(\varnothing)=0$
* $\mu$ is $\kappa$-additive

This means that $\kappa$ carries a structure similar to a probability measure, but defined on all subsets.

An equivalent formulation uses elementary embeddings. There exists a nontrivial embedding:

$$
j : V \to M
$$

with critical point $\kappa$, meaning:

$$
j(\kappa) > \kappa
$$

Measurable cardinals are much stronger than inaccessible cardinals.

### Supercompact Cardinals

A cardinal $\kappa$ is **supercompact** if it has extremely strong reflection properties.

One formulation is:

For every $\lambda > \kappa$, there exists an elementary embedding:

$$
j : V \to M
$$

such that:

* the critical point of $j$ is $\kappa$
* $j(\kappa) > \lambda$
* $M$ contains all sequences of length $\lambda$

Supercompact cardinals reflect large portions of the universe into smaller structures while preserving truth.

They sit high in the large cardinal hierarchy and imply many combinatorial and structural properties.

### Hierarchy of Strength

Large cardinal axioms are ordered by consistency strength.

If theory $T_1$ proves the consistency of $T_2$, then $T_1$ is stronger.

For example:

$$
\text{supercompact} \Rightarrow \text{measurable} \Rightarrow \text{inaccessible}
$$

This means:

* if a supercompact cardinal exists, then a measurable cardinal exists
* if a measurable cardinal exists, then an inaccessible cardinal exists

Each step increases strength and expressive power.

### Reflection Principles

A recurring theme is **reflection**.

Large cardinals often satisfy statements like:

$$
\text{properties of the whole universe already appear in smaller parts}
$$

For example, if a statement holds in $V$, it may already hold in some $V_\alpha$ with $\alpha < \kappa$.

This idea suggests that the universe of sets is highly uniform.

### Interaction with Forcing

Large cardinals interact deeply with forcing.

* Some large cardinal properties are preserved under certain forcing extensions.
* Others are destroyed by forcing.
* Forcing can be used to test the robustness of large cardinal assumptions.

Large cardinals often provide the strength needed to prove consistency results about forcing constructions.

### Role in Modern Set Theory

Large cardinal axioms guide the study of the set-theoretic universe.

They provide:

* a scale of stronger and stronger theories
* a framework for comparing consistency
* structure that explains regularity in mathematics

Many results in descriptive set theory, determinacy, and combinatorics depend on large cardinal assumptions.

In practice, large cardinals act as organizing principles. They describe how rich the universe of sets can be, and they connect deep areas of logic into a coherent hierarchy.
