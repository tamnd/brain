## 6.1 Definable Sets and Functions

Definability explains how formulas describe subsets and relations inside a structure. It turns logical expressions into geometric or algebraic objects.

Let $\mathcal M$ be a structure with domain $M$.

### Definable Sets

A subset $X \subseteq M$ is **definable** if there exists a formula $\varphi(x)$ such that:

$$
X = { a \in M \mid \mathcal M \models \varphi(a) }
$$

The formula $\varphi(x)$ acts as a description. It selects exactly those elements that satisfy it.

Example in arithmetic:

$$
\varphi(x) := \exists y,(x = y+y)
$$

defines the set of even numbers:

$$
{0,2,4,6,\dots}
$$

Here the structure is:

$$
(\mathbb{N}; 0, +)
$$

The definition depends on both the formula and the structure.

### Definable Sets with Parameters

Sometimes definitions require specific elements from the structure.

A subset $X \subseteq M$ is **definable with parameters** if there exists a formula $\varphi(x,a_1,\dots,a_n)$ with parameters $a_i \in M$ such that:

$$
X = { a \in M \mid \mathcal M \models \varphi(a,a_1,\dots,a_n) }
$$

Example in $(\mathbb{N}; <)$:

$$
\varphi(x,5) := x < 5
$$

defines:

$$
{0,1,2,3,4}
$$

Without the parameter $5$, this set cannot be described in the pure order language.

Parameters increase expressive power. They let formulas refer to specific elements.

### Definable Relations

Definability extends to higher dimensions.

A subset $R \subseteq M^n$ is definable if there exists a formula $\varphi(x_1,\dots,x_n)$ such that:

$$
R = { (a_1,\dots,a_n) \in M^n \mid \mathcal M \models \varphi(a_1,\dots,a_n) }
$$

Example:

$$
\varphi(x,y) := x < y
$$

defines the relation:

$$
{(a,b)\in M^2 : a < b}
$$

Example in arithmetic:

$$
\varphi(x,y) := \exists z,(x+z=y)
$$

defines the relation “$x \le y$”.

### Definable Functions

A function $f : M^n \to M$ is **definable** if its graph is definable.

That means there exists a formula $\varphi(x_1,\dots,x_n,y)$ such that:

$$
\mathcal M \models \varphi(a_1,\dots,a_n,b)
$$

if and only if:

$$
f(a_1,\dots,a_n)=b
$$

Example:

Addition in arithmetic is definable by:

$$
\varphi(x,y,z) := x + y = z
$$

This formula defines the graph of the addition function.

The key idea is that functions are treated as special relations that satisfy uniqueness.

### Definable Closure

Definability leads to a notion similar to algebraic closure.

An element $b \in M$ is **definable from** a set $A \subseteq M$ if there exists a formula $\varphi(x,a_1,\dots,a_n)$ with parameters from $A$ such that:

* $\mathcal M \models \varphi(b,a_1,\dots,a_n)$
* $b$ is the unique element satisfying $\varphi$

The set of all elements definable from $A$ is called the **definable closure** of $A$, written:

$$
\mathrm{dcl}(A)
$$

This captures elements that can be uniquely specified using parameters from $A$.

### Examples Across Structures

**Pure equality structure**

Consider a structure with only equality and no additional symbols.

In this setting, definable sets are very limited.

Without parameters, the only definable subsets of $M$ are:

* the empty set
* the whole domain

With parameters, definable sets are finite or cofinite subsets.

**Ordered structures**

In:

$$
(\mathbb{N}; <)
$$

definable sets correspond to simple order conditions.

With parameters, we can define initial segments like:

$$
x < a
$$

Without parameters, only trivial sets are definable.

**Arithmetic**

In:

$$
(\mathbb{N}; 0,1,+,\times,<)
$$

definability becomes much richer.

We can define:

* even numbers
* prime numbers
* divisibility relations
* polynomial conditions

For example:

$$
\mathrm{Prime}(x) := x>1 \land \forall y,\forall z,(x = yz \to (y=1 \lor z=1))
$$

### Invariance Under Automorphisms

Definable sets have a key structural property.

If $\sigma$ is an automorphism of $\mathcal M$, then definable sets are preserved by $\sigma$.

That means:

$$
a \in X \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \sigma(a) \in X
$$

for any definable set $X$.

This shows that definability captures structure that does not depend on naming specific elements.

### Perspective

Definable sets form the “visible geometry” of a structure.

They are the subsets that logic can describe.

In simple structures, definable sets are limited.

In richer structures, definable sets can encode deep arithmetic or geometric properties.

This idea will connect directly to types, where instead of describing sets of existing elements, we describe consistent properties that elements could satisfy.
