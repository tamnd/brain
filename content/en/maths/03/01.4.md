## 1.4 Normal Forms: CNF, DNF

Normal forms give formulas a standard shape. They are useful because they turn arbitrary logical expressions into regular patterns that are easier to analyze, compare, and compute with.

The two most common normal forms in propositional logic are:

| Form | Full name               | Shape                |
| ---- | ----------------------- | -------------------- |
| CNF  | conjunctive normal form | an AND of OR-clauses |
| DNF  | disjunctive normal form | an OR of AND-clauses |

Before defining them, we need one small term.

A **literal** is either a variable or the negation of a variable:

$$
p, \quad ¬p, \quad q, \quad ¬q
$$

So $p$ and $¬p$ are both literals. But $¬(p \lor q)$ is not a literal, because the negation is applied to a compound formula.

### Conjunctive Normal Form

A formula is in **conjunctive normal form**, or CNF, if it is a conjunction of clauses, where each clause is a disjunction of literals.

A typical CNF formula has the shape:

$$
(L_{11} \lor L_{12} \lor \cdots \lor L_{1m_1})
\land
(L_{21} \lor L_{22} \lor \cdots \lor L_{2m_2})
\land
\cdots
\land
(L_{k1} \lor L_{k2} \lor \cdots \lor L_{km_k})
$$

Each $L_{ij}$ is a literal.

Example:

$$
(p \lor ¬q \lor r) \land (¬p \lor s) \land (q \lor ¬r)
$$

This is CNF because the outer structure is a conjunction, and each part is a disjunction of literals.

You can read CNF as a list of requirements. Every clause must be true. Inside each clause, at least one literal must be true.

For example:

$$
(p \lor q) \land (¬p \lor r)
$$

means:

$$
p \lor q
$$

must be true, and

$$
¬p \lor r
$$

must also be true.

### Disjunctive Normal Form

A formula is in **disjunctive normal form**, or DNF, if it is a disjunction of terms, where each term is a conjunction of literals.

A typical DNF formula has the shape:

$$
(L_{11} \land L_{12} \land \cdots \land L_{1m_1})
\lor
(L_{21} \land L_{22} \land \cdots \land L_{2m_2})
\lor
\cdots
\lor
(L_{k1} \land L_{k2} \land \cdots \land L_{km_k})
$$

Each $L_{ij}$ is a literal.

Example:

$$
(p \land ¬q \land r) \lor (¬p \land s) \lor (q \land ¬r)
$$

This is DNF because the outer structure is a disjunction, and each part is a conjunction of literals.

You can read DNF as a list of possible ways for the formula to be true. At least one term must be true. Inside each term, all literals must be true.

For example:

$$
(p \land q) \lor (¬p \land r)
$$

means either:

$$
p \land q
$$

is true, or:

$$
¬p \land r
$$

is true.

### CNF and DNF by Example

Consider the formula:

$$
p \to (q \lor r)
$$

First remove the implication:

$$
¬p \lor (q \lor r)
$$

Then flatten the disjunction:

$$
¬p \lor q \lor r
$$

This formula is both CNF and DNF in a simple sense. It is one disjunctive clause, so it is CNF. It is also a disjunction of one-literal terms, so it is DNF.

Now consider:

$$
p \land (q \lor r)
$$

This is already CNF:

$$
(p) \land (q \lor r)
$$

To convert it to DNF, distribute $\land$ over $\lor$:

$$
p \land (q \lor r)
\equiv
(p \land q) \lor (p \land r)
$$

So the DNF form is:

$$
(p \land q) \lor (p \land r)
$$

### Converting to Normal Form

A systematic conversion usually follows these steps.

First, remove biconditionals:

$$
A \leftrightarrow B
\equiv
(A \to B) \land (B \to A)
$$

Second, remove implications:

$$
A \to B
\equiv
¬A \lor B
$$

Third, push negations inward using De Morgan laws:

$$
¬(A \land B) \equiv ¬A \lor ¬B
$$

$$
¬(A \lor B) \equiv ¬A \land ¬B
$$

and double negation:

$$
¬¬A \equiv A
$$

After this step, negation appears only directly in front of variables.

Fourth, distribute connectives.

To get CNF, distribute $\lor$ over $\land$:

$$
A \lor (B \land C)
\equiv
(A \lor B) \land (A \lor C)
$$

To get DNF, distribute $\land$ over $\lor$:

$$
A \land (B \lor C)
\equiv
(A \land B) \lor (A \land C)
$$

### CNF Conversion Example

Convert:

$$
¬(p \to q) \lor r
$$

Remove implication:

$$
¬(¬p \lor q) \lor r
$$

Push negation inward:

$$
(p \land ¬q) \lor r
$$

Distribute $\lor$ over $\land$:

$$
(p \lor r) \land (¬q \lor r)
$$

So a CNF form is:

$$
(p \lor r) \land (¬q \lor r)
$$

### DNF Conversion Example

Convert:

$$
¬(p \to q) \lor r
$$

Remove implication:

$$
¬(¬p \lor q) \lor r
$$

Push negation inward:

$$
(p \land ¬q) \lor r
$$

This is already DNF:

$$
(p \land ¬q) \lor r
$$

If desired, we may regard $r$ as a one-literal term.

### Normal Forms from Truth Tables

Truth tables also produce normal forms directly.

To build DNF, look at the rows where the formula is true. For each true row, make a conjunction that describes exactly that row. Then join those conjunctions with $\lor$.

Example:

| $p$          | $q$          | Formula      |
| ------------ | ------------ | ------------ |
| $\mathrm{T}$ | $\mathrm{T}$ | $\mathrm{T}$ |
| $\mathrm{T}$ | $\mathrm{F}$ | $\mathrm{F}$ |
| $\mathrm{F}$ | $\mathrm{T}$ | $\mathrm{T}$ |
| $\mathrm{F}$ | $\mathrm{F}$ | $\mathrm{F}$ |

The true rows are:

$$
p=\mathrm{T}, q=\mathrm{T}
$$

and

$$
p=\mathrm{F}, q=\mathrm{T}
$$

So the DNF is:

$$
(p \land q) \lor (¬p \land q)
$$

This simplifies to:

$$
q
$$

To build CNF, look at the rows where the formula is false. For each false row, make a disjunction that rules out exactly that row. Then join those disjunctions with $\land$.

Using the same table, the false rows are:

$$
p=\mathrm{T}, q=\mathrm{F}
$$

and

$$
p=\mathrm{F}, q=\mathrm{F}
$$

The clauses that rule them out are:

$$
(¬p \lor q)
$$

and

$$
(p \lor q)
$$

So the CNF is:

$$
(¬p \lor q) \land (p \lor q)
$$

This also simplifies to:

$$
q
$$

### Why Normal Forms Matter

CNF is especially important in satisfiability solving. A SAT solver usually receives input as a CNF formula and tries to find a valuation that satisfies all clauses.

DNF is useful when you want to describe all ways a formula can become true. It appears naturally in case analysis, switching theory, and Boolean function representation.

Normal forms can grow large. A formula may have a short original representation but an exponentially larger CNF or DNF. This size growth matters in automated reasoning and complexity theory.
