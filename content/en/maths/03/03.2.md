## 3.2 Sequent Calculus

Sequent calculus is a proof system based on sequents. A sequent has the form:

$$
\Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta
$$

The left side $\Gamma$ contains assumptions. The right side $\Delta$ contains conclusions.

You can read it as:

$$
\text{from all formulas in } \Gamma,\text{ at least one formula in } \Delta \text{ follows}
$$

For example:

$$
p,\ p \to q \Rightarrow q
$$

means that from $p$ and $p \to q$, we may conclude $q$.

### Sequents

In classical sequent calculus, both sides may contain several formulas:

$$
A_1,\dots,A_m \Rightarrow B_1,\dots,B_n
$$

This means:

$$
(A_1 \land \cdots \land A_m) \to (B_1 \lor \cdots \lor B_n)
$$

So the left side behaves like conjunction, and the right side behaves like disjunction.

Examples:

$$
p,q \Rightarrow p
$$

means:

$$
(p \land q) \to p
$$

And:

$$
p \Rightarrow p,q
$$

means:

$$
p \to (p \lor q)
$$

### Identity Rule

The identity rule says that every formula implies itself:

$$
A \Rightarrow A
$$

More generally, if the same formula appears on both sides, the sequent is immediately derivable:

$$
\Gamma, A \Rightarrow A, \Delta
$$

This is the starting point for many sequent proofs.

Example:

$$
p \Rightarrow p
$$

### Structural Rules

Structural rules control how assumptions and conclusions may be arranged.

**Weakening** allows extra formulas to be added.

Left weakening:

$$
\frac{\Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta}{\Gamma, A \Rightarrow \Delta}
$$

Right weakening:

$$
\frac{\Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta}{\Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta, A}
$$

This means extra assumptions or extra possible conclusions do not destroy a proof.

**Contraction** removes duplicates.

Left contraction:

$$
\frac{\Gamma, A, A \Rightarrow \Delta}{\Gamma, A \Rightarrow \Delta}
$$

Right contraction:

$$
\frac{\Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta, A, A}{\Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta, A}
$$

**Exchange** changes order.

Left exchange:

$$
\frac{\Gamma, A, B, \Pi \Rightarrow \Delta}{\Gamma, B, A, \Pi \Rightarrow \Delta}
$$

Right exchange:

$$
\frac{\Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta, A, B, \Lambda}{\Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta, B, A, \Lambda}
$$

These rules show that contexts behave like finite collections of formulas.

### Left and Right Rules

Each connective has rules for introducing it on the left side and on the right side.

A right rule explains how to prove a formula with that connective.

A left rule explains how to use a formula with that connective as an assumption.

This symmetry is one of the main strengths of sequent calculus.

### Conjunction Rules

To prove $A \land B$ on the right, prove both $A$ and $B$:

$$
\frac{\Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta, A \qquad \Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta, B}
{\Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta, A \land B}
$$

To use $A \land B$ on the left, use both parts as assumptions:

$$
\frac{\Gamma, A, B \Rightarrow \Delta}
{\Gamma, A \land B \Rightarrow \Delta}
$$

Example:

$$
p \land q \Rightarrow p
$$

Proof:

$$
\frac{
p,q \Rightarrow p
}{
p \land q \Rightarrow p
}
$$

The premise $p,q \Rightarrow p$ is immediate by identity, since $p$ appears on both sides.

### Disjunction Rules

To prove $A \lor B$ on the right, it is enough to prove one side.

One common presentation uses two rules:

$$
\frac{\Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta, A}
{\Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta, A \lor B}
$$

$$
\frac{\Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta, B}
{\Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta, A \lor B}
$$

To use $A \lor B$ on the left, prove the result from both cases:

$$
\frac{\Gamma, A \Rightarrow \Delta \qquad \Gamma, B \Rightarrow \Delta}
{\Gamma, A \lor B \Rightarrow \Delta}
$$

This matches proof by cases.

Example:

$$
p \Rightarrow p \lor q
$$

Proof:

$$
\frac{
p \Rightarrow p
}{
p \Rightarrow p \lor q
}
$$

### Implication Rules

To prove $A \to B$ on the right, move $A$ to the left and prove $B$:

$$
\frac{\Gamma, A \Rightarrow \Delta, B}
{\Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta, A \to B}
$$

This mirrors conditional proof.

To use $A \to B$ on the left, there are two obligations:

$$
\frac{
\Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta, A
\qquad
\Gamma, B \Rightarrow \Delta
}{
\Gamma, A \to B \Rightarrow \Delta
}
$$

The first premise shows that the antecedent $A$ is available. The second shows that if $B$ is available, the desired conclusion follows.

Example:

$$
p,\ p \to q \Rightarrow q
$$

Proof sketch:

First, $p \Rightarrow p$ by identity.

Second, $q \Rightarrow q$ by identity.

Then apply the left rule for implication to $p \to q$:

$$
\frac{
p \Rightarrow p
\qquad
p,q \Rightarrow q
}{
p,\ p \to q \Rightarrow q
}
$$

### Negation Rules

Negation can be treated as primitive or defined by implication into falsity:

$$
¬A \equiv A \to \bot
$$

When treated as primitive, the rules are:

Negation on the right:

$$
\frac{\Gamma, A \Rightarrow \Delta}
{\Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta, ¬A}
$$

Negation on the left:

$$
\frac{\Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta, A}
{\Gamma, ¬A \Rightarrow \Delta}
$$

The idea is simple. To prove $¬A$, assume $A$ on the left and derive contradiction. To use $¬A$, it can refute $A$.

### Quantifier Rules

For first-order logic, sequent calculus includes rules for quantifiers.

Universal right:

$$
\frac{\Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta, A(y)}
{\Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta, \forall x,A(x)}
$$

Here $y$ must be fresh. It must not occur in the assumptions in a way that gives it special information.

Universal left:

$$
\frac{\Gamma, A(t) \Rightarrow \Delta}
{\Gamma, \forall x,A(x) \Rightarrow \Delta}
$$

If $\forall x,A(x)$ is available, we may use it for any term $t$.

Existential right:

$$
\frac{\Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta, A(t)}
{\Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta, \exists x,A(x)}
$$

If $A(t)$ holds for some term $t$, then $\exists x,A(x)$ follows.

Existential left:

$$
\frac{\Gamma, A(y) \Rightarrow \Delta}
{\Gamma, \exists x,A(x) \Rightarrow \Delta}
$$

Here $y$ must be fresh. We reason from an arbitrary witness.

### Cut Rule

The cut rule allows an intermediate formula to be introduced and then used:

$$
\frac{
\Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta, A
\qquad
\Pi, A \Rightarrow \Lambda
}{
\Gamma, \Pi \Rightarrow \Delta, \Lambda
}
$$

The formula $A$ is called the cut formula.

You can think of cut as using a lemma. First prove $A$. Then use $A$ to prove something else. After that, the proof can be compressed into one derivation.

For example, if we have:

$$
\Gamma \Rightarrow A
$$

and:

$$
A \Rightarrow B
$$

then cut gives:

$$
\Gamma \Rightarrow B
$$

Cut is useful, but it can hide proof structure.

### Cut-Free Proofs

A proof is **cut-free** if it does not use the cut rule.

Cut-free proofs are important because they are more direct. Every formula appearing in the proof is closely related to formulas in the final sequent. This is called the subformula property.

The cut-elimination theorem says that, for standard sequent calculi, any proof using cut can be transformed into a proof without cut.

This theorem is one of the reasons sequent calculus is central in proof theory. It gives a precise way to analyze proof structure.
