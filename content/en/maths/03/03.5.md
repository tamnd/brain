## 3.5 Cut-Elimination

Cut-elimination is a central theorem about sequent calculus. It says that proofs using the cut rule can be transformed into proofs that do not use the cut rule.

The cut rule has the form:

$$
\frac{
\Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta, A
\qquad
\Pi, A \Rightarrow \Lambda
}{
\Gamma, \Pi \Rightarrow \Delta, \Lambda
}
$$

The formula $A$ is called the **cut formula**.

You can read the rule like this:

First prove $A$ from $\Gamma$.

Then use $A$, together with $\Pi$, to prove something in $\Lambda$.

Therefore, remove the intermediate formula $A$ and conclude directly from $\Gamma$ and $\Pi$.

### Cut as Lemma Use

Cut corresponds to the ordinary use of lemmas.

Suppose you want to prove $B$ from assumptions $\Gamma$.

You may first prove an intermediate result:

$$
\Gamma \Rightarrow A
$$

Then prove:

$$
A \Rightarrow B
$$

Using cut, you get:

$$
\Gamma \Rightarrow B
$$

This is exactly how much of mathematics works. We prove a useful lemma, then use it in a later argument.

So cut is not “bad” as a reasoning principle. It is natural and useful. The issue is that cut can hide the direct structure of a proof.

### Cut-Free Proofs

A proof is **cut-free** if it does not use the cut rule.

Cut-free proofs are more explicit. They do not rely on hidden intermediate formulas. Every step works directly with formulas related to the final sequent.

The cut-elimination theorem says:

$$
\text{if } \Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta \text{ has a proof, then it has a cut-free proof}
$$

More formally, for standard sequent calculi:

$$
\vdash \Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
\vdash_{\mathrm{cut\text{-}free}} \Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta
$$

This means cut is **admissible**. Adding cut may make proofs shorter and easier to write, but it does not make new sequents provable.

### A Simple Example

Suppose we have:

$$
p \Rightarrow p
$$

and:

$$
p \Rightarrow p \lor q
$$

Using cut with cut formula $p$:

$$
\frac{
p \Rightarrow p
\qquad
p \Rightarrow p \lor q
}{
p \Rightarrow p \lor q
}
$$

The result is already the second premise. The cut did no real work.

A cut-free proof is simply:

$$
\frac{
p \Rightarrow p
}{
p \Rightarrow p \lor q
}
$$

using the right rule for $\lor$.

This is a small example, but it shows the pattern. A proof may pass through an intermediate formula that can be removed.

### Principal Cuts

The interesting cases occur when the cut formula was introduced immediately before the cut in both premises.

For conjunction, suppose the cut formula is $A \land B$.

On the left premise, $A \land B$ may have been introduced on the right:

$$
\frac{
\Gamma \Rightarrow A
\qquad
\Gamma \Rightarrow B
}{
\Gamma \Rightarrow A \land B
}
$$

On the right premise, $A \land B$ may be used on the left:

$$
\frac{
\Pi, A, B \Rightarrow \Delta
}{
\Pi, A \land B \Rightarrow \Delta
}
$$

Then the cut:

$$
\frac{
\Gamma \Rightarrow A \land B
\qquad
\Pi, A \land B \Rightarrow \Delta
}{
\Gamma, \Pi \Rightarrow \Delta
}
$$

can be replaced by smaller cuts on $A$ and $B$.

Intuitively, instead of proving $A \land B$ and then breaking it apart, prove $A$ and $B$ directly and use them where needed.

The important measure decreases: the cut formula becomes simpler.

### Reduction Strategy

The proof of cut-elimination proceeds by repeatedly transforming cuts until none remain.

The usual strategy is:

1. Find an uppermost or highest-priority cut.
2. If the cut formula was not principal in one premise, move the cut upward past the last inference.
3. If the cut formula was principal in both premises, replace the cut by cuts on smaller formulas.
4. Repeat until all cuts disappear.

The process is controlled by a measure, usually involving:

$$
\text{complexity of the cut formula}
$$

and

$$
\text{height of the derivations above the cut}
$$

Each transformation decreases this measure in a well-founded ordering. That guarantees termination.

### Subformula Property

The major consequence of cut-elimination is the **subformula property**.

A cut-free proof of:

$$
\Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta
$$

contains only formulas that are subformulas of formulas already appearing in $\Gamma$ or $\Delta$.

This is powerful.

It means a cut-free proof cannot introduce arbitrary hidden lemmas. All formulas in the proof are visible inside the original problem.

For example, if the final sequent is:

$$
p \land q \Rightarrow q \land p
$$

then a cut-free proof only needs formulas built from:

$$
p, q, p \land q, q \land p
$$

It should not need an unrelated formula such as:

$$
r \to s
$$

### Consistency

Cut-elimination gives a direct consistency proof for many logics.

Let $\bot$ denote contradiction. If the system proved contradiction from no assumptions, we would have:

$$
\Rightarrow \bot
$$

By cut-elimination, there would be a cut-free proof of:

$$
\Rightarrow \bot
$$

But in many sequent systems, no cut-free proof of this sequent exists. There is no rule that can create $\bot$ on the right from nothing.

Therefore, the system is consistent.

This argument is structural. It does not rely on truth tables or models. It uses the shape of proofs themselves.

### Decidability

For propositional logic, cut-elimination also supports decidability.

Because cut-free proofs have the subformula property, proof search can be restricted to finitely many formulas. Given a sequent:

$$
\Gamma \Rightarrow \Delta
$$

we only need to consider subformulas of $\Gamma$ and $\Delta$.

This gives a finite search space for propositional logic. It is one reason sequent calculus can be used as a proof-search procedure.

In first-order logic, quantifiers complicate this picture. Proof search may fail to terminate because terms can grow without bound. Still, cut-elimination remains a central structural theorem.

### Interpolation

Cut-elimination also helps prove interpolation theorems.

A typical interpolation theorem says: if

$$
A \to B
$$

is valid, then there is an intermediate formula $C$ such that:

$$
A \to C
$$

and:

$$
C \to B
$$

are valid, and $C$ uses only the nonlogical symbols common to both $A$ and $B$.

The formula $C$ is called an interpolant.

Cut-free proofs expose the subformula structure needed to construct such intermediate formulas.

### Why Cut Is Still Useful

Even though cut can be eliminated, it remains useful in actual proof writing.

Without cut, proofs may become much longer. A lemma can compress an argument. Removing all lemmas may expand the proof dramatically.

So there are two perspectives:

| Perspective             | View of cut       |
| ----------------------- | ----------------- |
| Practical proof writing | useful lemma rule |
| Structural proof theory | eliminable rule   |
| Proof search            | often avoided     |
| Proof compression       | often essential   |

Cut-elimination says cut is not needed for provability. It does not say cut is not useful.

### Gentzen’s Insight

Gentzen introduced sequent calculus partly to prove cut-elimination. The theorem gave a new way to study logic through the structure of formal derivations.

The result showed that proofs are not just certificates of truth. They are mathematical objects with internal transformations, normal forms, and measurable structure.

Cut-elimination became a foundation for later work in proof theory, constructive logic, type theory, automated reasoning, and programming language semantics.
