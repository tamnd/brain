We define the formal language of propositional logic by specifying an alphabet of symbols together with rules that determine which expressions are well formed formulas, and this definition is inductive so that every formula is constructed from simpler formulas in a finite number of steps.

### Alphabet

The alphabet consists of propositional variables, logical connectives, and parentheses, and each category of symbols has a precise role in the construction of formulas.

A countable collection of propositional variables is fixed:
$$
p, q, r, p_1, p_2, \dots
$$

Each variable is an atomic symbol that represents a basic statement, but at the syntactic level it is treated only as a symbol without any assigned meaning or truth value.

The logical connectives are:

| Symbol | Name | Arity |
|---|---|---|
| $¬A$ | Negation | unary |
| $A \land B$ | Conjunction | binary |
| $A \lor B$ | Disjunction | binary |
| $A \to B$ | Implication | binary |
| $A \leftrightarrow B$ | Biconditional | binary |

Each connective has a fixed arity, and this determines how many formulas it combines and how larger expressions are formed from smaller ones.

Parentheses:
$$
( \quad )
$$
are included in the alphabet in order to specify grouping explicitly and to ensure that every expression has a well defined syntactic structure.

### Definition 1.1 (Formulas)

The set of formulas is defined inductively as follows.

Every propositional variable is a formula.

If $A$ is a formula, then:
$$
¬A
$$
is a formula.

If $A$ and $B$ are formulas, then:
$$
(A \land B), \quad (A \lor B), \quad (A \to B), \quad (A \leftrightarrow B)
$$
are formulas.

No other expressions are formulas.

This definition specifies the smallest set of expressions that contains all variables and is closed under the application of the logical connectives, so that every formula is obtained by finitely many applications of these rules.

### Example 1.2

The following expressions are formulas:
$$
p
$$
$$
¬p
$$
$$
(p \land q)
$$
$$
(p \lor (¬q))
$$
$$
((p \land q) \to r)
$$
$$
(¬(p \lor q) \leftrightarrow r)
$$

The following expressions are not formulas:
$$
\land p q
$$
$$
(p \land)
$$
$$
p q \to
$$
$$
((p \lor q)
$$

Each invalid expression fails to satisfy the inductive construction rules, either because it uses a connective without the required number of arguments or because it is not properly formed with respect to parentheses.

### Lemma 1.3 (Unique Readability)

Every formula admits a unique decomposition according to the formation rules.

More precisely, every formula is exactly one of the following:

a variable,

of the form $¬A$ for a unique formula $A$,

of the form $(A \circ B)$ for unique formulas $A$ and $B$ and a unique connective $\circ$.

### Proof

The proof proceeds by induction on the construction of formulas.

If the formula is a variable, then the statement is immediate.

If the formula is obtained by applying negation, then its form is uniquely determined as $¬A$, and the uniqueness of $A$ follows from the inductive hypothesis.

If the formula is obtained by applying a binary connective, then its form is uniquely determined as $(A \circ B)$, and the uniqueness of $A$ and $B$ follows from the inductive hypothesis.

### Convention 1.4 (Precedence and Associativity)

Parentheses may be omitted according to the precedence order:
$$
¬ \;>\; \land \;>\; \lor \;>\; \to \;>\; \leftrightarrow
$$

This means that negation binds most strongly, followed by conjunction, then disjunction, then implication, and finally biconditional.

Thus:
$$
¬p \land q \to r
$$
is interpreted as:
$$
((¬p \land q) \to r)
$$

Binary connectives are taken to associate to the right unless parentheses specify otherwise, so:
$$
p \to q \to r
$$
means:
$$
p \to (q \to r)
$$

### Definition 1.5 (Subformula)

The set of subformulas of a formula is defined inductively.

If $A$ is a variable, then its only subformula is $A$.

If $A = ¬B$, then the subformulas of $A$ are $A$ together with all subformulas of $B$.

If $A = (B \circ C)$, then the subformulas of $A$ are $A$ together with all subformulas of $B$ and all subformulas of $C$.

### Definition 1.6 (Length)

The length of a formula is the number of symbols that occur in it.

For example:
$$
(p \land q)
$$
has length $5$ when each symbol is counted individually.

### Definition 1.7 (Depth)

The depth of a formula is defined inductively.

If $A$ is a variable, then:
$$
\mathrm{depth}(A) = 0
$$

If $A = ¬B$, then:
$$
\mathrm{depth}(A) = \mathrm{depth}(B) + 1
$$

If $A = (B \circ C)$, then:
$$
\mathrm{depth}(A) = \max(\mathrm{depth}(B), \mathrm{depth}(C)) + 1
$$

### Lemma 1.8 (Tree Representation)

Every formula can be represented as a finite rooted tree whose leaves are variables and whose internal nodes are labeled by connectives.

### Proof

The representation is defined inductively.

A variable corresponds to a single node.

If $A = ¬B$, then the tree for $A$ is obtained by adding a new root labeled $¬$ with the tree of $B$ as its child.

If $A = (B \circ C)$, then the tree for $A$ is obtained by adding a new root labeled $\circ$ with the trees of $B$ and $C$ as its children.

### Lemma 1.9 (Structural Induction)

Let $P(A)$ be a property of formulas. If the following conditions hold:

$P(p)$ holds for every variable $p$,

$P(A)$ implies $P(¬A)$,

$P(A)$ and $P(B)$ together imply $P(A \circ B)$ for each connective $\circ$,

then $P(A)$ holds for all formulas $A$.

### Proof

The proof follows directly from the inductive definition of formulas, since every formula is constructed from variables by finitely many applications of the formation rules, and the property $P$ is preserved at each step.
