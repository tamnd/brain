1.1 Syntax: Variables, Connectives

We define the formal language of propositional logic. The goal is to describe exactly which expressions count as well-formed formulas. This is done by specifying an alphabet of symbols and rules for building formulas from those symbols. The definition is inductive, so every formula is constructed from simpler formulas in a finite number of steps.

Alphabet

The alphabet consists of propositional variables, logical connectives, and parentheses.

A countable set of propositional variables is given:
$$
p, q, r, p_1, p_2, \dots
$$

These variables are atomic symbols. They stand for basic statements, but at the level of syntax they are treated only as symbols with no internal structure.

The logical connectives are:

Symbol	Name	Arity
$¬A$	Negation	unary
$A \land B$	Conjunction	binary
$A \lor B$	Disjunction	binary
$A \to B$	Implication	binary
$A \leftrightarrow B$	Biconditional	binary

Each connective has a fixed arity. The symbol $¬$ takes one formula as input, while the others take two.

Parentheses:
$$
( \quad )
$$
are used to indicate how expressions are grouped. They are part of the formal language.

Formulas

The set of formulas is defined inductively.

Every propositional variable is a formula.

If $A$ is a formula, then:
$$
¬A
$$
is a formula.

If $A$ and $B$ are formulas, then:
$$
(A \land B), \quad (A \lor B), \quad (A \to B), \quad (A \leftrightarrow B)
$$
are formulas.

No other expressions are formulas.

This definition specifies the smallest set of expressions closed under these rules. Every formula must be obtained by finitely many applications of these clauses. This construction ensures that formulas have a well-defined structure.

Structure of Formulas

The inductive definition shows that every formula has a recursive structure. Each formula is either:

* a variable,
* a negation applied to a formula,
* or a binary combination of two formulas.

This structure can be viewed as a datatype:
$$
\text{Formula} ::= \text{Var} \mid \text{Not Formula} \mid \text{And Formula Formula} \mid \cdots
$$

This viewpoint is important because it allows definitions and proofs by recursion. It also corresponds directly to how formulas are represented in a proof assistant.

Examples

The following are formulas:
$$
p
$$
$$
¬p
$$
$$
(p \land q)
$$
$$
(p \lor (¬q))
$$
$$
((p \land q) \to r)
$$
$$
(¬(p \lor q) \leftrightarrow r)
$$

Each of these can be constructed step by step using the formation rules.

The following are not formulas:
$$
\land p q
$$
$$
(p \land)
$$
$$
p q \to
$$
$$
((p \lor q)
$$

These expressions fail because they cannot be obtained from the rules. In each case, some required part of the construction is missing.

Unique Readability

Each formula admits a unique decomposition according to the formation rules. More precisely, every formula is exactly one of the following:

* a variable,
* of the form $¬A$ for a unique formula $A$,
* of the form $(A \circ B)$ for unique formulas $A$, $B$ and a unique connective $\circ$.

This property ensures that parsing a formula yields a unique structure. It prevents ambiguity and allows recursive definitions to work correctly.

Parentheses and Precedence

Parentheses determine how formulas are grouped. Without parentheses, an expression may have more than one possible interpretation.

For example:
$$
p \land q \lor r
$$
can be read as:
$$
(p \land q) \lor r
$$
or as:
$$
p \land (q \lor r)
$$

To simplify notation, parentheses may be omitted according to a fixed precedence order:
$$
¬ ;>; \land ;>; \lor ;>; \to ;>; \leftrightarrow
$$

Thus:
$$
¬p \land q \to r
$$
is interpreted as:
$$
((¬p \land q) \to r)
$$

Binary connectives are taken to associate to the right unless parentheses indicate otherwise. For example:
$$
p \to q \to r
$$
means:
$$
p \to (q \to r)
$$

Subformulas

The subformulas of a formula are defined recursively.

A variable has itself as its only subformula.

If a formula is of the form $¬A$, then its subformulas consist of the formula itself together with all subformulas of $A$.

If a formula is of the form $(A \circ B)$, then its subformulas consist of the formula itself together with all subformulas of $A$ and all subformulas of $B$.

This definition reflects the way formulas are built and is used in recursive arguments.

Length and Depth

The length of a formula is the number of symbols it contains. For example:
$$
(p \land q)
$$
has length $5$ if each symbol is counted individually.

The depth of a formula measures how deeply connectives are nested. A variable has depth $0$. The depth increases by one each time a connective is applied. For a binary formula, the depth is one more than the maximum depth of its components.

These measures provide simple ways to describe the complexity of formulas and are useful in inductive proofs.

Tree Representation

Each formula can be represented as a finite rooted tree.

Leaves correspond to variables. A node labeled by $¬$ has one child. A node labeled by a binary connective has two children.

For example:
$$
((p \land q) \to r)
$$
corresponds to a tree with root $\to$, left subtree $(p \land q)$, and right subtree $r$.

This tree representation makes the recursive structure explicit and is directly suitable for implementation.

Structural Induction

The inductive definition of formulas yields a principle of structural induction.

To prove that a property $P(A)$ holds for all formulas $A$, it suffices to show:

* $P(p)$ holds for every variable $p$,
* if $P(A)$ holds, then $P(¬A)$ holds,
* if $P(A)$ and $P(B)$ hold, then $P(A \circ B)$ holds for each connective $\circ$.

This principle follows from the way formulas are constructed and will be used in later sections.
