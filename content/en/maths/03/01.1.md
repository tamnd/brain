1.1 Syntax: Variables, Connectives

We define the formal language of propositional logic. The aim is to specify exactly which expressions are well-formed formulas. This definition is inductive and determines the structure of all formulas.

1.1.1 Alphabet

The alphabet consists of the following symbols.

1. A countable set of propositional variables:
    $$
    p, q, r, p_1, p_2, \dots
    $$

Each variable represents a basic statement. At this stage, no truth value or meaning is assigned.

2. Logical connectives:

Symbol	Name	Arity
$¬A$	Negation	unary
$A \land B$	Conjunction	binary
$A \lor B$	Disjunction	binary
$A \to B$	Implication	binary
$A \leftrightarrow B$	Biconditional	binary

3. Parentheses:
    $$
    ( \quad )
    $$

Parentheses determine the grouping of expressions.

1.1.2 Definition (Formulas)

The set of formulas is defined inductively.

1. Every propositional variable is a formula.
2. If $A$ is a formula, then:
    $$
    ¬A
    $$
    is a formula.
3. If $A$ and $B$ are formulas, then:
    $$
    (A \land B), \quad (A \lor B), \quad (A \to B), \quad (A \leftrightarrow B)
    $$
    are formulas.
4. No other expressions are formulas.

This defines the smallest set of expressions closed under these rules. Every formula is obtained by finitely many applications of these clauses.

1.1.3 Inductive Structure

The definition of formulas gives a recursive structure.

Each formula is constructed using one of the following forms:

* a variable
* $¬A$ for some formula $A$
* $(A \circ B)$ for formulas $A$, $B$ and a connective $\circ$

This can be viewed as a datatype:
$$
\text{Formula} ::= \text{Var} \mid \text{Not Formula} \mid \text{And Formula Formula} \mid \cdots
$$

This perspective will be used when defining functions and proofs by recursion, and when implementing the language in a proof assistant.

1.1.4 Examples

The following are formulas:
$$
p
$$
$$
¬p
$$
$$
(p \land q)
$$
$$
(p \lor (¬q))
$$
$$
((p \land q) \to r)
$$
$$
(¬(p \lor q) \leftrightarrow r)
$$

The following are not formulas:
$$
\land p q
$$
$$
(p \land)
$$
$$
p q \to
$$
$$
((p \lor q)
$$

Each invalid expression fails to match the inductive rules.

1.1.5 Unique Readability

Every formula has a unique decomposition.

Each formula is exactly one of the following:

* a variable
* of the form $¬A$ for a unique formula $A$
* of the form $(A \circ B)$ for unique formulas $A$, $B$ and a unique connective $\circ$

This ensures that parsing a formula produces a unique structure.

1.1.6 Convention (Precedence and Associativity)

Parentheses may be omitted according to the precedence order:
$$
¬ ;>; \land ;>; \lor ;>; \to ;>; \leftrightarrow
$$

Thus:
$$
¬p \land q \to r
$$
means:
$$
((¬p \land q) \to r)
$$

Binary connectives are taken to associate to the right unless parentheses indicate otherwise. For example:
$$
p \to q \to r
$$
means:
$$
p \to (q \to r)
$$

1.1.7 Definition (Subformula)

The set of subformulas of a formula is defined inductively.

1. If $A$ is a variable, then its only subformula is $A$.
2. If $A = ¬B$, then the subformulas of $A$ are $A$ together with all subformulas of $B$.
3. If $A = (B \circ C)$, then the subformulas of $A$ are $A$ together with all subformulas of $B$ and all subformulas of $C$.

1.1.8 Definition (Length)

The length of a formula is the number of symbols it contains.

For example:
$$
(p \land q)
$$
has length $5$, counting:
$$
(, p, \land, q, )
$$

1.1.9 Definition (Depth)

The depth of a formula is defined inductively.

1. If $A$ is a variable, then:
    $$
    \mathrm{depth}(A) = 0
    $$
2. If $A = ¬B$, then:
    $$
    \mathrm{depth}(A) = \mathrm{depth}(B) + 1
    $$
3. If $A = (B \circ C)$, then:
    $$
    \mathrm{depth}(A) = \max(\mathrm{depth}(B), \mathrm{depth}(C)) + 1
    $$

1.1.10 Tree Representation

Each formula can be represented as a finite rooted tree.

* Leaves correspond to variables.
* A node labeled $¬$ has one child.
* A node labeled with a binary connective has two children.

For example:
$$
((p \land q) \to r)
$$
corresponds to a tree with root $\to$, left subtree $(p \land q)$, and right subtree $r$.

1.1.11 Structural Induction

Let $P(A)$ be a property of formulas. To prove that $P(A)$ holds for all formulas $A$, it suffices to show:

1. $P(p)$ holds for every variable $p$.
2. If $P(A)$ holds, then $P(¬A)$ holds.
3. If $P(A)$ and $P(B)$ hold, then $P(A \circ B)$ holds for each connective $\circ$.

This principle follows from the inductive definition and will be used in later arguments.
