## 1.5 Soundness and Completeness

Propositional logic has two sides.

The semantic side asks whether a formula is true under valuations.

The syntactic side asks whether a formula can be proved using formal rules.

Soundness and completeness explain how these two sides fit together.

### Semantic Validity

A formula $A$ is **valid** if it is true under every valuation.

We write:

$$
\models A
$$

This means:

$$
\text{for every valuation } v,\quad v(A)=\mathrm{T}
$$

Example:

$$
p \lor ¬p
$$

is valid because it is true whether $p$ is true or false.

Another example:

$$
(p \land q) \to p
$$

is valid because whenever $p \land q$ is true, $p$ must already be true.

Validity is a semantic notion. It depends on truth values and valuations.

### Syntactic Provability

A formula $A$ is **provable** if it can be derived from formal rules.

We write:

$$
\vdash A
$$

This means that there is a formal proof of $A$.

A proof is a finite sequence of formulas where each line is either an axiom or follows from earlier lines by an inference rule.

For example, a proof system may include a rule like modus ponens:

$$
\frac{A \qquad A \to B}{B}
$$

This says: if we have proved $A$, and we have proved $A \to B$, then we may infer $B$.

Provability is a syntactic notion. It depends only on symbols and rules.

### Soundness

A proof system is **sound** if every provable formula is valid.

In symbols:

$$
\vdash A \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \models A
$$

This says that formal proof never leads from correct rules to a false universal claim.

Soundness is the safety property of a proof system. If the system proves $A$, then $A$ is true under every valuation.

The basic reason soundness holds is local: each axiom is valid, and each inference rule preserves validity.

For modus ponens, the preservation step is easy to check. Suppose:

$$
v(A)=\mathrm{T}
$$

and

$$
v(A \to B)=\mathrm{T}
$$

Then $v(B)$ must be $\mathrm{T}$. If $B$ were false, $A \to B$ would be false. So modus ponens preserves truth.

### Completeness

A proof system is **complete** if every valid formula is provable.

In symbols:

$$
\models A \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \vdash A
$$

This says that the proof rules are strong enough to derive every formula that is true under all valuations.

Completeness is the reachability property of a proof system. It tells us that semantic truth can be captured by symbolic derivation.

For propositional logic, completeness means that truth-table validity and formal provability agree.

So if a formula is true in every row of its truth table, then there is a formal proof of it.

### Soundness and Completeness Together

When a proof system is both sound and complete, we have:

$$
\vdash A \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \models A
$$

This is a precise match between proof and truth.

The left side is syntactic:

$$
\vdash A
$$

It says $A$ can be derived by rules.

The right side is semantic:

$$
\models A
$$

It says $A$ is true under every valuation.

The theorem says these two ideas define the same class of formulas.

### Consequence from Premises

Often we reason from assumptions.

Semantic consequence is written:

$$
\Gamma \models A
$$

This means: every valuation that makes all formulas in $\Gamma$ true also makes $A$ true.

Syntactic consequence is written:

$$
\Gamma \vdash A
$$

This means: there is a formal derivation of $A$ using formulas from $\Gamma$ as assumptions.

Soundness from premises says:

$$
\Gamma \vdash A \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \Gamma \models A
$$

Completeness from premises says:

$$
\Gamma \models A \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \Gamma \vdash A
$$

Together:

$$
\Gamma \vdash A \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \Gamma \models A
$$

### Example

Let:

$$
\Gamma = {p \to q,\ p}
$$

We want to show:

$$
\Gamma \models q
$$

Take any valuation $v$ such that:

$$
v(p \to q)=\mathrm{T}
$$

and

$$
v(p)=\mathrm{T}
$$

Since $p$ is true and $p \to q$ is true, $q$ must be true. Therefore:

$$
{p \to q,\ p} \models q
$$

On the proof side, we use modus ponens:

$$
p \to q
$$

$$
p
$$

$$
q
$$

So:

$$
{p \to q,\ p} \vdash q
$$

The semantic argument and the proof-theoretic derivation agree.

### Why These Theorems Matter

Soundness gives trust. A proof system does not prove formulas that fail semantically.

Completeness gives coverage. A proof system can prove every formula that is semantically forced.

Together, they make formal logic usable as a foundation. We can reason syntactically, using rules and derivations, while knowing that this matches truth-table semantics.
