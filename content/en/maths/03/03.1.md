## 3.1 Natural Deduction

Natural deduction is a proof system built around the ordinary use of logical connectives. Each connective has rules for introducing it and rules for eliminating it.

An **introduction rule** tells you how to prove a formula with a certain connective.

An **elimination rule** tells you how to use a formula with that connective.

For example, to prove $A \land B$, you should prove $A$ and prove $B$. To use $A \land B$, you may extract $A$ or extract $B$.

### Judgments

The basic judgment has the form:

$$
\Gamma \vdash A
$$

This means that $A$ is derivable from assumptions $\Gamma$.

Here:

| Symbol   | Meaning      |
| -------- | ------------ |
| $\Gamma$ | assumptions  |
| $\vdash$ | is derivable |
| $A$      | conclusion   |

For example:

$$
p, p \to q \vdash q
$$

means that $q$ can be derived from the assumptions $p$ and $p \to q$.

### Assumptions

A proof may begin by using an assumption.

$$
\frac{}{A}
\quad
A \in \Gamma
$$

This rule says that if $A$ is one of the assumptions, then $A$ may be used in the proof.

Example:

$$
p \vdash p
$$

because $p$ is available as an assumption.

### Conjunction Rules

Conjunction has one introduction rule and two elimination rules.

To introduce conjunction:

$$
\frac{A \qquad B}{A \land B}
$$

If you have proved both $A$ and $B$, you may conclude $A \land B$.

To eliminate conjunction:

$$
\frac{A \land B}{A}
\qquad
\frac{A \land B}{B}
$$

If you have $A \land B$, you may use either component.

Example:

$$
p \land q \vdash p
$$

Proof:

$$
\frac{p \land q}{p}
$$

Example:

$$
p, q \vdash p \land q
$$

Proof:

$$
\frac{p \qquad q}{p \land q}
$$

### Implication Rules

Implication is handled by conditional reasoning.

To introduce implication, assume the antecedent temporarily. If under that assumption you can prove the consequent, then you may discharge the temporary assumption.

$$
\frac{
\begin{array}{c}
[A] \
\vdots \
B
\end{array}
}{A \to B}
$$

The brackets $[A]$ indicate that the assumption $A$ is temporary and later discharged.

This matches ordinary mathematical reasoning: “Assume $A$. Then prove $B$. Therefore, if $A$, then $B$.”

To eliminate implication, use modus ponens:

$$
\frac{A \qquad A \to B}{B}
$$

If $A$ is true and $A \to B$ is available, then $B$ follows.

Example:

$$
p, p \to q \vdash q
$$

Proof:

$$
\frac{p \qquad p \to q}{q}
$$

### Example: Proving $p \to (q \to p)$

We want to prove:

$$
\vdash p \to (q \to p)
$$

Begin by assuming $p$.

Then, inside that assumption, assume $q$.

From the assumption $p$, we still have $p$.

So from $q$ we can derive $p$, and therefore:

$$
q \to p
$$

Now discharge the assumption $p$ and conclude:

$$
p \to (q \to p)
$$

As a proof tree:

$$
\frac{
\begin{array}{c}
[p] \
\frac{
\begin{array}{c}
[q] \
p
\end{array}
}{q \to p}
\end{array}
}{p \to (q \to p)}
$$

The important point is that $q$ was assumed only temporarily. It was used to prove $q \to p$, then discharged.

### Disjunction Rules

Disjunction has two introduction rules.

$$
\frac{A}{A \lor B}
\qquad
\frac{B}{A \lor B}
$$

If you have $A$, you may conclude $A \lor B$. If you have $B$, you may also conclude $A \lor B$.

This is because $A \lor B$ requires at least one side to hold.

Disjunction elimination is proof by cases:

$$
\frac{
A \lor B
\qquad
\begin{array}{c}
[A] \
\vdots \
C
\end{array}
\qquad
\begin{array}{c}
[B] \
\vdots \
C
\end{array}
}{C}
$$

If $A \lor B$ is true, and both cases lead to $C$, then $C$ follows.

Example:

$$
p \lor q,\ p \to r,\ q \to r \vdash r
$$

Reasoning:

If $p$ holds, then $r$ follows from $p \to r$.

If $q$ holds, then $r$ follows from $q \to r$.

Since $p \lor q$ holds, in either case $r$ follows.

### Negation Rules

Negation is often defined by contradiction.

A common definition is:

$$
¬A \equiv A \to \bot
$$

Here $\bot$ means falsity or contradiction.

So to prove $¬A$, assume $A$ and derive contradiction.

$$
\frac{
\begin{array}{c}
[A] \
\vdots \
\bot
\end{array}
}{¬A}
$$

To eliminate negation:

$$
\frac{A \qquad ¬A}{\bot}
$$

If both $A$ and $¬A$ are available, contradiction follows.

### Falsity Rule

From contradiction, anything follows.

$$
\frac{\bot}{A}
$$

This rule is sometimes called explosion.

It means that once a contradiction has been derived, the proof context has collapsed.

### Classical Rule

The rules above can be used in both classical and intuitionistic settings, depending on which extra principles are admitted.

Classical natural deduction often includes one of the following principles.

Law of excluded middle:

$$
A \lor ¬A
$$

Double-negation elimination:

$$
\frac{¬¬A}{A}
$$

Proof by contradiction:

$$
\frac{
\begin{array}{c}
[¬A] \
\vdots \
\bot
\end{array}
}{A}
$$

These are valid in classical logic, but they are not accepted as general principles in intuitionistic logic.

### Quantifier Rules

In first-order logic, natural deduction also has rules for quantifiers.

Universal introduction:

$$
\frac{A(x)}{\forall x,A(x)}
$$

This is allowed only when $x$ was arbitrary and does not depend on any special assumption.

Universal elimination:

$$
\frac{\forall x,A(x)}{A(t)}
$$

If $A$ holds for every $x$, then it holds for a particular term $t$.

Existential introduction:

$$
\frac{A(t)}{\exists x,A(x)}
$$

If $A$ holds for a specific term $t$, then there exists some $x$ such that $A(x)$.

Existential elimination:

$$
\frac{
\exists x,A(x)
\qquad
\begin{array}{c}
[A(y)] \
\vdots \
C
\end{array}
}{C}
$$

Here $y$ must be fresh. This means $y$ should not appear in assumptions on which $C$ depends. The idea is: if some object satisfies $A$, and an arbitrary witness $y$ satisfying $A(y)$ lets you prove $C$, then $C$ follows.

### Example with Quantifiers

Suppose we have:

$$
\forall x,(P(x) \to Q(x))
$$

and:

$$
P(a)
$$

We can derive:

$$
Q(a)
$$

Proof:

From universal elimination:

$$
\forall x,(P(x) \to Q(x)) \vdash P(a) \to Q(a)
$$

Then use implication elimination with $P(a)$:

$$
\frac{P(a) \qquad P(a) \to Q(a)}{Q(a)}
$$

So:

$$
\forall x,(P(x) \to Q(x)),\ P(a) \vdash Q(a)
$$
