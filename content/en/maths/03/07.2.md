## 7.2 Cardinality and Countability

Cardinality measures the size of a set.

For a finite set, cardinality is just the number of elements.

For example:

$$
|{a,b,c}| = 3
$$

and:

$$
|\varnothing| = 0
$$

For infinite sets, cardinality requires more care. We cannot count all elements one by one and finish. Instead, we compare sets using functions.

### Same Cardinality

Two sets $A$ and $B$ have the same cardinality if there is a bijection:

$$
f : A \to B
$$

A bijection pairs every element of $A$ with exactly one element of $B$, and every element of $B$ is used exactly once.

We write:

$$
|A| = |B|
$$

when such a bijection exists.

Example:

$$
A={1,2,3}, \qquad B={a,b,c}
$$

The function:

$$
f(1)=a,\qquad f(2)=b,\qquad f(3)=c
$$

is a bijection, so:

$$
|A|=|B|
$$

This definition works for finite and infinite sets.

### Countable Sets

A set $A$ is **countable** if its elements can be listed in a sequence:

$$
a_0, a_1, a_2, a_3, \dots
$$

More formally, $A$ is countable if it is finite or there is a surjection:

$$
f : \mathbb{N} \to A
$$

A set is **countably infinite** if there is a bijection:

$$
f : \mathbb{N} \to A
$$

In that case:

$$
|A| = |\mathbb{N}|
$$

The cardinality of the natural numbers is written:

$$
\aleph_0
$$

So if $A$ is countably infinite, then:

$$
|A|=\aleph_0
$$

### The Integers Are Countable

At first, the integers may look larger than the natural numbers because they extend in two directions:

$$
\mathbb{Z}={\dots,-3,-2,-1,0,1,2,3,\dots}
$$

But they are countable because we can list them:

$$
0,1,-1,2,-2,3,-3,\dots
$$

This gives a sequence containing every integer exactly once.

So:

$$
|\mathbb{Z}| = |\mathbb{N}|
$$

Even though $\mathbb{Z}$ seems larger, it has the same infinite cardinality as $\mathbb{N}$.

### The Even Numbers Are Countable

Let:

$$
E={0,2,4,6,\dots}
$$

The function:

$$
f:\mathbb{N}\to E
$$

defined by:

$$
f(n)=2n
$$

is a bijection.

Therefore:

$$
|E|=|\mathbb{N}|
$$

This is one of the first unusual features of infinite sets: a proper subset can have the same cardinality as the whole set.

### The Rational Numbers Are Countable

The rational numbers are:

$$
\mathbb{Q}=\left{\frac{a}{b}:a\in\mathbb{Z},\ b\in\mathbb{Z},\ b\neq 0\right}
$$

This set is also countable.

A common idea is to arrange positive fractions in a grid:

$$
\frac{1}{1},\frac{1}{2},\frac{1}{3},\dots
$$

$$
\frac{2}{1},\frac{2}{2},\frac{2}{3},\dots
$$

$$
\frac{3}{1},\frac{3}{2},\frac{3}{3},\dots
$$

and then move through the grid diagonally.

Some fractions repeat the same rational number:

$$
\frac{1}{2}=\frac{2}{4}=\frac{3}{6}
$$

So we skip duplicates. This still gives a list containing every positive rational number. Then we insert $0$ and the negative rationals.

Hence:

$$
|\mathbb{Q}|=\aleph_0
$$

The rational numbers are dense on the number line, but they are still countable.

### Uncountable Sets

A set is **uncountable** if it cannot be listed in a sequence indexed by natural numbers.

The real numbers are uncountable:

$$
|\mathbb{R}| > |\mathbb{N}|
$$

A standard proof uses Cantor's diagonal argument.

Consider the real numbers between $0$ and $1$:

$$
(0,1)
$$

Suppose, for contradiction, that they can be listed:

$$
r_1, r_2, r_3, \dots
$$

Write each number in decimal form:

$$
r_1 = 0.d_{11}d_{12}d_{13}\dots
$$

$$
r_2 = 0.d_{21}d_{22}d_{23}\dots
$$

$$
r_3 = 0.d_{31}d_{32}d_{33}\dots
$$

Now construct a new real number:

$$
s = 0.c_1c_2c_3\dots
$$

Choose each digit $c_n$ different from $d_{nn}$.

For example, choose:

$$
c_n =
\begin{cases}
1, & d_{nn}\neq 1,\
2, & d_{nn}=1.
\end{cases}
$$

Then $s$ differs from $r_1$ in the first digit, from $r_2$ in the second digit, from $r_3$ in the third digit, and so on.

Therefore, $s$ is not anywhere in the list.

This contradicts the assumption that the list contained every real number in $(0,1)$.

So:

$$
(0,1)
$$

is uncountable, and therefore:

$$
\mathbb{R}
$$

is uncountable.

### Comparing Cardinalities

For sets $A$ and $B$, we write:

$$
|A| \leq |B|
$$

if there is an injection:

$$
f:A\to B
$$

This means that $A$ can be placed inside $B$ without collisions.

We write:

$$
|A| < |B|
$$

if:

$$
|A| \leq |B|
$$

but:

$$
|A| \neq |B|
$$

For example:

$$
|\mathbb{N}| < |\mathbb{R}|
$$

### Power Sets Are Larger

For any set $A$, its power set has strictly larger cardinality:

$$
|A| < |\mathcal{P}(A)|
$$

This is Cantor's theorem.

For finite sets, this is easy to see. If:

$$
|A|=n
$$

then:

$$
|\mathcal{P}(A)|=2^n
$$

and:

$$
n < 2^n
$$

For infinite sets, the theorem remains true.

In particular:

$$
|\mathbb{N}| < |\mathcal{P}(\mathbb{N})|
$$

The cardinality of $\mathcal{P}(\mathbb{N})$ is the same as the cardinality of the real numbers:

$$
|\mathcal{P}(\mathbb{N})| = |\mathbb{R}|
$$

This cardinality is often called the cardinality of the continuum.

### Finite, Countable, Uncountable

The main hierarchy is:

$$
\text{finite} \quad < \quad \text{countably infinite} \quad < \quad \text{uncountable}
$$

Examples:

| Set                       | Cardinality type   |
| ------------------------- | ------------------ |
| $\varnothing$             | finite             |
| ${1,2,3}$                 | finite             |
| $\mathbb{N}$              | countably infinite |
| $\mathbb{Z}$              | countably infinite |
| $\mathbb{Q}$              | countably infinite |
| $\mathbb{R}$              | uncountable        |
| $\mathcal{P}(\mathbb{N})$ | uncountable        |
