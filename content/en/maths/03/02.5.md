## 2.5 Validity and Entailment

Satisfaction tells us whether a formula is true in a particular structure.

Validity asks a stronger question: is the formula true in every structure?

### Validity

A sentence $A$ is **valid** if it is true in every structure for its language.

We write:

$$
\models A
$$

This means:

$$
\mathcal M \models A
$$

for every structure $\mathcal M$.

Example:

$$
\forall x,(x=x)
$$

is valid because every object in every structure is equal to itself.

Another example:

$$
\forall x,P(x) \to \exists x,P(x)
$$

is valid when domains are assumed nonempty. If everything has property $P$, then at least one object has property $P$.

Validity depends only on logical form, not on a particular interpretation of the predicate symbols.

### Non-Validity

A sentence is not valid if there is at least one structure in which it is false.

For example:

$$
\forall x,P(x)
$$

is not valid.

To see this, take a structure with domain:

$$
M={1,2}
$$

and interpret $P$ as:

$$
P^{\mathcal M}={1}
$$

Then $P(2)$ is false, so:

$$
\mathcal M \not\models \forall x,P(x)
$$

This structure is a **countermodel** to the sentence.

A countermodel is often the cleanest way to show that a sentence does not follow from logic alone.

### Entailment

Entailment describes logical consequence from assumptions.

Let $\Gamma$ be a set of sentences, and let $A$ be a sentence.

We write:

$$
\Gamma \models A
$$

This means: every model of $\Gamma$ is also a model of $A$.

Formally:

$$
\text{for every structure } \mathcal M,\quad
\mathcal M \models \Gamma \implies \mathcal M \models A
$$

So entailment says that $A$ is forced by $\Gamma$.

If all assumptions in $\Gamma$ are true in a structure, then $A$ must also be true in that structure.

### Example: Order Properties

Let $\Gamma$ contain these two assumptions:

$$
\forall x,\forall y,(x<y \to ¬(y<x))
$$

$$
\forall x,\forall y,\forall z,((x<y \land y<z) \to x<z)
$$

The first says $<$ is asymmetric.

The second says $<$ is transitive.

From these assumptions, we can derive:

$$
\forall x,¬(x<x)
$$

That is, nothing is less than itself.

So:

$$
\Gamma \models \forall x,¬(x<x)
$$

Why? Suppose some object $a$ satisfied:

$$
a<a
$$

Then by asymmetry, from $a<a$ we get:

$$
¬(a<a)
$$

That is impossible. Therefore no such $a$ exists.

### Validity as Entailment from No Assumptions

Validity is a special case of entailment.

When there are no assumptions, we write:

$$
\models A
$$

This is the same as:

$$
\varnothing \models A
$$

So validity means that $A$ follows from the empty set of assumptions.

It is true by logic alone.

### Equivalence

Two sentences $A$ and $B$ are **logically equivalent** if each entails the other:

$$
A \models B
$$

and

$$
B \models A
$$

Equivalently:

$$
\models A \leftrightarrow B
$$

This means $A$ and $B$ have the same truth value in every structure.

Example:

$$
¬\forall x,P(x)
$$

is equivalent to:

$$
\exists x,¬P(x)
$$

So:

$$
\models
\left(
¬\forall x,P(x)
\leftrightarrow
\exists x,¬P(x)
\right)
$$

### Satisfiability

A set of sentences $\Gamma$ is **satisfiable** if it has at least one model.

That means there is some structure $\mathcal M$ such that:

$$
\mathcal M \models \Gamma
$$

If no such structure exists, then $\Gamma$ is **unsatisfiable**.

Example:

$$
\Gamma = {\exists x,P(x),\ \forall x,¬P(x)}
$$

is unsatisfiable.

The first sentence says some object has property $P$.

The second says no object has property $P$.

No structure can make both true.

### Entailment and Unsatisfiability

Entailment can be tested through unsatisfiability.

We have:

$$
\Gamma \models A
$$

exactly when:

$$
\Gamma \cup {¬A}
$$

is unsatisfiable.

This is an important idea. To show that $A$ follows from $\Gamma$, assume the opposite of $A$ and show that contradiction is unavoidable.

For example, to show:

$$
{p \to q,\ p} \models q
$$

we check whether:

$$
{p \to q,\ p,\ ¬q}
$$

is satisfiable.

It is not. If $p$ is true and $p\to q$ is true, then $q$ must be true. But $¬q$ says $q$ is false.

So the assumptions force $q$.

### Semantic Consequence with Free Variables

Entailment can also be stated for formulas with free variables.

For formulas $A_1,\dots,A_n$ and $B$, we write:

$$
A_1,\dots,A_n \models B
$$

if every structure and every variable assignment that satisfies all $A_i$ also satisfies $B$.

Example:

$$
x<y,\ y<z \models x<z
$$

is valid in structures where $<$ is assumed transitive.

Without the transitivity assumption, it is not valid in all structures. A binary relation can contain $(a,b)$ and $(b,c)$ without containing $(a,c)$.

So the correct entailment includes the assumption:

$$
\forall x,\forall y,\forall z,((x<y \land y<z)\to x<z),\ x<y,\ y<z \models x<z
$$
