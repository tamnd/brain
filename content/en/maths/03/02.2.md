## 2.2 Quantifiers and Scope

Quantifiers let first-order logic talk about many objects at once.

There are two basic quantifiers:

| Symbol    | Name                   | Reading      |
| --------- | ---------------------- | ------------ |
| $\forall$ | universal quantifier   | for all      |
| $\exists$ | existential quantifier | there exists |

The universal quantifier makes a claim about every object in the domain.

The existential quantifier makes a claim about at least one object in the domain.

### Universal Quantifier

The formula:

$$
\forall x,A(x)
$$

means that $A(x)$ holds for every possible value of $x$ in the domain.

Example:

$$
\forall x,(x=x)
$$

This says every object is equal to itself.

Another example:

$$
\forall x,(\mathrm{Human}(x) \to \mathrm{Mortal}(x))
$$

This says every human is mortal.

The implication matters. The formula does not say every object is human. It says that if an object is human, then it is mortal.

### Existential Quantifier

The formula:

$$
\exists x,A(x)
$$

means that there is at least one object in the domain for which $A(x)$ holds.

Example:

$$
\exists x,\mathrm{Prime}(x)
$$

This says there exists a prime object.

Another example:

$$
\exists x,(x+1=3)
$$

This says there is an object whose successor or sum with $1$ is $3$.

### Scope

The **scope** of a quantifier is the part of the formula controlled by that quantifier.

In:

$$
\forall x,(P(x) \to Q(x))
$$

the scope of $\forall x$ is:

$$
P(x) \to Q(x)
$$

So both occurrences of $x$ are bound by $\forall x$.

In:

$$
(\forall x,P(x)) \to Q(x)
$$

the scope of $\forall x$ is only:

$$
P(x)
$$

The occurrence of $x$ in $Q(x)$ is free.

These two formulas are different. Parentheses are essential.

### Free and Bound Occurrences

A variable occurrence is **bound** if it lies inside the scope of a quantifier for that variable.

A variable occurrence is **free** if it does not.

Example:

$$
\forall x,(P(x) \to R(x,y))
$$

The occurrences of $x$ are bound. The occurrence of $y$ is free.

The formula still depends on the value assigned to $y$.

Another example:

$$
\exists y,\forall x,R(x,y)
$$

Here both $x$ and $y$ are bound. This is a sentence.

It says there is one object $y$ such that every object $x$ is related to it.

### Order of Quantifiers

The order of quantifiers matters.

Compare:

$$
\forall x,\exists y,R(x,y)
$$

with:

$$
\exists y,\forall x,R(x,y)
$$

The first says: for every $x$, there may be a possibly different $y$ such that $R(x,y)$ holds.

The second says: there is one fixed $y$ that works for every $x$.

These are usually not equivalent.

For example, let $R(x,y)$ mean:

$$
x < y
$$

In the natural numbers, the formula:

$$
\forall x,\exists y,(x<y)
$$

is true. Every natural number has a larger natural number.

But:

$$
\exists y,\forall x,(x<y)
$$

is false. There is no single natural number larger than every natural number.

### Restricted Quantifiers

Mathematics often uses restricted quantifiers.

For example:

$$
\forall x \in \mathbb{N},\ x+0=x
$$

This is informal shorthand for:

$$
\forall x,(x \in \mathbb{N} \to x+0=x)
$$

For existence:

$$
\exists x \in \mathbb{N},\ x^2=4
$$

means:

$$
\exists x,(x \in \mathbb{N} \land x^2=4)
$$

Notice the difference:

Universal restriction uses implication:

$$
\forall x \in S,\ A(x)
\quad \text{means} \quad
\forall x,(x \in S \to A(x))
$$

Existential restriction uses conjunction:

$$
\exists x \in S,\ A(x)
\quad \text{means} \quad
\exists x,(x \in S \land A(x))
$$

This distinction is important. It prevents accidental changes in meaning.

### Negating Quantifiers

Negation switches quantifiers.

The negation of “everything has property $P$” is “something does not have property $P$”:

$$
¬\forall x,P(x)
\equiv
\exists x,¬P(x)
$$

The negation of “something has property $P$” is “nothing has property $P$”:

$$
¬\exists x,P(x)
\equiv
\forall x,¬P(x)
$$

These are quantifier versions of De Morgan laws.

Example:

$$
¬\forall x,\mathrm{Prime}(x)
$$

means:

$$
\exists x,¬\mathrm{Prime}(x)
$$

Not every object is prime exactly when there is at least one object that is not prime.

Another example:

$$
¬\exists x,(x^2=2)
$$

means:

$$
\forall x,(x^2 \ne 2)
$$

There is no solution exactly when every object fails to be a solution.

### Variable Renaming

Bound variable names can be changed as long as the structure of binding is preserved.

For example:

$$
\forall x,P(x)
$$

is equivalent to:

$$
\forall y,P(y)
$$

The name of the bound variable is only a placeholder.

But we must avoid changing meaning by accidentally capturing a free variable.

For example:

$$
\forall x,R(x,y)
$$

can be safely renamed as:

$$
\forall z,R(z,y)
$$

But renaming $x$ to $y$ would give:

$$
\forall y,R(y,y)
$$

This changes the formula because the originally free $y$ becomes bound.

This error is called **variable capture**.
