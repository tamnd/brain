## 7.4 Arithmetic of Cardinals

Cardinal arithmetic studies how sizes of sets behave under operations such as sum, product, and exponentiation.

For finite sets, this agrees with ordinary arithmetic. For infinite sets, some results look surprising at first.

### Cardinal Addition

If $A$ and $B$ are disjoint sets, their cardinal sum is:

$$
|A| + |B| = |A \cup B|
$$

For finite sets, this is ordinary addition. If:

$$
A={1,2,3}
$$

and:

$$
B={4,5}
$$

then:

$$
|A|+|B|=3+2=5
$$

For infinite sets, adding a finite set often does not change the size.

For example:

$$
|\mathbb{N}| + 1 = |\mathbb{N}|
$$

because:

$$
\mathbb{N} \cup {\ast}
$$

can still be listed as a sequence:

$$
\ast,0,1,2,3,\dots
$$

So:

$$
\aleph_0 + 1 = \aleph_0
$$

Similarly:

$$
\aleph_0 + \aleph_0 = \aleph_0
$$

because two countable sets can be interleaved:

$$
a_0,b_0,a_1,b_1,a_2,b_2,\dots
$$

### Cardinal Multiplication

If $A$ and $B$ are sets, their cardinal product is:

$$
|A|\cdot |B| = |A \times B|
$$

where:

$$
A \times B = {(a,b):a\in A,\ b\in B}
$$

For finite sets, this is ordinary multiplication.

For countably infinite sets:

$$
\aleph_0 \cdot \aleph_0 = \aleph_0
$$

This means:

$$
|\mathbb{N}\times\mathbb{N}| = |\mathbb{N}|
$$

One way to see this is to list pairs diagonally:

$$
(0,0)
$$

$$
(0,1),(1,0)
$$

$$
(0,2),(1,1),(2,0)
$$

$$
(0,3),(1,2),(2,1),(3,0)
$$

and so on.

Every pair eventually appears.

### Cardinal Exponentiation

Cardinal exponentiation counts functions.

If $A$ and $B$ are sets, then:

$$
|B|^{|A|} = |B^A|
$$

where:

$$
B^A = {f : A \to B}
$$

So $|B|^{|A|}$ is the number of functions from $A$ to $B$.

For finite sets, if:

$$
|A|=m
$$

and:

$$
|B|=n
$$

then:

$$
|B^A|=n^m
$$

because each of the $m$ inputs has $n$ possible outputs.

For infinite sets, exponentiation produces important new cardinalities.

For example:

$$
2^{\aleph_0}
$$

is the cardinality of all functions:

$$
f:\mathbb{N}\to{0,1}
$$

Such functions are the same as infinite binary sequences:

$$
010011101\dots
$$

They are also the same as subsets of $\mathbb{N}$, because each subset has a characteristic function.

For $S\subseteq\mathbb{N}$, define:

$$
\chi_S(n)=
\begin{cases}
1, & n\in S,\
0, & n\notin S.
\end{cases}
$$

Thus:

$$
2^{\aleph_0}=|\mathcal{P}(\mathbb{N})|
$$

This is the cardinality of the continuum:

$$
2^{\aleph_0}=|\mathbb{R}|
$$

### Finite Arithmetic vs Infinite Arithmetic

Cardinal arithmetic behaves differently from ordinal arithmetic.

For cardinals:

$$
\aleph_0 + 1 = \aleph_0
$$

and:

$$
\aleph_0 + \aleph_0 = \aleph_0
$$

But for ordinals:

$$
\omega + 1 \neq \omega
$$

and:

$$
\omega + \omega \neq \omega
$$

The reason is that cardinals measure only size, while ordinals remember order.

Adding one final point changes the order type of $\omega$, but it does not change its size.

### Basic Infinite Cardinal Rules

Let $\kappa$ be an infinite cardinal. If $n$ is a nonzero finite cardinal, then:

$$
\kappa + n = \kappa
$$

and:

$$
\kappa \cdot n = \kappa
$$

Also:

$$
\kappa + \kappa = \kappa
$$

and:

$$
\kappa \cdot \kappa = \kappa
$$

These equalities hold for infinite cardinals under the usual set-theoretic assumptions, including the Axiom of Choice.

For example:

$$
|\mathbb{R}| + |\mathbb{N}| = |\mathbb{R}|
$$

Adding countably many points to the real line does not change its cardinality.

### Comparing Infinite Cardinals

The smallest infinite cardinal is:

$$
\aleph_0
$$

The next well-ordered cardinal is:

$$
\aleph_1
$$

Then:

$$
\aleph_2,\quad \aleph_3,\quad \dots
$$

In general, the aleph numbers list infinite well-orderable cardinalities:

$$
\aleph_0,\aleph_1,\aleph_2,\dots,\aleph_\alpha,\dots
$$

The continuum has cardinality:

$$
2^{\aleph_0}
$$

A major question is whether:

$$
2^{\aleph_0}=\aleph_1
$$

This is the **Continuum Hypothesis**.

It cannot be proved or disproved from the usual ZFC axioms, assuming those axioms are consistent.

### Cardinal Arithmetic and Power Sets

Cantor's theorem says:

$$
|A| < |\mathcal{P}(A)|
$$

In cardinal notation:

$$
\kappa < 2^\kappa
$$

for every cardinal $\kappa$.

So there is no largest cardinal. Starting from any set, its power set has strictly larger size.

For example:

$$
\aleph_0 < 2^{\aleph_0}
$$

and:

$$
2^{\aleph_0} < 2^{2^{\aleph_0}}
$$

This gives an infinite hierarchy of larger and larger cardinalities.
