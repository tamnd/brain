## What Is Nasal Mutation?

Nasal mutation is the second of Sindarin's three main initial consonant mutation systems (the others being soft mutation and mixed mutation). It is triggered by a preceding nasal consonant — the *n* of certain grammatical words causes the following initial consonant to shift, typically by nasalizing voiced stops or voicing voiceless stops.

Historically, Old Sindarin had nasal consonants at the end of certain grammatical words (the plural definite article, the dative preposition, certain prefixes). These nasal endings caused assimilation: the following initial stop took on a nasal quality or was voiced by the nasal environment. The nasal endings later eroded in pronunciation, but the mutations they triggered remained as a grammatical marker, encoded as an alternation in the following word's initial consonant.

The practical result: you see nasal mutation in three main environments:
1. After the plural definite article *in* (the — plural)
2. After the dative preposition *an* (to, for)
3. After certain verbal prefixes containing a nasal

## The Nasal Mutation Table

| Original Initial | After Nasal Mutation | Process |
|-----------------|---------------------|---------|
| **p** | **b** | voiceless bilabial stop → voiced bilabial stop |
| **t** | **d** | voiceless alveolar stop → voiced alveolar stop |
| **c** | **g** | voiceless velar stop → voiced velar stop |
| **b** | **m** | voiced bilabial stop → bilabial nasal |
| **d** | **n** | voiced alveolar stop → alveolar nasal |
| **g** | **ng** | voiced velar stop → velar nasal |
| **m** | **m** | already a nasal — unchanged |
| **gw** | **ngw** | voiced labiovelar → nasal labiovelar |
| **s** | **h** | sibilant → glottal fricative (same as soft mutation) |
| **f** | **f** | unchanged |
| **l, r, n, v, w** | unchanged | no mutation |

### Comparison: Nasal Mutation vs. Soft Mutation

This comparison is critical — the two mutations are often confused by learners:

| Original | Soft Mutation | Nasal Mutation | Difference? |
|----------|--------------|----------------|-------------|
| p | b | b | Same result |
| t | d | d | Same result |
| c | g | g | Same result |
| **b** | **v** | **m** | DIFFERENT |
| **d** | **dh** | **n** | DIFFERENT |
| **g** | zero (') | **ng** | DIFFERENT |
| m | v | m | DIFFERENT (soft changes m; nasal does not) |
| gw | (gw→w?) | ngw | Different |
| s | h | h | Same result |

For the voiceless stops p/t/c, both mutations give the same result (b/d/g), so context (which trigger is present) tells you which mutation is operating. For the voiced stops b/d/g, the mutations diverge completely: soft mutation produces spirants (v, dh, zero), while nasal mutation produces nasals (m, n, ng). This is the core diagnostic difference.

## Trigger 1: Plural Definite Article *in*

The plural form of the definite article is *in* (cf. singular *i*). The *in* article triggers nasal mutation on the initial consonant of the following plural noun.

**Important note on word order:** The noun must first be put into its plural form (via i-affection, Lesson 9), and *then* the article *in* triggers nasal mutation on whatever initial consonant the plural noun happens to start with.

| Singular | Plural (base) | With *in* (nasal mutation) | Translation |
|----------|-------------|--------------------------|-------------|
| *perian* | *periannath* | *in Mberiannath* | the hobbits (p→b) |
| *taur* | *taurin* | *in daurin* | the forests (t→d) |
| *coth* | *cyth* | *in gyth* | the enemies (c→g) |
| *barad* | *beraid* | *in Meraid* | the towers (b→m) |
| *dîr* | *dîr* (unchanged pl.) | *in nîr* | the men (d→n) |
| *galadh* | *gelaidh* | *in ngelaidh* | the trees (g→ng) |
| *mellon* | *mellyn* | *in mellyn* | the friends (m unchanged) |
| *gwaith* | *gwaith* | *in ngwaith* | the peoples (gw→ngw) |
| *orch* | *yrch* | *in yrch* | the orcs (vowel initial — no consonant to mutate) |

### Attested: *i·Mbair Annui*

One of the clearest attested examples of nasal mutation from the plural article appears in the phrase *i·Mbair Annui* ("the Western Lands"). Here:
- *Bair* is the plural of *bâr* / *bar* (home, dwelling, land)
- Under nasal mutation from the plural article *in* (which becomes *i·m* before b-): b → m
- So *in Bair* → *i·Mbair* (the homes/lands)
- *Annui* = western

The dot (·) marks the article-noun boundary; *i·Mbair* = *in* + *bair* with the nasal mutation of *b* to *m* represented by the written *Mb* (a cluster).

### Attested: *Cuio i Pheriannath anann!*

This famous toast from *The Lord of the Rings* ("May the Halflings live long!") uses:
- *i Pheriannath* — note **ph** here, not **mb**: this is actually **mixed mutation**, not nasal mutation, on the plural article. The article here triggers a voiceless stop mutation (p→ph = p is aspirated or spirantized)
- This reflects the mixed mutation triggered by the plural article in certain phonological environments — a preview of Lesson 30

The precise mutation behavior of the plural article has been a matter of scholarly discussion. For practical purposes:
- Voiced stops (b, d, g) clearly undergo nasal mutation after *in*: b→m, d→n, g→ng
- Voiceless stops (p, t, c) after *in* may show mixed mutation (p→ph/b) depending on the analysis

## Trigger 2: Preposition *an* (Dative)

The preposition *an* (to, for — dative) triggers nasal mutation. This was covered in Lesson 13, but we provide the complete paradigm here with additional examples.

### Full Paradigm: *an* + Each Initial Consonant

| *an* + Initial | Result | Example | Translation |
|---------------|--------|---------|-------------|
| *an* + p | *an b-* | *an berian* | for/to a hobbit |
| *an* + t | *an d-* | *an dîr* | for/to a man |
| *an* + c | *an g-* | *an galen* | for the green (c→g) |
| *an* + b | *an m-* | *an marad* | for/to a tower (b→m) |
| *an* + d | *an n-* | *an nîr* | for/to the man (d→n) |
| *an* + g | *an ng-* | *an Ngondor* | to Gondor (g→ng) |
| *an* + m | *an m-* | *an mellon* | for/to a friend (m unchanged) |
| *an* + gw | *an ngw-* | *an ngwaith* | for/to the people (gw→ngw) |
| *an* + vowel | *an -* | *an aran* | for/to a king (vowel, no change) |

### Dative with *an* in Context

- *an nîr en aran* = "for the king's man" — *an* + *dîr* (d→n) + *en aran* (genitive)
- *an Ngondor* = "to/for Gondor" — *an* + *Gondor* (G→ng)
- *Boe ammen* = "it is necessary for us" — *boe* (it is necessary) + *ammen* (an + men = for us: m unchanged, but the preposition contracts with the pronoun)

The contracted forms where *an* fuses with personal pronouns:
- *an* + *ni* (I/me) → *enni* ("to me") — attested in *Guren bêd enni*
- *an* + *le* (thee) → *le* or *aníl* in various contexts
- *an* + *men* (us) → *ammen* — attested in *boe ammen tírad* ("we must look upon")

## Trigger 3: Verbal Prefix *en-* (Re-, Again)

The prefix *en-* meaning "re-" or "again" carries a nasal and triggers nasal mutation on the following verbal root:

| Base Verb | With *en-* | Meaning |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| *car-* (to do/make) | *en-gar-* | to do again, redo (c→g) |
| *tol-* (to come) | *en-dol-* | to come again (t→d) |
| *ped-* (to speak) | *en-bed-* | to speak again (p→b) |
| *beria-* (to protect) | *en-meria-* | to protect again (b→m) |

Attestation of this prefix in this verbal use is limited in Tolkien's published writings; these forms follow the rules of nasal mutation but should be marked as Neo-Sindarin reconstructions.

## The Particle *na-* and Nasalization

In some analyses, the particle *na* (is, was) and its related forms may trigger mild nasalization in certain environments. This is a more advanced point and not fully attested; learners can treat *na* as not triggering mutation for now.

## Nasal Mutation vs. Soft Mutation: Full Comparison

| Context | Trigger | Mutation Type | Example |
|---------|---------|--------------|---------|
| Singular definite article | *i* | **soft** | *i varad* (tower, b→v) |
| Plural definite article | *in* | **nasal** | *in Meraid* (towers, b→m) |
| Adjective after noun | noun | **soft** | *aran veleg* (great king) |
| Direct object | verb | **soft** | *Tiron varad* (I watch a tower) |
| Dative preposition | *an* | **nasal** | *an marad* (for a tower) |
| Most prepositions | *ab, na, nu,* etc. | **soft** | *nu dhôr* (under the land) |
| Verbal negation | *ú-* | **soft** | *ú-'aladh* (without a tree) |
| "Re-" prefix | *en-* | **nasal** | *en-gar-* (to redo) |

The rule of thumb: **soft mutation is the default**; **nasal mutation is specifically triggered by nasal-containing grammatical words** (*in*, *an*, *en-*).

## Common Errors with Nasal Mutation

| Error | Correction | Why It Matters |
|-------|-----------|----------------|
| Using soft mutation after *in* (pl. article) | Use nasal mutation | *in beraid* ✗ → *in Meraid* ✓ (b→m, not b→v) |
| Using nasal mutation after *i* (sg. article) | Use soft mutation | *i marad* ✗ → *i varad* ✓ (b→v, not b→m) |
| Writing *in ngelaidh* as *in gelaidh* | *g* becomes *ng* — write it | The mutation must be written out |
| Thinking *m* changes after nasal mutation | *m* is already nasal — unchanged | *an mellon* stays *an mellon* |
| Confusing *g→zero* (soft) with *g→ng* (nasal) | They are opposite outcomes | After *i*: *i 'aladh* (g gone); after *in*: *in ngelaidh* (g→ng) |
