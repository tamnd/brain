## Stroke Order

The stroke order for **月** follows the standard rule of top-to-bottom and left-to-right. Note the "hook" (hane) at the end of the second stroke.

1. **Stroke 1:** The vertical line on the left, slightly curved inward.
2. **Stroke 2:** The top horizontal line, turning down to form a vertical line with a small hook at the bottom.
3. **Stroke 3:** The first horizontal line inside the box.
4. **Stroke 4:** The second horizontal line inside the box.

## Radical Analysis

The kanji **月** is a pictograph representing the crescent moon. It is its own radical (部首). When used as a component in other kanji (like 服 or 期), it is often called *nikuzuki* (肉月), where it represents "flesh" or "body parts," though this is a separate historical evolution from the "moon" character.

## Mnemonic

Imagine the kanji as a crescent moon hanging in the night sky. The two horizontal lines inside represent the "craters" or "shadows" visible on the surface of the moon when you look up at it on a clear night.

## Common Confusion Pairs

| Kanji | Reading | Meaning | How to distinguish |
|-------|---------|---------|-------------------|
| **月** | つき | Moon/Month | Has two horizontal lines inside. |
| **日** | ひ | Sun/Day | Has one horizontal line inside; box shape. |
| **目** | め | Eye | Has two horizontal lines inside, but the box is taller. |

*Note: **日** (Sun) and **月** (Moon) are often paired together to form the word **明日** (ashita - tomorrow), literally "bright day."*

## Usage Frequency Note

**月** is one of the most frequently used kanji in the Japanese language. It appears in every date (months) and every week (days of the week). It is essential for daily communication, both in formal writing and casual speech.
