## Stroke Order

The character **女** is written in 3 strokes. Follow the standard top-to-bottom, left-to-right rule:

1. **Stroke 1**: A diagonal stroke starting from the top right, sweeping down to the left.
2. **Stroke 2**: A short horizontal stroke starting from the left, moving right, and ending in a downward hook.
3. **Stroke 3**: A long horizontal stroke that crosses the first stroke, providing a stable base.

## Radical Analysis

The kanji **女** is a **pictograph** (象形文字 - *shōkei moji*). It depicts a person kneeling with their hands crossed in front of them, a posture that historically represented a woman in a submissive or graceful position. Because it is a basic character, it acts as a radical itself. It appears in many kanji related to gender, family, or human qualities (e.g., 妹 *imōto* - younger sister, 姉 *ane* - older sister, 娘 *musume* - daughter).

## Mnemonic

**The Kneeling Lady:** Imagine a woman kneeling on the floor, crossing her arms in front of her chest. The top diagonal stroke is her head and back, the middle hook is her crossed arms, and the bottom line is the floor she is kneeling upon.

| 女 | おんな | woman | The "kneeling" shape; 3 strokes. |
| 子 | こ | child | Represents a baby with arms; 3 strokes. |
| 汝 | なんじ | you (archaic) | Contains 女 but adds a water radical (氵) on the left. |

## Usage Frequency Note

**女** is one of the most fundamental kanji in the Japanese language. It is extremely common in both formal writing and daily speech. You will encounter it daily in contexts ranging from public signage (e.g., 女子トイレ *joshi toire* - women's restroom) to literature and casual conversation. It is essential for basic literacy.
