## Stroke Order

The kanji **新** is written in 13 strokes. It follows the standard rules of top-to-bottom and left-to-right.

1. **Left side (立):** Dot (1) → Horizontal (2) → Dot (3) → Dot (4) → Horizontal (5).
2. **Middle (木):** Vertical (6) → Horizontal (7) → Left slant (8) → Right slant (9).
3. **Right side (斤):** Left slant (10) → Horizontal (11) → Vertical (12) → Hook (13).

*Note: Pay attention to the "tree" (木) component in the middle, which is slightly compressed to fit the other radicals.*

## Radical Analysis

The kanji **新** is a phono-semantic compound:
*   **Left side (立):** Represents "standing."
*   **Middle (木):** Represents a "tree."
*   **Right side (斤):** The radical **斤** (axe/cleaver). 

**Meaning logic:** The original meaning relates to "cutting wood with an axe." When you cut a tree, you reveal the "new," fresh wood inside. Thus, the combination of "standing tree" and "axe" evolved to represent the concept of something "new."

## Mnemonic

Imagine a **tree** (木) **standing** (立) in the forest. A lumberjack takes his **axe** (斤) and chops it down. When he splits the trunk, the inside is bright, fresh, and **new** (新).

## Common Confusion Pairs

| Kanji | Reading | Meaning | How to distinguish |
|-------|---------|---------|-------------------|
| **新** | シン | New | Contains 斤 (axe) on the right. |
| **親** | シン | Parent/Intimate | Contains 見 (see) on the right. |

*   **Distinction:** **新** (New) implies cutting wood (axe), while **親** (Parent) implies "seeing" (見) your family often, leading to intimacy.

## Usage Frequency Note

**新** is one of the most frequently used kanji in the Japanese language. It appears daily in news headlines, product branding, and formal writing. It is essential for N5 learners as it appears in basic vocabulary like "newspaper" and "new."
