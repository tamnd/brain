JLPT N1 requires mastery of approximately 2,000+ kanji — effectively the complete Joyo kanji list (2,136 characters). The ~1,000 new kanji beyond N2 are concentrated in specialized domains: literary/classical, legal, medical, academic, and cultural. At N1, kanji study is inseparable from vocabulary study.

---

## How N1 Kanji Differs from N2

At N2, most kanji are high-frequency, everyday characters. At N1:
- **Rare on-readings** from Classical Chinese that don't appear in daily speech
- **Literary kun-readings** found in novels and poetry
- **Multiple meanings** depending on compound context
- **Visually similar kanji** that appear in different registers
- **Classical and archaic kanji** found in literary texts and historical documents
- **Place names and personal names** use many non-Joyo kanji

## Kanji Study Strategy for N1

### Priority Order

1. **Vocabulary-first approach**: Learn kanji as part of vocabulary, not in isolation. 醸成する learned in context beats memorizing 醸 alone.
2. **Literary reading**: Read Japanese essays, editorials, and short stories. Unknown kanji in context stick far better.
3. **Shin Kanzen Master Kanji N1**: Systematic coverage of N1 kanji vocabulary organized by theme — the best exam-focused resource.
4. **WaniKani levels 51–60**: Covers the hardest ~500 kanji, many of which appear at N1.
5. **Confused pairs drill**: Practice 紛らわしい漢字 with minimal-pair exercises — 微/徴、概/慨、践/戦 are exam favorites.
6. **Special readings flashcards**: Dedicate a separate deck to 熟字訓 and rare readings — these are directly tested in the vocabulary section.

### What N1 Kanji Tests Actually Ask

- Selecting the correct kanji for a word (spelling test format)
- Choosing the correct reading for an underlined compound
- Identifying which kanji looks similar but has a different meaning
- Understanding the meaning of rare kanji in context (reading comprehension)
- Stroke count questions (rare but possible)
