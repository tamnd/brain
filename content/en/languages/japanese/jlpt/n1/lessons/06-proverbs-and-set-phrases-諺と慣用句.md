## Overview

At the JLPT N1 level, proficiency is measured not just by your ability to understand literal meanings, but by your grasp of the cultural and historical subtext embedded in the language. Japanese proverbs (*kotowaza*) and set phrases (*kanyōku*) are the bedrock of sophisticated communication. They allow speakers to convey complex emotions, social critiques, and life philosophies with just a few words. Many of these phrases have roots in Buddhist sutras, classical Chinese literature, or the agrarian lifestyle of ancient Japan.

This lesson focuses on two main pillars: traditional proverbs that offer life advice and body-part idioms that describe personality traits or physical/mental states. By mastering these, you will transition from "speaking Japanese" to "thinking in Japanese," enabling you to navigate professional environments, academic texts, and deep interpersonal relationships with the nuance expected of a C1–C2 level learner.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson you can:
- Identify and correctly use classical Japanese proverb structures (e.g., the use of *~zu* and *~shi*).
- Utilize body-part idioms involving the head, hands, eyes, and ears to describe complex social situations.
- Differentiate between nature-based metaphors and understand their origins in Japanese folklore.
- Apply formal and casual idiomatic expressions appropriately based on social hierarchy and context.

## Grammar Points

### 1. Classical Proverb Structures: The "A yori B ga Yasushi" Pattern

Many N1-level proverbs utilize classical Japanese grammar. A common structure is **[Verb/Noun] + より + [Verb] + が易し (やすし)**. In modern Japanese, *yasushi* is replaced by *yasui* (easy), but the proverb retains the classical terminal form (*shūshikei*).

**Structure:** [Action A] より [Action B] が易し
*Meaning: Doing B is easier than worrying about/doing A.*

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
|----------|---------|--------|---------|
| 案ずるより産むが易し。 | あんずるよりうむがやすし。 | Anzuru yori umu ga yasushi. | It's easier to do it than to worry about it. |
| 言うは易く行うは難し。 | いうはやすくおこなうはかたし。 | Iu wa yasuku okonau wa katashi. | Easy to say, hard to do. |
| 聞くは一時の恥、聞かぬは一生の恥。 | きくはいっときのはじ、きかぬはいっしょうのはじ。 | Kiku wa ittoki no haji, kikanu wa isshō no haji. | To ask is a moment's shame, not to ask is a lifetime's shame. |

**Common mistake:** ❌ 案ずるより産むが易い (Anzuru yori umu ga yasui)
✓ 案ずるより産むが易し (Anzuru yori umu ga yasushi)
— While "yasui" is grammatically correct in modern Japanese, proverbs are fixed expressions. Changing the ending makes it sound uneducated or like a mistake rather than a quote.

### 2. Body-Part Idioms (Kanyōku) for Abstract States

Japanese uses body parts to describe psychological states. Unlike literal physical pain, these are used metaphorically. For N1, you must distinguish between the literal and the figurative based on context.

**Structure:** [Body Part] + が/を + [Verb/Adjective]

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
|----------|---------|--------|---------|
| 彼は本当に口が軽い。 | かれはほんとうにくちがかるい。 | Kare wa hontō ni kuchi ga karui. | He really has a big mouth (can't keep secrets). |
| 部長の指摘は耳が痛い。 | ぶちょうのしてきはみみがいたい。 | Buchō no shiteki wa mimi ga itai. | The manager's critique is hard to hear (because it's true). |
| 予算のことで頭が痛い。 | よさんのことであたまがいたい。 | Yosan no koto de atama ga itai. | I have a headache over the budget (it's a troubling problem). |
| 君は目が高いね。 | きみはめがたかいね。 | Kimi wa me ga takai ne. | You have excellent taste / a discerning eye. |

**Common mistake:** ❌ 手を借りる (Te o kariru) when you mean "to help someone."
✓ 手を貸す (Te o kasu) — To lend a hand (help someone).
✓ 手を借りる (Te o kariru) — To borrow a hand (receive help).
— Be careful with the direction of the action (giving vs. receiving).

## Dialogues

### Dialogue 1: At the Office (Professional Context)

**Setting:** Two colleagues, Sato and Tanaka, are discussing a difficult project and a junior employee who is struggling to fit in.

**Sato:** 田中さん、新人の鈴木君の件ですが、少し個性が強すぎてチームで浮いている気がしませんか。
(Tanaka-san, shinjin no Suzuki-kun no ken desu ga, sukoshi kosei ga tsuyosugite chīmu de uite iru ki ga shimasen ka.)
Tanaka-san, regarding the new hire Suzuki-kun, don't you feel his personality is a bit too strong and he's drifting away from the team?

**Tanaka:** ええ、日本では「出る杭は打たれる」と言いますからね。彼も少し協調性を覚えるべきかもしれません。
(Ee, Nihon de wa "deru kui wa utareru" to iimasu kara ne. Kare mo sukoshi kyōchōsei o oboeru beki kamoshiremasen.)
Yes, as they say in Japan, "the stake that sticks out gets hammered down." He might need to learn a bit more cooperativeness.

**Sato:** でも、彼の分析力に関しては目が高いですよ。昨日のレポートも鋭かったです。
(Demo, kare no bunsekiryoku ni kanshite wa me ga takai desu yo. Kinō no repōto mo surugatta desu.)
But, regarding his analytical skills, he has a discerning eye. Yesterday's report was also very sharp.

**Tanaka:** そうですね。今は苦労しているようですが、「石の上にも三年」です。彼が化けるのを待ちましょう。
(Sō desu ne. Ima wa kurō shite iru yō desu ga, "ishi no ue ni mo sannen" desu. Kare ga bakeru no o machimashō.)
That's true. He seems to be struggling now, but "three years on a stone." Let's wait for him to transform.

**Sato:** 私もできる限り彼に手を貸すつもりです。
(Watashi mo dekiru kagiri kare ni te o kasu tsumori desu.)
I intend to lend him a hand as much as I can.

### Dialogue 2: Casual Chat between Friends

**Setting:** Ken and Hiro are talking about Ken's upcoming marathon and his tendency to overthink things.

**Hiro:** ケン、来週のマラソン、そんなに不安そうな顔をしてどうしたんだ？
(Ken, raishū no marason, sonna ni fuansō na kao o shite dōshita n da?)
Ken, what's with the anxious face about next week's marathon?

**Ken:** 完走できるか心配で、夜も眠れないくらい頭が痛い問題なんだよ。
(Kansō dekiru ka shinpai de, yoru mo nemurenai kurai atama ga itai mondai nan da yo.)
I'm worried if I can finish it; it's a problem that's giving me a headache to the point I can't sleep.

**Hiro:** 案ずるより産むが易しだよ。練習は十分したんだろ？
(Anzuru yori umu ga yasushi da yo. Renshū wa jūbun shita n daro?)
It's easier to do than to worry. You've practiced enough, right?

**Ken:** まあね。でも、もし雨が降ったらとか考えちゃうんだ。
(Maa ne. Demo, moshi ame ga futtara toka kangaechau n da.)
Well, yeah. But I keep thinking, "what if it rains?"

**Hiro:** それは「捕らぬ狸の皮算用」の逆バージョンだな。心配しすぎだ。
(Sore wa "toranu tanuki no kawazanyō" no gyaku bājon da na. Shinpai sugida.)
That's like the reverse version of "counting badger pelts before you catch them." You're over-worrying.

**Ken:** はは、確かに。君にそう言われると、自分の弱気な性格に耳が痛いよ。
(Haha, tashika ni. Kimi ni sō iwareru to, jibun no yowaki na seikaku ni mimi ga itai yo.)
Haha, true. Hearing you say that, the truth about my timid personality hurts my ears.

## Cultural Note

### The Harmony of "Wa" and the "Hammered Stake"
The proverb *Deru kui wa utareru* (The stake that sticks out gets hammered down) is perhaps the most famous reflection of Japanese social structure. It highlights the value placed on *Wa* (和 - harmony) and collectivism. In many Western cultures, "the squeaky wheel gets the grease," encouraging individuals to speak up to get what they want. In Japan, however, maintaining the group's balance is often prioritized over individual brilliance. For an N1 learner, understanding this isn't just about vocabulary; it's about knowing when to use this phrase to describe social pressure or to warn someone about the risks of being too confrontational in a corporate setting.

### Nature and Animals in Proverbs
Japanese proverbs frequently use animals like cats, horses, and badgers (*tanuki*). The *tanuki*, in particular, is a staple of Japanese folklore, known for being a mischievous shapeshifter. The phrase *Toranu tanuki no kawazanyō* refers to the old practice of hunters calculating the price of a tanuki's fur before they had even caught the animal. Similarly, *Neko ni koban* refers to the *Koban* (oval gold coins from the Edo period). These phrases connect the modern speaker to Japan's history, showing how deeply the agrarian and feudal past still influences modern thought.
