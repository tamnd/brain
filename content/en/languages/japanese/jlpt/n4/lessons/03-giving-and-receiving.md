## Overview

In Japanese, the verbs used for "giving" and "receiving" change based on the social relationship between the speaker and the recipient. Unlike English, where "give" is universal, Japanese requires you to consider the direction of the action: are you giving to someone else, is someone giving to you, or are you receiving something? Mastering these verbs is a cornerstone of N4-level Japanese, as it reflects the cultural importance of social hierarchy and gratitude.

This lesson builds upon your knowledge of basic verb conjugations and particles. By understanding these three primary verbs and their "te-form" counterparts, you will be able to describe favors, assistance, and gifts with natural, polite Japanese. This skill is essential for moving beyond simple statements and into the realm of expressing interpersonal relationships and social obligations.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson you can:
- Correctly distinguish between あげる (ageru), くれる (kureru), and もらう (morau).
- Use the 〜てあげる, 〜てくれる, and 〜てもらう structures to describe favors.
- Offer assistance politely using 〜てあげましょうか.
- Understand the basic distinction between plain and humble/respectful forms (いただく/さしあげる).

## Grammar Points

### 1. The Core Verbs: あげる, くれる, もらう

**Structure:** [Giver] は [Recipient] に [Object] を [Verb]

These three verbs describe the flow of an object. **あげる (ageru)** is used when the speaker gives something to someone else. **くれる (kureru)** is used when someone gives something to the speaker (or someone in the speaker's "in-group"). **もらう (morau)** is used when the speaker receives something from someone.

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
|----------|---------|--------|---------|
| 私は友達に本をあげた。 | わたしはともだちにほんをあげた。 | Watashi wa tomodachi ni hon o ageta. | I gave a book to my friend. |
| 母が私にケーキをくれた。 | ははがわたしにけーきをくれた。 | Haha ga watashi ni kēki o kureta. | My mother gave me a cake. |
| 私は先生にペンをもらった。 | わたしはせんせいにぺんをもらった。 | Watashi wa sensei ni pen o moratta. | I received a pen from the teacher. |

**Common mistake:** ❌ 先生に本をあげました → ✓ 先生に本をさしあげました — When giving to a superior, use the humble verb さしあげる instead of the neutral あげる.

### 2. Expressing Favors: 〜てあげる, 〜てくれる, 〜てもらう

**Structure:** Verb-て + [あげる / くれる / もらう]

These patterns describe doing a favor for someone. 〜てあげる means "I do [verb] for someone." 〜てくれる means "Someone does [verb] for me." 〜てもらう means "I have someone do [verb] for me."

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
|----------|---------|--------|---------|
| 友達に日本語を教えてあげた。 | ともだちににほんごをおしえてあげた。 | Tomodachi ni nihongo o oshiete ageta. | I taught Japanese to my friend. |
| 兄が宿題を手伝ってくれた。 | あにがしゅくだいをおてつだってくれた。 | Ani ga shukudai o tetsudatte kureta. | My older brother helped me with my homework. |
| 医者に薬を飲んでもらった。 | いしゃにくすりをのんでもらった。 | Isha ni kusuri o nonde moratta. | I had the doctor take the medicine (for me). |

**Common mistake:** ❌ 先生が教えてあげました → ✓ 先生が教えてくださいました — If a superior does a favor for you, you must use the respectful form くださる.

## Dialogues

### Dialogue 1: At the station
**Context:** Tanaka is helping Sato carry a heavy bag.

**Tanaka:** 佐藤さん、その荷物、持ちましょうか？
(さとうさん、そのにもつ、もちましょうか？ / Satō-san, sono nimotsu, mochimashō ka?)
Sato-san, shall I carry that luggage for you?

**Sato:** あ、ありがとうございます。すみません。
(あ、ありがとうございます。すみません。 / A, arigatō gozaimasu. Sumimasen.)
Oh, thank you very much. I'm sorry (to trouble you).

**Tanaka:** いいえ。重そうですね。駅まで持ってあげますよ。
(いいえ。おもそうですね。えきまでもってあげますよ。 / Iie. Omosō desu ne. Eki made motte agemasu yo.)
No problem. It looks heavy. I will carry it to the station for you.

**Sato:** 本当に助かります。ありがとうございます。
(ほんとうにたすかります。ありがとうございます。 / Hontō ni tasukarimasu. Arigatō gozaimasu.)
That really helps. Thank you.

### Dialogue 2: Birthday gifts
**Context:** Two friends discussing gifts.

**Yumi:** 先週の誕生日、何をもらったの？
(せんしゅうのたんじょうび、なにをもらったの？ / Senshū no tanjōbi, nani o moratta no?)
What did you receive for your birthday last week?

**Ken:** 彼女が時計をくれたんだ。
(かのじょがとけいをくれたんだ。 / Kanojo ga tokei o kureta n da.)
My girlfriend gave me a watch.

**Yumi:** すごいね！私は友達にケーキを作ってあげたよ。
(すごいね！わたしはともだちにけーきをつくってあげたよ。 / Sugoi ne! Watashi wa tomodachi ni kēki o tsukutte ageta yo.)
That's amazing! I made a cake for my friend.

**Ken:** それは嬉しいね。
(それはうれしいね。 / Sore wa ureshii ne.)
That makes them happy, I'm sure.

## Cultural Note

In Japanese culture, the verbs of giving and receiving are deeply tied to the concept of *uchi-soto* (inside-outside). You use くれる when someone from your "in-group" (family, close friends) does something for you. Using the wrong verb can sound like you are demanding a favor or being overly arrogant.

Furthermore, the act of giving is often accompanied by the phrase "tsumaranai mono desu ga" (it is a boring thing, but...), which is a humble way to present a gift. It emphasizes the relationship over the value of the object itself, reflecting the Japanese value of modesty and social harmony.
