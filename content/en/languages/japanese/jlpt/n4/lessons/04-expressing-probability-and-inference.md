## Overview

In Japanese, expressing exactly how sure you are about a piece of information is a vital social skill. Unlike English, where we often rely on tone of voice, Japanese grammar uses specific suffixes to indicate whether you are guessing, reporting hearsay, or stating a logical expectation. This lesson is a cornerstone of N4-level communication, moving you beyond simple statements of fact into the nuanced world of subjective opinion.

By mastering these patterns, you will be able to discuss future plans, interpret social situations, and relay information you have heard from others. This lesson builds upon your knowledge of basic verb conjugations and sets the stage for more complex N3-level discussions regarding reports and formal observations.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson you can:
- Express varying degrees of probability using 〜かもしれない and 〜でしょう.
- Distinguish between hearsay (〜そうだ) and visual inference (〜ようだ/〜らしい).
- Use expectation markers like 〜はずだ to discuss logical outcomes.
- Correctly use adverbs like たぶん and きっと to emphasize your level of certainty.

## Grammar Points

### 1. 〜かもしれない (Might / Possibility)
**Structure:** Verb/Adj (plain form) + かもしれない / Noun/Na-Adj + かもしれない

This pattern expresses a 50/50 possibility. It is used when you are uncertain about an outcome. Note that for Na-adjectives and nouns, you drop the "da" before adding かもしれない.

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
|----------|---------|--------|---------|
| 明日は雨かもしれない。 | あしたはあめかもしれない。 | Ashita wa ame kamo shirenai. | It might rain tomorrow. |
| 彼が来るかもしれない。 | かれがくるかもしれない。 | Kare ga kuru kamo shirenai. | He might come. |
| 忙しいかもしれない。 | いそがしいかもしれない。 | Isogashii kamo shirenai. | I might be busy. |
| 失敗するかもしれない。 | しっぱいするかもしれない。 | Shippai suru kamo shirenai. | I might fail. |

**Common mistake:** ❌ 雨だかもしれない → ✓ 雨かもしれない — Do not include "da" with nouns.

### 2. 〜はずだ (Expectation / Should be)
**Structure:** Verb/Adj (plain form) + はずだ / Noun + のはずだ / Na-Adj + なはずだ

Use this when you have a strong reason to believe something is true based on logic or prior information. It is much stronger than "maybe."

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
|----------|---------|--------|---------|
| 彼はもう来るはずだ。 | かれはもうくるはずだ。 | Kare wa mō kuru hazu da. | He should be here by now. |
| 準備は終わったはずだ。 | じゅんびはおわったはずだ。 | Junbi wa owatta hazu da. | The preparation should be finished. |
| 彼は学生のはずだ。 | かれがくせいのはずだ。 | Kare wa gakusei no hazu da. | He should be a student. |

### 3. 〜そうだ (Hearsay)
**Structure:** Verb/Adj (plain form) + そうだ

This is used to report information you heard from someone else. It is distinct from "looks like" (which uses the stem of the adjective).

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
|----------|---------|--------|---------|
| 雨が降るそうだ。 | あめがふるそうだ。 | Ame ga furu sō da. | I heard it will rain. |
| 彼は忙しいそうだ。 | かれはいそがしいそうだ。 | Kare wa isogashii sō da. | I heard he is busy. |

## Dialogues

### Dialogue 1: Planning a Picnic
**A:** 明日、ピクニックに行きませんか？ (Ashita, pikunikku ni ikimasen ka? / Shall we go for a picnic tomorrow?)
**B:** 明日は雨かもしれないですね。 (Ashita wa ame kamo shirenai desu ne. / It might rain tomorrow, right?)
**A:** 天気予報では晴れるそうです。 (Tenki yohō de wa hareru sō desu. / The weather forecast says it will clear up.)
**B:** 本当ですか？じゃあ、行きましょう！ (Hontō desu ka? Jā, ikimashō! / Really? Then let's go!)

### Dialogue 2: Checking the Schedule
**A:** 田中さんはもう来ましたか？ (Tanaka-san wa mō kimashita ka? / Has Tanaka-san arrived yet?)
**B:** まだです。でも、もうすぐ来るはずですよ。 (Mada desu. Demo, mō sugu kuru hazu desu yo. / Not yet. But he should be here soon.)
**A:** 連絡はありましたか？ (Renraku wa arimashita ka? / Did you get a message from him?)
**B:** いいえ、でもきっと大丈夫です。 (Iie, demo kitto daijōbu desu. / No, but I'm sure it's fine.)

## Cultural Note

In Japanese culture, being overly direct can sometimes be perceived as aggressive or lacking in social awareness. Using these inference markers (〜かもしれない, 〜でしょう) allows speakers to "soften" their statements. This is known as *kūki o yomu* (reading the air). By not stating things as absolute facts, you leave room for the other person to disagree or provide new information without losing face.

Furthermore, the distinction between hearsay (〜そうだ) and visual inference (〜ようだ) is crucial. Japanese speakers value the source of information. If you say something is true, you are expected to know *how* you know it. Using these grammar points correctly signals to your listener that you are a culturally competent communicator who respects the nuance of information sharing.
