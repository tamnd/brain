## Overview

In Japanese, the word "if" does not have a single equivalent. Instead, the language uses four distinct conditional forms, each carrying specific nuances regarding time, causality, and context. Mastering these is a major milestone for N4 learners, as they allow you to move beyond simple statements and begin expressing complex thoughts, advice, and logical consequences.

This lesson breaks down the four primary conditionals: 〜たら (completed condition), 〜ば (hypothetical/natural condition), 〜なら (contextual condition), and 〜と (natural consequence). By understanding the subtle differences between these, you will be able to navigate everyday conversations, from giving directions to discussing hypothetical plans, with the precision of a native speaker.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson you can:
- Differentiate between the four conditional forms based on the speaker's intent.
- Use 〜たら to express "after" or "when" a condition is met.
- Use 〜ば for hypothetical situations or general rules.
- Use 〜なら to provide advice or respond to a topic introduced by someone else.
- Use 〜と to describe inevitable physical or logical consequences.

## Grammar Points

### 1. 〜たら (Conditional: Completed Condition)
**Structure:** Verb-た form + ら / い-adj-かった + ら / な-adj/Noun + だったら

The 〜たら form is the most versatile conditional. It implies that the first action must be completed before the second action occurs. It is often used for "when" or "after."

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
|----------|---------|--------|---------|
| 雨が降ったら、休みます。 | あめがふったら、やすみます。 | Ame ga futtara, yasumimasu. | If/When it rains, I will rest. |
| 着いたら、電話します。 | ついたら、でんわします。 | Tsuitara, denwa shimasu. | When I arrive, I will call you. |
| 暇だったら、来てください。 | ひまだったら、きてください。 | Hima dattara, kite kudasai. | If you are free, please come. |
| 食べたら、歯を磨きます。 | たべたら、はをみがきます。 | Tabetara, ha o migakimasu. | After I eat, I brush my teeth. |

**Common mistake:** ❌ 明日雨が降ったら、映画を見に行こう。 → ✓ 明日雨が降れば、映画を見に行こう。 (Use 〜ば or 〜なら for pure hypothetical future plans; 〜たら is better for "once it happens").

### 2. 〜ば (Conditional: Hypothetical/Natural)
**Structure:** Verb-e form + ば / い-adj-ければ / な-adj-であれば

The 〜ば form is used for hypothetical conditions. It is common in set phrases and general rules. It focuses on the condition itself rather than the completion of the action.

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
|----------|---------|--------|---------|
| 練習すれば、上手になります。 | れんしゅうすれば、じょうずになります。 | Renshuu sureba, jouzu ni narimasu. | If you practice, you will get better. |
| 安ければ、買います。 | やすければ、かいます。 | Yasukereba, kaimasu. | If it is cheap, I will buy it. |
| もし明日晴れれば、行きます。 | もしあしたはれれば、いきます。 | Moshi ashita harereba, ikimasu. | If it clears up tomorrow, I will go. |
| 困れば、相談してください。 | こまれば、そうだんしてください。 | Komareba, soudan shite kudasai. | If you are in trouble, please consult me. |

### 3. 〜なら (Conditional: Contextual)
**Structure:** Plain form + なら / Noun + なら

Use 〜なら when you want to respond to a topic mentioned by someone else or to give advice based on a specific context. It sets the stage: "Given that X is the case, then Y."

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
|----------|---------|--------|---------|
| 日本へ行くなら、京都がいいです。 | にほんへいくなら、きょうとがいいです。 | Nihon e iku nara, Kyoto ga ii desu. | If you are going to Japan, Kyoto is good. |
| 私なら、それを買いません。 | わたしなら、それをかいません。 | Watashi nara, sore o kaimasen. | If it were me, I wouldn't buy that. |
| 予定があるなら、帰りましょう。 | よていがあるなら、かえりましょう。 | Yotei ga aru nara, kaerimashou. | If you have plans, let's go home. |

### 4. 〜と (Conditional: Natural Consequence)
**Structure:** Plain form + と

Use 〜と for things that happen automatically, like physical laws or mechanical results. It cannot be used for requests or personal intentions.

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
|----------|---------|--------|---------|
| ボタンを押すと、電気がつきます。 | ボタンをおすと、でんきがつきます。 | Botan o osu to, denki ga tsukimasu. | If/When you press the button, the light turns on. |
| 春になると、暖かくなります。 | はるになると、あたたかくなります。 | Haru ni naru to, atatakaku narimasu. | When it becomes spring, it gets warm. |
| 右に曲がると、駅があります。 | みぎにまがると、えきがあります。 | Migi ni magaru to, eki ga arimasu. | If you turn right, there is a station. |

## Dialogues

### Dialogue 1: Planning a Weekend Trip
[Tanaka and Sato are discussing their weekend plans.]

Tanaka: 今週末、もし晴れたら海に行きませんか。
(こんしゅうまつ、もしはれたらうみに行きませんか / Konshuumatsu, moshi haretara umi ni ikimasen ka?)
"If it clears up this weekend, shall we go to the beach?"

Sato: いいですね。でも、もし雨が降ったらどうしますか。
(いいですね。でも、もしあめがふったらどうしますか / Ii desu ne. Demo, moshi ame ga futtara dou shimasu ka?)
"That sounds good. But if it rains, what shall we do?"

Tanaka: 雨なら、映画を見に行きましょう。
(あめなら、えいがをみにいきましょう / Ame nara, eiga o mi ni ikimashou.)
"If it's rain, let's go watch a movie."

Sato: それはいい考えです。
(それはいいかんがえです / Sore wa ii kangae desu.)
"That's a good idea."

### Dialogue 2: Giving Directions
[A tourist asks a local for directions to the post office.]

Tourist: すみません、郵便局はどこですか。
(すみません、ゆうびんきょくはどこですか / Sumimasen, yuubinkyoku wa doko desu ka?)
"Excuse me, where is the post office?"

Local: この道をまっすぐ行くと、銀行があります。
(このみちをまっすぐいくと、ぎんこうがあります / Kono michi o massugu iku to, ginkou ga arimasu.)
"If you go straight down this road, there is a bank."

Tourist: はい。
(はい / Hai.)
"Yes."

Local: その銀行を右に曲がると、郵便局が見えます。
(そのぎんこうをみぎにまがると、ゆうびんきょくがみえます / Sono ginkou o migi ni magaru to, yuubinkyoku ga miemasu.)
"If you turn right at that bank, you will see the post office."

## Grammar Drills

## Translation Practice

**English → Japanese**
1. If you eat this, you will feel better.
2. If it is summer, it is hot.
3. If you are going to Tokyo, please visit me.

**Japanese → English**
4. 練習すれば、上手になります。
5. 食べたら、薬を飲んでください。

**Answer Key:**
1. これを食べたら、元気になります。(Kore o tabetara, genki ni narimasu.)
2. 夏になると、暑いです。(Natsu ni naru to, atsui desu.)
3. 東京へ行くなら、私に会いに来てください。(Toukyou e iku nara, watashi ni ai ni kite kudasai.)
4. If you practice, you will become good at it.
5. After you eat, please take the medicine.

## Cultural Note

In Japanese, the use of conditionals is deeply tied to the concept of *enryo* (restraint) and social harmony. Using 〜たら or 〜ば can sometimes sound too direct if you are making a request. Often, Japanese speakers will use the conditional form followed by "〜いいですか" (Is it okay if...?) to soften a request, showing respect for the listener's time and convenience.

Furthermore, the distinction between these conditionals reflects a logical worldview. The use of 〜と for natural consequences highlights the Japanese appreciation for the predictable cycles of nature and mechanics, while the use of 〜なら demonstrates the importance of context—acknowledging what the other person has said before adding your own opinion or advice.
