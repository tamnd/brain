## Overview

In this lesson, we move beyond simple active sentences to explore the "voice" of Japanese verbs. Understanding the passive and causative forms is a major milestone for N4 learners, as these structures are essential for describing social interactions, workplace dynamics, and personal experiences. You will learn how to shift the focus of a sentence from the doer to the receiver, and how to express when someone is made or allowed to do something.

These forms are the building blocks for more advanced Japanese. By mastering these, you will be able to express nuance—such as feeling inconvenienced by someone else's actions or describing how you were influenced by others. This lesson bridges the gap between basic descriptive Japanese and the more complex, socially-aware language used in daily life.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson you can:
- Conjugate Group 1, 2, and irregular verbs into passive and causative forms.
- Use the particle `に` (ni) to identify the agent in passive sentences.
- Express "suffering passive" (adversative passive) to describe unwanted situations.
- Use the causative-passive form to describe being forced to do something against your will.

## Grammar Points

### 1. Passive Form (受け身 - Ukemi)

**Structure:** 
- Group 1: Change final `u` to `a` + `れる` (e.g., 書く → 書かれる)
- Group 2: Drop `る` + `られる` (e.g., 食べる → 食べられる)
- Irregular: する → される / 来る → 来られる

The passive form is used when the subject is the receiver of an action. In Japanese, the particle `に` (ni) marks the person who performed the action.

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
|----------|---------|--------|---------|
| 私は母に叱られた。 | わたしはははにしかれた。 | Watashi wa haha ni shikarareta. | I was scolded by my mother. |
| 彼は先生に褒められた。 | かれはせんせいにほめられた。 | Kare wa sensei ni homerareta. | He was praised by the teacher. |
| 私は犬に足を噛まれた。 | わたしはいぬにあしをかまれた。 | Watashi wa inu ni ashi o kamareta. | I had my foot bitten by a dog. |
| ケーキが弟に食べられた。 | けーきがおとうとにたべられた。 | Kēki ga otōto ni taberareta. | The cake was eaten by my younger brother. |

**Common mistake:** ❌ 私は母から叱られた。 → ✓ 私は母に叱られた。 — While `から` (kara) can sometimes be used, `に` (ni) is the standard particle for the agent of a passive verb.

### 2. Causative Form (使役 - Shieki)

**Structure:** 
- Group 1: Change final `u` to `a` + `せる` (e.g., 書く → 書かせる)
- Group 2: Drop `る` + `させる` (e.g., 食べる → 食べさせる)
- Irregular: する → させる / 来る → 来させる

The causative form expresses that someone is made to do something or allowed to do something. The person being made to do the action is marked with `に` (ni) or `を` (o).

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
|----------|---------|--------|---------|
| 母は私に野菜を食べさせた。 | はははわたしにやさいをたべさせた。 | Haha wa watashi ni yasai o tabeseta. | My mother made me eat vegetables. |
| 先生は学生に本を読ませた。 | せんせいはがくせいにほんをよませた。 | Sensei wa gakusei ni hon o yomaseta. | The teacher made the students read the book. |
| 父は私を旅行に行かせた。 | ちちはわたしをりょこうにいかせた。 | Chichi wa watashi o ryokō ni ikaseta. | My father let me go on the trip. |

**Common mistake:** ❌ 私は先生に読ませられた。 → ✓ 私は先生に読まされた。 — The causative-passive form for Group 1 verbs often contracts (e.g., 読ませられる becomes 読まされる).

## Dialogues

### Dialogue 1: At the office
**Tanaka:** 佐藤さん、昨日の会議で部長に怒られましたか。
**Reading:** さとうさん、きのうのかいぎでぶちょうにおこられましたか。 / Satō-san, kinō no kaigi de buchō ni okoraremashita ka.
**English:** Sato-san, were you scolded by the manager at yesterday's meeting?

**Sato:** はい、資料のミスを指摘されて、やり直させられました。
**Reading:** はい、しりょうのみすをしてきされて、やりなおさせられました。 / Hai, shiryō no misu o shiteki sarete, yarinaosaseraremashita.
**English:** Yes, my mistakes in the documents were pointed out, and I was made to redo them.

**Tanaka:** それは大変でしたね。
**Reading:** それはたいへんでしたね。 / Sore wa taihen deshita ne.
**English:** That sounds tough.

### Dialogue 2: Discussing childhood
**Yumi:** 子供の頃、親に厳しく育てられましたか。
**Reading:** こどものころ、おやにきびしくそだてられましたか。 / Kodomo no koro, oya ni kibishiku sodateraremashita ka.
**English:** Were you raised strictly by your parents when you were a child?

**Ken:** ええ、毎日ピアノを練習させられました。
**Reading:** ええ、まいにちぴあのをれんしゅうさせられました。 / Ee, mainichi piano o renshū saseraremashita.
**English:** Yes, I was made to practice the piano every day.

## Cultural Note

In Japanese, the passive voice is frequently used to express "adversative" feelings—situations where something happens to you that you find annoying or inconvenient. For example, saying "I was rained on" (雨に降られた) implies that the rain was an unwelcome event that ruined your day. This reflects a cultural tendency to focus on how external events impact the individual's state of mind.

Additionally, the causative form highlights the hierarchy in Japanese society. Using the causative form to say someone "made" you do something is a direct way of acknowledging authority. In professional settings, one must be careful with these forms to avoid sounding like you are complaining about your superiors, as the causative-passive often carries a strong connotation of being forced against one's will.
