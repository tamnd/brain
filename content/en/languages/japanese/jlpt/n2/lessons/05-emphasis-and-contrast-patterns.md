## Overview

In the N2 curriculum, the ability to nuance your speech is critical for moving beyond basic communication. This lesson focuses on four essential grammar patterns that allow you to highlight specific information, contrast items with varying degrees of intensity, and express logical expectations. These structures are frequently encountered in formal speeches, newspaper editorials, and professional business correspondence.

By mastering these patterns, you will transition from simply stating facts to framing arguments with the rhetorical sophistication expected of an intermediate-to-advanced learner. This lesson builds upon your existing knowledge of particles and conjunctions, providing the tools necessary to articulate complex relationships between ideas, which is a core requirement for passing the JLPT N2 exam and functioning in a Japanese workplace.

## Grammar Points

### 1. 〜こそ (koso)
**Structure:** Noun/Particle + こそ

**Explanation:** This particle is used to emphasize the word that precedes it. It translates to "precisely," "the very," or "it is [X] that..." It is often used to highlight a specific time, reason, or person that is the most important among others. Unlike `〜は` (wa), which marks a topic, `〜こそ` (koso) singles out the preceding element as the exclusive or primary focus.

**Usage Notes:** It is frequently used with `〜から` (e.g., `〜からこそ`) to emphasize that a specific reason is the *true* cause of a result.

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 今こそ行動すべきだ。 | いまこそこうどうすべきだ。 | Ima koso kōdō subeki da. | Now is the very time to act. |
| これこそ私が探していた本だ。 | これこそわたしがさがしていたほんだ。 | Kore koso watashi ga sagashite ita hon da. | This is the very book I was looking for. |
| あなたこそリーダーにふさわしい。 | あなたこそリーダーにふさわしい。 | Anata koso rīdā ni fusawashii. | You are the one who is fit to be a leader. |

---

### 2. 〜のみならず (nominarazu)
**Structure:** Noun/Verb/Adjective (Plain form) + のみならず

**Explanation:** This is a formal version of `〜だけでなく` (not only). It is used primarily in written language, speeches, or formal reports to add information. It implies that the scope is wider than the listener might expect.

**Usage Notes:** While `〜だけでなく` is common in daily conversation, `〜のみならず` is preferred in business documents and academic writing. It is often followed by `〜も` in the second clause to emphasize the addition. Unlike simple conjunctions, it functions as a formal connector that elevates the register of the entire sentence.

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 専門家のみならず一般人も参加する。 | せんもんかのみならずいっぱんじんもさんかする。 | Senmonka nominarazu ippanjin mo sanka suru. | Not only experts, but ordinary people will participate. |
| 彼は知識のみならず経験も豊富だ。 | かれはちしきのみならずけいけんもほうふだ。 | Kare wa chishiki nominarazu keiken mo hōfu da. | He has not only knowledge but also rich experience. |
| この計画は予算のみならず時間もかかる。 | このけいかくはよさんのみならずじかんもかかる。 | Kono keikaku wa yosan nominarazu jikan mo kakaru. | This plan requires not only budget but also time. |

---

### 3. 〜だけあって (dake atte)
**Structure:** Noun/Verb/Adjective (Plain form) + だけあって
*Note: For Na-adjectives and Nouns, use [Noun/Na-adj + である + だけあって].*

**Explanation:** This pattern is used to express that a result is natural or expected given the circumstances or the nature of the subject. It translates to "as expected of," or "being [X], it is natural that..."

**Usage Notes:** This is almost exclusively used for positive evaluations. It highlights that the outcome is a logical consequence of the subject's high quality, effort, or status. Do not use this for negative outcomes (e.g., "As expected of a lazy person, he failed" is unnatural).

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| プロだけあって、仕事が速い。 | ぷろだけあって、しごとがはやい。 | Puro dake atte, shigoto ga hayai. | As expected of a pro, the work is fast. |
| 高いだけあって、このワインは旨い。 | たかいだけあって、このわいんはうまい。 | Takai dake atte, kono wain wa umai. | As expected of something expensive, this wine is delicious. |
| 有名な店だけあって、行列ができている。 | ゆうめいなみせだけあって、ぎょうれつができている。 | Yūmei na mise dake atte, gyōretsu ga dekite iru. | As expected of a famous shop, there is a line. |

---

### 4. 〜はおろか (wa oroka)
**Structure:** Noun + はおろか

**Explanation:** This pattern is used to express "let alone" or "not to mention." It indicates that if the first (easier) thing is not true, then the second (more difficult) thing is definitely not true.

**Usage Notes:** This is used to emphasize a negative situation. **Crucially, the sentence must end with a negative predicate.** It creates a scale of difficulty where the first item is the "easier" or "more basic" task, and the second is the "harder" or "more advanced" task.

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 基礎はおろか応用もできない。 | きそはおろかおうようもできない。 | Kiso wa oroka ōyō mo dekinai. | I can't do the basics, let alone the applications. |
| 漢字はおろかひらがなも書けない。 | かんじはおろかひらがなもかけない。 | Kanji wa oroka hiragana mo kakenai. | I can't write kanji, let alone hiragana. |
| 忙しくて食事はおろか水も飲めない。 | いそがしくてしょくじはおろかみずものめない。 | Isogashikute shokuji wa oroka mizu mo nomenai. | I'm so busy I can't eat, let alone drink water. |

## Dialogues

### Dialogue 1: At a Professional Seminar
**Tanaka:** 今日の講演は素晴らしかったですね。
(Kyō no kōen wa subarashikatta desu ne.) / Today's lecture was wonderful.
**Sato:** ええ、専門家のみならず一般人にも分かりやすい内容でした。
(Ee, senmonka nominarazu ippanjin ni mo wakariyasui naiyō deshita.) / Yes, the content was easy to understand for not only experts but also ordinary people.
**Tanaka:** やはりプロだけあって、話し方が違いますね。
(Yahari puro dake atte, hanashikata ga chigaimasu ne.) / As expected of a pro, their way of speaking is different.
**Sato:** そうです。基礎はおろか、応用技術すら説明できていませんでした。
(Sō desu. Kiso wa oroka, ōyō gijutsu sura setsumei dekite imasen deshita.) / Exactly. They couldn't even explain the basics, let alone the advanced applications.

## Cultural Note

In Japanese communication, the use of emphatic particles like `〜こそ` reflects the culture's high-context nature. By explicitly highlighting a specific element, the speaker is often trying to build consensus or create a sense of urgency without being overly aggressive. It is a way of "pointing" at the truth of a situation so that the listener feels the same weight of importance.

Similarly, the use of `〜だけあって` (as expected of) is a common way to show respect in a professional setting. By acknowledging that a person's high performance is a natural result of their background or expertise, you are paying them a compliment while simultaneously validating their status. This reinforces social hierarchies and professional respect, which are cornerstones of Japanese business etiquette.

2. Why is `〜はおろか` used to emphasize a negative outcome? (Answer: It highlights that even the "easier" task is impossible, implying the "harder" task is even more impossible.)
3. What is the difference between `〜こそ` and standard emphasis particles like `〜は`? (Answer: `は` marks the topic; `こそ` singles out the element as the exclusive, most important focus.)
