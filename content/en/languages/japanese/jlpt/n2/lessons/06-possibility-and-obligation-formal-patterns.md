## Overview

In the N2 curriculum, transitioning from conversational to formal Japanese is essential for professional success and academic reading. This lesson focuses on high-level grammar patterns that express logical possibility, professional hesitation, and moral or social obligation. These structures are frequently encountered in business emails, news reports, and formal speeches.

By mastering these patterns, you will move beyond basic "can" (〜できる) and "should" (〜はず) constructions. You will learn to nuance your language, allowing you to decline requests politely, express objective possibilities, and articulate strong moral imperatives. This foundation is critical for the N2 reading and listening sections, where formal register is the standard.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson you can:
- Distinguish between objective possibility (〜うる) and subjective hesitation (〜かねる).
- Use formal written structures (〜べく) to express purpose in professional documents.
- Differentiate between moral obligation (〜べき) and emotional compulsion (〜ずにはいられない).
- Apply these patterns in professional dialogues and formal writing contexts.

## Grammar Points

### 1. 〜うる / 〜えない (Possibility)

**Structure:** Verb (stem) + うる / えない

This pattern expresses whether something is logically possible or impossible. Unlike 〜できる, which often refers to ability or permission, 〜うる focuses on the potential for an event to occur based on circumstances. It is common in formal writing and news.

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
|----------|---------|--------|---------|
| それはありうることだ。 | それはありうることだ。 | Sore wa ariuru koto da. | That is a possible thing. |
| 事故は起こりうる。 | じこはおこりうる。 | Jiko wa okoriuru. | Accidents can happen. |
| そんなことはありえない。 | そんなことはありえない。 | Sonna koto wa arienai. | That is impossible. |
| 変化は生じうる。 | へんかはしょうじうる。 | Henka wa shōjiuru. | Changes may occur. |

**Common mistake:** ❌ 彼は日本語を話しうる → ✓ 彼は日本語を話すことができる — "Uru" is for objective possibility, not personal ability.

---

### 2. 〜かねる (Hesitation/Inability)

**Structure:** Verb (stem) + かねる

This is a formal way to say "I cannot do something" or "I am unable to do something." It implies a sense of hesitation or that the speaker is not in a position to perform the action, often used in customer service or business to soften a refusal.

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
|----------|---------|--------|---------|
| お断りしかねます。 | おことわりしかねます。 | Okotowari shikanemasu. | I am afraid I must decline. |
| 承知しかねます。 | しょうちしかねます。 | Shōchi shikanemasu. | I cannot agree to that. |
| 判断しかねる状況だ。 | はんだんしかねるじょうきょうだ。 | Handan shikaneru jōkyō da. | It is a situation where I cannot judge. |
| 納得しかねます。 | なっとくしかねます。 | Nattoku shikanemasu. | I cannot accept that. |

**Common mistake:** ❌ 疲れていて歩きかねる → ✓ 疲れていて歩けない — "Kaneru" is for formal/polite refusal, not physical inability.

---

### 3. 〜べく (Purpose)

**Structure:** Verb (dictionary form) + べく

This is a formal, written-only pattern meaning "in order to." It is often used with verbs like "suru" (to do) or "doryoku suru" (to make an effort).

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
|----------|---------|--------|---------|
| 成功すべく努力する。 | せいこうすべくどりょくする。 | Seikō subeku doryoku suru. | I will strive to succeed. |
| 改善すべく会議を開く。 | かいぜんすべくかいぎをひらく。 | Kaizen subeku kaigi o hiraku. | We will hold a meeting to improve it. |
| 目的を達成すべく働く。 | もくてきをたっせいすべくはたらく。 | Mokuteki o tassei subeku hataraku. | I work to achieve the goal. |

---

### 4. 〜べき (Obligation)

**Structure:** Verb (dictionary form) + べき / べきだ

This expresses that something is the right thing to do, a moral duty, or a logical expectation. It is stronger than "should" (〜はず).

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
|----------|---------|--------|---------|
| 責任を果たすべきだ。 | せきにんをはたすべきだ。 | Sekinin o hatasu beki da. | One should fulfill their duty. |
| 今すぐ行くべきだ。 | いますぐいくべきだ。 | Ima sugu iku beki da. | You should go right now. |
| 考えるべき問題だ。 | かんがえるべきもんだいだ。 | Kangaeru beki mondai da. | It is a problem we should think about. |

---

### 5. 〜ずにはいられない (Compulsion)

**Structure:** Verb (nai-form) + ずにはいられない

This expresses an uncontrollable urge or feeling. It means "cannot help but do" or "cannot stop oneself from doing."

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
|----------|---------|--------|---------|
| 笑わずにはいられない。 | わらわずにはいられない。 | Warawazu ni wa irarenai. | I cannot help but laugh. |
| 泣かずにはいられない。 | なかずにはいられない。 | Nakazu ni wa irarenai. | I cannot help but cry. |
| 言わずにはいられない。 | いわずにはいられない。 | Iwazu ni wa irarenai. | I cannot help but say it. |

## Dialogues

### Dialogue 1: Business Meeting
**Context:** A manager is explaining why they cannot accept a proposal.

**Manager:** 今回の提案は、承知しかねます。
(こんかいのていあんは、しょうちしかねます / Konkai no teian wa, shōchi shikanemasu)
I cannot accept this proposal.

**Employee:** どのような理由でしょうか。
(どのようなりゆうでしょうか / Dono yō na riyū deshō ka)
What is the reason?

**Manager:** 予算の面で、実現はありえません。
(よさんのめんで、じつげんはありえません / Yosan no men de, jitsugen wa ariemasen)
In terms of budget, it is impossible to realize.

**Employee:** 改善すべく、再検討いたします。
(かいぜんすべく、さいけんとういたします / Kaizen subeku, saikentō itashimasu)
I will reconsider it to make improvements.

---

### Dialogue 2: Social Setting
**Context:** Two friends discussing a moving movie.

**Friend A:** あの映画、本当に感動したね。
(あのえいが、ほんとうにかんどうしたね / Ano eiga, hontō ni kandō shita ne)
That movie was really moving, wasn't it?

**Friend B:** 本当に。最後は泣かずにはいられなかったよ。
(ほんとうに。さいごはなかずにはいられなかったよ / Hontō ni. Saigo wa nakazu ni wa irarenakatta yo)
Truly. I couldn't help but cry at the end.

**Friend A:** 私も。あれは見るべき映画だね。
(わたしも。あれはみるべきえいがだね / Watashi mo. Are wa miru beki eiga da ne)
Me too. That is a movie everyone should see.

## Cultural Note

In Japanese business culture, the use of "〜かねる" (kaneru) is a vital tool for "saving face." Directly saying "No" (いいえ) is considered blunt and potentially rude. By using "〜かねます" (kanemasu), you are not just saying "I can't," but rather "I am in a position where I am unable to fulfill your request," which shifts the burden from your personal choice to the circumstances.

Similarly, the use of "〜べき" (beki) carries a weight of social responsibility. In a collectivist society, "what one should do" is often tied to one's role within a group (e.g., a student, an employee, a parent). When you use "〜べき," you are appealing to a shared sense of duty, which is a powerful way to persuade others or justify your own actions in a professional setting.
