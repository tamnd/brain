## Overview

In this lesson, we transition from static descriptions to dynamic action. You will learn the most fundamental Japanese verbs used to describe your daily routine, from waking up and eating to working and sleeping. Understanding these verbs is the cornerstone of building simple sentences about your life, which is a core requirement for the JLPT N5 exam.

Beyond simple vocabulary, we introduce the "て-form" (te-form), one of the most versatile and important conjugations in the Japanese language. By mastering the て-form and its connection to polite requests and progressive actions, you will move from simply stating facts to interacting with others and describing the world as it happens around you.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson you can:
- Categorize Japanese verbs into Group 1, 2, and 3.
- Use the particle を (o) to identify direct objects in a sentence.
- Make polite requests using the 〜てください (te-kudasai) structure.
- Describe ongoing actions using the 〜ています (te-imasu) present progressive form.
- Discuss your daily schedule using time-based adverbs like 毎日 (mainichi).

## Grammar Points

### 1. The Particle を (o)
**Structure:** [Object] + を + [Verb]

The particle を marks the direct object of a transitive verb. It indicates what is being acted upon. Note that while it is written as 'ha' (は) in hiragana, it is pronounced 'o'.

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
|----------|---------|--------|---------|
| 水を飲みます | みずをのみます | mizu o nomimasu | I drink water. |
| 本を読みます | ほんをよみます | hon o yomimasu | I read a book. |
| 日本語を話します | にほんごをはなします | nihongo o hanashimasu | I speak Japanese. |
| 映画を見ます | えいがをみます | eiga o mimasu | I watch a movie. |

**Common mistake:** ❌ 私は水が飲みます → ✓ 私は水を飲みます — The particle を is required for the object of the verb.

### 2. Polite Requests: 〜てください (te-kudasai)
**Structure:** [Verb-て form] + ください

To make a polite request, conjugate the verb into the て-form and add ください. This is essential for classroom instructions or polite social interactions.

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
|----------|---------|--------|---------|
| 食べてください | たべてください | tabete kudasai | Please eat. |
| 見てください | みてください | mite kudasai | Please look. |
| 書いてください | かいてください | kaite kudasai | Please write. |
| 来てください | きてください | kite kudasai | Please come. |

### 3. Present Progressive: 〜ています (te-imasu)
**Structure:** [Verb-て form] + います

This indicates an action currently in progress ("is doing") or a habitual state.

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
|----------|---------|--------|---------|
| 本を読んでいます | ほんをよんでいます | hon o yonde imasu | I am reading a book. |
| 日本語を勉強しています | にほんごをべんきょうしています | nihongo o benkyou shite imasu | I am studying Japanese. |

## Dialogues

### Dialogue 1: At the Library
[Tanaka]: すみません、本を読んでください。
[Reading/Romaji]: すみません、ほんをよんでください。 / Sumimasen, hon o yonde kudasai.
[English]: Excuse me, please read the book.

[Sato]: はい、わかりました。
[Reading/Romaji]: はい、わかりました。 / Hai, wakarimashita.
[English]: Yes, I understand.

[Tanaka]: 毎日、日本語を勉強していますか。
[Reading/Romaji]: まいにち、にほんごをべんきょうしていますか。 / Mainichi, nihongo o benkyou shite imasu ka.
[English]: Do you study Japanese every day?

[Sato]: はい、毎朝勉強しています。
[Reading/Romaji]: はい、まいあさべんきょうしています。 / Hai, maiasa benkyou shite imasu.
[English]: Yes, I study every morning.

### Dialogue 2: Morning Routine
[Ken]: 毎朝、何をしますか。
[Reading/Romaji]: まいあさ、なにをしますか。 / Maiasa, nani o shimasu ka.
[English]: What do you do every morning?

[Yumi]: 6時に起きます。そして、コーヒーを飲みます。
[Reading/Romaji]: ろくじにおきます。そして、こーひーをのみます。 / Rokuji ni okimasu. Soshite, koohii o nomimasu.
[English]: I wake up at 6:00. Then, I drink coffee.

[Ken]: パンを食べますか。
[Reading/Romaji]: ぱんをたべますか。 / Pan o tabemasu ka.
[English]: Do you eat bread?

[Yumi]: いいえ、食べません。
[Reading/Romaji]: いいえ、たべません。 / Iie, tabemasen.
[English]: No, I don't eat it.

## Cultural Note

In Japanese culture, the concept of "maintaining harmony" (wa) often influences how requests are made. Using ください is polite, but in very formal situations, Japanese speakers may use even softer language to avoid sounding demanding.

Additionally, the distinction between "every morning" (maiasa) and "every evening" (maiban) is significant in Japanese society, where routines are highly valued. Being able to describe your daily routine is often the first step in building rapport with Japanese colleagues or host families.
