## Overview

In this lesson, we move beyond the writing systems and into the heart of Japanese communication: social interaction. Learning how to greet others and introduce yourself is the most critical step for any beginner, as it establishes the polite tone required for navigating Japanese society. You will learn the essential "polite form" (*desu/masu*) that serves as the foundation for all N5-level communication.

By mastering these patterns, you will be able to initiate conversations, state your name and origin, and understand the basic structure of a Japanese sentence. This lesson bridges the gap between static vocabulary and active speech, preparing you for the more complex sentence structures and particle usage that follow in subsequent lessons.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:
- Use appropriate greetings based on the time of day and social context.
- Introduce yourself using the "A wa B desu" and "〜to iimasu" patterns.
- Ask simple questions using the question particle "ka."
- Express origin using the particle "kara" and the verb "kimasu."
- Understand the function of the topic particle "wa."

## Grammar Points

### 1. The Topic Particle "Wa" (は)
**Structure:** [Topic] + は + [Comment]

In Japanese, the particle **は (wa)** marks the topic of the sentence. It tells the listener what you are talking about. Note that although it is written with the character *ha*, it is pronounced *wa*.

*   **私は学生です。** (Watashi wa gakusei desu.) — As for me, I am a student.
*   **これは本です。** (Kore wa hon desu.) — As for this, it is a book.
*   **田中さんは先生です。** (Tanaka-san wa sensei desu.) — As for Tanaka-san, he is a teacher.

### 2. The Copula "Desu" (です)
**Structure:** [Noun] + です

The word **です (desu)** is the polite copula. It functions like "is," "am," or "are." It is placed at the very end of the sentence. Unlike English, it does not change based on the subject.

*   **私は田中です。** (Watashi wa Tanaka desu.) — I am Tanaka.
*   **彼は学生です。** (Kare wa gakusei desu.) — He is a student.
*   **それは辞書です。** (Sore wa jisho desu.) — That is a dictionary.

### 3. The Question Particle "Ka" (か)
**Structure:** [Sentence] + か

To turn a statement into a question, simply add the particle **か (ka)** at the end. In Japanese, you do not change the word order.

*   **学生ですか。** (Gakusei desu ka?) — Are you a student?
*   **田中さんですか。** (Tanaka-san desu ka?) — Are you Tanaka-san?
*   **これは何ですか。** (Kore wa nan desu ka?) — What is this?

### 4. Origin and Movement: "Kara" (から) and "Kimashita" (きました)
**Structure:** [Place] + から + 来ました

To say where you are from, use the particle **から (kara)**, meaning "from," followed by the verb **来ました (kimashita)**, meaning "came."

*   **アメリカから来ました。** (Amerika kara kimashita.) — I came from America.
*   **日本から来ました。** (Nihon kara kimashita.) — I came from Japan.
*   **どこから来ましたか。** (Doko kara kimashita ka?) — Where did you come from?

### 5. Introducing Yourself: "Desu" vs. "To iimasu"
*   **[Name] です (desu):** Simple identification. Used when someone asks your name.
*   **[Name] といいます (to iimasu):** Literally "I am called [Name]." This is more formal and sounds more natural in a self-introduction.

## Dialogues

### Dialogue 1: Meeting at School
**Tanaka**: はじめまして。田中です。
(Hajimemashite. Tanaka desu.) — Nice to meet you. I am Tanaka.

**Smith**: はじめまして。スミスです。アメリカから来ました。
(Hajimemashite. Sumisu desu. Amerika kara kimashita.) — Nice to meet you. I am Smith. I came from America.

**Tanaka**: 学生ですか。
(Gakusei desu ka?) — Are you a student?

**Smith**: はい、学生です。田中さんは？
(Hai, gakusei desu. Tanaka-san wa?) — Yes, I am a student. How about you, Tanaka-san?

**Tanaka**: 私も学生です。よろしくお願いします。
(Watashi mo gakusei desu. Yoroshiku onegaishimasu.) — I am also a student. Please treat me well.

## Cultural Note
In Japan, the phrase **"Yoroshiku onegaishimasu"** is essential. It essentially means "I am placing myself in your care." It acknowledges that social harmony is a collaborative effort. Additionally, always use the honorific **"-san"** after names to show respect. Never address someone by their name alone; it is considered rude.
