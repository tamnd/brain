## Overview

In this lesson, we transition from the "present/future" focus of earlier chapters to the "past." Being able to talk about what you did yesterday or how you felt last week is a fundamental milestone for any N5 learner. You will learn how to conjugate verbs, adjectives, and the copula (desu) to reflect completed actions and past states.

This lesson builds directly upon your knowledge of the polite *masu-form* and *desu* copula. By mastering these patterns, you will be able to recount your experiences, discuss your history, and clarify whether an action is finished or still ongoing. This is the bridge between simply stating facts and telling stories about your life.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson you can:
- Conjugate verbs into the past polite form (〜ました).
- Use the past tense for nouns and adjectives (〜でした).
- Express past experiences using the 〜たことがあります structure.
- Use time markers like "yesterday," "last week," and "last year" correctly.
- Distinguish between "already" (もう) and "not yet" (まだ).

## Grammar Points

### 1. Past Polite Tense (Verbs)

**Structure:** Verb-masu stem + ました / ませんでした

To turn a polite verb into the past tense, replace 〜ます (masu) with 〜ました (mashita) for affirmative, and 〜ません (masen) with 〜ませんでした (masen deshita) for negative.

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
|----------|---------|--------|---------|
| 食べました | たべました | tabemashita | I ate. |
| 行きませんでした | いきませんでした | ikimasen deshita | I did not go. |
| 買いました | かいました | kaimashita | I bought it. |
| 読みませんでした | よみませんでした | yomimasen deshita | I did not read. |

**Common mistake:** ❌ 食べましたた → ✓ 食べました — Do not double the past tense marker; the "mashita" suffix already contains the past meaning.

### 2. Past Tense (Nouns and Adjectives)

**Structure:** Noun/Na-Adj + でした / じゃありませんでした | I-Adj + かったです / くなかったです

For nouns and *na-adjectives*, replace です with でした. For *i-adjectives*, drop the final い and add かったです.

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
|----------|---------|--------|---------|
| 休みでした | やすみでした | yasumi deshita | It was a day off. |
| 暇じゃありませんでした | ひまじゃありませんでした | hima ja arimasen deshita | It was not free time. |
| 美味しかったです | おいしかったです | oishikatta desu | It was delicious. |
| 暑くなかったです | あつくなかったです | atsuku nakatta desu | It was not hot. |

### 3. Past Experience (〜たことがあります)

**Structure:** Verb (Plain Past) + ことがあります

This pattern is used to express that you have had a specific experience at some point in your life. Note that the verb must be in the "plain past" (ta-form) before ことがあります.

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
|----------|---------|--------|---------|
| 日本へ行ったことがあります | にほんへいったことがあります | nihon e itta koto ga arimasu | I have been to Japan. |
| 寿司を食べたことがあります | すしをたべたことがあります | sushi o tabeta koto ga arimasu | I have eaten sushi. |

## Dialogues

### Dialogue 1: Checking in on a friend
**Tanaka:** 佐藤さん、昨日は忙しかったですか。
(Satou-san, kinou wa isogashikatta desu ka?)
Satou-san, were you busy yesterday?

**Satou:** はい、とても忙しかったです。
(Hai, totemo isogashikatta desu.)
Yes, I was very busy.

**Tanaka:** もう宿題をしましたか。
(Mou shukudai o shimashita ka?)
Have you already done your homework?

**Satou:** いいえ、まだです。後でします。
(Iie, mada desu. Ato de shimasu.)
No, not yet. I will do it later.

### Dialogue 2: Talking about travel
**Ken:** 日本へ行ったことがありますか。
(Nihon e itta koto ga arimasu ka?)
Have you ever been to Japan?

**Yumi:** はい、去年行きました。
(Hai, kyonen ikimashita.)
Yes, I went last year.

**Ken:** 何を食べましたか。
(Nani o tabemashita ka?)
What did you eat?

**Yumi:** ラーメンを食べました。とても美味しかったです。
(Raamen o tabemashita. Totemo oishikatta desu.)
I ate ramen. It was very delicious.

## Cultural Note

In Japan, the concept of "already" (もう) and "not yet" (まだ) is deeply tied to social expectations. When someone asks "Mou tabemashita ka?" (Have you eaten yet?), it is often a polite way to initiate a conversation about food or to check if you are ready to proceed with an activity.

Additionally, when discussing the past, Japanese speakers often omit the subject (I/you) if it is clear from context. You will notice that "I" is rarely used in these sentences unless you are specifically emphasizing yourself. This reflects the high-context nature of Japanese communication, where the focus is on the action or state rather than the individual.
