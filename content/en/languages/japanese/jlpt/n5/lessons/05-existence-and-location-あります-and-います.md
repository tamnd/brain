## Overview

In this lesson, you will learn how to express the existence of things and people in Japanese. This is a fundamental skill for N5 learners, as it allows you to describe your surroundings, locate items, and talk about who is present in a room. You will move beyond simple "A is B" sentences and begin to build more complex descriptions of space.

Building on your knowledge of basic particles like *wa* and *ga*, you will now master the *ni* particle for location and the distinction between *arimasu* and *imasu*. These verbs form the backbone of descriptive Japanese and will prepare you for future lessons on giving directions and explaining complex scenes.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson you can:
- Distinguish between *arimasu* (inanimate) and *imasu* (animate) verbs.
- Use the particle *ni* to indicate the location of a person or object.
- Use spatial nouns (above, below, inside, etc.) to describe exact positions.
- Construct sentences describing where items are located in a room.

## Grammar Points

### 1. Existence: あります (arimasu) and います (imasu)

**Structure:** [Object/Person] + が + [Location] + に + [あります/います]

In Japanese, the verb for "to exist" changes based on whether the subject is alive or inanimate. Use **あります** for objects, plants, and non-living things. Use **います** for people and animals. The particle **に** marks the location where the subject exists.

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
|----------|---------|--------|---------|
| 机の上に本があります。 | つくえのうえにほんがあります。 | Tsukue no ue ni hon ga arimasu. | There is a book on the desk. |
| 教室に学生がいます。 | きょうしつにがくせいがいます。 | Kyōshitsu ni gakusei ga imasu. | There are students in the classroom. |
| 庭に猫がいます。 | にわにねこがいます。 | Niwa ni neko ga imasu. | There is a cat in the garden. |
| 鞄の中に鍵があります。 | かばんのなかにかぎがあります。 | Kaban no naka ni kagi ga arimasu. | There is a key in the bag. |

**Common mistake:** ❌ 椅子に猫があります。 → ✓ 椅子に猫がいます。 — *Neko* (cat) is animate, so you must use *imasu*.

### 2. Describing Location with Spatial Nouns

**Structure:** [Reference Point] + の + [Spatial Noun] + に + [Subject] + が + [あります/います]

To be specific about location, we use a reference point (like a desk) followed by the particle **の** and a spatial noun (like *ue* - above).

| Japanese | Reading | Romaji | English |
|----------|---------|--------|---------|
| 椅子の下に猫がいます。 | いすのしたにねこがいます。 | Isu no shita ni neko ga imasu. | The cat is under the chair. |
| 銀行の横に駅があります。 | ぎんこうのよこにえきがあります。 | Ginkō no yoko ni eki ga arimasu. | The station is next to the bank. |
| 友達の後ろに私がいます。 | ともだちのうしろにわたしがいます。 | Tomodachi no ushiro ni watashi ga imasu. | I am behind my friend. |
| 本屋の近くに公園があります。 | ほんやのちかくにこうえんがあります。 | Honya no chikaku ni kōen ga arimasu. | There is a park near the bookstore. |

## Dialogues

### Dialogue 1: Finding a lost item
[Setting: Two friends looking for a book in a library.]

**A:** 本はどこですか。 (ほんはどこですか。) / Hon wa doko desu ka. / Where is the book?
**B:** 机の上にあります。 (つくえのうえにあります。) / Tsukue no ue ni arimasu. / It is on the desk.
**A:** あ、本当だ。ありがとう。 (あ、ほんとうだ。ありがとう。) / A, hontō da. Arigatō. / Oh, you're right. Thanks.
**B:** いいえ。 (いいえ。) / Iie. / No problem.

### Dialogue 2: Looking for a pet
[Setting: A visitor looking for the family cat.]

**A:** 猫はどこにいますか。 (ねこはどこにいますか。) / Neko wa doko ni imasu ka. / Where is the cat?
**B:** 椅子の下にいます。 (いすのしたにいます。) / Isu no shita ni imasu. / It is under the chair.
**A:** かわいいですね。 (かわいいですね。) / Kawaii desu ne. / It's cute, isn't it?
**B:** はい、とてもかわいいです。 (はい、とてもかわいいです。) / Hai, totemo kawaii desu. / Yes, it is very cute.

**Answer Key:**

5. My friend is behind the chair.

## Cultural Note

In Japanese culture, the distinction between *arimasu* and *imasu* reflects a deep-seated animistic worldview where living things are categorized differently from inanimate objects. This linguistic habit reinforces the importance of acknowledging the "life force" or presence of living beings.

Additionally, when describing locations, Japanese speakers often use relative positions (e.g., "next to the bank") rather than absolute directions (e.g., "on 5th Avenue"). This reflects a cultural preference for landmarks and spatial relationships over abstract grid systems, making it essential to learn these spatial nouns to navigate Japan successfully.
