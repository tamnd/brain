Over **60% of English vocabulary** derives from Latin (directly or through Old French after the Norman Conquest in 1066), rising to **90%+ in technical, scientific, legal, and medical vocabulary**. Learning Latin dramatically expands your English vocabulary comprehension.

## How Latin Entered English

1. **Direct borrowings** (Latin → English): *legal, fact, animal, nature*
2. **French intermediary** (Latin → Old French → Middle English): *royal* (from Latin *regalis*), *reason* (from Latin *rationem*)
3. **Scientific Latin** (coined directly in Latin/Neo-Latin): *nucleus, oxygen, bacteria, data*
4. **Church Latin**: *altar, canon, clergy, verse*

Notable minimal pairs showing direct Latin vs. French-mediated borrowing:
| Latin Direct | French-Mediated | Latin Source |
|--------------|----------------|-------------|
| fragile | frail | *fragilis* |
| corona | crown | *corona* |
| ratio | reason | *ratio* |
| rotund | round | *rotundus* |
| capture | chase | *capere* |
| faction | fashion | *factio* |

## Practical Exercise: Finding Latin in English

Take any polysyllabic English word and try to find the Latin root:

- *communicate* → *com-* (together) + *mūnus* (gift, duty) → to share
- *education* → *ē-* (out) + *dūcere* (lead) → to lead out
- *manufacture* → *manus* (hand) + *facere* (make) → to make by hand
- *circumstance* → *circum* (around) + *stāre* (stand) → what stands around
- *vocabulary* → *vōx* (voice, word) + *-bulum* (means of) → collection of words
