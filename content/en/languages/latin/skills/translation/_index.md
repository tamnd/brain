Translation from Latin to English is the traditional core skill of Latin study. It is a valuable tool for checking comprehension and developing grammatical precision, but it should not be confused with the goal of Latin reading.

## Translation vs. Reading

| Skill | Process | Rate | Goal |
|-------|---------|------|------|
| Translation | Decode each word; produce English | 5–10 words/min | Grammar analysis; precision |
| Reading | Direct comprehension in Latin | 30–60 words/min | Fluency; engagement |

Use translation for:
- Grammar study (parsing and analyzing constructions)
- Checking your comprehension of difficult passages
- AP Latin and academic coursework
- Producing accurate text-specific interpretations

Don't use translation for:
- Building reading speed
- Developing intuitive Latin feel
- Getting through large amounts of text

## Translation Method

1. **Read the whole sentence in Latin first** — get the big picture before parsing individual words
2. **Identify the main verb** — it tells you tense, mood, voice, person, number
3. **Find the subject** — nominative noun or pronoun agreeing with the verb
4. **Find the direct object (if any)** — accusative noun
5. **Parse each remaining word** — its case tells you its syntactic function
6. **Render into natural English** — not word-for-word; Latin SOV becomes English SVO

## Example: Translating a Caesar Sentence

*Caesar, cum id nūntiātum esset, mātūrāvit ab urbe proficīscī.*

Step by step:
1. Main verb: *mātūrāvit* — "he hastened" (3rd sg. perfect active of *mātūrāre*)
2. Subject: *Caesar* (nominative)
3. Infinitive: *proficīscī* — "to set out" (deponent; complementary to *mātūrāvit*)
4. Prepositional phrase: *ab urbe* — "from the city"
5. *cum* clause: *id nūntiātum esset* — "when this had been announced" (cum + pluperfect passive subjunctive)

Translation: "Caesar, when this had been announced, hastened to set out from the city."

## Tools for Translation

| Tool | Use | URL |
|------|-----|-----|
| **Whitaker's Words** | Parse any inflected form | mk270.github.io/whitakers-words |
| **Logeion** | L&S + OLD definitions | logeion.uchicago.edu |
| **Perseus Word Study Tool** | Click-to-parse in context | perseus.tufts.edu |
| **Allen & Greenough** | Grammar constructions | dcc.dickinson.edu/grammar/latin |

## Translation Practice Resources

- **Perseus Digital Library** — most texts have parallel English translations; check your work
- **AP Latin past exams** — free response translation questions with sample answers
- **DCC Commentaries** — Caesar and other texts with detailed notes on constructions
- **Loeb Classical Library** — print/digital editions with facing Latin and English
