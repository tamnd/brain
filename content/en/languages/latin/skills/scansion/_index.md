## What Is Quantitative Meter?

Latin poetry uses **quantitative meter** — patterns of **long** (heavy) and **short** (light) syllables — rather than stress-based meter (which English uses). You cannot scan a line of Virgil or Catullus without knowing the length (quantity) of every syllable.

**Long syllable** (marked –): contains a long vowel or diphthong, OR is "long by position" (short vowel + 2 consonants)
**Short syllable** (marked ∪): contains a short vowel, not followed by two consonants

## Syllable Quantity Rules

### Long by Nature
A syllable is naturally long if it contains:
- A long vowel (marked with macron): ā, ē, ī, ō, ū
- A diphthong: ae, oe, au, eu, ei

### Long by Position
A syllable is long by position (even with a short vowel) if it contains:
- A short vowel followed by **two or more consonants** (even across a word boundary)
- Special pairs: qu counts as one consonant; x = ks (counts as two; makes previous syllable long)

### Short
A syllable is short if it contains a short vowel not followed by two consonants.

### Elision
When a word ends in a vowel (or vowel + -m) and the next word begins with a vowel or h, the final syllable of the first word is **elided** (suppressed) in verse — it doesn't count metrically.

*multum ille et terris* → *mult'ille et terris* (the final -um of multum is elided before *ille* beginning with a vowel)

## Dactylic Hexameter

The meter of Virgil's *Aeneid*, Ovid's *Metamorphoses*, Homer's epics, Horace's *Satires* and *Epistles*. The most important Latin meter.

**Structure**: 6 feet per line. Each foot is either a **dactyl** (– ∪ ∪) or a **spondee** (– –). The 5th foot is almost always a dactyl; the 6th foot is always a spondee (– –) or a disyllable.

Pattern: **( – ∪∪ / – ∪∪ / – ∪∪ / – ∪∪ / – ∪∪ / – – )**

Each dactyl (– ∪ ∪) can be replaced by a spondee (– –) except typically the 5th foot.

**Scanning procedure**:
1. Mark all vowel quantities (long by nature, diphthongs)
2. Apply long-by-position rule
3. Mark elisions
4. Divide into 6 feet, starting from the beginning
5. The line must end: foot 6 = 2 syllables (spondee or trochee)

**Example from Virgil, *Aeneid* 1.1**:
*Arma virumque canō, Trojae quī prīmus ab ōrīs*

```
Ar- ma vi- rum-que ca- nō Troi- ae quī prī- mus ab ō- rīs
–  ∪  ∪  –   –   ∪  ∪  –   –    –   –   ∪  ∪  –  –
1       2       3       4      5       6
```

## Elegiac Couplet

The meter of Catullus (some poems), Ovid (*Amores, Tristia, Heroides*), Tibullus, Propertius. A couplet consisting of:
1. **A dactylic hexameter** (same as above)
2. **A pentameter** (5-foot line, with a pause in the middle)

**Pentameter pattern**: – ∪∪ / – ∪∪ / – || – ∪∪ / – ∪∪ / –
(3 feet, then a mandatory pause, then 2 more dactyls, then a final long syllable)

The second half of the pentameter always consists of exactly two dactyls + one long syllable.

**Example from Ovid, *Amores* 1.1.1–2**:
Line 1 (hexameter): *Arma gravī numerō violentaque bella parābam*
Line 2 (pentameter): *ēdere, māteriā conveniente modīs*

## Hendecasyllable (Catullus)

11-syllable line; used extensively by Catullus (most of his shorter poems).

**Pattern**: – – / – ∪ ∪ / – ∪ / – ∪ / – –

The opening two syllables are always long (spondee); then a fixed sequence.

**Example from Catullus 1.1**: *Quōī dōnō lepidum novum libellum*

## Other Meters

| Meter | Pattern | Who Uses It |
|-------|---------|------------|
| **Alcaic** | Complex 4-line stanza | Horace *Odes* |
| **Sapphic** | 3 lines of 11 syll. + 1 of 5 | Horace *Odes*; Catullus 11, 51 |
| **Asclepiadean** | Various patterns | Horace *Odes* |
| **Iambic trimeter** | ∪– / ∪– / ∪– (6 feet) | Seneca *Tragedies*; Phaedrus *Fables* |
