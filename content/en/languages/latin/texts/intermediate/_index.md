## Intermediate Texts

### Caesar, *Bellum Gallicum* (Gallic War)

**Date**: 58–49 BCE | **Level**: Early Intermediate | **Genre**: Military memoir

The traditional first authentic text for most US Latin students. Caesar's own account of his conquest of Gaul. Clear, direct prose with relatively limited vocabulary.

**Characteristics**: Subject-Object-Verb order is typical; relatively simple subordinate clause structure; limited vocabulary (~2,500 words total for all 8 books); consistent grammatical usage.

**Best books to start with**:
- Book 1, chapters 1–7 (the opening: Helvetian migration) — very famous; clear
- Book 4, chapters 20–36 (first expedition to Britain) — shorter units
- Book 5 (the siege of Ambiorix) — dramatic narrative

**AP Latin**: Excerpts from Books 1, 4, 5, 6 are required for AP Latin.

**Resources**:
- DCC annotated edition with vocabulary: [dcc.dickinson.edu](https://dcc.dickinson.edu)
- Perseus text with word study tool: [perseus.tufts.edu](https://www.perseus.tufts.edu)
- Companion: *Caesar's Gallic War*, commentaries by Kelsey or Peskett

### Catullus (Selected Poems)

**Date**: ~84–54 BCE | **Level**: Intermediate | **Genre**: Lyric poetry (various meters)

The most diverse and personally revealing of Latin poets. Poems range from simple (1, 2, 3 — "Lesbia's sparrow") to complex (64 — the Peleus and Thetis epyllion). Most intermediate learners read selections.

**Best starting poems**:
- Poem 1 (dedication; hendecasyllables — easiest meter)
- Poem 2 (*Passer, deliciae meae puellae* — the sparrow)
- Poem 3 (death of the sparrow)
- Poem 5 (*Vivamus mea Lesbia atque amemus*)
- Poem 51 (translation of Sappho; direct declaration of love)
- Poem 85 (*Odi et amo* — 2-line poem; perfect for beginners)

**Note on meters**: Catullus uses many meters; the hendecasyllable (11-syllable line) is the most common and most accessible. You need basic scansion skills for Catullus.

### Ovid, *Metamorphoses*

**Date**: 8 CE | **Level**: Intermediate–Advanced | **Genre**: Epic poetry (dactylic hexameter)

15-book mythological epic covering transformations from creation to the deification of Julius Caesar. One of the most widely read Latin poems in both antiquity and modernity.

**Why Ovid before Virgil**: Ovid's hexameter is more regular and predictable than Virgil's; vocabulary and style more accessible; engaging mythological stories maintain motivation.

**Best starting sections**:
- Book 1.1–88 (Cosmogony — Creation)
- Book 1.452–567 (Apollo and Daphne)
- Book 3.138–252 (Actaeon)
- Book 4.55–166 (Pyramus and Thisbe)
- Book 6.1–145 (Arachne)

**Available**: Perseus; Dickinson edition for Books 10–11.

### Martial, Epigrams

**Date**: ~86–103 CE | **Level**: Early Intermediate | **Genre**: Satirical epigrams

Short (2–12 line), witty, often risqué epigrams. Ideal for intermediate learners: short texts with clear punchlines; vocabulary is straightforward; humor maintains motivation.

**Starting selection**: Book 1 epigrams 1–20 are the most accessible and the most anthologized.

---

### Pliny the Younger, Letters

**Date**: ~97–109 CE | **Level**: Intermediate | **Genre**: Epistolary prose

96 letters in 10 books covering life, culture, and events of the Roman Empire. Personal and conversational tone — the closest thing to readable Latin prose for intermediates.

**Famous letters** (excellent for class or self-study):
- **6.16** and **6.20**: Letters to Tacitus describing the eruption of Vesuvius and death of Pliny the Elder — the only eyewitness account of 79 AD eruption
- **9.6**: Pliny on dolphins; nature observation
- **10.96–97**: Correspondence with Emperor Trajan about early Christians — historically crucial

## Text Comparison

| Text | Prose/Verse | Difficulty | Best Entry Point |
|------|-------------|-----------|-----------------|
| Caesar *BG* | Prose | Easiest authentic prose | Book 1, ch. 1–7 |
| Pliny Letters | Prose | Accessible | 6.16, 6.20 (Vesuvius) |
| Martial Epigrams | Verse | Short; accessible | Book 1.1–20 |
| Catullus | Verse | Variable | Poems 2, 3, 5, 51, 85 |
| Ovid *Met.* | Verse | Intermediate | Book 1 (cosmogony); Apollo-Daphne |
