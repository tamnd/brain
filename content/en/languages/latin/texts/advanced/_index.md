## Advanced Texts

### Cicero (106–43 BCE)

The greatest Latin prose stylist. Complex periodic sentences, elaborate rhetorical structure, immense vocabulary. Mastering Cicero is the benchmark for Latin prose reading.

**Speeches** (rhetorical; most complex Ciceronian style):

| Text | Difficulty | Why |
|------|-----------|-----|
| *Pro Archia* | Moderate | Shorter; literary theme; good first Cicero |
| *In Catilinam* I | Moderate–High | Famous; dramatic; moderately complex |
| *In Catilinam* II–IV | High | Longer; more complex periods |
| *Pro Milone* | Very High | Most complex periodic style |

**Philosophical works** (less rhetorical complexity; very readable):

| Text | Length | Topic |
|------|--------|-------|
| *De Amicitia* | ~40 pages | On friendship |
| *De Senectute* | ~30 pages | On old age |
| *De Officiis* | ~120 pages | On duties/ethics |
| *Tusculan Disputations* | Long | Philosophy of death, pain, emotions |

**AP Latin**: *Pro Archia* and *In Catilinam* are frequently anthologized; students reading AP Latin should focus here.

### Virgil, *Aeneid* (19 BCE)

The pinnacle of the Latin literary canon. 12-book epic in dactylic hexameter following Aeneas from the fall of Troy to Italy. Dense poetic syntax, allusive diction, profound emotional depth.

**Warning**: The Aeneid is the hardest step up for most Latin readers. Allow more time per page than for any other text.

**Books by difficulty and content**:

| Book | Content | Recommended Order |
|------|---------|-------------------|
| Book 1 | Storm, Dido's welcome to Aeneas | Start here |
| Book 2 | Fall of Troy (Aeneas's narrative) | Second; dramatically gripping |
| Book 4 | Dido and Aeneas; Dido's death | Third; most emotionally powerful |
| Book 6 | Descent to the Underworld | Fourth; philosophical depth |
| Book 8 | Evander's Rome; Shield of Aeneas | Intermediate difficulty |
| Book 12 | Final battle; death of Turnus | Climax |

**AP Latin requires**: Books 1, 2, 4, 6 in Latin; Books 1, 2, 4, 6, 8, 12 in English.

**Resources**: Perseus; Pharr's *Vergil's Aeneid Books I–VI* (best teaching edition with complete vocabulary).

---

### Livy, *Ab Urbe Condita* (From the Foundation of the City)

**Date**: ~27 BCE – 17 CE | **Genre**: History | **Scope**: 142 books (35 survive)

Roman history from the founding of Rome to 9 BCE. Complex narrative prose, less difficult than Cicero's speeches.

**Best starting points**:
- Book 1 (founding myths: Romulus and Remus, the rape of Lucretia, etc.) — accessible
- Books 21–22 (Second Punic War, Hannibal crossing the Alps) — dramatic; most widely taught

### Horace (65–8 BCE)

**Odes** (4 books, Alcaic, Sapphic, Asclepiadean, and other meters):
The hardest classical Latin poetry to read because of dense lyric meters, compressed diction, and allusion. Begin with the Sapphic ode 1.22 (*Integer vitae*) — the most anthologized.

**Satires and Epistles** (dactylic hexameter):
Much more accessible than the Odes. Conversational tone; hexameter is familiar from Virgil. *Epistles* 1.1 and 2.3 (*Ars Poetica*) are widely read.

---

### Tacitus (56–120 CE)

The hardest classical Latin prose. Tacitus's style is characterized by extreme compression, archaic vocabulary, deliberate obscurity, and psychological depth. *Annals* covers the Julio-Claudian emperors; *Histories* covers 69–70 CE.

**For first-time Tacitus readers**: *Agricola* (biography of his father-in-law) — shorter, more accessible style, complete narrative.

**After *Agricola***: *Annals* Book 1, then *Annals* Book 4, then *Histories* Book 1.

**Resources**: DCC annotated edition of *Agricola*: [dcc.dickinson.edu](https://dcc.dickinson.edu).

---

### Seneca the Younger (4 BCE–65 CE)

**Moral Letters** (*Epistulae Morales*): 124 letters to Lucilius on Stoic philosophy. Conversational, direct, accessible prose — far easier than Tacitus. Ideal for advanced learners who want philosophical content.

**Tragedies** (9 plays in verse): *Medea*, *Thyestes*, *Oedipus*, and others. Complex tragic meter (iambic trimeter, various lyric meters); darker than the prose works.

---

### Sallust (86–35 BCE)

**Catiline** (*Bellum Catilinae*) and **Jugurtha** (*Bellum Jugurthinum*): Roman history in a highly compressed, archaic style that Tacitus later imitated. *Catiline* is the better entry point and directly complements reading Cicero's Catilinarians.

## Difficulty Ranking (Advanced Level)

From most to least difficult:
1. **Persius** (*Satires*) — notoriously obscure; the hardest classical Latin
2. **Tacitus** (*Annals*/*Histories*) — compressed, archaic, psychologically dense
3. **Horace** (*Odes*) — complex lyric meters + dense allusion
4. **Virgil** (*Aeneid*) — complex hexameter + mythological allusion
5. **Cicero** (major speeches) — complex periodic sentences; large vocabulary
6. **Sallust** — compressed, archaic; imitates Thucydides
7. **Livy** — long narrative; manageable periodic style
8. **Cicero** (philosophical works) — most accessible Cicero
9. **Pliny** (*Epistles*) — most readable advanced Latin
10. **Seneca** (*Moral Letters*) — conversational Stoic philosophy
