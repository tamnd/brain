Verbals are forms derived from verbs that function as nouns, adjectives, or adverbs. Latin has more verbal forms than almost any other language.

## Infinitives (6 Forms)

| | Active | Passive |
|---|---|---|
| **Present** | *amāre* — to love | *amārī* — to be loved |
| **Perfect** | *amāvisse* — to have loved | *amātus esse* — to have been loved |
| **Future** | *amātūrus esse* — to be about to love | *amātum īrī* — to be about to be loved |

**Uses**:
- **Complementary infinitive**: completes the meaning of verbs like *possum, dēbeō, volō, nōlō, coepiō, dēsinō* — *possum venīre* "I am able to come"
- **Indirect statement (ACI)**: after verbs of saying/thinking — see [Syntax](../syntax/)
- **Historical infinitive**: present infinitive replaces imperfect indicative in rapid narrative for vividness — *hostēs fugere, mīlitēs sequī* — "The enemies were fleeing, the soldiers pursuing"
- **Subject/object**: *errāre est hūmānum* — "To err is human"

## Participles (4 Forms)

| Participle | Formation | Declension | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Present Active** | present stem + -ns/-ntis | 3rd decl. adj. | "loving" (while doing) |
| **Perfect Passive (PPP)** | 4th principal part | 1st-2nd decl. adj. | "having been loved" |
| **Future Active** | PPP stem + -ūrus/-a/-um | 1st-2nd decl. adj. | "about to love" |
| **Gerundive (Future Passive)** | present stem + -ndus/-a/-um | 1st-2nd decl. adj. | "needing to be loved" |

*Note*: Latin has **no present passive participle** and **no perfect active participle**. This gap is significant — it's why the ablative absolute uses the PPP (which is passive) and the gerundive.

### Present Active Participle (*amāns, amantis*)

Declines as a 3rd-declension adjective (one termination):
- Nom. sg.: *amāns* (m/f/n same)
- Gen. sg.: *amantis*
- Abl. sg.: *amante* (predicative) or *amantī* (attributive)

**Usage**: Describes ongoing action simultaneous with the main verb.
- *puer cantāns intrāvit* — "the singing boy entered" / "the boy, while singing, entered"
- *mīlitēs pugnantēs vīdī* — "I saw the soldiers fighting"

### Perfect Passive Participle (PPP)

Declines as a 1st-2nd declension adjective (*amātus, amāta, amātum*).
**Must agree** with the noun it modifies in gender, number, case.

**Uses**:
- Attributive: *milēs vulnerātus* — "the wounded soldier"
- In ablative absolute: *oppidō captō* — "the town having been captured"
- In passive periphrastic (with *esse*): *amātus est* — "he was loved" (perfect passive)

### Future Active Participle

Declines as *amātūrus, amātūra, amātūrum*.
Expresses an action **about to happen** or **intended**.

- *Caesar dictūrus erat* — "Caesar was about to speak"
- Used in the active periphrastic: *dictūrus sum* — "I am about to say"

### Gerundive (Future Passive Participle)

Declines as *amandus, amanda, amandum*. Expresses **necessity or obligation** (someone or something needs to be done).

**Passive Periphrastic** (gerundive + *esse*): Expresses obligation; agent in dative (*dative of agent*).
- *liber legendus est* — "The book must be read" (lit. "The book is needing-to-be-read")
- *mihi eundum est* — "I must go" (lit. "It is going-to-be-gone by me")
- *tibi haec facienda erant* — "You had to do these things"

**Gerundive vs. Gerund**: Both use -ndus/-ndus stem, but:
- **Gerundive** is an adjective — agrees with a noun
- **Gerund** is a noun — only appears in oblique cases (gen., dat., acc., abl.)

## Gerund

A **verbal noun** (neuter 2nd declension). Appears only in oblique cases; the nominative is supplied by the infinitive.

| Case | Form | Use | Example |
|------|------|-----|---------|
| Gen. | *amandī* | of loving | *ars amandī* — "the art of loving" |
| Dat. | *amandō* | for loving | *amandō vacat* — "he has leisure for loving" |
| Acc. | *amandum* | loving (object of *ad*) | *ad amandum* — "for the purpose of loving" |
| Abl. | *amandō* | by/in/through loving | *amandō beātus est* — "he is happy by loving" |

**Gerund-Gerundive Replacement Rule**: When the gerund would take an object, Latin usually prefers the gerundive construction:
- Gerund with object: *legendum librum* (unusual)
- Preferred: *liber legendus* (gerundive agrees with *liber*)
- *causa bellum gerendi* → preferred: *causa gerendi bellī* → actually preferred: *causa bellī gerendī* — "for the sake of waging war"

## Supine

A 4th-declension verbal noun. Rare; only two cases used:

| Case | Form | Use | Example |
|------|------|-----|---------|
| **Acc.** | *amātum* | purpose after verbs of motion | *lūsum iērunt* — "they went to play" |
| **Abl.** | *amātū* | with certain adjectives | *mīrābile dictū* — "wonderful to say"; *difficile factū* — "difficult to do" |

The accusative supine expresses **purpose** (like a purpose clause or *ad* + gerund/gerundive) but is restricted to verbs of motion.
