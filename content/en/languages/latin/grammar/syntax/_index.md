Latin syntax is where the language becomes most challenging and most powerful. This page covers the constructions that appear in all classical prose and poetry.

## Ablative Absolute

A noun or pronoun + a participle, **both in the ablative case**, forming an adverbial phrase independent of the main clause. It can express time, cause, condition, or concession.

**Structure**: [Noun/Pronoun (abl.) + Participle (abl.)]

| Example | Literally | Natural Translation |
|---------|-----------|---------------------|
| *hīs rēbus gestīs* | these things having been accomplished | After these things were done / When these things were done |
| *Caesare duce* | Caesar being leader | Under Caesar's leadership / With Caesar as leader |
| *sole oriente* | the sun rising | At sunrise / When the sun rises |
| *hoste victo* | the enemy having been defeated | Since the enemy was defeated |

**Rule**: The noun in the ablative absolute must NOT be the subject or object of the main clause. If it is the same person/thing, use a regular participle.

*Wrong*: *Caesar, hostibus victis, discessit* — if Caesar was doing the beating, use: *Caesar hostes vincens discessit* (while defeating)
*Right*: *Caesar, hostibus victis, discessit* — "Caesar, the enemies having been defeated, departed" (enemies ≠ Caesar)

**Special case**: When no participle is available (Latin has no present passive or perfect active participle of *esse*), a **noun + noun** or **noun + adjective** ablative absolute is used: *Romulo rege* — "Romulus being king."

## Indirect Statement (ACI — *Accusativus cum Infinitivo*)

After verbs of **saying, thinking, knowing, perceiving, or declaring**, the subject of the reported thought goes into the **accusative** and the verb becomes an **infinitive**.

**Structure**: [Main verb of saying/thinking] + [Subject (acc.)] + [Infinitive]

**Tense of the infinitive is relative to the main verb**:
- **Present infinitive**: same time as main verb
- **Perfect infinitive**: prior to main verb
- **Future infinitive**: subsequent to main verb

| Latin | Breakdown | Translation |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| *Dīcō Caesarem venīre* | say + Caesar (acc.) + come (pres. inf.) | "I say that Caesar is coming" |
| *Dīcō Caesarem vēnisse* | say + Caesar (acc.) + come (perf. inf.) | "I say that Caesar came" |
| *Dīcō Caesarem ventūrum esse* | say + Caesar (acc.) + come (fut. inf.) | "I say that Caesar will come" |
| *Dīxit Caesarem venīre* | said + Caesar (acc.) + come (pres. inf.) | "He said that Caesar was coming" |
| *Scīvī tē errāvisse* | I knew + you (acc.) + err (perf. inf.) | "I knew that you had erred" |

**Reflexive pronoun** *sē* (acc.) refers to the **subject of the main verb**, not the infinitive's subject: *Caesar dīxit sē venīre* — "Caesar said that he himself was coming."

**Common verbs introducing ACI**: *dīcere* (say), *putāre* (think), *crēdere* (believe), *scīre* (know), *sentīre* (feel/perceive), *vidēre* (see), *audīre* (hear), *nūntiāre* (announce), *affirmare* (affirm), *negāre* (deny — introduces ACI with positive infinitive!)

## Conditional Sentences

Six types of conditional sentences in Latin:

| Type | Name | If-Clause (Protasis) | Then-Clause (Apodosis) | Example |
|------|------|---------------------|------------------------|---------|
| 1 | **Simple Fact** | Indicative (any tense) | Indicative (any tense) | *Sī id facis, errās* — "If you do this, you err" |
| 2 | **Future More Vivid (FMV)** | Fut. or Fut. Perf. Indic. | Future Indicative | *Sī id faciēs, errābis* — "If you do this, you will err" |
| 3 | **Future Less Vivid (FLV)** | Present Subjunctive | Present Subjunctive | *Sī id faciās, errēs* — "If you were to do this, you would err" |
| 4 | **Present Contrary-to-Fact** | Imperfect Subjunctive | Imperfect Subjunctive | *Sī id facerēs, errārēs* — "If you were doing this (but you aren't), you would be erring" |
| 5 | **Past Contrary-to-Fact** | Pluperfect Subjunctive | Pluperfect Subjunctive | *Sī id fēcissēs, errāvissēs* — "If you had done this (but you didn't), you would have erred" |
| 6 | **Mixed** | Pluperf. Subj. or Imperf. Subj. | Imperf. Subj. or Pluperf. Subj. | *Sī id fēcissēs, nunc errārēs* — "If you had done this (past), you would now be erring (present)" |

**Memory aid for CTF types**: The contrary-to-fact types use imperfect (present CTF) and pluperfect (past CTF) subjunctive in BOTH clauses.

## Subjunctive Uses in Dependent Clauses

### Purpose Clauses (*ut/nē* + subjunctive)

Answers "why?" — the purpose of the main action.

| Example | Translation |
|---------|-------------|
| *Venit ut vidēret* | "He came in order to see" / "He came so that he might see" |
| *Venit nē vidērētur* | "He came so that he might not be seen" |
| *Lēgātōs mīsit quī pācem peterent* | "He sent ambassadors to seek peace" (relative purpose clause) |

Sequence: primary main verb → present subjunctive; historic main verb → imperfect subjunctive.

### Result Clauses (*ut/ut nōn* + subjunctive)

Answers "what was the result?" — introduced by *tam, ita, sic, adeō, tantus, tālis* in the main clause.

| Example | Translation |
|---------|-------------|
| *Tam fortis erat ut vincī nōn posset* | "He was so brave that he could not be defeated" |
| *Adeō territus est ut fugeret* | "He was so terrified that he fled" |

**Distinguish from purpose**: Purpose = deliberate goal; Result = actual consequence. Purpose uses *nē* for negative; Result uses *ut nōn*.

### Fear Clauses (*timeō nē/ut* + subjunctive)

**Opposite polarity**: *nē* = "that" (fears it will happen); *ut* = "that not" (fears it won't happen).

| Example | Translation |
|---------|-------------|
| *Timeō nē veniat* | "I fear that he is coming" |
| *Timeō ut veniat* | "I fear that he is not coming" |

### Indirect Questions (question word + subjunctive)

After verbs of asking, knowing, showing, wondering — the embedded question takes the subjunctive.

| Example | Translation |
|---------|-------------|
| *Rogāvit quis esset* | "He asked who he was" |
| *Nescio quid faciās* | "I don't know what you are doing" |
| *Miror cur discesserit* | "I wonder why he left" |

### *Cum* Clauses

*Cum* + subjunctive has three distinct uses:

| Use | Tense of Subj. | Meaning | Example |
|-----|----------------|---------|---------|
| **Temporal** (when) | Impf. or Plupf. Subj. | historical "when" (circumstance) | *cum Caesar vēnisset, loquēbātur* — "when Caesar had come, he was speaking" |
| **Causal** (since/because) | Any tense Subj. | gives the reason | *cum hoc vērum esset, discessimus* — "since this was true, we left" |
| **Concessive** (although) | Any tense + *tamen* in apodosis | contrary to expectation | *cum hoc vērum esset, tamen discessit* — "although this was true, he nevertheless left" |

*Cum* + indicative = simple time clause ("when," "whenever," "as often as").

### Indirect Commands (*ut/nē* + subjunctive)

After verbs of commanding, asking, urging, persuading — the content of the command takes *ut/nē* + subjunctive.

| Example | Translation |
|---------|-------------|
| *Imperāvit ut discēderent* | "He ordered that they depart" / "He ordered them to depart" |
| *Ōrāvit nē dīceret* | "He begged that he not speak" |

**Common verbs**: *imperāre, rogāre, hortārī, ōrāre, persuādēre, monēre, petere*

### Relative Clauses of Characteristic

When the antecedent is **indefinite, general, or negative**, the relative clause takes the subjunctive.

| Example | Translation |
|---------|-------------|
| *Nēmō est quī hoc nesciat* | "There is no one who does not know this" |
| *Sunt quī putent* | "There are those who think" |
| *Quis est quī hoc velīt?* | "Who is there who would want this?" |

## Sequence of Tenses

The tense of the subjunctive in a dependent clause follows the tense of the main verb:

| Main Verb Tense | Sequence | Pres. Subj. (same time) | Perf. Subj. (prior time) |
|----------------|----------|------------------------|-------------------------|
| Present, Future, Fut. Perf. | **Primary** | Present | Perfect |
| Imperfect, Perfect, Pluperfect | **Historic** | Imperfect | Pluperfect |

*Exception*: The perfect indicative can follow either primary or historic sequence depending on its meaning (perfect of completed action with present result → primary; simple past narrative → historic).
