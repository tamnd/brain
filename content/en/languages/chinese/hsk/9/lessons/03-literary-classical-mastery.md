## Overview

Full mastery of Classical Chinese literary culture is the apex of Chinese language attainment, requiring not only the ability to parse difficult 文言文 but to engage with classical literature aesthetically, to produce texts in classical registers when called upon, and to understand the continuous conversation that Chinese literary culture maintains with its own past. The touchstone anthology for this mastery is the 《古文观止》, the seventeenth-century canonical collection of classical prose whose title — "classical writing perfected, nothing need be added" — encapsulates the standard. At HSK 9, the learner is expected to approach this tradition not as an outsider decoding a foreign language but as a participant in a living literary culture that extends from the Book of Songs through Tang poetry to contemporary writers who deliberately engage classical forms. This lesson addresses the highest dimensions of classical Chinese literacy: 骈文 (parallel prose), classical poetry as a productive genre, and the critical vocabulary of the traditional 诗话 tradition.

## Competency Goals

- Read annotated and unannotated classical Chinese prose of high difficulty (Six Dynasties 骈文, Han 赋, Tang 古文 in the Han Yu tradition) without significant loss of meaning.
- Analyze a Tang or Song poem using the native critical vocabulary of 诗话 (鉴赏, 境界, 炼字, 押韵, 平仄, 对仗), identifying how specific technical choices create aesthetic effects.
- Produce a short composition in classical prose register (approximately 100 characters) on a given topic, using correct 文言文 grammar and appropriate vocabulary.
- Engage with 《古文观止》 anthology as a sustained reading experience, understanding the editorial logic of the collection and its role in constructing the classical literary canon.
- Understand the continuing presence of classical Chinese literary forms in contemporary writing, public life, and cultural production, including 对联, ceremonial addresses, and inscriptions.

## Linguistic Analysis

### 骈文: The Grammar of Parallel Prose

骈文 (parallel prose) reached its apex in the Six Dynasties period (220-589 CE) and has been alternately celebrated (for its beauty and craft) and criticized (for sacrificing meaning to form). Its fundamental grammar is the paired couplet: every clause must be matched by a syntactically parallel clause of the same length, with corresponding grammatical categories (noun/noun, verb/verb, adjective/adjective) and, in the most demanding practice, corresponding tonal patterns. The effect at its best is an incantatory, architectural beauty; at its worst, meaningless ornament.

The 《滕王阁序》of Wang Bo (王勃, 650-676 CE) is universally cited as the masterwork of Tang 骈文. Its most famous couplet: 落霞与孤鹜齐飞，秋水共长天一色 — "Falling clouds and the lone duck fly together; autumn water shares one color with the vast sky." The grammatical parallelism: 落霞(N)与(conj)孤鹜(N)齐飞(VP) / 秋水(N)共(conj)长天(N)一色(VP). The semantic parallelism maps to visual space: vertical (flying) in the first clause, horizontal (color spreading) in the second. The couplet is studied in Chinese secondary education and is one of the most quoted lines in the language.

Graded examples:
1. Simple 骈文 couplet: 夕阳无限好，只是近黄昏 — this is actually a 绝句 couplet, not technical 骈文, but illustrates parallel structure.
2. Technical 骈文: 潦水尽而寒潭清，烟光凝而暮山紫 — (from 《滕王阁序》) Two matched clauses: 潦水尽/烟光凝 (subject + verb), 而 (conjunction), 寒潭清/暮山紫 (subject + adjective). Visual: water clarifying, mountain purpling.
3. Modern deployment: A contemporary memorial inscription deploys 骈文 couplets to signal formal, ceremonial register and to invoke the classical literary tradition.

### Tang Poetry: The Craft of 炼字

The Tang dynasty produced what most Chinese literary critics and educated readers consider the highest achievement of Chinese poetry, and the technical craft of Tang regulated verse (律诗 and 绝句) is a subject of inexhaustible study. The concept of 炼字 — "word refinement," the poet's art of selecting the single most precise and resonant character for a given position — is central to classical poetics. The famous story of Jia Dao (贾岛) debating with Han Yu whether to use 推 ("push") or 敲 ("knock") in a line about a monk at a moonlit gate has given Chinese literary culture the idiom 推敲 (to weigh options, to revise carefully), illustrating how seriously this craft was taken.

Wang Anshi's most cited example of 炼字 is his use of 绿 in the line 春风又绿江南岸 — "The spring wind has again made the Jiangnan shore green." 绿 here is used as a causative verb (to make green), not an adjective. Wang Anshi is said to have cycled through 到, 过, 满, 入 before settling on 绿, because 绿 alone captures the active process of spring arriving and making things green — it is a verb of transformation rather than mere description. This is 炼字 at its most consequential.

Graded examples:
1. Simple 炼字 analysis: Du Fu's 感时花溅泪，恨别鸟惊心 — 溅 (splash) and 惊 (startle) are verbalized metaphors: flowers splash tears, birds startle the heart. The active verb choice intensifies the pathetic fallacy.
2. Tonal 炼字: In a regulated verse line, the character chosen must meet both semantic and tonal requirements (平/仄 at specific positions). A word perfect in meaning but wrong in tone cannot be used — the craft of 炼字 must satisfy both simultaneously.
3. Expert analysis: Li Shangyin's 春蚕到死丝方尽，蜡炬成灰泪始干 — 丝 (silk threads) is a homophone for 思 (longing), making the entire image double. 炼字 here operates at the level of phonological resonance, not just semantic precision.

### Classical Poetry Reading: The 诗话 Critical Method

Traditional Chinese literary criticism developed in the 诗话 genre: discursive, anecdotal prose essays in which poets and critics offered aesthetic judgments, reported authorial intentions, and debated questions of taste. The 诗话 method is not systematic theory but cultivated judgment: it names qualities (清, 沉郁, 飘逸) and argues for their value through exemplification. Wang Guowei's 《人间词话》is the most philosophically systematic of the classical 诗话 works, organizing traditional aesthetic judgment around the central concept of 境界 (artistic realm/world).

Wang Guowei's theory: a poem achieves 境界 when it creates a world that is fully real (有我之境 or 无我之境), where images and emotions are completely fused rather than merely juxtaposed. 有我之境 is the mode where the observer's emotion transforms the scene (泪眼问花花不语，乱红飞过秋千去 — "I asked the flowers with tearful eyes, but the flowers did not answer; the scattered petals flew past the swing"); 无我之境 is the mode where the observer dissolves into the scene without imposing emotion (采菊东篱下，悠然见南山 — "Picking chrysanthemums by the eastern fence, I see South Mountain in the distance" — the seeing is pure and unweighted).

Graded examples:
1. 有我之境: Li Yu's 问君能有几多愁，恰似一江春水向东流 — emotion actively shapes the image (sorrow = a river flowing east).
2. 无我之境: Wang Wei's 明月松间照，清泉石上流 — moon shines, spring flows; the observer is present but absent.
3. 境界 analysis in critical prose: 此词境界阔大，意脉绵长，以自然意象映照人生忧患，而不著一字感叹，所谓"不著一字，尽得风流"是也。

## Authentic Corpus Text

The following is the most celebrated passage of 《滕王阁序》by Wang Bo (王勃), considered the finest 骈文 in the Tang collection:

> 豫章故郡，洪都新府。星分翼轸，地接衡庐。襟三江而带五湖，控蛮荆而引瓯越。物华天宝，龙光射牛斗之墟；人杰地灵，徐孺下陈蕃之榻。雄州雾列，俊采星驰。台隍枕夷夏之交，宾主尽东南之美。
>
> 落霞与孤鹜齐飞，秋水共长天一色。渔舟唱晚，响穷彭蠡之滨；雁阵惊寒，声断衡阳之浦。

**Translation:** "An ancient commandery of Yuzhang, a new prefecture of Hongdu: the stars of Wing and Axle-pin divide here; the land adjoins Heng Mountain and Mount Lu. It clasps the Three Rivers at its collar and belts the Five Lakes; it controls the Man-Jing region and draws in Ou-Yue. Its material wealth is heaven's treasure: the dragon-light shoots to the constellation of Ox and Dipper's void. Its people are heroes and its land is numinous: Xu Ru descended to rest on Chen Fan's specially unrolled couch. Grand prefectures arrayed like mist; brilliant talent racing like stars. The ramparts straddle the meeting of Yi and Xia; guests and hosts exhaust all the beauty of the southeast. Falling clouds and the lone duck fly together; autumn water shares one color with the vast sky. The fishing boats sing in the evening, their voices reaching to the shores of Poyang Lake; the formations of geese are startled by the cold, their cries breaking off at the shores of Hengyang."

**Linguistic commentary:** The passage exemplifies 骈文 at its technical height. Every clause is matched: 豫章故郡/洪都新府 (place + historical adjective + noun), 星分翼轸/地接衡庐 (subject + verb + object). The celebrated couplet 落霞与孤鹜齐飞/秋水共长天一色 deploys 与/共 as paired conjunctions, 齐飞/一色 as parallel predicates, and exploits the contrast between movement (飞) and stasis (色) to create dimensional depth. The density of allusion (徐孺下陈蕃之榻 refers to a specific story of the Eastern Han scholar Xu Ru whom the official Chen Fan honored with a specially lowered couch reserved only for him) requires annotated reading for most modern readers. Yet the visual couplet is immediately apprehensible even without allusive knowledge.

## Critical Questions

1. The couplet 落霞与孤鹜齐飞，秋水共长天一色 has been described as the most beautiful in Chinese literature. Attempt to analyze what specifically creates this aesthetic effect: the visual content, the grammatical structure, the sound, the spatial movement, or some combination. Can you isolate the decisive element, or is the beauty an emergent property of the whole?
2. Wang Guowei distinguishes 有我之境 (where the poet's emotion shapes the scene) from 无我之境 (where the poet dissolves into the scene). Using two poems you know well, demonstrate that this distinction is productive: that it identifies a genuinely different aesthetic mode in each. Then challenge the distinction: can you find a poem where both modes are simultaneously present?
3. The 《古文观止》 anthology was compiled with specific editorial criteria: what constitutes "perfection" in classical prose. What does the selection of texts reveal about the aesthetic values and cultural assumptions of the editors (Wu Chucai and Wu Diaohou, writing in the early Qing dynasty)? What might a different editor include or exclude, and what would this reveal about different constructions of the classical literary canon?
4. The concept of 炼字 implies that there is a single best word for any given position in a poem. Is this a defensible aesthetic position, or does it imply a view of language (as a fixed system with determinate meaning) that is philosophically problematic? How might a contemporary linguistic view of meaning and context challenge the 炼字 ideal?
5. The 骈文 tradition was criticized in the 古文运动 (Tang and Song classical prose movement) for sacrificing meaning to form. Is this criticism valid? Using the Wang Bo passage as evidence, make the best possible case both for and against 骈文's aesthetic value.

## Advanced Production Task

This lesson's production task has two parts.

Part 1: Write a 100-character original composition in classical Chinese prose register on one of these topics: (a) a farewell to a departing friend; (b) a reflection on the beauty of a specific season; (c) a brief meditation on the nature of learning. Use correct 文言文 grammar throughout (including at minimum one 者...也 construction, one use of 之 as a structural particle, and one sentence-final particle). You may use modern vocabulary items where necessary, but the grammar and register must be classical.

Part 2: Annotate a classical poem of your choice (Tang, Song, or Ming-Qing) using the 诗话 critical method. Write 200-250 Chinese characters of critical commentary addressing: (a) the poem's 境界 — what world or aesthetic space does it create? (b) one specific instance of 炼字 — identify the key word and analyze its effect; (c) your overall aesthetic judgment of the poem, stated with the confidence and specificity of a 诗话 entry, not the hedged language of an academic analysis. The tone should be that of an informed connoisseur making a personal aesthetic judgment, not a student summarizing existing scholarship.

## Scholarly Note

The place of classical Chinese literary culture in contemporary China is a topic of active political, educational, and cultural debate. The secondary school curriculum has substantially increased the proportion of classical literature since the 2017 curriculum reform, reflecting an official cultural policy of 传承与弘扬中华优秀传统文化 (inheriting and promoting excellent traditional Chinese culture). This has intensified the presence of 文言文 in examination preparation and in cultural discourse, but has also raised questions about the relationship between classical literacy and genuine cultural engagement — whether the examination-driven reading of classical texts produces authentic aesthetic appreciation or merely instrumental text-decoding.

From a sinological perspective, the tradition of 古文观止 and the 诗话 critical method represent one of the most developed and internally coherent literary critical traditions in world culture, yet they remain largely unknown outside East Asian academic circles. The concepts of 境界, 神韵, 风骨, and 兴象 do not have adequate equivalents in Western literary criticism, and the attempt to apply Western critical frameworks (structuralism, reader-response theory, new historicism) to classical Chinese texts often generates more distortion than illumination. Scholars such as Stephen Owen, whose work on Tang poetry combines sinological depth with comparative literary sophistication, and 叶嘉莹 (Ye Jiaying), whose lifelong project has been to articulate a non-Western phenomenological aesthetics adequate to classical Chinese verse, have done the most to build a critical language for classical Chinese literature that honors its internal logic while remaining accessible to readers outside the tradition. Their work represents the most ambitious current attempt to answer the question that this lesson poses at its deepest level: what does it mean to truly master classical Chinese literary culture?
