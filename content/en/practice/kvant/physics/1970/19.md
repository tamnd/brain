## Problem

![Fig. 1](//www.kvant.digital/media/common/1970-11-038-02.png?_v=2)

Fig. 1

The shape of the communicating vessels is shown in Figure 1. In which direction will the water flow through the tube connecting the vessels if the water in one of the vessels is heated?

## Setup and Assumptions

The system consists of two communicating vessels connected by a horizontal tube near their bottoms. The left vessel has a narrow upper part, while the right vessel has a wider upper part, as shown in the figure. Both vessels are initially filled with water and open to the atmosphere.

Let the initial temperature of the water be $T$, and suppose the water in one vessel is heated uniformly by $\Delta T$. The density changes from $\rho$ to

$$\rho'=\rho-\Delta\rho,$$

with $\Delta\rho>0$.

Let the initial height of the water column in the heated vessel be $h$. If its cross sectional area is $S$, its initial mass is

$$m=\rho Sh.$$

The heated vessel is assumed to contain water of uniform density $\rho'$. Viscosity affects only the transient motion and does not determine the direction of flow. The vessels are rigid and stationary.

## Physical Principles

Hydrostatic equilibrium gives

$$p=p_0+\rho gh,$$

where $p_0$ is atmospheric pressure.

Water flows through the connecting tube from the side where the pressure at the tube level is larger toward the side where it is smaller.

Heating changes the density and hence the volume,

$$V=\frac{m}{\rho}.$$

If the vessel has cross sectional area $S$, a volume increment $\Delta V$ raises the free surface by

$$\Delta h=\frac{\Delta V}{S}.$$

The pressure difference between the two vessels must be obtained from the actual hydrostatic pressures at the tube level.

## Derivation

Assume first that the water in the left vessel is heated. Its density changes from $\rho$ to $\rho'=\rho-\Delta\rho$. The volume increase is

$$\Delta V = m\left(\frac1{\rho'}-\frac1\rho\right) \approx m\frac{\Delta\rho}{\rho^2}.$$

Since

$$m=\rho S_1h_1,$$

this becomes

$$\Delta V = \frac{\rho S_1h_1\,\Delta\rho}{\rho^2} = S_1h_1\frac{\Delta\rho}{\rho}.$$

Hence

$$\Delta h_1 = \frac{\Delta V}{S_1} = h_1\frac{\Delta\rho}{\rho}.$$

The new height of the heated column is

$$h_1+\Delta h_1 = h_1\left(1+\frac{\Delta\rho}{\rho}\right).$$

The pressure at the level of the connecting tube is

$$p_L = p_0+\rho' g(h_1+\Delta h_1).$$

Substituting the expressions above gives

$$p_L = p_0 + (\rho-\Delta\rho) g h_1 \left(1+\frac{\Delta\rho}{\rho}\right) = p_0 + \rho gh_1 \left(1-\frac{\Delta\rho}{\rho}\right) \left(1+\frac{\Delta\rho}{\rho}\right).$$

Hence

$$p_L = p_0 + \rho gh_1 \left( 1- \left(\frac{\Delta\rho}{\rho}\right)^2 \right).$$

To first order in $\Delta\rho/\rho$,

$$p_L\approx p_0+\rho gh_1.$$

The unheated vessel has pressure

$$p_R=p_0+\rho gh_2.$$

Initially the communicating vessels are in equilibrium, so

$$\rho gh_1=\rho gh_2,$$

which implies

$$p_R=p_0+\rho gh_1.$$

Consequently,

$$p_L-p_R = -\rho gh_1 \left(\frac{\Delta\rho}{\rho}\right)^2.$$

The pressure difference is of second order. Its sign is negative, so after uniform heating of the left vessel,

$$p_L<p_R.$$

Water flows through the connecting tube from the right vessel into the heated left vessel.

If the right vessel is heated instead, the same computation gives

$$p_R = p_0 + \rho gh_2 \left( 1- \left(\frac{\Delta\rho}{\rho}\right)^2 \right),$$

while

$$p_L=p_0+\rho gh_1.$$

Since initially $h_1=h_2$,

$$p_L-p_R = \rho gh_1 \left(\frac{\Delta\rho}{\rho}\right)^2 >0.$$

Water then flows from the left vessel into the heated right vessel.

The cross sectional areas $S_1$ and $S_2$ disappear from the final expressions. The larger rise of the free surface in the narrow vessel is exactly compensated, to first order, by the decrease in density. The remaining pressure difference is proportional to $(\Delta\rho/\rho)^2$, and its sign shows that the pressure at the tube level is slightly smaller in the heated vessel.

## Result

If the left vessel is heated, water flows from the right vessel into the left vessel.

If the right vessel is heated, water flows from the left vessel into the right vessel.

The direction of flow is always toward the vessel whose water is heated.

Thus

$$\boxed{\text{Water flows from the colder vessel into the heated vessel.}}$$

## Sanity Checks

The calculation gives

$$p_{\text{heated}} = p_{\text{initial}} \left( 1- \left(\frac{\Delta\rho}{\rho}\right)^2 \right),$$

so the first order contributions from the increase of height and the decrease of density cancel exactly. A prediction based only on the rise of the liquid level misses this cancellation.

The pressure correction has dimensions

$$\rho gh \left(\frac{\Delta\rho}{\rho}\right)^2,$$

which are the dimensions of pressure.

When $\Delta\rho\to0$,

$$p_L-p_R\to0,$$

and the system approaches the original hydrostatic equilibrium.

The final answer does not depend on the widths of the upper parts of the vessels. Their effect enters through the height increase $\Delta h=\Delta V/S$, but this dependence cancels in the product $\rho'h$. The pressure imbalance is determined by the exact expression for $\rho'h$, rather than by the free surface displacement alone.
