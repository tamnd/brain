## Problem

Two identical heavy steel balls rotate on light rods of lengths $l$ and $2l$ about the points $O_1$ and $O$, whose separation is $3l$ (Fig. 3). At the initial moment, the balls are at the points $A$ and $B$, with velocities $v$ and $2v$, respectively. How many times will the balls collide during the time $t$? After what time will the balls have collided $k$ times? Assume the collisions of the balls to be perfectly elastic.

## Setup and Assumptions

Two identical heavy steel balls move in a horizontal plane on light rigid rods. One ball rotates about the fixed point $O_1$ on a circle of radius $l$, while the other rotates about the fixed point $O$ on a circle of radius $2l$. The distance between the rotation centers is $O_1O=3l$. At the initial instant, the balls are at the points $A$ and $B$ shown in the figure, with velocities $v_1=v$ and $v_2=2v$, respectively. The rods are massless, the balls are treated as point masses, friction is neglected, and collisions are perfectly elastic. The problem asks for the number of collisions during a time interval $t$ and for the time at which the total number of collisions reaches a prescribed value $k$.

We adopt an inertial reference frame fixed to the rotation centers and assume the balls move with uniform circular motion along their respective rods. The balls can collide only if their positions coincide in space, which may happen at certain points where their trajectories approach one another.

## Physical Principles

The angular velocity of a body moving uniformly in a circle is given by

$\omega = \frac{v}{R},$

where $v$ is the linear speed and $R$ is the radius of the circular path. Collisions between identical point masses that are perfectly elastic result in an exchange of velocities. This principle allows one to determine the collision instants by computing the times when the two bodies occupy the same position, with the understanding that exchanging velocities is dynamically equivalent to allowing the balls to pass through one another.

A collision can occur only at points where the circular paths intersect or at points where the separation between the trajectories equals zero. In the present geometry, the distance between the rotation centers and the radii of the circles imply that the circles are tangent at a single point. Let $P$ denote this tangency point. Collisions are therefore possible only when both balls occupy this point. Unlike the original solution, we do not assume that the balls start at $P$; we will derive the times at which they first and subsequent reach the tangency point in a consistent manner.

## Derivation

We assign angular coordinates $\varphi_1$ and $\varphi_2$ to the first and second balls, measured from the tangency point $P$ in the direction of motion. Let the linear speeds be $v_1=v$ and $v_2=2v$, so that the angular velocities are

$\omega_1 = \frac{v_1}{l} = \frac{v}{l}, \qquad \omega_2 = \frac{v_2}{2l} = \frac{2v}{2l} = \frac{v}{l}.$

The angular velocities of both balls are therefore equal. Let $\varphi_1(0)$ and $\varphi_2(0)$ denote the initial angular positions of the balls relative to $P$, which are determined from the given positions $A$ and $B$. Since the balls start at different positions along their respective circles, $\varphi_1(0)$ and $\varphi_2(0)$ may differ. The evolution of the angular coordinates is given by

$\varphi_1(t) = \varphi_1(0) + \omega t, \qquad \varphi_2(t) = \varphi_2(0) + \omega t.$

A collision occurs whenever both balls occupy the tangency point $P$, which corresponds to

$\varphi_1(t_n) = 2\pi n, \qquad \varphi_2(t_n) = 2\pi m,$

for some integers $n$ and $m$. Substituting the expressions for $\varphi_1(t)$ and $\varphi_2(t)$ gives

$\varphi_1(0) + \omega t_n = 2\pi n, \qquad \varphi_2(0) + \omega t_n = 2\pi m.$

Subtracting these equations yields

$\varphi_1(0) - \varphi_2(0) = 2\pi (n - m),$

which shows that collisions occur whenever the initial angular difference is an integer multiple of $2\pi$. Let $\Delta\varphi = \varphi_1(0) - \varphi_2(0)$; then the collision times are

$t_k = \frac{2\pi k - \Delta\varphi}{\omega}, \qquad k \in \mathbb{Z}, \quad k \ge k_{\min},$

where $k_{\min}$ is the smallest integer such that $t_k \ge 0$. This formula generalizes the previous assumption that the balls start at the same point and automatically incorporates any initial phase difference.

Since $\omega = v/l$, we can write explicitly

$t_k = \frac{2\pi k - \Delta\varphi}{v/l} = \frac{l}{v} \left(2\pi k - \Delta\varphi \right), \qquad k = k_{\min}, k_{\min}+1, \dots.$

The interval between successive collisions is

$T = \frac{2\pi l}{v},$

which coincides with the common rotation period of each ball. Each collision advances $k$ by one integer, so the number of collisions in a time interval $[0, t]$ is

$N(t) = \left\lfloor \frac{t \, v}{2\pi l} + \frac{\Delta\varphi}{2\pi} \right\rfloor - \left\lfloor \frac{\Delta\varphi}{2\pi} \right\rfloor.$

The first term counts all possible collisions up to time $t$, and the second term subtracts those that would have occurred before $t=0$. This formula reduces to $N(t) = \lfloor vt/(2\pi l) \rfloor + 1$ if $\Delta\varphi = 0$, recovering the trivial case in which both balls start at the tangency point.

Similarly, the time of the $k$-th collision is

$t_k = \frac{l}{v} \left(2\pi (k-1) + \Delta\varphi \right), \quad k = 1,2,\dots,$

which accounts for the initial angular difference and ensures that $t_1 \ge 0$.

Elastic collisions of identical masses do not alter this schedule. At each collision, the balls exchange velocities, which is dynamically equivalent to continuing the original motions through the tangency point. Therefore the sequence of collision times remains valid.

## Result

The angular velocities of the two balls are equal:

$\omega_1 = \omega_2 = \frac{v}{l}.$

Collisions occur whenever the angular positions satisfy $\varphi_1(t) - \varphi_2(t) = 2\pi n$, leading to the collision times

$t_k = \frac{l}{v} \left( 2\pi (k-1) + \Delta\varphi \right), \quad k = 1,2,\dots.$

The number of collisions up to time $t$ is

$N(t) = \left\lfloor \frac{vt}{2\pi l} + \frac{\Delta\varphi}{2\pi} \right\rfloor - \left\lfloor \frac{\Delta\varphi}{2\pi} \right\rfloor.$

For the special case $\Delta\varphi = 0$, corresponding to both balls starting at the tangency point, these formulas reduce to

$t_k = (k-1)\frac{2\pi l}{v}, \qquad N(t) = \left\lfloor \frac{vt}{2\pi l} \right\rfloor + 1.$

## Sanity Checks

The collision period $T = 2\pi l/v$ has units of time, consistent with dimensions. Increasing $v$ decreases $T$ inversely, while increasing $l$ increases $T$ proportionally. The counting formula is dimensionless inside the floor function, and the time of the $k$-th collision has units of time. The dependence on the initial angular difference $\Delta\varphi$ ensures that collisions are correctly offset if the balls do not start at the same point. The equal angular velocities guarantee that the balls periodically coincide at the tangency point, consistent with uniform circular motion and perfectly elastic collisions.
