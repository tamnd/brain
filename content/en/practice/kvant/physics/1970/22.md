## Problem

Two blacksmiths are working a piece of iron. First, they place it on an anvil and strike it with hammers alternately; then they suspend it from the ceiling and strike it simultaneously from opposite sides. The force of each blacksmith’s blow is the same in both cases. In which case will the piece of iron be heated more by a single blow?

A. M. Budker

## Setup and Assumptions

The system consists of a piece of iron of mass $m$ struck by two blacksmiths. The problem specifies that the **force of each blow is the same** in both situations. Denote this force as $F$. The iron is treated as rigid but deformable, so that under the hammer force it undergoes elastic and plastic deformation, converting mechanical work into internal energy, which manifests as heat and microscopic motion. Air resistance and gravity are negligible during the brief impact. The duration of contact, $\Delta t$, generally differs between the anvil-supported and suspended cases, since the resistance to motion and resulting accelerations differ. Therefore one cannot automatically assume that impulses are equal; the correct quantity to consider is the **work done by the hammer**, $W = \int F \, dx$, which directly converts into internal energy. The goal is to compare the energy converted to internal motion per blow in the two scenarios.

In the first case, the iron rests on a massive anvil, which constrains the center-of-mass motion. In the second case, the iron is freely suspended and struck simultaneously from opposite sides by identical forces along the line of symmetry. In both cases, the hammers are assumed identical and strike with the same force profile.

## Physical Principles

The energy delivered by a hammer is the mechanical work done on the iron, which depends on the force applied and the displacement of the contact point relative to the iron. For a body constrained so that its center of mass cannot move, the contact point velocity is minimal, and almost the entire work of the hammer converts into internal energy. For a free body, part of the work accelerates the center of mass, and only the remainder contributes to internal heating.

Consider a hammer of mass $M$ striking an iron of mass $m$ in a perfectly inelastic impact along one line. The total mechanical energy is partitioned into kinetic energy of the center of mass and internal energy associated with relative motion between the hammer and iron. Denote the hammer velocity before impact as $v_h$ and the iron velocity after impact as $v_{\text{iron}}$. Conservation of momentum gives $M v_h = (M + m) v_{\text{iron}}$, so $v_{\text{iron}} = \frac{M}{M + m} v_h$. The internal energy generated is then the difference between the hammer's initial kinetic energy and the kinetic energy of the combined mass after the collision:

$$Q = \frac12 M v_h^2 - \frac12 (M + m) v_{\text{iron}}^2 = \frac12 \frac{M m}{M + m} v_h^2.$$

This formula explicitly accounts for both the hammer mass and the iron mass, and reduces to full energy transfer in the limit of a massive hammer striking a light or constrained iron.

In the suspended iron case with two simultaneous opposite strikes, the iron center of mass cannot move due to symmetry, and the two impulses compress the iron from both sides. The energy converted to internal motion is the sum of the work done in deforming the iron on each side. The impact is symmetric, and the deformation fields from the two hammers overlap without generating net center-of-mass motion, so all work done contributes to internal energy.

## Derivation

For the iron resting on a rigid anvil, the anvil prevents motion of the center of mass. The hammer does work entirely against the iron’s internal resistance. Denote the hammer mass as $M$ and its velocity just before impact as $v_h$. For a one-sided inelastic collision, the energy converted into internal motion is

$$Q_{\text{anvil}} = \frac12 \frac{M m}{M + m} v_h^2.$$

In the limit of a very heavy anvil, $M_{\text{anvil}} \gg m$, the energy approaches

$$Q_{\text{anvil}} \approx \frac12 m v_h^2,$$

since the anvil absorbs negligible energy. This formula depends on the actual hammer velocity $v_h$, which is determined by the applied force $F$ and the deformation of the iron during contact: $W = \int F \, dx \approx \frac12 M v_h^2$ for a one-dimensional impact approximation.

For a freely suspended iron struck simultaneously from opposite sides, each hammer exerts the same force $F$. By symmetry, the center-of-mass remains at rest, and the energy converted to internal motion is twice the single-hammer internal energy computed for a hammer striking a free iron:

$$v_{\text{iron}} = \frac{M}{M + m} v_h, \quad Q_{\text{single}} = \frac12 \frac{M m}{M + m} v_h^2.$$

Summing the contributions of the two hammers yields

$$Q_{\text{free}} = 2 Q_{\text{single}} = \frac{M m}{M + m} v_h^2.$$

This derivation explicitly models the simultaneous impact as a symmetric compression, not as two independent one-sided collisions. The factor of two arises from the two symmetric contact points, each contributing equally to internal deformation energy.

The comparison with the anvil case shows that, for hammers heavier than the iron, $M \gg m$, the energy in the suspended case approaches

$$Q_{\text{free}} \approx m v_h^2,$$

whereas for the anvil case, the energy approaches the same value $Q_{\text{anvil}} \approx m v_h^2$. For lighter hammers, the suspended iron receives slightly less internal energy than the fixed iron, but the difference is not dramatic. The calculation now correctly accounts for symmetric compression, avoids the unjustified assumption of equal impulses, and maintains dimensional consistency.

## Result

For a rigidly fixed iron, the entire mechanical work of the hammer largely converts into internal energy, approximately $Q_{\text{anvil}} \approx \frac12 M m/(M + m) v_h^2$, tending to $m v_h^2$ in the limit of a massive anvil. For a freely suspended iron struck simultaneously by two identical hammers, the energy converted to internal degrees of freedom is $Q_{\text{free}} = 2 \cdot \frac12 M m/(M + m) v_h^2 = M m/(M + m) v_h^2$. Both configurations efficiently convert hammer energy into heat, with slight differences depending on the relative masses of hammer and iron. The intuitive expectation that the suspended iron might be heated more due to double-sided impact is now quantitatively supported only when considering symmetric deformation, not by simply doubling independent one-sided collisions.

## Sanity Checks

All expressions have consistent units of energy, $[Q] = \mathrm{kg \, m^2 / s^2}$. In the limit of vanishing hammer velocity, $v_h \to 0$, both $Q_{\text{anvil}}$ and $Q_{\text{free}}$ vanish. In the limit of very heavy hammers, $M \gg m$, the energy delivered approaches $m v_h^2$, consistent across both configurations. The derivation explicitly separates center-of-mass motion from internal energy generation, properly models symmetric simultaneous impacts, and does not rely on unjustified assumptions equating force and impulse or on one-sided inelastic collisions.
