## Problem

By what factor is the illuminance on a moonlit night at full moon smaller than on a sunny day, assuming the Moon and the Sun are at the same elevation above the horizon? Assume that the illuminated hemisphere of the Moon scatters light uniformly into space. Take the radius of the Moon to be $2000~\text{км}$ and the distance from the Moon to the Earth to be $400\,000~\text{км}$.

## Setup and Assumptions

The physical system consists of the Sun, the Moon, and an observer on the Earth. The required quantity is the factor by which the illuminance on the Earth's surface during a full moon is smaller than the illuminance during direct sunlight.

Let

$R = 2.0\times10^3\ \text{km}$

be the Moon's radius and

$D = 4.0\times10^5\ \text{km}$

be the distance from the Moon to the Earth. The Sun and the Moon are assumed to have the same elevation above the horizon, so the cosine factor associated with the inclination of incoming rays is identical in both cases and cancels from the ratio of illuminances.

The statement that the illuminated hemisphere of the Moon scatters light uniformly into space is stronger than merely saying that the reflected power is spread over the hemisphere. It means that every surface element of the illuminated hemisphere reradiates its reflected power isotropically into the full solid angle $4\pi$. Under this model the Moon is treated as a perfectly reflecting diffuser with albedo equal to $1$. The actual lunar albedo is much smaller, about $0.12$, but no albedo is specified in the problem, so the intended solution uses the idealized assumption of complete reflection.

The Earth is far enough from the Moon that the distance from every lunar surface element to the Earth may be replaced by the common value $D$.

Let $F_\odot$ denote the solar irradiance at the location of the Moon and the Earth.

## Physical Principles

The solar power intercepted by the Moon equals the solar irradiance multiplied by the Moon's geometric cross section:

$P_{\rm abs}=F_\odot \pi R^2.$

Under the assumption of perfect reflection, the same power is reradiated:

$P_{\rm emit}=F_\odot \pi R^2.$

The illuminated hemisphere has area

$S_{\rm hem}=2\pi R^2.$

Hence the emitted power per unit area of the illuminated hemisphere is

=\frac{F_\odot \pi R^2}{2\pi R^2} =\frac{F_\odot}{2}.$$For an element $dA$, the emitted power is$$dP_{\rm emit}=\frac{F_\odot}{2}\,dA.$$Because the emission is isotropic, this power is distributed uniformly over a sphere of area $4\pi D^2$ centered at the element. The irradiance produced at the Earth by this element is thus$$dF=\frac{dP_{\rm emit}}{4\pi D^2} =\frac{F_\odot\,dA}{8\pi D^2}.$$This step supplies the factor $4$ in the final answer. No additional $\cos\theta$ factor appears, because the problem assumes isotropic emission from each surface element. A Lambertian surface would require an angular integration of intensity proportional to $\cos\theta$, but that is not the model stated in the problem. Illuminance is proportional to irradiance. Since both sunlight and moonlight have the same spectral origin, their illuminance ratio is equal to their irradiance ratio. ## Derivation Each surface element $dA$ of the illuminated hemisphere contributes$$dF=\frac{F_\odot\,dA}{8\pi D^2}.$$Integrating over the entire illuminated hemisphere gives$$F_{\rm moon} =\int_{\rm hemisphere} dF =\frac{F_\odot}{8\pi D^2} \int_{\rm hemisphere} dA.$$Since$$\int_{\rm hemisphere} dA =2\pi R^2,$$one obtains$$F_{\rm moon} =\frac{F_\odot}{8\pi D^2} \cdot 2\pi R^2 =\frac{F_\odot R^2}{4D^2}.$$The irradiance from direct sunlight is$$F_{\rm sun}=F_\odot.$$Hence$$\frac{F_{\rm moon}}{F_{\rm sun}} =\frac{R^2}{4D^2},$$and the factor by which moonlight is weaker than sunlight is$$K=\frac{F_{\rm sun}}{F_{\rm moon}} =\frac{4D^2}{R^2}.$$## Result The symbolic expression for the required factor is$$K=\frac{4D^2}{R^2}.$$Substituting the given values,$$K =\frac{4(4.0\times10^5\ \text{km})^2} {(2.0\times10^3\ \text{km})^2}.$$Since$$\frac{D}{R} =\frac{4.0\times10^5}{2.0\times10^3} =200,$$it follows that$$K =4\cdot 200^2 =4\cdot 40000 =1.6\times10^5.$$Thus the illuminance on a moonlit night at full moon is smaller than the illuminance on a sunny day by$$\boxed{1.6\times10^5}.$$Equivalently,$$\frac{F_{\rm moon}}{F_{\rm sun}} =\frac{1}{1.6\times10^5} \approx 6.3\times10^{-6}.$$## Sanity Checks The ratio is dimensionless because only the combination $(D/R)^2$ appears. If the Moon were moved farther away, the received moonlight would decrease as $D^{-2}$, exactly as required by the inverse square law. If the Moon's radius were doubled, the intercepted solar power would increase by a factor of $4$, and the moonlight received at the Earth would also increase by a factor of $4$, consistent with the dependence on $R^2$. The factor $4$ in the denominator can be traced explicitly to two independent geometric factors. The intercepted solar power is spread over the illuminated hemisphere, producing a factor $1/2$ in the emitted power per unit area. Each surface element then radiates into the full solid angle $4\pi$, producing another factor $1/2$ relative to the intercepted power. Their product yields the overall factor $1/4$ in$$F_{\rm moon} =\frac{F_\odot R^2}{4D^2}.$$Under the idealized assumption of perfect reflection and isotropic reradiation specified above, the numerical result is self consistent. A realistic lunar albedo would increase the weakening factor by approximately the reciprocal of the albedo, but that correction lies outside the assumptions of the problem.
