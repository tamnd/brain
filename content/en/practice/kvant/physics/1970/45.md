## Problem

F45. A spherical capacitor filled with a dielectric and charged to some potential difference is discharging through its dielectric. What will be the magnetic field of the discharge currents in the space between the spheres?

## Setup and Assumptions

A spherical capacitor consists of two concentric conducting spheres of radii $a$ and $b$, with $a<b$. The space between the spheres is completely filled with a homogeneous dielectric having permittivity $\varepsilon$ and finite conductivity $\sigma$. The capacitor is initially charged and then discharges through the dielectric.

The required quantity is the magnetic field $\mathbf B$ produced by the discharge currents in the dielectric region $a<r<b$.

The dielectric is assumed isotropic and homogeneous. The discharge is sufficiently slow that quasistatic electrodynamics applies. The geometry remains perfectly spherically symmetric throughout the process. Edge effects, radiation, and inductive distortions of the electric field are neglected. The reference frame is the rest frame of the capacitor.

Because of spherical symmetry, all physical quantities depend only on the radial coordinate $r$ and time $t$.

## Physical Principles

The electric field inside a spherical capacitor is radial:

$$\mathbf E = E(r,t)\,\mathbf e_r .$$

The conduction current density in the dielectric is given by Ohm's law:

$$\mathbf j = \sigma \mathbf E .$$

The displacement current density is

$$\mathbf j_d = \frac{\partial \mathbf D}{\partial t} = \varepsilon \frac{\partial \mathbf E}{\partial t}.$$

The magnetic field is determined by the Ampère-Maxwell equation:

$$\nabla\times\mathbf B = \mu_0 \left( \mathbf j + \frac{\partial \mathbf D}{\partial t} \right).$$

Charge conservation requires

$$\nabla\cdot \left( \mathbf j + \frac{\partial \mathbf D}{\partial t} \right) =0.$$

## Derivation

Because the system is spherically symmetric, the electric field has the form

$$\mathbf E(r,t)=E(r,t)\,\mathbf e_r .$$

The conduction current density is also purely radial:

$$\mathbf j(r,t)=\sigma E(r,t)\,\mathbf e_r .$$

The displacement current density is radial as well:

$$\mathbf j_d(r,t) = \varepsilon \frac{\partial E}{\partial t} \,\mathbf e_r .$$

Hence the total current density is

$$\mathbf j_{\text{tot}} = \mathbf j+\mathbf j_d = \left( \sigma E + \varepsilon \frac{\partial E}{\partial t} \right)\mathbf e_r .$$

Consider a spherical surface of radius $r$ lying between the electrodes. The total current through that surface is

$$I_{\text{tot}} = 4\pi r^2 \left( \sigma E + \varepsilon \frac{\partial E}{\partial t} \right).$$

Charge conservation implies that the same total current must cross every concentric spherical surface. During discharge, no free charge accumulates inside the dielectric volume. Since the current enters the dielectric from one electrode and leaves through the other, the net current crossing any closed spherical surface entirely within the dielectric must be zero.

Equivalently, the total current density satisfies

$$\nabla\cdot\mathbf j_{\text{tot}}=0.$$

For a purely radial field this gives

$$\frac1{r^2} \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left( r^2 j_{\text{tot}} \right) =0.$$

Therefore

$$r^2 j_{\text{tot}} = C(t),$$

where $C(t)$ is independent of $r$.

To determine $C(t)$, use Gauss's law. The electric field between the spheres is

$$E(r,t) = \frac{Q(t)} {4\pi\varepsilon r^2}.$$

Substituting into the total current density,

$$j_{\text{tot}} = \sigma \frac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon r^2} + \varepsilon \frac1{4\pi\varepsilon r^2} \frac{dQ}{dt},$$

or

$$j_{\text{tot}} = \frac1{4\pi r^2} \left( \frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon}Q + \frac{dQ}{dt} \right).$$

The charge on the capacitor decreases according to

$$\frac{dQ}{dt} = -\frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon}Q,$$

which is the standard relaxation law for a conducting dielectric.

Substituting this relation into the expression for $j_{\text{tot}}$ yields

$$j_{\text{tot}} = \frac1{4\pi r^2} \left( \frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon}Q - \frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon}Q \right) =0.$$

Thus

$$\mathbf j+\frac{\partial\mathbf D}{\partial t}=0 \qquad (a<r<b).$$

The Ampère-Maxwell equation becomes

$$\nabla\times\mathbf B=0.$$

The magnetic field must also respect the complete spherical symmetry of the problem. There is no preferred axis about which magnetic field lines could circulate. The only vector field compatible with spherical symmetry and vanishing at infinity is

$$\mathbf B=0.$$

Hence the magnetic field vanishes everywhere in the dielectric region.

## Result

The conduction current density and the displacement current density are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction:

$$\mathbf j = - \frac{\partial\mathbf D}{\partial t}.$$

Their sum is identically zero:

$$\mathbf j+\frac{\partial\mathbf D}{\partial t}=0.$$

Applying the Ampère-Maxwell equation gives

$$\nabla\times\mathbf B=0.$$

Spherical symmetry then requires

$$\boxed{\mathbf B=0}.$$

No numerical values are given in the problem. The magnetic field has the numerical value

$$\boxed{B=0\ \text{T}}.$$

## Sanity Checks

The result has the correct dimensions. The magnetic field is found to be exactly zero, which is a valid quantity with units of tesla.

If the conductivity tends to zero, the capacitor does not discharge. Both the conduction current and the time variation of the electric field vanish, leaving $B=0$.

If the conductivity is finite, the conduction current is nonzero. However, the decreasing electric field produces a displacement current density of equal magnitude and opposite sign. The source term in the Ampère-Maxwell equation remains zero, so the magnetic field is still zero.

The strongest consistency check comes from symmetry. A nonzero magnetic field would require a distinguished direction in space, but a pair of concentric spheres possesses complete spherical symmetry. No such direction exists.

The step most vulnerable to error is the sign in the relaxation equation

$$\frac{dQ}{dt} = -\frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon}Q.$$

Using the opposite sign would make the conduction and displacement currents add instead of cancel, producing an incorrect nonzero magnetic field.
