## Problem

F56. Why does a flag “flutter” in the wind? (IX–X)

## Setup and Assumptions

A flexible flag of length $L$ and surface mass density $\mu$ is attached along one edge and exposed to a uniform wind of speed $v$. The surrounding air has density $\rho$. The goal is to explain the appearance of self-sustained oscillations, commonly called fluttering, by identifying the physical mechanism that renders the flat configuration unstable under airflow.

The flag is modeled as a thin, flexible sheet undergoing small transverse deflections $y(x,t)$, where $x$ runs along the flag from the clamped edge at $x=0$ to the free edge at $x=L$. The airflow is treated as incompressible and inviscid, with wind speed much smaller than the speed of sound. Viscous effects are neglected in estimating instantaneous pressure differences, although they ultimately dissipate energy in the air. The analysis is performed in the laboratory frame, where the flag support is stationary and the air moves with speed $v$.

The derivation is restricted to the linear regime of small slopes,

$$\left|\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}\right| \ll 1,$$

and small oscillation amplitudes. Nonlinear effects are ignored.

## Physical Principles

For incompressible, inviscid flow, Bernoulli's principle relates the local pressure $p$ and the flow speed $u$ along a streamline by

$$p+\frac12\rho u^2=\text{const}.$$

The transverse motion of the flag is governed by Newton's second law,

$$\mu \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial t^2} = F_{\rm elastic}+F_{\rm aero}.$$

A mechanical oscillation grows only if the aerodynamic force performs positive net work on the flag. The central issue is whether the pressure perturbation generated by the flow is exactly synchronized with the deformation or whether it responds with a delay.

A fluid cannot instantaneously rearrange itself everywhere when the sheet moves. A disturbance created at one point is convected downstream by the mean flow. The characteristic convection time for a disturbance to travel a distance comparable with a wavelength $\lambda=2\pi/k$ is

$$\tau \sim \frac{\lambda}{v} = \frac{2\pi}{kv}.$$

If the flag oscillates with angular frequency $\omega$, the pressure field at time $t$ depends on the shape of the flag at an earlier time $t-\tau$. This finite response time is the origin of the aerodynamic phase lag.

## Derivation

Consider a small element of the flag displaced slightly from its equilibrium flat position. The local slope

$$\theta \approx \frac{\partial y}{\partial x}$$

acts as a small angle between the plate and the incoming flow. The incident velocity $v$ can be decomposed into a component tangent to the plate and a component normal to it. For small $\theta$,

$$v_n=v\sin\theta \approx v\theta = v\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}.$$

The airflow must be deflected by this normal velocity. The momentum flux associated with that deflection is proportional to $\rho v,v_n$, giving

$$\rho v\,v_n = \rho v^2 \frac{\partial y}{\partial x}.$$

A pressure difference has the same dimensions as momentum flux. In linear theory the only available dimensionless factor is a coefficient determined by geometry and the detailed flow field, so

$$\Delta p = C\rho v^2 \frac{\partial y}{\partial x},$$

where $C$ is a dimensionless coefficient of order unity.

The instantaneous transverse velocity of the flag is

$$\dot y = \frac{\partial y}{\partial t}.$$

If the pressure responded instantaneously according to the preceding formula, the power delivered by the air would be

$$P(t) = \int_0^L \Delta p\,\dot y\,dx = C\rho v^2 \int_0^L \frac{\partial y}{\partial x} \frac{\partial y}{\partial t} \,dx.$$

For a traveling wave

$$y(x,t) = A\sin(kx-\omega t),$$

the derivatives are

$$\frac{\partial y}{\partial x} = Ak\cos(kx-\omega t),$$

and

$$\frac{\partial y}{\partial t} = -A\omega\cos(kx-\omega t).$$

Hence

$$\frac{\partial y}{\partial x} \frac{\partial y}{\partial t} = -A^2k\omega \cos^2(kx-\omega t).$$

Substituting into the power integral gives

$$P(t) = -C\rho v^2A^2k\omega \int_0^L \cos^2(kx-\omega t)\,dx.$$

Averaging over one oscillation period $T=2\pi/\omega$,

$$\langle P\rangle = -C\rho v^2A^2k\omega \int_0^L \left( \frac1T \int_0^T \cos^2(kx-\omega t)\,dt \right)dx.$$

Since

$$\frac1T \int_0^T \cos^2(kx-\omega t)\,dt = \frac12,$$

one obtains

$$\langle P\rangle = -\frac12 C\rho v^2A^2k\omega L.$$

This calculation establishes only that, for the traveling-wave disturbance chosen above, the instantaneous slope law yields negative average work. It does not prove that every possible motion is damped. It shows that a purely instantaneous pressure proportional to slope does not provide the positive work needed for the growth of this representative disturbance.

The missing ingredient is the finite aerodynamic response time. Since the pressure field is determined by the flag shape at the earlier time $t-\tau$, the linear pressure law becomes

$$\Delta p(x,t) = C\rho v^2 \frac{\partial y}{\partial x}(x,t-\tau).$$

For small delays, Taylor expansion gives

$$\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}(x,t-\tau) = \frac{\partial y}{\partial x}(x,t) - \tau \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \left( \frac{\partial y}{\partial x} \right) + O(\tau^2).$$

Keeping only the leading correction,

$$\Delta p = C\rho v^2 \frac{\partial y}{\partial x} - C\rho v^2\tau \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x\,\partial t}.$$

For the traveling wave,

$$\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x\,\partial t} = Ak\omega \sin(kx-\omega t).$$

Since

$$\frac{\partial y}{\partial t} = -A\omega \cos(kx-\omega t),$$

and

$$\frac{\partial y}{\partial x} = Ak \cos(kx-\omega t),$$

the mixed derivative has magnitude

$$\left| \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x\,\partial t} \right| \sim k \left| \frac{\partial y}{\partial t} \right|.$$

Using the convection estimate

$$\tau \sim \frac{1}{kv},$$

which differs from $\lambda/v$ only by the numerical factor $2\pi$, the delayed contribution becomes

$$C\rho v^2\tau \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x\,\partial t} \sim C\rho v^2 \frac{1}{kv} k \frac{\partial y}{\partial t} = C\rho v \frac{\partial y}{\partial t}.$$

Thus the finite response time automatically generates a velocity-dependent term of the form

$$\Delta p = C\rho v^2 \frac{\partial y}{\partial x} + D\rho v \frac{\partial y}{\partial t},$$

where the dimensionless coefficient $D$ is of order $C$ and its sign depends on the detailed unsteady flow. The crucial point is that the velocity term is not introduced ad hoc. It is the first correction produced by a delayed aerodynamic response.

The factor $\rho v$ has the correct dimensions because

$$[\rho v\dot y] = \left( \frac{\rm kg}{m^3} \right) \left( \frac{m}{s} \right) \left( \frac{m}{s} \right) = \frac{\rm kg}{m\,s^2},$$

which are the units of pressure.

The cycle-averaged power becomes

$$\langle P\rangle = C\rho v^2 \left\langle \int_0^L \frac{\partial y}{\partial x} \frac{\partial y}{\partial t} \,dx \right\rangle + D\rho v \left\langle \int_0^L \left( \frac{\partial y}{\partial t} \right)^2 dx \right\rangle .$$

The first term is the previously computed contribution. For the second term,

$$\left( \frac{\partial y}{\partial t} \right)^2 = A^2\omega^2 \cos^2(kx-\omega t).$$

Its average over one period is

$$\left\langle \left( \frac{\partial y}{\partial t} \right)^2 \right\rangle = \frac12 A^2\omega^2.$$

Hence

$$\left\langle \int_0^L \left( \frac{\partial y}{\partial t} \right)^2 dx \right\rangle = \frac12 A^2\omega^2L,$$

and

$$\langle P\rangle = -\frac12 C\rho v^2A^2k\omega L + \frac12 D\rho vA^2\omega^2L.$$

When

$$D\omega > Cvk,$$

the average power is positive:

$$\langle P\rangle>0.$$

This inequality should be interpreted only as the instability condition of the linear toy model. Its purpose is to show explicitly how a phase-lag term can overcome the instantaneous contribution and produce net energy transfer from the wind to the flag.

The flag responds to this aerodynamic driving according to its elasticity and inertia. Bending stiffness $B$ and tension $T$ contribute restoring forces

$$F_{\rm elastic} = - B\frac{\partial^4 y}{\partial x^4} + T\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x^2},$$

while the mass per unit length $\mu$ resists acceleration. The interaction between these restoring forces and the delayed aerodynamic pressure produces traveling disturbances that can grow rather than decay.

## Result

The linearized aerodynamic pressure generated by a small deformation of the flag scales as

$$\Delta p \simeq C\rho v^2 \frac{\partial y}{\partial x}.$$

This scaling follows from the normal flow velocity

$$v_n = v\frac{\partial y}{\partial x},$$

and the associated momentum flux $\rho v v_n$.

A pressure law depending only on the instantaneous slope does not supply positive work to the traveling-wave disturbance analyzed above. Flutter appears because the airflow does not respond instantaneously. Disturbances are convected downstream in a finite time, so the pressure depends on an earlier shape of the flag.

Expanding the delayed response in powers of the convection time produces a correction proportional to the velocity of the sheet. That velocity-dependent term is phase shifted relative to the displacement and can perform positive work over a cycle. When the positive work exceeds the losses associated with elasticity, sound radiation, wake formation, and viscous dissipation, a small disturbance grows.

The physical mechanism is that a deformation alters the flow, the altered flow modifies the pressure distribution after a finite delay, and that delayed pressure response extracts kinetic energy from the mean wind and transfers it to the flag. The flat configuration then becomes dynamically unstable, leading to self-sustained fluttering.

## Sanity Checks

The dimensions of the pressure law are correct. Since

$$[\rho] = {\rm kg\,m^{-3}}, \qquad [v^2] = {\rm m^2\,s^{-2}}, \qquad \left[ \frac{\partial y}{\partial x} \right] = 1,$$

the pressure difference has units

$$[\Delta p] = {\rm kg\,m^{-1}\,s^{-2}} = {\rm Pa}.$$

For the delayed contribution,

$$[\rho v y_t] = \left( \frac{\rm kg}{m^3} \right) \left( \frac{m}{s} \right) \left( \frac{m}{s} \right) = \frac{\rm kg}{m\,s^2},$$

which is also pressure.

If

$$v\to0,$$

then both aerodynamic terms vanish and the airflow cannot supply energy to the flag.

If

$$\rho\to0,$$

the pressure difference disappears and no aerodynamic instability is possible.

The estimate

$$\tau\sim\frac{1}{kv}$$

shows that the phase lag becomes weaker when disturbances are convected very rapidly compared with the oscillation period. The appearance of the velocity term from the delayed response is consistent with the general theory of self-excited oscillations, where growth requires a force component that performs positive average work on the motion.
