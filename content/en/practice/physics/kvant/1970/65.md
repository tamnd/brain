## Problem

The plates of a parallel-plate capacitor are charged to potentials $+\phi$ and $-\phi$ relative to the ground. The capacitance of the capacitor formed by the plates is $C$, and the capacitances of the capacitors formed by each plate with the ground are $-C_1$. By what factor will the electric field intensity between the plates change if one of them is grounded?

L. G. Aslamazov

## Setup and Assumptions

Two large parallel plates form an ideal parallel-plate capacitor of capacitance $C$. Each plate also has a capacitance $C_1$ with respect to the ground, modeling stray capacitance to earth. Initially the plates are maintained at potentials $+\phi$ and $-\phi$ relative to the ground. The plates are treated as perfectly conducting, and edge effects are neglected so that all capacitances are lumped and linear. The ground is an infinite charge reservoir that can supply or absorb charge without changing its potential.

The crucial point is to determine what quantities remain fixed when one plate is grounded. The statement of the problem specifies only the initial potentials of the plates; it does not state that the plates remain connected to voltage sources after grounding. Consequently, one cannot assume that the ungrounded plate continues to have potential $+\phi$.

The physically natural interpretation is that the capacitor is first charged so that the plates have potentials $+\phi$ and $-\phi$, and then one plate is connected to ground. During this operation the ungrounded plate remains isolated, so its total charge is conserved. The grounded plate may exchange charge with the earth until its potential becomes zero. Under this interpretation the answer depends on both $C$ and $C_1$.

## Physical Principles

For two conductors $A$ and $B$ with potentials $V_A$ and $V_B$, mutual capacitance $C$ between them, and capacitance $C_1$ from each conductor to ground, the conductor charges are

$$Q_A=C(V_A-V_B)+C_1V_A,$$

$$Q_B=C(V_B-V_A)+C_1V_B.$$

Initially,

$$V_A=\phi,\qquad V_B=-\phi.$$

Hence

$$Q_{A0} =C(\phi-(-\phi))+C_1\phi =(2C+C_1)\phi,$$

and

$$Q_{B0} =C(-\phi-\phi)+C_1(-\phi) =-(2C+C_1)\phi.$$

When plate $B$ is grounded,

$$V_B=0.$$

Since plate $A$ is not connected either to ground or to a voltage source, its total charge cannot change. Therefore

$$Q_A=Q_{A0}=(2C+C_1)\phi.$$

This conservation law must be imposed together with the capacitance relations.

The electric field between the plates is proportional to the potential difference:

$$E=\frac{V_A-V_B}{d}.$$

## Derivation

After grounding plate $B$, the charge on plate $A$ is

$$Q_A=C(V_A-0)+C_1V_A=(C+C_1)V_A.$$

Charge conservation on plate $A$ gives

$$(C+C_1)V_A=(2C+C_1)\phi.$$

Solving for the new potential of the isolated plate,

$$V_A=\phi\,\frac{2C+C_1}{C+C_1}.$$

Since plate $B$ is grounded,

$$V_B=0.$$

The initial electric field between the plates is

$$E_0=\frac{\phi-(-\phi)}{d} =\frac{2\phi}{d}.$$

The final electric field is

$$E_1=\frac{V_A-0}{d} =\frac{\phi}{d}\, \frac{2C+C_1}{C+C_1}.$$

Hence

$$\frac{E_1}{E_0} = \frac{\displaystyle \frac{\phi}{d}\frac{2C+C_1}{C+C_1}} {\displaystyle \frac{2\phi}{d}} = \frac{2C+C_1}{2(C+C_1)}.$$

Thus the field after grounding is smaller than the original field by the factor

$$\boxed{\frac{E_1}{E_0} =\frac{2C+C_1}{2(C+C_1)}}.$$

## Result

$$\boxed{\frac{E_1}{E_0} =\frac{2C+C_1}{2(C+C_1)}}.$$

Equivalently,

$$E_1 = E_0\, \frac{2C+C_1}{2(C+C_1)}.$$

## Sanity Checks

If the stray capacitance is negligible, $C_1\to0$, then

$$\frac{E_1}{E_0} = \frac{2C}{2C} =1.$$

This agrees with the familiar result for an isolated charged capacitor. Grounding one plate merely shifts the reference potential, while the voltage difference between the plates remains unchanged.

If the capacitance to ground is much larger than the interplate capacitance, $C_1\gg C$, then

$$\frac{E_1}{E_0} = \frac{2C+C_1}{2(C+C_1)} \longrightarrow \frac12.$$

In this limit the isolated plate is strongly coupled to ground through its own capacitance, so its potential approaches $+\phi$ after the other plate is grounded, giving approximately half the original voltage difference.

The ratio satisfies

$$\frac12 \le \frac{2C+C_1}{2(C+C_1)} \le 1,$$

which is physically reasonable: grounding one plate cannot increase the field, and the field reduction ranges from none at all when $C_1=0$ to a factor of $1/2$ when $C_1\gg C$.

This corrected solution removes the unjustified assumption that the ungrounded plate remains at fixed potential. The charge conservation condition on the isolated plate is applied explicitly, leading to the field-change factor that depends on both $C$ and $C_1$.
