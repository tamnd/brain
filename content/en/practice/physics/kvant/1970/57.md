## Problem

Two identical balls connected by a weightless spring move along a smooth horizontal floor with the same velocity perpendicular to a vertical wall. Describe how the system collides with the wall. How will the balls move after the impact? The collision of a ball with the wall is perfectly elastic, and the duration of the collision is negligibly small.

## Setup and Assumptions

Consider two identical point masses, each of mass $m$, connected by a weightless spring with stiffness $k$ and natural length $\ell_0$. The system moves along a smooth horizontal floor toward a rigid vertical wall. The line joining the masses is perpendicular to the wall so that the first mass reaches the wall before the second. Initially, both masses move with the same velocity $v_0$ toward the wall, and the spring is unstretched. The collision of a mass with the wall is perfectly elastic, and the duration of the collision is negligibly short compared with the spring oscillation period. Consequently, during a collision, the impulse from the spring is negligible, and the wall entirely determines the instantaneous change in the velocity of the colliding mass. Denote the mass that reaches the wall first as $m_1$ and the second mass as $m_2$. The subsequent motion of the system is conveniently described using the center-of-mass coordinate

$X = \frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}$

and the relative coordinate

$r = x_2 - x_1.$

The relative motion is governed by the spring and, for equal masses, satisfies

$\ddot r + \frac{2k}{m}(r - \ell_0) = 0.$

The center-of-mass motion evolves according to the net external forces acting on the system, which include impulses from the wall at each collision.

## Physical Principles

A perfectly elastic collision of a mass with a rigid wall reverses the component of its velocity perpendicular to the wall while preserving its magnitude. Since the collision is instantaneous, the velocity of the colliding mass immediately before and after impact is entirely determined by the wall, and the spring does not appreciably alter it during the collision. Between collisions, the center-of-mass accelerates only when a mass is in contact with the wall; otherwise, it moves with constant velocity. The relative motion between the masses obeys simple harmonic oscillation governed by the spring with angular frequency

$\omega = \sqrt{\frac{2k}{m}}.$

Each collision instantaneously changes the velocity of the colliding mass, modifying both the center-of-mass velocity and the relative velocity. Therefore, the sequence of collisions must be analyzed iteratively to account for these discontinuous velocity changes.

## Derivation

Immediately before the first collision, both masses move toward the wall with velocity $v_0$,

$v_1^- = v_2^- = -v_0.$

The first collision occurs when $m_1$ reaches the wall. The collision is instantaneous, and the wall reverses the velocity of $m_1$ while $m_2$ continues unperturbed,

$v_1^+ = +v_0, \qquad v_2^+ = -v_0.$

The center-of-mass velocity immediately after the first collision is

$V_{\rm cm} = \frac{v_1^+ + v_2^+}{2} = 0,$

and the relative velocity is

$\dot r = v_2^+ - v_1^+ = -2v_0.$

At the moment of collision, the spring is unstretched, so $r(0) = \ell_0$. Defining $q = r - \ell_0$, the relative motion satisfies

$\ddot q + \omega^2 q = 0, \qquad q(0) = 0, \quad \dot q(0) = -2v_0.$

Solving this differential equation yields

$q(t) = -\frac{2v_0}{\omega} \sin(\omega t), \qquad r(t) = \ell_0 - \frac{2v_0}{\omega} \sin(\omega t).$

The positions of the masses relative to the center-of-mass are

$x_1(t) = X(t) - \frac{r(t)}{2}, \qquad x_2(t) = X(t) + \frac{r(t)}{2}.$

At the first collision, $x_1(0) = 0$, hence $X(0) = \ell_0/2$, and before the second collision,

$x_2(t) = \ell_0 - \frac{v_0}{\omega} \sin(\omega t).$

The second mass reaches the wall when $x_2(t_1) = 0$, giving

$\sin(\omega t_1) = \frac{\omega \ell_0}{v_0}, \qquad t_1 = \frac{1}{\omega} \arcsin\left(\frac{\omega \ell_0}{v_0}\right),$

provided $v_0 > \omega \ell_0$. Immediately before the second collision, the velocity of $m_2$ is

$v_2^- = \frac{d}{dt} x_2(t_1^-) = -v_0 \cos(\omega t_1).$

The wall reverses this velocity instantaneously,

$v_2^+ = +v_0 \cos(\omega t_1).$

At this moment, the velocity of $m_1$ is

$v_1(t_1) = \frac{d}{dt} x_1(t_1^-) = v_0 \cos(\omega t_1),$

so the center-of-mass velocity immediately after the second collision is

$V_{\rm cm}^+ = \frac{v_1 + v_2^+}{2} = \frac{v_0 \cos(\omega t_1) + v_0 \cos(\omega t_1)}{2} = v_0 \cos(\omega t_1).$

The relative velocity immediately after the second collision is

$\dot r^+ = v_2^+ - v_1 = v_0 \cos(\omega t_1) - v_0 \cos(\omega t_1) = 0.$

Thus the second collision resets the relative motion. Defining $q = r - \ell_0$, the new initial conditions for relative motion after $t_1$ are

$q(t_1) = r(t_1) - \ell_0 = -\frac{2v_0}{\omega} \sin(\omega t_1), \qquad \dot q(t_1^+) = 0.$

The relative motion for $t > t_1$ therefore satisfies

$q(t) = q(t_1) \cos[\omega (t - t_1)] = -\frac{2v_0}{\omega} \sin(\omega t_1) \cos[\omega (t - t_1)], \qquad r(t) = \ell_0 + q(t).$

The positions of the masses evolve according to

$x_1(t) = X(t) - \frac{r(t)}{2}, \qquad x_2(t) = X(t) + \frac{r(t)}{2},$

with the center-of-mass moving uniformly at velocity $V_{\rm cm}^+ = v_0 \cos(\omega t_1)$ until the next collision. Subsequent collisions with the wall can be analyzed iteratively. Each collision instantaneously reverses the velocity of the colliding mass, modifies the center-of-mass velocity, and changes the initial conditions for relative motion. The motion alternates between harmonic oscillation of the relative coordinate and uniform translation of the center-of-mass until the amplitude of the spring oscillation becomes insufficient for a mass to reach the wall. Energy conservation between collisions guarantees that the total kinetic plus potential energy remains constant, with discontinuous redistribution between center-of-mass and relative motion at each impact. The amplitude of relative oscillation after each collision can be computed from the values of $r$ and $\dot r$ at the moment of impact.

## Summary

The first collision reverses the velocity of $m_1$ while $m_2$ continues unperturbed, initiating relative oscillations with amplitude $2v_0/\omega$. If $v_0 > \omega \ell_0$, the second mass reaches the wall at time $t_1$, reverses its velocity, and modifies the center-of-mass velocity to $V_{\rm cm} = v_0 \cos(\omega t_1)$. The relative motion is simultaneously reset according to the positions and velocities at the moment of impact. Subsequent collisions are determined iteratively, with each impact producing an instantaneous change in the center-of-mass and relative velocities. Between collisions, the relative motion is harmonic with frequency $\omega$, and the center-of-mass moves uniformly. The complete motion consists of a sequence of alternating collisions with the wall and harmonic oscillations of the relative coordinate. The center-of-mass velocity is not constant in general; it changes at each collision. The motion is periodic only in special cases where the timing of collisions produces a repeating sequence, otherwise it consists of alternating uniform translations and relative harmonic oscillations. The positions and velocities of the masses after any collision are fully determined by the initial conditions and the iterative application of the elastic collision rule.
