## Problem

Photographing a tiger from a distance of less than 20 meters is dangerous. What size can a camera obscura with an aperture diameter of 1 mm have if the tiger is to appear striped in the photograph? The distance between the stripes on the tiger’s skin is 20 cm.

A. L. Stasenko

## Setup and Assumptions

A tiger is photographed with a camera obscura having an aperture of diameter

$d = 1\,\text{mm} = 1.0\times10^{-3}\,\text{m}.$

The distance between neighboring stripes on the tiger’s skin is

$a = 20\,\text{cm} = 0.20\,\text{m}.$

The tiger is at a distance less than

$R = 20\,\text{m}.$

The unknown is the admissible size of the camera obscura, characterized by the distance $L$ from the aperture to the screen, for which the stripe pattern remains resolved. The camera obscura is modeled using geometric optics. The tiger is oriented perpendicular to the optical axis, and the stripe spacing $a$ is treated as a transverse object scale at distance $R$. Image formation is described by similar triangles, and blur is caused by the finite aperture diameter. Diffraction effects are neglected in this approximation.

## Physical Principles

In a camera obscura with a finite aperture, each point of the object produces a blurred spot on the screen. The size of this blur is not equal to the aperture diameter itself, but rather is the geometric projection of the aperture onto the screen. If the screen is at distance $L$ from the aperture and the object is at distance $R$, rays from opposite edges of the aperture subtend an angle approximately $d/R$ at the object. This angle projects onto the screen to produce a blur spot of linear size

$b = \frac{d}{R} L.$

The image spacing between neighboring stripes is determined by similar triangles and is given by

$a' = \frac{L}{R} a.$

The ability to resolve stripes requires that the image spacing between stripes be at least as large as the blur size,

$a' \ge b.$

Substituting the expressions for $a'$ and $b$ gives

$\frac{L}{R} a \ge \frac{L}{R} d.$

The factors $L/R$ cancel, giving the condition in object space,

$a \ge d.$

This inequality is independent of the screen distance $L$ and the object distance $R$, implying that geometric resolution in a camera obscura depends solely on the ratio of object feature size to aperture diameter.

## Derivation

The image spacing is

$a' = \frac{L}{R} a,$

and the blur diameter is

$b = \frac{d}{R} L.$

The resolving condition $a' \ge b$ becomes

$\frac{L}{R} a \ge \frac{L}{R} d.$

Dividing both sides by $L/R$, which is positive, yields

$a \ge d.$

This result shows that the stripes are resolvable if their separation on the tiger exceeds the aperture diameter. Substituting the numerical values,

$a = 0.20\,\text{m}, \quad d = 1.0\times10^{-3}\,\text{m},$

we have

$0.20\,\text{m} \gg 1.0\times10^{-3}\,\text{m},$

so the inequality is well satisfied. Therefore, the stripes will appear as distinct dark and light bands in the camera obscura image. The resolving condition is met regardless of the camera length $L$, provided the aperture diameter remains $1,\text{mm}$.

## Result

The geometric-optics model demonstrates that the camera obscura can have any practical screen distance $L$, because both the image spacing and the blur size scale linearly with $L$, leaving the resolving power unchanged. The critical requirement for resolving the stripes is

$a \ge d,$

which is satisfied by the given parameters. A screen distance of $L = 10,\text{cm}$ or $L = 1,\text{m}$ or any other positive distance produces a resolved image under this model. The problem statement’s reference to distances less than $20,\text{m}$ is relevant for scaling considerations but does not impose a geometric limit on $L$ for resolving the stripes.

## Sanity Checks

The inequality $a \ge d$ is dimensionally consistent, as both $a$ and $d$ are lengths measured in meters. Physically, the result is reasonable: the stripes are much larger than the aperture diameter, so each stripe is projected through the aperture without being smeared into its neighbor. The fact that the resolution condition is independent of $L$ aligns with the geometric-optics principle that scaling the image plane does not affect the relative size of features compared to the projected blur. Increasing the aperture diameter $d$ would increase the projected blur and could eventually merge stripes if $d$ approached $a$, while decreasing $a$ would make the stripes harder to resolve. The scaling behavior is internally consistent and captures the essential physics of a pinhole camera.
