## Problem

F55. Estimate to what height an arrow shot vertically upward from a bow will rise. The mass of the arrow is 20 g, the length of the bowstring is 1 m. The bowstring is drawn back by 5 cm. Assume the tension of the bowstring...

## Setup and Assumptions

The physical system consists of a bow and an arrow of mass

$m = 20\,\text{g} = 0.020\,\text{kg}.$

The bowstring has length

$L = 1.0\,\text{m}$

and is drawn back by

$x = 5\,\text{cm} = 0.050\,\text{m}.$

The maximum height $h$ of the arrow is determined by the conversion of elastic energy stored in the bow into kinetic energy of the arrow, and subsequently into gravitational potential energy. The bowstring is treated as an ideal linear spring with effective spring constant $k$, the bow is considered massless, air resistance is neglected, and the acceleration due to gravity is

$g = 9.8\,\text{m/s}^2.$

The crucial distinction in this corrected solution is that the effective spring constant $k$ is derived directly from the problem’s given information on bowstring tension rather than an external assumption about stored energy. If the maximum tension in the drawn bowstring is provided as $T_{\max}$, then $k$ can be determined from the geometry of the drawn bowstring as $k = T_{\max}/\Delta L$, where $\Delta L$ is the total geometric elongation.

## Physical Principles

The elastic force in the bowstring obeys Hooke’s law for small displacements, expressed as

$F = k \Delta L,$

where $\Delta L$ is the increase in the string’s length relative to its natural length. The elastic potential energy stored in the bow is therefore

$E_{\rm el} = \frac{1}{2} k (\Delta L)^2.$

This energy is entirely converted into the kinetic energy of the arrow at the instant of release:

$\frac{1}{2} k (\Delta L)^2 = \frac{1}{2} m v^2.$

As the arrow rises, its kinetic energy is transformed into gravitational potential energy:

$m g h = \frac{1}{2} m v^2 = \frac{1}{2} k (\Delta L)^2.$

Hence the maximum height is

$h = \frac{k (\Delta L)^2}{2 m g}.$

This relation explicitly expresses the height in terms of the effective spring constant $k$ and the geometric elongation $\Delta L$, both determined from the bow’s mechanical properties and the problem’s stated tension, avoiding any external assumptions.

## Derivation

The midpoint of the bowstring is displaced by $x = 0.050,\text{m}$, so each half of the string forms a straight segment of length

$\ell = \sqrt{(L/2)^2 + x^2} = \sqrt{0.25 + 0.0025} = \sqrt{0.2525} \approx 0.5025\,\text{m}.$

The total drawn length is

$2 \ell \approx 1.005\,\text{m},$

so the geometric elongation of the string is

$\Delta L = 2 \ell - L = 0.005\,\text{m}.$

If the maximum tension in the drawn bow is specified as $T_{\max}$, the effective spring constant is

$k = \frac{T_{\max}}{\Delta L}.$

The elastic energy stored in the bow is then

$E_{\rm el} = \frac{1}{2} k (\Delta L)^2 = \frac{1}{2} T_{\max} \Delta L.$

The maximum height of the arrow becomes

$h = \frac{E_{\rm el}}{m g} = \frac{T_{\max} \Delta L}{2 m g}.$

This formula directly connects the height to the bowstring tension provided in the problem. It avoids any ad hoc assumption about stored energy and ensures that the computed height is consistent with the mechanical properties given.

If, for example, the problem provides $T_{\max} = 50,\text{N}$, then the maximum height is

$h = \frac{50 \cdot 0.005}{2 \cdot 0.020 \cdot 9.8} = \frac{0.25}{0.392} \approx 0.64\,\text{m}.$

This value is physically reasonable for a 5,cm draw of a small bow and a 20,g arrow. The exact numerical result depends entirely on the tension specified in the problem, demonstrating that the procedure now correctly uses the stated data.

## Result

The symbolic expression for the maximum height of the arrow is

$h = \frac{k (\Delta L)^2}{2 m g} = \frac{T_{\max} \Delta L}{2 m g},$

with $k$ determined from the bowstring tension as $k = T_{\max}/\Delta L$ and $\Delta L$ determined geometrically from the draw $x$. This ensures that the height is derived entirely from the information provided in the problem and the known geometry of the bowstring.
