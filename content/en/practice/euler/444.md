[Project Euler Problem 444](https://projecteuler.net/problem=444)

## Solution

Starting from scratch, the key observation is that under optimal play the game has a surprisingly simple equilibrium:

Let $E(p)$ be the expected number of players still seated when the game ends.

A player compares:

- taking the best scratched ticket currently available, versus
- continuing with an unknown ticket.

By backward induction, the optimal strategy becomes a threshold rule: a player stays iff the currently best revealed ticket is below the continuation value. This induces exactly the same survival probability as a classical record process. The probability that the $i$-th player survives to the end is therefore $1/i$, giving

$$E(p)=\sum_{i=1}^{p}\frac1i = H_p$$

where $H_p$ is the $p$-th harmonic number. This identity also matches the given check:

$$E(111)=H_{111}=5.2912\ldots$$

as stated in the problem.

Now define

$$S_1(N)=\sum_{p=1}^{N}H_p$$

and recursively

$$S_k(N)=\sum_{p=1}^{N}S_{k-1}(p).$$

Using repeated summation (hockey-stick/binomial convolution), one gets the closed form

$$S_k(N)
=
\binom{N+k}{k}
\left(H_{N+k}-H_k\right).$$

Thus for $N=10^{14}$ and $k=20$,

$$S_{20}(10^{14})
=
\binom{10^{14}+20}{20}
\left(H_{10^{14}+20}-H_{20}\right).$$

Evaluating with high precision (using Euler–Maclaurin for the huge harmonic number and exact arithmetic for the binomial coefficient) gives:

$$S_{20}(10^{14})
\approx
1.2008567222003318\times 10^{263}.$$

Rounded to **10 significant digits** in the required scientific notation:

**Answer: 1.200856722e263**
