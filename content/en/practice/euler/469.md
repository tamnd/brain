[Project Euler Problem 469](https://projecteuler.net/problem=469)

## Solution

Let $A_n$ be the expected number of **occupied** chairs at the end of the process for a **line** of $n$ chairs, where adjacent occupied chairs are forbidden.

Let $B_n$ be the corresponding expectation for the original **circle** of $n$ chairs.

The required quantity is

$$E(n)=\frac{\text{expected empty chairs}}{n}
=1-\frac{B_n}{n}.$$

We are asked for $E(10^{18})$.

---

# 1. Mathematical analysis

## Step 1: Recurrence for a line

Consider a line of $n$ chairs.

The first knight chooses uniformly among the $n$ chairs.

If the first knight sits at position $k$, then chairs $k-1$ and $k+1$ become unusable, leaving two independent subproblems:

- left segment of length $k-2$,
- right segment of length $n-k-1$.

Therefore

$$A_n
=
1+\frac1n\sum_{k=1}^n\left(A_{k-2}+A_{n-k-1}\right),$$

with $A_0=0$ and $A_m=0$ for $m<0$.

By symmetry,

$$A_n
=
1+\frac{2}{n}\sum_{j=0}^{n-2} A_j.
\tag{1}$$

---

## Step 2: Convert to generating functions

Define

$$F(x)=\sum_{n\ge0} A_n x^n.$$

Multiply (1) by $n x^n$ and sum over $n\ge1$:

$$\sum_{n\ge1} nA_n x^n
=
\sum_{n\ge1} n x^n
+
2\sum_{n\ge1}\sum_{j=0}^{n-2}A_j x^n.$$

Now:

$$\sum_{n\ge1} nA_n x^n = xF'(x),$$

$$\sum_{n\ge1} n x^n = \frac{x}{(1-x)^2},$$

and

$$\sum_{n\ge1}\sum_{j=0}^{n-2}A_j x^n
=
\frac{x^2}{1-x}F(x).$$

Hence

$$xF'(x)
=
\frac{x}{(1-x)^2}
+
\frac{2x^2}{1-x}F(x).$$

Divide by $x$:

$$F'(x)-\frac{2x}{1-x}F(x)=\frac1{(1-x)^2}.$$

This linear ODE has integrating factor

$$\mu(x)=e^{2x}(1-x)^2.$$

Therefore

$$\frac{d}{dx}\left(F(x)e^{2x}(1-x)^2\right)=e^{2x}.$$

Integrating and using $F(0)=0$:

$$F(x)e^{2x}(1-x)^2
=
\frac{e^{2x}-1}{2}.$$

Thus

$$F(x)
=
\frac{1-e^{-2x}}{2(1-x)^2}.
\tag{2}$$

---

## Step 3: Asymptotics

We know

$$\frac1{(1-x)^2}
=
\sum_{n\ge0}(n+1)x^n.$$

From (2),

$$F(x)
=
\frac12(1-e^{-2x})(1-x)^{-2}.$$

The dominant singularity is at $x=1$, and $1-e^{-2x}$ is analytic there, so

$$A_n \sim \frac{1-e^{-2}}{2}n.$$

Hence the occupied density on a long line tends to

$$\frac{1-e^{-2}}{2}.$$

---

## Step 4: Convert back to the circle

For the circular table:

after the first knight sits down, both neighboring chairs are blocked, leaving a line of length $n-3$.

Therefore

$$B_n = 1 + A_{n-3}.$$

So

$$\frac{B_n}{n}
\to
\frac{1-e^{-2}}{2}.$$

Hence

$$E(n)
=
1-\frac{B_n}{n}
\to
1-\frac{1-e^{-2}}{2}
=
\frac{1+e^{-2}}{2}.$$

Since $10^{18}$ is enormous, the difference from the limit is negligible (indeed exponentially small relative to the requested precision).

Thus

$$E(10^{18})
=
\frac{1+e^{-2}}{2}.$$

Numerically,

$$e^{-2}=0.135335283236612691\ldots$$

so

$$E(10^{18})
=
0.567667641618306345\ldots$$

Rounded to 14 decimal places:

$$0.56766764161831$$

---

# 2. Python implementation

```
from decimal import Decimal, getcontext

# high precision
getcontext().prec = 50

# compute E = (1 + e^{-2}) / 2
E = (Decimal(1) + (-Decimal(2)).exp()) / Decimal(2)

# print rounded to 14 decimal places
print(f"{E:.14f}")
```

# 4. Final verification

We are told:

- $E(4)=1/2=0.5$,
- $E(6)=5/9\approx0.555555$.

Our limiting value

$$0.56766764161831$$

is slightly larger, which is completely reasonable as the finite-size effects diminish.

Also:

- occupied density tends to about $0.4323$,
- empty density tends to about $0.5677$,

consistent with random greedy independent sets on large cycles.
