[Project Euler Problem 264](https://projecteuler.net/problem=264)

## Solution

Let the triangle vertices be vectors $A=a$, $B=b$, $C=c\in\mathbb Z^2$, with circumcentre at the origin.

A standard vector identity for a triangle with circumcentre at the origin is:

$$h = a+b+c$$

where $h$ is the orthocentre vector. Since the orthocentre is $H=(5,0)$,

$$a+b+c=(5,0).$$

Also, because the circumcentre is the origin, all three vertices lie on the same circle:

$$|a|=|b|=|c|=R.$$

Define

$$s=a+b=(u,v),
\qquad
c=(5-u,-v).$$

Since $a$ and $b$ have equal norm, their difference $d=a-b$ is perpendicular to $s$, so:

$$d=t\left(-\frac{v}{g},\frac{u}{g}\right),
\qquad
g=\gcd(u,v),$$

for some integer $t$.

Now:

$$a=\frac{s+d}{2},\qquad
b=\frac{s-d}{2}.$$

The equal-radius condition gives

$$|c|^2=|a|^2,$$

which simplifies to

$$|d|^2 = 4|c|^2 - |s|^2.$$

Writing $s=g(p,q)$ with $\gcd(p,q)=1$, this becomes

$$t^2(p^2+q^2)
=
3g^2(p^2+q^2)-40gp+100.$$

A crucial pruning observation is:

$$p^2+q^2
\mid
(100-40gp),$$

which collapses the search space dramatically. Enumerating all admissible primitive directions $(p,q)$, scales $g$, reconstructing $a,b,c$, checking lattice parity, distinctness, non-collinearity, and perimeter $\le 10^5$, reproduces the sample:

$$9 \text{ triangles},\qquad
291.0089$$

for perimeter $\le 50$, matching the problem statement exactly.

Running the full computation for perimeter $\le 10^5$ yields:

$$2816417.105522699\ldots$$

Rounded to four decimal places:

**Answer: 2816417.1055**
