# LeetCode 2383 - Minimum Hours of Training to Win a Competition

## Problem Understanding

The problem gives us two initial values, `initialEnergy` and `initialExperience`, representing the player's starting energy and experience before entering a sequence of competitions.

There are `n` opponents, and each opponent has:

- `energy[i]`, the amount of energy required to defeat them
- `experience[i]`, the amount of experience required to defeat them

The opponents must be faced in order. To defeat opponent `i`, the player must satisfy two strict conditions at the moment of the fight:

- current energy > `energy[i]`
- current experience > `experience[i]`

After defeating an opponent:

- energy decreases by `energy[i]`
- experience increases by `experience[i]`

Before the competition begins, the player may train. Every hour of training increases either:

- energy by `1`, or
- experience by `1`

The goal is to determine the minimum total number of training hours needed so that all opponents can be defeated in sequence.

An important detail is that the comparisons are strict. Having equal energy or equal experience is not enough. The player must always have strictly greater values.

The constraints are small:

- `n <= 100`
- all values are at most `100`

This tells us that even straightforward simulation approaches are completely feasible. There is no need for advanced optimization or complex data structures.

Several edge cases are important:

- The player may already have enough energy and experience, requiring `0` training hours.
- Energy is consumed permanently, so total energy requirements accumulate across all fights.
- Experience grows after every victory, so earlier fights help with later fights.
- Because the requirement is strictly greater, if current experience equals an opponent's experience, at least one additional hour of training is required.

## Approaches

### Brute Force Approach

A brute force strategy would repeatedly simulate the competition while incrementally increasing energy or experience until all opponents can be defeated.

One possible implementation would try every possible amount of training, checking whether the competition becomes winnable. Another version could simulate training hour by hour and greedily decide which stat to increase.

This works because the search space is small enough for the given constraints. However, it is unnecessarily inefficient and more complicated than needed.

The main weakness is that it repeatedly re-simulates the same battles many times. We can determine the exact amount of training required directly without trial and error.

### Optimal Greedy Observation

The key insight is that energy and experience behave differently.

For energy:

- Energy only decreases during fights.
- It never increases again.
- Therefore, we only care about having enough total starting energy.

To defeat all opponents, we need:

$$\text{initialEnergy} > \sum energy[i] - \text{remaining margin}$$

More precisely, since energy must remain strictly greater before each fight, the minimum required starting energy is:

$$\sum energy[i] + 1$$

So the required energy training is:

$$\max(0, \sum energy[i] + 1 - initialEnergy)$$

Experience behaves differently:

- Experience increases after each victory.
- Therefore, we process opponents one by one.
- Whenever current experience is not strictly greater than the opponent's experience, we train just enough to make it valid.

This greedy strategy is optimal because any missing experience must eventually be added anyway, and adding the minimum necessary amount at each step never hurts future fights.

| Approach | Time Complexity | Space Complexity | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Brute Force | O(n × k) | O(1) | Repeatedly simulates fights for different training values |
| Optimal | O(n) | O(1) | Direct greedy computation for energy and experience |

Here, `k` represents the amount of extra training tested in the brute force solution.

## Algorithm Walkthrough

1. Compute the total energy required across all opponents.

Since energy decreases permanently after every fight, the player must start with strictly more energy than the total energy consumption. Therefore, the minimum starting energy required is:

$$\sum energy[i] + 1$$
2. Determine how much additional energy training is needed.

If the current `initialEnergy` is already large enough, no training is required. Otherwise, train exactly the missing amount.
3. Initialize the current experience.

Set a variable `currentExperience` equal to `initialExperience`.
4. Process opponents one by one.

For each opponent:

- If `currentExperience` is less than or equal to the opponent's experience, train enough hours so that:

$$currentExperience > experience[i]$$
- The exact amount needed is:

$$experience[i] - currentExperience + 1$$
5. Add the required training hours.

Increase both:

- total training hours
- current experience

by the amount trained.
6. Simulate defeating the opponent.

After winning, increase `currentExperience` by `experience[i]`.
7. Continue until all opponents are processed.

### Why it works

The algorithm is correct because it always performs the minimum training necessary at every step.

For energy, the total consumption is fixed, so the only requirement is starting with enough energy to survive all fights.

For experience, additional experience gained from victories helps future fights. Therefore, whenever current experience is insufficient, the optimal strategy is to train exactly enough to become strictly greater than the current opponent. Any extra training would only increase the answer unnecessarily.

## Python Solution

```python
from typing import List

class Solution:
    def minNumberOfHours(
        self,
        initialEnergy: int,
        initialExperience: int,
        energy: List[int],
        experience: List[int]
    ) -> int:

        total_training = 0

        # Energy training
        required_energy = sum(energy) + 1
        if initialEnergy < required_energy:
            total_training += required_energy - initialEnergy

        # Experience training
        current_experience = initialExperience

        for opponent_experience in experience:

            if current_experience <= opponent_experience:
                needed = opponent_experience - current_experience + 1
                total_training += needed
                current_experience += needed

            current_experience += opponent_experience

        return total_training
```

The implementation starts by calculating the minimum required starting energy. Since all energy losses are known in advance, we can compute the exact amount needed directly using the sum of the `energy` array.

The experience portion is handled incrementally. The variable `current_experience` tracks the player's experience throughout the competition.

Before each fight, the code checks whether the current experience is strictly greater than the opponent's experience. If not, the code computes the exact additional amount required and adds it to both the answer and the current experience.

After defeating the opponent, the player's experience increases naturally by the opponent's experience value.

This closely follows the greedy logic described earlier.

## Go Solution

```go
func minNumberOfHours(initialEnergy int, initialExperience int, energy []int, experience []int) int {
	totalTraining := 0

	// Energy training
	totalEnergyNeeded := 1
	for _, e := range energy {
		totalEnergyNeeded += e
	}

	if initialEnergy < totalEnergyNeeded {
		totalTraining += totalEnergyNeeded - initialEnergy
	}

	// Experience training
	currentExperience := initialExperience

	for _, exp := range experience {

		if currentExperience <= exp {
			needed := exp - currentExperience + 1
			totalTraining += needed
			currentExperience += needed
		}

		currentExperience += exp
	}

	return totalTraining
}
```

The Go implementation follows the exact same logic as the Python version.

Slices are used naturally for the `energy` and `experience` arrays. Since all values are small integers, integer overflow is not a concern in Go's standard `int` type.

Unlike Python, Go does not provide a built-in `sum()` function, so the total energy requirement is computed manually with a loop.

Nil slices are handled safely because ranging over a nil slice simply performs zero iterations.

## Edge Cases

### Strict Equality Cases

One of the most common bugs is forgetting that the requirements are strictly greater, not greater than or equal to.

For example, if current experience is `5` and the opponent's experience is also `5`, the player still loses. The implementation correctly handles this using:

```
if current_experience <= opponent_experience:
```

The `<=` condition ensures equal values still trigger training.

### Energy Exactly Equal to Total Consumption

Suppose the total opponent energy is `10` and the player starts with exactly `10` energy.

This is not enough because before the final fight the player's energy would not remain strictly greater than the opponent's energy. The implementation correctly requires:

```
sum(energy) + 1
```

This extra `1` guarantees strict inequality throughout the competition.

### Experience Growth Across Battles

A naive implementation might independently compare the initial experience against every opponent. That would be incorrect because defeating opponents increases experience.

The implementation properly updates:

```
current_experience += opponent_experience
```

after every victory, allowing earlier fights to help with later ones.
