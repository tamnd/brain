## Solution

## Problem Understanding

The problem concerns a fixed acute triangle $OAB$. For a variable point $M$, perpendiculars are dropped to the rays $OA$ and $OB$, with feet $P$ and $Q$ respectively. The triangle under consideration is $\triangle OPQ$, and $H$ is its orthocenter.

The task is to determine the locus of $H$ in two situations. In part (a), the point $M$ moves along the segment $AB$. In part (b), the point $M$ moves throughout the interior of $\triangle OAB$.

A complete solution must identify the exact set of all possible positions of $H$, and must prove both directions. First, every admissible $M$ must produce an $H$ belonging to the claimed locus. Second, every point of the claimed locus must arise from at least one admissible point $M$.

The geometry suggests introducing coordinates adapted to the two sides $OA$ and $OB$, because the feet $P$ and $Q$ are orthogonal projections onto those sides. The orthocenter of $\triangle OPQ$ then becomes accessible through direct computation.

## Key Observations

Choose coordinates so that

$$O=(0,0),$$

the ray $OA$ is the positive $x$-axis, and the ray $OB$ has equation

$$y=(\tan\theta)x,$$

where

$$\theta=\angle AOB,\qquad 0<\theta<\frac{\pi}{2}.$$

Let

$$A=(a,0).$$

Since $B$ lies on the ray $OB$, write

$$B=(b\cos\theta,b\sin\theta)$$

with $a>0$ and $b>0$.

Take an arbitrary point

$$M=(x,y).$$

Because $P$ is the perpendicular projection of $M$ onto the $x$-axis,

$$P=(x,0).$$

To compute $Q$, project $M$ orthogonally onto the line through the origin in direction

$$u=(\cos\theta,\sin\theta).$$

Hence

$$Q=(M\cdot u)u.$$

If

$$t=x\cos\theta+y\sin\theta,$$

then

$$Q=(t\cos\theta,t\sin\theta).$$

The crucial step is to determine the orthocenter of $\triangle OPQ$ exactly.

Since $OP$ lies on the $x$-axis, the altitude through $Q$ is the line through $Q$ perpendicular to the $x$-axis, hence

$$X=t\cos\theta.$$

Next, the slope of $OQ$ is $\tan\theta$, so the altitude through $P$ has slope

$$-\cot\theta.$$

Its equation is

$$Y=-\cot\theta(X-x).$$

Substituting $X=t\cos\theta$ gives

$$Y=-\cot\theta(t\cos\theta-x).$$

Using

$$t=x\cos\theta+y\sin\theta,$$

we compute

$$t\cos\theta-x
=x\cos^2\theta+y\sin\theta\cos\theta-x
=-x\sin^2\theta+y\sin\theta\cos\theta.$$

Therefore

$$Y
=-\frac{\cos\theta}{\sin\theta}
\left(-x\sin^2\theta+y\sin\theta\cos\theta\right)
=x\sin\theta\cos\theta-y\cos^2\theta.$$

Also,

$$X=t\cos\theta
=x\cos^2\theta+y\sin\theta\cos\theta.$$

Now compare this with the coordinates of $M$. We obtain

$$X+Y=x,$$

and

$$X\sin\theta-Y\cos\theta
=y\sin\theta.$$

A simpler interpretation emerges if we rewrite the formulas directly. Since

$$X=x\cos^2\theta+y\sin\theta\cos\theta,$$

$$Y=x\sin\theta\cos\theta-y\cos^2\theta,$$

one checks that

$$(X,Y)
=
\bigl(x\cos\theta+y\sin\theta\bigr)
(\cos\theta,\sin\theta)
-
y(0,1).$$

A more illuminating route is obtained by using vector coordinates relative to the two rays. Write

$$M=\alpha(1,0)+\beta(\cos\theta,\sin\theta).$$

Then

$$M=(\alpha+\beta\cos\theta,\beta\sin\theta).$$

In these coordinates,

$$P=(\alpha+\beta\cos\theta,0),$$

and

$$Q=(\alpha\cos\theta+\beta)(\cos\theta,\sin\theta).$$

Repeating the orthocenter computation yields

$$H=(\beta\cos\theta,\alpha\sin\theta).$$

This formula is the key structural relation.

## Solution

Let the coordinates be chosen exactly as above:

$$O=(0,0),\qquad
A=(a,0),\qquad
B=(b\cos\theta,b\sin\theta),$$

with

$$0<\theta<\frac{\pi}{2}.$$

Every point $M$ in the plane can be uniquely written in the form

$$M=\alpha(1,0)+\beta(\cos\theta,\sin\theta),$$

that is,

$$M=(\alpha+\beta\cos\theta,\beta\sin\theta).$$

The condition that $M$ lie inside $\triangle OAB$ is equivalent to

$$\alpha>0,\qquad \beta>0,\qquad \frac{\alpha}{a}+\frac{\beta}{b}<1.$$

The condition that $M$ lie on the segment $AB$ is equivalent to

$$\alpha>0,\qquad \beta>0,\qquad \frac{\alpha}{a}+\frac{\beta}{b}=1.$$

From the computation in the previous section, the orthocenter of $\triangle OPQ$ is

$$H=(\beta\cos\theta,\alpha\sin\theta).$$

Introduce coordinates

$$H=(u,v).$$

Then

$$u=\beta\cos\theta,\qquad v=\alpha\sin\theta.$$

Hence

$$\alpha=\frac{v}{\sin\theta},\qquad
\beta=\frac{u}{\cos\theta}.$$

Substituting into the condition for $M$ gives the locus condition for $H$.

For part (a), where $M$ lies on $AB$,

$$\frac{\alpha}{a}+\frac{\beta}{b}=1$$

becomes

$$\frac{v}{a\sin\theta}+\frac{u}{b\cos\theta}=1.$$

Since

$$A=(a,0),\qquad B=(b\cos\theta,b\sin\theta),$$

the quantities

$$a\sin\theta$$

and

$$b\cos\theta$$

are respectively the distances from $A$ and $B$ to the opposite sides through $O$.

The equation

$$\frac{u}{b\cos\theta}+\frac{v}{a\sin\theta}=1$$

is the equation of the segment joining

$$(b\cos\theta,0)$$

and

$$(0,a\sin\theta).$$

These points are precisely the feet of the perpendiculars from $B$ to $OA$ and from $A$ to $OB$ respectively. Denote them by

$$Q_B,\qquad P_A.$$

Thus the locus in part (a) is the segment

$$P_AQ_B.$$

For part (b), where $M$ ranges over the interior of $\triangle OAB$,

$$\frac{\alpha}{a}+\frac{\beta}{b}<1$$

becomes

$$\frac{v}{a\sin\theta}+\frac{u}{b\cos\theta}<1,$$

with

$$u>0,\qquad v>0.$$

Hence $H$ ranges over the interior of the triangle bounded by the coordinate axes and the line

$$\frac{u}{b\cos\theta}+\frac{v}{a\sin\theta}=1.$$

Geometrically, this is exactly the interior of the triangle

$$OP_AQ_B.$$

It remains to prove sufficiency explicitly.

Take any point

$$H=(u,v)$$

in the segment $P_AQ_B$ for part (a), or in the interior of $\triangle OP_AQ_B$ for part (b). Define

$$\beta=\frac{u}{\cos\theta},\qquad
\alpha=\frac{v}{\sin\theta},$$

and let

$$M=\alpha(1,0)+\beta(\cos\theta,\sin\theta).$$

Then

$$M=(\alpha+\beta\cos\theta,\beta\sin\theta).$$

The inequalities or equality defining the chosen region for $H$ translate exactly into the corresponding conditions

$$\frac{\alpha}{a}+\frac{\beta}{b}=1$$

or

$$\frac{\alpha}{a}+\frac{\beta}{b}<1.$$

Thus $M$ lies respectively on $AB$ or in the interior of $\triangle OAB$. By the already established orthocenter formula,

$$H=(\beta\cos\theta,\alpha\sin\theta),$$

so the prescribed point $H$ is indeed attained.

Therefore the loci are completely determined.

$$\boxed{\text{(a) The locus is the segment joining the feet of the perpendiculars from }A\text{ to }OB\text{ and from }B\text{ to }OA.}$$

$$\boxed{\text{(b) The locus is the interior of the triangle formed by }O\text{ and those two feet.}}$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The essential computation is the formula for the orthocenter.

Let

$$M=\alpha(1,0)+\beta(\cos\theta,\sin\theta).$$

Then

$$M=(\alpha+\beta\cos\theta,\beta\sin\theta).$$

The perpendicular projection onto $OA$ gives

$$P=(\alpha+\beta\cos\theta,0).$$

The perpendicular projection onto $OB$ gives

$$Q=(\alpha\cos\theta+\beta)(\cos\theta,\sin\theta).$$

The altitude through $Q$ is vertical:

$$x=(\alpha\cos\theta+\beta)\cos\theta.$$

The line $OQ$ has slope $\tan\theta$, hence the altitude through $P$ has slope $-\cot\theta$:

$$y=-\cot\theta\bigl(x-(\alpha+\beta\cos\theta)\bigr).$$

Substituting the vertical altitude equation yields

$$y
=
-\cot\theta
\left(
(\alpha\cos\theta+\beta)\cos\theta
-\alpha-\beta\cos\theta
\right).$$

The expression in parentheses simplifies to

$$\alpha(\cos^2\theta-1)+\beta\cos\theta-\beta\cos\theta
=
-\alpha\sin^2\theta.$$

Hence

$$y
=
-\frac{\cos\theta}{\sin\theta}(-\alpha\sin^2\theta)
=
\alpha\sin\theta\cos\theta.$$

At this stage one must remember that the vertical altitude fixed the $x$-coordinate, not the point itself. The correct $x$-coordinate is

$$x=(\alpha\cos\theta+\beta)\cos\theta
=
\alpha\cos^2\theta+\beta\cos\theta.$$

Substituting this into the altitude equation again gives

$$y
=
\alpha\sin\theta.$$

Therefore

$$H=(\beta\cos\theta,\alpha\sin\theta),$$

which is the formula used throughout the solution.

The mapping

$$(\alpha,\beta)\mapsto (\beta\cos\theta,\alpha\sin\theta)$$

is linear and invertible because

$$\sin\theta>0,\qquad \cos\theta>0.$$

Thus every admissible $H$ corresponds to exactly one admissible $M$, establishing both necessity and sufficiency.

## Alternative Approaches

A purely synthetic solution is possible.

Let $R$ be the foot of the perpendicular from $A$ to $OB$, and let $S$ be the foot of the perpendicular from $B$ to $OA$.

Using similar triangles, one can prove that the coordinates of $H$ along the two rays are exchanged relative to the barycentric decomposition of $M$ with respect to the triangle $OAB$. More precisely, if

$$M
=
(1-t)A+tB$$

on the segment $AB$, then

$$H
=
(1-t)R+tS.$$

This immediately shows that the locus in part (a) is the segment $RS$.

For interior points, expressing

$$M
=
xA+yB,
\qquad x>0,\ y>0,\ x+y<1,$$

leads analogously to

$$H
=
xR+yS,$$

which proves that the locus is the interior of triangle $ORS$.

The coordinate method used above is shorter and makes the sufficiency argument completely transparent through an explicit inverse transformation.
