**Difficulty:** 🔴 Hard  
**Topics:** Array, Binary Search, Sliding Window, Prefix Sum

## Solution

## Problem Understanding

The problem gives us a list of fruit locations on a one dimensional infinite number line. Each element in `fruits` is a pair `[position, amount]`, meaning that there are `amount` fruits available at coordinate `position`.

We start at `startPos`, and we may walk left or right along the x-axis. Moving one unit costs one step, and we may use at most `k` total steps.

Whenever we visit a position containing fruits, we automatically collect all fruits there. Since every fruit position is unique and sorted in ascending order, we can think of the fruit positions as ordered checkpoints on the number line.

The goal is to determine the maximum number of fruits we can collect while staying within the step budget.

A very important detail is that movement is continuous. If we move from position 5 to position 8, we pass through 6 and 7. Therefore, any fruit positions along the path are also collected automatically.

The constraints are large:

- `fruits.length` can be up to `10^5`
- positions can be as large as `2 * 10^5`
- `k` can also be up to `2 * 10^5`

These constraints immediately tell us that quadratic solutions are impossible. We need something around `O(n)` or `O(n log n)`.

Another critical observation is that the fruit positions are already sorted. Sorted input often suggests using techniques such as:

- sliding window
- prefix sums
- binary search
- two pointers

There are several edge cases that can easily cause bugs:

- We may not be able to reach any fruit at all.
- All reachable fruits may lie entirely on one side of `startPos`.
- The optimal route may require going left first and then right, or right first and then left.
- The starting position itself may contain fruits.
- A naive implementation may incorrectly compute travel distance when changing direction.

The hardest part of the problem is computing the minimum number of steps needed to cover a segment of fruit positions.

Suppose we collect every fruit between positions `left` and `right`. If `startPos` lies inside that interval, there are two possible strategies:

1. Go left first, then reverse and go right.
2. Go right first, then reverse and go left.

The minimum required steps become:

$$\min(
2 \cdot (startPos - left) + (right - startPos),
2 \cdot (right - startPos) + (startPos - left)
)$$

This formula is the key insight that enables the optimal solution.

## Approaches

### Brute Force

A brute force approach would consider every possible interval of fruit positions and determine whether the interval can be collected within `k` steps.

For every pair `(i, j)`:

- Compute the total fruits from `i` to `j`
- Compute the minimum travel distance needed to cover positions `fruits[i][0]` through `fruits[j][0]`
- If the required distance is at most `k`, update the answer

Since there are `O(n^2)` intervals, this approach is far too slow for `n = 10^5`.

Even with prefix sums for fast range summation, the interval enumeration alone is quadratic.

### Optimal Approach

The key observation is that the fruit positions are sorted, and the valid intervals form a monotonic structure.

If a window `[left, right]` requires too many steps, then expanding it further will never reduce the required distance.

This property allows us to use a sliding window.

We maintain a window of fruit positions that can all be harvested within `k` steps. As we extend the right boundary, we shrink the left boundary whenever the travel cost becomes too large.

To efficiently compute fruit totals inside the window, we maintain a running sum.

The only nontrivial part is computing the minimum steps needed to cover the current interval.

For an interval `[leftPos, rightPos]`:

- Going left first costs:

$$2 \cdot (startPos - leftPos) + (rightPos - startPos)$$

- Going right first costs:

$$2 \cdot (rightPos - startPos) + (startPos - leftPos)$$

We take the minimum of these two values.

Because each pointer moves at most `n` times, the overall complexity becomes linear.

| Approach | Time Complexity | Space Complexity | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Brute Force | O(n²) | O(1) or O(n) | Enumerates all intervals |
| Optimal | O(n) | O(1) | Sliding window with interval distance computation |

## Algorithm Walkthrough

1. Initialize two pointers, `left` and `right`, representing the current window of fruit positions.
2. Maintain a running variable `current_fruits` storing the total fruits inside the current window.
3. Iterate `right` from `0` to `n - 1`.
4. Add the fruit amount at `right` into `current_fruits`.
5. Let:

- `leftPos = fruits[left][0]`
- `rightPos = fruits[right][0]`
6. Compute the minimum steps needed to harvest every fruit between `leftPos` and `rightPos`.

The required travel distance is:

$$\min(
2 \cdot \max(0, startPos - leftPos) + \max(0, rightPos - startPos),
2 \cdot \max(0, rightPos - startPos) + \max(0, startPos - leftPos)
)$$

The `max(0, ...)` terms handle intervals entirely on one side of `startPos`.

1. While the required distance exceeds `k`:

- Remove the fruit amount at `left` from `current_fruits`
- Increment `left`
- Recompute the interval distance
2. After ensuring the window is valid, update the answer using `current_fruits`.
3. Continue until all positions have been processed.

### Why it works

The sliding window always represents a contiguous interval of fruit positions.

For any interval, the minimum possible walking distance is exactly the smaller of:

- going left first then right
- going right first then left

If the interval becomes invalid, moving `left` forward can only decrease the required travel distance. Therefore, once an interval is invalid, expanding it further cannot restore validity without shrinking the left boundary.

Because both pointers only move forward, every fruit position enters and leaves the window at most once, guaranteeing linear time complexity.

## Python Solution

```python
from typing import List

class Solution:
    def maxTotalFruits(self, fruits: List[List[int]], startPos: int, k: int) -> int:
        def steps_needed(left_pos: int, right_pos: int) -> int:
            left_distance = max(0, startPos - left_pos)
            right_distance = max(0, right_pos - startPos)

            return min(
                2 * left_distance + right_distance,
                2 * right_distance + left_distance
            )

        left = 0
        current_fruits = 0
        answer = 0

        for right in range(len(fruits)):
            current_fruits += fruits[right][1]

            while left <= right and steps_needed(
                fruits[left][0],
                fruits[right][0]
            ) > k:
                current_fruits -= fruits[left][1]
                left += 1

            answer = max(answer, current_fruits)

        return answer
```

The implementation follows the sliding window strategy directly.

The helper function `steps_needed()` computes the minimum walking distance required to cover the current interval. It calculates how far the interval extends to the left and right of `startPos`.

If the entire interval lies on one side, one of those distances becomes zero automatically.

Inside the main loop:

- `right` expands the window
- `current_fruits` tracks the sum inside the window
- the `while` loop shrinks the window until it becomes feasible again

At every valid window, we update the maximum harvested fruits.

The solution is efficient because every fruit position is processed at most twice:

- once when entering the window
- once when leaving the window

## Go Solution

```go
func maxTotalFruits(fruits [][]int, startPos int, k int) int {
    stepsNeeded := func(leftPos, rightPos int) int {
        leftDistance := max(0, startPos-leftPos)
        rightDistance := max(0, rightPos-startPos)

        option1 := 2*leftDistance + rightDistance
        option2 := 2*rightDistance + leftDistance

        return min(option1, option2)
    }

    left := 0
    currentFruits := 0
    answer := 0

    for right := 0; right < len(fruits); right++ {
        currentFruits += fruits[right][1]

        for left <= right &&
            stepsNeeded(fruits[left][0], fruits[right][0]) > k {

            currentFruits -= fruits[left][1]
            left++
        }

        answer = max(answer, currentFruits)
    }

    return answer
}

func min(a, b int) int {
    if a < b {
        return a
    }
    return b
}

func max(a, b int) int {
    if a > b {
        return a
    }
    return b
}
```

The Go implementation mirrors the Python logic closely.

Because Go does not provide built in `min` and `max` helpers for integers, we define them manually.

Slices are used naturally for the `fruits` array. Since all arithmetic stays within reasonable integer limits, standard `int` values are sufficient.

The sliding window technique guarantees linear complexity because the `left` and `right` pointers never move backward.

Although there is a nested `while` loop, each element is removed from the window at most once. Therefore, the total number of iterations across the entire execution remains linear.

## Edge Cases

One important edge case occurs when `k = 0`. In this scenario, the player cannot move at all, so only fruits located exactly at `startPos` may be collected. A buggy implementation might accidentally allow intervals extending beyond the starting position. The sliding window handles this correctly because any interval requiring positive movement immediately exceeds the step limit.

Another critical edge case is when every fruit position lies entirely on one side of `startPos`. Many incorrect solutions assume the interval always spans both sides and incorrectly apply the turning formula. This implementation avoids that issue by using:

$$\max(0, startPos - leftPos)$$

and

$$\max(0, rightPos - startPos)$$

which naturally reduce the unused side distance to zero.

A third tricky case occurs when the optimal route requires changing direction. For example, the best solution may involve moving left first and then turning right. A naive greedy strategy that commits to one direction would fail. The algorithm handles this correctly because the distance formula explicitly considers both possible traversal orders and chooses the minimum.

Another subtle case is when the starting position itself contains fruits. Since harvesting occurs automatically upon reaching a position, fruits at `startPos` should always be included even if no movement happens. The sliding window naturally includes such positions because intervals may begin and end at `startPos` with zero travel cost.
