## Exploration

The previous construction failed because adding a small geometric perturbation does not control the ratios when the original sequence varies wildly. Before building a corrected argument, test the ratio condition on small cases.

For $n=1$, there is no ratio condition at all. Any $b_1>a_1$ with

$$b_1<\frac{1+q}{1-q}a_1$$

works. Since

$$\frac{1+q}{1-q}>1,$$

this is always possible.

For $n=2$, the ratio condition becomes

$$q<\frac{b_2}{b_1}<\frac1q.$$

Suppose $a_1=1$, $a_2=100$, and $q=\frac12$. The flawed construction gave

$$b_1=1+\frac c2,\qquad b_2=100+c,$$

hence

$$\frac{b_2}{b_1}\approx 100,$$

which violates

$$\frac{b_2}{b_1}<2.$$

Thus the original approach genuinely fails.

The ratio condition suggests that neighboring terms cannot differ too much. A natural way to enforce this is to define each $b_k$ using nearby $a_i$ with geometric weights. Try

$$b_k=\sum_{i=1}^n a_i q^{|i-k|}.$$

Test this on small examples.

Take $n=2$. Then

$$b_1=a_1+qa_2,\qquad b_2=qa_1+a_2.$$

Compute

$$b_2-qb_1=(1-q^2)a_2>0,$$

so

$$\frac{b_2}{b_1}>q.$$

Similarly,

$$b_1-qb_2=(1-q^2)a_1>0,$$

so

$$\frac{b_2}{b_1}<\frac1q.$$

The ratio condition holds automatically.

Take $n=3$. Then

$$b_1=a_1+qa_2+q^2a_3,$$

$$b_2=qa_1+a_2+qa_3,$$

$$b_3=q^2a_1+qa_2+a_3.$$

Now

$$b_2-qb_1=(1-q^2)(a_2+qa_3)>0,$$

and

$$b_1-qb_2=(1-q^2)a_1>0.$$

Again,

$$q<\frac{b_2}{b_1}<\frac1q.$$

The same pattern appears between $b_2$ and $b_3$.

Next check the sum bound. Since

$$b_k=\sum_{i=1}^n a_i q^{|i-k|},$$

we have

$$\sum_{k=1}^n b_k
=
\sum_{i=1}^n a_i\sum_{k=1}^n q^{|i-k|}.$$

The inner sum satisfies

$$\sum_{k=1}^n q^{|i-k|}
<
1+2\sum_{m=1}^\infty q^m
=
\frac{1+q}{1-q}.$$

This exactly matches the required constant.

The construction survives all small-case tests and directly addresses the flaw in the rejected proof.

## Problem Understanding

We are given positive real numbers

$$a_1,a_2,\dots,a_n$$

and a parameter

$$0<q<1.$$

We must construct positive numbers

$$b_1,b_2,\dots,b_n$$

such that three conditions hold simultaneously.

First, every $b_k$ must strictly exceed the corresponding $a_k$:

$$b_k>a_k.$$

Second, consecutive terms must satisfy the two-sided ratio bound

$$q<\frac{b_{k+1}}{b_k}<\frac1q.$$

Since $0<q<1$, neighboring terms may vary, but only by a controlled factor.

Third, the total sum must remain bounded:

$$\sum_{k=1}^n b_k
<
\frac{1+q}{1-q}
\sum_{k=1}^n a_k.$$

The challenge is that the $a_k$ may fluctuate arbitrarily. A valid construction must work even when adjacent $a_k$ differ enormously.

## Key Observations

The failed proof tried to perturb the sequence by a tiny geometric correction. That cannot work uniformly because the ratios are still largely governed by the original $a_k$.

Instead, the construction should smooth the sequence globally. The ratio condition suggests that neighboring terms should share much of the same data. A symmetric weighted average using geometric weights naturally has this property.

Define

$$b_k=\sum_{i=1}^n a_i q^{|i-k|}.$$

Every $a_i$ contributes to every $b_k$, with exponentially decaying influence as the distance from $k$ increases.

This construction immediately gives

$$b_k>a_k,$$

because the term with $i=k$ equals $a_k$ and all other terms are nonnegative.

The ratio condition becomes manageable because the expressions for $b_k$ and $b_{k+1}$ differ only by one power of $q$. Subtracting suitable multiples produces telescoping cancellations with strictly positive remainders.

The sum bound follows from estimating the geometric kernel:

$$\sum_{k=1}^n q^{|i-k|}
<
1+2\sum_{m=1}^\infty q^m
=
\frac{1+q}{1-q}.$$

## Solution

Define

$$b_k=\sum_{i=1}^n a_i q^{|i-k|}
\qquad (k=1,2,\dots,n).$$

We verify that this sequence satisfies all required conditions.

First consider condition (a). Since the term corresponding to $i=k$ equals $a_k$, we have

$$b_k
=
a_k+\sum_{i\ne k} a_i q^{|i-k|}.$$

All numbers $a_i$ are positive and $q^{|i-k|}>0$, hence

$$b_k>a_k.$$

Now prove condition (b). Fix $k$ with $1\le k\le n-1$.

Compute

$$b_{k+1}-qb_k.$$

Using the definition,

$$b_{k+1}
=
\sum_{i=1}^n a_i q^{|i-(k+1)|},$$

and

$$qb_k
=
\sum_{i=1}^n a_i q^{|i-k|+1}.$$

Separate the indices into the two ranges $i\le k$ and $i\ge k+1$.

If $i\le k$, then

$$|i-(k+1)|=k+1-i,$$

and

$$|i-k|+1=(k-i)+1=k+1-i.$$

Thus the corresponding terms cancel exactly.

If $i\ge k+1$, then

$$|i-(k+1)|=i-k-1,$$

and

$$|i-k|+1=(i-k)+1=i-k+1.$$

Hence

$$q^{|i-(k+1)|}-q^{|i-k|+1}
=
q^{i-k-1}-q^{i-k+1}
=
(1-q^2)q^{i-k-1}.$$

Therefore

$$b_{k+1}-qb_k
=
(1-q^2)\sum_{i=k+1}^n a_i q^{i-k-1}.$$

Every term on the right-hand side is positive, so

$$b_{k+1}-qb_k>0.$$

Hence

$$\frac{b_{k+1}}{b_k}>q.$$

Next compute

$$b_k-qb_{k+1}.$$

For $i\ge k+1$, the terms cancel exactly, while for $i\le k$ we obtain

$$q^{k-i}-q^{k+2-i}
=
(1-q^2)q^{k-i}.$$

Thus

$$b_k-qb_{k+1}
=
(1-q^2)\sum_{i=1}^k a_i q^{k-i}.$$

Again every term is positive, so

$$b_k-qb_{k+1}>0.$$

Hence

$$\frac{b_{k+1}}{b_k}<\frac1q.$$

Combining the two inequalities gives

$$q<\frac{b_{k+1}}{b_k}<\frac1q,$$

which proves condition (b).

Finally prove condition (c). Summing over $k$ gives

$$\sum_{k=1}^n b_k
=
\sum_{k=1}^n\sum_{i=1}^n a_i q^{|i-k|}.$$

Interchanging the order of summation,

$$\sum_{k=1}^n b_k
=
\sum_{i=1}^n a_i\sum_{k=1}^n q^{|i-k|}.$$

Fix $i$. Then

$$\sum_{k=1}^n q^{|i-k|}
=
1+\sum_{m=1}^{i-1} q^m+\sum_{m=1}^{n-i} q^m.$$

Since all terms are positive,

$$\sum_{k=1}^n q^{|i-k|}
<
1+2\sum_{m=1}^\infty q^m.$$

Because

$$\sum_{m=1}^\infty q^m=\frac{q}{1-q},$$

we obtain

$$\sum_{k=1}^n q^{|i-k|}
<
1+\frac{2q}{1-q}
=
\frac{1+q}{1-q}.$$

Therefore

$$\sum_{k=1}^n b_k
<
\sum_{i=1}^n a_i\cdot\frac{1+q}{1-q}
=
\frac{1+q}{1-q}\sum_{i=1}^n a_i.$$

All three required conditions hold. This completes the construction.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The critical flaw in the rejected proof was the unsupported assertion that adding a tiny geometric perturbation forces

$$\frac{b_{k+1}}{b_k}$$

into the interval

$$(q,1/q).$$

That statement is false when the original sequence varies sharply.

The corrected construction avoids this issue entirely. The ratio bounds are not obtained from limiting behavior or asymptotics. They follow from exact identities:

$$b_{k+1}-qb_k
=
(1-q^2)\sum_{i=k+1}^n a_i q^{i-k-1},$$

and

$$b_k-qb_{k+1}
=
(1-q^2)\sum_{i=1}^k a_i q^{k-i}.$$

Since every coefficient is strictly positive, both differences are strictly positive. No approximation argument is involved.

The sum estimate is also strict. For fixed $i$,

$$\sum_{k=1}^n q^{|i-k|}
=
1+\sum_{m=1}^{i-1}q^m+\sum_{m=1}^{n-i}q^m.$$

Each finite geometric sum is strictly smaller than

$$\sum_{m=1}^\infty q^m,$$

hence

$$\sum_{k=1}^n q^{|i-k|}
<
1+2\sum_{m=1}^\infty q^m
=
\frac{1+q}{1-q}.$$

This produces the strict inequality required in condition (c).

For $n=1$, the construction gives

$$b_1=a_1,$$

which does not satisfy the strict inequality $b_1>a_1$. Thus the formula must be adjusted in this special case. Choose any

$$b_1
\in
\left(a_1,\frac{1+q}{1-q}a_1\right).$$

Such a number exists because

$$\frac{1+q}{1-q}>1.$$

For $n\ge2$, the original construction already gives strict inequality in condition (a), because each $b_k$ contains at least one additional positive term besides $a_k$.

## Alternative Approaches

Another successful approach defines two auxiliary sequences recursively:

$$u_k=\max(a_k,qu_{k-1}),$$

moving from left to right, and

$$v_k=\max(u_k,qv_{k+1}),$$

moving from right to left. One then sets

$$b_k=v_k+\varepsilon$$

for sufficiently small $\varepsilon>0$. The recursive structure enforces the ratio bounds directly.

That method is more technical because the sum estimate requires careful telescoping inequalities. The symmetric convolution

$$b_k=\sum_{i=1}^n a_i q^{|i-k|}$$

is more transparent. The ratio bounds emerge from exact cancellations, and the sum bound follows immediately from geometric-series estimates.
