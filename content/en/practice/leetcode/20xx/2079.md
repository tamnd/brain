## Solution

## Problem Understanding

In this problem, we are given a row of plants where each plant requires a certain amount of water. The plants are positioned on a one dimensional axis, with plant `i` located at position `x = i`. There is also a river at position `x = -1`, and that is the only place where we can refill the watering can.

We start at the river with a completely full watering can whose maximum capacity is given by `capacity`. We must water the plants strictly from left to right. The important restriction is that we are not allowed to refill early. We may only go back to the river when the remaining water is insufficient to completely water the next plant.

The task is to compute the total number of walking steps required to water all plants.

The input consists of:

- `plants`, where `plants[i]` is the amount of water required by the `i`th plant
- `capacity`, the maximum amount of water the watering can can hold

The output is a single integer representing the total walking distance.

The movement rules are important:

- Moving one unit along the x-axis costs one step
- Going from the river at `x = -1` to plant `i` costs `i + 1` steps
- Returning from plant `i` to the river also costs `i + 1` steps

The constraints are relatively small:

- `n <= 1000`
- Each plant requirement can be large, up to `10^6`
- `capacity` can be as large as `10^9`

These constraints tell us that we do not need highly sophisticated optimization techniques. A direct simulation is completely feasible.

There are several important edge cases:

- A plant may require exactly the remaining amount of water
- We may need to refill before almost every plant
- There may be only one plant
- The watering can capacity may exactly match some plant requirements
- The problem guarantees `max(plants[i]) <= capacity`, so every plant can always be watered after a refill

That final guarantee is very important because it means we never encounter an impossible situation.

## Approaches

### Brute Force Approach

A straightforward approach is to literally simulate every movement step by step.

We could track the gardener's current position and simulate movement one unit at a time while updating the step count. Whenever the remaining water is insufficient for the next plant, we walk back to the river step by step, refill, and then walk forward again.

This approach is correct because it directly models the process described in the problem statement. Every movement is explicitly counted.

However, this approach is unnecessarily detailed. We do not actually need to simulate each individual movement because the walking distance can be computed mathematically. If we are currently at plant `i - 1` and need to refill before watering plant `i`, then:

- Returning to the river costs `i` steps
- Walking from the river to plant `i` costs `i + 1` steps

Instead of simulating all intermediate positions, we can directly add these distances.

### Optimal Approach

The key insight is that we only need to track:

- The current amount of water remaining
- The current plant index
- The total number of steps

For every plant:

- We always spend one step moving from the previous position to the current plant
- Before watering the plant, we check whether the remaining water is enough
- If not, we add the round trip distance needed to refill

Suppose we are about to water plant `i`.

If we must refill:

- We are currently at plant `i - 1`
- Returning to the river costs `i` steps
- Coming back to plant `i` costs `i + 1` steps

Together, this refill operation adds `2 * i + 1` movement steps. However, a cleaner implementation is:

- Add `2 * i` extra steps when refilling
- Still add the normal forward movement step afterward

This leads to a very clean simulation.

| Approach | Time Complexity | Space Complexity | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Brute Force | O(total distance walked) | O(1) | Simulates every individual movement |
| Optimal | O(n) | O(1) | Direct simulation using arithmetic distances |

## Algorithm Walkthrough

1. Initialize three variables:

- `steps = 0` to store the total walking distance
- `water = capacity` to track remaining water
- Iterate through the plants from left to right
2. For each plant at index `i`, first check whether the remaining water is enough to water it completely.
3. If `water < plants[i]`, then we must refill before watering this plant.

- We are currently standing at plant `i - 1`
- Returning to the river costs `i` steps
- Walking back to plant `i` costs another `i` extra steps beyond the normal forward move
- Therefore, add `2 * i` to `steps`
- Reset `water = capacity`
4. Move to the current plant.

- Add `1` to `steps`
- This represents moving one position forward
5. Water the current plant.

- Subtract `plants[i]` from `water`
6. Continue until all plants are watered.
7. Return `steps`.

### Why it works

The algorithm maintains the invariant that before processing plant `i`, the gardener is effectively positioned at plant `i - 1` with the correct remaining water amount.

Whenever the remaining water is insufficient, the algorithm adds exactly the extra distance required to return to the river and come back. Since every plant is processed exactly once in order, and every required refill is counted exactly once, the total number of steps is computed correctly.

## Python Solution

```python
from typing import List

class Solution:
    def wateringPlants(self, plants: List[int], capacity: int) -> int:
        steps = 0
        water = capacity

        for i, needed in enumerate(plants):
            if water < needed:
                steps += 2 * i
                water = capacity

            steps += 1
            water -= needed

        return steps
```

The implementation directly follows the algorithm described earlier.

The variable `steps` tracks the total walking distance, while `water` stores the remaining water in the watering can.

We iterate through the plants using `enumerate` so we have both the plant index and the required amount of water.

Before watering a plant, we check whether the current water supply is sufficient. If not, we simulate a refill by adding `2 * i` extra steps and resetting the water level back to full capacity.

After handling any required refill, we always move one step forward to the current plant and subtract the plant's water requirement from the remaining water.

The implementation is compact because the movement distances can be computed mathematically rather than simulated position by position.

## Go Solution

```go
func wateringPlants(plants []int, capacity int) int {
    steps := 0
    water := capacity

    for i, needed := range plants {
        if water < needed {
            steps += 2 * i
            water = capacity
        }

        steps++
        water -= needed
    }

    return steps
}
```

The Go implementation is nearly identical to the Python version.

Go uses slices instead of Python lists, and the `range` loop provides both the index and the current plant requirement.

All values safely fit inside Go's standard `int` type because the maximum possible step count is well within integer limits for the given constraints.

There are no special nil handling requirements because the constraints guarantee at least one plant.

## Edge Cases

One important edge case occurs when the watering can becomes exactly empty after watering a plant. In this situation, the algorithm must not immediately refill. The rules state that refilling only happens before watering the next plant if there is insufficient water. The implementation handles this correctly because it checks refill conditions only at the beginning of each iteration.

Another important case is when every plant requires exactly the full capacity of the watering can. In this scenario, the gardener must refill before every plant except the first one. This can easily produce off by one errors in step counting. The implementation correctly adds `2 * i` extra steps before each refill and then still adds the forward movement step afterward.

A third edge case is when the watering can capacity is much larger than the total water required by all plants. In this situation, no refill should ever occur. The algorithm naturally handles this because the refill condition `water < needed` never becomes true.

A subtle edge case involves the very first plant. Since we start at the river, moving to plant `0` costs exactly one step. The implementation handles this uniformly with all other plants by always adding one movement step per iteration.

Finally, the problem guarantees that every plant requirement is at most the watering can capacity. Without this guarantee, some plants could never be watered even after refilling. Because the guarantee exists, the algorithm never needs to handle impossible states.
