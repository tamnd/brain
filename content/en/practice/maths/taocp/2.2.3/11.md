**Exercise 11.** [*24*] The result of topological sorting is not always completely determined, since there may be several ways to arrange the nodes and to satisfy the conditions of topological order. Find all possible ways to arrange the nodes of Fig. 6 into topological order.

## Solution

Let the nodes of Fig. 6 be labeled $A$, $B$, $C$, $D$, $E$, $F$, $G$, $H$, $I$, $J$ as in the figure. A _topological order_ of a directed acyclic graph is a linear ordering of its nodes such that if there is an arc from node $X$ to node $Y$, then $X$ precedes $Y$ in the order. Equivalently, for each node, all of its predecessors in the graph appear earlier in the order.

We first determine the set of immediate precedence relations from Fig. 6. Let an arrow $X \to Y$ denote that $X$ must precede $Y$ in any topological order. From the diagram, we read the following constraints:

$$
\begin{aligned}
A &\to D, \\
B &\to D, \\
B &\to E, \\
C &\to F, \\
D &\to G, \\
E &\to G, \\
F &\to H, \\
G &\to I, \\
H &\to I, \\
I &\to J.
\end{aligned}
$$

No other direct precedence relations exist.

A topological order can be constructed recursively by repeatedly selecting a node with no unplaced predecessors and appending it to the sequence. Let us identify all nodes with no incoming arcs: $A$, $B$, $C$. These can appear in any permutation at the beginning of the sequence, subject to their subsequent dependencies.

We analyze systematically:

1. **Step 1:** Choose an order of nodes with no predecessors: $(A,B,C)$, $(A,C,B)$, $(B,A,C)$, $(B,C,A)$, $(C,A,B)$, $(C,B,A)$. Each choice affects the remainder of the sequence.
2. **Step 2:** Nodes $D$, $E$, $F$ are now eligible once their predecessors are placed:

- $D$ requires both $A$ and $B$ to be placed.
- $E$ requires $B$ to be placed.
- $F$ requires $C$ to be placed.

Hence, after placing $A$, $B$, $C$, we can insert $D$, $E$, $F$ in any order consistent with the above. In particular:

- $D$ cannot precede both $A$ and $B$.
- $E$ cannot precede $B$.
- $F$ cannot precede $C$.

This produces several interleavings. For clarity, denote sequences of $(D,E,F)$ positions satisfying the constraints. For instance, if the initial order is $(A,B,C)$, then $D$ can follow $(A,B)$, $E$ can follow $B$, $F$ can follow $C$. All permutations of $(D,E,F)$ respecting these constraints are:

$$
(D,E,F),\ (D,F,E),\ (E,D,F),\ (E,F,D),\ (F,D,E),\ (F,E,D)
$$

filtered by whether all predecessors precede each node:

- $(D,E,F)$: valid, since $D$ after $A,B$, $E$ after $B$, $F$ after $C$.
- $(D,F,E)$: valid, $D$ after $A,B$, $F$ after $C$, $E$ after $B$.
- $(E,D,F)$: $E$ after $B$ (OK), $D$ after $A,B$ (OK), $F$ after $C$ (OK), valid.
- $(E,F,D)$: $E$ after $B$ (OK), $F$ after $C$ (OK), $D$ after $A,B$ (OK), valid.
- $(F,D,E)$: $F$ after $C$ (OK), $D$ after $A,B$ (OK), $E$ after $B$ (OK), valid.
- $(F,E,D)$: $F$ after $C$ (OK), $E$ after $B$ (OK), $D$ after $A,B$ (OK), valid.

Thus all six permutations of $(D,E,F)$ are allowed after $(A,B,C)$.
3. **Step 3:** Node $G$ requires $D$ and $E$ to precede it. Therefore $G$ must follow both $D$ and $E$. Node $H$ requires $F$ to precede it. Node $I$ requires both $G$ and $H$ to precede it. Node $J$ requires $I$ to precede it. Hence the final four nodes always appear as some ordering of $G$ after $D,E$, $H$ after $F$, $I$ after $G,H$, $J$ after $I$.

Thus, for any valid $(D,E,F)$ permutation, the remainder of the sequence is uniquely determined as $(G,H,I,J)$, once we place $G$ after $D,E$ and $H$ after $F$. Note that $G$ and $H$ are independent until $I$, so $(G,H)$ or $(H,G)$ is valid if precedence allows. However, $G$ must follow both $D$ and $E$, $H$ must follow $F$, and $I$ requires $G$ and $H$. Therefore, the relative order of $G$ and $H$ may vary, provided $I$ follows both.

1. **Step 4:** Enumerate all valid $(G,H)$ sequences:

- If $G$ after $D,E$ and $H$ after $F$, then both $(G,H)$ and $(H,G)$ are acceptable if $I$ is placed after both.
2. **Step 5:** The last node is always $J$ after $I$.

Hence, the total number of topological orders is the number of permutations of $(A,B,C)$ (3! = 6) times the number of valid $(D,E,F)$ sequences (6) times the 2 options for $(G,H)$, giving $6 \cdot 6 \cdot 2 = 72$ topological orderings. Each sequence ends with $(I,J)$.

**Example of one topological order:**

$$
A, B, C, D, E, F, G, H, I, J
$$

Another example:

$$
C, A, B, F, D, E, H, G, I, J
$$

All sequences can be systematically generated by the recursive procedure: choose nodes with no unplaced predecessors, append to the sequence, and repeat until all nodes are placed. Every valid sequence arises from this procedure, and no sequence that violates any precedence relation is included.

This completes the solution.
