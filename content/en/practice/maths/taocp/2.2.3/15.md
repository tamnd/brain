[Section 2.2.3: Linked Allocation](../)

**Exercise 15.** [*M25*] Show that for any partial ordering on a finite set $S$ there is a *unique* set of irredundant relations that characterizes this ordering, as in (18) and Fig. 6. Is the same fact true also when $S$ is an infinite set?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 41s  

## Solution

Let $\preceq$ be a partial ordering on a finite set $S$. Following the terminology of (18) and Fig. 6, call a relation $x \prec y$ _irredundant_ if it cannot be inferred transitively from other relations. More precisely, define an ordered pair $(x,y)$, with $x \ne y$ and $x \preceq y$, to be irredundant when there exists no element $z \in S$ satisfying

$$
x \prec z \prec y.
$$

The problem asks whether every partial ordering is characterized by a unique set of such relations.

Let $R$ denote the set of all irredundant relations:

$$
R=\{(x,y)\in S\times S : x\prec y \text{ and there is no } z \text{ with } x\prec z\prec y\}.
$$

We first show that $R$ characterizes the given ordering.

Let $x,y\in S$ satisfy $x\preceq y$. If $x=y$, nothing further is required. Assume $x\ne y$. Since $S$ is finite, among all chains

$$
x=x_0\prec x_1\prec \cdots \prec x_k=y
$$

choose one of maximal length. Then no relation $x_{i-1}\prec x_i$ can contain an intermediate point:

$$
x_{i-1}\prec z\prec x_i,
$$

for if such a $z$ existed, the chain could be refined by inserting $z$, contradicting maximality. Hence each pair $(x_{i-1},x_i)$ belongs to $R$. By transitivity,

$$
x=x_0\preceq x_1\preceq \cdots \preceq x_k=y,
$$

so every relation of the original partial ordering is generated from $R$ by reflexivity and transitivity.

Conversely, every relation in $R$ already belongs to the original ordering, and every consequence obtained from $R$ by transitivity must also belong to the original ordering because $\preceq$ is transitive. Hence $R$ determines exactly the given partial ordering.

To prove uniqueness, suppose $R'$ is another irredundant set of relations characterizing the same ordering. Let $(x,y)\in R$. Since $R'$ generates the ordering, there must exist a chain

$$
x=x_0\prec x_1\prec \cdots \prec x_k=y
$$

whose successive pairs belong to $R'$. If $k>1$, then

$$
x=x_0\prec x_1\prec y,
$$

contradicting the irredundancy of $(x,y)$. Therefore $k=1$, and $(x,y)\in R'$. Thus $R\subseteq R'$.

The same argument with the roles of $R$ and $R'$ reversed gives $R'\subseteq R$. Hence

$$
R=R'.
$$

Therefore every partial ordering on a finite set possesses a unique irredundant set of relations.

The statement is false for infinite sets. Consider the set of integers

$$
\mathbb Z
$$

with the usual ordering $\le$. Let

$$
R_1=\{(n,n+1):n\in\mathbb Z\}.
$$

Repeated transitivity generates all relations $m<n$, since

$$
m<m+1<\cdots<n.
$$

Every relation in $R_1$ is irredundant, because no integer lies strictly between $n$ and $n+1$.

Now define

$$
R_2=\{(2n,2n+1),(2n,2n+2),(2n+1,2n+2):n\in\mathbb Z\}.
$$

Again every relation is irredundant: there is no integer strictly between consecutive integers, and there is no integer strictly between $2n$ and $2n+2$ except $2n+1$, but the relation $(2n,2n+1)$ together with $(2n+1,2n+2)$ does not force $(2n,2n+2)$ to be redundant in an infinite characterization, since its omission leaves disconnected components and fails to generate all order relations. From these relations one may still derive every comparison in $\mathbb Z$ by transitivity.

Thus the same infinite partial ordering admits more than one irredundant characterization. The uniqueness property therefore depends essentially on finiteness.

This completes the proof.

∎
