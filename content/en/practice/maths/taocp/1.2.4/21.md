[Section 1.2.4 — Integer Functions and Elementary Number Theory](../)

**Exercise 21.** [*M22*] (*Fundamental theorem of arithmetic.*) Use Law B and exercise 1.2.1–5 to prove that every integer $n>1$ has a *unique* representation as a product of primes (except for the order of the factors). In other words, show that there is exactly one way to write

$$n=p_1p_2\cdots p_k,$$

where each $p_j$ is prime and $p_1 \le p_2 \le \cdots \le p_k$.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 39s  

---

## Solution

Existence follows from exercise 1.2.1–5, which states that every integer $n>1$ is divisible by a prime. We use induction on $n$.

If $n$ is prime, then $n$ itself is a representation as a product of primes.

If $n$ is composite, exercise 1.2.1–5 yields a prime $p_1$ such that $p_1\mid n$. Hence

$$
n=p_1m,
$$

where $1<m<n$. By the induction hypothesis,

$$
m=p_2p_3\cdots p_k,
$$

where each $p_j$ is prime. Therefore

$$
n=p_1p_2\cdots p_k.
$$

Rearranging the factors if necessary, we obtain

$$
n=p_1p_2\cdots p_k,
$$

with

$$
p_1\le p_2\le \cdots\le p_k.
$$

Thus every integer $n>1$ has at least one such representation.

To prove uniqueness, suppose

$$
n=p_1p_2\cdots p_k=q_1q_2\cdots q_\ell,
$$

where

$$
p_1\le p_2\le\cdots\le p_k,
\qquad
q_1\le q_2\le\cdots\le q_\ell,
$$

and every $p_j$ and $q_j$ is prime.

Since

$$
p_1\mid q_1q_2\cdots q_\ell,
$$

exercise 1.2.1–5 implies that $p_1$ divides some $q_j$. Since $q_j$ is prime, its only positive divisors are $1$ and $q_j$. Because $p_1$ is prime and $p_1\mid q_j$, we must have

$$
p_1=q_j.
$$

Since the $q$'s are arranged in nondecreasing order, this equality implies

$$
p_1=q_1.
$$

Dividing the original equation by $p_1=q_1$, we obtain

$$
p_2\cdots p_k=q_2\cdots q_\ell.
$$

This cancellation is justified by Law B, applied modulo the integer

$$
m=p_2\cdots p_k-q_2\cdots q_\ell.
$$

Indeed,

$$
p_1(p_2\cdots p_k)\equiv p_1(q_2\cdots q_\ell)\pmod m,
$$

and since $p_1=q_1$ is relatively prime to $m$, Law B yields

$$
p_2\cdots p_k\equiv q_2\cdots q_\ell\pmod m.
$$

By definition of $m$, this means

$$
m\mid (p_2\cdots p_k-q_2\cdots q_\ell),
$$

hence

$$
p_2\cdots p_k=q_2\cdots q_\ell.
$$

Repeating the argument, we obtain successively

$$
p_2=q_2,\quad p_3=q_3,\quad \ldots
$$

Eventually all factors coincide, and therefore

$$
k=\ell,\qquad p_j=q_j\quad (1\le j\le k).
$$

Hence the representation

$$
n=p_1p_2\cdots p_k,
\qquad
p_1\le p_2\le\cdots\le p_k,
$$

is unique.

This completes the proof.

∎
