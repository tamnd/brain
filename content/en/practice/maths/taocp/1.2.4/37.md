[Section 1.2.4: Integer Functions and Elementary Number Theory](../)

**Exercise 37.** &#9654; [*M30*] Let $m$ and $n$ be integers, $n>0$. Show that

$$
\sum_{0 \le k < n} \left\lfloor \frac{mk+x}{n} \right\rfloor
= \frac{(m-1)(n-1)}{2} + \frac{d-1}{2} + d\left\lfloor \frac{x}{d} \right\rfloor,
$$

where $d$ is the greatest common divisor of $m$ and $n$, and $x$ is any real number.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 57s  

---

## Setup

Let

$$
S(x)=\sum_{0\le k<n}\left\lfloor \frac{mk+x}{n}\right\rfloor,
$$

where $m,n\in \mathbf Z$, $n>0$, and let

$$
d=(m,n).
$$

We are to prove that

$$
S(x)=\frac{(m-1)(n-1)}2+\frac{d-1}2+d\left\lfloor \frac{x}{d}\right\rfloor.
$$

Write

$$
m=dm',\qquad n=dn',
$$

so that $(m',n')=1$.

## Solution

For each integer $k$ with $0\le k<n$,

$$
\left\lfloor \frac{mk+x}{n}\right\rfloor
=
\left\lfloor \frac{dm'k+x}{dn'}\right\rfloor.
$$

Write

$$
k=qn'+r,
\qquad 0\le q<d,\qquad 0\le r<n'.
$$

Since every integer $k$ with $0\le k<n=dn'$ is represented uniquely in this form,

$$
S(x)
=
\sum_{q=0}^{d-1}\sum_{r=0}^{n'-1}
\left\lfloor
\frac{dm'(qn'+r)+x}{dn'}
\right\rfloor.
$$

Now

$$
\frac{dm'(qn'+r)+x}{dn'}
=
m'q+\frac{dm'r+x}{dn'},
$$

hence

$$
\left\lfloor
\frac{dm'(qn'+r)+x}{dn'}
\right\rfloor
=
m'q+
\left\lfloor
\frac{dm'r+x}{dn'}
\right\rfloor.
$$

Therefore

$$
S(x)
=
\sum_{q=0}^{d-1}\sum_{r=0}^{n'-1}m'q
+
\sum_{q=0}^{d-1}\sum_{r=0}^{n'-1}
\left\lfloor
\frac{dm'r+x}{dn'}
\right\rfloor.
$$

The first double sum equals

$$
n'm'\sum_{q=0}^{d-1}q
=
n'm'\frac{d(d-1)}2.
$$

The second double sum is independent of $q$, hence equals

$$
d\sum_{r=0}^{n'-1}
\left\lfloor
\frac{dm'r+x}{dn'}
\right\rfloor.
$$

Thus

$$
S(x)
=
n'm'\frac{d(d-1)}2
+
d\sum_{r=0}^{n'-1}
\left\lfloor
\frac{m'r+x/d}{n'}
\right\rfloor.
\tag{1}
$$

Since $(m',n')=1$, multiplication by $m'$ permutes the residue classes modulo $n'$. Therefore the numbers

$$
m'r\bmod n',
\qquad 0\le r<n',
$$

are precisely the integers

$$
0,1,\ldots,n'-1
$$

in some order. Write

$$
m'r=n' t_r+s_r,
\qquad 0\le s_r<n'.
$$

Then $s_r$ runs through $0,1,\ldots,n'-1$, and

$$
\left\lfloor
\frac{m'r+x/d}{n'}
\right\rfloor
=
t_r+
\left\lfloor
\frac{s_r+x/d}{n'}
\right\rfloor.
$$

Hence

$$
\sum_{r=0}^{n'-1}
\left\lfloor
\frac{m'r+x/d}{n'}
\right\rfloor
=
\sum_{r=0}^{n'-1} t_r
+
\sum_{s=0}^{n'-1}
\left\lfloor
\frac{s+x/d}{n'}
\right\rfloor.
\tag{2}
$$

Since

$$
m'r=n't_r+s_r,
$$

summing over $r$ gives

$$
m'\sum_{r=0}^{n'-1}r
=
n'\sum_{r=0}^{n'-1}t_r
+
\sum_{s=0}^{n'-1}s.
$$

Using

$$
\sum_{r=0}^{n'-1}r
=
\sum_{s=0}^{n'-1}s
=
\frac{n'(n'-1)}2,
$$

we obtain

$$
n'\sum_{r=0}^{n'-1}t_r
=
(m'-1)\frac{n'(n'-1)}2,
$$

hence

$$
\sum_{r=0}^{n'-1}t_r
=
\frac{(m'-1)(n'-1)}2.
\tag{3}
$$

Next, let

$$
y=\frac{x}{d}.
$$

Write

$$
y=\lfloor y\rfloor+\theta,
\qquad 0\le \theta<1.
$$

Then

$$
\left\lfloor \frac{s+y}{n'}\right\rfloor
=
\left\lfloor
\frac{s+\theta}{n'}+\frac{\lfloor y\rfloor}{n'}
\right\rfloor.
$$

Since $0\le s+\theta<n'$, we have

$$
0\le \frac{s+\theta}{n'}<1.
$$

Therefore

$$
\left\lfloor \frac{s+y}{n'}\right\rfloor
=
\left\lfloor \frac{\lfloor y\rfloor}{n'}\right\rfloor
$$

or

$$
\left\lfloor \frac{\lfloor y\rfloor}{n'}\right\rfloor+1,
$$

according as

$$
s+\theta<n'\left(y-\left\lfloor \frac{\lfloor y\rfloor}{n'}\right\rfloor\right)
$$

or not. A simpler computation results by writing

$$
\lfloor y\rfloor=an'+b,
\qquad 0\le b<n'.
$$

Then

$$
y=an'+b+\theta,
$$

and therefore

$$
\frac{s+y}{n'}
=
a+\frac{s+b+\theta}{n'}.
$$

Hence

$$
\left\lfloor \frac{s+y}{n'}\right\rfloor
=
a+
\left\lfloor \frac{s+b+\theta}{n'}\right\rfloor.
$$

Since

$$
0\le s+b+\theta<2n',
$$

the second floor is $0$ or $1$. It equals $1$ precisely when

$$
s\ge n'-b-\theta.
$$

There are exactly $b$ such integers $s$ in the range $0\le s<n'$. Consequently

$$
\sum_{s=0}^{n'-1}
\left\lfloor \frac{s+y}{n'}\right\rfloor
=
an'+b
=
\lfloor y\rfloor.
\tag{4}
$$

Substituting (3) and (4) into (2),

$$
\sum_{r=0}^{n'-1}
\left\lfloor
\frac{m'r+x/d}{n'}
\right\rfloor
=
\frac{(m'-1)(n'-1)}2
+
\left\lfloor \frac{x}{d}\right\rfloor.
$$

Using this in (1),

$$
S(x)
=
n'm'\frac{d(d-1)}2
+
d\frac{(m'-1)(n'-1)}2
+
d\left\lfloor \frac{x}{d}\right\rfloor.
$$

Since $m=dm'$ and $n=dn'$,

$$
n'm'\frac{d(d-1)}2
=
\frac{mn-md}{2},
$$

and

$$
d\frac{(m'-1)(n'-1)}2
=
\frac{mn-m-n+d}{2}.
$$

Adding these terms,

$$
\frac{mn-md}{2}
+
\frac{mn-m-n+d}{2}
=
\frac{(m-1)(n-1)}2+\frac{d-1}2.
$$

Therefore

$$
S(x)
=
\frac{(m-1)(n-1)}2
+
\frac{d-1}2
+
d\left\lfloor \frac{x}{d}\right\rfloor.
$$

This is the required identity.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification

Take $m=4$, $n=6$, so $d=2$. Let $x=1.3$.

The left side is

$$
\sum_{0\le k<6}\left\lfloor \frac{4k+1.3}{6}\right\rfloor
=
0+0+1+2+2+3
=
8.
$$

The right side is

$$
\frac{(4-1)(6-1)}2+\frac{2-1}2+2\left\lfloor \frac{1.3}{2}\right\rfloor
=
\frac{15}2+\frac12+0
=
8.
$$

The two sides agree.

## Notes

When $d=1$, the formula reduces to

$$
\sum_{0\le k<n}\left\lfloor \frac{mk+x}{n}\right\rfloor
=
\frac{(m-1)(n-1)}2+\lfloor x\rfloor.
$$

This special case expresses the fact that the residues

$$
mk\bmod n,
\qquad 0\le k<n,
$$

form a permutation of $0,1,\ldots,n-1$ whenever $(m,n)=1$.
