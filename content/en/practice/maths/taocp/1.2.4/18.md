[Section 1.2.4: Integer Functions and Elementary Number Theory](../)

**Exercise 18.** [*M15*] Using Law B, prove the other half of Law D: If $a \equiv b$ (modulo $r$) and $a \equiv b$ (modulo $s$), then $a \equiv b$ (modulo $rs$), provided that $r \perp s$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 5m27s  

---

## Solution

Assume that

$$
a \equiv b \pmod r
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
a \equiv b \pmod s,
$$

with $r \perp s$.

By the definition of congruence, we have

$$
r \mid (a-b)
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
s \mid (a-b).
$$

Hence there exist integers $k$ and $\ell$ such that

$$
a-b = kr,
\qquad
a-b = \ell s.
$$

Since $r \perp s$, Law B implies that there exist integers $m$ and $n$ such that

$$
mr + ns = 1.
$$

Multiply both sides by $a-b$:

$$
a-b = (a-b)(mr+ns).
$$

Distributing gives

$$
a-b = m r(a-b) + n s(a-b).
$$

Because $a-b = \ell s$, the first term becomes

$$
m r(a-b) =
m r(\ell s) =
m\ell\, rs,
$$

which is divisible by $rs$.

Because $a-b = kr$, the second term becomes

$$
n s(a-b) =
n s(kr) =
nk\, rs,
$$

which is also divisible by $rs$.

Therefore both terms on the right-hand side are multiples of $rs$, so their sum is a multiple of $rs$. Hence

$$
rs \mid (a-b).
$$

By the definition of congruence,

$$
a \equiv b \pmod{rs}.
$$

Thus, if $a \equiv b \pmod r$ and $a \equiv b \pmod s$, with $r \perp s$, then

$$
a \equiv b \pmod{rs}.
$$

$\square$
