[Section 3.2.1.3: Potency](../)

**Exercise 8.** &#9654; [*M26*] [M26] A random number generator defined by $X_{n+1} = (2^{17} + 3)X_n \bmod 2^{35}$ and $X_0 = 1$ was subjected to the following test: Let $Y_n = \lfloor 20X_n/2^{35} \rfloor$; then $Y_n$ should be a random integer between 0 and 19, and the triples $(Y_{5n}, Y_{5n+1}, Y_{5n+2})$ should take on each of the 8000 possible values from $(0, 0, 0)$ to $(19, 19, 19)$ with nearly equal frequency. But with 1,000,000 values of $n$ tested, many triples never occurred, and others occurred much more often than they should have. Can you account for this failure?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 16m48s  

---

## Setup

The generator is

$$
X_{n+1}\equiv aX_n \pmod{2^{35}},
\qquad
a=2^{17}+3,
\qquad
X_0=1.
$$

Since $X_0=1$, the sequence is

$$
X_n\equiv a^n \pmod{2^{35}}.
$$

The experiment forms

$$
Y_n=\left\lfloor \frac{20X_n}{2^{35}}\right\rfloor,
$$

and examines the triples

$$
(Y_{5n},Y_{5n+1},Y_{5n+2}).
$$

If the generator behaved randomly, the $20^3=8000$ possible triples would occur with nearly equal frequency. The problem asks for an explanation of the observed failure.

## Solution

Set

$$
A=a^5.
$$

Then

$$
X_{5n+r}\equiv A^n a^r \pmod{2^{35}},
\qquad r=0,1,2.
$$

Hence every tested triple is determined by

$$
T_n=(X_{5n},X_{5n+1},X_{5n+2})
     =(u_n,\;au_n,\;a^2u_n)
     \pmod{2^{35}},
$$

where

$$
u_n=A^n.
$$

The crucial fact is that $A$ is extremely close to $1$ modulo $2^{35}$. Compute

$$
a=3+2^{17}.
$$

Using the binomial theorem,

$$
a^5=(3+2^{17})^5.
$$

Modulo $2^{35}$ all terms containing $(2^{17})^2=2^{34}$ except the first surviving one vanish, because their exponents of $2$ are at least $51$. Therefore

$$
a^5
\equiv
3^5+5\cdot 3^4\,2^{17}
\pmod{2^{35}}.
$$

Since $3^5=243$ and $5\cdot3^4=405$,

$$
A\equiv 243+405\cdot2^{17}\pmod{2^{35}}.
$$

The odd number $243$ satisfies

$$
243-1=242=2\cdot121,
$$

so

$$
v_2(243-1)=1.
$$

Furthermore,

$$
405\cdot2^{17}
$$

is divisible by $2^{17}$. Hence

$$
A-1
=
242+405\cdot2^{17},
$$

and the first term has exact $2$-adic valuation $1$. Consequently

$$
v_2(A-1)=1.
$$

Exercise 11 of Section 3.2.1.2 gives the order of an odd number modulo a power of $2$. Since

$$
A\equiv 1+2k,\qquad k\ \text{odd},
$$

the order of $A$ modulo $2^{35}$ is only

$$
2^{35-1}=2^{34}.
$$

Thus the subsequence ${u_n}$ has period $2^{34}$, far smaller than the full period $2^{33}$ that would correspond to independent behavior in the three-dimensional test. More importantly, $A$ lies very near $1$, so consecutive values of $u_n$ satisfy

$$
u_{n+1}=Au_n
      \equiv u_n+(A-1)u_n
      \pmod{2^{35}},
$$

with $A-1$ highly structured.

The potency argument from Section 3.2.1.3 explains the resulting geometric restriction. For the multiplier $A$,

$$
B=A-1.
$$

Since $v_2(B)=1$,

$$
B^k
$$

is divisible by $2^{35}$ only when

$$
k\ge 35.
$$

Hence the sequence generated by multiplication by $A$ has very large potency. Equation (5) shows that such sequences are governed by a low-degree polynomial in the index when reduced modulo sufficiently large powers of $2$. Therefore triples

$$
(u_n,u_{n+1},u_{n+2})
$$

occupy only a comparatively small collection of parallel planes in three-dimensional space, the same phenomenon discussed in the text for low-potency generators, although here it appears through the closely related structure of the powers of a number extremely near $1$.

Multiplication by the fixed constants $1$, $a$, and $a^2$ merely applies an invertible linear transformation. Therefore the tested triples

$$
(X_{5n},X_{5n+1},X_{5n+2})
$$

also lie on a restricted family of planes. When these values are compressed into twenty intervals by

$$
Y_n=\left\lfloor \frac{20X_n}{2^{35}}\right\rfloor,
$$

many cells of the $20\times20\times20$ cube receive no points at all, while other cells receive an excessive concentration of points.

This geometric concentration accounts for the observed fact that many of the $8000$ possible triples never occurred and others occurred much more frequently than expected.

## Verification

The key congruence is

$$
a^5=(3+2^{17})^5
    \equiv 243+405\cdot2^{17}
    \pmod{2^{35}},
$$

because every term containing $(2^{17})^2$ contributes a factor $2^{34}$ and, after multiplication by the remaining factor $2^{17}$, becomes divisible by $2^{51}$.

Hence

$$
A-1=242+405\cdot2^{17},
$$

and

$$
v_2(A-1)=1.
$$

Thus $A$ is congruent to $1+2k$ with $k$ odd. By the order formula of Exercise 11, the powers of $A$ modulo $2^{35}$ possess strong algebraic structure. The sampled values

$$
X_{5n+r}=a^rA^n
$$

inherit that structure, so the corresponding triples cannot fill three-dimensional space uniformly.

## Notes

The test examines every fifth value. This replaces the original multiplier $a$ by the effective multiplier $A=a^5$. The failure becomes understandable only after studying $A$, not $a$ itself. The fifth power is exceptionally close to $1$ modulo $2^{35}$, and this proximity creates the strong lattice structure detected by the experiment.
