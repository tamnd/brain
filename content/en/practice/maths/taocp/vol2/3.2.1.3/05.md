[Section 3.2.1.3: Potency](../)

**Exercise 5.** [*M20*] Given that $m = p_1^{e_1} \cdots p_r^{e_r}$ and $a = 1 + kp_1^{f_1} \cdots p_r^{f_r}$, where $a$ satisfies the conditions of Theorem 3.2.1.2A and $k$ is relatively prime to $m$, show that the potency is $\max(\lceil e_i/f_i \rceil) = \lceil e_1/f_1 \rceil)$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 12m52s  

---

## Solution

Let

$$
m=p_1^{e_1}p_2^{e_2}\cdots p_r^{e_r},
$$

and let

$$
a=1+k p_1^{f_1}p_2^{f_2}\cdots p_r^{f_r},
$$

where $k$ is relatively prime to $m$. Write

$$
b=a-1=k p_1^{f_1}p_2^{f_2}\cdots p_r^{f_r}.
$$

Since $a$ satisfies the conditions of Theorem 3.2.1.2A, each prime divisor of $m$ divides $b$; hence $f_i\ge1$ for all $i$.

By definition, the potency is the least integer $s$ such that

$$
b^s\equiv0\pmod m.
$$

Because

$$
b^s
=
k^s p_1^{s f_1}p_2^{s f_2}\cdots p_r^{s f_r},
$$

and $\gcd(k,m)=1$, the divisibility condition $m\mid b^s$ is equivalent to

$$
p_i^{e_i}\mid b^s
\qquad (1\le i\le r).
$$

For a fixed $i$, the exponent of $p_i$ in $b^s$ is $s f_i$. Therefore

$$
p_i^{e_i}\mid b^s
\iff
s f_i\ge e_i.
$$

Hence $b^s\equiv0\pmod m$ exactly when

$$
s\ge \frac{e_i}{f_i}
\qquad (1\le i\le r).
$$

The least integer satisfying the $i$th inequality is

$$
\left\lceil \frac{e_i}{f_i}\right\rceil.
$$

Consequently, the least integer satisfying all of them simultaneously is

$$
s
=
\max_{1\le i\le r}
\left\lceil \frac{e_i}{f_i}\right\rceil.
$$

This is the potency.

The statement of the exercise also writes this value as

$$
\max\!\left(\left\lceil\frac{e_i}{f_i}\right\rceil\right)
=
\left\lceil\frac{e_1}{f_1}\right\rceil.
$$

This equality means that the indexing has been arranged so that

$$
\left\lceil\frac{e_1}{f_1}\right\rceil
=
\max_{1\le i\le r}
\left\lceil\frac{e_i}{f_i}\right\rceil.
$$

Therefore the potency is

$$
\boxed{\max_{1\le i\le r}\left\lceil\frac{e_i}{f_i}\right\rceil
=
\left\lceil\frac{e_1}{f_1}\right\rceil }.
$$

This completes the proof.

∎
