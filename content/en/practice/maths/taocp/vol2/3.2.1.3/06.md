[Section 3.2.1.3: Potency](../)

**Exercise 6.** &#9654; [*20*] Which of the values of $m = w \pm 1$ in Table 3.2.1.1–1 can be used in a linear congruential sequence of maximum period whose potency is 4 or more? (Use the result of exercise 5.)

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m  

---

## Solution

By Exercise 5, if

$$
m=p_1^{e_1}\cdots p_r^{e_r},
\qquad
a=1+k\,p_1^{f_1}\cdots p_r^{f_r},
$$

where $a$ satisfies the conditions of Theorem 3.2.1.2A and $\gcd(k,m)=1$, then the potency is

$$
\max_i\Bigl\lceil \frac{e_i}{f_i}\Bigr\rceil .
$$

To obtain potency at least $4$, it is therefore necessary and sufficient that

$$
\Bigl\lceil \frac{e_i}{f_i}\Bigr\rceil \ge 4
$$

for at least one prime divisor $p_i$ of $m$.

For a maximum-period linear congruential sequence, Theorem 3.2.1.2A requires $f_i\ge1$ for every prime divisor of $m$. Hence

$$
\Bigl\lceil \frac{e_i}{f_i}\Bigr\rceil
\le e_i .
$$

Therefore a necessary condition for potency $\ge4$ is that some prime divisor of $m$ occur to exponent at least $4$ in the factorization of $m$.

Conversely, if some prime divisor occurs to exponent $e_i\ge4$, choose

$$
a=1+\prod_j p_j .
$$

Then $f_j=1$ for all $j$, and Exercise 5 gives

$$
\text{potency}
=
\max_j e_j .
$$

Hence the potency is at least $4$. Thus:

> A modulus $m$ admits a maximum-period linear congruential sequence of potency at least $4$ if and only if some prime power $p^e\mid m$ has $e\ge4$.

We now inspect the values $m=w\pm1$ appearing in Table 3.2.1.1-1.

For the word lengths used in the table, the factorizations are

$$
2^{15}-1=7\cdot31\cdot151,
$$

$$
2^{15}+1=3^2\cdot11\cdot331,
$$

$$
2^{31}-1=2147483647,
$$

$$
2^{31}+1=3^2\cdot715827883,
$$

$$
2^{35}-1=31\cdot601\cdot1801,
$$

$$
2^{35}+1=3^2\cdot71\cdot87211,
$$

$$
10^{6}-1=3^3\cdot7\cdot11\cdot13\cdot37,
$$

$$
10^{6}+1=101\cdot9901.
$$

In every case, the largest exponent of any prime factor is at most $3$. No factorization contains a prime fourth power.

Therefore no modulus $m=w\pm1$ listed in Table 3.2.1.1-1 can have potency $4$ or more in a maximum-period linear congruential sequence.

Thus the answer is

$$
\boxed{\text{None of the values }m=w\pm1\text{ in Table 3.2.1.1-1.}
}
$$

This completes the proof.

∎
