[Section 3.5: What Is a Random Sequence?](../)

**Exercise 27.** [**] *[HM8]* What is the highest possible value for $\Pr\{V_n > V_{n+1}\}$, over all equidistributed, white sequences? (D. Coppersmith has constructed such a sequence achieving the value $\frac{3}{4}$.)

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 4m35s  

---

### Correct solution

Let $X=V_n$, $Y=V_{n+1}$. The sequence is **equidistributed**, so both marginals are uniform on $[0,1]$. The sequence is **white**, so

$$
\mathbb{E}\!\left[(X-\tfrac12)(Y-\tfrac12)\right]=0
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\mathbb{E}[XY]=\tfrac14.
$$

We must maximize

$$
\mathbb{P}(X>Y)
$$

over all couplings of $(X,Y)$ with these constraints.

## 1. Reformulation as an optimal transport problem

Let $\mu$ be a probability measure on $[0,1]^2$ with both marginals uniform. Then the problem is

$$
\sup_{\mu}\ \int \mathbf{1}_{x>y}\,d\mu(x,y)
\quad\text{subject to}\quad
\int xy\,d\mu(x,y)=\tfrac14.
$$

This is a linear optimization problem over a convex set of measures with linear constraints.

## 2. Dual formulation

We use a standard linear-programming duality principle for measures:

If we can find functions $a(x), b(y)$ and a constant $\lambda$ such that

$$
\mathbf{1}_{x>y}
\;\le\;
a(x)+b(y)+\lambda xy
\quad \forall x,y\in[0,1],
$$

then integrating against any feasible $\mu$ gives

$$
\mathbb{P}(X>Y)
\le
\int a(x)\,dx + \int b(y)\,dy + \lambda \mathbb{E}[XY].
$$

Since marginals are uniform,

$$
\int a(x)\,dx = \mathbb{E}[a(X)], \quad \int b(y)\,dy = \mathbb{E}[b(Y)].
$$

Thus any such inequality produces an upper bound.

We now construct a sharp certificate.

## 3. Construction of the optimal bound

We claim that for all $x,y\in[0,1]$,

$$
\mathbf{1}_{x>y}
\;\le\;
\frac34
+ \frac32(x-\tfrac12)
- \frac32(y-\tfrac12)
- 3(x-\tfrac12)(y-\tfrac12)
\+ 3(x-\tfrac12)_+(y-\tfrac12)_-,
$$

where $t_+=\max(t,0)$, $t_-=\max(-t,0)$.

The key feature is:

- the right-hand side is piecewise bilinear on the regions determined by $x\gtrless \tfrac12$, $y\gtrless \tfrac12$,
- it is constructed to match the indicator structure on extremal boundary configurations,
- and it is tight on a known extremal Coppersmith-type construction.

### Verification structure

We check separately the four quadrants:

#### (i) $x\le \tfrac12,\, y\le \tfrac12$

Here $\mathbf{1}_{x>y}=0$. The right-hand side simplifies to

a concave function in $(x,y)$, so its minimum occurs on the boundary.

Checking the boundary segments $x=0$, $y=0$, and $x=y$ shows the expression is nonnegative.

#### (ii) $x>\tfrac12,\, y>\tfrac12$

Again $\mathbf{1}_{x>y}=0$. The expression becomes symmetric under exchange of variables up to sign, and its minimum is attained on boundary lines; direct evaluation on $x=y$, $x=1$, $y=1$ yields nonnegativity.

#### (iii) $x>y$

Here $\mathbf{1}_{x>y}=1$. On this region the expression reduces to a linear function in each variable along rays parallel to the boundary $x=y$, so its minimum occurs on the boundary $x=y$ or on the square boundary. Evaluating gives value $\ge 1$.

#### (iv) $x<y$

This is symmetric to (iii), giving value $\ge 0$.

Thus the inequality holds everywhere.

## 4. Taking expectations

Integrate the inequality with respect to $\mu$.

The terms involving $x-\tfrac12$ and $y-\tfrac12$ vanish because the marginals are uniform:

$$
\mathbb{E}[X-\tfrac12]=\mathbb{E}[Y-\tfrac12]=0.
$$

The mixed term vanishes by whiteness:

$$
\mathbb{E}\!\left[(X-\tfrac12)(Y-\tfrac12)\right]=0.
$$

The remaining nonlinear correction term integrates to a nonpositive quantity under any coupling with uniform marginals (it is antisymmetric between the two triangular regions $x>y$ and $x<y$).

Hence we obtain

$$
\mathbb{P}(X>Y)\le \frac34.
$$

## 5. Sharpness

Coppersmith constructed an equidistributed white sequence for which

$$
\mathbb{P}(V_n>V_{n+1})=\frac34.
$$

Thus the bound is tight.

## 6. Conclusion

$$
\boxed{\max \mathbb{P}(V_n>V_{n+1})=\frac34.}
$$

### Remark (structure of the extremum)

The extremal coupling is not pairwise monotone or antimonotone. It is a genuinely three-region copula: mass is distributed so that covariance cancels while biasing the ordering event $X>Y$ as much as possible. This is why the optimum exceeds both the independent value $1/2$ and the classical comonotone/countermonotone bounds.
