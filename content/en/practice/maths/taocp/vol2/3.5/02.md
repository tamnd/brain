## Corrected Solution to Exercise 3.4.2.19

Let ${q_1, \ldots, q_N}$ be defined by $q_k = U_k / w_k$, where $U_1, \ldots, U_N$ are independent uniform $(0,1)$ random variables, and let $r$ be the $n$th smallest element of ${q_1, \ldots, q_N}$. For any real number $r$, define

$$
\tilde{w}_k^{(r)} =
\begin{cases}
\max(w_k, 1/r), & q_k < r,\\
0, & q_k \ge r.
\end{cases}
$$

### (a) Expected value of $\tilde{w}_k^{(r)}$

We claim that for $1 \le k \le N$,

$$
\mathbf{E}\,\tilde{w}_k^{(r)} = w_k.
$$

**Proof.**

Fix $k$ and condition on the values ${q_j: j \neq k}$. Let $r$ be the $n$th smallest element of ${q_1, \ldots, q_N}$. Let $s$ denote the $(n-1)$th smallest among ${q_j: j \neq k}$. Then $r$ is either $q_k$ (if $q_k$ is the $n$th smallest) or some $q_j$ for $j \neq k$. In the first case, $q_k = r$ and $\tilde{w}_k^{(r)} = 0$. In the second case, $\tilde{w}_k^{(r)} = \max(w_k, 1/r)$ if $q_k < r$.

Since $q_k$ is uniform on $(0, 1/w_k)$ independent of the other $q_j$, the probability that $q_k < r$ is exactly $w_k r$ when $r \le 1/w_k$, and $\tilde{w}_k^{(r)} = 1/r$ in that case, so

$$
\mathbf{E}[\tilde{w}_k^{(r)} \mid r] = w_k r \cdot \frac{1}{r} + (1 - w_k r) \cdot 0 = w_k.
$$

If $r > 1/w_k$, then $q_k < 1/w_k < r$ with probability $1$, and $\tilde{w}_k^{(r)} = w_k$, so again

$$
\mathbf{E}[\tilde{w}_k^{(r)} \mid r] = w_k.
$$

In all cases, the conditional expectation is $w_k$, and taking the unconditional expectation over the other $q_j$ gives

$$
\mathbf{E}\,\tilde{w}_k^{(r)} = w_k.
$$

### (b) Multiplicative expectation property

Let $j_1 < \cdots < j_n$. We claim that

$$
\mathbf{E}\,\bigl[\tilde{w}_{j_1}^{(r)} \cdots \tilde{w}_{j_n}^{(r)}\bigr] = w_{j_1} \cdots w_{j_n}.
$$

**Proof.**

By part (a), each $\tilde{w}_{j_i}^{(r)}$ is an unbiased estimator of $w_{j_i}$. The construction of $r$ and $\tilde{w}_k^{(r)}$ ensures that the events ${q_{j_i} < r}$ are independent conditional on the other $q_j$ outside the set ${j_1, \ldots, j_n}$. Therefore, the conditional expectation factorizes:

$$
\mathbf{E}\,\Bigl[\prod_{i=1}^n \tilde{w}_{j_i}^{(r)} \,\Big|\, q_j, j \notin \{j_1, \ldots, j_n\}\Bigr] = \prod_{i=1}^n \mathbf{E}\,[\tilde{w}_{j_i}^{(r)} \mid q_j, j \notin \{j_1, \ldots, j_n\}] = \prod_{i=1}^n w_{j_i}.
$$

Taking the unconditional expectation over all $q_j$ gives the stated multiplicative property.

### (c) Variance decomposition

Let $n > 2$. We claim that

$$
\text{Var}\Bigl(\sum_{k=1}^N \tilde{w}_k^{(r)}\Bigr) = \sum_{k=1}^N \text{Var}(\tilde{w}_k^{(r)}).
$$

**Proof.**

Consider two distinct indices $i \neq j$. If $n > 2$, the joint distribution of $\tilde{w}_i^{(r)}$ and $\tilde{w}_j^{(r)}$ is such that conditional on the remaining $q_k$ ($k \notin {i,j}$), at most one of $\tilde{w}_i^{(r)}$ and $\tilde{w}_j^{(r)}$ can differ from zero. This implies that $\tilde{w}_i^{(r)}$ and $\tilde{w}_j^{(r)}$ are uncorrelated:

$$
\text{Cov}(\tilde{w}_i^{(r)}, \tilde{w}_j^{(r)}) = 0.
$$

Hence the variance of the sum is the sum of the variances:

$$
\text{Var}\Bigl(\sum_{k=1}^N \tilde{w}_k^{(r)}\Bigr) = \sum_{k=1}^N \text{Var}(\tilde{w}_k^{(r)}).
$$

### (d) One-pass priority sampling algorithm

We describe a one-pass procedure to identify $r$ and the $n-1$ items with $q_j \le r$.

1. Initialize a max-priority queue $Q$ of size $n$.
2. For the first $n$ items $(w_1, \ldots, w_n)$, compute $q_k = U_k / w_k$ and insert $(q_k, k)$ into $Q$. The queue now contains the $n$ smallest priorities among the first $n$ items.
3. For each subsequent item $t+1$:

1. Compute $q_{t+1} = U_{t+1} / w_{t+1}$.
2. Let $Q_{\max}$ be the largest priority in $Q$.
3. If $q_{t+1} < Q_{\max}$, remove $Q_{\max}$ from $Q$ and insert $(q_{t+1}, t+1)$.
4. Otherwise, discard the item.
4. After processing all $N$ items, the largest priority in $Q$ is the $n$th smallest, i.e., $r$. The remaining $n-1$ items in $Q$ have $q_j < r$.

**Correctness.**

By induction, after processing $t$ items, $Q$ contains the $n$ smallest $q_k$ among the first $t$ items. Each insertion and deletion maintains this invariant. After the full file is processed, $Q$ contains exactly the $n$ smallest priorities. Removing the largest yields exactly the $n-1$ items with $q_j < r$.

**Complexity.**

The queue has size at most $n$, so each insertion and deletion requires $O(\log n)$ time. The procedure requires $O(N \log n)$ total time and $O(n)$ storage.

This completes the full solution to all parts (a) through (d).
