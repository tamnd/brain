**Exercise 24.** &#9654; [*M35*] [M35] (J. G. van der Corput.) (a) Prove that the $[0\,..\,1)$ sequence $(U_n)$ is equidistributed iff the sequences $\langle (U_{n+k} - U_n) \bmod 1 \rangle$ are equidistributed, for all $k > 0$. (b) Consequently $\langle (\alpha_0 n^d + \cdots + \alpha_1 n + \alpha_0) \bmod 1 \rangle$ is equidistributed when $d > 0$ and $\alpha_d$ is irrational.

**Verified:** yes

## Solution

For a real number $x$, write $\mathbf e(x)=e^{2\pi i x}$.

We shall use the Weyl criterion: A sequence $(U_n)$ in $[0,1)$ is equidistributed if and only if, for every nonzero integer $h$,

$$
\lim_{N\to\infty}\frac1N\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}\mathbf e(hU_n)=0.
$$

### (a)

We prove both implications.

#### If $(U_n)$ is equidistributed, then every difference sequence is equidistributed.

Fix $k>0$.

Since removing finitely many terms does not affect equidistribution, the shifted sequence $(U_{n+k})$ is also equidistributed. Hence the pair

$$
(U_n,U_{n+k})
$$

is $2$-distributed. Indeed, Exercise 23(a) applies to any fixed pair of coordinates, because replacing $(U_n,U_{n+1})$ by $(U_n,U_{n+k})$ merely changes the index shift, and the omission of the first $k-1$ pairs has no effect on limiting frequencies.

Exercise 23(a) states that, for every integers $c_1,c_2$, not both zero,

$$
(c_1U_n+c_2U_{n+k})\bmod1
$$

is equidistributed. Choosing $c_1=-1$ and $c_2=1$ gives

$$
(U_{n+k}-U_n)\bmod1,
$$

which is therefore equidistributed.

#### If every difference sequence is equidistributed, then $(U_n)$ is equidistributed.

Assume that, for every $k>0$,

$$
V_n^{(k)}=(U_{n+k}-U_n)\bmod1
$$

is equidistributed.

To prove that $(U_n)$ is equidistributed, it is enough, by Weyl's criterion, to show that

$$
\lim_{N\to\infty}\frac1N\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}\mathbf e(hU_n)=0
$$

for every nonzero integer $h$.

Fix such an integer $h$, and define

$$
a_n=\mathbf e(hU_n).
$$

Then $|a_n|=1$.

By the hypothesis, the sequence $(V_n^{(k)})$ is equidistributed. Applying Weyl's criterion to this sequence gives

$$
\lim_{N\to\infty}
\frac1N
\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}
\mathbf e\!\left(h(U_{n+k}-U_n)\right)
=0,
$$

that is,

$$
\lim_{N\to\infty}
\frac1N
\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}
a_{n+k}\overline{a_n}
=0
$$

for every fixed $k>0$.

Now let

$$
A_N=\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}a_n.
$$

By the van der Corput inequality,

$$
|A_N|^2
\le
\frac{N+H-1}{H}
\sum_{|r|<H}
\left(1-\frac{|r|}{H}\right)
\sum_{n}
a_{n+r}\overline{a_n},
$$

where the inner sum extends over all indices for which both terms exist.

Divide by $N^2$. Since $|a_n|=1$, the contribution of the finitely many boundary terms is $O(H/N)$, uniformly in $r$. Therefore

$$
\frac{|A_N|^2}{N^2}
\le
\frac1H
+
2\sum_{r=1}^{H-1}
\left(1-\frac rH\right)
\Re\!\left(
\frac1N
\sum_{n=0}^{N-r-1}
a_{n+r}\overline{a_n}
\right)
+
o(1).
$$

For each fixed $r$, the hypothesis implies

$$
\frac1N
\sum_{n=0}^{N-r-1}
a_{n+r}\overline{a_n}
\longrightarrow0.
$$

Hence, taking the limit superior,

$$
\limsup_{N\to\infty}
\frac{|A_N|^2}{N^2}
\le
\frac1H.
$$

Since $H$ is arbitrary,

$$
\lim_{N\to\infty}\frac{A_N}{N}=0.
$$

Thus

$$
\lim_{N\to\infty}
\frac1N
\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}
\mathbf e(hU_n)=0
$$

for every nonzero integer $h$. By Weyl's criterion, $(U_n)$ is equidistributed.

Therefore,

$$
(U_n)\text{ is equidistributed}
\iff
\bigl((U_{n+k}-U_n)\bmod1\bigr)
\text{ is equidistributed for every }k>0.
$$

### (b)

Let

$$
P(n)=\alpha_dn^d+\alpha_{d-1}n^{d-1}+\cdots+\alpha_1n+\alpha_0,
$$

where $d>0$ and $\alpha_d$ is irrational.

We prove by induction on $d$ that

$$
(P(n)\bmod1)
$$

is equidistributed.

If $d=1$, then

$$
P(n)=\alpha_1n+\alpha_0,
$$

with irrational $\alpha_1$. By the classical theorem on irrational rotations, this sequence is equidistributed.

Now assume the result for degree $d-1$, where $d>1$.

Fix any $k>0$. By the binomial theorem,

$$
\begin{aligned}
P(n+k)-P(n)
&=
\alpha_d\bigl((n+k)^d-n^d\bigr)
+\cdots \\
&=
d\alpha_dk\,n^{d-1}
+\text{terms of lower degree}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus $P(n+k)-P(n)$ is a polynomial of degree $d-1$ whose leading coefficient is

$$
d\alpha_dk.
$$

Since $d$ and $k$ are positive integers and $\alpha_d$ is irrational, $d\alpha_dk$ is also irrational.

By the induction hypothesis,

$$
\bigl((P(n+k)-P(n))\bmod1\bigr)
$$

is equidistributed for every $k>0$.

Applying part (a), the sequence

$$
(P(n)\bmod1)
$$

is equidistributed.

By induction, every polynomial sequence

$$
\bigl(\alpha_dn^d+\alpha_{d-1}n^{d-1}+\cdots+\alpha_1n+\alpha_0\bigr)\bmod1
$$

is equidistributed whenever $d>0$ and $\alpha_d$ is irrational.

This completes the proof. ∎
