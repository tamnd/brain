[Section 3.5: What Is a Random Sequence?](../)

**Exercise 24.** &#9654; [*M35*] [M35] (J. G. van der Corput.) (a) Prove that the $[0\,..\,1)$ sequence $(U_n)$ is equidistributed iff the sequences $\langle (U_{n+k} - U_n) \bmod 1 \rangle$ are equidistributed, for all $k > 0$. (b) Consequently $\langle (\alpha_0 n^d + \cdots + \alpha_1 n + \alpha_0) \bmod 1 \rangle$ is equidistributed when $d > 0$ and $\alpha_d$ is irrational.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m12s  

---

## Setup

Part (a) asks for a characterization of equidistribution in terms of the difference sequences

$V_n^{(k)}=(U_{n+k}-U_n)\bmod 1,\qquad k>0.$

The statement to be proved is that $(U_n)$ is equidistributed if and only if every sequence $(V_n^{(k)})$ is equidistributed.

Part (b) asks for an application to the polynomial sequence

$U_n=(\alpha_d n^d+\alpha_{d-1}n^{d-1}+\cdots+\alpha_1n+\alpha_0)\bmod 1,$

where $d>0$ and $\alpha_d$ is irrational. The goal is to prove that $(U_n)$ is equidistributed.

## Solution

### (a)

Assume first that $(U_n)$ is equidistributed. Exercise 23(a), with $k=2$, states that a sequence is $2$-distributed if and only if every sequence

$\langle(c_1U_n+c_2U_{n+1})\bmod1\rangle,$

with integers $c_1,c_2$ not both zero, is equidistributed. Since a $2$-distributed sequence is in particular $1$-distributed, repeated application of Definition B shows that

$\langle(U_n,U_{n+1},\ldots,U_{n+k})\rangle$

is $(k+1)$-distributed whenever the original sequence is $\infty$-distributed. Van der Corput's argument requires only the case obtained from Weyl's criterion of Exercise 22. For every nonzero integer $h$,

$$
\lim_{N\to\infty}\frac1N
\sum_{0\le n<N}
e^{2\pi i hU_n}
=0.
$$

Consider

$$
S_N(h)=
\sum_{0\le n<N}
e^{2\pi i hU_n}.
$$

Then

$$
|S_N(h)|^2
=
\sum_{0\le m,n<N}
e^{2\pi i h(U_n-U_m)}.
$$

Write $r=n-m$. The terms with $r=0$ contribute $N$, and for $r>0$ there are $N-r$ pairs with $n-m=r$. Hence

$$
|S_N(h)|^2
=
N+
2\Re
\sum_{r=1}^{N-1}
\sum_{m=0}^{N-r-1}
e^{2\pi i h(U_{m+r}-U_m)}.
$$

Divide by $N^2$:

$$
\frac{|S_N(h)|^2}{N^2}
=
\frac1N+
2\Re
\sum_{r=1}^{N-1}
\frac{N-r}{N}
\left(
\frac1{N-r}
\sum_{m=0}^{N-r-1}
e^{2\pi i h(U_{m+r}-U_m)}
\right).
$$

If each difference sequence

$$
\langle(U_{n+r}-U_n)\bmod1\rangle
$$

is equidistributed, Exercise 22 implies that, for every nonzero integer $h$,

$$
\lim_{N\to\infty}
\frac1{N-r}
\sum_{m=0}^{N-r-1}
e^{2\pi i h(U_{m+r}-U_m)}
=0.
$$

Therefore

$$
\lim_{N\to\infty}
\frac{|S_N(h)|^2}{N^2}=0,
$$

and consequently

$$
\lim_{N\to\infty}
\frac1N
\sum_{0\le n<N}
e^{2\pi i hU_n}=0.
$$

Exercise 22 with $k=1$ shows that $(U_n)$ is equidistributed.

Conversely, suppose $(U_n)$ is equidistributed. Fix $k>0$. For every nonzero integer $h$,

$$
\frac1N
\sum_{0\le n<N}
e^{2\pi i h((U_{n+k}-U_n)\bmod1)}
=
\frac1N
\sum_{0\le n<N}
e^{2\pi i h(U_{n+k}-U_n)}.
$$

The same computation used above yields

$$
\frac1N
\sum_{0\le n<N}
e^{2\pi i h(U_{n+k}-U_n)}
\longrightarrow0,
$$

because

$$
\frac1N
\sum_{0\le n<N}
e^{2\pi i hU_n}
\longrightarrow0
$$

by Exercise 22 with $k=1$. Hence Exercise 22 again implies that

$$
\langle(U_{n+k}-U_n)\bmod1\rangle
$$

is equidistributed. Therefore the two properties are equivalent.

### (b)

Proceed by induction on the degree $d$.

For $d=1$,

$$
U_n=(\alpha_1n+\alpha_0)\bmod1,
$$

with $\alpha_1$ irrational. By the argument in Section 3.5 and Weyl's theorem, this sequence is equidistributed.

Assume that every polynomial sequence of degree less than $d$ whose leading coefficient is irrational is equidistributed. Let

$$
P(n)=\alpha_dn^d+\alpha_{d-1}n^{d-1}+\cdots+\alpha_0,
$$

where $\alpha_d$ is irrational, and set

$$
U_n=P(n)\bmod1.
$$

For any fixed positive integer $k$,

$$
P(n+k)-P(n)
=
\alpha_d\bigl((n+k)^d-n^d\bigr)
+\alpha_{d-1}\bigl((n+k)^{d-1}-n^{d-1}\bigr)
+\cdots .
$$

The binomial theorem gives

$$
(n+k)^d-n^d
=
dk\,n^{d-1}
+\text{terms of lower degree}.
$$

Hence

$$
P(n+k)-P(n)
=
(dk\alpha_d)n^{d-1}
+\text{terms of degree at most }d-2.
$$

Since $dk$ is a nonzero integer and $\alpha_d$ is irrational, the coefficient $dk\alpha_d$ is irrational. Therefore

$$
\langle(P(n+k)-P(n))\bmod1\rangle
$$

is a polynomial sequence of degree $d-1$ whose leading coefficient is irrational. By the induction hypothesis, it is equidistributed.

Part (a) states that if all the difference sequences are equidistributed, then $(U_n)$ itself is equidistributed. Therefore

$$
\langle(\alpha_dn^d+\cdots+\alpha_1n+\alpha_0)\bmod1\rangle
$$

is equidistributed whenever $d>0$ and $\alpha_d$ is irrational.

## Verification

For fixed $k$,

$$
P(n+k)-P(n)
=
dk\alpha_dn^{d-1}+O(n^{d-2}),
$$

so the degree decreases by one and the new leading coefficient remains irrational. Repeated differencing eventually reaches the linear case, which is equidistributed. Part (a) then propagates equidistribution back to the original sequence.

The induction is therefore complete.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Notes

Repeated application of part (a) is the classical van der Corput difference method. It reduces the equidistribution of a polynomial sequence to the corresponding statement for lower-degree polynomials, and after finitely many steps only the linear case remains.
