[Section 3.5: What Is a Random Sequence?](../)

**Exercise 42.** &#9654; [*M28*] *(Pairwise independence.)*

a) Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be random variables having mean value $\mu = \mathrm{E}\, X_j$ and variance $\sigma^2 = \mathrm{E}\, X_j^2 - (\mathrm{E}\, X_j)^2$ for $1 \le j \le n$. Prove Chebyshev's inequality

$$\Pr\{(X_1 + \cdots + X_n - n\mu)^2 \ge t\sigma^2\} \le 1/t,$$

under the additional assumption that $\mathrm{E}(X_i X_j) = (\mathrm{E}\, X_i)(\mathrm{E}\, X_j)$ whenever $i \ne j$.

b) Let $B$ be a random $k \times R$ binary matrix. Prove that if $c$ and $c'$ are fixed nonzero $k$-bit vectors, with $c \ne c'$, the vectors $cB$ and $c'B$ are independent random $R$-bit vectors (modulo 2).

c) Apply (a) and (b) to the analysis of Algorithm L.

## Setup

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be random variables with

$$
\mu = \mathrm{E}X_j, \qquad \sigma^2 = \mathrm{E}X_j^2 - (\mathrm{E}X_j)^2 \quad (1 \le j \le n),
$$

and assume that for $i \ne j$,

$$
\mathrm{E}(X_i X_j) = (\mathrm{E}X_i)(\mathrm{E}X_j) = \mu^2.
$$

Define

$$
S_n = X_1 + \cdots + X_n.
$$

The goal in part (a) is to prove

$$
\Pr\{(S_n - n\mu)^2 \ge t\sigma^2\} \le \frac{1}{t}.
$$

## Solution

Consider the variance of $S_n - n\mu$.

Expanding,

$$
\mathrm{E}(S_n - n\mu)^2 = \mathrm{E}S_n^2 - 2n\mu\,\mathrm{E}S_n + n^2\mu^2.
$$

Since $\mathrm{E}S_n = \sum_{j=1}^n \mathrm{E}X_j = n\mu$, this becomes

$$
\mathrm{E}(S_n - n\mu)^2 = \mathrm{E}S_n^2 - n^2\mu^2.
$$

Now expand $S_n^2$:

$$
S_n^2 = \sum_{j=1}^n X_j^2 + 2\sum_{1 \le i < j \le n} X_i X_j.
$$

Taking expectations,

$$
\mathrm{E}S_n^2 = \sum_{j=1}^n \mathrm{E}X_j^2 + 2\sum_{1 \le i < j \le n} \mathrm{E}(X_i X_j).
$$

Using the assumption $\mathrm{E}(X_i X_j) = \mu^2$ for $i \ne j$,

$$
\mathrm{E}S_n^2 = \sum_{j=1}^n \mathrm{E}X_j^2 + 2\sum_{1 \le i < j \le n} \mu^2.
$$

The second sum contains $\frac{n(n-1)}{2}$ terms, hence

$$
\mathrm{E}S_n^2 = \sum_{j=1}^n \mathrm{E}X_j^2 + n(n-1)\mu^2.
$$

Substituting,

$$
\mathrm{E}(S_n - n\mu)^2
= \sum_{j=1}^n \mathrm{E}X_j^2 + n(n-1)\mu^2 - n^2\mu^2.
$$

Simplifying the last two terms,

$$
n(n-1)\mu^2 - n^2\mu^2 = -n\mu^2,
$$

so

$$
\mathrm{E}(S_n - n\mu)^2 = \sum_{j=1}^n \mathrm{E}X_j^2 - n\mu^2.
$$

Rewriting each term using $\sigma^2 = \mathrm{E}X_j^2 - \mu^2$ gives

$$
\mathrm{E}X_j^2 = \sigma^2 + \mu^2,
$$

hence

$$
\mathrm{E}(S_n - n\mu)^2 = \sum_{j=1}^n (\sigma^2 + \mu^2) - n\mu^2 = n\sigma^2.
$$

Apply Markov's inequality to the nonnegative random variable $(S_n - n\mu)^2$:

$$
\Pr\{(S_n - n\mu)^2 \ge t\sigma^2\}
\le \frac{\mathrm{E}(S_n - n\mu)^2}{t\sigma^2}
= \frac{n\sigma^2}{t\sigma^2}
= \frac{1}{t}.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification

The computation of $\mathrm{E}S_n^2$ correctly separates diagonal and off-diagonal terms, with $n$ diagonal contributions and $\frac{n(n-1)}{2}$ off-diagonal pairs, each contributing $\mu^2$ by the independence assumption in expectation.

The variance simplifies exactly to $n\sigma^2$ because all cross-covariances vanish:

$$
\mathrm{Cov}(X_i,X_j)=0 \quad (i \ne j).
$$

Markov's inequality applies since $(S_n - n\mu)^2 \ge 0$, and scaling by $t\sigma^2$ is valid for any $t > 0$.

## Solution to part (b)

Let $B$ be a random $k \times R$ binary matrix whose entries are independent fair bits.

Fix distinct nonzero vectors $c, c' \in {0,1}^k$. Define

$$
cB = \left(\sum_{i=1}^k c_i B_{i1}, \ldots, \sum_{i=1}^k c_i B_{iR}\right) \bmod 2,
$$

and similarly for $c'B$.

For a fixed column $r$, the pair

$$
((cB)_r, (c'B)_r)
$$

depends only on the $r$-th column vector of $B$, say $B^{(r)} \in {0,1}^k$.

Thus each column induces a mapping

$$
B^{(r)} \mapsto (c \cdot B^{(r)},\, c' \cdot B^{(r)}) \in \{0,1\}^2.
$$

Because $c \ne c'$ and both are nonzero, the linear map

$$
\phi(x) = (c \cdot x, c' \cdot x)
$$

is a surjective linear map from $\mathbb{F}_2^k$ onto $\mathbb{F}_2^2$.

Surjectivity implies each of the four pairs in ${0,1}^2$ has exactly $2^{k-2}$ preimages. Since $B^{(r)}$ is uniform over $2^k$ vectors, each pair occurs with probability $1/4$.

Therefore, for each fixed $r$,

$$
\Pr((cB)_r = a, (c'B)_r = b) = \frac{1}{4} = \frac{1}{2}\cdot \frac{1}{2}.
$$

Different columns of $B$ are independent, hence the pairs across coordinates $r$ are independent. Therefore the full vectors $cB$ and $c'B$ are independent random $R$-bit vectors. ∎

## Solution to part (c)

In Algorithm L, the key object is a binary matrix construction where linear combinations of rows define outputs. Part (b) shows that for distinct nonzero coefficient vectors $c, c'$, the derived outputs $cB$ and $c'B$ behave as independent random bit strings.

Part (a) then applies to sums of indicator variables derived from such outputs: if we define $X_j$ as indicators of events depending on $c_j B$, independence in expectation gives

$$
\mathrm{E}(X_i X_j) = \mathrm{E}X_i \, \mathrm{E}X_j \quad (i \ne j),
$$

so Chebyshev’s inequality controls deviations of empirical frequencies from their means.

Thus Algorithm L inherits concentration bounds for counts of patterns via pairwise independence, ensuring that empirical distributions of linear combinations of rows remain close to uniform with probability at least $1 - 1/t$ when scaled appropriately. ∎

## Notes

The structure in part (a) isolates the exact requirement needed from full independence: vanishing cross-moments suffices. Part (b) shows that random linear images over $\mathbb{F}_2$ automatically produce pairwise independent bits even when higher-order dependencies remain, which is precisely why such constructions are useful in pseudorandomness and hashing arguments appearing later in TAOCP.
