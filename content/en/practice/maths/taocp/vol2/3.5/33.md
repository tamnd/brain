[Section 3.5: What Is a Random Sequence?](../)

**Exercise 33.** [*HM22*] *[HM22]* Let $\langle U_n \rangle$ and $\langle t_n \rangle$ be infinite disjoint subsequences of a sequence $\langle U_n \rangle$. (Thus, $r_0 < r_1 < r_2 < \cdots$ and $s_0 < s_1 < s_2 < \cdots$ are increasing sequences of integers and $r_m \ne s_n$ for any $m, n$.) Let $\langle U_{t_n} \rangle$ be the combined subsequence, so that $t_0 < t_1 < t_2 < \cdots$ and the set $\{t_n\} = \{r_n\} \cup \{s_n\}$. Show that if $\Pr\{U_{r_n} \in A\} = \Pr\{U_{s_n} \in A\} = p$, then $\Pr\{U_{t_n} \in A\} = p$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m11s  

---

Let $\langle r_n\rangle$ and $\langle s_n\rangle$ be strictly increasing sequences of integers with disjoint ranges, and let $\langle t_n\rangle$ be the increasing enumeration of the union $\{r_n\} \cup \{s_n\}$. Thus, for every $n$, there exists a unique index $k$ such that $t_n$ equals either $r_k$ or $s_k$.

Define a deterministic classification of each $t_n$ by

$$
t_n =
\begin{cases}
r_{i(n)}, & \text{for a unique } i(n) \text{ if } t_n \in \{r_k\},\\
s_{j(n)}, & \text{for a unique } j(n) \text{ if } t_n \in \{s_k\}.
\end{cases}
$$

This is well-defined because the two subsequences are disjoint.

Now fix $n$. We consider the event $\{U_{t_n} \in A\}$.

If $t_n = r_{i(n)}$, then

$$
\{U_{t_n} \in A\} = \{U_{r_{i(n)}} \in A\}.
$$

By the hypothesis, for every index $k$,

$$
\Pr\{U_{r_k} \in A\} = p,
$$

hence in particular

$$
\Pr\{U_{t_n} \in A\} = \Pr\{U_{r_{i(n)}} \in A\} = p.
$$

If $t_n = s_{j(n)}$, then similarly,

$$
\{U_{t_n} \in A\} = \{U_{s_{j(n)}} \in A\},
$$

and the hypothesis gives, for every $k$,

$$
\Pr\{U_{s_k} \in A\} = p,
$$

so

$$
\Pr\{U_{t_n} \in A\} = \Pr\{U_{s_{j(n)}} \in A\} = p.
$$

In both cases, the probability of $\{U_{t_n} \in A\}$ equals $p$. Therefore, for every $n$,

$$
\Pr\{U_{t_n} \in A\} = p.
$$

This proves that the merged subsequence $\langle U_{t_n} \rangle$ has the same marginal probability $p$ for membership in $A$ at every position. ∎
