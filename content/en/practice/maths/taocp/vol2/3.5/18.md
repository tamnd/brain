**Exercise 18.** &#9654; [*HM22*] Prove that if $U_0, U_1, \ldots$ is $k$-distributed, so is the sequence $V_0, V_1, \ldots$, where $V_n = \lfloor nU_n \rfloor / n$.

## Solution

Let

$$
V_n=\frac{\lfloor nU_n\rfloor}{n}.
$$

We must prove that the sequence $V_0,V_1,\ldots$ satisfies Definition B whenever $U_0,U_1,\ldots$ does.

Fix real numbers

$$
0\le u_i<v_i\le 1 \qquad (1\le i\le k).
$$

Let $S(n)$ be the statement

$$
u_1\le V_n<v_1,\;
u_2\le V_{n+1}<v_2,\;
\ldots,\;
u_k\le V_{n+k-1}<v_k.
$$

By Definition B, it suffices to show that

$$
\Pr(S(n))
=
(v_1-u_1)\cdots(v_k-u_k).
\tag{1}
$$

For $m\ge1$ and any interval $[u,v)$, define

$$
A_m(u,v)
=
\left\{
x:\;
u\le \frac{\lfloor mx\rfloor}{m}<v
\right\}.
$$

Since

$$
u\le \frac{\lfloor mx\rfloor}{m}<v
\iff
mu\le \lfloor mx\rfloor < mv,
$$

the set $A_m(u,v)$ is a union of intervals

$$
\bigcup_{\lceil mu\rceil\le r< mv}
\left[\frac r m,\frac{r+1}{m}\right).
$$

Hence

$$
\frac{\lceil mu\rceil}{m}
\le x
<
\frac{\lfloor mv\rfloor}{m}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
x\in A_m(u,v),
$$

and

$$
x\in A_m(u,v)
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
u-\frac1m
\le x
<
v.
$$

Therefore

$$
\left[\frac{\lceil mu\rceil}{m},\frac{\lfloor mv\rfloor}{m}\right)
\subseteq
A_m(u,v)
\subseteq
\left[u-\frac1m,v\right).
\tag{2}
$$

Apply (2) with $m=n+i-1$. If

$$
T(n)
:
u_1\le U_n-\frac1n <v_1,\ldots,
u_k\le U_{n+k-1}-\frac1{n+k-1}<v_k,
$$

then $T(n)\Rightarrow S(n)$. Likewise, if

$$
R(n)
:
\frac{\lceil (n+i-1)u_i\rceil}{\,n+i-1\,}
\le U_{n+i-1}
<
\frac{\lfloor (n+i-1)v_i\rfloor}{\,n+i-1\,},
\quad
1\le i\le k,
$$

then $R(n)\Rightarrow S(n)$.

Consequently,

$$
\Pr(R(n))
\le
\Pr(S(n))
\le
\Pr(T(n)).
\tag{3}
$$

Since the sequence $U_0,U_1,\ldots$ is $k$-distributed, Definition B gives

$$
\Pr(T(n))
=
\prod_{i=1}^k
\left(
v_i-u_i+\frac1{n+i-1}
\right),
\tag{4}
$$

and

$$
\Pr(R(n))
=
\prod_{i=1}^k
\left(
\frac{\lfloor (n+i-1)v_i\rfloor}{n+i-1}
-
\frac{\lceil (n+i-1)u_i\rceil}{n+i-1}
\right).
\tag{5}
$$

For each fixed $i$,

$$
\frac{\lfloor (n+i-1)v_i\rfloor}{n+i-1}
\to v_i,
\qquad
\frac{\lceil (n+i-1)u_i\rceil}{n+i-1}
\to u_i,
$$

as $n\to\infty$. Hence the right-hand side of (5) tends to

$$
\prod_{i=1}^k (v_i-u_i),
\tag{6}
$$

and the right-hand side of (4) also tends to

$$
\prod_{i=1}^k (v_i-u_i).
\tag{7}
$$

Taking limits in (3) and using (6) and (7), the squeeze theorem yields

$$
\Pr(S(n))
=
\prod_{i=1}^k (v_i-u_i).
$$

This is exactly equation (5) for the sequence $V_0,V_1,\ldots$. Therefore $V_0,V_1,\ldots$ is $k$-distributed.

This completes the proof.

∎
