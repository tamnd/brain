[Section 3.5: What Is a Random Sequence?](../)

**Exercise 18.** &#9654; [*HM22*] Prove that if $U_0, U_1, \ldots$ is $k$-distributed, so is the sequence $V_0, V_1, \ldots$, where $V_n = \lfloor nU_n \rfloor / n$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m51s  

---

The original argument fails because it replaces the deterministic notion of $k$-distribution (as an asymptotic statement about empirical frequencies of $k$-blocks) with an incorrect probabilistic convergence framework. The correct proof must work entirely with counting functions and fixed boxes in $[0,1]^k$.

## Corrected solution

Let $U_0,U_1,\ldots$ be $k$-distributed in the sense that for every box

$$
B = \prod_{j=1}^k [a_j,b_j) \subseteq [0,1]^k,
$$

we have

$$
\frac{1}{N}\#\Bigl\{0 \le n < N : (U_n,\ldots,U_{n+k-1}) \in B\Bigr\}
\to \lambda(B)
= \prod_{j=1}^k (b_j-a_j).
$$

Define

$$
V_n = \frac{\lfloor nU_n \rfloor}{n}.
$$

Then for every $n$,

$$
0 \le U_n - V_n < \frac{1}{n}.
$$

We prove that $V_0,V_1,\ldots$ is also $k$-distributed.

### Step 1: Set up the counting problem

Fix a box

$$
B = \prod_{j=1}^k [a_j,b_j) \subseteq [0,1]^k.
$$

Let

$$
N_V(B;N) = \#\{0 \le n < N : (V_n,\ldots,V_{n+k-1}) \in B\},
$$

and similarly $N_U(\cdot;N)$.

We must show

$$
\frac{N_V(B;N)}{N} \to \prod_{j=1}^k (b_j-a_j).
$$

### Step 2: Relating $U$ and $V$ coordinatewise

From

$$
0 \le U_n - V_n < \frac{1}{n},
$$

we get for each coordinate $j$,

$$
V_{n+j-1} \le U_{n+j-1} < V_{n+j-1} + \frac{1}{n}.
$$

### Step 3: Inner and outer approximating boxes

Fix $\varepsilon > 0$. For all sufficiently large $n$, we have $1/n < \varepsilon$.

Define fixed boxes

$$
B^- = \prod_{j=1}^k [a_j+\varepsilon,\, b_j-\varepsilon],
\quad
B^+ = \prod_{j=1}^k [a_j-\varepsilon,\, b_j+\varepsilon].
$$

(If any interval becomes empty, the argument is trivial for small $\varepsilon$, so assume $\varepsilon$ is small enough.)

### Step 4: Key inclusions

For all sufficiently large $n$:

**Lower inclusion.** If

$$
(U_n,\ldots,U_{n+k-1}) \in B^-,
$$

then for each $j$,

$$
U_{n+j-1} \in [a_j+\varepsilon, b_j-\varepsilon],
$$

so

$$
V_{n+j-1} \in [a_j, b_j],
$$

hence

$$
(V_n,\ldots,V_{n+k-1}) \in B.
$$

Thus, for all large $n$,

$$
\mathbf{1}_{(U_n,\ldots,U_{n+k-1}) \in B^-}
\le
\mathbf{1}_{(V_n,\ldots,V_{n+k-1}) \in B}.
$$

**Upper inclusion.** If

$$
(V_n,\ldots,V_{n+k-1}) \in B,
$$

then for each $j$,

$$
V_{n+j-1} \in [a_j,b_j],
$$

so

$$
U_{n+j-1} \in \left[a_j - \frac{1}{n},\, b_j + \frac{1}{n}\right]
\subseteq [a_j-\varepsilon, b_j+\varepsilon]
$$

for all sufficiently large $n$. Hence

$$
(U_n,\ldots,U_{n+k-1}) \in B^+.
$$

Thus, for all large $n$,

$$
\mathbf{1}_{(V_n,\ldots,V_{n+k-1}) \in B}
\le
\mathbf{1}_{(U_n,\ldots,U_{n+k-1}) \in B^+}.
$$

### Step 5: Summing over $n < N$

For sufficiently large $N$,

$$
N_U(B^-;N) + O(1)
\le N_V(B;N)
\le N_U(B^+;N) + O(1),
$$

where the $O(1)$ accounts for finitely many exceptional indices $n$ where $1/n \ge \varepsilon$, which does not affect limits after dividing by $N$.

Dividing by $N$,

$$
\frac{N_U(B^-;N)}{N}
\;\le\;
\frac{N_V(B;N)}{N}
\;\le\;
\frac{N_U(B^+;N)}{N}
\;+\; o(1).
$$

### Step 6: Apply $k$-distribution of $U_n$

Since $U_n$ is $k$-distributed,

$$
\frac{N_U(B^-;N)}{N} \to \lambda(B^-),
\quad
\frac{N_U(B^+;N)}{N} \to \lambda(B^+),
$$

where

$$
\lambda(B^-)=\prod_{j=1}^k (b_j-a_j-2\varepsilon),
\quad
\lambda(B^+)=\prod_{j=1}^k (b_j-a_j+2\varepsilon).
$$

Hence,

$$
\liminf_{N\to\infty} \frac{N_V(B;N)}{N}
\ge \prod_{j=1}^k (b_j-a_j-2\varepsilon),
$$

$$
\limsup_{N\to\infty} \frac{N_V(B;N)}{N}
\le \prod_{j=1}^k (b_j-a_j+2\varepsilon).
$$

### Step 7: Let $\varepsilon \to 0$

By continuity of the product,

$$
\prod_{j=1}^k (b_j-a_j-2\varepsilon) \to \prod_{j=1}^k (b_j-a_j),
\quad
\prod_{j=1}^k (b_j-a_j+2\varepsilon) \to \prod_{j=1}^k (b_j-a_j).
$$

Therefore,

$$
\lim_{N\to\infty} \frac{N_V(B;N)}{N}
=
\prod_{j=1}^k (b_j-a_j).
$$

## Conclusion

For every box $B \subseteq [0,1]^k$, the empirical frequency of $k$-blocks of $V_n$ converges to $\lambda(B)$. Hence $V_0,V_1,\ldots$ is $k$-distributed. âˆŽ
