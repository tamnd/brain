[Section 3.5: What Is a Random Sequence?](../)

**Exercise 15.** &#9654; [*HM30*] Show that an $\infty$-distributed sequence passes the "coupon-collector's test" when there are two kinds (kinds) of coupons, in the following sense: Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be an $\infty$-distributed binary sequence. Let $f(0) = 0$ and for $n \ge 1$ let $f(n)$ be the smallest integer $m > f(n-1)$ such that $\{X_{f(n-1)+1}, \ldots, X_m\}$ is the set $\{0, 1\}$. Prove that $\Pr(f(n) - f(n-1) = k) = 2^{2-k}$, for $k \ge 2$. (See exercise 7.)

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m25s  

---

We restart the argument from the definition of the increment and compute its distribution directly, keeping careful track of all admissible configurations.

Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be an $\infty$-distributed binary sequence and define $f(0)=0$. For $n \ge 1$, $f(n)$ is the first index $m>f(n-1)$ such that both symbols $0$ and $1$ occur among $X_{f(n-1)+1}, \ldots, X_m$. Let

$$
T_n = f(n)-f(n-1).
$$

By shift-invariance of all finite-dimensional distributions implied by $\infty$-distribution, each $T_n$ has the same law as $T_1$. Hence it suffices to compute $T:=T_1=f(1)$.

### Step 1: Characterization of the event $T=k$

For $k \ge 2$, the event $\{T=k\}$ means:

1. Among $X_1, \ldots, X_{k-1}$, only one symbol appears.
2. Among $X_1, \ldots, X_k$, both symbols appear.

The first condition forces $X_1=\cdots=X_{k-1}$, so there are exactly two cases:

- $X_1=\cdots=X_{k-1}=0$, or
- $X_1=\cdots=X_{k-1}=1$.

In the first case, the second condition forces $X_k=1$.

In the second case, it forces $X_k=0$.

Thus $\{T=k\}$ is the disjoint union of the two cylinder events

$$
\{X_1=\cdots=X_{k-1}=0,\, X_k=1\}, \quad \{X_1=\cdots=X_{k-1}=1,\, X_k=0\}.
$$

These are the only admissible configurations.

### Step 2: Probabilities from $\infty$-distribution

Since the sequence is $\infty$-distributed, every specified binary pattern of length $k$ has probability $2^{-k}$. In particular,

$$
\Pr(X_1=\cdots=X_{k-1}=0, X_k=1)=2^{-k},
$$

and similarly,

$$
\Pr(X_1=\cdots=X_{k-1}=1, X_k=0)=2^{-k}.
$$

These two events are disjoint, so we add their probabilities:

$$
\Pr(T=k)=2^{-k}+2^{-k}=2^{1-k}.
$$

### Step 3: Verification and normalization

We check consistency:

$$
\sum_{k=2}^\infty 2^{1-k} = 2 \sum_{k=2}^\infty 2^{-k} = 2 \cdot \frac{1/4}{1-1/2} = 1.
$$

Hence this is a valid probability distribution.

### Step 4: Extension to all increments

For each $n$, the block $X_{f(n-1)+1}, X_{f(n-1)+2}, \ldots$ has the same finite-dimensional distributions as the original sequence (by $\infty$-distribution, i.e. shift-invariance of all finite patterns). Therefore the same argument applies verbatim to each increment $T_n$, so all $T_n$ are identically distributed with the same law.

### Conclusion

For all $n \ge 1$ and $k \ge 2$,

$$
\boxed{\Pr(f(n)-f(n-1)=k)=2^{1-k}}.
$$

### Remark on the stated formula

The expression $2^{2-k}$ appearing in the exercise statement cannot be correct for a probability distribution on $k \ge 2$, since

$$
\sum_{k=2}^\infty 2^{2-k} = 2.
$$

The correct distribution derived from the definition and $\infty$-distribution is $2^{1-k}$, which is properly normalized and matches the combinatorial structure of “waiting until the first change of symbol.”
