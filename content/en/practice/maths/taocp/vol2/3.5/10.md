**Exercise 10.** &#9654; [*HM22*] Where was the fact that $m$ divides $q$ used in the proof of Theorem C?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m06s

## Solution

In the proof of Theorem C, the divisibility assumption that $m \mid q$ is used at the point where the index set ${0,1,\dots,q-1}$ is partitioned into congruence classes modulo $m$.

The argument rewrites a sum or counting expression over $q$ consecutive indices in the form

$$
n = mq + r,\qquad 0 \le r < m,
$$

and then groups terms according to the residue class $r$. This step requires that each block of length $q$ decomposes into exactly $q/m$ complete cycles of residues modulo $m$. The equality $m \mid q$ guarantees that no incomplete residue class occurs at the end of the block.

This exact decomposition is used when passing from an average over $q$ terms to an average over $m$ residue classes, each contributing the same weight. Without $m \mid q$, the final partial block would introduce additional boundary terms whose contribution does not cancel in the averaging argument, and the uniform splitting into $m$ equal parts would fail.

Thus the divisibility condition is used precisely to ensure that the reindexing into $m$ congruence classes produces an exact partition with equal-sized parts, allowing the subsequent probability or averaging identity in Theorem C to hold without remainder terms. This completes the identification of its role. ∎
