[Section 3.5: What Is a Random Sequence?](../)

**Exercise 10.** &#9654; [*HM22*] Where was the fact that $m$ divides $q$ used in the proof of Theorem C?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 7m19s  

---

The proof of Theorem C uses the hypothesis \(m \mid q\) at the point where one must show that the residue classes

\[
0,\; m,\; 2m,\; \ldots,\; \left(\frac{q}{m}-1\right)m
\]

are distinct modulo \(q\).

Suppose that two of these multiples are congruent modulo \(q\):

\[
im \equiv jm \pmod q .
\]

Then

\[
q \mid (i-j)m .
\]

At this stage the argument writes \(q=mt\), which is possible only because \(m\mid q\). Substituting \(q=mt\) gives

\[
mt \mid (i-j)m,
\]

hence

\[
t \mid (i-j).
\]

Since \(0 \le i,j < t=q/m\), we have

\[
|i-j|<t,
\]

so the only multiple of \(t\) that \(i-j\) can be is \(0\). Therefore \(i=j\).

This proves that the multiples \(0,m,2m,\ldots,(q/m-1)m\) are distinct modulo \(q\), and consequently that there are exactly \(q/m\) residue classes satisfying the required condition.

Thus the fact that \(m\) divides \(q\) is used precisely when replacing \(q\) by \(mt\) in order to deduce from

\[
q \mid (i-j)m
\]

that

\[
\frac{q}{m} \mid (i-j),
\]

which is the key step establishing the distinctness of the residue classes.
