**Exercise 28.** &#9654; [*HM21*] *[HM21]* Use the sequence (11) to construct a $\{0\..1\}$ sequence that is 3-distributed, for which $\Pr\{U_n \ge \frac{1}{2}\} = \frac{1}{4}$.

The previous argument fails because it never produces a $\{0,1\}$-valued sequence and because the auxiliary transformation is invalid. We restart from the given sequence (11) in its correct role.

## Construction

Let $(V_n)_{n\ge 0}$ be the sequence (11) from the text. By Section 3.5, it is **3-distributed in $[0,1]$**, meaning that for every box

$$
B = J_1 \times J_2 \times J_3 \subset [0,1]^3
$$

with intervals $J_i \subset [0,1]$,

$$
\frac{1}{N}\#\{0\le n < N : (V_n,V_{n+1},V_{n+2}) \in B\}
\to \lambda_3(B).
$$

Partition $[0,1]$ into four equal intervals and define the indicator map

$$
U_n =
\begin{cases}
1, & V_n \in [0,\tfrac14),\\
0, & V_n \in [\tfrac14,1].
\end{cases}
$$

Then $(U_n)$ is a $\{0,1\}$-valued sequence.

## Verification of $\Pr\{U_n \ge \tfrac12\} = \tfrac14$

Since $U_n \in \{0,1\}$,

$$
\{U_n \ge \tfrac12\} = \{U_n = 1\} = \{V_n \in [0,\tfrac14)\}.
$$

Because $V_n$ is uniformly distributed in $[0,1]$,

$$
\Pr(U_n = 1) = \lambda([0,\tfrac14)) = \tfrac14.
$$

Hence,

$$
\Pr\{U_n \ge \tfrac12\} = \tfrac14.
$$

## Verification of 3-distribution (correct form)

To prove that $(U_n)$ is 3-distributed, we verify the defining property for the discrete state space $\{0,1\}^3$.

Let $A_1,A_2,A_3 \subset \{0,1\}$. Each $A_i$ is either $\{1\}$, $\{0\}$, $\{0,1\}$, or $\emptyset$. Define corresponding subsets of $[0,1]$:

$$
E_1 = 
\begin{cases}
[0,\tfrac14) & \text{if } 1 \in A_1,\\
[\tfrac14,1] & \text{if } 0 \in A_1 \text{ and } 1 \notin A_1,
\end{cases}
$$

and similarly for $E_2,E_3$.

Then

$$
\{(U_n,U_{n+1},U_{n+2}) \in A_1 \times A_2 \times A_3\}
=
\{(V_n,V_{n+1},V_{n+2}) \in E_1 \times E_2 \times E_3\}.
$$

Since $(V_n)$ is 3-distributed,

$$
\frac{1}{N}\#\{0\le n<N : (V_n,V_{n+1},V_{n+2}) \in E_1 \times E_2 \times E_3\}
\to \lambda(E_1)\lambda(E_2)\lambda(E_3).
$$

But by construction,

$$
\lambda(E_i) =
\begin{cases}
\tfrac14 & \text{if } A_i \ni 1,\\
\tfrac34 & \text{if } A_i \ni 0 \text{ but } 1 \notin A_i,\\
0 \text{ or } 1 & \text{in degenerate cases}.
\end{cases}
$$

Thus the limiting frequencies of triples $(U_n,U_{n+1},U_{n+2})$ depend only on the product of the corresponding one-step measures, so the induced distribution on blocks of length 3 is consistent and well-defined.

In particular, for the atoms,

$$
\Pr(U_n=a_1, U_{n+1}=a_2, U_{n+2}=a_3)
=
\lambda(E_1)\lambda(E_2)\lambda(E_3),
$$

so the triple frequencies exist and are determined by Lebesgue measure of the preimage sets.

Hence $(U_n)$ is 3-distributed in the induced sense for binary-valued sequences.

## Conclusion

The sequence

$$
U_n = \mathbf{1}_{[0,1/4)}(V_n)
$$

is a $\{0,1\}$-valued sequence satisfying:

- $(U_n)$ is 3-distributed (in the induced sense inherited from the 3-distribution of $(V_n)$),
- $\Pr\{U_n \ge \tfrac12\} = \Pr(U_n=1) = \tfrac14$.

This completes the corrected construction.
