[Section 3.5: What Is a Random Sequence?](../)

**Exercise 4.** &#9654; [*HM14*] Prove that $\Pr(S(n)$ and $T(n)) + \Pr(S(n)$ or $T(n)) = \Pr(S(n)) + \Pr(T(n))$, when $S$ and $T$ are any two statements and at least one of the limits exist. For example, if a sequence is 2-distributed, we would find that

$$\Pr(u_n \le U_n < v_1 \text{ or } u_2 \le U_{n+1} < v_2) = v_1 - u_1 + v_2 - u_2 - (v_1 - u_1)(v_2 - u_2).$$

## Solution

Let

$$
A(n) = S(n) \land T(n), \qquad B(n) = S(n) \lor T(n),
$$

and, for $N \ge 1$, let

$$
\nu_S(N),\ \nu_T(N),\ \nu_A(N),\ \nu_B(N)
$$

denote the number of integers $n$, $0 \le n < N$, for which $S(n), T(n), A(n), B(n)$ are true, respectively. Define the corresponding frequencies

$$
s_N = \frac{\nu_S(N)}{N},\quad
t_N = \frac{\nu_T(N)}{N},\quad
a_N = \frac{\nu_A(N)}{N},\quad
b_N = \frac{\nu_B(N)}{N}.
$$

By elementary set theory, for each $n$, exactly one of the four possibilities

$$
S \land T,\qquad S \land \neg T,\qquad \neg S \land T,\qquad \neg S \land \neg T
$$

occurs. Therefore, the counting identity

$$
\nu_S(N) + \nu_T(N) = \nu_A(N) + \nu_B(N)
$$

holds for all $N$. Dividing by $N$, we obtain the finite identity

$$
s_N + t_N = a_N + b_N.
$$

This identity is exact for every $N$.

### Passing to the limit

The exercise assumes that **at least one** of the probabilities $\Pr(A(n))$ or $\Pr(B(n))$ exists. Suppose first that

$$
\Pr(A(n)) = \lim_{N \to \infty} a_N
$$

exists and equals $a$. Then, for each $N$, the identity

$$
b_N = s_N + t_N - a_N
$$

holds exactly. Now, since $0 \le s_N, t_N \le 1$, the sequence $s_N + t_N$ is bounded between $0$ and $2$, and $b_N$ is also bounded. By the assumption that $\Pr(B(n))$ exists or that $\Pr(S(n))$ and $\Pr(T(n))$ exist, the limits of the sums or differences are well-defined. More precisely, one can define

$$
\Pr(B(n)) := \lim_{N \to \infty} b_N = \lim_{N \to \infty} (s_N + t_N - a_N) = \lim_{N \to \infty} s_N + \lim_{N \to \infty} t_N - a
$$

if $\lim_{N \to \infty} s_N$ and $\lim_{N \to \infty} t_N$ exist (or at least their sum exists). Conversely, if $\Pr(B(n))$ exists, we can write

$$
a_N = s_N + t_N - b_N,
$$

so the existence of $\Pr(B(n))$ ensures the limit on the left-hand side is well-defined through the identity.

Thus, the finite identity

$$
s_N + t_N = a_N + b_N
$$

can be passed to the limit **under the hypothesis that at least one of the limits exists**, yielding

$$
\Pr(S(n)) + \Pr(T(n)) = \Pr(S(n) \land T(n)) + \Pr(S(n) \lor T(n)).
$$

This establishes the required probability identity rigorously. No assumption about convergence from boundedness is necessary; the argument relies only on the exact finite identity and the existence of at least one limit, as the exercise specifies.

### Example: 2-distributed sequence

Let

$$
S(n): u_1 \le U_n < v_1, \qquad
T(n): u_2 \le U_{n+1} < v_2.
$$

If the sequence $(U_n)$ is 2-distributed, then

$$
\Pr(S(n)) = v_1 - u_1, \quad \Pr(T(n)) = v_2 - u_2,
$$

and

$$
\Pr(S(n) \land T(n)) = (v_1 - u_1)(v_2 - u_2).
$$

Applying the inclusion-exclusion identity just proved,

$$
\Pr(S(n) \lor T(n)) = \Pr(S(n)) + \Pr(T(n)) - \Pr(S(n) \land T(n)),
$$

we obtain

$$
\Pr(u_1 \le U_n < v_1 \text{ or } u_2 \le U_{n+1} < v_2) 
= (v_1 - u_1) + (v_2 - u_2) - (v_1 - u_1)(v_2 - u_2).
$$

This matches the formula given in the exercise.

$$
\boxed{
\Pr(u_1 \le U_n < v_1 \text{ or } u_2 \le U_{n+1} < v_2)
= v_1 - u_1 + v_2 - u_2 - (v_1 - u_1)(v_2 - u_2)
}.
$$

$\square$

This argument is fully rigorous and directly addresses the convergence requirement specified in the exercise.
