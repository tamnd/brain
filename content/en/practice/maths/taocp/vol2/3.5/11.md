**Exercise 11.** [*M10*] Use Theorem C to prove that if a sequence $(U_n)$ is $\infty$-distributed, so is the subsequence $(U_{2n})$.

**Verified:** yes

**Solution to Exercise 3.5.11 [_M10_]**

Let $(U_n)$ be an $\infty$-distributed sequence. By Definition C, $(U_n)$ is $k$-distributed for every positive integer $k$. We want to show that the subsequence $(U_{2n})$ is also $\infty$-distributed; that is, $(U_{2n})$ is $k$-distributed for every $k$.

Fix a positive integer $k$ and consider a $k$-tuple $(\alpha_0, \alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_{k-1})$ of $b$-ary digits (or intervals in the $[0,1)$ case). Let $N$ be a large positive integer. We examine the frequency of this $k$-tuple in the first $N$ terms of the subsequence $(U_{2n})$, namely

$$
U_2, U_4, \ldots, U_{2N}.
$$

Each occurrence of $(\alpha_0, \alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_{k-1})$ in $(U_{2n})$ corresponds to a $2k$-tuple

$$
(U_{2n}, U_{2n+1}, U_{2n+2}, U_{2n+3}, \ldots, U_{2n+2k-2}, U_{2n+2k-1})
$$

in $(U_n)$ where the even-indexed positions match the desired $k$-tuple, and the odd-indexed positions are arbitrary.

By Theorem C, for any $2k$-tuple in $(U_n)$, the limiting frequency exists and is exactly $1/b^{2k}$. Therefore, to count the occurrences of our fixed $k$-tuple in $(U_{2n})$, we sum over all possible sequences of the $k$ intermediate terms $(U_{2n+1}, U_{2n+3}, \ldots, U_{2n+2k-1})$. There are exactly $b^k$ possible sequences of these intermediate terms, and each occurs with frequency $1/b^{2k}$. Summing over all $b^k$ possibilities gives

$$
\sum_{(\text{all intermediate terms})} \frac{1}{b^{2k}} = \frac{b^k}{b^{2k}} = \frac{1}{b^k}.
$$

Hence the limiting frequency of the $k$-tuple $(\alpha_0, \alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_{k-1})$ in $(U_{2n})$ exists and equals $1/b^k$. This holds for every $k$-tuple. Therefore $(U_{2n})$ is $k$-distributed.

Since $k$ was arbitrary, $(U_{2n})$ is $k$-distributed for all $k$, and thus $\infty$-distributed.

∎
