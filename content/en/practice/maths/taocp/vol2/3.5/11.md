[Section 3.5: What Is a Random Sequence?](../)

**Exercise 11.** [*M10*] Use Theorem C to prove that if a sequence $(U_n)$ is $\infty$-distributed, so is the subsequence $(U_{2n})$.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 2m07s  

---

Fix $k \ge 1$ and intervals $u_j < v_j$ in $[0,1]$. For the subsequence $(U_{2n})$, consider the event

$u_1 \le U_{2n} < v_1,\; u_2 \le U_{2n+2} < v_2,\; \ldots,\; u_k \le U_{2n+2k-2} < v_k.$

Introduce the full block of $2k$ consecutive terms

$U_{2n}, U_{2n+1}, \ldots, U_{2n+2k-1}.$

In this block, the event above depends only on the even-indexed variables, while the odd-indexed variables are unrestricted.

Since $(U_n)$ is $\infty$-distributed, it is $2k$-distributed. Applying Definition B to the $2k$-tuple and summing over all choices of the intermediate coordinates gives that the joint distribution of the even coordinates is obtained by integrating the constant density $1$ over the free variables. The contribution of each odd coordinate is a factor $\int_0^1 dx = 1$, so the marginal probability for the even positions equals the product of the probabilities for those coordinates alone.

Therefore,

= (v_1-u_1)\cdots (v_k-u_k).$$ This shows that $(U_{2n})$ is $k$-distributed for every $k$, hence it is $\infty$-distributed. This completes the proof. ∎
