[Section 3.5: What Is a Random Sequence?](../)

**Exercise 23.** [*M32*] [M32] (a) Show that a $[0\,..\,1)$ sequence $(U_n)$ is $k$-distributed if and only if all of the sequences $\langle (c_1 U_n + c_2 U_{n+1} + \cdots + c_k U_{n+k-1}) \bmod 1 \rangle$ are 1-distributed, whenever $c_2, \ldots, c_k$ are integers not all zero. (b) Show that a $b$-ary sequence $(X_n)$ is $k$-distributed if and only if all of the sequences $\langle (c_1 X_n + c_2 X_{n+1} + \cdots + c_k X_{n+k-1}) \bmod b \rangle$ are 1-distributed, whenever $c_1, \ldots, c_k$ are integers with $\gcd(c_k, b) = 0$.

Part (a) is already correct in substance; we restate it cleanly for completeness, then give a fully rigorous repair of part (b), where the original argument breaks down.

## (a)

We use Weyl’s criterion for $k$-distribution on $[0,1)^k$: a sequence $(U_n)$ is $k$-distributed if and only if for every nonzero integer vector $a=(a_1,\dots,a_k)$,

$$
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \exp\!\big(2\pi i (a_1U_n+\cdots+a_kU_{n+k-1})\big)\to 0.
$$

Fix an integer vector $c=(c_1,\dots,c_k)\neq 0$ and define

$$
Y_n(c)=\big(c_1U_n+\cdots+c_kU_{n+k-1}\big)\bmod 1.
$$

### Forward direction

Assume $(U_n)$ is $k$-distributed. For any integer $m\neq 0$,

$$
\exp(2\pi i m Y_n(c))=\exp\!\big(2\pi i (mc_1U_n+\cdots+mc_kU_{n+k-1})\big).
$$

Since $c\neq 0$, the vector $mc\neq 0$, so Weyl’s criterion in dimension $k$ gives

$$
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}\exp(2\pi i m Y_n(c))\to 0.
$$

Hence $(Y_n(c))$ is 1-distributed.

### Reverse direction

Assume every $(Y_n(c))$ is 1-distributed. Fix a nonzero integer vector $a$. Take $c=a$. Then 1-distribution of $Y_n(a)$ implies that for every $m\neq 0$,

$$
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}\exp\!\big(2\pi i m(a_1U_n+\cdots+a_kU_{n+k-1})\big)\to 0.
$$

In particular for $m=1$, the Weyl criterion in dimension $k$ holds for $a$, so $(U_n)$ is $k$-distributed.

## (b)

We now treat the $b$-ary case carefully. Here $X_n\in\{0,1,\dots,b-1\}$, and $k$-distribution is tested by additive characters

$$
\chi_m(x)=\exp\!\left(\frac{2\pi i mx}{b}\right),\qquad m\in\mathbb Z,\ m\not\equiv 0\pmod b.
$$

The correct Weyl criterion says that $(X_n)$ is $k$-distributed if and only if for every nonzero integer vector $a=(a_1,\dots,a_k)$,

$$
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}\exp\!\left(\frac{2\pi i (a_1X_n+\cdots+a_kX_{n+k-1})}{b}\right)\to 0,
$$

where $a$ is taken modulo $b$.

We define

$$
Z_n(c)=\big(c_1X_n+\cdots+c_kX_{n+k-1}\big)\bmod b.
$$

The key subtlety is that not every integer vector is immediately admissible in the hypothesis: we only assume 1-distribution of $Z_n(c)$ when $\gcd(c_k,b)=1$ (the condition in the statement; otherwise the linear form may collapse mod $b$ and fail to detect characters).

A second structural fact is essential: shifting indices preserves $k$-distribution. If $(X_n)$ is $k$-distributed, then so is $(X_{n+r})$, since it is just a reindexing of $k$-blocks.

### Forward direction

Assume $(X_n)$ is $k$-distributed. Fix $c$ with $\gcd(c_k,b)=1$. Fix $m\not\equiv 0\pmod b$. Then

$$
\exp\!\left(\frac{2\pi i m Z_n(c)}{b}\right)
=
\exp\!\left(\frac{2\pi i (mc_1X_n+\cdots+mc_kX_{n+k-1})}{b}\right).
$$

Because $c\not\equiv 0\pmod b$, the vector $mc\not\equiv 0\pmod b$. Hence Weyl’s criterion for $k$-distribution implies the averages tend to $0$. Therefore each $Z_n(c)$ is 1-distributed.

### Reverse direction (the delicate part)

Assume that for every integer vector $c$ with $\gcd(c_k,b)=1$, the sequence $Z_n(c)$ is 1-distributed. We prove $k$-distribution of $(X_n)$.

Let $a=(a_1,\dots,a_k)$ be any nonzero vector modulo $b$. We must show

$$
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}\exp\!\left(\frac{2\pi i (a_1X_n+\cdots+a_kX_{n+k-1})}{b}\right)\to 0.
$$

If $a_k\not\equiv 0\pmod b$, we can directly take $c=a$. Then $\gcd(c_k,b)=1$ after scaling $a$ by an integer $t$ invertible modulo $b$, since multiplying $a$ by such a $t$ does not change whether the Weyl sums vanish. Hence we may assume $c_k$ is invertible mod $b$, and the hypothesis gives 1-distribution of $Z_n(c)$. Taking $m=1$ yields the desired vanishing for $a$.

If $a_k\equiv 0\pmod b$, choose the smallest index $j$ such that $a_j\not\equiv 0\pmod b$. Consider the shifted sequence $X'_n=X_{n+j-1}$. This sequence is still $k$-distributed if and only if the original is, so it suffices to prove $k$-distribution for $X'$. In the shifted coordinates, the vector $a$ becomes a vector whose last component is nonzero. We are thus reduced to the previous case.

In all cases, every nontrivial Fourier coefficient vanishes, so by Weyl’s criterion $(X_n)$ is $k$-distributed.

## Conclusion

Part (a) follows directly from Weyl’s criterion and is unchanged.

In part (b), the missing step is the correct handling of admissible coefficient vectors in $(\mathbb Z/b\mathbb Z)^k$ and the need to use index shifting to ensure a nonzero last coefficient compatible with the hypothesis. Once these structural points are made explicit, the equivalence follows exactly as in the real case.
