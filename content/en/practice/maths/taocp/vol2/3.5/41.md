[Section 3.5: What Is a Random Sequence?](../)

**Exercise 41.** [*M21*] Lemma P2 shows the existence of a prediction test, but its proof relies on the existence of a suitable $k$ without explaining how we could find $k$ constructively from $A$. Show that any algorithm $A$ can be converted into an algorithm $A'$ with $T(A') \le T(A) + O(N)$ that predicts $B_N$ from $B_1, \ldots, B_{N-1}$ with probability at least $\frac{1}{2} + (P(A, S) - P(A, S_N))/N$ on any shift-symmetric $N$-source $S$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 14m01s

$$
\textbf{Let }p_j=P(A,H_j)\qquad(0\le j\le N),
$$

where $H_j$ is the hybrid source whose first $j$ bits come from $S$ and whose remaining $N-j$ bits are independent unbiased bits. Thus

$$
p_0=P(A,S_N),\qquad p_N=P(A,S).
$$

For each $k\in\{0,\ldots,N-1\}$, let $A_k^P$ be the prediction algorithm defined in Lemma P1. Exercise 40 gives

$$
P(A_k^P,S)-P(A_k^P,S_N)=p_{k+1}-p_k.
\tag{1}
$$

The proof of Lemma P2 shows that $A_k^P$ predicts $B_{k+1}$ from $B_1,\ldots,B_k$.

The difficulty is that Lemma P2 selects a favorable $k$ nonconstructively. We instead average over all $k$.

### Construction of the predictors $D_k$

Fix $k$.

Define a predictor $D_k$ for the last bit $B_N$ as follows. Given $B_1,\ldots,B_{N-1}$, it extracts the block

$$
(B_{N-k},\ldots,B_{N-1}),
$$

feeds this $k$-bit block to the algorithm $A_k^P$, and outputs the prediction produced by $A_k^P$.

Equivalently, $D_k$ applies exactly the same computation as $A_k^P$, but on the final $k$ observed bits rather than on the initial $k$ bits.

We now justify the crucial identity

$$
P(D_k,S)-P(D_k,S_N)=p_{k+1}-p_k.
\tag{2}
$$

### Why $D_k$ has the same advantage as $A_k^P$

Consider first the source $S$.

Since $S$ is shift-symmetric, every cyclic shift of

$$
(B_1,\ldots,B_N)
$$

has the same distribution. In particular, shifting by $N-k-1$ positions maps

$$
(B_1,\ldots,B_k,B_{k+1})
$$

to

$$
(B_{N-k},\ldots,B_{N-1},B_N).
$$

Hence the joint distributions

$$
(B_1,\ldots,B_k,B_{k+1})
$$

and

$$
(B_{N-k},\ldots,B_{N-1},B_N)
$$

are identical under $S$.

The success probability of a predictor depends only on the joint distribution of the observed bits and the bit to be predicted. Therefore $A_k^P$, when predicting $B_{k+1}$ from $B_1,\ldots,B_k$, has exactly the same success probability as $D_k$, when predicting $B_N$ from $B_{N-k},\ldots,B_{N-1}$. Thus

$$
P(D_k,S)=P(A_k^P,S).
\tag{3}
$$

Now consider the fully random source $S_N$. Its bits are independent unbiased bits, so every $(k+1)$-tuple of coordinates has the same distribution. Consequently,

$$
(B_1,\ldots,B_k,B_{k+1})
$$

and

$$
(B_{N-k},\ldots,B_{N-1},B_N)
$$

again have identical distributions, giving

$$
P(D_k,S_N)=P(A_k^P,S_N).
\tag{4}
$$

Subtracting (4) from (3) and using (1),

$$
P(D_k,S)-P(D_k,S_N)
=
P(A_k^P,S)-P(A_k^P,S_N)
=
p_{k+1}-p_k,
$$

which proves (2).

### Construction of $A'$

Algorithm $A'$ operates as follows.

1. Choose $k$ uniformly from $\{0,\ldots,N-1\}$.
2. Run $D_k$.
3. Output the prediction produced by $D_k$.

This construction is completely explicit from $A$. No search for a favorable $k$ is required.

### Running time

Each predictor $D_k$ executes exactly the computation of $A_k^P$. The construction of $A_k^P$ from $A$ in Lemma P1 requires only linear additional bookkeeping on an $N$-bit input. Therefore

$$
T(D_k)\le T(A)+O(N)
$$

uniformly in $k$.

Algorithm $A'$ performs one choice of $k$ and then runs a single predictor $D_k$. The cost of selecting $k$ is negligible compared with $O(N)$. Hence

$$
T(A')\le T(A)+O(N).
$$

### Success probability on the random source

Under $S_N$, the target bit $B_N$ is independent of all observed bits $B_1,\ldots,B_{N-1}$.

Therefore no predictor using only those observed bits can succeed with probability different from $1/2$. In particular,

$$
P(D_k,S_N)=\frac12
$$

for every $k$, and hence

$$
P(A',S_N)=\frac12.
\tag{5}
$$

### Averaging over $k$

Since $A'$ chooses $k$ uniformly,

$$
P(A',S)-P(A',S_N)
=
\frac1N
\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}
\bigl(P(D_k,S)-P(D_k,S_N)\bigr).
$$

Using (2),

$$
P(A',S)-P(A',S_N)
=
\frac1N
\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}(p_{k+1}-p_k).
$$

The sum telescopes:

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}(p_{k+1}-p_k)
=
p_N-p_0
=
P(A,S)-P(A,S_N).
$$

Therefore

$$
P(A',S)-P(A',S_N)
=
\frac{P(A,S)-P(A,S_N)}{N}.
$$

Combining this with (5),

$$
P(A',S)
=
\frac12+
\frac{P(A,S)-P(A,S_N)}{N}.
$$

Thus $A'$ predicts $B_N$ from $B_1,\ldots,B_{N-1}$ with probability at least

$$
\boxed{
\frac12+\frac{P(A,S)-P(A,S_N)}{N}
}
$$

and satisfies

$$
\boxed{
T(A')\le T(A)+O(N).
}
$$

This is exactly the required constructive version of Lemma P2. ∎
