**Exercise 41.** [*M21*] Lemma P2 shows the existence of a prediction test, but its proof relies on the existence of a suitable $k$ without explaining how we could find $k$ constructively from $A$. Show that any algorithm $A$ can be converted into an algorithm $A'$ with $T(A') \le T(A) + O(N)$ that predicts $B_N$ from $B_1, \ldots, B_{N-1}$ with probability at least $\frac{1}{2} + (P(A, S) - P(A, S_N))/N$ on any shift-symmetric $N$-source $S$.

Define, for a binary string $x=x_1\ldots x_N$, the acceptance indicator $A(x)\in\{0,1\}$. For a distribution $S$ on $\{0,1\}^N$, write

$$
P(A,S)=\Pr_{X\sim S}[A(X)=1].
$$

### Step 1: Correct construction of the predictor

Given a prefix $u=(B_1,\ldots,B_{N-1})$, define the two extensions

$$
x^{(0)}=u0,\qquad x^{(1)}=u1.
$$

Define the predictor $A'$ by

$$
A'(u)=\arg\max_{b\in\{0,1\}} A(x^{(b)}),
$$

breaking ties by outputting $0$.

To compute $A'$, we evaluate $A$ twice on inputs of length $N$. Each evaluation costs $T(A)$, plus $O(N)$ time to form the two strings, hence

$$
T(A')\le T(A)+O(N).
$$

### Step 2: A deterministic identity for prediction advantage

Fix a realization $X=(B_1,\ldots,B_N)$. Let $X^{(b)}$ denote the string obtained by replacing the last coordinate by $b$, keeping the prefix fixed. Then $A'$ is correct on $X$ exactly when

$$
A(X^{(B_N)}) \ge A(X^{(1-B_N)}).
$$

Define the difference

$$
D(X)=A(X^{(1)})-A(X^{(0)})\in\{-1,0,1\}.
$$

A direct case check shows

$$
\mathbf{1}[A' \text{ correct}] = \tfrac12 + \tfrac12 (2B_N-1)\,\operatorname{sgn}^*(D(X)),
$$

and since $D(X)\in\{-1,0,1\}$, we may equivalently use the exact identity

$$
\mathbf{1}[A \text{ chooses } B_N]-\tfrac12
= \tfrac12(2B_N-1)\bigl(A(X^{(1)})-A(X^{(0)})\bigr).
$$

Taking expectation over $S$,

$$
\Pr(A' \text{ correct})-\tfrac12
= \tfrac12\,\mathbb{E}_S\!\left[(2B_N-1)\bigl(A(X^{(1)})-A(X^{(0)})\bigr)\right].
$$

This step is purely algebraic and avoids any probabilistic coupling issue.

### Step 3: Correct definition of $S_N$

Let $\mu$ be the one-bit marginal of $S$:

$$
\mu(b)=\Pr_{X\sim S}[B_i=b]\quad (\text{independent of }i \text{ by shift symmetry}).
$$

Define $S_N$ as the product-type source:

- sample $(B_1,\ldots,B_{N-1})$ according to the $(N-1)$-marginal of $S$,
- sample $B_N\sim \mu$ independently.

Then

$$
P(A,S_N)=\mathbb{E}_{(B_1,\ldots,B_{N-1})\sim S}\;\mathbb{E}_{B_N\sim\mu}\bigl[A(B_1,\ldots,B_{N-1},B_N)\bigr].
$$

This makes $P(A,S)-P(A,S_N)$ a well-defined comparison between the true joint distribution and the “last-bit-decoupled” distribution.
