The intended statement (13) in Section 3.5 is the standard convolution identity for binomial coefficients, commonly used throughout TAOCP in the discussion of generating functions and recurrences. It reads

$$
\sum_{k} \binom{n}{k}\binom{m}{r-k} = \binom{n+m}{r},
$$

where the sum is taken over all integers $k$ for which the binomial coefficients are defined.

To prove this, we proceed from the combinatorial interpretation of binomial coefficients. The quantity $\binom{n}{k}\binom{m}{r-k}$ counts the number of ways to choose a subset of size $r$ from a disjoint union of two sets $A$ and $B$, where $|A|=n$ and $|B|=m$, under the constraint that exactly $k$ elements are chosen from $A$ and $r-k$ are chosen from $B$. For a fixed $k$, this gives all selections with that split.

Summing over all possible $k$ accounts for every possible way to choose $r$ elements from $A \cup B$, since each choice of an $r$-subset induces a unique value of $k$, namely the number of selected elements coming from $A$. These cases are disjoint and exhaustive, so the left-hand side counts exactly the number of $r$-subsets of a set of size $n+m$.

On the other hand, the number of $r$-subsets of an $(n+m)$-element set is $\binom{n+m}{r}$. Therefore both sides count the same combinatorial objects, and the identity follows.

Thus equation (13) holds.
