[Section 3.5: What Is a Random Sequence?](../)

**Exercise 6.** [*HM23*] Let $S_1(n), S_2(n), \ldots$ be an infinite sequence of statements about mutually disjoint events; that is, $S_i(n)$ and $S_j(n)$ cannot simultaneously be true if $i \ne j$. Assume that $\Pr(S_j(n))$ exists for each $j \ge 1$. Show that $\Pr(S_1(n))$ is true for some $j \ge 1) \ge \sum_{j \ge 1} \Pr(S_j(n))$, and give an example to show that equality need not hold.

Let $S_1(n), S_2(n), \ldots$ be pairwise disjoint events for a fixed $n$. Define

$$
A_m(n) = \bigcup_{j=1}^m S_j(n), \quad A(n) = \bigcup_{j\ge 1} S_j(n).
$$

Since the events are mutually disjoint, finite additivity of probability gives, for every $m \ge 1$,

$$
\Pr(A_m(n)) = \sum_{j=1}^m \Pr(S_j(n)).
$$

The sequence $A_m(n)$ is increasing in $m$, because $A_m(n) \subseteq A_{m+1}(n)$. By monotonicity of probability,

$$
\Pr(A(n)) \ge \Pr(A_m(n)) \quad \text{for every } m \ge 1.
$$

Combining this with the finite additivity identity yields

$$
\Pr(A(n)) \ge \sum_{j=1}^m \Pr(S_j(n)) \quad \text{for every } m \ge 1.
$$

Taking the supremum over all $m$ gives

$$
\Pr\!\left(\bigcup_{j\ge 1} S_j(n)\right) \ge \sup_{m \ge 1} \sum_{j=1}^m \Pr(S_j(n))
= \sum_{j\ge 1} \Pr(S_j(n)),
$$

where the last equality is the definition of the infinite sum of nonnegative terms. This proves the required inequality.

To show that equality need not hold, we construct a finitely additive probability measure that is not countably additive. Let the sample space be $\mathbb{N}$, and define a probability measure $P$ such that $P(\{j\}) = 0$ for every $j \in \mathbb{N}$, but $P(\mathbb{N}) = 1$. Such a measure can be obtained, for example, from a Banach limit or an asymptotic density extension.

Now define $S_j(n) = \{j\}$. These events are pairwise disjoint, and each satisfies

$$
\Pr(S_j(n)) = 0.
$$

Hence

$$
\sum_{j\ge 1} \Pr(S_j(n)) = 0.
$$

However,

$$
\Pr\!\left(\bigcup_{j\ge 1} S_j(n)\right) = \Pr(\mathbb{N}) = 1.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\Pr\!\left(\bigcup_{j\ge 1} S_j(n)\right) > \sum_{j\ge 1} \Pr(S_j(n)),
$$

showing that equality need not hold.
