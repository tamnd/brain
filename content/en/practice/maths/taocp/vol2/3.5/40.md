## Setup

Let $\langle U_n \rangle$ be a $k$-distributed $[0,1)$ sequence in the sense of Definition B. Let $f(x_1,\ldots,x_k)$ be a Riemann-integrable function on the unit cube $[0,1]^k$.

Lemma P1 asserts that if the convergence formula

$$
\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}\sum_{0\le j<n} f(U_j,\ldots,U_{j+k-1})
=
\int_0^1\cdots\int_0^1 f(x_1,\ldots,x_k)\,dx_1\cdots dx_k
$$

holds for all characteristic functions of $k$-dimensional intervals

$$
I = [u_1,v_1)\times\cdots\times[u_k,v_k),
$$

then it holds for all Riemann-integrable $f$.

It remains to justify the passage from indicator functions of such intervals to arbitrary Riemann-integrable functions.

## Solution

Let $I=[u_1,v_1)\times\cdots\times[u_k,v_k)$ and let $\mathbf{1}_I$ denote its characteristic function. For each $j\ge 0$, the term $\mathbf{1}_I(U_j,\ldots,U_{j+k-1})$ equals $1$ exactly when

$$
u_\ell \le U_{j+\ell-1} < v_\ell \quad (1\le \ell \le k),
$$

and equals $0$ otherwise.

By Definition B,

$$
\Pr(u_1 \le U_n < v_1,\ldots,u_k \le U_{n+k-1} < v_k)
= \prod_{\ell=1}^k (v_\ell-u_\ell).
$$

Thus the defining frequency condition for $k$-distributed sequences yields

$$
\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}\sum_{0\le j<n}\mathbf{1}_I(U_j,\ldots,U_{j+k-1})
=
\prod_{\ell=1}^k (v_\ell-u_\ell).
$$

The right-hand side equals the $k$-dimensional volume of $I$, that is,

$$
\int_0^1\cdots\int_0^1 \mathbf{1}_I(x_1,\ldots,x_k)\,dx_1\cdots dx_k.
$$

Let $S$ be a finite disjoint union of such intervals $I_m$. Define

$$
\mathbf{1}_S = \sum_m \mathbf{1}_{I_m}.
$$

For each fixed $n$, linearity of finite sums gives

$$
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{0\le j<n}\mathbf{1}_S(U_j,\ldots,U_{j+k-1})
=
\sum_m \frac{1}{n}\sum_{0\le j<n}\mathbf{1}_{I_m}(U_j,\ldots,U_{j+k-1}).
$$

Taking limits termwise yields

$$
\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}\sum_{0\le j<n}\mathbf{1}_S(U_j,\ldots,U_{j+k-1})
=
\sum_m \prod_{\ell=1}^k (v_{\ell,m}-u_{\ell,m}),
$$

which equals

$$
\int_0^1\cdots\int_0^1 \mathbf{1}_S(x_1,\ldots,x_k)\,dx_1\cdots dx_k.
$$

Let $f$ be Riemann-integrable on $[0,1]^k$. For every $\varepsilon>0$, there exist step functions $g$ and $h$, each a finite linear combination of characteristic functions of such intervals, satisfying

$$
g(x) \le f(x) \le h(x), \qquad
\int (h-g) < \varepsilon.
$$

Applying the already established result to $g$ and $h$ gives

$$
\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}\sum_{0\le j<n} g(U_j,\ldots,U_{j+k-1})
=
\int g,
$$

$$
\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}\sum_{0\le j<n} h(U_j,\ldots,U_{j+k-1})
=
\int h.
$$

For each $n$, the inequalities $g\le f\le h$ imply

$$
\frac{1}{n}\sum g(U_j,\ldots,U_{j+k-1})
\le
\frac{1}{n}\sum f(U_j,\ldots,U_{j+k-1})
\le
\frac{1}{n}\sum h(U_j,\ldots,U_{j+k-1}).
$$

Taking limsup and liminf gives

$$
\int g \le \underline{L} \le \overline{L} \le \int h,
$$

where

$$
\underline{L}=\liminf_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}\sum f(U_j,\ldots,U_{j+k-1}),\quad
\overline{L}=\limsup_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}\sum f(U_j,\ldots,U_{j+k-1}).
$$

Hence

$$
0 \le \overline{L}-\underline{L} \le \int h - \int g < \varepsilon.
$$

Since $\varepsilon$ is arbitrary, $\overline{L}=\underline{L}$. Therefore the limit exists.

Let $L$ denote this limit. From the inequalities above,

$$
\int g \le L \le \int h.
$$

Letting $\varepsilon \to 0$ forces $L=\int f$, since step functions approximate $f$ in the Riemann sense.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification

The argument first establishes equality of time averages and integrals for characteristic functions of $k$-dimensional half-open intervals using Definition B directly. Linearity extends the result to finite disjoint unions without introducing overlap terms.

The Riemann integrability hypothesis provides step-function bounds $g\le f\le h$ with arbitrarily small integral gap. The sandwich inequalities propagate through finite sums termwise, preserving order. The limsup and liminf bounds follow from monotonicity of limits under inequalities. The equality of limsup and liminf follows from the vanishing integral gap, forcing convergence of Cesàro averages.

## Notes

The essential structure is the extension of an equality on a generating algebra of sets (finite unions of axis-aligned intervals) to its closure under Riemann approximation. The role of $k$-distribution is precisely to identify empirical frequencies of cylinder sets with Lebesgue measure on $[0,1]^k$, after which the convergence statement becomes an ergodic-type extension to Riemann-integrable observables.
