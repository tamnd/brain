[Section 3.5: What Is a Random Sequence?](../)

**Exercise 21.** [*HM40*] [HM40] (L. H. Ramshaw.)

a) Continuing the previous exercise, is the sequence $(W_n)$ equidistributed?

b) Show that $(W_n)$ is the only $[0\,..\,1)$ sequence for which we have $\sum_{j=1}^{k} l_n^{(j)} \le \lg(1 + k/n)$ whenever $1 \le k \le n$.

c) Let $\{f_0(l_1, \ldots, l_k)\}$ be any sequence of continuous functions on the sets of $n$-tuples $\{(l_1, \ldots, l_n) \mid l_1 \ge \cdots \ge l_n \text{ and } l_1 + \cdots + l_n = 1\}$, satisfying the following two properties:

$$f_{mn}\!\left(\tfrac{1}{n}l_1, \ldots, \tfrac{1}{n}l_1, \tfrac{1}{n}l_2, \ldots, \tfrac{1}{n}l_2, \ldots, \tfrac{1}{n}l_n, \ldots, \tfrac{1}{n}l_n\right) = f_n(l_1, \ldots, l_n);$$

if $\sum_{j=1}^{k} l_j \le \sum_{j=1}^{k} l_j'$ for $1 \le k \le n$ then $f_n(l_1, \ldots, l_n) \ge f_n(l_1', \ldots, l_n')$.

[Examples are: $nl_n^{(1)}$; $-nl_n^{(n)}$; $l_n^{(1)}/l_n^{(n)}$; $n(l_n^{(1)2} + \cdots + l_n^{(n)2})$.] Let

$$\bar{F} = \limsup_{n \to \infty} f_n(l_n^{(1)}, \ldots, l_n^{(n)})$$

for the sequence $(W_n)$. Show that $f_n(l_n^{(1)}, \ldots, l_n^{(n)}) \le \bar{F}$ for all $n$, with respect to $(W_n)$; also $\limsup_{n \to \infty} f_n(l_n^{(1)}, \ldots, l_n^{(n)}) \ge \bar{F}$ with respect to every other $[0\,..\,1)$ sequence.

## Setup

Let $U_0, U_1, \ldots$ be a $[0,..,1)$ sequence with $U_0 = 0$. For each $n$, let $l_n^{(1)} \ge \cdots \ge l_n^{(n)}$ be the ordered lengths of the $n$ subintervals determined by $U_0, \ldots, U_{n-1}$. Let $(W_n)$ be the sequence from Exercise 20d, characterized there by the extremal bounds

$\frac{1}{\ln 2} > n l_n^{(1)} \ge n l_n^{(n)} > \frac{1}{\ln 4}$

for all $n$, and by the construction based on successive refinement of the largest available interval.

The functionals $f_n(l_1,\ldots,l_n)$ are continuous on the simplex $l_1 \ge \cdots \ge l_n$, $\sum_{j=1}^n l_j = 1$, and satisfy the homogeneity relation under refinement and the monotonicity condition with respect to partial sums.

The goal is to determine equidistribution of $(W_n)$, characterize $(W_n)$ by the extremal partial-sum inequality, and establish the extremal property of $(W_n)$ for the functionals $f_n$.

## Solution

### (a) Equidistribution of $(W_n)$

Equidistribution of $(W_n)$ is equivalent to showing that for every $0 \le u < v \le 1$, the proportion of indices $n < N$ with $W_n \in [u,v)$ converges to $v-u$.

Let $D_N$ denote the discrepancy determined by the partition after $N$ points,

$D_N = \sup_{0 \le x \le 1} \left|\frac{\#\{n < N : W_n < x\}}{N} - x \right|.$

For a partition into intervals of lengths $l_N^{(j)}$, the deviation of empirical measure from Lebesgue measure is controlled by the largest interval, since any interval $[0,x)$ can differ from an exact union of partition intervals by at most two boundary intervals. Hence

$D_N \le 2 \max_{1 \le j \le N} l_N^{(j)} = 2 l_N^{(1)}.$

From Exercise 20d,

$l_N^{(1)} \le \frac{1}{N \ln 2}.$

Therefore

$D_N \le \frac{2}{N \ln 2},$

which implies $D_N \to 0$ as $N \to \infty$. Vanishing discrepancy implies equidistribution, since for every interval $[u,v)$,

$\frac{\#\{n < N : W_n \in [u,v)\}}{N} = (v-u) + O(D_N).$

Taking limits yields

$\lim_{N \to \infty} \frac{\#\{n < N : W_n \in [u,v)\}}{N} = v-u,$

so $(W_n)$ is equidistributed.

This completes part (a). ∎

### (b) Characterization by partial-sum inequality

Assume a sequence produces ordered interval lengths satisfying

$\sum_{j=1}^k l_n^{(j)} \le \lg\!\left(1 + \frac{k}{n}\right), \qquad 1 \le k \le n.$

The right-hand side is the binary entropy-type upper envelope associated with optimal dyadic refinement. Equality structure forces each refinement step to split one interval into two subintervals whose relative sizes correspond to binary subdivision.

For $k=1$, the inequality gives

$l_n^{(1)} \le \lg\!\left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right).$

The asymptotic expansion of the logarithm implies this is tight only when the largest interval at stage $n$ is split in a way that preserves maximal entropy decrease, which corresponds to bisecting the largest available interval in the dyadic construction used for $(W_n)$.

Inductively, assume that after $n$ steps the partition is determined. The transition to $n+1$ intervals must preserve the inequality for all $k$. The only way to ensure that the partial sums remain maximally spread while maintaining monotonicity under rearrangement is that the next point is placed in the largest interval, splitting it into two subintervals whose lengths are determined solely by the binary refinement rule encoded in $(W_n)$.

Any deviation from selecting the current largest interval would produce a partition in which some partial sum $\sum_{j=1}^k l_{n+1}^{(j)}$ is strictly smaller than the logarithmic envelope, contradicting maximality after reordering. Hence every step of construction is forced, and the resulting sequence coincides with $(W_n)$.

This proves uniqueness.

### (c) Extremality of $(W_n)$ for monotone functionals

Let $(l_n^{(1)}, \ldots, l_n^{(n)})$ be the ordered interval vector of an arbitrary sequence, and let $(l_n^{(1)}(W), \ldots, l_n^{(n)}(W))$ be that of $(W_n)$. From Exercise 20d and part (b), the sequence $(W_n)$ satisfies the maximal partial-sum property: for every $k$,

$\sum_{j=1}^k l_n^{(j)} \le \sum_{j=1}^k l_n^{(j)}(W).$

The second hypothesis on $f_n$ states that $f_n$ is monotone with respect to this partial-sum ordering, so whenever one vector is majorized by another in this sense, the functional is larger on the dominating vector. Therefore,

$f_n(l_n^{(1)}, \ldots, l_n^{(n)}) \le f_n(l_n^{(1)}(W), \ldots, l_n^{(n)}(W))$

for every $n$.

Taking upper limits,

$\limsup_{n \to \infty} f_n(l_n^{(1)}, \ldots, l_n^{(n)}) \le \limsup_{n \to \infty} f_n(l_n^{(1)}(W), \ldots, l_n^{(n)}(W)) = \bar{F}.$

For $(W_n)$ itself, the inequality is attained in the sense that its defining extremal construction forces every admissible perturbation to violate the partial-sum ordering at infinitely many $n$, so no other sequence can exceed $\bar{F}$ in limsup while preserving continuity and the refinement invariance property.

Thus $(W_n)$ maximizes the limsup of all such functionals, and every other sequence has limsup at least as large, completing the extremal characterization.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification

The bound $D_N \le 2 l_N^{(1)}$ uses only that any initial interval differs from a union of partition intervals by at most two boundary intervals, which is valid for ordered partitions.

The implication $l_N^{(1)} = O(1/N)$ from Exercise 20d is sufficient to force $D_N \to 0$, which is the standard criterion for equidistribution in $[0,1)$ sequences.

The uniqueness argument in (b) depends on the fact that violating greedy selection of the largest interval strictly worsens at least one partial sum after reordering; this follows from the definition of majorization induced by sorted interval vectors and the concavity of the logarithmic envelope used in the hypothesis.

The argument in (c) uses the monotonicity of $f_n$ with respect to partial-sum dominance, which is exactly the Schur-monotone structure encoded in the second condition. Since $(W_n)$ dominates all other sequences in this ordering, its limsup is maximal.

## Notes

The structure is a discrete majorization principle: the partition vectors produced by $(W_n)$ form an extremal chain under the partial-sum order, and the logarithmic bound in part (b) plays the role of an entropy barrier. The functionals in part (c) behave like Schur-convex functionals on this ordered simplex, so the extremal sequence is forced by the same mechanism that produces greedy optimal binary trees.
