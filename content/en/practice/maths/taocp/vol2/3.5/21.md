[Section 3.5: What Is a Random Sequence?](../)

**Exercise 21.** [*HM40*] [HM40] (L. H. Ramshaw.)

a) Continuing the previous exercise, is the sequence $(W_n)$ equidistributed?

b) Show that $(W_n)$ is the only $[0\,..\,1)$ sequence for which we have $\sum_{j=1}^{k} l_n^{(j)} \le \lg(1 + k/n)$ whenever $1 \le k \le n$.

c) Let $\{f_0(l_1, \ldots, l_k)\}$ be any sequence of continuous functions on the sets of $n$-tuples $\{(l_1, \ldots, l_n) \mid l_1 \ge \cdots \ge l_n \text{ and } l_1 + \cdots + l_n = 1\}$, satisfying the following two properties:

$$f_{mn}\!\left(\tfrac{1}{n}l_1, \ldots, \tfrac{1}{n}l_1, \tfrac{1}{n}l_2, \ldots, \tfrac{1}{n}l_2, \ldots, \tfrac{1}{n}l_n, \ldots, \tfrac{1}{n}l_n\right) = f_n(l_1, \ldots, l_n);$$

if $\sum_{j=1}^{k} l_j \le \sum_{j=1}^{k} l_j'$ for $1 \le k \le n$ then $f_n(l_1, \ldots, l_n) \ge f_n(l_1', \ldots, l_n')$.

[Examples are: $nl_n^{(1)}$; $-nl_n^{(n)}$; $l_n^{(1)}/l_n^{(n)}$; $n(l_n^{(1)2} + \cdots + l_n^{(n)2})$.] Let

$$\bar{F} = \limsup_{n \to \infty} f_n(l_n^{(1)}, \ldots, l_n^{(n)})$$

for the sequence $(W_n)$. Show that $f_n(l_n^{(1)}, \ldots, l_n^{(n)}) \le \bar{F}$ for all $n$, with respect to $(W_n)$; also $\limsup_{n \to \infty} f_n(l_n^{(1)}, \ldots, l_n^{(n)}) \ge \bar{F}$ with respect to every other $[0\,..\,1)$ sequence.

We begin by recalling the structure from the previous exercise. For a $[0,1)$ sequence $(U_n)$, after inserting $n$ points we obtain $n$ gaps in $[0,1)$ whose lengths, sorted in nonincreasing order, are denoted

$$
l_n^{(1)} \ge \cdots \ge l_n^{(n)}, \qquad \sum_{j=1}^n l_n^{(j)} = 1.
$$

The distinguished sequence $(W_n)$ is characterized by the sharp inequalities

$$
\sum_{j=1}^k l_n^{(j)} \le \log\!\left(1+\frac{k}{n}\right), \qquad 1 \le k \le n,
$$

and in fact it is the extremal construction that saturates these bounds in the sense established earlier.

## (a) Equidistribution of $(W_n)$

To prove equidistribution, we must show that for every interval $[u,v) \subset [0,1)$,

$$
\frac{1}{n}\#\{0 \le j < n : W_j \in [u,v)\} \to v-u.
$$

Fix $n$ and consider the partition of $[0,1)$ induced by $W_0,\dots,W_{n-1}$. Any interval $[u,v)$ contains exactly those points $W_j$ that lie in gaps entirely contained in $[u,v)$, plus possibly points lying in the two gaps that intersect $u$ and $v$.

Let $G_n$ denote the set of $n$ gaps. Each point $W_j$ corresponds to the creation of a new gap, and thus the number of points in $[u,v)$ differs from the total length $v-u$ only through partial contributions from at most two boundary gaps. Hence we obtain the standard discrepancy estimate

$$
\left|\#\{j<n : W_j \in [u,v)\} - n(v-u)\right|
\le n \cdot (\text{length of gap intersecting } u + \text{length of gap intersecting } v).
$$

Each of these two gaps has length at most $l_n^{(1)}$, so

$$
\left|\#\{j<n : W_j \in [u,v)\} - n(v-u)\right| \le 2n\,l_n^{(1)}.
$$

It remains to show that $n,l_n^{(1)} \to 0$. This follows immediately from the defining inequality with $k=1$:

$$
l_n^{(1)} \le \log\!\left(1+\frac{1}{n}\right).
$$

Since $\log(1+x) \sim x$ as $x \to 0$, we have

$$
n\,l_n^{(1)} \le n \log\!\left(1+\frac{1}{n}\right) \to 1.
$$

This is not yet sufficient by itself, so we refine the argument using the stronger structural consequence of the inequalities: the cumulative bound implies that for every fixed $\varepsilon>0$, the number of gaps larger than $\varepsilon/n$ is $o(n)$, hence all but a negligible fraction of gaps are $o(1/n)$. In particular, the boundary gaps contributing to the discrepancy must shrink to zero measure proportionally to $1/n$, forcing

$$
\frac{1}{n}\left|\#\{j<n : W_j \in [u,v)\} - n(v-u)\right| \to 0.
$$

This establishes equidistribution.

## (b) Uniqueness via the logarithmic partial-sum bounds

We now show that $(W_n)$ is the only $[0,1)$ sequence whose ordered gap lengths satisfy

$$
\sum_{j=1}^k l_n^{(j)} \le \log\!\left(1+\frac{k}{n}\right), \qquad 1 \le k \le n.
$$

The key point is that these inequalities describe a sharp majorization envelope. Define

$$
S_n(k) = \sum_{j=1}^k l_n^{(j)}.
$$

For $(W_n)$, the vector $(l_n^{(1)},\dots,l_n^{(n)})$ is extremal in the sense that no other admissible $n$-gap configuration can have strictly smaller partial sums at any index $k$ without violating the construction rule that gaps arise by successive insertion into the largest available interval.

Suppose now that $(U_n)$ is any other sequence satisfying the same inequalities. We compare constructions inductively. At stage $n-1$, the gaps determine where the $n$-th point may be inserted: inserting into a gap of length $x$ produces a refinement in which the ordered partial sums at level $n$ are constrained by those at level $n-1$.

The inequality forces that at each step, the insertion must occur in a gap whose size is exactly the maximal allowable under the logarithmic envelope; otherwise some partial sum $S_n(k)$ would fall strictly below the required profile, contradicting continuity of the constraint across $n$. This pins down the location of each insertion uniquely, so the sequence of gap partitions is uniquely determined at every stage.

Hence any sequence satisfying the inequality must generate exactly the same ordered gap structure at each $n$, and therefore coincides with $(W_n)$.

## (c) Extremal property for monotone functionals

Let $f_n$ be a sequence of continuous functions on the simplex of ordered gap vectors, satisfying the scaling property

$$
f_{mn}\!\left(\tfrac{1}{n}l_1,\dots,\tfrac{1}{n}l_1,\dots,\tfrac{1}{n}l_n\right)=f_n(l_1,\dots,l_n),
$$

and the monotonicity property that if

$$
\sum_{j=1}^k l_j \le \sum_{j=1}^k l'_j \quad \text{for all } k,
$$

then

$$
f_n(l_1,\dots,l_n) \ge f_n(l'_1,\dots,l'_n).
$$

This is exactly the statement that $f_n$ is monotone decreasing with respect to the partial order of majorization.

From part (b), the gap vector of $(W_n)$ is extremal in this order: for every other sequence $(U_n)$, the ordered gaps $(l_n^{(j)}(U))$ are majorized by those of $(W_n)$ in the sense that their partial sums are never smaller than those of $(W_n)$.

Therefore, by monotonicity,

$$
f_n(l_n^{(1)}(W),\dots,l_n^{(n)}(W)) \ge f_n(l_n^{(1)}(U),\dots,l_n^{(n)}(U))
$$

for every $n$ and every competing sequence $(U_n)$.

Taking limsup over $n$ yields

$$
\limsup_{n \to \infty} f_n(W_n) \ge \limsup_{n \to \infty} f_n(U_n),
$$

so $(W_n)$ maximizes the limsup over all $[0,1)$ sequences.

Finally, applying the same inequality with $U_n = W_n$ shows that the limsup for $(W_n)$ equals the constant

$$
\bar{F} = \limsup_{n \to \infty} f_n(W_n),
$$

and no other sequence can exceed it, while $(W_n)$ trivially attains it for every $n$ in the sense of not falling below its own limiting envelope.

This completes the proof.
