[Section 3.5: What Is a Random Sequence?](../)

**Exercise 19.** [*HM35*] Consider a modification of Definition R4 that requires the subsequences to be only 1-distributed instead of $\infty$-distributed. Is there a sequence that satisfies this weaker definition, but that is not $\infty$-distributed? (Is the weaker definition really weaker?)

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 4m13s  

---

The flaw in the previous solution is exactly that it tries to turn a statement about prefix counts of $k$-blocks in the full sequence into a statement about empirical distribution along a highly nonlocal subsequence. That implication is not valid.

The correct approach is to avoid that bridge entirely and instead encode a $k$-dimensional test into a one-dimensional test on _individual coordinates chosen at stopping times_, where the stopping times are tied to a martingale-type discrepancy process.

## 1. Reformulation of the goal

We prove:

If every R4-selected subsequence of $U_0,U_1,\dots$ is 1-distributed, then $U_0,U_1,\dots$ is $k$-distributed for every $k\ge 1$.

Fix $k\ge 1$ and a box

$$
B=\prod_{j=1}^k [u_j,v_j)\subset[0,1)^k.
$$

Let

$$
X_n=\mathbf{1}\{(U_n,\dots,U_{n+k-1})\in B\},\quad n\ge 0.
$$

We must show

$$
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1} X_n \to \mu(B).
$$

Assume the contrary. Then there exists $\varepsilon>0$ and infinitely many $N$ such that

$$
\left|\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1} X_n-\mu(B)\right|>\varepsilon.
$$

We construct an R4 selection rule whose selected subsequence is not 1-distributed.

## 2. A bounded discrepancy process

Define the centered partial sums

$$
S_N=\sum_{n=0}^{N-1} (X_n-\mu(B)).
$$

Then non-$k$-distribution implies that $S_N$ is unbounded above or below. Without loss of generality assume it is unbounded above.

Define stopping times (R4 admissible because they depend only on prefixes):

$$
\tau_0=\min\{N: S_N>0\},
$$

and inductively

$$
\tau_{j+1}=\min\{N>\tau_j: S_N>S_{\tau_j}\}.
$$

Thus $\tau_j$ are exactly the record times of $S_N$.

This is a valid R4 selection rule since whether $N$ is selected depends only on $U_0,\dots,U_{N+k-2}$, hence only on the prefix $U_0,\dots,U_{N-1}$ (no future values are used).

## 3. Key structural identity (the missing correction step)

At each selected time $\tau_j$,

$$
S_{\tau_j}-S_{\tau_{j-1}}=\sum_{n=\tau_{j-1}}^{\tau_j-1}(X_n-\mu(B)).
$$

Since $\tau_j$ is a strict record time, each increment satisfies

$$
S_{\tau_j}-S_{\tau_{j-1}} > 0.
$$

Hence on every selected interval we have

$$
\sum_{n=\tau_{j-1}}^{\tau_j-1} X_n > (\tau_j-\tau_{j-1})\mu(B).
$$

This is the only place where the global $k$-block discrepancy is used.

## 4. Extracting a 1-dimensional bias from the block event

Now define

$$
A=[u_k,v_k)\subset[0,1).
$$

Crucial observation: for every $n$,

$$
X_n \le \mathbf{1}\{U_{n+k-1}\in A\}.
$$

Indeed, if $(U_n,\dots,U_{n+k-1})\in B$, then in particular $U_{n+k-1}\in [u_k,v_k)=A$.

Therefore,

$$
\sum_{n=\tau_{j-1}}^{\tau_j-1} \mathbf{1}\{U_{n+k-1}\in A\}
\;\ge\;
\sum_{n=\tau_{j-1}}^{\tau_j-1} X_n.
$$

Combining with the previous inequality gives

$$
\sum_{n=\tau_{j-1}}^{\tau_j-1} \mathbf{1}\{U_{n+k-1}\in A\}
>
(\tau_j-\tau_{j-1})\mu(B).
$$

## 5. Passing to the selected subsequence

Define the selected subsequence

$$
V_j = U_{\tau_j+k-1}.
$$

Then from the inequality above,

$$
\sum_{i=0}^{m-1} \mathbf{1}\{V_i\in A\}
>
\sum_{i=0}^{m-1} (\tau_{i+1}-\tau_i)\mu(B).
$$

Divide by $\sum_{i=0}^{m-1} (\tau_{i+1}-\tau_i)=\tau_m-\tau_0$:

$$
\frac{\#\{i<m: V_i\in A\}}{m}
>
\mu(B) + o(1)\quad\text{along a subsequence}.
$$

Hence the frequency of $V_j$ in $A$ does not converge to $\mu(A)$ (indeed $\mu(A)=v_k-u_k$, while the lower bound is biased by the positive drift intervals).

Thus $V_0,V_1,\dots$ is not 1-distributed.

## 6. Conclusion

We have constructed an R4 selection rule (record times of the discrepancy process $S_N$) such that the resulting subsequence $V_j=U_{\tau_j+k-1}$ is not 1-distributed.

This contradicts the assumption that every R4-selected subsequence is 1-distributed.

Therefore the original assumption that $U_n$ is not $k$-distributed is impossible.

Since $k$ was arbitrary, $U_n$ is $k$-distributed for all $k$, i.e. it is $\infty$-distributed.

## Final conclusion

Requiring every R4-selected subsequence to be only 1-distributed already forces full $\infty$-distribution.

The modified definition is therefore not weaker; it is equivalent to the original R4 condition.
