[Section 3.5: What Is a Random Sequence?](../)

**Exercise 19.** [*HM35*] Consider a modification of Definition R4 that requires the subsequences to be only 1-distributed instead of $\infty$-distributed. Is there a sequence that satisfies this weaker definition, but that is not $\infty$-distributed? (Is the weaker definition really weaker?)

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m06s  

---

## Setup

Let $U_0, U_1, \ldots$ be an infinite sequence of real numbers in $[0,1)$. Definition R4 in Section 3.5 (with the modification) requires that _every subsequence formed by selecting one term from each block of length $k$_ is 1-distributed, rather than $\infty$-distributed. Explicitly, for any positive integer $k$ and integers $0 \le j < k$, the subsequence

$U_j, U_{j+k}, U_{j+2k}, \ldots$

satisfies

$\Pr(a \le U_{j+mk} < b) = b - a$

for all $0 \le a < b \le 1$, where $m = 0,1,2,\ldots$. The question asks whether this weaker property implies that the original sequence $U_0, U_1, \ldots$ is $\infty$-distributed, or equivalently, whether there exists a sequence satisfying this weaker definition that fails to be $2$-distributed (or higher).

We must produce either a sequence satisfying the modified R4 but not $\infty$-distributed, or prove that no such sequence exists.

## Solution

We construct a counterexample. Let $V_0, V_1, V_2, \ldots$ be any 1-distributed sequence. Define the sequence $U_n$ by interleaving $V_n$ with its complement:

$$
U_0 = V_0, \quad U_1 = 1 - V_0, \quad U_2 = V_1, \quad U_3 = 1 - V_1, \quad \ldots
$$

Formally, for $n \ge 0$, write $n = 2m$ or $n = 2m+1$:

$$
U_{2m} = V_m, \quad U_{2m+1} = 1 - V_m.
$$

We check that this sequence satisfies the modified R4. Consider the subsequences obtained by taking every $2$nd term, starting at $j=0$ and $j=1$. Then

$$
\begin{aligned}
U_0, U_2, U_4, \ldots &= V_0, V_1, V_2, \ldots, \\
U_1, U_3, U_5, \ldots &= 1 - V_0, 1 - V_1, 1 - V_2, \ldots.
\end{aligned}
$$

Since $V_n$ is 1-distributed, the subsequence $V_0, V_1, \ldots$ is 1-distributed. Likewise, $1 - V_0, 1 - V_1, \ldots$ is 1-distributed because for any interval $[a,b)$,

$$
\Pr(a \le 1 - V_n < b) = \Pr(1-b < V_n \le 1 - a) = (1-a) - (1-b) = b - a.
$$

Thus the two subsequences formed by taking every second term are 1-distributed. More generally, any subsequence formed by taking one term from each block of length $k=2$ is either $V_n$ or $1 - V_n$, both of which are 1-distributed. Therefore the sequence $U_n$ satisfies the modified R4 for $k=2$.

However, $U_n$ is not 2-distributed. Consider the pair $(U_0, U_1) = (V_0, 1 - V_0)$. The probability that $U_0 < 1/2$ and $U_1 < 1/2$ is zero, because if $V_0 < 1/2$, then $1 - V_0 > 1/2$. Thus

$$
\Pr(U_0 < 1/2, U_1 < 1/2) = 0 \neq \frac{1}{4}.
$$

Hence the sequence fails to be 2-distributed, and therefore is not $\infty$-distributed.

This construction generalizes to any $k \ge 2$ by interleaving $k$ sequences $V_n^{(0)}, \ldots, V_n^{(k-1)}$ with $V_n^{(0)}$ 1-distributed and the others chosen to enforce deterministic dependencies between consecutive $k$-tuples. Each subsequence formed by picking one element from each block remains 1-distributed, but the original sequence fails to satisfy 2-distribution or higher.

## Verification

Let $V_n$ be any 1-distributed sequence. Define $U_{2n} = V_n$, $U_{2n+1} = 1 - V_n$.

1. The subsequences $U_0, U_2, \ldots$ and $U_1, U_3, \ldots$ are both 1-distributed, verified above.
2. The pair distribution fails: $\Pr(U_{2n} < 1/2, U_{2n+1} < 1/2) = 0 \neq 1/4$.
3. Higher-order tuples $(U_0, U_1, U_2)$ etc. inherit deterministic dependence; thus the sequence cannot be 2-distributed.

Hence the example satisfies the modified R4 but is not $\infty$-distributed.

## Notes

This shows that the weaker definition of R4 is strictly weaker. It is sufficient to guarantee 1-distribution along subsequences but does not impose independence or higher-order $k$-distribution. The interleaving technique can be generalized to construct sequences that are $k$-1-distributed along blocks but not $k$-distributed, providing a method for systematically separating local 1-distribution from global $k$-distribution.

This completes the proof.

∎

$$
\boxed{\text{The modified R4 is strictly weaker; counterexamples exist.}}
$$
