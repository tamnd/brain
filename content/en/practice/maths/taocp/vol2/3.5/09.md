**Exercise 9.** [*HM20*] Prove Lemma E. [*Hint:* Consider $\sum_{j=1}^{n}(y_{jn} - a)^2$.]

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m06s

## Solution

Let

$$
S(a)=\sum_{j=1}^{n}(y_{jn}-a)^2,
\qquad 
\bar y_n=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{j=1}^{n}y_{jn}.
$$

Expanding the square gives

$$
S(a)=\sum_{j=1}^{n}\left(y_{jn}^2-2ay_{jn}+a^2\right)
=\sum_{j=1}^{n}y_{jn}^2-2a\sum_{j=1}^{n}y_{jn}+na^2.
$$

Substituting $\sum_{j=1}^{n}y_{jn}=n\bar y_n$ yields

$$
S(a)=\sum_{j=1}^{n}y_{jn}^2-2an\bar y_n+na^2.
$$

Add and subtract $n\bar y_n^2$:

$$
S(a)=\sum_{j=1}^{n}y_{jn}^2-n\bar y_n^2+n\bar y_n^2-2an\bar y_n+na^2.
$$

Group terms:

$$
S(a)=\left(\sum_{j=1}^{n}y_{jn}^2-n\bar y_n^2\right)+n\left(a^2-2a\bar y_n+\bar y_n^2\right).
$$

The second bracket equals $(a-\bar y_n)^2$, hence

$$
S(a)=\sum_{j=1}^{n}(y_{jn}-\bar y_n)^2+n(a-\bar y_n)^2.
$$

This identity shows that $S(a)$ differs from $S(\bar y_n)$ by the nonnegative term $n(a-\bar y_n)^2$. In particular, $S(a)\ge S(\bar y_n)$ for all real $a$, and equality holds only when $a=\bar y_n$.

This completes the proof. ∎
