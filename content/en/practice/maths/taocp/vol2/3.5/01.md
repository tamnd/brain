[Section 3.5: What Is a Random Sequence?](../)

**Exercise 1.** [*10*] Can a periodic sequence be equidistributed?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m06s  

---

Yes. Let a sequence have period $m$, and let its period consist of the values $a_0,a_1,\ldots,a_{m-1}$. For any interval $[u,v)$, the number of terms of one period that lie in this interval is some integer $r=r(u,v)$. As $n\to\infty$, complete periods dominate the count, hence

$$
\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{\nu(n)}{n}=\frac{r}{m}.
$$

Therefore a periodic sequence is equidistributed if and only if

$$
\frac{r(u,v)}{m}=v-u
$$

for every interval $[u,v)$.

This is impossible for a nontrivial periodic sequence, since $\frac{r(u,v)}{m}$ can assume only the finitely many values $0,\frac1m,\ldots,\frac{m}{m}$, while $v-u$ ranges over all real numbers in $[0,1]$. For example, if $0<v-u<1/m$, then $v-u$ cannot equal $r/m$ for any integer $r$. Hence no periodic sequence can satisfy equation (2) for all $u$ and $v$. Therefore a periodic sequence cannot be equidistributed.

$$
\boxed{\text{No periodic sequence is equidistributed.}}
$$
