[Section 3.5: What Is a Random Sequence?](../)

**Exercise 1.** [*10*] Can a periodic sequence be equidistributed?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m19s  

---

No. Let a periodic sequence have period $p$, so that $U_{n+p}=U_n$ for all $n$. Then the sequence is completely determined by the finite set of values $U_0,\ldots,U_{p-1}$.

For any interval $[u,v)$, let $m$ be the number of indices $j$ with $0\le j<p$ such that $u\le U_j<v$. In each complete period the interval contains exactly $m$ terms, hence

$$
\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{\nu(n)}{n}=\frac{m}{p}.
$$

Therefore an equidistributed periodic sequence would have to satisfy

$$
\frac{m}{p}=v-u
$$

for every choice of $u$ and $v$. But $\frac{m}{p}$ can assume only the finitely many values $0,\frac1p,\ldots,\frac{p}{p}$, whereas $v-u$ can be any real number in $[0,1]$. Choosing, for example, an interval of length $1/(2p)$ gives a contradiction. Hence no periodic sequence can be equidistributed.

$$
\boxed{\text{A periodic sequence cannot be equidistributed.}}
$$
