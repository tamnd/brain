[Section 3.5: What Is a Random Sequence?](../)

**Exercise 13.** [*HM27*] Show that an $\infty$-distributed $\{0 \ldots 1\}$ sequence passes the "gap test" in the following sense: If $0 \le \alpha < \beta \le 1$ and $p = \beta - \alpha$, let $f(0) = 0$, and for $n \ge 1$ let $f(n)$ be the smallest integer $m > f(n-1)$ such that $\alpha \le U_m < \beta$; then

$$\Pr(f(n) - f(n-1) = k) = p(1-p)^{k-1}.$$

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m22s  

---

## Setup

Let $0 \le \alpha < \beta \le 1$ and set $p = \beta - \alpha$. Define the indicator event

$S_n \;:\; \alpha \le U_n < \beta.$

For an $\infty$-distributed sequence ${U_n}$, every finite block of variables has joint distribution given by Definition B for all $k$. In particular, for any choice of intervals $I_1,\ldots,I_k \subseteq [0,1)$ that are finite unions of half-open subintervals, the probability of membership conditions over a block ${U_n,\ldots,U_{n+k-1}}$ is determined by additivity from products of interval probabilities.

Define $f(0)=0$ and for $n \ge 1$ let $f(n)$ be the smallest integer $m>f(n-1)$ such that $S_m$ holds. The quantity $f(n)-f(n-1)$ is the waiting time between the $(n-1)$-st and $n$-th occurrences of $S_m$.

The goal is to prove

$\Pr(f(n)-f(n-1)=k)=p(1-p)^{k-1}.$

## Solution

Fix $n$ and write $T_n = f(n)-f(n-1)$. The value $T_n = k$ means that starting from index $f(n-1)+1$, the event $S_m$ fails for $k-1$ consecutive indices and then holds at the $k$-th index. Formally,

$T_n = k \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \bigcap_{j=1}^{k-1} S_{f(n-1)+j}^c \;\cap\; S_{f(n-1)+k}.$

The distribution of any finite pattern of events of the form $S_m$ and $S_m^c$ is determined by expressing complements as unions of disjoint intervals:

$S_m^c = \{U_m \in [0,\alpha)\} \cup \{U_m \in [\beta,1)\}.$

Each event in the intersection above is a product set in $[0,1)^k$ formed from intervals in each coordinate, and the $\infty$-distribution property implies that probabilities of such product sets factor across coordinates. Additivity over the disjoint union defining $S_m^c$ yields

$\Pr(S_m^c)=1-p,$

and more generally, for any finite selection of distinct indices, the joint probability of specified memberships in $S$ or $S^c$ equals the product of the corresponding one-dimensional probabilities.

Applying this factorization to the block starting at $f(n-1)+1$ gives

= \Pr(S_{f(n-1)+1}^c,\ldots,S_{f(n-1)+k-1}^c,S_{f(n-1)+k}).$$
The joint event consists of $k-1$ occurrences of $S^c$ and one occurrence of $S$. By multiplicativity over coordinates,
$$\Pr(T_n = k) = (1-p)^{k-1}p.$$
The expression does not depend on $n$, since the joint distribution of any finite block depends only on the lengths of intervals used in each coordinate and not on the starting index, a consequence of Definition B applied uniformly to all $k$. Thus,
$$\Pr(f(n)-f(n-1)=k)=p(1-p)^{k-1}.$$ ## Verification Each event $S_m$ is defined by a single interval condition on $U_m$, so products of such events correspond to products of intervals in $[0,1)^k$. The $\infty$-distribution hypothesis supplies exact probabilities for all such products, and complements are unions of two such intervals, allowing reduction of mixed events to finite disjoint unions of product sets. The event $T_n=k$ is exactly a cylinder set specifying a fixed pattern of length $k$ in consecutive coordinates, and the resulting probability is the product of $k-1$ factors $(1-p)$ and one factor $p$, yielding $p(1-p)^{k-1}$. ## Notes The structure is identical to the classical geometric waiting-time law for independent Bernoulli trials with success probability $p$. The role of $\infty$-distribution is precisely to enforce independence for all finite coordinate blocks through product probabilities on interval cylinders.
