[Section 3.5: What Is a Random Sequence?](../)

**Exercise 13.** [*HM27*] Show that an $\infty$-distributed $\{0 \ldots 1\}$ sequence passes the "gap test" in the following sense: If $0 \le \alpha < \beta \le 1$ and $p = \beta - \alpha$, let $f(0) = 0$, and for $n \ge 1$ let $f(n)$ be the smallest integer $m > f(n-1)$ such that $\alpha \le U_m < \beta$; then

$$\Pr(f(n) - f(n-1) = k) = p(1-p)^{k-1}.$$

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 7m34s  

---

## Setup

Let

$A_n=\{\alpha \le U_n < \beta\},\qquad p=\beta-\alpha.$

Since $(U_n)$ is $\infty$-distributed, it is $k$-distributed for every positive integer $k$. By Definition B,

$\Pr(A_n)=p,$

and for any specified pattern of membership and nonmembership in the interval $[\alpha,\beta)$ over finitely many consecutive indices, the probability is obtained by multiplying the corresponding factors $p$ and $1-p$.

The quantity

$f(n)-f(n-1)$

is the number of positions from the $(n-1)$st hit of the interval $[\alpha,\beta)$ to the next hit, counting the terminal hit itself.

The required statement is

$\Pr\bigl(f(n)-f(n-1)=k\bigr)=p(1-p)^{k-1}\qquad(k\ge1).$

## Solution

$f(n)-f(n-1)=k$

## Verification

For $k=1$ there are no intervening failures. The formula gives

$$
\Pr(f(n)-f(n-1)=1)=p,
$$

which is the probability that the very next term lies in $[\alpha,\beta)$.

For $k=2$ there is one failure followed by one success. The formula gives

$$
\Pr(f(n)-f(n-1)=2)=p(1-p),
$$

which matches the probability of the pattern

$$
A_{m+1}^{\,c}\cap A_{m+2}.
$$

For general $k$, the event consists of exactly $k-1$ failures and one success in prescribed positions, so the product

$$
(1-p)^{k-1}p
$$

is consistent with Definition B.

## Notes

The obtained law is the geometric distribution with parameter $p$. Thus an $\infty$-distributed sequence reproduces the same gap distribution that would arise from independent Bernoulli trials having success probability $p$.
