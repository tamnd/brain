[Section 3.5: What Is a Random Sequence?](../)

**Exercise 37.** [*M37*] (D. Coppersmith.) Define a sequence that satisfies Definition R4 but not Definition R5. [*Hint:* Consider changing $U_0, U_1, U_4, U_9, \ldots$ in a truly random sequence.]

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 5m28s  

---

Let $U=(U_n)_{n\ge0}$ be a sequence satisfying Definition R5. By Exercise 35, every admissible R4 place-selection applied to $U$ yields a subsequence whose limiting frequency of $1$'s is $1/2$.

Define a new sequence $X=(X_n)$ by

$$
X_n=
\begin{cases}
0,& n=m^2\text{ for some }m\ge0,\\[2mm]
U_n,& \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
$$

We prove that $X$ satisfies Definition R4 but not Definition R5.

## Failure of R5

Let

$$
Q=\{0,1,4,9,16,\ldots\}
$$

be the set of squares. Since $Q$ is computable, Definition R5 allows the computable subsequence

$$
X_0,X_1,X_4,X_9,X_{16},\ldots .
$$

But by construction

$$
X_{m^2}=0 \qquad(m\ge0),
$$

so this subsequence is

$$
0,0,0,\ldots
$$

and therefore has limiting frequency of $1$'s equal to $0$, not $1/2$.

Hence $X$ does not satisfy Definition R5.

## Verification of R4

Let $S$ be an arbitrary admissible R4 place-selection rule. Apply $S$ to $X$.

Denote by

$$
b_1<b_2<b_3<\cdots
$$

the positions selected by $S$.

We must show that

$$
\lim_{r\to\infty}\frac1r\sum_{k=1}^r X_{b_k}=\frac12 .
$$

### Step 1. Construct a corresponding selection rule on $U$

Because $S$ bases each decision only on previously seen symbols, we may define another admissible R4 rule $S'$ acting on $U$ as follows.

Whenever $S$ would see a previously revealed value of $X$, the rule $S'$ supplies the corresponding value of $X$, namely

$$
X_n=
\begin{cases}
0,& n\in Q,\\
U_n,& n\notin Q.
\end{cases}
$$

Since $Q$ is computable, $S'$ is again an admissible R4 place-selection.

By construction, $S'$ makes exactly the same sequence of selection decisions on $U$ that $S$ makes on $X$. Therefore the selected positions are again

$$
b_1,b_2,b_3,\ldots .
$$

Hence

$$
\lim_{r\to\infty}\frac1r\sum_{k=1}^r U_{b_k}
=\frac12
$$

by Exercise 35.

It remains to compare the selected values from $U$ and $X$.

### Step 2. Count how many selected positions are squares

Let

$$
M(r)=\#\{k\le r:\ b_k\in Q\}.
$$

The selected subsequences of $U$ and $X$ differ only at those selected positions that belong to $Q$.

Since the sequence $b_1<b_2<\cdots$ is strictly increasing, for every $k$,

$$
b_k\ge k-1.
$$

Therefore, if $b_k$ is a square and $k\le r$, then

$$
b_k\le b_r.
$$

Consequently,

$$
M(r)
\le
\#\{n\le b_r:\ n\in Q\}.
$$

The number of squares not exceeding $N$ is

$$
\lfloor\sqrt N\rfloor+1,
$$

so

$$
M(r)\le \sqrt{\,b_r\,}+1.
$$

Since $b_r\ge r-1$,

$$
\sqrt{b_r}+1
\le b_r^{1/2}+1.
$$

More importantly,

$$
M(r)\le \sqrt{b_r}+1
\le \sqrt{r+b_r}+1.
$$

Because $b_r\ge r-1$, we have $r\le b_r+1$, hence

$$
M(r)\le \sqrt{b_r}+1=o(r).
$$

Indeed,

$$
\frac{M(r)}{r}
\le
\frac{\sqrt{b_r}+1}{r}
\le
\frac{\sqrt{r}+1}{r}
\longrightarrow0,
$$

since $b_r\ge r-1$ implies $\sqrt{b_r}=O(\sqrt r)$.

Thus

$$
M(r)=o(r).
$$

This is the crucial point: among the first $r$ selected terms, at most $o(r)$ come from square positions. No property of the selection rule is needed beyond the fact that the selected indices form an increasing sequence.

### Step 3. Transfer the limiting frequency

For each $k$,

$$
|X_{b_k}-U_{b_k}|
\le
\mathbf 1_{\{b_k\in Q\}}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\left|
\frac1r\sum_{k=1}^r X_{b_k}
-
\frac1r\sum_{k=1}^r U_{b_k}
\right|
\le
\frac{M(r)}{r}.
$$

Since $M(r)=o(r)$,

$$
\frac{M(r)}{r}\to0.
$$

Hence

$$
\lim_{r\to\infty}
\left(
\frac1r\sum_{k=1}^r X_{b_k}
-
\frac1r\sum_{k=1}^r U_{b_k}
\right)
=0.
$$

Using

$$
\lim_{r\to\infty}\frac1r\sum_{k=1}^r U_{b_k}
=\frac12,
$$

we obtain

$$
\lim_{r\to\infty}\frac1r\sum_{k=1}^r X_{b_k}
=\frac12.
$$

Since $S$ was an arbitrary admissible R4 place-selection, every R4-selected subsequence of $X$ has limiting frequency $1/2$.

Therefore $X$ satisfies Definition R4.

## Conclusion

The sequence

$$
X_n=
\begin{cases}
0,& n=m^2,\\
U_n,& \text{otherwise},
\end{cases}
$$

obtained from an R5 sequence $U$ by changing all square-indexed terms to $0$, satisfies Definition R4 but fails Definition R5. Thus it is the required example. ∎
