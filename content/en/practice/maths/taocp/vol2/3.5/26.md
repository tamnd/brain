[Section 3.5: What Is a Random Sequence?](../)

**Exercise 26.** [*HM34*] *[HM34]* (J. Franklin.) A white sequence, as defined in the previous exercise, can definitely fail to be random. Let $U_0, U_1, \ldots$ be an $\infty$-distributed sequence, and define the sequence $V_0, V_1, \ldots$ as follows:

$$\begin{aligned}
(V_{2n-1}, V_{2n}) &= (U_{2n-1}, U_{2n}) & &\text{if } (U_{2n-1}, U_{2n}) \in G, \\
(V_{2n-1}, V_{2n}) &= (U_{2n}, U_{2n-1}) & &\text{if } (U_{2n-1}, U_{2n}) \notin G,
\end{aligned}$$

where $G$ is the set

$$\{(x, y) \mid x - \tfrac{1}{2} \le y \le x \text{ or } x + \tfrac{1}{2} \le y\}.$$

Show that (a) $V_0, V_1, \ldots$ is equidistributed and white; (b) $\Pr\{V_n > V_{n+1}\} = \frac{1}{4}$. (This points out a weakness of the serial correlation test.)

Let $(U_n)$ be an $\infty$-distributed sequence on $[0,1)$, so any finite collection of distinct $U_n$ is independent and each $U_n\sim U[0,1)$.

The construction defines a transformation applied **within disjoint pairs**

$$
(U_{2m-1},U_{2m}) \mapsto (V_{2m-1},V_{2m}),
$$

but crucially, this does not make $(V_n)$ i.i.d., because adjacent pairs sometimes lie in the same transformed block and sometimes straddle two independent blocks. The earlier solution’s main structural mistake was treating the entire sequence as i.i.d.

## 1. Distribution of each transformed pair

Fix $m$ and write $(X,Y)=(U_{2m-1},U_{2m})$, uniform on $[0,1]^2$. Define

$$
(V_{2m-1},V_{2m}) = T(X,Y),
$$

where $T$ is either the identity or swap depending on membership in $G$.

Both branches preserve Lebesgue measure on $[0,1]^2$, and they partition the square (up to boundary null sets). Hence the pushforward measure is uniform. Therefore

$$
(V_{2m-1},V_{2m}) \sim U([0,1]^2).
$$

In particular, each $V_n\sim U[0,1)$.

Independence across different $m$ is preserved because each transformed pair depends only on disjoint independent input blocks.

## 2. Equidistribution and whiteness (part a)

### Equidistribution

Each $V_n$ is uniformly distributed on $[0,1)$, and the sequence is stationary in distribution at the level of pairs. Standard arguments for $\infty$-distributed sequences apply blockwise: for any interval $A\subset[0,1)$,

$$
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}\mathbf{1}_{\{V_n\in A\}} \to |A|
\quad \text{almost surely},
$$

so $(V_n)$ is equidistributed.

### Whiteness

Whiteness requires that for fixed $k\ge 1$,

$$
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \left(V_n-\tfrac12\right)\left(V_{n+k}-\tfrac12\right)\to 0.
$$

There are three cases:

1. **Both indices in different original blocks:** independence gives expectation $0$.
2. **Indices within the same transformed pair:** the joint distribution is symmetric uniform on $[0,1]^2$, hence

$$
\mathbb{E}\left[\left(V_{2m-1}-\tfrac12\right)\left(V_{2m}-\tfrac12\right)\right]=0.
$$

This follows by symmetry under swapping coordinates on half the domain and antisymmetry of the integrand.
3. **Indices separated across different blocks:** independence again gives expectation $0$.

Thus all lag correlations vanish, and by the strong law for bounded functions of independent blocks, $(V_n)$ is white.

## 3. Computing $\Pr\{V_n > V_{n+1}\}$ (part b)

The key correction is that adjacent pairs do **not** have a single common distribution. There are two distinct cases depending on the parity of $n$.

### Case 1: $n=2m-1$ (within a transformed block)

We compute

$$
\mathbb{P}(V_{2m-1} > V_{2m}).
$$

Let $(X,Y)=(U_{2m-1},U_{2m})$. Then:

- If $(X,Y)\in G$, we keep $(V_{2m-1},V_{2m})=(X,Y)$, so we need $X>Y$.
- If $(X,Y)\notin G$, we swap, so $(V_{2m-1},V_{2m})=(Y,X)$, and we need $Y>X$.

Thus

$$
\mathbb{P}(V_{2m-1} > V_{2m})
=
\mathbb{P}(G\cap\{X>Y\}) + \mathbb{P}(G^c\cap\{Y>X\}).
$$

A direct geometric computation on $[0,1]^2$ gives:

$$
\mathbb{P}(G\cap\{X>Y\})=\frac{3}{8}, \qquad
\mathbb{P}(G^c\cap\{Y>X\})=\frac{3}{8},
$$

hence

$$
\mathbb{P}(V_{2m-1} > V_{2m})=\frac{3}{4}.
$$

### Case 2: $n=2m$ (across two independent blocks)

Now

$$
(V_{2m},V_{2m+1})
$$

comes from two independent transformed blocks. Each coordinate is $U[0,1)$, and the blocks are independent. Hence

$$
(V_{2m},V_{2m+1}) \sim \text{i.i.d. } U[0,1).
$$

Therefore,

$$
\mathbb{P}(V_{2m} > V_{2m+1}) = \frac{1}{2}.
$$

### 4. Asymptotic frequency over all $n$

The event $\{V_n > V_{n+1}\}$ alternates between the two regimes:

- odd $n$: probability $3/4$,
- even $n$: probability $1/2$.

Each occurs with asymptotic frequency $1/2$. Hence the limiting average is

$$
\mathbb{P}(V_n > V_{n+1})
=
\frac12\cdot \frac34 + \frac12\cdot \frac12
=
\frac{3}{8} + \frac{1}{4}
=
\frac{5}{8}.
$$

## 5. Final conclusion

$$
\boxed{
\mathbb{P}\{V_n > V_{n+1}\} = \frac{5}{8}.
}
$$

## Summary of corrections

1. The sequence $(V_n)$ is **not i.i.d.**, because adjacency depends on whether indices lie within a transformed block or across blocks.
2. The previous “parity averaging to $\tfrac14$” was based on a false i.i.d. assumption.
3. Correct analysis requires splitting into:

- within-block dependent pairs,
- cross-block independent pairs.
4. Combining the two regimes yields $\frac{5}{8}$, not $\frac{1}{4}$.
