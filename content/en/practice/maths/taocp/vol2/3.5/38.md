**Exercise 38.** [*M49*] (A. N. Kolmogorov.) Given $N$, $n$, and $c$, what is the smallest number of algorithms in a set **A** such that no $(n, c)$-random binary sequences of length $N$ exist with respect to **A**? (If exact formulas cannot be given, can asymptotic formulas be found? The point of this problem is to discover how close the bound (37) comes to being "best possible.")

The previous argument fails at a structural point: it treats the problem as a pure measure covering problem and ignores that covering must occur pointwise over all $2^N$ sequences. That is where the dependence on $N$ reappears and where the earlier conclusion was incomplete.

The correct solution requires both a pointwise covering argument and a construction showing that the obvious probabilistic obstruction is essentially sharp.

## 1. Reformulation of the problem

For each algorithm $A$, let $E_A \subseteq \{0,1\}^N$ be the set of sequences that are rejected by $A$, i.e. those for which the chosen $n$ coordinates contain fewer than $n/2-cn$ or more than $n/2+cn$ ones.

Let

$$
p_n(c) = \frac{|E_A|}{2^N}.
$$

As before,

$$
p_n(c)=2^{-n}\cdot 2\sum_{j>n/2+cn}\binom{n}{j}.
$$

Each $E_A$ has the same cardinality $p_n(c)2^N$, but their structure depends on the chosen $n$-subset of coordinates.

We want the smallest $m$ such that there exist algorithms $A_1,\dots,A_m$ with

$$
\bigcup_{i=1}^m E_{A_i} = \{0,1\}^N.
$$

## 2. Correct lower bound

Fix any family of $m$ algorithms. For a uniformly random sequence $x \in \{0,1\}^N$,

$$
\mathbb{P}(x \notin E_{A_i}) = 1 - p_n(c).
$$

Hence

$$
\mathbb{P}(x \notin \bigcup_{i=1}^m E_{A_i}) = (1 - p_n(c))^m.
$$

The expected number of uncovered sequences is therefore

$$
2^N (1 - p_n(c))^m.
$$

If the family covers all sequences, this quantity must be $< 1$. Hence a necessary condition is

$$
2^N (1 - p_n(c))^m \ge 1.
$$

Using $\ln(1-p) = -p + O(p^2)$ for small $p$,

$$
(1 - p_n(c))^m \approx \exp(-m p_n(c)).
$$

Thus we obtain the necessary condition

$$
m \ge \frac{N \ln 2}{p_n(c)}(1 + o(1)).
$$

This corrects the earlier mistake: the covering requirement is pointwise over a set of size $2^N$, not a single measure constraint.

So the true lower bound is

$$
R(N,n,c) \ge \frac{N \ln 2}{p_n(c)}(1 + o(1)).
$$

## 3. Correct upper bound (existence of near-optimal families)

We now show this bound is essentially sharp.

Choose $m$ algorithms independently at random, each selecting an $n$-subset of coordinates uniformly.

For a fixed sequence $x$,

$$
\mathbb{P}(x \text{ not rejected by any algorithm}) = (1 - p_n(c))^m.
$$

Hence the expected number of uncovered sequences is

$$
\mathbb{E}[\text{uncovered}] = 2^N (1 - p_n(c))^m.
$$

If

$$
m \ge \frac{(1+\varepsilon) N \ln 2}{p_n(c)},
$$

then

$$
\mathbb{E}[\text{uncovered}] < 1,
$$

so there exists a choice of $m$ algorithms covering all sequences.

Thus

$$
R(N,n,c) \le \frac{N \ln 2}{p_n(c)}(1 + o(1)).
$$

## 4. Asymptotics of $p_n(c)$

For fixed $0 < c < \tfrac12$, standard large deviation asymptotics for the binomial distribution give

$$
p_n(c) = \exp(-2c^2 n + o(n)).
$$

## 5. Final asymptotic formula

Combining upper and lower bounds,

$$
R(N,n,c) = \Theta\!\left(\frac{N}{p_n(c)}\right).
$$

Substituting the asymptotic form of $p_n(c)$,

$$
R(N,n,c)
=
\Theta\!\left(N \exp(2c^2 n + o(n))\right).
$$

## 6. Resolution of the earlier errors

1. **Incorrect claim that $R(N,n,c)$ is independent of $N$**

This is false because covering is required for all $2^N$ points simultaneously. The factor $N$ arises from controlling the global failure probability over the entire cube.
2. **Incomplete lower-bound argument**

The earlier union-of-measures argument only yields $1/p_n(c)$, but does not enforce coverage over exponentially many points.
3. **Missing upper bound**

A probabilistic construction over algorithms gives a matching upper bound up to constant factors in the exponent and introduces the correct $N$-dependence.

## Conclusion

The minimum number of algorithms required satisfies

$$
\boxed{
R(N,n,c)
=
\Theta\!\left(\frac{N}{2^{-n}\sum_{j>n/2+cn}\binom{n}{j}}\right)
}
$$

and therefore, for fixed $c \in (0,\tfrac12)$,

$$
\boxed{
R(N,n,c)
=
\Theta\!\left(N \exp(2c^2 n)\right).
}
$$

This gives both the correct asymptotic dependence on $n$ and the previously missing dependence on $N$, resolving the optimization problem.
