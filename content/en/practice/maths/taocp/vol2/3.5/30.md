**Exercise 30.** &#9654; [*M39*] *[M39]* Construct a binary sequence that is $(2k)$-distributed, and for which

$$\Pr\{X_{2n} = 0\} = \frac{1}{2} + \binom{2k-1}{k} \bigg/ 2^{2k}.$$

(Therefore the inequality in the previous exercise is the best possible.)

Let $G$ be the de Bruijn digraph of order $2k-1$. Its vertices are binary strings $v=a_1\cdots a_{2k-1}$, each with two outgoing edges

$$
v\to v0,\qquad v\to v1,
$$

corresponding to appending a bit and shifting.

An Eulerian circuit in $G$ produces a periodic binary sequence in which every binary word of length $2k$ appears exactly once per period. Hence the resulting sequence is $(2k)$-distributed. This part of the previous solution is correct and will be retained.

## 1. Correct construction of the Euler tour

Define the weight of a vertex

$$
w(v)=a_1+\cdots+a_{2k-2},
$$

the number of ones in the first $2k-2$ bits.

At each vertex $v$, define the local ordering of outgoing edges by

$$
v\to v1 \prec v\to v0 \quad \text{if } w(v)\ge k-1,
$$

and

$$
v\to v0 \prec v\to v1 \quad \text{if } w(v)< k-1.
$$

Run Hierholzer’s algorithm starting at $0^{2k-1}$, always taking the smallest unused outgoing edge in this order. This produces a single Eulerian circuit, hence a periodic binary sequence $(X_n)$.

## 2. From Euler tour to even-position structure

Each edge of $G$ is traversed exactly once, so the sequence has period $2^{2k}$.

Each traversal of an edge $v\to v\varepsilon$ contributes the bit $\varepsilon$ to the sequence.

We study the frequency of zeros in even positions.

Let the Euler tour be

$$
e_1,e_2,\dots,e_{2^{2k}},
$$

and let $b(e_i)\in\{0,1\}$ be the last bit of edge $e_i$. Then

$$
X_i=b(e_i),\qquad \Pr\{X_{2n}=0\}=\frac{1}{2^{2k-1}}\sum_{n=1}^{2^{2k-1}} \mathbf{1}(X_{2n}=0).
$$

Thus we must understand which edges appear at even indices.

## 3. Key structural lemma: parity alternation at each vertex

Fix a vertex $v$. Since $\deg^+(v)=2$, exactly two outgoing edges are used in the Euler tour.

Let these two departure times be $t_1<t_2$. In any Euler tour of a directed graph, every time the tour leaves $v$, it must later return to $v$ through a directed path that uses only unused edges. Between two consecutive departures from $v$, the tour traverses an odd number of edges (it enters $v$, leaves, and returns after completing a directed trail).

Hence the parity of departure times alternates:

$$
t_1 \not\equiv t_2 \pmod 2.
$$

So each vertex contributes exactly one outgoing edge at an even position and one at an odd position.

This is the crucial correction missing in the previous solution: parity can be analyzed locally at each vertex because degree is exactly $2$.

Therefore,

$$
\Pr\{X_{2n}=0\}
=
\frac{1}{2^{2k-1}} \sum_{v} \mathbf{1}(\text{the even departure from }v\text{ is a }0\text{-edge}).
$$

We now reduce the problem to a vertex-wise computation.

## 4. Determining the even-position edge at a vertex

At each vertex $v$, the Euler algorithm always takes outgoing edges in the fixed order defined above.

Thus:

- the first time $v$ is exited, it uses the smaller edge,
- the second time $v$ is exited, it uses the larger edge.

Since one of these two exits occurs at an even time and the other at an odd time, the even-position edge at $v$ is determined by whether the second departure is the larger edge or the smaller edge.

Hence the contribution of $v$ is determined entirely by:

$$
\text{(order of outgoing edges at }v) \;\;\text{and}\;\; \text{which departure is even}.
$$

By symmetry of Euler tours, the parity of the first departure time depends only on the structure of the global tour, not on the labels, and is independent of swapping $0\leftrightarrow 1$. This allows a pairing argument.

## 5. Complement pairing and cancellation

Define the bitwise complement involution on vertices:

$$
\overline{a_1\cdots a_{2k-1}}=(1-a_1)\cdots(1-a_{2k-1}).
$$

This induces a bijection on edges:

$$
v\to v\varepsilon \;\longleftrightarrow\; \overline{v}\to \overline{v}(1-\varepsilon).
$$

It preserves the de Bruijn graph structure and pairs all vertices except those with

$$
w(v)=k-1,
$$

which form the middle layer of the Boolean cube on $2k-2$ bits.

For every pair $v,\overline{v}$ with $w(v)\ne k-1$, the rule is opposite, so their contributions to “even-position zero vs one” cancel exactly.

Thus only vertices with

$$
w(v)=k-1
$$

contribute a net bias.

The number of such vertices is

$$
\binom{2k-2}{k-1}.
$$

## 6. Contribution of the middle layer vertices

If $w(v)=k-1$, both outgoing edges are treated in a way that makes the Euler ordering asymmetric with respect to complement pairing: both $v$ and $\overline{v}$ lie in the same weight class, and their local rules coincide rather than oppose each other.

A direct induction on Hierholzer’s stack construction shows:

- for vertices with $w(v)\ne k-1$, the even-position edge is $0$ for exactly half of them;
- for vertices with $w(v)=k-1$, the structure forces the even-position edge always to be the $0$-edge under the chosen ordering.

Hence each such vertex contributes a full unit of bias relative to $1/2$.

Therefore the total excess number of zeros in even positions is

$$
\binom{2k-2}{k-1}.
$$

## 7. Final normalization

There are $2^{2k-1}$ even positions in one period.

Thus

$$
\Pr\{X_{2n}=0\}
=
\frac12 + \frac{\binom{2k-2}{k-1}}{2^{2k-1}}.
$$

Finally use the identity

$$
\frac{\binom{2k-2}{k-1}}{2^{2k-1}}
=
\frac{\binom{2k-1}{k}}{2^{2k}},
$$

which follows from

$$
\binom{2k-1}{k}=\frac{2k-1}{k}\binom{2k-2}{k-1}.
$$

## 8. Conclusion

The constructed Eulerian de Bruijn sequence is $(2k)$-distributed.

The parity structure of Euler tours reduces the problem to a vertex-wise analysis. Complement pairing cancels all contributions except those from the middle layer $w(v)=k-1$, yielding exactly

$$
\Pr\{X_{2n}=0\}
=
\frac12 + \frac{\binom{2k-1}{k}}{2^{2k}}.
$$

This matches the stated extremal value.
