[Section 3.5: What Is a Random Sequence?](../)

**Exercise 16.** [*HM38*] Does the coupon-collector's test hold for $\infty$-distributed sequences when there are more than two kinds of coupons? (See the previous exercise.)

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 1m39s  

---

We start from the definition of an $\infty$-distributed sequence. A $b$-ary sequence $X_1,X_2,\dots$ is called $\infty$-distributed if for every fixed $k \ge 1$, every block of length $k$,

$$
(X_i, X_{i+1}, \ldots, X_{i+k-1})
$$

has probability $b^{-k}$ of taking any prescribed value in $\{0,1,\ldots,b-1\}^k$. Equivalently, every finite pattern appears with the same probability as if the symbols were i.i.d. uniform, even though global independence is not assumed.

We consider the coupon-collector stopping times. Let $f(0)=0$, and for $n \ge 1$, let $f(n)$ be the first index after $f(n-1)$ such that the block

$$
X_{f(n-1)+1}, \ldots, X_{f(n)}
$$

contains all symbols $0,1,\ldots,b-1$. Define $T_n = f(n)-f(n-1)$. The question is whether the distribution of $T_n$ matches the classical coupon-collector waiting time for $b$ equally likely independent coupons, when $b \ge 3$.

We analyze the first waiting time $T_1$, since stationarity of the $\infty$-distribution implies the same distribution for all $T_n$. Fix $k \ge b$. The event $\{T_1 > k\}$ means that after $k$ draws, not all symbols have appeared. Equivalently, there exists at least one symbol $a \in \{0,\ldots,b-1\}$ that does not appear among $X_1,\ldots,X_k$.

For a fixed symbol $a$, the event that $a$ is absent is

$$
\{X_1 \ne a, \ldots, X_k \ne a\}.
$$

By the $\infty$-distribution property, every length-$k$ pattern occurs with probability $b^{-k}$, so each of the $(b-1)^k$ sequences over the alphabet $\{0,\ldots,b-1\} \setminus \{a\}$ has probability $b^{-k}$. Hence

$$
\Pr(a \text{ is absent}) = \frac{(b-1)^k}{b^k}.
$$

More generally, for any subset $S \subseteq \{0,\ldots,b-1\}$ of size $s$, the probability that all symbols lie in $S$ is

$$
\Pr(X_1,\ldots,X_k \in S) = \frac{s^k}{b^k}.
$$

Now we compute $\Pr(T_1 > k)$ by inclusion–exclusion over missing symbols. Let $A_a$ be the event that symbol $a$ is absent from the first $k$ positions. Then

$$
\Pr(T_1 > k) = \Pr\left(\bigcup_{a} A_a\right)
= \sum_{j=1}^b (-1)^{j+1} \binom{b}{j} \frac{(b-j)^k}{b^k}.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\Pr(T_1 = k)
= \Pr(T_1 > k-1) - \Pr(T_1 > k)
$$

gives

$$
\Pr(T_1 = k)
= \sum_{j=1}^b (-1)^{j+1} \binom{b}{j}
\left(\frac{(b-j)^{k-1} - (b-j)^k}{b^k}\right),
\quad k \ge b.
$$

This expression depends only on $b$ and matches exactly the classical coupon-collector distribution obtained under i.i.d. uniform sampling. The key point is that the $\infty$-distributed property ensures that probabilities of cylinder events depend only on counts of allowed symbols, exactly as in the independent uniform model. No higher-order dependence affects these counts.

Finally, we note that in the classical coupon-collector model the same inclusion–exclusion derivation yields the same formula for $\Pr(T_1 = k)$. Since both models produce identical probabilities for all events of the form “first $k$ draws miss a given subset of symbols,” the waiting-time distributions coincide.

Hence the coupon-collector’s test does hold for $\infty$-distributed sequences when there are more than two kinds of coupons.
