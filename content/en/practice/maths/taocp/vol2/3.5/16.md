**Solution to Exercise 3.5.16 [HM38]**  

We are asked whether the coupon-collector's test holds for $\infty$-distributed sequences when there are more than two kinds of coupons. Let us carefully analyze this situation.  

Let $U_1, U_2, \dots$ be an $\infty$-distributed sequence, that is, a sequence of independent random variables uniformly distributed on $[0,1]$. Suppose we have $m \ge 2$ kinds of coupons, labeled $1,2,\dots,m$, and let the sequence of coupons $C_n$ be generated by dividing the unit interval into $m$ subintervals of equal length $1/m$, so that  
\[
C_n = i \quad \text{if} \quad U_n \in \left[ \frac{i-1}{m}, \frac{i}{m} \right), \quad i = 1,\dots,m.
\]  
This is the standard construction used in the coupon-collector problem.  

The coupon-collector's test checks whether the number of trials needed to collect all $m$ coupons behaves as it should probabilistically. Specifically, let $T$ denote the number of trials needed to see each coupon at least once. For a truly uniform and independent sequence, it is well known that  
\[
\Pr(T \le n) = \sum_{k=0}^{m} (-1)^k \binom{m}{k} \left(1 - \frac{k}{m}\right)^n.
\]  

Since an $\infty$-distributed sequence is composed of independent uniform $[0,1]$ random variables, the mapping to $m$ equally sized subintervals preserves independence and uniformity across the $m$ coupons. Therefore, for any $m \ge 2$, the sequence of observed coupons $(C_1, C_2, \dots)$ is exactly equivalent to a sequence of independent draws from the uniform distribution on $\{1,2,\dots,m\}$.  

Consequently, the standard coupon-collector analysis applies. In particular, the probability that all coupons appear after $n$ trials satisfies the known formula above. Since an $\infty$-distributed sequence produces independent uniform values on $[0,1]$, and the subinterval mapping preserves independence, the coupon-collector test is satisfied. There is no restriction to $m=2$; the argument works for any finite $m$.  

Thus, for $\infty$-distributed sequences, the coupon-collector's test **holds for any finite number of coupons**. The essential properties used are independence and uniformity, both of which remain true after partitioning $[0,1]$ into $m$ intervals.  

**Conclusion:** Yes, the coupon-collector's test holds for $\infty$-distributed sequences when there are more than two kinds of coupons, and the standard probability formula for $T$ applies without modification.
