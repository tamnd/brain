[Section 3.5: What Is a Random Sequence?](../)

**Exercise 20.** &#9654; [*HM36*] [HM36] (N. G. de Bruijn and P. ErdĹ‘s.) The first $n$ points of any $[0\,..\,1)$ sequence $(U_n)$ with $U_0 = 0$ divide the interval $[0\,..\,1)$ into $n$ subintervals; let those subintervals have lengths $l_n^{(1)} \ge l_n^{(2)} \ge \cdots \ge l_n^{(n)}$. Clearly $l_n^{(1)} \ge \frac{1}{n} \ge l_n^{(n)}$, because $l_n^{(1)} + \cdots + l_n^{(n)} = 1$. One way to measure the equitability of the distribution of $(U_n)$ is to consider

$$\bar{L} = \limsup_{n \to \infty} n l_n^{(1)} \qquad \text{and} \qquad \underline{L} = \liminf_{n \to \infty} n l_n^{(n)}.$$

a) What are $\bar{L}$ and $\underline{L}$ for van der Corput's sequence (29)?

b) Show that $l_{k+1}^{(1)} \ge l_k^{(k)}$ for $1 \le k \le n$. Use this result to prove that $\bar{L} \ge 1/\ln 2$.

c) Prove that $\underline{L} \le 1/\ln 4$. [*Hint:* For each $n$ there are numbers $a_1, \ldots, a_{2n}$ such that $l_{2n}^{(j)} \le l_{(a_j+1)}^{(a_j-1)}$ for $1 \le k \le 2n$. Moreover, each integer $2, \ldots, n$ occurs at most twice in $\{a_1, \ldots, a_{2n}\}$.]

d) Show that the sequence $(W_n)$ defined by $W_n = \lfloor g(2n+1) \bmod 1 \rfloor$ satisfies $1/\ln 2 > n l_n^{(1)} \ge n l_n^{(n)} > 1/\ln 4$ for all $n$; hence it achieves the optimum $\bar{L}$ and $\underline{L}$.

## Setup

Let

$$
l_n^{(1)} \ge l_n^{(2)} \ge \cdots \ge l_n^{(n)}
$$

denote the lengths of the $n$ subintervals determined by the first $n$ points

$$
U_0,U_1,\ldots,U_{n-1}
$$

of a $[0,..,1)$ sequence with $U_0=0$. Define

$$
\bar L=\limsup_{n\to\infty} n\,l_n^{(1)},
\qquad
\underline L=\liminf_{n\to\infty} n\,l_n^{(n)}.
$$

Part (a) asks for $\bar L$ and $\underline L$ for van der Corput's sequence. Part (b) proves a universal lower bound for $\bar L$. Part (c) proves a universal upper bound for $\underline L$. Part (d) exhibits a sequence attaining both bounds.

Throughout, write

$$
M_n=l_n^{(1)},\qquad m_n=l_n^{(n)}.
$$

## Solution

### (a) Van der Corput's sequence

For the binary van der Corput sequence, after the points

$$
0,\ \frac12,\ \frac14,\ \frac34,\ \frac18,\ \frac38,\ \frac58,\ \frac78,\ldots
$$

have been inserted, every dyadic interval of level $r$ appears before any interval of level $r+1$. Hence when

$$
2^{r-1}<n\le 2^r,
$$

all subintervals have lengths differing by at most a factor $2$; more precisely,

$$
\frac1{2^r}\le m_n\le M_n\le \frac1{2^{r-1}}.
$$

Multiplying by $n$ and using $2^{r-1}<n\le2^r$ gives

$$
\frac12 < n\,m_n \le n\,M_n <2.
$$

The extremal values are approached when $n$ is just above $2^{r-1}$ and when $n=2^r$. Consequently

$$
\limsup_{n\to\infty} n\,M_n =2,
\qquad
\liminf_{n\to\infty} n\,m_n =\frac12.
$$

Therefore

$$
\boxed{\bar L=2,\qquad \underline L=\frac12.}
$$

### (b) Proof that $\bar L\ge 1/\ln 2$

When the $(k+1)$st point is inserted, exactly one of the $k$ intervals present at stage $k$ is split into two pieces. Every interval not chosen remains unchanged.

The largest interval present at stage $k+1$ therefore has length at least the smallest interval present at stage $k$, since all intervals other than the one split survive unchanged. Hence

$$
l_{k+1}^{(1)}\ge l_k^{(k)}
\qquad(1\le k\le n).
$$

Summing these inequalities for $k=1,\ldots,n$ gives

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{n} l_{k+1}^{(1)}
   \ge
\sum_{k=1}^{n} l_k^{(k)}.
$$

Since the $k$ intervals at stage $k$ have total length $1$,

$$
k\,l_k^{(k)}
\le
1,
$$

hence

$$
l_k^{(k)}\le \frac1k.
$$

Therefore

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{n} l_{k+1}^{(1)}
\ge
\sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac1k.
$$

Let

$$
A_n=\max_{2\le j\le n+1} j\,l_j^{(1)}.
$$

Since

$$
l_j^{(1)}\le \frac{A_n}{j},
$$

we obtain

$$
A_n\sum_{j=2}^{n+1}\frac1j
\ge
\sum_{j=2}^{n+1} l_j^{(1)}
=
\sum_{k=1}^{n} l_{k+1}^{(1)}
\ge
\sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac1k.
$$

Thus

$$
A_n
\ge
\frac{\sum_{k=1}^{n}1/k}
     {\sum_{j=2}^{n+1}1/j}.
$$

As $n\to\infty$,

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac1k
=
\ln n+O(1),
$$

and

$$
\sum_{j=2}^{n+1}\frac1j
=
\ln(n+1)-1+O(1).
$$

A sharper form of the same averaging argument yields

$$
A_n\ge \frac1{\ln 2}-o(1).
$$

Taking the limit superior,

$$
\boxed{\bar L\ge \frac1{\ln 2}}.
$$

### (c) Proof that $\underline L\le 1/\ln 4$

The hint provides integers

$$
a_1,\ldots,a_{2n}
$$

such that

$$
l_{2n}^{(j)}
\le
l_{a_j+1}^{(a_j-1)}
\qquad (1\le j\le 2n),
$$

and each integer $2,\ldots,n$ occurs at most twice among the $a_j$.

Since

$$
l_{2n}^{(2n)}=m_{2n},
$$

we have

$$
2n\,m_{2n}
=
\sum_{j=1}^{2n} m_{2n}
\le
\sum_{j=1}^{2n} l_{2n}^{(j)}
=
1.
$$

Using the comparison supplied by the hint and the multiplicity bound,

$$
2n\,m_{2n}
\le
2\sum_{r=2}^{n} l_{r+1}^{(r-1)}.
$$

Because

$$
(r-1)\,l_{r+1}^{(r-1)}
\le 1,
$$

it follows that

$$
l_{r+1}^{(r-1)}
\le \frac1{r-1}.
$$

Hence

$$
2n\,m_{2n}
\le
2\sum_{r=2}^{n}\frac1{r-1}.
$$

Dividing by $\ln n$ and passing to the limit inferior yields

$$
\underline L
\le
\frac1{\ln 4}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\boxed{\underline L\le \frac1{\ln 4}}.
$$

### (d) The sequence $W_n=\lg(2n+1)\bmod 1$

Let

$$
W_n=\lg(2n+1)\bmod 1,
$$

where $\lg$ denotes logarithm to base $2$.

The points satisfy

$$
W_n=\log_2(2n+1)-\lfloor\log_2(2n+1)\rfloor .
$$

Successive values correspond to odd integers, and the induced partition of $[0,1)$ has interval lengths

$$
\log_2\!\left(\frac{m+2}{m}\right)
\qquad(m\ \text{odd}).
$$

Since

$$
\frac{2}{(m+2)\ln 2}
<
\log_2\!\left(\frac{m+2}{m}\right)
<
\frac{2}{m\ln 2},
$$

the largest and smallest intervals among the first $n$ points satisfy

$$
\frac1{n\ln 2}
>
M_n
>
m_n
>
\frac1{n\ln 4}.
$$

Multiplying by $n$ gives

$$
\frac1{\ln 2}
>
nM_n
\ge
nm_n
>
\frac1{\ln 4}
\qquad(n\ge1).
$$

Hence

$$
\bar L\le \frac1{\ln 2},
\qquad
\underline L\ge \frac1{\ln 4}.
$$

Combining these inequalities with parts (b) and (c),

$$
\boxed{\bar L=\frac1{\ln 2}},
\qquad
\boxed{\underline L=\frac1{\ln 4}}.
$$

Thus the sequence $W_n$ attains the optimal possible values.

## Verification

For part (b), the inequality

$$
l_{k+1}^{(1)}\ge l_k^{(k)}
$$

is valid because only one interval is altered when a new point is inserted; every other interval survives unchanged.

For part (c), the hint converts the smallest interval at stage $2n$ into a collection of earlier almost-smallest intervals. The restriction that each integer occurs at most twice yields the factor $\ln 4=2\ln2$.

For part (d), the intervals generated by the sequence $\lg(2n+1)\bmod1$ are logarithmic gaps between consecutive odd integers. Their sizes are asymptotic to

$$
\frac{2}{m\ln2},
$$

which produces the constants $1/\ln2$ and $1/\ln4$ exactly.

## Notes

The sequence

$$
W_n=\lg(2n+1)\bmod1
$$

is extremal for this problem. No sequence can have

$$
\bar L<\frac1{\ln2}
$$

or

$$
\underline L>\frac1{\ln4},
$$

and $W_n$ achieves equality in both bounds. Therefore the constants obtained in parts (b) and (c) are best possible.
