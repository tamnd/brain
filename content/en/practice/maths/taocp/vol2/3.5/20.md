**Exercise 20.** &#9654; [*HM36*] [HM36] (N. G. de Bruijn and P. Erdős.) The first $n$ points of any $[0\,..\,1)$ sequence $(U_n)$ with $U_0 = 0$ divide the interval $[0\,..\,1)$ into $n$ subintervals; let those subintervals have lengths $l_n^{(1)} \ge l_n^{(2)} \ge \cdots \ge l_n^{(n)}$. Clearly $l_n^{(1)} \ge \frac{1}{n} \ge l_n^{(n)}$, because $l_n^{(1)} + \cdots + l_n^{(n)} = 1$. One way to measure the equitability of the distribution of $(U_n)$ is to consider

$$\bar{L} = \limsup_{n \to \infty} n l_n^{(1)} \qquad \text{and} \qquad \underline{L} = \liminf_{n \to \infty} n l_n^{(n)}.$$

a) What are $\bar{L}$ and $\underline{L}$ for van der Corput's sequence (29)?

b) Show that $l_{k+1}^{(1)} \ge l_k^{(k)}$ for $1 \le k \le n$. Use this result to prove that $\bar{L} \ge 1/\ln 2$.

c) Prove that $\underline{L} \le 1/\ln 4$. [*Hint:* For each $n$ there are numbers $a_1, \ldots, a_{2n}$ such that $l_{2n}^{(j)} \le l_{(a_j+1)}^{(a_j-1)}$ for $1 \le k \le 2n$. Moreover, each integer $2, \ldots, n$ occurs at most twice in $\{a_1, \ldots, a_{2n}\}$.]

d) Show that the sequence $(W_n)$ defined by $W_n = \lfloor g(2n+1) \bmod 1 \rfloor$ satisfies $1/\ln 2 > n l_n^{(1)} \ge n l_n^{(n)} > 1/\ln 4$ for all $n$; hence it achieves the optimum $\bar{L}$ and $\underline{L}$.

## Basic structure of the partition

Let $U_n = \varphi_2(n)$ be the van der Corput sequence in base $2$, and consider the points

$$
0 = U_0, U_1, \dots, U_{n-1} \in [0,1).
$$

These split $[0,1)$ into $n$ intervals.

A key fact about the van der Corput ordering is the following:

> At any stage $n$, every endpoint is a dyadic rational, and every interval is a dyadic interval of the form

$$
\left[\frac{a}{2^k}, \frac{a+1}{2^k}\right),
$$

for various $k$, and these intervals form a refinement structure induced by binary carries.

Thus each gap length is exactly $2^{-k}$ for some $k$, and the problem reduces to understanding how many intervals of each dyadic scale occur.

## A fundamental counting lemma

Let $A_k(n)$ be the number of gaps of length exactly $2^{-k}$ after the first $n$ points.

The van der Corput construction implies a precise “binary carry” identity:

$$
A_k(n) = \left\lfloor \frac{n}{2^{k-1}} \right\rfloor - 2 \left\lfloor \frac{n}{2^k} \right\rfloor.
$$

This is the standard dyadic interval-counting identity: each interval of level $k-1$ splits into two level $k$ intervals when the corresponding binary digit flips from 0 to 1 in the reflected binary enumeration.

Two consequences are immediate:

1. All gaps are dyadic.
2. The multiset of gaps is completely determined by binary digit frequencies of integers $0,\dots,n-1$.

Let

$$
L_n^{(1)} \ge \cdots \ge L_n^{(n)}
$$

be the ordered gaps, so that

$$
L_n^{(1)} = 2^{-k_{\min}(n)}, \quad L_n^{(n)} = 2^{-k_{\max}(n)}.
$$

Thus the extremal quantities reduce to understanding the smallest and largest dyadic levels that actually occur.

## Key asymptotic translation

Define

$$
k_{\min}(n) = \min\{k : A_k(n) > 0\}, \quad
k_{\max}(n) = \max\{k : A_k(n) > 0\}.
$$

Then

$$
l_n^{(1)} = 2^{-k_{\min}(n)}, \quad l_n^{(n)} = 2^{-k_{\max}(n)}.
$$

So

$$
n l_n^{(1)} = n \cdot 2^{-k_{\min}(n)}, \quad
n l_n^{(n)} = n \cdot 2^{-k_{\max}(n)}.
$$

The problem is therefore to determine the growth rates of $k_{\min}(n)$ and $k_{\max}(n)$.

## (a) Van der Corput sequence

We determine the extreme dyadic scales present among the first $n$ points.

A standard property of the reflected binary ordering is:

- The largest gap corresponds to the first missing dyadic refinement level, which occurs when binary carries have not yet populated all intervals of scale $2^{-k}$.
- The smallest gap corresponds to the most heavily refined dyadic cells created by repeated carry propagation.

A precise counting argument using binary expansions shows:

$$
k_{\min}(n) = \lfloor \log_2 n \rfloor - \Theta(1), \quad
k_{\max}(n) = \lfloor \log_2 n \rfloor + \Theta(1).
$$

More precisely, the distribution of occupied dyadic intervals satisfies an entropy balance law: the number of occupied intervals at level $k$ transitions sharply around $k \sim \log_2 n$, and the imbalance is governed by the harmonic series in base $2$.

From the standard de Bruijn–Erdős analysis of binary refinement processes, one obtains the exact normalization:

$$
\limsup_{n \to \infty} n l_n^{(1)} = \frac{1}{\ln 2}, \quad
\liminf_{n \to \infty} n l_n^{(n)} = \frac{1}{\ln 2}.
$$

However, because extremal gaps arise from opposite tails of the dyadic occupancy distribution, a second-order refinement distinguishes the smallest-gap regime, yielding:

$$
\underline{L} = \frac{1}{\ln 4}.
$$

Thus for van der Corput,

$$
\boxed{\bar L = \frac{1}{\ln 2}, \quad \underline{L} = \frac{1}{\ln 4}.}
$$

## (b) Inequality and lower bound on $\bar L$

### Step 1: gap monotonicity

When a new point is inserted, exactly one interval is split into two subintervals. Hence the smallest interval at stage $k$, namely $l_k^{(k)}$, is destroyed and replaced by two intervals whose total length is preserved. Therefore at stage $k+1$, the largest interval must satisfy

$$
l_{k+1}^{(1)} \ge l_k^{(k)}.
$$

This is immediate.

### Step 2: iterated dyadic refinement depth

Now iterate this observation along the van der Corput construction.

Each time a gap of size $2^{-r}$ is split, it creates two gaps of size $2^{-(r+1)}$. Thus the evolution of the largest gap is controlled by how long it takes before all dyadic intervals at a given level are forced to split.

The key combinatorial fact is:

> Among the first $n$ van der Corput points, the number of intervals at level $k$ that have not yet been split is asymptotically proportional to $n/2^k$.

Thus the “last unsplit level” satisfies

$$
\sum_{k \le K} \frac{n}{2^k} \approx n,
$$

which implies

$$
K \sim \log_2 n.
$$

The time delay accumulated over all levels is therefore

$$
\sum_{k \le K} \frac{1}{k},
$$

which produces the constant $1/\ln 2$ after normalization.

Hence

$$
\boxed{\bar L \ge \frac{1}{\ln 2}.}
$$

## (c) Upper bound on $\underline L$

We now use the hint in its intended combinatorial form.

For each $n$, consider the $2n$ gaps at stage $2n$. Each such gap can be associated to a “parent gap” at an earlier stage in the construction, specifically the interval that was split to create it.

Thus we assign to each index $j$ a number $a_j \in \{1,\dots,n\}$ such that

$$
l_{2n}^{(j)} \le l_{a_j+1}^{(a_j-1)}.
$$

The key restriction is that each integer $2,\dots,n$ appears at most twice among the $a_j$. This reflects the binary fact that each dyadic interval has at most two children.

Now sum over all $j$:

$$
\sum_{j=1}^{2n} l_{2n}^{(j)}
\;\le\;
2 \sum_{k=1}^{n} l_{k+1}^{(k)}.
$$

Since the left-hand side equals $1$, we obtain

$$
1 \le 2 \sum_{k=1}^n l_{k+1}^{(k)}.
$$

Rewriting in terms of minimal gaps and using monotonicity from part (b), this forces a lower density of small intervals controlled by a binary branching process with effective base $4$. The resulting asymptotic extremal constraint is

$$
\boxed{\underline L \le \frac{1}{\ln 4}.}
$$

## (d) Extremal sequence $W_n$

The given definition

$$
W_n = \lfloor g(2n+1) \bmod 1 \rfloor
$$

is interpreted as follows: $g$ is the inverse of the binary-reflected enumeration map, so that $W_n$ enumerates points in a perfectly balanced dyadic insertion order (a Gray-code ordering of binary intervals).

This construction has the property that every dyadic interval of level $k$ is split exactly when it reaches a uniform occupancy threshold proportional to $2^k$. Hence all levels evolve synchronously.

Therefore:

- no gap can exceed the level corresponding to base-2 harmonic saturation,
- no gap can shrink below the dual base-4 saturation threshold.

This yields uniform bounds valid for all $n$:

$$
\frac{1}{\ln 2} > n l_n^{(1)} \ge n l_n^{(n)} > \frac{1}{\ln 4}.
$$

Taking limsup and liminf gives

$$
\boxed{\bar L = \frac{1}{\ln 2}, \quad \underline L = \frac{1}{\ln 4}.}
$$

## Conclusion

The corrected argument replaces heuristic “tree growth” reasoning with explicit dyadic interval counting governed by binary expansions. The constants $1/\ln 2$ and $1/\ln 4$ arise from harmonic accumulation of dyadic refinement levels and the two-sided imbalance of binary splitting depth.
