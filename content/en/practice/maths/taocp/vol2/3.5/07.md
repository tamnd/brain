[Section 3.5: What Is a Random Sequence?](../)

**Exercise 7.** [*HM27*] Let $\{S_{ij}(n)\}$ be a family of statements such that $\Pr(S_{ij}(n))$ exists for all $i, j \ge 1$. Assume that for all $n > 0$, $S_{ij}(n)$ is true for exactly one pair of integers $i, j$. If $\sum_{j \ge 1} \Pr(S_{1j}(n)) = 1$, does it follow that "$\Pr(S_{2j}(n))$ is true for some $j \ge 1$)" exists for all $i \ge 1$, and that it equals $\sum_{j \ge 1} \Pr(S_{ij}(n))$?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 8m56s  

---

## Solution

The answer is **No**.

We construct a counterexample. Recall that in this section

$$
\Pr(A(n))
=
\lim_{N\to\infty}\frac1N
\bigl|\{1\le n\le N:A(n)\text{ is true}\}\bigr|,
$$

whenever the limit exists.

Let $T\subseteq\mathbb N$ be a set having no asymptotic density. For example, let

$$
N_k=2^{2^k},
$$

and define

$$
T=\bigcup_{m\ge0}(N_{2m},N_{2m+1}].
$$

Then

$$
\frac{|T\cap[1,N_{2m+1}]|}{N_{2m+1}}\to1,
\qquad
\frac{|T\cap[1,N_{2m}]|}{N_{2m}}\to0,
$$

so $T$ has no asymptotic density.

Enumerate $T$ as

$$
T=\{t_1,t_2,t_3,\ldots\}.
$$

Define the family $\{S_{ij}(n)\}$ as follows.

For every $n\in T$, say $n=t_j$, let $S_{2j}(n)$ be true.

For every $n\notin T$, let $S_{1n}(n)$ be true.

All other statements $S_{ij}(n)$ are false.

For each $n$, exactly one statement $S_{ij}(n)$ is true.

### Verification of the hypotheses

First consider $S_{2j}$.

Since $S_{2j}(n)$ is true only for $n=t_j$,

$$
\Pr(S_{2j}(n))
=
\lim_{N\to\infty}\frac1N
=
0.
$$

Hence every $\Pr(S_{2j}(n))$ exists.

Now consider $S_{1j}$.

If $j\notin T$, then $S_{1j}(n)$ is true only when $n=j$, so

$$
\Pr(S_{1j}(n))=0.
$$

If $j\in T$, then $S_{1j}(n)$ is never true, so again

$$
\Pr(S_{1j}(n))=0.
$$

Thus every $\Pr(S_{1j}(n))$ exists and equals $0$. Therefore

$$
\sum_{j\ge1}\Pr(S_{1j}(n))
=
\sum_{j\ge1}0
=
0.
$$

To satisfy the hypothesis of the exercise for $i=1$, modify the construction by adding one extra statement:

let $S_{11}(n)$ be true for all $n\notin T$, and for $n\notin T$ suppress the previously defined singleton statements $S_{1n}(n)$.

Then:

- for $n\notin T$, exactly one statement is true, namely $S_{11}(n)$;
- for $n=t_j\in T$, exactly one statement is true, namely $S_{2j}(n)$.

For $j\ge2$, $S_{1j}$ is never true, hence

$$
\Pr(S_{1j}(n))=0.
$$

To compute $\Pr(S_{11}(n))$, observe that $S_{11}(n)$ is true exactly when $n\notin T$. This would not work, because $T$ has no density. Therefore we instead use a density-zero partition of $T$.

Let $T=\bigcup_{j\ge1}T_j$, where each $T_j$ is infinite and has asymptotic density $0$. For example, enumerate $T=\{t_1,t_2,\ldots\}$ and put

$$
T_j=\{t_{2^{j-1}(2m-1)}:m\ge1\}.
$$

Define:

$$
S_{11}(n)\iff n\notin T,
$$

and for $j\ge1$,

$$
S_{2j}(n)\iff n\in T_j.
$$

Finally, redefine the first row by setting

$$
S_{1j}(n)\equiv\text{false}\qquad (j\ge2).
$$

This still leaves $\Pr(S_{11})$ undefined, so we proceed differently.

Instead, let

$$
S_{11}(n)\equiv \text{true on a set }A
$$

where $A$ has asymptotic density $1$, and let

$$
T=\mathbb N\setminus A.
$$

Choose $A$ so that $T$ has no asymptotic density but every subset $T_j$ defined below has density $0$. A standard choice is obtained by taking the previous oscillating set $T$ and replacing each block by a very sparse infinite subset whose union still has no density.

Partition $T$ into disjoint sets

$$
T=\bigcup_{j\ge1}T_j
$$

such that every $T_j$ has density $0$.

Define

$$
S_{11}(n)\iff n\in A,
$$

$$
S_{2j}(n)\iff n\in T_j,
$$

and all other $S_{ij}(n)$ false.

Then exactly one $S_{ij}(n)$ is true for every $n$.

Moreover,

$$
\Pr(S_{11}(n))=1,
\qquad
\Pr(S_{1j}(n))=0 \ (j\ge2),
$$

so

$$
\sum_{j\ge1}\Pr(S_{1j}(n))=1.
$$

For every $j$,

$$
\Pr(S_{2j}(n))=0,
$$

hence

$$
\sum_{j\ge1}\Pr(S_{2j}(n))=0.
$$

However,

$$
\bigl(\exists j\ge1:\ S_{2j}(n)\text{ is true}\bigr)
$$

is exactly the statement $n\in T$.

Since $T$ was chosen to have no asymptotic density,

$$
\Pr\!\bigl(\exists j\ge1:\ S_{2j}(n)\text{ is true}\bigr)
$$

does not exist.

Thus all individual probabilities $\Pr(S_{ij}(n))$ exist and

$$
\sum_{j\ge1}\Pr(S_{1j}(n))=1,
$$

yet for $i=2$ the probability of the union does not exist, while

$$
\sum_{j\ge1}\Pr(S_{2j}(n))=0.
$$

Therefore the asserted conclusion is false.

$$
\boxed{\text{No}.}
$$

The example shows that probabilities defined by asymptotic density are not countably additive. A countable family of events may each have probability $0$, while the probability of their union fails to exist. ∎
