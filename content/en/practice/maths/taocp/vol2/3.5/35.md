[Section 3.5: What Is a Random Sequence?](../)

**Exercise 35.** &#9654; [*HM35*] (D. W. Loveland.) Show that if a binary sequence $(X_n)$ is R5-random, and if $(a_n)$ is any computable sequence as in Definition R4, then $\Pr\{X_{a_n} = 1\} \ge \frac{1}{2}$ and $\Pr\{X_{a_n} = 1\} \le \frac{1}{2}$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m30s  

---

Let $(X_n)$ be R5-random and let $(a_n)$ be a computable sequence of positive integers as in Definition R4. Define the selected subsequence

$$
Y_n = X_{a_n}.
$$

We interpret

$$
\Pr\{X_{a_n}=1\}
$$

as the limiting frequency

$$
\lim_{N\to\infty}\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n<N} X_{a_n},
$$

provided this limit exists.

## 1. The sequence $(a_n)$ defines an R4 selection rule

In Definition R4, a selection rule is a computable procedure that, independently of the values of $(X_n)$, specifies an increasing sequence of indices to be selected.

Since $(a_n)$ is computable and does not depend on $(X_n)$, we can define a selection rule $S$ that at stage $n$ outputs the index $a_n$. This rule is computable and admissible in the sense of R4.

Thus $S$ selects exactly the subsequence $(X_{a_n})$.

## 2. Application of R5-randomness

By Definition of R5-randomness, the sequence $(X_n)$ has the property that for every computable R4 selection rule $S$ that selects an infinite subsequence, the selected subsequence has limiting frequency of ones equal to $1/2$; that is, if $Y_n$ is the subsequence produced by $S$, then

$$
\lim_{N\to\infty}\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n<N} Y_n = \frac{1}{2}.
$$

Applying this to the rule defined by $(a_n)$, we obtain

$$
\lim_{N\to\infty}\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n<N} X_{a_n} = \frac{1}{2}.
$$

In particular, the limit exists, since R5-randomness asserts convergence of the frequency for every computable selection rule.

Hence,

$$
\Pr\{X_{a_n}=1\} = \frac{1}{2}.
$$

## 3. Derivation of the required inequalities

From the equality

$$
\Pr\{X_{a_n}=1\} = \frac{1}{2},
$$

it follows immediately that

$$
\Pr\{X_{a_n}=1\} \le \frac{1}{2}
\quad \text{and} \quad
\Pr\{X_{a_n}=1\} \ge \frac{1}{2}.
$$

## Conclusion

The computable sequence $(a_n)$ defines a valid R4 selection rule. R5-randomness guarantees that every such computable selection produces a subsequence whose limiting frequency of ones exists and equals $1/2$. Therefore,

$$
\Pr\{X_{a_n}=1\} = \frac{1}{2},
$$

which implies both required inequalities. ∎
