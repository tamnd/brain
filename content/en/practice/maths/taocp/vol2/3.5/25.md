[Section 3.5: What Is a Random Sequence?](../)

**Exercise 25.** [*HM20*] *[HM20]* A sequence is called a "white sequence" if all serial correlations are zero; that is, if the equation in Corollary S is true for all $k \ge 1$. (By Corollary S, an $\infty$-distributed sequence is white.) Show that if a $\{0\..1\}$ sequence is equidistributed, it is white if and only if

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n} \sum_{0 \le j < n} (U_j - \tfrac{1}{2})(U_{j+k} - \tfrac{1}{2}) = 0, \qquad \text{for all } k \ge 1.$$

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 8m45s

Let $(U_j)$ be a $\{0,1\}$-valued equidistributed sequence. Equidistribution means that for every interval $[a,b)\subset[0,1]$,

$$
\frac{1}{n}\#\{j<n: a \le U_j < b\} \to b-a.
$$

Since the values are only $0$ and $1$, this implies in particular

$$
\frac{1}{n}\#\{j<n: U_j=0\}\to \frac12,\qquad 
\frac{1}{n}\#\{j<n: U_j=1\}\to \frac12.
$$

Fix $k\ge 1$. Write

$$
p_{ab}(k)=\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}\#\{j<n: U_j=a,\; U_{j+k}=b\},\qquad a,b\in\{0,1\},
$$

when the limit exists. Whiteness (via Corollary S) is equivalent, in this binary setting, to

$$
p_{ab}(k)=\frac14 \quad \text{for all } a,b\in\{0,1\}.
$$

Indeed, choosing intervals that separate $0$ and $1$, for example $[0,\tfrac12)$ and $[\tfrac12,1]$, the rectangle condition in Corollary S forces all four joint frequencies to equal $1/4$.

We now prove the equivalence.

## 1. Whiteness implies vanishing covariance

Assume whiteness holds. Then for each $a,b\in\{0,1\}$,

$$
p_{ab}(k)=\frac14.
$$

Now expand

$$
(U_j-\tfrac12)(U_{j+k}-\tfrac12)
=U_jU_{j+k}-\tfrac12 U_j-\tfrac12 U_{j+k}+\tfrac14.
$$

Taking averages and limits termwise:

### (i) Joint term

$$
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{j<n} U_jU_{j+k} \to p_{11}(k)=\frac14.
$$

### (ii) Marginal terms

Equidistribution gives

$$
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{j<n} U_j \to \frac12,\qquad 
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{j<n} U_{j+k} \to \frac12.
$$

### (iii) Constant term

$$
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{j<n} \tfrac14 = \tfrac14.
$$

Therefore the limit of the covariance expression is

$$
\frac14 - \tfrac12\cdot\frac12 - \tfrac12\cdot\frac12 + \frac14
= \frac14 - \frac14 - \frac14 + \frac14
=0.
$$

Hence

$$
\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}\sum_{j<n}(U_j-\tfrac12)(U_{j+k}-\tfrac12)=0.
$$

## 2. Vanishing covariance implies whiteness

Assume now that for every $k\ge 1$,

$$
\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}\sum_{j<n}(U_j-\tfrac12)(U_{j+k}-\tfrac12)=0.
\tag{1}
$$

From equidistribution,

$$
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{j<n} U_j \to \frac12,\qquad 
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{j<n} U_{j+k} \to \frac12.
\tag{2}
$$

Define

$$
p_{11}(k)=\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}\sum_{j<n} U_jU_{j+k},
$$

assuming the limit exists (we will determine it).

Expanding (1) and taking limits using (2),

$$
0 = p_{11}(k) - \frac12\cdot\frac12 - \frac12\cdot\frac12 + \frac14.
$$

Thus

$$
p_{11}(k) - \frac14 = 0,
\qquad\text{so}\qquad
p_{11}(k)=\frac14.
$$

Now use the marginal limits. Since

$$
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{j<n} U_j \to \frac12,
$$

we have

$$
p_{10}(k)+p_{11}(k)=\frac12,\qquad p_{01}(k)+p_{11}(k)=\frac12.
$$

Substituting $p_{11}(k)=\frac14$,

$$
p_{10}(k)=\frac14,\qquad p_{01}(k)=\frac14.
$$

Finally,

$$
p_{00}(k)=1-p_{11}(k)-p_{10}(k)-p_{01}(k)=\frac14.
$$

Hence all four joint frequencies equal $1/4$.

## 3. Conclusion (whiteness)

For a $\{0,1\}$-valued equidistributed sequence, the rectangle conditions in Corollary S reduce exactly to the joint frequencies $p_{ab}(k)$. Since all four equal $1/4$, the sequence is white.

Conversely, whiteness immediately yields these joint frequencies, which forces the covariance to vanish.

Therefore,

$$
\text{whiteness} \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad 
\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n} \sum_{j<n} (U_j - \tfrac{1}{2})(U_{j+k} - \tfrac{1}{2}) = 0
\quad \text{for all } k \ge 1.
$$

This completes the proof.
