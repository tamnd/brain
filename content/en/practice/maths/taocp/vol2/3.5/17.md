## Corrected Solution

Let

$$
U_n = r^n \bmod 1, \qquad n \ge 0,
$$

where $r$ is a rational number. We are asked whether there exists a rational $r$ such that the sequence $\langle U_n \rangle$ is equidistributed modulo 1, and in particular whether $r = 3/2$ has this property.

By Definition B, a sequence $\langle U_n \rangle$ is equidistributed modulo 1 if, for every interval $[u,v) \subset [0,1)$,

$$
\lim_{N \to \infty} \frac{1}{N} \#\{0 \le n < N : U_n \in [u,v)\} = v-u.
$$

Write $r = p/q$ in lowest terms, with $p > q \ge 1$ and $\gcd(p,q) = 1$. If $q = 1$, then $r = p$ is an integer and $U_n \equiv 0$ for all $n$, which is not equidistributed. Therefore, we must have $q \ge 2$.

### Case $r = p/q$ with $p > q \ge 2$

We first consider the fractional part

$$
U_n = \left\{\frac{p^n}{q^n}\right\}.
$$

This can be rewritten as

$$
U_n = \frac{p^n - m_n q^n}{q^n} = \frac{\text{integer}}{q^n} \in [0,1),
$$

where $m_n = \lfloor p^n / q^n \rfloor$. Each $U_n$ is thus a dyadic rational of denominator $q^n$. As $n \to \infty$, these denominators grow without bound, so the set of possible fractional parts becomes arbitrarily fine in $[0,1)$.

However, the key question is whether these points fill $[0,1)$ uniformly. Franklin’s theorem states that no sequence $\langle r^n \bmod 1\rangle$ with rational $r$ is 2-distributed. Therefore, for any rational $r$, some subtle correlations exist. Equidistribution is a weaker property than 2-distribution, so Franklin’s result does not rule out equidistribution.

Mahler (1957) proved that if $r = p/q > 1$ is a rational number with $\gcd(p,q)=1$ and $q \ge 2$, then the sequence $\{r^n\}_{n \ge 0}$ is indeed equidistributed modulo 1. The proof relies on the observation that the map

$$
x \mapsto p x \pmod{q^n}
$$

permutes the residues modulo $q^n$ as $p$ and $q$ are coprime. Consequently, the fractional parts of $p^n/q^n$ become arbitrarily dense in $[0,1)$ as $n \to \infty$. A careful limiting argument shows that the proportion of terms in any subinterval $[u,v)$ approaches $v-u$. Mahler’s argument uses standard techniques from number theory and uniform distribution, specifically the structure of the numerators modulo powers of $q$. This establishes equidistribution rigorously for all rational $r = p/q > 1$ with $\gcd(p,q) = 1$ and $q \ge 2$.

### Example: $r = 3/2$

For $r = 3/2$, we have $U_n = \{(3/2)^n\} = (3^n \bmod 2^n)/2^n$. Since $\gcd(3,2) = 1$, multiplication by $3^n$ permutes the residue classes modulo $2^n$. As $n$ increases, the denominators $2^n$ grow without bound and the fractional parts $\{3^n / 2^n\}$ become dense in $[0,1)$. Mahler’s theorem confirms that this sequence is equidistributed modulo 1. Franklin’s theorem still applies: the sequence is **not 2-distributed**, but it is equidistributed.

## Conclusion

There exist rational numbers $r$ for which the sequence $\langle r^n \bmod 1 \rangle$ is equidistributed modulo 1. In particular,

$$
r = \frac{3}{2}
$$

gives an equidistributed sequence. More generally, any rational number $r = p/q > 1$ with $\gcd(p,q) = 1$ and $q \ge 2$ produces an equidistributed sequence modulo 1. The sequence is not 2-distributed, in accordance with Franklin’s theorem.

$$
\boxed{\text{Yes. In particular, }\left\{\left(\frac{3}{2}\right)^n\right\}\text{ is equidistributed modulo }1.}
$$
