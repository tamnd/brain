[Section 3.5: What Is a Random Sequence?](../)

**Exercise 43.** [*26*] It seems just as difficult to find the factors of any *fixed* $R$-bit Blum integer $M$ as to find the factors of a *random* $R$-bit integer. Why then is Theorem P stated for random $M$ instead of fixed $M$?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 8m18s  

---

The key issue is that Theorem P is an _average-case_ statement whose proof depends essentially on averaging over the distribution of $R$-bit integers. This cannot be removed without changing the nature of the argument.

Let $M$ be a Blum integer, so $M=pq$ with $p \equiv q \equiv 3 \pmod 4$. The heuristic observation in the problem is that “fixed hard instances” do not look fundamentally different from “random instances.” However, this intuition does not justify replacing a distributional theorem by a worst-case one.

Theorem P is stated for a random $R$-bit integer $M$ because its proof uses probabilistic properties that hold _with high probability over $M$_, such as estimates on the distribution of auxiliary quantities derived from $M$ and the likelihood that certain randomized subprocedures succeed. The analysis typically bounds an expectation of the form

$$
\mathbb{E}_{M \in \{R\text{-bit integers}\}}[T(M)],
$$

or shows that a randomly chosen $M$ satisfies a needed structural condition with probability $1 - o(1)$. None of these statements imply that _every fixed_ $M$ has the same behavior.

To pass from random $M$ to a fixed $M$, one would need a _worst-case to average-case reduction_ or a _random self-reducibility_ property for factoring Blum integers: namely, a method that transforms any fixed instance $M$ into a distribution of instances indistinguishable (for the algorithm) from uniform random $R$-bit Blum integers while preserving the ability to recover factors. No such general reduction is known for integer factoring.

Thus, even if it “feels” equally difficult to factor a fixed Blum integer and a random one, this is not currently a theorem. The statement of Theorem P is restricted to random $M$ because only in that setting the probabilistic analysis is valid and no adversarial choice of $M$ can invalidate the distributional assumptions used in the proof.
