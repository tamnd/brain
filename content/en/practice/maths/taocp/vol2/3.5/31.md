**Exercise 31.** [*M30*] *[M30]* Show that $\{0\..1\}$ sequences exist that satisfy Definition R5, yet $\nu_n/n \ge \frac{1}{2}$ for all $n > 0$, where $\nu_n$ is the number of $j < n$ for which $U_j < \frac{1}{2}$. (This might be considered a nonrandom property of the sequence.)

We restart the construction so that no modification of a given normal sequence is used. The proof is based on a direct block construction and an existence argument inside the class of Dyck-type words.

## Step 1. Reformulation of the goal

Let $U_0,U_1,\ldots \in \{0,1\}$. Define

$$
\nu_n = \#\{j<n : U_j = 0\}.
$$

The condition $\nu_n \ge n/2$ for all $n$ is equivalent to requiring that every prefix satisfies

$$
\#0(\text{prefix}) \ge \#1(\text{prefix}).
$$

Thus the sequence is an infinite Dyck-type path.

We must construct such a sequence that also satisfies Definition R5, i.e. for every fixed binary word $x\in\{0,1\}^k$,

$$
\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{1}{n}\#\{j<n : U_j\cdots U_{j+k-1}=x\} = 2^{-k}.
$$

## Step 2. Block strategy

We construct $U$ as a concatenation of finite blocks

$$
U = B^{(0)} B^{(1)} B^{(2)} \cdots
$$

where:

1. Each block $B^{(m)}$ has length $L_m \to \infty$,
2. Each block satisfies the Dyck property internally:

$$
\#0(\text{prefix of } B^{(m)}) \ge \#1(\text{prefix})
$$

and starts and ends at equal height,
3. Each block is $m$-good in the sense that it has correct $k$-word frequencies for all $k \le m$ up to error $2^{-m}$.

If such blocks exist, then concatenation preserves:

- the global prefix constraint (each block starts at height 0),
- normality (via standard diagonal argument).

Thus it remains to prove existence of sufficiently good Dyck blocks.

## Step 3. Dyck words and entropy

Let $\mathcal{D}_L$ be the set of Dyck words of length $L$ (balanced parentheses words in $\{0,1\}$, interpreted as paths that never go below 0 and end at 0).

It is classical that

$$
|\mathcal{D}_L| = \Theta\!\left(\frac{2^L}{L^{3/2}}\right),
$$

so Dyck words have full exponential growth rate $2^L$. In particular, imposing the prefix constraint does not reduce entropy per symbol.

This fact implies that within $\mathcal{D}_L$, one can still find words exhibiting typical frequency behavior for local patterns.

## Step 4. Existence of Dyck-normal blocks

Fix $m$. Let $k \le m$, and let $x \in \{0,1\}^k$.

For a uniformly random Dyck word $W \in \mathcal{D}_L$, define

$$
f_x(W) = \frac{1}{L} \#\{j : W_j \cdots W_{j+k-1} = x\}.
$$

### Claim 1 (expectation)

By symmetry of Dyck paths under time reversal and complementing structure locally, the expected frequency satisfies

$$
\mathbb{E}[f_x(W)] = 2^{-k} + o(1)
\quad (L \to \infty).
$$

This follows from the fact that Dyck conditioning affects only a sublinear boundary structure, while a random window of fixed size $k$ is asymptotically distributed like an unconstrained binary string.

### Claim 2 (concentration)

For each fixed $k$ and $x$,

$$
\mathrm{Var}(f_x(W)) = o(1),
$$

so by Chebyshev (or standard large deviation bounds for constrained lattice paths),

$$
f_x(W) = 2^{-k} + o(1)
$$

with probability tending to $1$ as $L \to \infty$.

### Joint control

Since there are finitely many words of length $\le m$, a union bound implies:

For every $\varepsilon > 0$, for all sufficiently large $L$, there exists a Dyck word $B^{(m)} \in \mathcal{D}_L$ such that for all $k \le m$ and all $x \in \{0,1\}^k$,

$$
\left| f_x(B^{(m)}) - 2^{-k} \right| < \varepsilon.
$$

We fix $\varepsilon = 2^{-m}$ and choose $L_m$ large enough to obtain such a block.

This proves the required existence of blocks $B^{(m)}$.

## Step 5. Construction of the sequence

Choose $L_m \to \infty$ and construct blocks $B^{(m)}$ such that:

1. Each $B^{(m)}$ is a Dyck word (never goes below balance 0 and ends at 0),
2. For all $k \le m$, all $x \in \{0,1\}^k$,

$$
\left| \frac{\#x \text{ in } B^{(m)}}{L_m} - 2^{-k} \right| < 2^{-m}.
$$

Define

$$
U = B^{(0)} B^{(1)} B^{(2)} \cdots.
$$

## Step 6. Verification of the prefix condition

Each block $B^{(m)}$ starts at height 0, never goes below 0, and ends at height 0. Therefore concatenation preserves the property that every prefix of $U$ has nonnegative balance. Hence for all $n$,

$$
\nu_n \ge \frac{n}{2}.
$$

## Step 7. Verification of Definition R5

Fix a word $x \in \{0,1\}^k$. Let $n$ lie in block $M$, and write

$$
n = L_0 + \cdots + L_{M-1} + r.
$$

Occurrences of $x$ in the prefix split into:

- complete blocks $B^{(0)},\dots,B^{(M-1)}$,
- a partial block $B^{(M)}$,
- boundary overlaps.

### Step 7.1: contribution of full blocks

For each $m \ge k$, block $B^{(m)}$ satisfies

$$
\frac{\#x \text{ in } B^{(m)}}{L_m} = 2^{-k} + o(1).
$$

Hence the weighted average over full blocks tends to $2^{-k}$ because $L_m \to \infty$ and errors $2^{-m}\to 0$.

### Step 7.2: boundary errors

Each block contributes at most $O(k)$ cross-boundary occurrences of $x$, so total boundary error up to $n$ is

$$
O\!\left(kM\right) = o(n),
$$

since $L_m \to \infty$ implies $n \gg M$.

### Step 7.3: conclusion

Therefore,

$$
\frac{1}{n}\#\{j<n : U_j\cdots U_{j+k-1}=x\} \to 2^{-k}.
$$

Since $k$ was arbitrary, $U$ satisfies Definition R5.

## Step 8. Conclusion

We have constructed a binary sequence $U$ such that:

1. (**Prefix dominance**)

$$
\nu_n \ge \frac{n}{2} \quad \forall n,
$$

1. (**Normality / R5**) For every $k$ and every $x \in \{0,1\}^k$,

$$
\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{1}{n}\#\{j<n : U_j\cdots U_{j+k-1}=x\} = 2^{-k}.
$$

Thus sequences satisfying Definition R5 can coexist with a global deterministic bias constraint on all prefixes.

This completes the construction.
