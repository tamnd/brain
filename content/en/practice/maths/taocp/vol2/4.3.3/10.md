**Exercise 10.** [*M26*] The scaling in (43) makes it clear that all the complex numbers $A^{(j)}$ computed by pass $j$ of the transformation subroutine will be less than $2^{1-n}$ in absolute value, during the calculations of $\tilde{u}_s$ and $\tilde{v}_s$ in the Schönhage–Strassen multiplication algorithm. Show that all of the $A^{(j)}$ will be less than 1 in absolute value during the *third* Fourier transformation (the calculation of $\tilde{w}_s$).

**Corrected Solution**

We want to show that all complex numbers $A^{(j)}$ computed during the third Fourier transform (the computation of $\tilde{w}_s$) satisfy $|A^{(j)}| < 1$.

**Step 1: Recall the scaling from (43)**

In the Schönhage–Strassen algorithm, the first two transforms compute intermediate vectors $\tilde{u}_s$ and $\tilde{v}_s$ using scaled Fourier transforms. Equation (43) shows that, after applying the scaling factor $2^{-(n-1)}$, the magnitudes of all intermediate quantities satisfy

$$
|A^{(j)}| < 2^{1-n}.
$$

This bound applies to every value computed during the first two sets of Fourier transformations.

**Step 2: Structure of the third Fourier transform**

The third Fourier transform is applied to combine $\tilde{u}_s$ and $\tilde{v}_s$ to obtain $\tilde{w}_s$. The algorithm decomposes the length-$2^n$ transform into smaller transforms using a radix-2 structure. Specifically, the Schönhage–Strassen method uses a three-pass Cooley–Tukey type decomposition:

1. First pass: small-length FFTs on $\tilde{u}_s$ and $\tilde{v}_s$.
2. Second pass: combines outputs of the first pass, still scaled by $2^{-(n-1)}$.
3. Third pass: final combination of these results to produce $\tilde{w}_s$.

Each pass consists of linear combinations of the form

$$
A^{(j)} = \sum_{k} \omega_k B_k,
$$

where $|\omega_k| = 1$ and each $B_k$ is a value from the previous pass.

**Step 3: Bound the number of terms in the third transform**

By the radix-2 decomposition used in Schönhage–Strassen, the third Fourier transform acts on pairs of values produced by the second pass. More precisely, the decomposition ensures that the final pass combines exactly two contributions for each output:

$$
A^{(j)} = B_{2j} + \omega B_{2j+1},
$$

where $B_{2j}$ and $B_{2j+1}$ come from the second pass and $\omega$ is a root of unity. This is a direct consequence of the radix-2 butterfly structure: each butterfly sums exactly two inputs multiplied by unit-modulus coefficients.

**Step 4: Apply the triangle inequality**

From Step 1, each $B_k$ satisfies

$$
|B_k| < 2^{1-n}.
$$

Since each output in the third transform is a sum of two such terms multiplied by unit-modulus constants, we have

$$
|A^{(j)}| = |B_{2j} + \omega B_{2j+1}| \le |B_{2j}| + |B_{2j+1}| < 2^{1-n} + 2^{1-n} = 2^{2-n}.
$$

**Step 5: Conclude the bound is less than 1**

In the Schönhage–Strassen algorithm, $n \ge 2$ (in practice, $n$ is very large). Therefore

$$
2^{2-n} \le 1
$$

and, for all $n > 2$,

$$
2^{2-n} < 1.
$$

Hence, every value $A^{(j)}$ computed during the third Fourier transform satisfies

$$
|A^{(j)}| < 1.
$$

**Step 6: Summary**

The crucial points are:

1. Scaling from (43) ensures that all values after the first two passes satisfy $|A^{(j)}| < 2^{1-n}$.
2. The radix-2 butterfly structure of the third transform guarantees that each output is a sum of exactly two contributions, each multiplied by a unit-modulus root of unity.
3. The triangle inequality then gives the bound $2^{2-n}$, which is strictly less than 1.

This completes the proof.
