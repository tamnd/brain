**Corrected Solution to Exercise 4.3.3.9**

We are asked to consider the Fourier transformation method as in the text, but with $\omega$ replaced by $\omega^q$, and to relate the resulting numbers to the standard Fourier coefficients.

Let $(u_0, u_1, \ldots, u_{k-1})$ be a sequence of numbers, and let $\omega = e^{2\pi i / k}$ denote a primitive $k$th root of unity. The standard discrete Fourier transform (DFT) is defined by

$$
\hat{u}_j = \sum_{m=0}^{k-1} u_m \, \omega^{jm}, \quad j = 0,1,\ldots,k-1.
$$

Now suppose we replace $\omega$ by $\omega^q$, where $q$ is a fixed integer. Then the modified Fourier coefficients are

$$
\tilde{u}_j = \sum_{m=0}^{k-1} u_m \, (\omega^q)^{jm} = \sum_{m=0}^{k-1} u_m \, \omega^{q j m}.
$$

Observe that this expression is exactly the standard Fourier transform evaluated at index $qj$ modulo $k$. More precisely, since $\omega^k = 1$, exponents are taken modulo $k$, so

$$
\tilde{u}_j = \sum_{m=0}^{k-1} u_m \, \omega^{(qj) m} = \hat{u}_{qj \bmod k}.
$$

Thus each modified coefficient $\tilde{u}_j$ is simply the standard Fourier coefficient $\hat{u}_{qj \bmod k}$.

**Remark on indexing:**

- If $q$ is coprime to $k$, the map $j \mapsto qj \bmod k$ is a permutation of ${0,1,\ldots,k-1}$. In this case, the modified coefficients are exactly a reordering of the standard Fourier coefficients.
- If $\gcd(q,k) > 1$, some coefficients may coincide, reflecting the fact that the powers $\omega^{qj}$ are repeated for multiple $j$ values.

**Conclusion:**

The simple relation is

$$
\boxed{\tilde{u}_j = \hat{u}_{q j \bmod k}}, \quad j = 0,1,\ldots,k-1,
$$

where $\tilde{u}_j$ are the coefficients obtained with $\omega \mapsto \omega^q$ and $\hat{u}_j$ are the standard Fourier coefficients.

This fully answers the exercise.
