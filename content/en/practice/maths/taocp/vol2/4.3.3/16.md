**Exercise 16.** &#9654; [*25*] [25] Prove that it takes only $O(K \log K)$ arithmetic operations to evaluate the discrete Fourier transform (35), even when $K$ is not a power of 2. [*Hint:* Rewrite (35) in the form

$$\hat{u}_t = \omega^{-t^2/2} \sum_{0 \le s < K} \omega^{(s+t)^2/2} \omega^{-s^2/2} u_s$$

and express this sum as a convolution product.]

Start with the DFT

$$
\hat{u}_t=\sum_{s=0}^{K-1} u_s \,\omega^{st}, \quad \omega=e^{-2\pi i/K}.
$$

Assume a consistent choice of $\omega^{1/2}$ and $\omega^{1/2\cdot n}$ (any fixed branch suffices since only algebraic identities are used).

## 1. Quadratic phase identity

Using

$$
(s+t)^2=s^2+t^2+2st \quad \Rightarrow \quad st=\frac{(s+t)^2-s^2-t^2}{2},
$$

we obtain

$$
\omega^{st}=\omega^{(s+t)^2/2}\,\omega^{-s^2/2}\,\omega^{-t^2/2}.
$$

Substituting into the DFT,

$$
\hat{u}_t=\omega^{-t^2/2}\sum_{s=0}^{K-1} u_s\,\omega^{-s^2/2}\,\omega^{(s+t)^2/2}.
$$

Define

$$
a_s = u_s\,\omega^{-s^2/2}, \quad b_r=\omega^{r^2/2}.
$$

Then

$$
\hat{u}_t=\omega^{-t^2/2}\sum_{s=0}^{K-1} a_s\,b_{s+t}.
$$

## 2. Identification as a linear convolution-type sum

Define the sequence $b_r$ for $r=0,1,\dots,2K-2$ (we only need values in this range because $s+t\in[0,2K-2]$).

Set

$$
c_t=\sum_{s=0}^{K-1} a_s\,b_{t+s}, \quad 0\le t\le K-1.
$$

This is not a cyclic convolution. It is a **linear cross-correlation**, since the index is $t+s$, not $t-s$.

To convert it into a standard convolution, reverse one sequence. Define

$$
\tilde{b}_r = b_{-r}.
$$

Then

$$
c_t=\sum_{s=0}^{K-1} a_s\,b_{t+s}
=\sum_{s=0}^{K-1} a_s\,\tilde{b}_{-(t+s)}.
$$

Let $r=K-1-s$ or more cleanly rewrite in standard convolution form by extending both sequences with zeros outside their ranges and defining

$$
(a*b)_t=\sum_{s} a_s\,\tilde{b}_{t-s}.
$$

With this definition,

$$
c_t = (a*\tilde{b})_{t+K-1}
$$

(up to a fixed index shift, which does not affect complexity or computability).

Thus the problem reduces to a **linear convolution of two length-$O(K)$** sequences.

## 3. Reduction to FFT of a zero-padded convolution

Both sequences are supported on $O(K)$ indices:

- $a_s$ for $0\le s\le K-1$
- $\tilde{b}_s$ for $|s|\le K-1$, after reversal/extension

Hence their convolution has support in a range of length at most $2K-1$.

Embed both sequences into an array of length

$$
M \ge 2K-1.
$$

Choose $M$ as a highly composite number of size $M=\Theta(K)$ (for example, the smallest product of small primes exceeding $2K$). Using the general Cooley–Tukey FFT, a discrete Fourier transform of length $M$ can be computed in $O(M\log M)$ arithmetic operations for such composite $M$.

Compute:

1. $A=\mathrm{DFT}(a)$
2. $B=\mathrm{DFT}(\tilde{b})$
3. Pointwise product $C_k=A_kB_k$
4. Inverse transform $c=\mathrm{DFT}^{-1}(C)$

This yields the linear convolution.

## 4. Final assembly

Once $c_t$ is obtained,

$$
\hat{u}_t = \omega^{-t^2/2}\,c_t.
$$

This final multiplication costs $O(K)$ operations.

## 5. Complexity

Each FFT or inverse FFT on size $M=\Theta(K)$ costs $O(K\log K)$ operations. We perform a constant number of such transforms, so the total cost is

$$
O(K\log K).
$$

## Conclusion

The discrete Fourier transform can be computed for arbitrary $K$ by:

- rewriting the kernel via quadratic phases,
- reducing the problem to a linear convolution of two $O(K)$-length sequences (Bluestein’s method),
- computing that convolution using FFT on a zero-padded array of size $\Theta(K)$.

Therefore,

$$
\boxed{\text{The DFT for general } K \text{ can be evaluated in } O(K\log K)\text{ arithmetic operations.}}
$$
