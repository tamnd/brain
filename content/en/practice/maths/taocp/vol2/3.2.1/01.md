[Section 3.2.1: The Linear Congruential Method](../)

**Exercise 1.** [*10*] Example (3) shows a situation in which $X_4 = X_0$, so the sequence begins again from the beginning. Give an example of a linear congruential sequence with $m = 10$ for which $X_0$ never appears again in the sequence.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1h12m  

---

A linear congruential sequence has the form

$$
X_{n+1} \equiv aX_n + c \pmod m.
$$

Take $m=10$, $a=2$, $c=1$, and $X_0=0$. Then

$$
X_{n+1} \equiv 2X_n+1 \pmod{10}.
$$

Computing the terms:

$$
X_0=0,
$$

$$
X_1 \equiv 2\cdot 0+1 \equiv 1 \pmod{10},
$$

$$
X_2 \equiv 2\cdot 1+1 \equiv 3 \pmod{10},
$$

$$
X_3 \equiv 2\cdot 3+1 \equiv 7 \pmod{10},
$$

$$
X_4 \equiv 2\cdot 7+1 \equiv 15 \equiv 5 \pmod{10},
$$

$$
X_5 \equiv 2\cdot 5+1 \equiv 11 \equiv 1 \pmod{10}.
$$

From this point the sequence repeats:

$$
1,3,7,5,1,3,7,5,\ldots
$$

Thus the sequence is

$$
0,1,3,7,5,1,3,7,5,\ldots
$$

and $X_0=0$ never appears again after the initial term. This gives the required example of a linear congruential sequence with modulus $m=10$ for which $X_0$ does not reoccur.
