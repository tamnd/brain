**Exercise 4.** [*11*] Prove Eq. (6).

## Solution

Equation (2) defines the linear congruential sequence by

$$
X_{n+1}\equiv aX_n+c \pmod m.
$$

We prove equation (6),

$$
X_{n+k}\equiv a^kX_n+\frac{a^k-1}{b}c \pmod m,
\qquad k\ge0,
$$

where $b=a-1$ by equation (4).

The proof is by induction on $k$.

For $k=0$,

$$
a^0X_n+\frac{a^0-1}{b}c
=
X_n+\frac{1-1}{b}c
=
X_n,
$$

hence

$$
X_{n+0}\equiv X_n \pmod m.
$$

Therefore the formula holds when $k=0$.

Assume that for some $k\ge0$,

$$
X_{n+k}\equiv a^kX_n+\frac{a^k-1}{b}c \pmod m.
$$

Using the recurrence relation,

$$
X_{n+k+1}
\equiv aX_{n+k}+c
\pmod m.
$$

Substituting the induction hypothesis gives

$$
X_{n+k+1}
\equiv
a\left(a^kX_n+\frac{a^k-1}{b}c\right)+c
\pmod m.
$$

Expanding,

$$
X_{n+k+1}
\equiv
a^{k+1}X_n+\frac{a(a^k-1)}{b}c+c
\pmod m.
$$

Since $b=a-1$,

$$
\frac{a(a^k-1)}{b}c+c
=
\frac{a(a^k-1)+b}{b}c
=
\frac{a^{k+1}-a+a-1}{b}c
=
\frac{a^{k+1}-1}{b}c.
$$

Hence

$$
X_{n+k+1}
\equiv
a^{k+1}X_n+\frac{a^{k+1}-1}{b}c
\pmod m.
$$

This is the required formula with $k+1$ in place of $k$.

Therefore, by induction,

$$
X_{n+k}\equiv a^kX_n+\frac{a^k-1}{b}c \pmod m
$$

for all $k\ge0$ and $n\ge0$. Writing the congruence in the equivalent residue-class form yields

$$
X_{n+k}
=
\left(a^kX_n+\frac{a^k-1}{b}c\right)\bmod m,
$$

which is equation (6).

This completes the proof.

∎
