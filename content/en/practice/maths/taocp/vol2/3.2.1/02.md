**Exercise 2.** &#9654; [*M20*] Show that if $a$ and $m$ are relatively prime, the number $X_0$ will always appear in the period.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 18m39s  

---

## Solution

Assume that $(a,m)=1$. Let ${X_n}$ be the linear congruential sequence defined by

$$
X_{n+1}\equiv aX_n+c \pmod m.
$$

Since there are only $m$ possible residue classes modulo $m$, some value must eventually repeat. Let $X_r=X_s$ with $0\le r<s$ and $s-r$ minimal. Then the sequence is periodic from $X_r$ onward.

To show that $X_0$ appears in the period, it suffices to prove that the mapping

$$
T(x)\equiv ax+c \pmod m
$$

is a permutation of the residue classes modulo $m$.

Suppose that

$$
T(x_1)\equiv T(x_2)\pmod m.
$$

Then

$$
ax_1+c\equiv ax_2+c\pmod m,
$$

hence

$$
a(x_1-x_2)\equiv0\pmod m.
$$

Because $(a,m)=1$, the number $a$ has a multiplicative inverse modulo $m$. Multiplying by this inverse gives

$$
x_1-x_2\equiv0\pmod m,
$$

so $x_1\equiv x_2\pmod m$. Therefore $T$ is one-to-one, hence a permutation of the $m$ residue classes.

A permutation decomposes the set of residue classes into disjoint cycles. Since $T$ is a permutation, every element has a unique predecessor as well as a unique successor. Starting from $X_0$, the sequence

$$
X_0,\;X_1,\;X_2,\ldots
$$

moves through the cycle containing $X_0$. Because the cycle is finite, it eventually returns to its starting point. Thus there exists an integer $p>0$ such that

$$
X_p=X_0.
$$

Consequently $X_0$ belongs to the period of the sequence.

This completes the proof.
