[Section 3.2.1: The Linear Congruential Method](../)

**Exercise 3.** [*M10*] If $a$ and $m$ are not relatively prime, explain why the sequence will be somewhat handicapped and probably not very random; hence we will generally want the multiplier $a$ to be relatively prime to the modulus $m$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 18m51s  

---

If $a$ and $m$ are not relatively prime, there exists a nontrivial common factor $d > 1$ such that $d \mid a$ and $d \mid m$. Then every term of the linear congruential sequence

$X_{n+1} \equiv a X_n + c \pmod{m}$

satisfies $X_n \equiv r \pmod{d}$ for some fixed residue $r$, because multiplication by $a$ preserves divisibility by $d$ and addition of $c$ modulo $d$ cycles through at most $d$ residues. Consequently, the sequence cannot visit all residues modulo $m$, and many values are systematically omitted.

This restriction confines the sequence to a proper subset of possible states, reducing its period and causing obvious regularities. Such sequences fail to emulate uniform randomness effectively, which is why $a$ is generally chosen to be relatively prime to $m$, ensuring that multiplication by $a$ can reach all residues modulo $m$ and maximizing the potential period.

This completes the proof. ∎
