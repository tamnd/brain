The flawed solution attempts to describe specific factorizations, but the actual question is to identify structural patterns visible in the table of factorizations of numbers of the form $w \pm 1$, where $w$ is typically a power such as $2^e$ or $3^e$. The correct approach is to extract general number-theoretic regularities that explain why the same primes and factor patterns recur across rows.

## Correct solution

Table 1 exhibits systematic patterns that are consequences of elementary properties of the expressions $a^e - 1$ and $a^e + 1$, where $a \in \{2,3\}$. The key structure is governed by divisibility and multiplicative order.

### 1. Repetition of prime factors across different rows

A prime $p$ that divides $a^e - 1$ must satisfy

$$
a^e \equiv 1 \pmod p,
$$

so the multiplicative order of $a \bmod p$ divides $e$. Consequently:

- If $p \mid a^d - 1$, then $p \mid a^e - 1$ whenever $d \mid e$.
- Therefore, primes appearing in the factorization for smaller exponents reappear systematically at multiples of those exponents.

This explains the strong repetition of the same small primes across different entries of $a^e - 1$ in the table.

### 2. Structure of factorizations of $a^e - 1$

The identity

$$
a^e - 1 = \prod_{d \mid e} \Phi_d(a)
$$

implies that new prime factors enter only through cyclotomic factors corresponding to divisors of $e$.

Thus:

- Factorizations of $a^e - 1$ become richer as $e$ acquires new divisors.
- When $e$ is prime, $a^e - 1$ factors as $(a-1)\Phi_e(a)$, so all nontrivial structure is concentrated in one large component.
- When $e$ is highly composite, more distinct cyclotomic components appear, leading to more varied factorization patterns.

This explains the irregular but structured growth in the number of prime factors.

### 3. Systematic divisibility in $a^e + 1$

The expressions $a^e + 1$ behave differently and depend strongly on the parity of $e$.

#### For $2^e + 1$:

- If $e$ is odd, then

$$
2^e \equiv -1 \pmod 3,
$$

so $3 \mid 2^e + 1$.
- If $e$ is even, no such fixed small divisor exists in general, and factorizations tend to be less regular.

#### For $3^e + 1$:

- Since $3 \equiv -1 \pmod 4$,

$$
3^e \equiv (-1)^e \pmod 4,
$$

so if $e$ is odd,

$$
3^e + 1 \equiv 0 \pmod 4.
$$

Thus the table reflects predictable small-prime factors arising from congruences, especially powers of 2 and 3.

### 4. Contrast between $a^e - 1$ and $a^e + 1$

A visible pattern in the table is:

- $a^e - 1$ has highly structured factor reuse due to the divisor lattice of $e$.
- $a^e + 1$ is governed instead by parity and cyclotomic factors of even index, so its factorization pattern is less uniform.

In particular, $a^e + 1$ only acquires systematic small factors under specific congruence conditions (such as modulo 3 or 4), whereas $a^e - 1$ exhibits factor inheritance along divisibility chains of exponents.

### 5. Persistence of small primes

Across the table, small primes appear repeatedly because:

- They correspond to low multiplicative orders of $a$ modulo $p$.
- Small orders divide many exponents $e$, so the same primes recur frequently.

This creates a visible “background structure” of persistent small factors, over which larger, more sporadic prime factors appear.

### 6. Interpretation of the overall pattern

The dominant pattern in Table 1 is not randomness of factorizations but the interaction between:

- divisibility relations among exponents $e$,
- multiplicative orders of $a$ modulo primes,
- and cyclotomic factorization of $a^e \pm 1$.

This explains both the repetition of primes and the structured differences between $a^e - 1$ and $a^e + 1$.

## Conclusion

The table is structured by arithmetic constraints rather than accidental factorizations. The recurring primes, the inheritance of factors along exponent divisibility, and the parity-dependent behavior of $a^e + 1$ are the central observable patterns.
