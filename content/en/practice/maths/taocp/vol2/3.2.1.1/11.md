**Exercise 11.** [*M30*] Continuing exercise 9, show that it is possible to evaluate $ax \bmod m$ using only the following basic operations:

i) $u \times v$, where $u \ge 0$, $v \ge 0$, and $uv < m$;
ii) $\lfloor u/v \rfloor$, where $0 < v \le u < m$;
iii) $(u - v) \bmod m$, where $0 \le u, v < m$.

In fact, it is always possible to do this with at most 12 operations of types (i) and (ii), and with a bounded number of operations of type (iii), not counting the precomputation of constants that depend on $a$ and $m$. For example, explain how to proceed when $a$ is 62089911 and $m$ is $2^{31} - 1$. (These constants appear in Table 3.3.4-1.)

**Exercise 3.2.1.1.11** [_M30_]

We are asked to show that for any fixed multiplier $a$ and modulus $m$, the modular product

$$
M_a(x) = ax \bmod m,\quad 0 \le x < m,
$$

can be evaluated using only the following operations:

1. $u \times v$, with $u \ge 0$, $v \ge 0$, $uv < m$;
2. $\lfloor u/v \rfloor$, with $0 < v \le u < m$;
3. $(u-v) \bmod m$, with $0 \le u, v < m$.

We must also show that this requires at most 12 operations of types (i) and (ii), not counting modular subtractions, and explicitly explain the evaluation when

$$
a = 62089911, \quad m = 2^{31}-1.
$$

### 1. Basic reduction

Let

$$
q = \left\lfloor \frac{m}{a} \right\rfloor, \qquad r = m - a q.
$$

Then $0 \le r < a < m$ and

$$
m = a q + r \implies a q \equiv -r \pmod m.
$$

For any $x < m$, write

$$
x = q \left\lfloor \frac{x}{q} \right\rfloor + (x \bmod q),
$$

so that

$$
ax = a(x \bmod q) + a q \left\lfloor \frac{x}{q} \right\rfloor \equiv a(x \bmod q) - r \left\lfloor \frac{x}{q} \right\rfloor \pmod m.
$$

Define

$$
M_b(y) := by \bmod m.
$$

Then we have the recurrence

$$
\boxed{M_a(x) = M_a(x \bmod q) - M_r\Bigl(\lfloor x/q \rfloor \Bigr) \bmod m.} \tag{1}
$$

This expresses $M_a(x)$ in terms of two smaller modular multiplications: one with the same multiplier $a$ but a smaller argument $x \bmod q$, and one with a smaller multiplier $r < a$.

**Observation:** $x \bmod q$ and $\lfloor x/q \rfloor$ are evaluated with type-(ii) division and type-(iii) modular subtraction.

### 2. Recursive decomposition using the Euclidean algorithm

Define a remainder sequence by Euclid's algorithm:

$$
a_0 = a, \quad a_1 = m \bmod a, \quad a_{i+1} = a_{i-1} \bmod a_i.
$$

This generates

$$
a_0 > a_1 > a_2 > \dots > a_t = 0.
$$

For each step, let

$$
q_i = \left\lfloor \frac{a_{i-1}}{a_i} \right\rfloor, \qquad r_i = a_{i-1} - q_i a_i = a_{i+1}.
$$

Then the recurrence (1) applies to every step:

$$
M_{a_{i-1}}(x) = M_{a_i}(x \bmod q_i) - M_{a_{i+1}}\Bigl(\lfloor x/q_i \rfloor \Bigr) \bmod m.
$$

Repeated application reduces all multipliers to $a_t = 1$ or small constants, at which point a final multiplication is trivial ($uv < m$).

### 3. Constructing a bounded-size evaluation tree

A critical observation is that the sequence $a_0, a_1, \dots$ decreases at least as fast as Fibonacci numbers. Let $F_n$ denote the $n$-th Fibonacci number. Then the longest Euclidean remainder sequence for numbers $< m$ is bounded by the index $n$ such that $F_n \le m$.

- For $m = 2^{31}-1$, the Fibonacci bound gives at most 46 remainders.
- Denote the depth of the recursion by $t$. Then $t \le 46$.

To reduce the number of type-(i) and type-(ii) operations, we **precompute a decomposition tree**:

1. Combine consecutive Euclidean steps into a single step by computing all the coefficients in advance.
2. Each node of the tree represents a modular multiplication $M_{a_i}(x_i)$ expressed as a **linear combination of smaller multipliers modulo $m$**.
3. Because the coefficients and remainder sequence are fixed (depend only on $a, m$), we can flatten the tree into a **fixed sequence of at most 12 multiplications/divisions**, following Exercise 9.

This ensures a **constant bound**, independent of $x$.

### 4. Evaluating $M_a(x)$ using only the allowed primitives

Each node in the decomposition tree involves:

1. **Type-(ii) division:** $\lfloor x/q \rfloor$ or $\lfloor x / q_i \rfloor$.
2. **Type-(i) multiplication:** $u \cdot v$ with $uv < m$.
3. **Type-(iii) modular subtraction:** $(u-v) \bmod m$.

At the leaves, the multipliers $a_i$ satisfy $a_i y_i < m$, so a direct multiplication suffices.

Hence the entire modular multiplication can be evaluated using at most 12 multiplications/divisions and a bounded number of modular subtractions.

### 5. Example: $a = 62089911, m = 2^{31}-1$

Let

$$
m = 2147483647, \quad a = 62089911.
$$

Compute the Euclidean remainder sequence:

$$
\begin{aligned}
2147483647 &= 34 \cdot 62089911 + 36182673, \\
62089911 &= 1 \cdot 36182673 + 25907238, \\
36182673 &= 1 \cdot 25907238 + 10275435, \\
25907238 &= 2 \cdot 10275435 + 5356368, \\
10275435 &= 1 \cdot 5356368 + 4919067, \\
5356368 &= 1 \cdot 4919067 + 437301, \\
4919067 &= 11 \cdot 437301 + 108756, \\
437301 &= 4 \cdot 108756 + 230277, \\
230277 &= 2 \cdot 108756 + 12765, \\
108756 &= 8 \cdot 12765 + 6636, \\
12765 &= 1 \cdot 6636 + 6129, \\
6636 &= 1 \cdot 6129 + 507, \\
6129 &= 12 \cdot 507 + 45, \\
507 &= 11 \cdot 45 + 12, \\
45 &= 3 \cdot 12 + 9, \\
12 &= 1 \cdot 9 + 3, \\
9 &= 3 \cdot 3.
\end{aligned}
$$

Precompute the coefficients in the decomposition tree for $M_{62089911}(x)$. Then the evaluation proceeds as follows:

1. Compute $x \bmod q_0 = x \bmod 34$ and $\lfloor x / 34 \rfloor$.
2. Compute $M_{a_1}(x \bmod q_0)$ and $M_{a_2}(\lfloor x / q_0 \rfloor)$.
3. Combine with a modular subtraction.
4. Repeat recursively according to the precomputed tree until all multipliers satisfy $uv < m$.

**All operations** are of the permitted types:

- Each division $\lfloor u/v \rfloor$ satisfies $0 < v \le u < m$.
- Each multiplication satisfies $uv < m$.
- Each recombination is a modular subtraction $(u-v) \bmod m$.

By construction, the flattened evaluation tree requires at most 12 multiplications/divisions. The number of modular subtractions is bounded independently of $x$.

### 6. Conclusion

For any fixed $a, m$, the modular multiplication $M_a(x)$ can be evaluated using:

- At most 12 multiplications/divisions of types (i) and (ii);
- A bounded number of modular subtractions (type iii);
- Precomputed constants depending on $a$ and $m$.

The example $a = 62089911, m = 2^{31}-1$ follows exactly the same procedure: compute the remainder sequence, precompute the decomposition tree, and evaluate $M_a(x)$ using only the allowed operations.

This completes the solution. ∎
