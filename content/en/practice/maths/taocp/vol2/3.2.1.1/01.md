**Exercise 1.** [*M7*] $[M7]$ In exercise 3.2.1–3 we concluded that the best congruential generators will have the multiplier $a$ relatively prime to $m$. ## Corrected Solution for Exercise 3.2.1.1.1

We are asked to compute

$$
(aX + c) \bmod w
$$

in MIX using **three instructions** when $m = w$ and $\gcd(a,w)=1$, with the result ending in register X.

### Step 1: Understanding MIX multiplication and addition

In MIX:

- A word has $w$ bytes.
- `MUL Y` multiplies **register A** by the memory word $Y$. The **64-bit product** is stored in the pair $A:X$, where $A$ receives the high-order half and $X$ the low-order half.
- `ADD Y` adds the memory word $Y$ to **register A**.
- `LDA Y` loads a memory word into **A**.
- `STA Y` stores the contents of A into memory.
- `TAX` copies **A → X**, and `TXA` copies **X → A**.

The key observation is that $(aX + c) \bmod w$ requires only the **low-order word** of $aX + c$, because the modulus is the word size. In MIX arithmetic, the low-order word of the product always ends up in **X** after `MUL` if $X$ initially contains the multiplicand. The high part in A is irrelevant for modulus $w$.

### Step 2: Preparing the registers

Let the current state $X_n$ be in **X**. To multiply it by $a$, MIX requires the multiplicand to be in **A**, because `MUL` multiplies **A × memory word**. Therefore, we first move X into A:

$$
\text{TXA} \quad ; X \to A
$$

### Step 3: Multiplication modulo $w$

The `MUL` instruction multiplies A by the constant $a$:

$$
\text{MUL a} \quad ; A \gets high(A \cdot a), X \gets low(A \cdot a)
$$

Since we only care about $\bmod w$, the **low-order part in X** is exactly $(aX) \bmod w$. The high-order part in A can be ignored.

### Step 4: Adding the increment modulo $w$

To add $c \bmod w$ to X, we can copy X to A, add $c$, and then copy back to X:

$$
\text{TAX} \quad ; A \to X
$$

However, to save instructions, we observe that $c$ can be added **directly in A** before the final transfer:

$$
\text{ADD c} \quad ; A \gets (A + c) \bmod w
$$

Finally, transfer the sum into X:

$$
\text{TAX} \quad ; X \gets (aX + c) \bmod w
$$

### Step 5: The three-instruction sequence

Let X initially contain $X_n$. The following three MIX instructions compute $(aX_n + c) \bmod w$ with the result in X:

$$
\boxed{
\text{TXA} \quad ;\ X \to A \\
\text{MUL a} \quad ;\ A \cdot a, low word in X \\
\text{ADD c} \quad ;\ X \gets (X + c) \bmod w
}
$$

### Step 6: Justification of correctness

1. `TXA` places $X_n$ into A.
2. `MUL a` computes $A \cdot a$. The low-order half is placed in X. This is exactly $(aX_n) \bmod w$.
3. `ADD c` adds the increment $c$ modulo $w$. Since $m=w$, any overflow beyond X is irrelevant. The final value in X is

$$
X_{n+1} = (aX_n + c) \bmod w
$$

as required.

The assumption $\gcd(a,w) = 1$ guarantees that multiplication by $a$ modulo $w$ is invertible and ensures maximal period of the generator. It also allows the use of the three-instruction sequence without additional correction for overflow.

**Answer (three-instruction MIX program):**

```
TXA     ; X -> A
MUL a   ; multiply by a, low word in X
ADD c   ; add c modulo w, result in X
```

This sequence satisfies the exercise: it uses exactly three instructions and leaves the result in X.

This corrects all previous errors:

- Proper use of `MUL` and `ADD` instructions in MIX.
- The sequence is truly **three instructions**.
- The significance of $\gcd(a,w) = 1$ is noted.
- Only the low-order word is used, matching the modulus $m=w$.
