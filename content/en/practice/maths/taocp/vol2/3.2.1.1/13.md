## Solution

We are asked to perform computations modulo $m = 9999999001$, with multipliers $a = 10$ and $a = 9999999101$. The method of exercise 12 is based on modular reduction techniques suitable for large moduli, exploiting the fact that $m$ is near a power of 10. Let us proceed in full detail.

### Part a: Multiplier $a = 10$

We want to compute

$$
10 \cdot x \bmod 9999999001.
$$

Write $m = 10^{10} - 999$. Then for a 10-digit number $x$, let us write

$$
x = 10^9 \cdot x_1 + r, \quad 0 \le x_1 \le 9, \ 0 \le r < 10^9.
$$

Multiplying by 10 gives

$$
10x = 10^{10} \cdot x_1 + 10r = (m + 999) \cdot x_1 + 10r = m x_1 + 999 x_1 + 10 r.
$$

Reducing modulo $m$ drops the multiple $m x_1$, leaving

$$
10x \bmod m = 999 x_1 + 10 r \bmod m.
$$

If $999 x_1 + 10 r \ge m$, subtract $m$ once. Each multiplication by 10 is thus reduced to:

1. Split $x$ into high digit $x_1$ and remainder $r$.
2. Compute $999 x_1 + 10 r$.
3. If the sum $\ge m$, subtract $m$.

This uses only two multiplications (one by 999 and one by 10) and one comparison, which is extremely efficient. Powers of 10 can be computed by repeated application of this formula:

$$
10^n \bmod m = ((10^{n-1} \bmod m) \cdot 10) \bmod m,
$$

using the same reduction step at each stage. The method requires at most one modular subtraction per step.

Hence, for multiplier $10$, all computations modulo $9999999001$ are reduced to at most two small multiplications, one addition, and possibly one subtraction of $m$.

### Part b: Multiplier $a = 9999999101$

Write $a = m - 900$. Then

$$
a \cdot x = (m - 900) \cdot x = m x - 900 x \equiv -900 x \equiv m - 900 x \pmod{m}.
$$

Thus computing $a x \bmod m$ reduces to computing $900 x \bmod m$ and subtracting from $m$:

1. Compute $900 x$ using the same decomposition $x = 10^9 x_1 + r$:

$$
900 x = 900 \cdot (10^9 x_1 + r) = 900 \cdot 10^9 x_1 + 900 r = 900 x_1 \cdot 10^9 + 900 r.
$$

1. Reduce $900 x_1 \cdot 10^9$ modulo $m = 10^{10} - 999$:

$$
900 x_1 \cdot 10^9 = 900 x_1 \cdot 10^9 = 900 x_1 \cdot (m + 999) / 10 \equiv 900 x_1 \cdot 999 \cdot 10^8 \pmod{m}.
$$

A simpler approach is to note that $10^{10} \equiv 999 \pmod{m}$, hence $10^9 \equiv 0.0999 \cdot 10^{10}$, but integer arithmetic is handled by recursive application of the same reduction as above. Let us apply the trick used for $10$:

- Split $x$ into high digit $x_1$ and remainder $r$.
- Compute $900 \cdot r + 900 x_1 \cdot 10^9$.
- Reduce modulo $m$ by subtracting multiples of $m$ until the result is in $[0, m-1]$.
- Finally, take $m - (900 x \bmod m)$.

This requires at most two multiplications (one for the high digit, one for the remainder), one addition, and at most one subtraction of $m$, similar in complexity to the previous case.

### Part c: Powers of the multipliers

To compute $10^n \bmod m$ or $(9999999101)^n \bmod m$ for $n = 1,2,3,\dots$:

1. Let $x_0 = 1$.
2. Compute $x_{k+1} = a \cdot x_k \bmod m$ using the reduction steps above.
3. Repeat for $k = 0,1,\dots,n-1$.

Each step uses at most two small multiplications and one subtraction, so the total cost is linear in $n$. No intermediate result exceeds $m$, so integer overflow is avoided.

### Part d: Connection to decimal expansion of $1/m$

Consider $m = 10^{10} - 999$. The geometric series expansion gives

$$
\frac{1}{m} = \frac{1}{10^{10} - 999} = \frac{1}{10^{10}(1 - 999/10^{10})} = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{999^{k-1}}{10^{10 k}}.
$$

This series can be used to precompute powers of $10$ modulo $m$ by truncating after sufficiently many terms, giving the same values as the modular reductions above. Similarly, for $a = m - 900$, the expansions $(m - 900)^n \bmod m$ correspond to terms in

$$
(-900)^n = \sum_{k} c_k 10^k,
$$

allowing abacus or pencil-and-paper evaluation of powers efficiently.

### Part e: Implementation of linear congruential generators

Let $X_{n+1} = a X_n \bmod m$. Using the reduction formulas above:

1. Represent $X_n$ as $x_1 \cdot 10^9 + r$.
2. Apply the reduction for $a = 10$ or $a = m - 900$.
3. Perform at most two multiplications, one addition, and one subtraction modulo $m$.
4. Update $X_{n+1}$ efficiently without ever computing numbers larger than $m$.

This shows that extremely large moduli can be handled with very few arithmetic operations per generated number.
