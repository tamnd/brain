**Exercise 10.** [*M26*] The solution to exercise 9(b) sometimes works also when $a^2 > m$. Exactly how many multipliers $a$ are there for which the intermediate results in that method never exceed $m$, for all $x$ between $0$ and $m$?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 13m19s  

---

## Setup

Let $m$ be a positive integer modulus. We consider the computation of the linear congruential product $ax \bmod m$, for integer $x$ satisfying $0 \le x \le m$. Exercise 3.2.1.1.9(b) provides an efficient method to compute $ax \bmod m$ without exceeding $m$ in intermediate values when $a^2 \le m$. Here we are asked to determine exactly how many integers $a$ satisfy the condition that the method of exercise 9(b) produces no intermediate result larger than $m$, **even when $a^2 > m**$.

Define the quotient

$$
q = \lfloor m/a \rfloor
$$

and the remainder

$$
r = m \bmod a = m - qa.
$$

By exercise 9(a), for any integer $x$,

$$
a(x - (x \bmod q)) \equiv \lfloor x/q \rfloor (m - r) \pmod m.
$$

The method of 9(b) computes

$$
ax \bmod m = a(x \bmod q) - \lfloor x/q \rfloor r,
$$

adjusting by adding $m$ if necessary to keep the result nonnegative. To avoid any intermediate result exceeding $m$, it is required that both terms satisfy

$$
0 \le a(x \bmod q) \le m, \quad 0 \le \lfloor x/q \rfloor r \le m,
$$

for all $0 \le x \le m$.

Our task is therefore to find all integers $a$, $1 \le a \le m$, such that the maximum of $a(x \bmod q)$ and $\lfloor x/q \rfloor r$ over $0 \le x \le m$ does not exceed $m$, even though $a^2 > m$.

## Solution

Consider first the term $a(x \bmod q)$. The maximum value of $x \bmod q$ is $q - 1$, so the maximum of $a(x \bmod q)$ is

$$
a(q - 1) = a(\lfloor m/a \rfloor - 1) = aq - a.
$$

Since $q = \lfloor m/a \rfloor$, we have

$$
aq \le a \cdot \frac{m}{a} = m,
$$

and therefore

$$
a(q - 1) = aq - a \le m - a.
$$

Thus the first term never exceeds $m$ if and only if

$$
m - a \le m \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad a \ge 0,
$$

which is always satisfied for positive $a$.

Next, consider the second term, $\lfloor x/q \rfloor r$. The maximum occurs when $\lfloor x/q \rfloor$ is maximal. The maximal quotient is

$$
\lfloor m/q \rfloor.
$$

But by definition, $q = \lfloor m/a \rfloor$, hence

$$
m/q \le a + 1.
$$

Since $\lfloor m/q \rfloor \le a$, the maximum of $\lfloor x/q \rfloor r$ is

$$
\lfloor m/q \rfloor r \le a r.
$$

Recall that $r = m - aq < a$. Therefore

$$
\lfloor x/q \rfloor r \le a r < a^2.
$$

To guarantee that $a r \le m$, it is necessary that

$$
a r \le m.
$$

Substituting $r = m - aq$ gives

$$
a(m - aq) \le m \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad a(m - a\lfloor m/a \rfloor) \le m.
$$

Let $m = a q + r$, $0 \le r < a$. Then

$$
a r \le m \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad a r \le a q + r \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad (a - 1) r \le a q.
$$

Since $q = \lfloor m/a \rfloor$ and $r = m - a q$, the inequality becomes

$$
(a - 1)(m - a q) \le a q \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad (a - 1) m - a(a - 1)q \le a q \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad (a - 1) m \le a^2 q \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad (a - 1) m \le a^2 \lfloor m/a \rfloor.
$$

Write $m = a q + r$ again. Then

$$
(a - 1)(a q + r) \le a^2 q \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad (a - 1)a q + (a - 1) r \le a^2 q \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad (a - 1) r \le a q,
$$

which reproduces the previous inequality. Since $r < a$, the left-hand side satisfies

$$
(a - 1) r < (a - 1) a.
$$

Thus $(a - 1) r \le a q$ can hold only if $a q \ge (a - 1) r \ge (a - 1)(0) = 0$, which is always true. To determine the values of $a$ for which $a^2 > m$ but the inequality still holds, write

$$
(a - 1) r \le a q \quad \text{with } r = m - a q.
$$

Substitute $r = m - a q$:

$$
(a - 1)(m - a q) \le a q \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad (a - 1) m - a(a - 1) q \le a q \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad (a - 1) m \le a^2 q \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad (a^2 - a) q \ge (a - 1) m.
$$

Dividing both sides by $a - 1$ (positive) gives

$$
(a + 1) q \ge m.
$$

But $q = \lfloor m/a \rfloor$, so $(a + 1) \lfloor m/a \rfloor \ge m$. Let $m = k a + s$ with $0 \le s < a$, then $q = \lfloor m/a \rfloor = k$, and the inequality becomes

$$
(a + 1) k \ge k a + s \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad k \ge s.
$$

Since $s < a$ and $k = \lfloor m/a \rfloor$, the only way $k \ge s$ can hold for all $s$ is if $k = 1$ (i.e., $a \ge m/2$). Then $s = m - a$, and the inequality reduces to $1 \ge m - a \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad a \ge m - 1$.

Thus the only integers $a$ for which $a^2 > m$ but the intermediate results never exceed $m$ are

$$
a = m - 1, \quad a = m.
$$

However, $a = m$ is excluded because $a < m$ in exercise 9. Therefore the unique multiplier is

$$
\boxed{a = m - 1}.
$$

Hence there is exactly **one** such multiplier.

## Verification

Let $a = m - 1$. Then $q = \lfloor m/a \rfloor = 1$, $r = m - aq = m - (m - 1) \cdot 1 = 1$. The intermediate terms in the method of 9(b) are $a(x \bmod q) = (m - 1)(x \bmod 1) = 0$, and $\lfloor x/q \rfloor r = x \cdot 1 = x \le m$. Therefore all intermediate values are bounded by $m$. For any $a > m - 1$, $a = m$ is forbidden by the exercise constraints, and for any smaller $a$ with $a^2 > m$, the inequality $(a - 1) r \le a q$ fails for some $x$, as previously derived. This confirms the conclusion.

## Notes

The key observation is that the intermediate-product bound $m$ is exceeded precisely when $a$ grows beyond $m/2$, except in the extremal case $a = m - 1$, where $q = 1$ and $r = 1$ so that the multiplication by $x \bmod q$ is trivial. This case corresponds to the so-called "inverse modulus" multiplier often used in linear congruential sequences of maximal period with $c = 0$.

This completes the proof.

∎

**Answer:**

$$
\boxed{1}
$$
