**Exercise 3.2.1.1.14** [_M35_] Generalize the ideas of the previous two exercises, obtaining a large family of linear congruential generators with extremely large moduli.

**Solution:**

A **linear congruential generator (LCG)** produces a sequence of integers $x_0, x_1, x_2, \dots$ according to the recurrence

$$
x_{n+1} \equiv a x_n + c \pmod m,
$$

where $m$ is the modulus, $a$ is the multiplier, $c$ is the increment, and $x_0$ is the seed. The period of the sequence depends on the choice of $a$, $c$, and $m$.

To construct a **large family of LCGs with extremely large moduli**, it is necessary to generalize the constructions from the previous exercises, which considered:

1. **Moduli that are powers of small primes**, such as $m = 2^e$, allowing large moduli with efficient arithmetic.
2. **Moduli that are products of distinct primes**, such as $m = p_1 p_2 \cdots p_k$, giving theoretically analyzable periods.

The key to ensuring a **maximal period** for an LCG with modulus $m$ and increment $c \neq 0$ is given by the **Hull–Dobell theorem**. This theorem states that the LCG has full period $m$ if and only if:

1. $\gcd(c, m) = 1$.
2. $a-1$ is divisible by all prime factors of $m$.
3. If $4 \mid m$, then $4 \mid (a-1)$.

The previous flawed solution omitted the third condition, which is essential whenever $m$ is divisible by a power of 2 greater than 2.

### Step 1: Choose a large modulus $m$

Let $p_1, p_2, \dots, p_r$ be distinct primes, and let $e_1, e_2, \dots, e_r \ge 1$ be integers. Define

$$
m = \prod_{i=1}^r p_i^{e_i}.
$$

This generalizes the earlier constructions: if $r = 1$ and $p_1 = 2$, it reproduces a power-of-two modulus. If $e_i = 1$ for all $i$, it reproduces a product-of-primes modulus.

### Step 2: Construct the multiplier $a$

To satisfy the Hull–Dobell conditions, $a$ must be chosen carefully with respect to each prime power $p_i^{e_i}$ dividing $m$.

- For each odd prime $p_i$, choose $a$ such that

$$
a \equiv 1 \pmod{p_i}.
$$

- If $m$ is divisible by 4 (i.e., $e_1 \ge 2$ for $p_1 = 2$), also require

$$
a \equiv 1 \pmod 4.
$$

A systematic construction is:

$$
a = 1 + k \cdot \mathrm{lcm}\Big(4^{\delta_2} \prod_{p_i \text{ odd}} p_i \Big),
$$

where $\delta_2 = 1$ if $2 \mid m$ and $e_1 \ge 2$, and $\delta_2 = 0$ otherwise, and $k$ is an integer chosen so that $1 \le k < m$ and $\gcd(a, m) = 1$. This guarantees that $a-1$ is divisible by all prime factors of $m$ and satisfies the condition for modulus divisible by 4.

This construction can be further refined modulo each prime power using **Chinese remainder theorem** to control residues modulo $p_i^{e_i}$, ensuring maximal period for general composite moduli.

### Step 3: Choose the increment $c$

To satisfy the first Hull–Dobell condition, select $c$ such that

$$
\gcd(c, m) = 1.
$$

Any integer $c$ relatively prime to $m$ works. Typical choices include $c = 1$ or other small integers.
