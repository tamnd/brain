[Section 3.2.1.1: Choice of Modulus](../)

**Exercise 12.** &#9654; [*M28*] Consider computations by pencil and paper or an abacus.

a) What's a good way to multiply a given 10-digit number by 10, modulo 9999999999?
b) Same question, but multiply instead by 9999999900 (modulo 9999999999).
c) Explain how to compute the powers $999999900^n \bmod 9999999999$, for $n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$
d) Relate such computations to the decimal expansion of $1/9999999999$.
e) Show that these ideas make it possible to implement certain kinds of linear congruential generators that have extremely large moduli, using only a few operations per generated number.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 19m45s

## Setup

Let

$$
m=9999999999=10^{10}-1.
$$

A $10$-digit number will be written as

$$
x=d_9d_8\cdots d_1d_0
 =\sum_{k=0}^{9} d_k10^k,
\qquad 0\le d_k\le 9.
$$

Since

$$
10^{10}\equiv 1 \pmod m,
$$

multiplication by powers of $10$ modulo $m$ can be performed by cyclic movement of decimal digits. The exercise asks for efficient pencil-and-paper methods based on this congruence.

## Solution

### (a)

Because

$$
10^{10}\equiv 1 \pmod m,
$$

we have

$$
10x
 =d_9\,10^{10}+\sum_{k=0}^{8} d_k10^{k+1}
 \equiv d_9+\sum_{k=0}^{8} d_k10^{k+1}
 \pmod m.
$$

Thus multiplication by $10$ modulo $m$ moves every digit one position to the left and transfers the leading digit to the units position.

If

$$
x=d_9d_8\cdots d_1d_0,
$$

then

$$
10x \bmod m
=
d_8d_7\cdots d_1d_0d_9.
$$

Hence the operation is a cyclic left shift of the ten decimal digits.

Therefore

$$
\boxed{\text{multiply by }10\text{ modulo }9999999999
\text{ by a cyclic left shift of the digits}.}
$$

### (b)

Since

$$
9999999900
=10^{10}-100,
$$

and $10^{10}\equiv1\pmod m$,

$$
9999999900\equiv 1-100=-99\pmod m.
$$

Therefore

$$
9999999900\,x\equiv x-100x\pmod m.
$$

Now

$$
100x\pmod m
$$

is obtained by a cyclic left shift of two decimal positions. Writing

$$
x=d_9d_8\cdots d_1d_0,
$$

we have

$$
100x\equiv d_7d_6\cdots d_0d_9d_8\pmod m.
$$

Hence multiplication by $9999999900$ reduces to subtracting this two-place cyclic shift from $x$, modulo $m$.

Equivalently,

$$
9999999900\,x\equiv -99x\pmod m,
$$

so the computation requires one cyclic shift and a subtraction.

### (c)

Let

$$
a=999999900=10^{10}-100= m-99.
$$

Hence

$$
a\equiv -99 \pmod m.
$$

Therefore

$$
a^n\equiv (-99)^n \pmod m.
$$

Since

$$
99^n=(100-1)^n,
$$

multiplication by $99$ modulo $m$ is obtained from

$$
99x\equiv 100x-x\pmod m,
$$

where $100x\bmod m$ is merely a two-digit cyclic shift.

Consequently the sequence

$$
a^n\bmod m
$$

may be generated recursively from

$$
P_1=a,
\qquad
P_{n+1}\equiv -99\,P_n \pmod m,
$$

using only a two-digit cyclic shift and a subtraction at each step.

A more explicit description follows from the congruence

$$
100^5=10^{10}\equiv1\pmod m.
$$

Thus

$$
99\equiv100-1,
$$

and arithmetic modulo $m$ naturally decomposes into five blocks of two decimal digits. Writing

$$
x=b_4b_3b_2b_1b_0,
\qquad 0\le b_j<100,
$$

multiplication by $100$ becomes a cyclic shift of the five blocks, and multiplication by $99$ becomes "cyclic shift minus identity." Hence all powers of $a$ can be generated with a constant amount of work per power.

### (d)

Since

$$
\frac1{10^{10}-1}
=
\frac1{10^{10}}\,
\frac1{1-10^{-10}},
$$

the geometric-series expansion gives

$$
\frac1{9999999999}
=
10^{-10}+10^{-20}+10^{-30}+\cdots.
$$

Therefore

$$
\frac1{9999999999}
=
0.0000000001\,0000000001\,0000000001\cdots.
$$

The decimal expansion repeats with period $10$ because multiplication by $10$ modulo $10^{10}-1$ is a cyclic permutation of the ten digit positions. More generally, the cyclic digit shifts appearing in parts (a) and (c) are manifestations of the identity

$$
10^{10}\equiv1\pmod{10^{10}-1},
$$

which is exactly the congruence underlying the repeating decimal expansion above.

### (e)

Let

$$
m=10^N-1.
$$

Represent a residue modulo $m$ by its $N$ decimal digits. If the multiplier $a$ is a sparse linear combination of powers of $10$, for example

$$
a=\pm10^{r_1}\pm10^{r_2}\pm\cdots\pm10^{r_t},
$$

with small fixed $t$, then

$$
ax\bmod m
$$

is obtained by adding and subtracting a fixed number of cyclic digit shifts, because

$$
10^N\equiv1\pmod m.
$$

The amount of work is independent of the size of $m$; only the number of nonzero terms in the representation of $a$ matters.

Linear congruential generators

$$
X_{n+1}\equiv aX_n+c\pmod m
$$

can therefore be implemented for extremely large values of $m=10^N-1$ by storing the digits of $X_n$ and replacing general multiplication by a few cyclic shifts together with a few additions or subtractions. The cost per generated number remains bounded even when $N$ is very large.

## Verification

For part (a),

$$
10^{10}\equiv1\pmod{9999999999}
$$

implies

$$
10(d_9\cdot10^9)
=d_9\cdot10^{10}
\equiv d_9,
$$

while every other digit moves one place left. Hence the cyclic-shift rule is correct.

For part (b),

$$
9999999900+99
=9999999999=m,
$$

so

$$
9999999900\equiv-99\pmod m.
$$

Also,

$$
100x\bmod m
$$

is a two-place cyclic shift because

$$
100=10^2.
$$

For part (c),

$$
100^5=10^{10}\equiv1\pmod m,
$$

which confirms that arithmetic may be organized in five two-digit blocks.

For part (d),

$$
(10^{10}-1)\sum_{k\ge1}10^{-10k}
=
1,
$$

so the stated repeating decimal is exactly $1/(10^{10}-1)$.

For part (e), every term $10^{r_i}x\bmod m$ is a cyclic shift of the digits of $x$, since powers differing by multiples of $N$ are congruent modulo $m$. Therefore sparse multipliers require only a few shifts and additions.

## Notes

The same ideas apply with any base $b$ and modulus

$$
m=b^N-1.
$$

Then

$$
b^N\equiv1\pmod m,
$$

so multiplication by $b^r$ modulo $m$ is a cyclic shift of $N$ base-$b$ digits. This provides a practical way to construct linear congruential generators with very large moduli when the multiplier has a sparse representation in base $b$.
