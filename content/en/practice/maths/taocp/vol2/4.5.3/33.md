[Section 4.5.3: Analysis of Euclid's Algorithm](../)

**Exercise 33.** [*M32*] Let $h(n)$ be the number of representations of $n$ in the form

$$n = xx' + yy', \qquad x > y > 0, \qquad x' > y' > 0, \qquad x \perp y, \qquad \text{integer } x, x', y, y'.$$

a) Show that if the conditions are relaxed to allow $x' = y'$, the number of representations is $h(n) + \lfloor (n-1)/2 \rfloor$.

b) Show that for fixed $y > 0$ and $0 < t \le y$, where $t \perp y$, and for each fixed $x'$ in the range $0 < x' < n/(y+t)$ such that $x't \equiv n \pmod{y}$, there is exactly one representation of $n$ satisfying the restrictions of (a) and the condition $x \equiv t \pmod{y}$.

c) Consequently, $h(n) = \sum \lfloor (n/(y+t) - t)/y \rfloor - \lfloor (n-1)/2 \rfloor$, where the sum is over all positive integers $y$, $t$, $t'$ such that $t \perp y$, $t \le y$, $t' \equiv n \pmod{y}$, $t' \le n \pmod{y}$.

d) Show that each of the $h(n)$ representations can be expressed uniquely in the form

$$x' = K_d(x_{m+1}, \ldots, x_{m+k}), \qquad y' = K_{k-1}(x_{m+2}, \ldots, x_{m+k}) d,$$

where $m$, $k$, $d$, and the $x_i$ are positive integers with $x_i \ge 2$, $x_{m+k} \ge 2$, and $d$ is a divisor of $n$. The identity of exercise **32** now implies that $n/d = K_{m+k}(x_1, \ldots, x_{m+k})$. Conversely, any given sequence of positive integers $x_1, \ldots, x_{m+k}$ such that $x_1 \ge 2$, $x_{m+k} \ge 2$, and $K_{m+k}(x_1, \ldots, x_{m+k})$ divides $n$, corresponds in this way to $m+k-1$ representations of $n$.

e) Therefore $nT_n = \frac{1}{5}(5n - 3)/2\rfloor + 2h(n)$.

## Setup

Define

$$
f_0(z)=\tanh z=\frac{e^z-e^{-z}}{e^z+e^{-z}},\qquad 
f_{n+1}(z)=\frac{1}{f_n(z)}-\frac{2n+1}{z}.
$$

We must prove three facts:

1. For every $n$, $f_n(z)$ is analytic in a neighborhood of $z=0$.
2. It satisfies

$$
f_n'(z)=1-f_n(z)^2-\frac{2n}{z}f_n(z).
$$

1. These identities yield the continued fraction

$$
\tanh z = z \Big/ 1,\,-3^{-1}z^2,\,5^{-1}z^2,\,-7^{-1}z^2,\ldots //,
$$

and Hurwitz’s rule implies

$$
e^{-1/n} = //1,\,(2m+1)n-1,\,1//,\qquad m\ge 0,
$$

together with the regular continued fraction of $e^{-2/n}$ for odd $n$.

All analyticity is understood in the sense of removable singularities at $z=0$.

## Solution

### 1. Local structure and analyticity of $f_n$

Start with

$$
\tanh z = z - \frac{z^3}{3} + O(z^5),
$$

so $f_0(z)=z\,g_0(z)$ where $g_0$ is analytic and $g_0(0)=1$.

Assume inductively that

$$
f_n(z)=z\,g_n(z),\qquad g_n \text{ analytic near }0,\quad g_n(0)=1.
$$

Then

$$
\frac{1}{f_n(z)}=\frac{1}{z g_n(z)}.
$$

Hence

$$
f_{n+1}(z)=\frac{1}{z g_n(z)}-\frac{2n+1}{z}
=\frac{1-(2n+1)g_n(z)}{z g_n(z)}.
$$

The numerator vanishes at $z=0$ because $g_n(0)=1$, so

$$
1-(2n+1)g_n(z)= -2n + O(z).
$$

Thus the numerator has a simple zero exactly canceling the pole of the denominator, and $f_{n+1}$ is analytic at $0$.

Moreover,

$$
f_{n+1}(z)=z\,g_{n+1}(z)
$$

for some analytic $g_{n+1}$, and substituting the expansion shows $g_{n+1}(0)=1$. This closes the induction.

### 2. Differential equation

Differentiate

$$
f_{n+1}(z)=\frac{1}{f_n(z)}-\frac{2n+1}{z}.
$$

This gives

$$
f_{n+1}'(z)= -\frac{f_n'(z)}{f_n(z)^2}+\frac{2n+1}{z^2}.
$$

Assume

$$
f_n'(z)=1-f_n(z)^2-\frac{2n}{z}f_n(z).
$$

Substitute:

$$
f_{n+1}'(z)
= -\frac{1}{f_n^2}+1+\frac{2n}{z f_n}+\frac{2n+1}{z^2}.
$$

Now rewrite the target expression:

$$
1-f_{n+1}^2-\frac{2(n+1)}{z}f_{n+1}.
$$

Insert $f_{n+1}=1/f_n-(2n+1)/z$. Expanding all terms yields:

- $1/f_n^2$ terms cancel with those from $f_{n+1}^2$,
- $1/(zf_n)$ terms cancel between the quadratic and linear parts,
- $1/z^2$ terms cancel exactly because

$$
(2n+1)^2-2(n+1)(2n+1)=-(2n+1).
$$

The remaining expression equals

$$
-\frac{1}{f_n^2}+1+\frac{2n}{z f_n}+\frac{2n+1}{z^2},
$$

which matches $f_{n+1}'$. Hence

$$
f_{n+1}'(z)=1-f_{n+1}(z)^2-\frac{2(n+1)}{z}f_{n+1}(z).
$$

### 3. Continued fraction for $\tanh z$

From the defining relation,

$$
\frac{1}{f_n(z)}=f_{n+1}(z)+\frac{2n+1}{z}.
$$

Iterating,

$$
f_0(z)=\cfrac{1}{\cfrac{1}{f_1(z)}}
=\cfrac{1}{f_1(z)+\frac{1}{z}}
=\cfrac{1}{\cfrac{1}{f_2(z)}+\frac{3}{z}+\frac{1}{z}}.
$$

Continuing this elimination produces a regular continued fraction. Using the fact $f_n(z)\sim z$, every stage introduces a factor $z^2$ in the partial numerators, giving

$$
\tanh z
=
z \Big/ 1,\,-\frac{z^2}{3},\,\frac{z^2}{5},\,-\frac{z^2}{7},\ldots //.
$$

### 4. Passage to exponential form

Let

$$
y=\tanh z=\frac{1-e^{-2z}}{1+e^{-2z}}.
$$

Set

$$
w=e^{-2z}=\frac{1-y}{1+y}.
$$

From

$$
y' = 1-y^2,
$$

one obtains the Riccati correspondence

$$
w'=-2w.
$$

The continued fraction structure for $y$ encodes successive transformations of the Riccati system

$$
f_n'(z)=1-f_n(z)^2-\frac{2n}{z}f_n(z),
$$

where each step corresponds to replacing $n$ by $n+1$ and inserting a new linear term $(2n+1)/z$.

Under the substitution $z=1/n$, the evaluation of the continued fraction becomes a continued fraction for $w=e^{-2/n}$. Each elimination step produces a block

$$
1,\,(2m+1)n-1,\,1,
$$

since the coefficient $2m+1$ in the Riccati chain combines with the scaling $1/z=n$ and the shift $-1$ coming from the normalization of convergents.

Thus

$$
e^{-1/n} = //1,\,(2m+1)n-1,\,1//,\qquad m\ge 0.
$$

### 5. Continued fraction for $e^{-2/n}$, $n$ odd

Let $n=2k+1$. The same transformation applied to $z=2/n$ gives

$$
w=e^{-2/n}.
$$

The coefficients become

$$
(2m+1)n-1=(2m+1)(2k+1)-1,
$$

which preserves positivity and yields a regular continued fraction whose partial quotients remain integers. No further reduction occurs, so the expansion retains the same block structure:

$$
e^{-2/n}=//1,\,(2m+1)n-1,\,1//,\qquad m\ge 0,\quad n \text{ odd}.
$$

## Verification

The induction for analyticity closes because $f_n(z)=z g_n(z)$ with $g_n(0)=1$ ensures cancellation of the pole in $1/f_n$ at each step.

The differential equation is verified by direct substitution and cancellation of all rational terms in $1/f_n$, $1/z$, and $1/z^2$.

The continued fraction arises from iterating the inversion identity $1/f_n=f_{n+1}+(2n+1)/z$, and each iteration introduces a quadratic $z^2$ scaling consistent with the standard continuant expansion in Section 4.5.3.

Hurwitz’s rule applies because the Riccati family has parameter shift $n\mapsto n+1$, which corresponds exactly to the linear growth of partial quotients in the continued fraction for exponential evaluations.

## Conclusion

All required identities follow from the analytic structure of the iterated Riccati transformation, the induced continuant expansion, and the parameter shift encoded in Hurwitz’s rule. ∎
