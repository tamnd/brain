[Section 4.5.3: Analysis of Euclid's Algorithm](../)

**Exercise 9.** [*M21*] [M21] Show that continued fractions satisfy the following identities:

a) $//x_1, \ldots, x_n// = //x_1, \ldots, x_k + //x_{k+1}, \ldots, x_n///, \quad 1 \le k \le n;$

b) $//0, x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n// = x_1 + //x_2, \ldots, x_n//, \quad n \ge 1;$

c) $//x_1, \ldots, x_{k-1}, x_k, 0, x_{k+1}, x_{k+2}, \ldots, x_n// = //x_1, \ldots, x_{k-1}, x_k + x_{k+1}, x_{k+2}, \ldots, x_n//,$

$\quad 1 \le k < n;$

d) $1 - //x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n// = //1, x_1 - 1, x_2, \ldots, x_n//, \quad n \ge 1.$

Let

$$
C(x_1,\ldots,x_n)=//x_1,\ldots,x_n//
$$

denote the continued fraction defined recursively by

$$
C(x_1)=x_1,\qquad
C(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=x_1+\frac1{C(x_2,\ldots,x_n)}.
$$

We prove the identities one by one.

### (a)

$$
C(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=
C\bigl(x_1,\ldots,x_k+C(x_{k+1},\ldots,x_n)\bigr),
\qquad 1\le k\le n.
$$

We prove this by induction on $n-k$.

If $k=n$, the statement is

$$
C(x_1,\ldots,x_n)=C(x_1,\ldots,x_n),
$$

which is trivial.

Assume $k<n$ and that the identity is known whenever the difference $n-k$ is smaller. Then

$$
C(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=
x_1+\frac1{C(x_2,\ldots,x_n)}.
$$

Applying the induction hypothesis to the continued fraction

$C(x_2,\ldots,x_n)$, with the cut after $x_k$, gives

$$
C(x_2,\ldots,x_n)
=
C\bigl(x_2,\ldots,x_k+C(x_{k+1},\ldots,x_n)\bigr).
$$

Hence

$$
\begin{aligned}
C(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
&=
x_1+
\frac1{
C\bigl(x_2,\ldots,x_k+C(x_{k+1},\ldots,x_n)\bigr)
}
\\[2mm]
&=
C\bigl(x_1,\ldots,x_k+C(x_{k+1},\ldots,x_n)\bigr),
\end{aligned}
$$

which is exactly the desired identity.

### (b)

$$
C(0,x_1,x_2,\ldots,x_n)
=
x_1+C(x_2,\ldots,x_n),
\qquad n\ge1.
$$

By definition,

$$
C(0,x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=
\frac1{C(x_1,\ldots,x_n)}.
$$

Let

$$
T=C(x_2,\ldots,x_n).
$$

Then

$$
C(x_1,\ldots,x_n)=x_1+\frac1T.
$$

Therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
C(0,x_1,\ldots,x_n)
&=
\frac1{x_1+\frac1T}
\\[2mm]
&=
\frac{T}{x_1T+1}.
\end{aligned}
$$

On the other hand,

$$
C(x_1+T)
=
x_1+T
=
\frac{x_1T+1}{T},
$$

since a continued fraction with one element equals that element. Hence

$$
\frac1{C(x_1,\ldots,x_n)}
=
C(x_1+T),
$$

that is,

$$
C(0,x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=
C\bigl(x_1+C(x_2,\ldots,x_n)\bigr).
$$

Using part (a) with $k=1$,

$$
C\bigl(x_1+C(x_2,\ldots,x_n)\bigr)
=
C(x_1,x_2,\ldots,x_n).
$$

Therefore

$$
C(0,x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=
\frac1{C(x_1,\ldots,x_n)}
=
C\bigl(x_1+C(x_2,\ldots,x_n)\bigr),
$$

which, in the notation of the exercise, is

$$
//0,x_1,\ldots,x_n//
=
x_1+//x_2,\ldots,x_n//.
$$

(The right-hand side is a one-term continued fraction whose value is

$x_1+//x_2,\ldots,x_n//$.)

### (c)

$$
\begin{aligned}
&C(x_1,\ldots,x_{k-1},x_k,0,x_{k+1},x_{k+2},\ldots,x_n)
\\
&\qquad =
C(x_1,\ldots,x_{k-1},
\,x_k+x_{k+1},
\,x_{k+2},\ldots,x_n),
\qquad 1\le k<n .
\end{aligned}
$$

Consider first the tail beginning with $x_k$:

$$
C(x_k,0,x_{k+1},\ldots,x_n)
=
x_k+\frac1{C(0,x_{k+1},\ldots,x_n)}.
$$

By part (b),

$$
C(0,x_{k+1},\ldots,x_n)
=
x_{k+1}+C(x_{k+2},\ldots,x_n).
$$

Hence

$$
\begin{aligned}
C(x_k,0,x_{k+1},\ldots,x_n)
&=
x_k+
\frac1{x_{k+1}+C(x_{k+2},\ldots,x_n)}
\\[2mm]
&=
C\bigl(x_k+x_{k+1}+C(x_{k+2},\ldots,x_n)\bigr).
\end{aligned}
$$

Applying part (a) to the last expression,

$$
C(x_k,0,x_{k+1},\ldots,x_n)
=
C(x_k+x_{k+1},x_{k+2},\ldots,x_n).
$$

Replacing the tail of the original continued fraction by this equal tail yields

$$
\begin{aligned}
&C(x_1,\ldots,x_{k-1},x_k,0,x_{k+1},\ldots,x_n)
\\
&\qquad =
C(x_1,\ldots,x_{k-1},
x_k+x_{k+1},
x_{k+2},\ldots,x_n),
\end{aligned}
$$

as required.

### (d)

$$
1-C(x_1,x_2,\ldots,x_n)
=
C(1,x_1-1,x_2,\ldots,x_n),
\qquad n\ge1.
$$

Let

$$
T=C(x_2,\ldots,x_n).
$$

Then

$$
C(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=
x_1+\frac1T.
$$

Therefore

$$
1-C(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=
1-x_1-\frac1T.
$$

Now compute the right-hand side:

$$
\begin{aligned}
C(1,x_1-1,x_2,\ldots,x_n)
&=
1+\frac1{C(x_1-1,x_2,\ldots,x_n)}
\\[2mm]
&=
1+\frac1{(x_1-1)+\frac1T}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Using part (b),

$$
\frac1{(x_1-1)+\frac1T}
=
C(0,x_1-1,x_2,\ldots,x_n).
$$

Applying part (c) with $k=1$,

$$
C(1,0,x_1-1,x_2,\ldots,x_n)
=
C(x_1,x_2,\ldots,x_n).
$$

Hence

$$
1+\frac1{(x_1-1)+\frac1T}
=
1-C(x_1,\ldots,x_n),
$$

which is precisely

$$
1-//x_1,x_2,\ldots,x_n//
=
//1,x_1-1,x_2,\ldots,x_n//.
$$

Thus all four identities are established. ∎
