**Exercise 6.** [*M23*] [M23] Prove that the regular continued fraction expansion of a number is *unique* in the following sense: If $B_1, B_2, \ldots$ are positive integers, then the infinite continued fraction $//B_1, B_2, \ldots//$ is an irrational number $X$ between 0 and 1 whose regular continued fraction has $A_n = B_n$ for all $n \ge 1$; and if $B_1, \ldots, B_m$ are positive integers with $B_m > 1$, then the regular continued fraction for $X = //B_1, \ldots, B_m//$ has $A_n = B_n$ for $1 \le n \le m$.

## Solution

First consider the infinite continued fraction

$$
X=//B_1,B_2,\ldots//,
$$

where each $B_n$ is a positive integer.

Let

$$
X^{(n)}=//B_1,\ldots,B_n//
$$

be the $n$th convergent.

By Exercise 5, the infinite continued fraction exists because every $B_n\ge1$. Equation (12) gives

$$
\bigl|X^{(n+1)}-X^{(n)}\bigr|
=
\frac1{
K_n(B_1,\ldots,B_n)\,
K_{n+1}(B_1,\ldots,B_{n+1})
}.
$$

Since all $B_j\ge1$, recurrence (4) implies

$$
K_n(B_1,\ldots,B_n)\ge K_n(1,\ldots,1)=F_{n+1}.
$$

Hence

$$
\bigl|X^{(n+1)}-X^{(n)}\bigr|
\le
\frac1{F_{n+1}F_{n+2}}.
$$

The series

$$
\sum_{n\ge1}\frac1{F_{n+1}F_{n+2}}
$$

converges, since $F_n$ grows exponentially. Therefore

$$
\sum_{n\ge1}
\bigl|X^{(n+1)}-X^{(n)}\bigr|
$$

converges, so $(X^{(n)})$ is a Cauchy sequence. Hence $X^{(n)}\to X$ for some limit $X$, and $0<X<1$.

We next show that $X$ is irrational.

Suppose, on the contrary, that $X$ is rational. By the discussion following (15), the regular continued fraction of $X$ is finite:

$$
X=//A_1,\ldots,A_r//,
\qquad A_r\ge2.
$$

For a rational number, extending the regular continued fraction process beyond the last quotient produces no further terms. Hence for every $n\ge r$,

$$
X=//A_1,\ldots,A_r//
   =//A_1,\ldots,A_r,0,\ldots,0//.
$$

Consequently the sequence of finite truncations is eventually constant, so

$$
X^{(n)}=X
\qquad (n\ge r).
$$

In particular,

$$
X^{(r+1)}=X^{(r)}.
$$

But

$$
X^{(r+1)}
=
\frac1{
B_1+\displaystyle\frac1{
\cdots+\frac1{
B_r+\frac1{B_{r+1}}
}}}
$$

and

$$
X^{(r)}
=
\frac1{
B_1+\displaystyle\frac1{
\cdots+\frac1{B_r}
}}.
$$

Since $B_{r+1}>0$, the quantities $B_r$ and

$B_r+\frac1{B_{r+1}}$ are different, hence

$$
X^{(r+1)}\ne X^{(r)},
$$

a contradiction. Therefore $X$ is irrational.

Now apply the regular continued fraction algorithm (10). Put

$$
X_0=X,
\qquad
A_{n+1}=\lfloor1/X_n\rfloor,
\qquad
X_{n+1}=1/X_n-A_{n+1}.
$$

Since

$$
X
=
\frac1{
B_1+\displaystyle\frac1{
B_2+\displaystyle\frac1{\cdots}
}},
$$

we have

$$
\frac1X
=
B_1+
\frac1{
B_2+\displaystyle\frac1{\cdots}
}.
$$

Let

$$
T_1=
\frac1{
B_2+\displaystyle\frac1{\cdots}
}.
$$

Because $B_2\ge1$, we have

$$
0<T_1<1.
$$

Therefore

$$
B_1<\frac1X<B_1+1,
$$

so

$$
A_1=\Bigl\lfloor\frac1X\Bigr\rfloor=B_1.
$$

Furthermore,

$$
X_1
=
\frac1X-A_1
=
T_1
=
//B_2,B_3,\ldots//.
$$

Applying the same argument to $X_1$ gives

$$
A_2=B_2,
\qquad
X_2=//B_3,B_4,\ldots//.
$$

Continuing inductively, for every $n\ge1$,

$$
A_n=B_n,
\qquad
X_n=//B_{n+1},B_{n+2},\ldots//.
$$

Hence the regular continued fraction of $X$ is precisely

$$
X=//B_1,B_2,\ldots//.
$$

This proves the infinite case.

Now consider the finite continued fraction

$$
X=//B_1,\ldots,B_m//,
$$

where $B_m>1$.

By equation (5), $X$ is rational.

Again define

$$
X_0=X,
\qquad
A_{n+1}=\lfloor1/X_n\rfloor,
\qquad
X_{n+1}=1/X_n-A_{n+1}.
$$

Since

$$
X
=
\frac1{
B_1+\displaystyle\frac1{
B_2+\cdots+\frac1{B_m}
}},
$$

let

$$
T_1
=
\frac1{
B_2+\cdots+\frac1{B_m}
}.
$$

Because every $B_j$ is positive,

$$
0<T_1<1.
$$

Hence

$$
B_1<\frac1X<B_1+1,
$$

and therefore

$$
A_1=B_1.
$$

Moreover,

$$
X_1
=
\frac1X-A_1
=
T_1
=
//B_2,\ldots,B_m//.
$$

Repeating the same argument yields

$$
A_j=B_j,
\qquad
X_j=//B_{j+1},\ldots,B_m//
\qquad
(1\le j<m).
$$

In particular,

$$
X_{m-1}=//B_m//=\frac1{B_m}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\frac1{X_{m-1}}=B_m,
$$

and since $B_m>1$,

$$
A_m
=
\Bigl\lfloor\frac1{X_{m-1}}\Bigr\rfloor
=
B_m.
$$

Finally,

$$
X_m
=
\frac1{X_{m-1}}-A_m
=
B_m-B_m
=
0.
$$

Thus the regular continued fraction terminates exactly after the $m$th partial quotient, and

$$
A_n=B_n
\qquad
(1\le n\le m).
$$

Therefore the regular continued fraction expansion is unique in the stated sense. ∎
