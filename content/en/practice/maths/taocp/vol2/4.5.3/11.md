[Section 4.5.3: Analysis of Euclid's Algorithm](../)

**Exercise 11.** [*M30*] [M30] (J.-A. Serret, 1850.) Let $X = A_0 + //\!A_1, A_2, A_3, A_4, \ldots//$ and $Y = B_0 + //\!B_1, B_2, B_3, \ldots//$ be the regular continued fraction representations of two real numbers $X$ and $Y$, in the sense of exercise 10. Show that these representations "eventually agree," in the sense that $A_{m+k} = B_{s+k}$ for some $m$ and $s$ and for all $k \ge 0$, if and only if we have $X = (qY + r)/(sY + t)$ for some integers $q$, $r$, $s$, $t$ with $|qt - rs| = 1$. (This theorem is the analog, for continued fraction representations, of the simple result that the representations of $X$ and $Y$ in the decimal system eventually agree if and only if $X = (10^q Y + r)/10^s$ for some integers $q$, $r$, and $s$.)

## Setup

Let

$$
X=A_0+//\!A_1,A_2,A_3,\ldots//
$$

and

$$
Y=B_0+//\!B_1,B_2,B_3,\ldots//
$$

be the regular continued fraction representations of two irrational numbers.

We must prove that the following conditions are equivalent:

1. There exist integers $m,s\ge0$ such that

$$
A_{m+k}=B_{s+k}\qquad(k\ge0).
$$

1. There exist integers $q,r,s,t$ satisfying

$$
|qt-rs|=1
$$

and

$$
X=\frac{qY+r}{sY+t}.
$$

Exercise 10 will be used repeatedly. It states that if a number has regular continued fraction expansion

$$
Z=C_0+//\!C_1,C_2,\ldots//,
$$

then $1/Z$ has a regular continued fraction expansion obtained by prefixing finitely many partial quotients to the sequence $C_0,C_1,C_2,\ldots$. Hence the operations

$$
Z\mapsto Z+n,\qquad Z\mapsto \frac1Z
$$

with $n\in\mathbf Z$ correspond to modifying only a finite initial segment of the continued fraction.

## Solution

Assume first that the continued fractions eventually agree. Let

$$
T=//\!A_m,A_{m+1},A_{m+2},\ldots//
   =//\!B_s,B_{s+1},B_{s+2},\ldots// .
$$

Then $X$ and $Y$ are obtained from the same tail $T$ by attaching finite initial blocks.

Consider a finite continued fraction prefix

$$
c_0+//\!c_1,\ldots,c_n,T// .
$$

Starting with $T$, each step

$$
Z\mapsto c+Z
$$

or

$$
Z\mapsto \frac1Z
$$

is a linear fractional transformation

$$
Z\mapsto \frac{aZ+b}{cZ+d}
$$

whose matrix is respectively

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
1&c\\
0&1
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\begin{pmatrix}
0&1\\
1&0
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Both matrices have determinant $\pm1$. Therefore every finite composition has the form

$$
Z\mapsto \frac{aZ+b}{cZ+d},
\qquad |ad-bc|=1.
$$

Hence there exist integers $a,b,c,d$ and $a',b',c',d'$ with

$$
|ad-bc|=|a'd'-b'c'|=1
$$

such that

$$
X=\frac{aT+b}{cT+d},
\qquad
Y=\frac{a'T+b'}{c'T+d'}.
$$

Let

$$
M=
\begin{pmatrix}
a&b\\
c&d
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
N=
\begin{pmatrix}
a'&b'\\
c'&d'
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Since $\det N=\pm1$, the inverse matrix $N^{-1}$ has integer entries. Solving the second equation for $T$ and substituting into the first yields

$$
X=\frac{qY+r}{sY+t},
$$

where

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
q&r\\
s&t
\end{pmatrix}
=MN^{-1}.
$$

Its determinant is

$$
qt-rs=\det(M)\det(N^{-1})=\pm1.
$$

Thus condition 2 holds.

For the converse, assume

$$
X=\frac{qY+r}{sY+t},
\qquad |qt-rs|=1.
$$

Let

$$
M=
\begin{pmatrix}
q&r\\
s&t
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Since $\det M=\pm1$, $M$ belongs to the group generated by

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
1&n\\
0&1
\end{pmatrix}
\quad(n\in\mathbf Z)
$$

and

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
0&1\\
1&0
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Equivalently, the transformation

$$
Y\mapsto \frac{qY+r}{sY+t}
$$

can be written as a finite composition of the operations

$$
Z\mapsto Z+n,
\qquad
Z\mapsto \frac1Z.
$$

By exercise 10, each such operation changes only a finite initial segment of the regular continued fraction expansion. Therefore applying finitely many such operations to $Y$ alters only finitely many partial quotients.

Consequently the regular continued fraction expansions of $X$ and $Y$ coincide from some point onward. Hence there exist integers $m$ and $s$ such that

$$
A_{m+k}=B_{s+k}\qquad(k\ge0).
$$

Condition 1 follows.

The two conditions are therefore equivalent.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification

Suppose the common tail is $T$. Any finite prefix of a continued fraction is produced from $T$ by repeated applications of

$$
Z\mapsto Z+n,\qquad Z\mapsto \frac1Z.
$$

These correspond to unimodular matrices

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
1&n\\
0&1
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\begin{pmatrix}
0&1\\
1&0
\end{pmatrix},
$$

so the resulting number is always a linear fractional transformation of $T$ with determinant $\pm1$.

Conversely, every linear fractional transformation with determinant $\pm1$ is a finite product of these generators. Exercise 10 shows that each generator modifies only finitely many initial partial quotients. Therefore two numbers related by such a transformation must have continued fractions that eventually agree.

## Notes

The theorem states that eventual agreement of regular continued fractions is exactly the equivalence relation induced by the action of the unimodular group

$$
\mathrm{GL}(2,\mathbf Z)
$$

on the real line via linear fractional transformations. Serret's theorem is one of the fundamental links between continued fractions and modular transformations.
