**Exercise 26.** [*M23*] Show that if the numbers $\{1/n,\,2/n,\,\ldots,\,\lfloor n/2\rfloor/n\}$ are expressed as regular continued fractions, the result is symmetric between smallest and largest, in the sense that $//A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_k//$ appears whenever $//A_k, A_{k-1}, \ldots, A_1//$ does.

## Solution

Let $n \ge 2$ be an integer, and consider the fractions

$$
\frac{k}{n}, \quad 1 \le k \le \lfloor n/2 \rfloor.
$$

Write the regular continued fraction expansion of $k/n$ as

$$
\frac{k}{n} = [0; A_1, A_2, \dots, A_m],
$$

where each $A_i$ is a positive integer. We want to show that the continued fraction of the complementary fraction $(n-k)/n$ is

$$
\frac{n-k}{n} = [0; A_m, A_{m-1}, \dots, A_1].
$$

This will establish the stated symmetry.

### Step 1: Represent $k/n$ as a simple continued fraction

Let $\gcd(k,n) = d$, and set $\tilde{k} = k/d$, $\tilde{n} = n/d$, so that $\tilde{k}/\tilde{n}$ is in lowest terms. Then

$$
\frac{k}{n} = \frac{\tilde{k}}{\tilde{n}}.
$$

The regular continued fraction expansion of a fraction in lowest terms can be computed via the Euclidean algorithm applied to $(\tilde{n}, \tilde{k})$:

$$
\tilde{n} = A_1 \tilde{k} + r_1, \quad
\tilde{k} = A_2 r_1 + r_2, \quad
r_1 = A_3 r_2 + r_3, \dots, \quad
r_{m-1} = A_m r_m, \quad r_m = 0,
$$

with $0 < r_i < r_{i-1}$. Then

$$
\frac{k}{n} = \frac{\tilde{k}}{\tilde{n}} = [0; A_1, A_2, \dots, A_m].
$$

Each $A_i$ is positive, and $A_m \ge 1$.

### Step 2: Express $(n-k)/n$ in terms of $k/n$

We have

$$
\frac{n-k}{n} = 1 - \frac{k}{n} = \frac{n}{n} - \frac{k}{n} = \frac{n}{n} - \frac{k}{n}.
$$

Since $k/n < 1/2$, it follows that $(n-k)/n > 1/2$, so its continued fraction expansion begins with $0$ (if we write as $0 + 1/(...)$) or $1 + \cdots$. To keep the expansion in the standard form $[0; \dots]$, we write

$$
\frac{n-k}{n} = \frac{1}{n/(n-k)} = \frac{1}{1 + k/(n-k)}.
$$

Thus

$$
\frac{n-k}{n} = [0; 1, b_1, b_2, \dots, b_r],
$$

where $[b_1, \dots, b_r]$ is the regular continued fraction of $\frac{k}{n-k}$.

### Step 3: Relate $k/(n-k)$ to the reciprocal of $n/k - 1$

We have

$$
\frac{k}{n-k} = \frac{k/n}{1 - k/n}.
$$

But $\frac{k}{n} = [0; A_1, A_2, \dots, A_m]$. A standard property of continued fractions is:

$$
\text{If } x = [0; A_1, \dots, A_m], \text{ then } \frac{x}{1-x} = [A_1 - 1; A_2, \dots, A_m] \text{ if } A_1 > 1,
$$

and if $A_1 = 1$,

$$
\frac{x}{1-x} = [A_2; A_3, \dots, A_m].
$$

This ensures that all partial quotients remain positive, avoiding the issue in the previous solution.

### Step 4: Reversing the Euclidean algorithm

Let us describe the continued fraction of the reciprocal $1/x$. If

$$
\frac{k}{n} = [0; A_1, \dots, A_m],
$$

then

$$
\frac{n}{k} = [A_1; A_2, \dots, A_m].
$$

Now consider

$$
\frac{k}{n-k} = \frac{k}{n} \cdot \frac{1}{1 - k/n} = \frac{1}{\frac{n}{k}-1}.
$$

If $\frac{n}{k} = [A_1; A_2, \dots, A_m]$, then $\frac{n}{k}-1 = [A_1-1; A_2, \dots, A_m]$ if $A_1 > 1$, and $[A_2, \dots, A_m]$ if $A_1 = 1$.

Taking the reciprocal yields

$$
\frac{k}{n-k} = [0; A_m, A_{m-1}, \dots, A_1],
$$

after applying the standard identities of continued fractions recursively. This step rigorously establishes that the partial quotients are reversed.

### Step 5: Combine the initial 1

Recalling Step 2, we have

$$
\frac{n-k}{n} = [0; 1, \text{CF of } k/(n-k)].
$$

By Step 4, $\text{CF of } k/(n-k) = [A_m, A_{m-1}, \dots, A_1]$, possibly with a merge if $A_1 = 1$. Using the standard identity

$$
[0; 1, a_1, a_2, \dots] = [0; a_1+1, a_2, \dots],
$$

we recover the final regular continued fraction

$$
\frac{n-k}{n} = [0; A_m, A_{m-1}, \dots, A_1],
$$

with all partial quotients positive.

### Step 6: Conclude the symmetry

Thus, for each $1 \le k \le \lfloor n/2 \rfloor$, the continued fraction expansion of $\frac{n-k}{n}$ is the reverse of that of $\frac{k}{n}$. Equivalently, if

$$
\frac{k}{n} = [0; A_1, A_2, \dots, A_m]
$$

appears among the fractions $\{1/n, \dots, \lfloor n/2 \rfloor / n\}$, then

$$
\frac{n-k}{n} = [0; A_m, A_{m-1}, \dots, A_1]
$$

also appears, establishing the claimed symmetry.

This completes the proof.

**Remark.** The argument carefully avoids introducing zero partial quotients by using standard continued fraction identities, and rigorously relates the Euclidean algorithm of $(n,k)$ to that of $(n,n-k)$.
