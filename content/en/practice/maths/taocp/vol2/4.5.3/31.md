**Exercise 31.** &#9654; [*M35*] Find the worst case of the modification of Euclid's algorithm suggested in exercise **30**: What are the smallest inputs $u > v > 0$ that require $n$ division steps?

## Setup

Let the modified algorithm be defined as follows. At a division step, write

$u=qv+r,\qquad 0\le r<v.$

Instead of replacing $(u,v)$ by $(v,r)$, replace it by

$(v,\rho),\qquad \rho=\min(r,v-r).$

Since

$\gcd(v,r)=\gcd(v,v-r),$

the algorithm preserves the greatest common divisor. The process terminates when the second component becomes $0$.

Let $M_n$ denote the smallest value of the larger input $u$ among all pairs $u>v>0$ that require exactly $n$ division steps in this modified algorithm. The problem asks for the pair $(u,v)$ attaining $M_n$.

The ordinary Euclidean algorithm is analyzed by continued fractions whose partial quotients satisfy

$A_i\ge1,\qquad A_n\ge2.$

For the modified algorithm, the remainder chosen at each step satisfies

$0\le \rho\le \frac12 v.$

Hence, if

$u=qv+r,$

then either $\rho=r$ with $r\le v/2$, or $\rho=v-r$ with $r>v/2$. In the latter case

$u=(q+1)v-\rho.$

Therefore every division step may be written uniquely in the form

u=a\,v\pm \rho,\qquad a\ge1,\qquad 0\le \rho\le \frac12 v. \tag{1}

The sign is chosen so that $\rho$ is the remainder used in the next step.

## Notes

The modification can shorten many computations because remainders larger than $v/2$ are replaced by their complements. However, the Fibonacci inputs already produce remainders strictly less than $v/2$ at every stage. Consequently the modification has no effect on the extremal case, and Theorem F remains valid without alteration.
