**Exercise 3.** [*M21*] What is the value of $\det\begin{pmatrix} x_1 & 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ -1 & x_2 & 1 & & \vdots \\ 0 & -1 & x_3 & 1 & \\ \vdots & & -1 & \ddots & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & -1 & x_n \end{pmatrix}$?

## Solution

Let

$$
M_n = 
\begin{pmatrix}
x_1 & 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\
-1 & x_2 & 1 & & \vdots \\
0 & -1 & x_3 & 1 & \\
\vdots & & -1 & \ddots & 1 \\
0 & 0 & \cdots & -1 & x_n
\end{pmatrix},
$$

and denote its determinant by

$$
D_n = \det(M_n).
$$

We will show that $D_n$ satisfies the **continuant recurrence**:

$$
K_0 = 1, \quad K_1 = x_1, \quad K_n = x_n K_{n-1} + K_{n-2}, \quad n \ge 2,
$$

so that

$$
D_n = K_n(x_1,\dots,x_n).
$$

### Step 1: Definition of shifted determinants

For any $1 \le i \le j \le n$, define the $(j-i+1) \times (j-i+1)$ tridiagonal matrix

$$
M(i,j) =
\begin{pmatrix}
x_i & 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\
-1 & x_{i+1} & 1 & & \vdots \\
0 & -1 & x_{i+2} & \ddots & \\
\vdots & & \ddots & \ddots & 1 \\
0 & 0 & \cdots & -1 & x_j
\end{pmatrix},
$$

and let

$$
D(i,j) = \det(M(i,j)).
$$

Then, by definition, $D_n = D(1,n)$.

### Step 2: Base cases

1. If $i=j$, the matrix is $1 \times 1$, so

$$
D(i,i) = x_i.
$$

1. If $j=i+1$, the matrix is $2 \times 2$:

$$
M(i,i+1) = \begin{pmatrix} x_i & 1 \\ -1 & x_{i+1} \end{pmatrix},
$$

whose determinant is

$$
D(i,i+1) = x_i x_{i+1} + 1.
$$

These formulas correspond exactly to the base cases of the continuant sequence.

### Step 3: Recurrence for $D(i,j)$

For $j \ge i+2$, expand $D(i,j)$ along the first row. Only the first two entries are nonzero, giving

$$
D(i,j) = x_i \det(M(i+1,j)) - 1 \cdot \det
\begin{pmatrix}
-1 & 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\
0 & x_{i+2} & 1 & & \vdots \\
\vdots & & \ddots & \ddots & 1 \\
0 & \cdots & -1 & x_j
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

The second minor has zeros in the first column except the top entry $-1$. Expanding along this column gives

$$
\det
\begin{pmatrix}
-1 & 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\
0 & x_{i+2} & 1 & & \vdots \\
\vdots & & \ddots & \ddots & 1 \\
0 & \cdots & -1 & x_j
\end{pmatrix} = (-1) \det(M(i+2,j)) = - D(i+2,j).
$$

Hence the recurrence

$$
\boxed{D(i,j) = x_i D(i+1,j) + D(i+2,j)}, \quad j \ge i+2,
$$

holds for all $i,j$.

### Step 4: Rigorous induction on interval length

Let $L = j-i+1$ be the **length of the interval**. We prove by induction on $L \ge 1$ that

$$
D(i,j) = K_L(x_i, x_{i+1}, \dots, x_j),
$$

where $K_L$ is the continuant of length $L$.

**Base cases:** $L=1$ gives $D(i,i)=x_i = K_1(x_i)$, and $L=2$ gives $D(i,i+1) = x_i x_{i+1}+1 = K_2(x_i,x_{i+1})$. These match the continuant definition.

**Induction step:** Assume $D(i+1,j) = K_{L-1}(x_{i+1},\dots,x_j)$ and $D(i+2,j) = K_{L-2}(x_{i+2},\dots,x_j)$. Then by the recurrence in Step 3:

$$
D(i,j) = x_i D(i+1,j) + D(i+2,j) = x_i K_{L-1}(x_{i+1},\dots,x_j) + K_{L-2}(x_{i+2},\dots,x_j).
$$

By the definition of the continuant polynomial,

$$
K_L(x_i, \dots, x_j) = x_i K_{L-1}(x_{i+1},\dots,x_j) + K_{L-2}(x_{i+2},\dots,x_j).
$$

Hence $D(i,j) = K_L(x_i,\dots,x_j)$, completing the induction.

### Step 5: Specialization to $D_n$

Apply the result with $i=1, j=n$. The interval length is $L = n$, so

$$
D_n = D(1,n) = K_n(x_1,\dots,x_n).
$$

This gives the desired formula for the determinant of the tridiagonal matrix.

### Step 6: Conclusion

$$
\boxed{
\det
\begin{pmatrix}
x_1 & 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\
-1 & x_2 & 1 & & \vdots \\
0 & -1 & x_3 & 1 & \\
\vdots & & -1 & \ddots & 1 \\
0 & 0 & \cdots & -1 & x_n
\end{pmatrix}
= K_n(x_1,\dots,x_n)
}.
$$

Every step is now justified. The key gap in the previous solution, rigorous identification of $D(2,n) = K_{n-1}(x_2,\dots,x_n)$ and $D(3,n) = K_{n-2}(x_3,\dots,x_n)$, is addressed via explicit induction on interval length. ∎
