[Section 4.5.3: Analysis of Euclid's Algorithm](../)

**Exercise 20.** [*HM20*] Deduce (38) from (37).

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 5m11s  

---

Let $K_n(x_1,\dots,x_n)$ denote the continuant defined in Section 4.5.3. Equation (37) gives the standard last-term expansion

$$
(37)\quad K_n(x_1,\dots,x_n)=x_n\,K_{n-1}(x_1,\dots,x_{n-1})+K_{n-2}(x_1,\dots,x_{n-2}).
$$

We now deduce the corresponding first-term expansion (38).

Consider the determinant definition of the continuant. Reversing the order of rows and columns shows that the continuant is invariant under reversal of arguments:

$$
K_n(x_1,\dots,x_n)=K_n(x_n,\dots,x_1).
$$

Apply (37) to the reversed sequence $(x_n,\dots,x_1)$:

$$
K_n(x_n,\dots,x_1)
= x_1\,K_{n-1}(x_n,\dots,x_2) + K_{n-2}(x_n,\dots,x_3).
$$

Using invariance under reversal again on each continuant on the right-hand side gives

$$
K_{n-1}(x_n,\dots,x_2)=K_{n-1}(x_2,\dots,x_n),
\quad
K_{n-2}(x_n,\dots,x_3)=K_{n-2}(x_3,\dots,x_n).
$$

Substituting back yields

$$
K_n(x_1,\dots,x_n)
= x_1\,K_{n-1}(x_2,\dots,x_n) + K_{n-2}(x_3,\dots,x_n),
$$

which is equation (38).

Thus (38) follows directly from (37) by applying the continuant recurrence to the reversed argument sequence and using symmetry under reversal. ∎
