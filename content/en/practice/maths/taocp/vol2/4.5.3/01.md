[Section 4.5.3: Analysis of Euclid's Algorithm](../)

**Exercise 1.** &#9654; [*20*] Since the quotient $\lfloor u/v \rfloor$ is equal to unity more than 40 percent of the time in Algorithm 4.5.2A, it may be advantageous on some computers to make a test for this case and to avoid the division when the quotient is unity. Is the following MIX program for Euclid's algorithm more efficient than Program 4.5.2A?

```mix
LDX  U      LDX u - u.
JMP  2F
1H  STX  V      v ← rX.
    SUB  V      rA ← u - v.
    CMPA V

                SRAX 5   rAX ← rA.
                JL   2F  Is u - v < v?
                DIV  V   rX ← rAX mod v.
2H  LDX  V      v ← rX.
                JXNZ 1B  Done if rX = 0.
```

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 7m07s  

---

## Solution

First we verify that the proposed program is correct. After that we compare its actual running time with that of Program 4.5.2A by counting the instructions executed on MIX.

Suppose that at the beginning of an iteration we have

$$
u>v>0,
$$

with $u$ in register $A$ and $v$ in register $X$.

At label $1H$ the instruction

```
STX V
```

stores $v$ in memory. Then

```
SUB V
```

replaces $u$ by $u-v$, and

```
CMPA V
```

compares $u-v$ with $v$.

If

$$
u-v<v,
$$

then

$$
u<2v,
$$

hence

$$
\left\lfloor\frac uv\right\rfloor =1.
$$

After

```
SRAX 5
```

the value $u-v$ is in register $X$. The branch

```
JL 2F
```

is taken, so no division is performed. The next iteration therefore starts with

$$
(u',v')=(v,u-v),
$$

which is exactly the result of Euclid's algorithm when the quotient equals $1$.

If

$$
u-v\ge v,
$$

then

$$
u\ge2v,
$$

so

$$
\left\lfloor\frac uv\right\rfloor>1.
$$

The branch is not taken and the instruction

```
DIV V
```

computes

$$
u\bmod v
$$

in register $X$. Hence the next iteration begins with

$$
(u',v')=(v,u\bmod v),
$$

exactly as in Program 4.5.2A. Therefore the modified program computes the same gcd.

Now we examine efficiency.

Program 4.5.2A has one loop iteration for every Euclidean reduction. In each iteration it performs one division. Since a MIX division requires $12$ units of time, every reduction costs at least those $12$ units, together with the remaining instructions of the loop.

In the modified program every iteration contains the additional instructions

```
SUB V
CMPA V
SRAX 5
JL 2F
```

before the decision whether or not a division is necessary.

From the MIX timing table,

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{SUB} &=2,\\
\text{CMPA}&=2,\\
\text{SRAX}&=2,\\
\text{conditional jump}&=1.
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus these four instructions consume

$$
2+2+2+1=7
$$

time units.

If the quotient is $1$, the division is avoided and $12$ units are saved. Hence a quotient $1$ produces a net gain of

$$
12-7=5
$$

units.

If the quotient exceeds $1$, the division is still executed, and the extra test has merely added $7$ units to the running time.

The exercise states that the quotient is equal to $1$ more than $40\%$ of the time. Let there be $N$ Euclidean reductions, and let $N_1$ of them have quotient $1$. Then

$$
N_1>0.4N.
$$

Relative to Program 4.5.2A, the total change in running time is

$$
7(N-N_1)-5N_1
=
7N-12N_1.
$$

Using $N_1>0.4N$, we obtain

$$
7N-12N_1
<
7N-4.8N
=
2.2N>0.
$$

Hence the modified program requires more time than Program 4.5.2A.

Equivalently, the quotient would have to equal $1$ during more than

$$
\frac{7}{12}
$$

of the reductions before the saving from omitted divisions could outweigh the cost of the extra test.

Therefore the fact that the quotient is $1$ slightly more than $40\%$ of the time is not enough. On MIX, the proposed program is **not more efficient** than Program 4.5.2A.

$$
\boxed{\text{The program is correct, but it is slower than Program 4.5.2A on MIX.}}
$$
