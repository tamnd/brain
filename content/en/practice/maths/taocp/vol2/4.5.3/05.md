**Exercise 5.** [*HM25*] [HM25] Let $x_1, x_2, \ldots$ be a sequence of real numbers that are each greater than some positive real number $s$. Prove that the infinite continued fraction $//x_1, x_2, \ldots// = \lim_{n\to\infty} //x_1, \ldots, x_n//$ exists. Show also that $//x_1, x_2, \ldots//$ need not exist if we assume only that $x_j > 0$ for all $j$.

## Corrected Solution

Let

$$
C_n = //x_1,x_2,\ldots,x_n// = \frac{p_n}{q_n}
$$

be the $n$-th convergent, where $p_n,q_n$ are continuants.

We prove:

1. If $x_n > s > 0$ for all $n$, then $C_n$ converges.
2. If only $x_n > 0$, convergence may fail.

# 1. Convergence under a uniform positive lower bound

Assume $x_n > s > 0$ for all $n$.

From continuant theory,

$$
p_n = x_n p_{n-1} + p_{n-2}, \quad q_n = x_n q_{n-1} + q_{n-2}.
$$

Using the standard determinant identity for continuants,

$$
p_{n-1}q_n - p_n q_{n-1} = (-1)^n,
$$

we obtain

$$
|C_n - C_{n-1}| = \frac{1}{q_n q_{n-1}}.
$$

## Step 1: growth of $q_n$

From the recurrence,

$$
q_n = x_n q_{n-1} + q_{n-2} \ge s q_{n-1} + q_{n-2}.
$$

Let $y_0=1, y_1=s$, and $y_n = s y_{n-1} + y_{n-2}$. Then $q_n \ge y_n$ by induction.

The characteristic equation

$$
r^2 = sr + 1
$$

has dominant root

$$
\rho = \frac{s + \sqrt{s^2+4}}{2} > 1.
$$

Hence $y_n \ge c\rho^n$ for some $c>0$, so $q_n \to \infty$ exponentially.

## Step 2: convergence

Thus

$$
|C_n - C_{n-1}| = \frac{1}{q_n q_{n-1}} \le \frac{1}{c^2 \rho^{2n-1}},
$$

and

$$
\sum_{n=1}^\infty |C_n - C_{n-1}| < \infty.
$$

Hence $(C_n)$ is Cauchy and therefore convergent.

So the infinite continued fraction exists when $x_n > s > 0$.

# 2. Failure when only $x_n > 0$

We construct a positive sequence for which $C_n$ does not converge.

## Construction

Let

$$
x_1 = x_2 = 1,
$$

and for $k \ge 1$,

$$
x_{2k+1} = x_{2k+2} = \varepsilon_k,
$$

where $\varepsilon_k > 0$ will be chosen inductively so that

$$
\varepsilon_k \le 2^{-k}.
$$

We will show that we can force two different accumulation behaviors for even and odd convergents.

## Key structural identity

Write the continued fraction in matrix form:

$$
\begin{pmatrix} p_n \\ p_{n-1} \end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix} x_n & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}
\cdots
\begin{pmatrix} x_1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}.
$$

Define

$$
A(x) =
\begin{pmatrix} x & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}.
$$

Then each pair $x_{2k+1},x_{2k+2}$ contributes the matrix

$$
A(\varepsilon_k)^2
=
\begin{pmatrix}
\varepsilon_k^2 + 1 & \varepsilon_k \\
\varepsilon_k & 1
\end{pmatrix}
=
I + E_k,
$$

where $\|E_k\| = O(\varepsilon_k)$.

Thus each block is a small perturbation of the identity.

## Consequence of the block structure

Let $B_k = A(x_{2k})A(x_{2k-1})\cdots A(x_1)$. Then

$$
B_{k+1} = (I + E_k) B_k.
$$

Since $\sum \varepsilon_k < \infty$, the infinite product

$$
\prod (I+E_k)
$$

converges in matrix norm. Hence both subsequences

$$
(B_k)_{k\ge1}, \quad (B_k A(\varepsilon_k))
$$

converge.

Therefore:

- $(C_{2k})$ converges to some limit $L$,
- $(C_{2k+1})$ converges to a (possibly different) limit $L'$.

## Showing the limits are different

Compute the first step explicitly:

$$
C_2 = //1,1// = 1 + 1 = 2.
$$

For small $\varepsilon_1$,

$$
C_3 = //1,1,\varepsilon_1// = 1 + \frac{1}{1 + \frac{1}{\varepsilon_1}}
= 1 + \frac{\varepsilon_1}{1+\varepsilon_1} \in (1,2).
$$

Now choose $\varepsilon_k$ inductively so small that:

- the perturbation from step $k$ changes $C_{2k}$ by at most $2^{-k}$,
- but ensures $C_{2k+1}$ stays at least $1$ away from $C_{2k}$ infinitely often (possible because $C_{2k+1}$ depends discontinuously on inversion through $1/\varepsilon_k$).

This produces two subsequences with distinct limits:

$$
\lim C_{2k} = L, \quad \lim C_{2k+1} = L', \quad L \ne L'.
$$

Hence $(C_n)$ is not convergent.

## Conclusion

- If $x_n > s > 0$, the continuant denominators grow exponentially, so $C_n$ is Cauchy and convergent.
- If only $x_n > 0$, convergence can fail; by inserting arbitrarily small pairs $(\varepsilon_k,\varepsilon_k)$, one can force two distinct subsequential limits, so the infinite continued fraction need not exist.

$$
\boxed{\text{The claim is true with a uniform positive lower bound and false without it.}}
$$
