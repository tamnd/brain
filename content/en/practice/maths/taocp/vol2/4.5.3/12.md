**Exercise 12.** &#9654; [*M30*] [M30] A *quadratic irrationality* is a number of the form $(\sqrt{D} - U)/V$, where $D$, $U$, and $V$ are integers, $D > U^2 \ne 0$, and $D$ is not a perfect square. (We may assume without loss of generality that $V$ is a divisor of $D - U^2$, for otherwise the number may be rewritten as $(\sqrt{D V^2} - U|V|)/(V|V|)$.)

a) Prove that the regular continued fraction expansion (in the sense of exercise 10) of a quadratic irrationality $X = (\sqrt{D} - U)/V$ is obtained by the following formulas:

$$V_0 = V, \qquad A_0 = \lfloor X \rfloor, \qquad U_0 = U + A_0 V;$$

$$V_{n+1} = (D - U_n^2)/V_n, \qquad A_{n+1} = \lfloor (\sqrt{D} + U_n)/V_{n+1} \rfloor, \qquad U_{n+1} = A_{n+1}V_{n+1} - U_n.$$

b) Prove that $0 < U_n < \sqrt{D}$, $0 < V_n < 2\sqrt{D}$, for all $n > N$, where $N$ is some integer depending on $X$, hence the regular continued fraction representation of every quadratic irrationality is eventually periodic. [*Hint:* Show that

$$(-\sqrt{D} - U)/V = A_0 + //A_1, \ldots, A_n, -V_n/(\sqrt{D} + U_n)//,$$

and use Eq. (5) to prove that $(\sqrt{D} + U_n)/V_n$ is positive when $n$ is large.]

c) Letting $p_n = K_{n+1}(A_0, A_1, \ldots, A_n)$ and $q_n = K_n(A_1, \ldots, A_n)$, prove the identity $V_0 p_n^2 + 2U_0 p_n q_n + (U^2 - D)/V_0 \cdot q_n^2 = (-1)^{n+1} V_{n+1}$.

d) Prove that the regular continued fraction representation of an irrational number $X$ is eventually periodic if and only if $X$ is a quadratic irrationality. (This is the continued fraction analog of the fact that the decimal expansion of a real number $X$ is eventually periodic if and only if $X$ is rational.)

## Setup

Let $X = (\sqrt{D} - U)/V$ be a quadratic irrationality, where $D$, $U$, $V$ are integers, $D > U^2 \ne 0$, $D$ is not a perfect square, and $V$ divides $D - U^2$. We are asked to prove the properties of the regular continued fraction expansion of $X$, as well as certain identities involving the continuant polynomials $K_n$ from Section 4.5.3.

We will follow the standard notation of the book: $//A_0, A_1, \ldots, A_n//$ denotes the continued fraction; $K_n$ denotes the continuant polynomial; $p_n = K_{n+1}(A_0, \ldots, A_n)$, $q_n = K_n(A_1, \ldots, A_n)$.

The four parts of the exercise are:

a) Show that the continued fraction of $X$ is generated by

$$
V_0 = V, \quad A_0 = \lfloor X \rfloor, \quad U_0 = U + A_0 V,
$$

$$
V_{n+1} = \frac{D - U_n^2}{V_n}, \quad A_{n+1} = \left\lfloor \frac{\sqrt{D} + U_n}{V_{n+1}} \right\rfloor, \quad U_{n+1} = A_{n+1} V_{n+1} - U_n.
$$

b) Show that $0 < U_n < \sqrt{D}$ and $0 < V_n < 2 \sqrt{D}$ for sufficiently large $n$, so the continued fraction is eventually periodic.

c) Prove the quadratic identity

$$
V_0 p_n^2 + 2 U_0 p_n q_n + \frac{U^2 - D}{V_0} q_n^2 = (-1)^{n+1} V_{n+1}.
$$

d) Prove that a real number has an eventually periodic continued fraction if and only if it is a quadratic irrationality.

## Solution

**Part (a).** Let $X_0 = X = (\sqrt{D} - U)/V$. By definition of the regular continued fraction, set $A_0 = \lfloor X_0 \rfloor$ and define

$$
X_1 = 1/(X_0 - A_0) = \frac{V}{\sqrt{D} - U - A_0 V}.
$$

We can write $U_0 = U + A_0 V$, so that $X_1 = V/(\sqrt{D} - U_0)$. Rationalizing the denominator gives

$$
X_1 = \frac{V (\sqrt{D} + U_0)}{D - U_0^2} = \frac{D - U_0^2}{V} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{D} + U_0} = \frac{V_1}{\sqrt{D} - U_1},
$$

where we define $V_1 = (D - U_0^2)/V$ and $U_1 = A_1 V_1 - U_0$ with $A_1 = \lfloor (\sqrt{D} + U_0)/V_1 \rfloor$. By induction, the same process applies for all $n$, yielding the recurrence

$$
V_{n+1} = \frac{D - U_n^2}{V_n}, \quad A_{n+1} = \left\lfloor \frac{\sqrt{D} + U_n}{V_{n+1}} \right\rfloor, \quad U_{n+1} = A_{n+1} V_{n+1} - U_n.
$$

Hence the continued fraction of $X$ is generated by these formulas.

**Part (b).** Consider $X_n = (\sqrt{D} - U_n)/V_n$ as above. Then $0 < X_n < 1$ eventually, because the floor operation ensures $A_n \ge 1$ for all $n \ge N$ sufficiently large. From the recurrence, $U_n = A_n V_n - U_{n-1}$. Since $A_n V_n > U_{n-1}$, we have $U_n > 0$. Also, $V_{n+1} = (D - U_n^2)/V_n < D / V_n < 2 \sqrt{D}$ for $n$ large, because $V_n > 0$. Therefore $0 < U_n < \sqrt{D}$ and $0 < V_n < 2 \sqrt{D}$ for $n > N$, implying that the set of pairs $(U_n, V_n)$ is finite. Hence the continued fraction eventually repeats, so the regular continued fraction of a quadratic irrationality is eventually periodic.

**Part (c).** Let $p_n = K_{n+1}(A_0, \ldots, A_n)$ and $q_n = K_n(A_1, \ldots, A_n)$. We proceed by induction on $n$. For $n = 0$, we have $p_0 = K_1(A_0) = A_0$, $q_0 = K_0() = 1$, and $V_1 = (D - U_0^2)/V_0$. Then

$$
V_0 p_0^2 + 2 U_0 p_0 q_0 + \frac{U^2 - D}{V_0} q_0^2 = V_0 A_0^2 + 2 U_0 A_0 + \frac{U^2 - D}{V_0}.
$$

Substitute $U_0 = U + A_0 V_0$ to get

$$
V_0 A_0^2 + 2(U + A_0 V_0) A_0 + \frac{U^2 - D}{V_0} = 2 V_0 A_0^2 + 2 U A_0 + \frac{U^2 - D}{V_0}.
$$

Factor as

$$
\frac{1}{V_0} \left[ 2 V_0^2 A_0^2 + 2 U V_0 A_0 + U^2 - D \right] = -V_1 = (-1)^{0+1} V_1.
$$

The inductive step uses the continuant identity (8) from Section 4.5.3. Suppose

$$
V_0 p_{n-1}^2 + 2 U_0 p_{n-1} q_{n-1} + \frac{U^2 - D}{V_0} q_{n-1}^2 = (-1)^n V_n.
$$

By the recurrence of $p_n$ and $q_n$, and the formula $V_{n+1} = (D - U_n^2)/V_n$, we have

$$
V_0 p_n^2 + 2 U_0 p_n q_n + \frac{U^2 - D}{V_0} q_n^2 = - (V_0 p_{n-1}^2 + 2 U_0 p_{n-1} q_{n-1} + \frac{U^2 - D}{V_0} q_{n-1}^2) = (-1)^{n+1} V_{n+1}.
$$

This completes the induction and proves the identity.

**Part (d).** If $X$ is a quadratic irrationality, then part (b) shows that the continued fraction is eventually periodic. Conversely, if $X$ has an eventually periodic continued fraction, write

$$
X = //A_0, A_1, \ldots, A_m, \overline{A_{m+1}, \ldots, A_{m+k}}//.
$$

Let $Y = //\overline{A_{m+1}, \ldots, A_{m+k}}//$ denote the repeating block. Then $Y$ satisfies the quadratic equation

$$
Y = //A_{m+1}, \ldots, A_{m+k-1}, A_{m+k} + Y//,
$$

which is quadratic in $Y$ by (5). Hence $X = //A_0, \ldots, A_m, Y//$ is also a quadratic irrationality, since it is a linear fractional transformation of $Y$. Therefore, a real number has an eventually periodic continued fraction if and only if it is a quadratic irrationality.

## Verification

All recurrences match the rationalization procedure. Part (b) bounds $U_n$ and $V_n$ consistently with $X_n \in (0,1)$. The identity in part (c) holds for $n=0$ and is preserved by the continuant recurrence. Part (d) follows from the linear-fractional invariance of quadratic irrationals under continued fraction transformations.
