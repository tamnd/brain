**Exercise 24.** [*M22*] What is the average value of a partial quotient $A_n$ in the regular continued fraction expansion of a random real number?

## Solution

Let $X$ be a real number chosen uniformly at random from the interval $[0,1)$. Denote its regular continued fraction expansion by $X = //A_1, A_2, A_3, \ldots//$, where $A_1, A_2, \ldots$ are the partial quotients. The goal is to determine the average value of $A_n$ for large $n$.

We first recall from Section 4.5.3 that the regular continued fraction algorithm is defined by the recurrence

$$
X_0 = X, \qquad A_{n+1} = \lfloor 1/X_n \rfloor, \qquad X_{n+1} = 1/X_n - A_{n+1}, \quad n \ge 0. \tag{10 revisited}
$$

Equivalently, for any fixed $n \ge 1$, the event $A_n = k$ corresponds to $X_{n-1}$ lying in the interval

$$
\frac{1}{k+1} \le X_{n-1} < \frac{1}{k}, \qquad k = 1, 2, 3, \ldots
$$

because $A_n = \lfloor 1/X_{n-1} \rfloor$. To compute the average value of $A_n$, we need the distribution of $X_{n-1}$ for large $n$.

By the classical result of Gauss on continued fractions, the limiting distribution of $X_n$ exists and is independent of $n$ for $n \to \infty$. Denote this distribution function by $F(x)$ on $[0,1)$, then

$$
F(x) = \frac{\log(1+x)}{\log 2}, \quad 0 \le x < 1,
$$

with density

$$
f(x) = \frac{dF}{dx} = \frac{1}{\log 2} \cdot \frac{1}{1+x}. \tag{GF1}
$$

Hence, the limiting probability that $A_n = k$ is

$$
\Pr(A_n = k) = \int_{1/(k+1)}^{1/k} f(x) \, dx = \frac{1}{\log 2} \int_{1/(k+1)}^{1/k} \frac{dx}{1+x}.
$$

Evaluating the integral gives

$$
\Pr(A_n = k) = \frac{1}{\log 2} \left[ \log(1 + 1/k) - \log(1 + 1/(k+1)) \right] = \frac{1}{\log 2} \log\frac{(k+1)^2}{k(k+2)}.
$$

This is the classical Gauss–Kuzmin distribution. The expected value of $A_n$ in the limit $n \to \infty$ is

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbb{E}[A_n] &= \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} k \, \Pr(A_n = k) = \frac{1}{\log 2} \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} k \, \log\frac{(k+1)^2}{k(k+2)} \\
&= \frac{1}{\log 2} \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} k \, \log\left(1 + \frac{1}{k} \right) - k \, \log\left(1 + \frac{1}{k+1}\right) \\
&= \frac{1}{\log 2} \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \log\left(1 + \frac{1}{k}\right)^k - \log\left(1 + \frac{1}{k+1}\right)^k.
\end{aligned}
$$

This series diverges logarithmically, because for large $k$

$$
k \log\frac{(k+1)^2}{k(k+2)} = k \log\left(1 + \frac{1}{k^2 + 2k}\right) \sim \frac{1}{k} \quad \text{as } k \to \infty.
$$

Therefore, $\mathbb{E}[A_n] = \infty$. In other words, the average partial quotient is unbounded; the tail of the Gauss–Kuzmin distribution is sufficiently heavy to make the mean diverge.

For completeness, we note that although the mean is infinite, the median partial quotient is finite. More precisely, the probability that $A_n \le k$ is

$$
\Pr(A_n \le k) = \sum_{j=1}^{k} \frac{1}{\log 2} \log\frac{(j+1)^2}{j(j+2)} = \frac{1}{\log 2} \log(k+1),
$$

so the median grows slowly, approximately doubling for each factor of $2$ in $1/\Pr(A_n \le k)$. This illustrates that most partial quotients are small, but occasionally very large values occur, making the mean diverge.

This completes the solution.
