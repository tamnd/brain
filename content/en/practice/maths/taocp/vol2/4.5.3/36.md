**Exercise 36.** [*M25*] (G. H. Bradley.) What is the smallest value of $u_n$ such that the calculation of $\gcd(u_1, \ldots, u_n)$ by Algorithm 4.5.2C requires $N$ divisions, if Euclid's algorithm is used throughout? Assume that $N \ge n \ge 3$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 5m43s

## Solution

Let

$$
g_k=\gcd(u_1,\ldots,u_k)\qquad (1\le k\le n).
$$

Algorithm 4.5.2C computes

$$
g_2=\gcd(u_1,u_2),\qquad
g_3=\gcd(g_2,u_3),\qquad \ldots,\qquad
g_n=\gcd(g_{n-1},u_n).
$$

Let $t_k$ be the number of divisions performed by Euclid's algorithm when computing

$$
\gcd(g_{k-1},u_k).
$$

Then

$$
N=\sum_{k=2}^{n} t_k.
$$

We seek the smallest possible value of $u_n$.

### 1. The Fibonacci extremal property

Let Euclid's algorithm be applied to positive integers $a>b$. If it performs exactly $t$ divisions, the quotients are

$$
a=q_1b+r_1,\quad
b=q_2r_1+r_2,\quad \ldots,\quad
r_{t-2}=q_tr_{t-1}+0,
$$

with $r_{t-1}>0$.

For a fixed number $t$ of divisions, the smallest possible pair $(a,b)$ is obtained when every quotient equals $1$. Working backwards from the final remainder $r_{t-1}=1$, one obtains

$$
r_{t-2}=1,\quad
r_{t-3}=2,\quad
r_{t-4}=3,\quad \ldots,
$$

hence

$$
(a,b)=(F_{t+2},F_{t+1}),
$$

where $F_1=F_2=1$.

Therefore, among all coprime pairs requiring exactly $t$ divisions, the smallest possible second entry is $F_{t+1}$, attained by

$$
(F_{t+2},F_{t+1}).
$$

More generally, if $\gcd(a,b)=d$, write

$$
a=dA,\qquad b=dB,\qquad \gcd(A,B)=1.
$$

Euclid's algorithm performs the same sequence of divisions on $(a,b)$ and $(A,B)$. Hence, for fixed gcd $d$ and fixed division count $t$,

$$
b\ge dF_{t+1},
$$

with equality for

$$
(a,b)=(dF_{t+2},\,dF_{t+1}).
$$

In particular, the pair

$$
(F_{m+2},F_{m+1})
$$

requires exactly $m$ divisions.

### 2. A lower bound for $u_n$

Consider the final computation

$$
g_n=\gcd(g_{n-1},u_n).
$$

Let it require $t_n$ divisions.

Applying the preceding extremal result with $d=g_n$,

$$
u_n\ge g_nF_{t_n+1}.
$$

Since $g_n\ge1$,

$$
u_n\ge F_{t_n+1}.
$$

Thus minimizing $u_n$ requires making $t_n$ as small as possible.

To bound $t_n$, we must determine the smallest possible value of each earlier $t_k$.

For $k\ge2$, the computation of $\gcd(g_{k-1},u_k)$ is a computation of the gcd of two positive integers. Euclid's algorithm always begins with a division of the larger number by the smaller one. Since the inputs are positive, at least one division is performed. Therefore

$$
t_k\ge1\qquad (2\le k\le n).
$$

This bound is sharp. If one argument is a multiple of the other, for example $(2d,d)$, Euclid's algorithm performs exactly one division:

$$
2d=2\cdot d+0.
$$

Hence the minimum possible contribution of any stage is exactly one division.

Since

$$
N=\sum_{k=2}^{n} t_k,
$$

and there are $n-2$ stages preceding the final one,

$$
N
=t_n+\sum_{k=2}^{n-1} t_k
\ge t_n+(n-2).
$$

Therefore

$$
t_n\le N-n+2.
$$

Combining this with $u_n\ge F_{t_n+1}$ gives

$$
u_n\ge F_{\,N-n+3}.
$$

### 3. Construction attaining the bound

Let

$$
m=N-n+2.
$$

We construct inputs for which the total number of divisions is exactly $N$ and

$$
u_n=F_{m+1}.
$$

Set

$$
g_n=1,\qquad
g_{n-1}=F_{m+2},\qquad
u_n=F_{m+1}.
$$

Since consecutive Fibonacci numbers are coprime, and since the pair

$$
(F_{m+2},F_{m+1})
$$

requires exactly $m$ divisions, the final gcd computation contributes $m$ divisions.

Now choose

$$
g_k=F_{m+2}\qquad (2\le k\le n-1).
$$

For each $k=2,\ldots,n-1$, let

$$
u_k=2F_{m+2}.
$$

Then

$$
\gcd(g_{k-1},u_k)=F_{m+2}=g_k,
$$

and Euclid's algorithm performs exactly one division:

$$
2F_{m+2}
=
2\cdot F_{m+2}+0.
$$

Finally take

$$
u_1=F_{m+2}.
$$

Thus

$$
t_2=t_3=\cdots=t_{n-1}=1,
\qquad
t_n=m.
$$

The total number of divisions is

$$
(n-2)+m
=
(n-2)+(N-n+2)
=
N.
$$

The last input is

$$
u_n=F_{m+1}=F_{\,N-n+3}.
$$

### 4. Minimality

Every admissible input satisfies

$$
u_n\ge F_{\,N-n+3}.
$$

The construction above attains equality.

Therefore the smallest possible value of $u_n$ is

$$
\boxed{F_{\,N-n+3}}.
$$

Here $F_1=F_2=1$ denotes the Fibonacci sequence. ∎
