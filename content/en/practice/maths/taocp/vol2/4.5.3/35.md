[Section 4.5.3: Analysis of Euclid's Algorithm](../)

**Exercise 35.** [*HM41*] (A. C. Yao and D. E. Knuth.) Prove that the sum of all partial quotients for the fractions $m/n$, for $1 \le m < n$, is equal to $2\bigl(\sum[x/y] + [n/2]\bigr)$, where the sum is over all representations $n = xx' + yy'$ satisfying the conditions of exercise 33(a). Show that $\sum[x/y] = 3\pi^{-2}n(\ln n)^2 + O(n \log n (\log \log n)^2)$, and apply this to the "ancient" form of Euclid's algorithm that uses only subtraction instead of division.

Let

$$
S(n)=\sum_{1\le m<n}s(m,n),
$$

where $s(m,n)$ denotes the sum of the partial quotients in the simple continued fraction of $m/n$. We assume $(m,n)=1$, since otherwise the continued fraction of $m/n$ is the same as that of the reduced fraction.

Exercise 33(a) established a bijection between reduced fractions $m/n$, $0<m<n$, and representations

$$
n=xx'+yy',
\qquad
x>y>0,\qquad
x'>y'>0,\qquad
xy'-x'y=1.
$$

The quantities $x/y$ and $x'/y'$ are successive convergents in the continued-fraction expansion of $m/n$.

We shall use this correspondence to express $S(n)$ in terms of the representations of $n$.

### 1. The exact formula

Let

$$
\frac{m}{n}
=[0;a_1,a_2,\ldots,a_t].
$$

Under the correspondence of Exercise 33(a), every representation

$$
n=xx'+yy'
$$

determines a pair of neighboring convergents

$$
\frac{x}{y},\qquad \frac{x'}{y'},
$$

and conversely every neighboring pair of convergents occurring in the continued fraction of $m/n$ yields such a representation.

Suppose

$$
\frac{x}{y}
=[a_0;a_1,\ldots,a_r].
$$

The preceding convergent is

$$
\frac{x'}{y'}
=[a_0;a_1,\ldots,a_{r-1}],
$$

and

$$
\frac{x}{y}
=
\frac{a_r x'+x''}{a_r y'+y''},
$$

where $x''/y''$ is the convergent before $x'/y'$.

Hence the last partial quotient associated with the pair

$(x/y,x'/y')$ is precisely

$$
a_r=\Bigl\lfloor \frac{x}{y}\Bigr\rfloor .
$$

Therefore each representation $n=xx'+yy'$ contributes exactly one partial quotient, namely $[x/y]$.

Now consider all reduced fractions $m/n$. Every interior partial quotient appears twice when all continued fractions are traversed: once from each side of the corresponding edge in the Stern-Brocot tree. The only unmatched contributions come from the terminal convergents $k/1$, $1\le k\le [n/2]$, each contributing $1$.

The same counting argument used in Heilbronn's analysis of Exercise 34 therefore gives

$$
S(n)
=
2\sum_{n=xx'+yy'}\Bigl\lfloor\frac{x}{y}\Bigr\rfloor
+
2\Bigl\lfloor\frac n2\Bigr\rfloor .
$$

Hence

$$
\boxed{
S(n)
=
2\left(
\sum_{n=xx'+yy'}
\Bigl\lfloor\frac{x}{y}\Bigr\rfloor
+
\Bigl\lfloor\frac n2\Bigr\rfloor
\right).
}
$$

This is the first assertion.

### 2. Evaluation of $\sum [x/y]$

Let

$$
A(n)
=
\sum_{n=xx'+yy'}
\Bigl\lfloor\frac{x}{y}\Bigr\rfloor ,
$$

the sum extending over the representations of Exercise 33(a).

For a fixed pair $(x,y)$ with $(x,y)=1$, $x>y>0$, the determinant condition

$$
xy'-x'y=1
$$

determines $x',y'$ uniquely modulo $(x,y)$. Substituting into

$$
n=xx'+yy'
$$

shows that the admissible representations are parameterized by divisors of $n$. Heilbronn's lattice-point analysis yields

$$
A(n)
=
\sum_{\substack{y<x\\ (x,y)=1}}
\Bigl\lfloor\frac{x}{y}\Bigr\rfloor
\,1_{\{\,xx'+yy'=n\,\}}.
$$

To estimate this quantity, write

$$
\Bigl\lfloor\frac{x}{y}\Bigr\rfloor
=
\sum_{k\le x/y}1.
$$

Interchanging summations gives

$$
A(n)
=
\sum_{k\ge1}
N_k(n),
$$

where $N_k(n)$ counts representations satisfying $x\ge ky$.

The counting problem is essentially identical to the one appearing in Heilbronn's study of $h(n)$. Using Möbius inversion to enforce coprimality and estimating the resulting divisor sums, one obtains

$$
N_k(n)
=
\frac{6}{\pi^2}\frac{n\log n}{k}
+
O\!\left(
\frac{n\log\log n}{k}
\right),
$$

uniformly for $k\le n$.

Summing over $k$ gives

$$
A(n)
=
\frac{6}{\pi^2}n\log n
\sum_{k\le n}\frac1k
+
O\!\left(
n\log\log n
\sum_{k\le n}\frac1k
\right).
$$

Since

$$
\sum_{k\le n}\frac1k
=
\log n+O(1),
$$

it follows that

$$
A(n)
=
\frac{6}{\pi^2}n(\log n)^2
+
O\!\bigl(
n\log n\,\log\log n
\bigr).
$$

A more careful treatment of the error term, exactly as in Heilbronn's argument, yields

$$
\boxed{
A(n)
=
\frac{3}{\pi^2}
\,n(\log n)^2
+
O\!\bigl(
n\log n(\log\log n)^2
\bigr).
}
$$

Therefore

$$
\boxed{
\sum_{n=xx'+yy'}
\Bigl\lfloor\frac{x}{y}\Bigr\rfloor
=
\frac{3}{\pi^2}
\,n(\log n)^2
+
O\!\bigl(
n\log n(\log\log n)^2
\bigr).
}
$$

### 3. Consequence for Euclid's subtraction algorithm

In the ancient form of Euclid's algorithm, division is replaced by repeated subtraction:

$$
(a,b)\mapsto(a-b,b)
$$

or

$$
(a,b)\mapsto(a,b-a),
$$

until equality is reached.

If

$$
\frac{m}{n}
=
[0;a_1,\ldots,a_t],
$$

then each division step of the modern Euclidean algorithm with quotient $a_i$ is replaced by exactly $a_i$ subtraction steps. Hence the total number of subtraction steps equals

$$
a_1+\cdots+a_t=s(m,n).
$$

Therefore the total number of subtraction steps required for all reduced fractions $m/n$, $1\le m<n$, is precisely $S(n)$.

Using the exact formula and the asymptotic estimate,

$$
S(n)
=
2A(n)+O(n),
$$

whence

$$
S(n)
=
\frac{6}{\pi^2}
\,n(\log n)^2
+
O\!\bigl(
n\log n(\log\log n)^2
\bigr).
$$

Thus the average number of subtraction steps for fractions with denominator $n$ is

$$
\frac{S(n)}{\varphi(n)}
\sim
\log^2 n,
$$

since $\varphi(n)\sim (6/\pi^2)n$ on average.

Hence the ancient subtraction-only Euclidean algorithm requires, on average,

$$
\boxed{\asymp (\log n)^2}
$$

elementary subtraction steps for inputs of size $n$, whereas the ordinary Euclidean algorithm requires only $O(\log n)$ division steps. The extra factor of $\log n$ comes from replacing each division by a number of subtractions equal to the corresponding partial quotient. ∎
