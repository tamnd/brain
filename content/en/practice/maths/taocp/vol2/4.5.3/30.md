[Section 4.5.3: Analysis of Euclid's Algorithm](../)

**Exercise 30.** &#9654; [*HM22*] The following "greedy" variant of Euclid's algorithm is often suggested: Instead of replacing $v$ by $u \bmod v$ during the division step, replace it by $|(u \bmod v) - v|$ if $u \bmod v > \frac{1}{2}v$. For example, if $u = 26$ and $v = 7$, we have $\gcd(26, 7) = \gcd(-2, 7) = \gcd(2, 7)$. Compare this procedure with Euclid's algorithm; estimate the number of division steps this method saves, on the average.

## Solution

Let

$$
u=qv+r,\qquad 0\le r<v.
$$

Ordinary Euclid replaces $(u,v)$ by $(v,r)$.

The proposed variant replaces $r$ by

$$
r^*=\min(r,v-r).
$$

Indeed, if $r>v/2$, the new remainder is $v-r=|(u\bmod v)-v|$; otherwise it is $r$.

Since

$$
\gcd(v,r)=\gcd(v,v-r),
$$

every step preserves the gcd. Therefore the algorithm is correct.

The essential feature is that after each division,

$$
0\le r^*\le \frac v2.
$$

Thus every remainder is at most half the preceding divisor.

### Relation with nearest-integer division

Write

$$
u=mv+\rho,
$$

where $m$ is the nearest integer to $u/v$ and

$$
-\frac v2\le \rho\le \frac v2 .
$$

Then $|\rho|=r^*$. Hence the algorithm is exactly the Euclidean algorithm based on nearest-integer quotients rather than floor quotients.

Equivalently, if

$$
x=\frac uv>1,
$$

ordinary Euclid uses the map

$$
x\mapsto \frac1{\{x\}},
$$

while the present algorithm uses

$$
x\mapsto \frac1{|x-\operatorname{nint}(x)|},
$$

where $\operatorname{nint}(x)$ denotes the nearest integer.

Thus the algorithm corresponds to the continued-fraction expansion obtained from nearest-integer quotients.

### Comparison with ordinary Euclid

For ordinary Euclid, a division step produces a remainder $r$ satisfying

$$
0<r<v.
$$

For the modified algorithm,

$$
0<r^*\le \frac v2.
$$

Hence every step reduces the smaller argument by at least a factor $2$:

$$
r^*\le \frac v2.
$$

Consequently the number of division steps is certainly no more than

$$
\log_2 v + O(1).
$$

This already shows that the modified algorithm is faster than ordinary Euclid in situations where ordinary Euclid repeatedly encounters quotient $1$.

The classical worst case for Euclid occurs when successive quotients are all $1$, namely for consecutive Fibonacci numbers. Let

$$
(F_{n+1},F_n)
$$

be such an input. Ordinary Euclid requires $n-1$ divisions.

For the modified algorithm,

$$
F_{k+1}=F_k+F_{k-1},
$$

and the remainder after division is

$$
F_{k-1}.
$$

Since

$$
F_{k-1}>\frac12F_k
$$

for $k\ge3$, the algorithm replaces it by

$$
F_k-F_{k-1}=F_{k-2}.
$$

Hence one division changes

$$
(F_{k+1},F_k)
\longrightarrow
(F_k,F_{k-2}),
$$

skipping an intermediate Fibonacci index. Thus roughly two ordinary Euclidean steps are compressed into one. On these worst-case inputs the number of divisions is asymptotically halved.

### Average behaviour

A rigorous average-case analysis requires the invariant measure of the nearest-integer continued-fraction transformation. One cannot simply reuse the Gauss-Kuzmin distribution of ordinary continued fractions, because the dynamical system is different.

However, the qualitative effect is clear.

In ordinary Euclid, the only time a remainder exceeds $v/2$ is when the quotient is $1$. Such steps are precisely the slow steps of the algorithm. The modified procedure replaces

$$
r
$$

by

$$
v-r,
$$

which is smaller, and therefore accelerates the subsequent computation.

For random inputs, quotient $1$ occurs with substantial frequency. Each occurrence tends to eliminate approximately one of the long runs of quotient-$1$ steps that dominate Euclid's running time. Since the modification acts only on those steps and leaves all steps with quotient at least $2$ unchanged, the improvement is significant but not dramatic.

The nearest-integer Euclidean algorithm is a classical variant whose average number of divisions is known to be about $70\%$ of that of ordinary Euclid. Thus the saving is of order

$$
0.3\,T_E(N),
$$

where $T_E(N)$ denotes the average number of Euclidean divisions for inputs of size $N$.

Since

$$
T_E(N)\sim
\frac{12\ln2}{\pi^2}\ln N,
$$

the number of divisions saved is approximately

$$
0.3\cdot \frac{12\ln2}{\pi^2}\ln N
\approx
0.25\,\ln N.
$$

Therefore the greedy variant saves a constant fraction of the divisions, roughly $30\%$ on average, while cutting the Fibonacci worst case almost exactly in half.

$$
\boxed{\text{The modified algorithm is the nearest-integer Euclidean algorithm.}}
$$

$$
\boxed{\text{It preserves the gcd, halves the Fibonacci worst case, and saves about }30\%\text{ of the division steps on average.}}
$$

This is the appropriate comparison with ordinary Euclid's algorithm. ∎
