[Section 4.5.3: Analysis of Euclid's Algorithm](../)

**Exercise 16.** [*HM30*] (L. Euler, 1731.) Let $f_0(z) = (e^z - e^{-z})/(e^z + e^{-z}) = \tanh z$, and let $f_{n+1}(z) = 1/f_n(z) - (2n+1)/z$. Prove that, for all $n$, $f_n(z)$ is an analytic function of the complex variable $z$ in a neighborhood of the origin, and it satisfies the differential equation $f_n'(z) = 1 - f_n(z)^2 - 2nf_n(z)/z$. Use this fact to prove that

$$\tanh z = z \Big/ 1, -3^{-1}z^2, 5^{-1}z^{-1}, -7z^{-1}, \ldots /\!/$$

then apply Hurwitz's rule (exercise 14) to prove that

$$e^{-1/n} = //\,1,\,(2m+1)n - 1,\,1\,//,\qquad m \ge 0.$$

(This notation denotes the infinite continued fraction $//1,\,n-1,\,1,\,1,\,3n-1,\,1,\,1,\,5n-1,\,1,\,\ldots//$.) Also find the regular continued expansion of $e^{-2/n}$ when $n > 0$ is odd.

## Setup

Let

$$
f_0(z)=\tanh z=\frac{e^z-e^{-z}}{e^z+e^{-z}}, \qquad 
f_{n+1}(z)=\frac{1}{f_n(z)}-\frac{2n+1}{z}.
$$

All functions are considered in a punctured neighborhood of $z=0$. Analyticity at $0$ means removable singularity at $0$.

The goal is to prove:

1. Each $f_n(z)$ is analytic near $0$.
2. Each satisfies

$$
f_n'(z)=1-f_n(z)^2-\frac{2n}{z}f_n(z).
$$

1. From this differential system, derive the continued fraction for $\tanh z$.
2. Apply Hurwitz’s rule (Exercise 14) to obtain continued fractions for $e^{-1/n}$ and $e^{-2/n}$.

## 1. Analyticity and differential equation

### Base case $n=0$

The power series of $\tanh z$ at $0$ is

$$
\tanh z = z - \frac{z^3}{3} + \frac{2z^5}{15} - \cdots,
$$

hence $\tanh z$ is analytic at $0$.

Differentiation gives

$$
f_0'(z)=\operatorname{sech}^2 z = 1-\tanh^2 z = 1-f_0(z)^2,
$$

which is the required formula for $n=0$.

### Inductive hypothesis

Assume:

1. $f_n$ is analytic in a punctured neighborhood of $0$ and has a simple zero at $0$.
2. It satisfies

$$
f_n'(z)=1-f_n(z)^2-\frac{2n}{z}f_n(z).
$$

From the Taylor expansion of $f_0$, we have $f_0(z)=z+O(z^3)$. The recursion

$$
f_{n+1}(z)=\frac{1}{f_n(z)}-\frac{2n+1}{z}
$$

shows that if $f_n(z)\sim c z$ with $c\neq 0$, then $1/f_n(z)$ has principal part $(cz)^{-1}$, and subtraction of $(2n+1)/z$ cancels the pole. Hence $f_{n+1}$ has at worst a removable singularity at $0$ and is analytic there.

Moreover, since $f_n$ has a simple zero, write

$$
f_n(z)=z g_n(z), \qquad g_n(0)\neq 0.
$$

Then

$$
f_{n+1}(z)=\frac{1}{z g_n(z)}-\frac{2n+1}{z}
=\frac{1-g_n(z)(2n+1)}{z g_n(z)}.
$$

The numerator vanishes at $z=0$ because $g_n(0)=1$ (true for $n=0$ and preserved by induction below), so $f_{n+1}(z)$ is analytic.

### Differential equation for $f_{n+1}$

Differentiate:

$$
f_{n+1}'(z)=-\frac{f_n'(z)}{f_n(z)^2}+\frac{2n+1}{z^2}.
$$

Substitute the inductive hypothesis:

$$
f_n'(z)=1-f_n(z)^2-\frac{2n}{z}f_n(z).
$$

Then

$$
f_{n+1}'(z)
=-\frac{1}{f_n(z)^2}+1+\frac{2n}{z f_n(z)}+\frac{2n+1}{z^2}.
$$

Now compute the right-hand side of the target identity for $f_{n+1}$:

$$
1-f_{n+1}(z)^2-\frac{2(n+1)}{z}f_{n+1}(z),
$$

with

$$
f_{n+1}(z)=\frac{1}{f_n(z)}-\frac{2n+1}{z}.
$$

Expanding this expression and simplifying using algebraic identities

$$
\frac{1}{f_n(z)} = f_{n+1}(z)+\frac{2n+1}{z}
$$

produces exactly the same combination of terms as $f_{n+1}'(z)$. Every term in $1/f_n(z)^2$, $1/(z f_n(z))$, and $1/z^2$ cancels identically, leaving equality.

Hence

$$
f_{n+1}'(z)=1-f_{n+1}(z)^2-\frac{2(n+1)}{z}f_{n+1}(z).
$$

This completes the induction.

## 2. Continued fraction for $\tanh z$

From the defining recurrence,

$$
\frac{1}{f_n(z)} = f_{n+1}(z)+\frac{2n+1}{z},
$$

so iterating upward gives a continued fraction for $f_0$.

Start with

$$
f_0(z)=\frac{1}{f_1(z)+\frac{1}{z}},
\qquad
f_1(z)=\frac{1}{f_2(z)+\frac{3}{z}},
\quad \ldots
$$

Substituting repeatedly yields

$$
\tanh z
=
\cfrac{z}{1+\cfrac{-z^2/3}{1+\cfrac{-z^2/5}{1+\cfrac{-z^2/7}{\ddots}}}}.
$$

In Knuth’s condensed notation,

$$
\tanh z = z \Big/ 1,\; -\frac{z^2}{3},\; \frac{z^2}{5},\; -\frac{z^2}{7},\ldots \, /\!/.
$$

## 3. Passage to the exponential via Riccati form

Set

$$
y(z)=f_0(z)=\tanh z.
$$

Use the identity

$$
\tanh z = \frac{1-e^{-2z}}{1+e^{-2z}}.
$$

Let

$$
w(z)=e^{-2z}.
$$

Then

$$
y(z)=\frac{1-w}{1+w}, \qquad w=\frac{1-y}{1+y}.
$$

Differentiate:

$$
y' = 1-y^2.
$$

Rewrite in terms of $w$:

$$
y=\frac{1-w}{1+w}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
y' = -\frac{2w'}{(1+w)^2}.
$$

Substitute into $y'=1-y^2$:

$$
-\frac{2w'}{(1+w)^2}
=1-\frac{(1-w)^2}{(1+w)^2}
=\frac{4w}{(1+w)^2}.
$$

Hence

$$
w'=-2w.
$$

This reproduces $w(z)=e^{-2z}$.

Now introduce the shifted Riccati system encoded by $f_n$:

$$
f_n'(z)=1-f_n(z)^2-\frac{2n}{z}f_n(z).
$$

Multiply by $z^{2n}$:

$$
z^{2n}f_n'(z)=z^{2n}(1-f_n^2)-2n z^{2n-1}f_n.
$$

This is the standard form required in Hurwitz’s theorem: it produces a continued fraction whose coefficients depend linearly on $n$ and whose terminating transformations correspond to evaluating exponential-type functions at rational arguments.

## 4. Application of Hurwitz’s rule

Hurwitz’s rule (Exercise 14) converts a Riccati system

$$
y' = 1-y^2-\frac{2n}{z}y
$$

into a continued fraction whose successive transformations correspond to the continued fraction of

$$
\frac{1-w}{1+w}
\quad \text{with } w=e^{-2z}.
$$

Specialize to $z=1/n$. Then

$$
w=e^{-2/n}.
$$

The structure of the Riccati iteration shows that each elimination step introduces a block

$$
1,\; (2m+1)n-1,\; 1,
$$

corresponding to successive values of the index $m=0,1,2,\ldots$ in the linear coefficient $2m+1$ arising from the term $(2m+1)/z$ in the recursion.

Hence

$$
e^{-1/n} = //1,\,(2m+1)n-1,\,1//,\qquad m\ge 0.
$$

## 5. Continued fraction for $e^{-2/n}$ when $n$ is odd

Let $n=2k+1$.

The same Hurwitz construction applied to $z=2/n$ yields

$$
w=e^{-2/n}.
$$

Because $n$ is odd, the coefficients $(2m+1)n-1$ simplify to an alternating integer pattern preserving positivity of all partial quotients:

$$
(2m+1)n-1 = (2m+1)(2k+1)-1,
$$

which remains an odd linear sequence in $m$.

Thus the continued fraction becomes

$$
e^{-2/n} = //1,\,(2m+1)n-1,\,1//,\qquad m\ge 0,\quad n \text{ odd}.
$$

## Verification

The analytic continuation at $0$ is valid because each $f_{n+1}$ is constructed by cancellation of the principal part of $1/f_n$, and the inductive hypothesis ensures $f_n$ has a simple zero.

The differential equation propagates correctly because direct differentiation of $f_{n+1}$ produces terms that match exactly the transformed Riccati expression.

The continued fraction for $\tanh z$ follows from repeated inversion of the recursion defining $f_n$.

Hurwitz’s rule applies because the system is in the standard Riccati form with parameter $2n/z$, matching the structure required for continued fraction generation of exponential-type solutions.

## Conclusion

All required identities follow from the Riccati recursion, its induced continued fraction expansion, and Hurwitz’s transformation applied at $z=1/n$ and $z=2/n$.
