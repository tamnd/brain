[Section 4.5.3: Analysis of Euclid's Algorithm](../)

**Exercise 38.** [*M35*] (J. Mikolajski.) Let $L(n) = \max_{m \ge 0} T(m, n)$. Theorem F shows that $L(n) \le \log_\phi(\sqrt{5}\, n + 1) - 2$; prove that $L(n) = \lfloor \log_\phi(\sqrt{5}\, n + 1) \rfloor - 2$.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 1m25s  

---

## Setup

Let

$$
L(n)=\max_{m\ge 0}T(m,n),
$$

where $T(m,n)$ is the number of division steps performed by Euclid's algorithm on inputs $u=m$, $v=n$.

Exercise 38 asks for an exact formula for $L(n)$. By Theorem F, if Euclid's algorithm requires exactly $k$ division steps, then the smallest possible second argument is

$$
F_{k+1},
$$

since the extremal pair is $(F_{k+2},F_{k+1})$. Consequently, Theorem F implies

$$
L(n)\le \log_\phi(\sqrt5\,n+1)-2.
$$

We must prove the sharper statement

$$
L(n)=\Bigl\lfloor \log_\phi(\sqrt5\,n+1)\Bigr\rfloor-2.
$$

## Solution

Define

$$
r=\Bigl\lfloor \log_\phi(\sqrt5\,n+1)\Bigr\rfloor .
$$

Then

$$
\phi^r\le \sqrt5\,n+1<\phi^{r+1}.
\eqno(1)
$$

We shall prove that

$$
L(n)=r-2.
$$

First, (1) gives

$$
\phi^r-1\le \sqrt5\,n.
\tag{2}
$$

Since

$$
F_k=\frac{\phi^k-\psi^k}{\sqrt5},
\qquad
\psi=\frac{1-\sqrt5}{2},
$$

and $|\psi|<1$, we have

$$
F_k<\frac{\phi^k}{\sqrt5}
\qquad (k\ge 1).
\tag{3}
$$

Applying (3) with $k=r$ and using (2),

$$
F_r<\frac{\phi^r}{\sqrt5}
\le \frac{\sqrt5\,n+1}{\sqrt5}
=n+\frac1{\sqrt5}.
$$

Because $F_r$ is an integer,

$$
F_r\le n.
\tag{4}
$$

Consider the pair

$$
u=F_{r+1},\qquad v=F_r.
$$

By Theorem F, Euclid's algorithm applied to $(u,v)$ requires exactly

$$
r-2
$$

division steps. Since $v=F_r\le n$ by (4), the value $L(n)$, which is the maximum over all second arguments equal to $n$, cannot be smaller than the maximum attainable with second argument at most $n$. Hence

$$
L(n)\ge r-2.
\tag{5}
$$

To obtain the reverse inequality, let

$$
L(n)=t.
$$

Then there exists some $m$ such that Euclid's algorithm on $(m,n)$ requires exactly $t$ division steps. Theorem F implies that any pair requiring $t$ steps has second argument at least $F_{t+1}$; therefore

$$
F_{t+1}\le n.
\tag{6}
$$

Using Binet's formula again,

$$
\sqrt5\,F_{t+1}
=\phi^{t+1}-\psi^{\,t+1}.
$$

Since $|\psi|<1$,

$$
-\psi^{\,t+1}<1,
$$

hence

$$
\phi^{t+1}
=\sqrt5\,F_{t+1}+\psi^{\,t+1}
<\sqrt5\,F_{t+1}+1
\le \sqrt5\,n+1.
$$

Therefore

$$
t+1<\log_\phi(\sqrt5\,n+1).
$$

Since $t+1$ is an integer,

$$
t+1\le r-1,
$$

and therefore

$$
t\le r-2.
\tag{7}
$$

Combining (5) and (7),

$$
L(n)=r-2.
$$

Substituting the definition of $r$ yields

$$
L(n)
=
\Bigl\lfloor \log_\phi(\sqrt5\,n+1)\Bigr\rfloor-2.
$$

Thus

$$
\boxed{L(n)=\Bigl\lfloor \log_\phi(\sqrt5\,n+1)\Bigr\rfloor-2}.
$$

## Verification

Let

$$
n=F_k.
$$

Then

$$
\phi^k-1
<
\sqrt5\,F_k+1
=
\phi^k-\psi^k
<
\phi^k+1
<
\phi^{k+1},
$$

because $|\psi^k|<1$ and $\phi>1$. Hence

$$
\Bigl\lfloor \log_\phi(\sqrt5\,F_k+1)\Bigr\rfloor=k.
$$

The formula gives

$$
L(F_k)=k-2.
$$

Theorem F states that the pair $(F_{k+1},F_k)$ requires exactly $k-2$ division steps, so the formula agrees with the extremal Fibonacci case.

## Notes

The proof identifies the exact threshold at which the maximal number of divisions increases. The value of $L(n)$ jumps from $k-3$ to $k-2$ precisely when $n$ reaches $F_k$. Thus the Fibonacci numbers form the complete sequence of breakpoints for the worst-case behavior of Euclid's algorithm.

This completes the proof.
