## Problem Understanding

We are asked to compute the product of the 2×2 matrices

$$
M_0 \cdot M_1 \cdot M_2 \cdots M_n,
$$

where

$$
M_0 = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0\end{pmatrix}, \quad 
M_k = \begin{pmatrix}x_1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0\end{pmatrix} \text{ for } k \ge 1.
$$

The goal is to evaluate this product explicitly, not numerically for specific values, in terms of the entries and their recurrence.

Each matrix is 2×2. Observing the structure, we note that both matrices have the form

$$
\begin{pmatrix}a & 1 \\ 1 & 0\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Multiplying matrices of this form produces a recurrence relation in the top-left entry and in the bottom-left entry, similar to Fibonacci sequences. The problem is to exploit this structure and provide a closed form for the product.

## Approach

The brute-force approach would be to multiply each matrix sequentially, keeping the entries symbolic. Let us denote

$$
\begin{pmatrix}p_k & q_k \\ r_k & s_k\end{pmatrix} = M_0 M_1 \cdots M_k.
$$

From the first multiplication:

$$
M_0 \cdot M_1 = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0\end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix}x_1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}x_1+1 & 1 \\ x_1 & 1\end{pmatrix}.
$$

We see that if we multiply by another $M_1$, the top-left entry satisfies the recurrence

$$
p_{k+1} = x_1 p_k + p_{k-1}, \quad p_0 = 1, \quad p_1 = x_1 + 1.
$$

Similarly, the bottom-left entry $r_k$ satisfies the same recurrence with initial conditions $r_0 = 1, r_1 = x_1$. This is exactly a Fibonacci-type recurrence with variable coefficient $x_1$.

Therefore, rather than multiplying all matrices explicitly, we define sequences $F_k$ and $G_k$ satisfying

$$
F_0 = 1, \quad F_1 = x_1 + 1, \quad F_{k+1} = x_1 F_k + F_{k-1},
$$

$$
G_0 = 1, \quad G_1 = x_1, \quad G_{k+1} = x_1 G_k + G_{k-1}.
$$

Then, after multiplying $M_0$ with $n$ copies of $M_1$, the product matrix is

$$
\begin{pmatrix}F_n & F_{n-1} \\ G_n & G_{n-1}\end{pmatrix}.
$$

## Corrected Solution

1. Denote $F_n$ as the top-left entry after $n$ copies of $M_1$, and $G_n$ as the bottom-left entry.
2. Use the recurrence relations:

$$
F_{k+1} = x_1 F_k + F_{k-1}, \quad F_0 = 1, \quad F_1 = x_1 + 1,
$$

$$
G_{k+1} = x_1 G_k + G_{k-1}, \quad G_0 = 1, \quad G_1 = x_1.
$$

1. The top-right entry is $F_{n-1}$, the bottom-right entry is $G_{n-1}$.
2. Therefore the final matrix is

$$
\begin{pmatrix}F_n & F_{n-1} \\ G_n & G_{n-1}\end{pmatrix}.
$$

### Verification

Multiply sequentially for small $n$ to confirm:

- $n=1$:

$$
M_0 M_1 = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0\end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix}x_1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}x_1+1 & 1 \\ x_1 & 1\end{pmatrix},
$$

matches $F_1 = x_1 + 1, F_0 = 1, G_1 = x_1, G_0 = 1$.

- $n=2$:

$$
M_0 M_1^2 = \begin{pmatrix}F_2 & F_1 \\ G_2 & G_1\end{pmatrix}, \quad F_2 = x_1 F_1 + F_0 = x_1(x_1+1) + 1 = x_1^2 + x_1 + 1,
$$

$$
G_2 = x_1 G_1 + G_0 = x_1^2 + 1,
$$

correctly produces the entries by direct multiplication.

### Python Implementation

```
def matrix_product(x1, n):
    # initialize sequences
    F0, F1 = 1, x1 + 1
    G0, G1 = 1, x1
    if n == 0:
        return [[1, 1], [1, 0]]
    elif n == 1:
        return [[F1, F0], [G1, G0]]
    for _ in range(2, n + 1):
        F_next = x1 * F1 + F0
        G_next = x1 * G1 + G0
        F0, F1 = F1, F_next
        G0, G1 = G1, G_next
    return [[F1, F0], [G1, G0]]

# example usage:
x1 = 2
n = 3
product_matrix = matrix_product(x1, n)
print(product_matrix)
```

This computes the matrix product symbolically for any $x_1$ and number of repetitions $n$.

### Explanation

The key insight is to recognize the 2×2 matrix structure produces a linear recurrence. The top-left entry satisfies $F_{k+1} = x_1 F_k + F_{k-1}$ with initial conditions derived from the first multiplication. The bottom-left entry satisfies the same recurrence with shifted initial conditions. Once the recurrence is identified, the matrix product reduces to computing the $n$-th term of these sequences. This method avoids explicit symbolic multiplication of $n+1$ matrices, which would be cumbersome.
