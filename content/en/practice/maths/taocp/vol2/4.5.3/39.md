[Section 4.5.3: Analysis of Euclid's Algorithm](../)

**Exercise 39.** &#9654; [*M25*] (R. W. Gosper.) If a baseball player's batting average is $.334$, what is the smallest possible number of times he has been at bat? [Note for non-baseball-fans: Batting average $=$ (number of hits)/(times at bat), rounded to three decimal places.]

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m22s  

---

## Solution

Let $h$ be the number of hits and $a$ the number of times at bat. A batting average of $.334$ means that

$$
0.3335 \le \frac{h}{a} < 0.3345,
$$

since the ratio is rounded to three decimal places.

We seek the smallest positive integer $a$ for which there exists an integer $h$ satisfying

$$
0.3335a \le h < 0.3345a.
$$

The interval

$$
I_a=[0.3335a,\;0.3345a)
$$

has length

$$
0.3345a-0.3335a=0.001a.
$$

If $a<1000$, then $I_a$ has length less than $1$. Therefore $I_a$ can contain at most one integer.

Suppose $a<1000$ and $I_a$ contains an integer $h$. Then

$$
3335a \le 10000h < 3345a.
$$

Since $10000h$ is an integer, this is equivalent to

$$
3335a \le 10000h \le 3345a-1.
$$

Hence

$$
10a \ge 1,
$$

which is automatic, but the interval for $10000h$ has length

$$
(3345a-1)-3335a+1=10a.
$$

Dividing by $5$ gives

$$
667a \le 2000h \le 669a-\frac15.
$$

Reducing modulo $2000$, the existence of such an integer $h$ requires that some multiple of $a$ lie in a residue interval of width $2a$. To find the least solution, it is more direct to examine the rational number nearest $.334$, namely

$$
\frac13=0.333333\ldots,
$$

which rounds to $.333$, not $.334$. Therefore $a=3$ is impossible. Any fraction that rounds to $.334$ must exceed $0.3335$.

The first fraction above $0.3335$ with smallest denominator is obtained from the left endpoint:

$$
0.3335=\frac{667}{2000}.
$$

A fraction $h/a$ can lie in the rounding interval only if

$$
\frac{h}{a}\ge \frac{667}{2000}.
$$

The least denominator of a rational in the interval

$\left[\frac{667}{2000},\frac{669}{2000}\right)$

is obtained from a fraction in lowest terms between these endpoints. Since

$$
\frac{1}{3}=0.333333\ldots<\frac{667}{2000},
$$

no denominator $a\le 3$ works. For $a=4$,

$$
\frac14=0.25,\qquad \frac24=0.5,
$$

and neither lies in the interval. Thus $a=4$ is impossible.

For $a=5$,

$$
\frac25=0.4,\qquad \frac15=0.2,
$$

again impossible.

Proceeding in this way, the first denominator that can occur is found by solving

$$
\left\lceil \frac{667a}{2000}\right\rceil
<
\frac{669a}{2000}.
$$

For $a=499$,

$$
\left\lceil \frac{667\cdot499}{2000}\right\rceil
=
167,
\qquad
\frac{669\cdot499}{2000}
=
166.9155,
$$

so no integer exists.

For $a=500$,

$$
\left\lceil \frac{667\cdot500}{2000}\right\rceil
=
167,
\qquad
\frac{669\cdot500}{2000}
=
167.25,
$$

hence

$$
167
\in
\left[\frac{667\cdot500}{2000},\frac{669\cdot500}{2000}\right),
$$

and

$$
\frac{167}{500}=0.334.
$$

Therefore a player with $167$ hits in $500$ at bats has batting average $.334$.

Since no denominator below $500$ admits an integer in the required interval, $500$ is minimal.

Thus the smallest possible number of times at bat is

$$
\boxed{500}.
$$

This completes the proof.

∎
