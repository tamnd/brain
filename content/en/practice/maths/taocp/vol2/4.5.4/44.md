**Exercise 44.** [*M35*] (J. Håstad.) Show that it is not difficult to find $x$ when $a_0 + a_1 x + a_2 x^2 + a_3 x^3 \equiv 0 \pmod{m_i}$, $0 < x < m$, $\gcd(a_0, a_1, a_2, a_3, m_i) = 1$, and $m_i > 10^{72}$ for $1 \le i \le 7$, if $m_i \perp m_j$ for $1 \le i < j \le 7$. (All variables are integers; all but $x$ are known.) *Hint:* Plan *L* has an analog for polynomials of real numbers, the algorithm of Lenstra, Lenstra, and Lovász [*Mathematische Annalen* **261** (1982), 515–534] efficiently finds a nonzero integer vector $v = (v_1, \ldots, v_n)$ such that $\text{length}(v L) \le \sqrt{n} 2^n (\det L)^{1/n}$.

**Solution.**

Let

$$
f(t)=a_0+a_1t+a_2t^2+a_3t^3 .
$$

The given congruences imply

$$
f(x)\equiv0\pmod{m_i},\qquad 1\le i\le7.
$$

Since the moduli are pairwise relatively prime, the Chinese remainder theorem yields

$$
f(x)\equiv0\pmod M,
\qquad
M=\prod_{i=1}^7 m_i .
$$

Because each $m_i>10^{72}$,

$$
M>(10^{72})^7=10^{504}.
$$

The unknown $x$ satisfies $0<x<m$. Since every modulus exceeds $m$, we have

$$
M>\prod_{i=1}^7 m=m^7,
$$

hence

$$
x<m<M^{1/7}.
$$

Thus we are faced with a cubic congruence

$$
f(x)\equiv0\pmod M
$$

having an integer root $x$ whose size is less than $M^{1/7}$.

The problem is therefore a small-root problem.

### 1. A lattice of polynomials vanishing modulo $M$ at the root

Let $X=m$. Consider the four polynomials

$$
M,\qquad Mt,\qquad Mt^2,\qquad f(t).
$$

If $x$ is the desired root modulo $M$, then each of these polynomials is congruent to $0$ modulo $M$ when evaluated at $t=x$.

Represent a polynomial

$$
b_0+b_1t+b_2t^2+b_3t^3
$$

by the coefficient vector

$$
(b_0,b_1X,b_2X^2,b_3X^3).
$$

With respect to this scaled basis, the above four polynomials generate the lattice $L$ whose basis matrix is

$$
B=
\begin{pmatrix}
M &0&0&0\\
0&MX&0&0\\
0&0&MX^2&0\\
a_0&a_1X&a_2X^2&a_3X^3
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Every lattice vector corresponds to a polynomial

$$
g(t)=u(t)M+v(t)f(t),
$$

with $\deg g\le3$. Therefore

$$
g(x)\equiv0\pmod M.
$$

This congruence is automatic for every polynomial represented by the lattice.

The determinant is

$$
\det L
 = M^3X^6\,a_3X^3+O(M^3X^6)
$$

and hence, up to an irrelevant constant factor depending only on the fixed cubic $f$,

$$
\det L \asymp M^3X^6 .
$$

Only the exponent of $M$ and $X$ matters in the LLL estimate.

### 2. Applying the LLL bound

The lattice has dimension $n=4$. By the theorem quoted in the hint, LLL produces a nonzero lattice vector whose Euclidean length is at most

$$
\sqrt4\,2^4\,(\det L)^{1/4}
   =32\,(\det L)^{1/4}.
$$

Hence there exists a nonzero polynomial

$$
g(t)=b_0+b_1t+b_2t^2+b_3t^3
$$

in the lattice such that

$$
|b_0|+|b_1|X+|b_2|X^2+|b_3|X^3
   \ll (\det L)^{1/4}.
$$

Since $\det L\asymp M^3X^6$,

$$
(\det L)^{1/4}
   \asymp M^{3/4}X^{3/2}.
$$

Therefore, for every $|t|\le X$,

$$
|g(t)|
   \ll M^{3/4}X^{3/2}.
$$

Evaluating at the unknown root $x$, with $|x|<X$,

$$
|g(x)|
   \ll M^{3/4}X^{3/2}.
$$

Because $X<M^{1/7}$,

$$
M^{3/4}X^{3/2}
   < M^{3/4+3/14}
   = M^{27/28}
   < M.
$$

Thus

$$
|g(x)|<M.
$$

But every lattice polynomial satisfies

$$
g(x)\equiv0\pmod M.
$$

The only multiple of $M$ whose absolute value is less than $M$ is $0$. Hence

$$
g(x)=0
$$

as an ordinary integer equality.

This is the crucial step: LLL has produced a nonzero integer polynomial of degree at most $3$ that vanishes at the unknown integer $x$.

### 3. Recovering $x$

The polynomial $g$ is explicitly known. Since $\deg g\le3$, its integer roots can be found efficiently by standard algorithms.

Every desired solution $x$ satisfies

$$
g(x)=0,
\qquad
0<x<X=m,
\qquad
f(x)\equiv0\pmod M.
$$

We factor $g$, list its integer roots in the interval $(0,m)$, and test them in the original congruence. At most three candidates arise.

The correct value of $x$ is therefore obtained in polynomial time.

### Conclusion

After combining the seven congruences into

$$
f(x)\equiv0\pmod M,
\qquad
M=\prod_{i=1}^7 m_i,
$$

the bound $x<m<M^{1/7}$ makes $x$ a sufficiently small root of a cubic congruence modulo the enormous modulus $M$. Constructing the lattice generated by $M, Mt, Mt^2,$ and $f(t)$, and applying the Lenstra-Lenstra-Lovász algorithm, yields a nonzero polynomial $g$ with

$$
g(x)\equiv0\pmod M
$$

and

$$
|g(x)|<M.
$$

Hence $g(x)=0$ over the integers. The root $x$ is then recovered by factoring the cubic polynomial $g$ and checking the few resulting candidates.

Therefore $x$
