**Exercise 3.** [*M20*] Show that there is a number $P$ with the following property: If $1000 \le n \le 1000000$, then $n$ is prime if and only if $\gcd(n, P) = 1$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m49s  

---

## Solution

Let us construct a number $P$ with the desired property. Consider the set of all primes less than $1000$:

$2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, \ldots, 997.$

Let

$P = \prod_{p < 1000} p.$

We claim that, for any integer $n$ satisfying $1000 \le n \le 1000000$, we have

$n \text{ is prime} \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \gcd(n, P) = 1.$

First, suppose $n$ is prime and $n \ge 1000$. Then none of the primes less than $1000$ divides $n$, because $n$ itself is at least $1000$. Hence $n$ shares no prime factors with $P$, and therefore $\gcd(n, P) = 1$.

Conversely, suppose $1000 \le n \le 1000000$ and $\gcd(n, P) = 1$. If $n$ were composite, it would have a prime factor $p \le \sqrt{n} \le \sqrt{1000000} = 1000$. But then $p$ would divide both $n$ and $P$, contradicting the assumption that $\gcd(n, P) = 1$. Therefore $n$ must be prime.

This completes the proof. ∎

The construction is explicit: $P$ is the product of all primes less than $1000$. Its use in primality testing is immediate: for any $n$ in the given range, checking whether $\gcd(n, P) = 1$ suffices to determine if $n$ is prime.
