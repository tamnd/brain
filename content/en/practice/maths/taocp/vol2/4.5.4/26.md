**Exercise 26.** &#9654; [*M25*] *[M25]* (H. C. Pocklington, 1914.) Let $N = fr + 1$, where $0 < r \le f + 1$. Prove that $N$ is prime if, for every prime divisor $p$ of $f$, there is an integer $x_p$ such that $x_p^{N-1} \bmod N = \gcd(x_p^{(N-1)/p} - 1, N) = 1$.

**Solve time:** 3m56s

## Solution

Let

$$
N=fr+1,\qquad 0<r\le f+1,
$$

and suppose that for every prime divisor $p$ of $f$ there exists an integer $x_p$ such that

$$
x_p^{N-1}\equiv 1 \pmod N,
$$

and

$$
\gcd\!\left(x_p^{(N-1)/p}-1,\;N\right)=1.
$$

We shall prove that $N$ is prime.

Assume, to the contrary, that $N$ is composite. Then $N$ has a prime divisor $q$ with

$$
q\le \sqrt N,
$$

for example the least prime divisor of $N$.

Fix a prime divisor $p$ of $f$. Since $q\mid N$, the congruence

$$
x_p^{N-1}\equiv1\pmod N
$$

implies

$$
x_p^{N-1}\equiv1\pmod q.
$$

Also,

$$
\gcd\!\left(x_p^{(N-1)/p}-1,N\right)=1
$$

implies that $q$ does not divide $x_p^{(N-1)/p}-1$. Hence

$$
x_p^{(N-1)/p}\not\equiv1\pmod q.
$$

Let $d$ be the multiplicative order of $x_p$ modulo $q$. Since

$$
x_p^{N-1}\equiv1\pmod q,
$$

we have

$$
d\mid (N-1).
$$

If $p\nmid d$, then $d$ divides $(N-1)/p$, because $d\mid(N-1)$ and $p$ is prime. It would follow that

$$
x_p^{(N-1)/p}\equiv1\pmod q,
$$

contrary to the previous paragraph. Therefore $p\mid d$.

Since $d$ is the order of an element of the multiplicative group $(\mathbb Z/q\mathbb Z)^\times$, Lagrange's theorem gives

$$
d\mid(q-1).
$$

Hence $p\mid(q-1)$.

This argument is valid for every prime divisor $p$ of $f$. Therefore every prime factor of $f$ divides $q-1$. Since $f\mid(N-1)$, the exponent of each prime factor of $f$ in $d$ is at least as large as its exponent in $f$; equivalently, for each prime power $p^e\Vert f$, the same argument applied repeatedly shows that $p^e\mid d$. Thus

$$
f\mid d,
$$

and therefore

$$
f\mid(q-1).
$$

Since $q\le\sqrt N$ and $N=fr+1$ with $r\le f+1$,

$$
N=fr+1\le f(f+1)+1=f^2+f+1.
$$

Hence

$$
q\le\sqrt N\le\sqrt{f^2+f+1}.
$$

But

$$
f^2+f+1<(f+1)^2=f^2+2f+1,
$$

so

$$
\sqrt{f^2+f+1}<f+1.
$$

Therefore

$$
q<f+1,
$$

and consequently

$$
q-1<f.
$$

On the other hand, $f\mid(q-1)$ and $q-1>0$. The only positive multiple of $f$ that is strictly less than $f$ is $0$, which is impossible. This contradiction shows that no such prime divisor $q$ can exist.

Therefore $N$ is not composite. Hence $N$ is prime.

$$
\boxed{N\ \text{is prime}.}
$$
