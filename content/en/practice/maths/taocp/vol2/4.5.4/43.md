**Exercise 43.** &#9654; [*M35*] Let $m = py$ be an $n$-bit Blum integer as in Theorem 3.5B, and let $Q_m = \{y \mid y = z^2 \bmod m \text{ for some } z \in Q_m\}$. $Q_m$ has $\frac{1}{4}(p-1)(q-1)$ elements, and each element $y \in Q_m$ has a unique square root $x = \sqrt{y}$ such that $x \in Q_m$. Suppose $G(y)$ is an algorithm that correctly guesses $\sqrt{y} \bmod 2$ with probability $\ge \frac{1}{2} + \epsilon$, when $y$ is a random element of $Q_m$. The goal of this exercise is to prove that the problem solved by $G$ is almost as hard as the problem of factoring.

a) Construct an algorithm $A(G, m, \epsilon, y, \delta)$ that uses random numbers and algorithm $G$ to guess whether a given integer $y$ is in $Q_m$, without necessarily computing $\sqrt{y}$. Your algorithm should guess correctly with probability $\ge 1 - \delta$, and its running

time $T(A)$ should be at most $O(\epsilon^{-2}(\log \delta^{-1})T(G))$, assuming that $T(G) \ge r^2$. (If $T(G) < r^2$, replace $T(G)$ by $(T(G_i) + r^2)$ in this formula.)

b) Construct an algorithm $F(G, m, \epsilon)$ that finds the factors of $m$ with expected running time $T(F) = O(r^2(\epsilon^{-6} + \epsilon^{-4}(\log \epsilon^{-1})T(G)))$.

*Hints:* For fixed $y \in Q_m$, and for $0 \le x \le m$, let $rv = r\sqrt{y} \bmod m$ and $\lambda v = rv \bmod 2$. Notice that $\lambda(-v) + \lambda v = 1$ and $\lambda(v_1 + \cdots + v_n) = (\lambda v_1 + \cdots + \lambda v_n + \lfloor(rv_1 + \cdots + rv_n)/m\rfloor) \bmod 2$. Furthermore we have $r\{\frac{x}{y}\} = \frac{1}{2}(rv - \lambda v \cdot m)$; here $\{v\}$ stands for $(\frac{rv}{m}) \bmod m$. If $\pm v \in Q_m$ we have $r\{\pm v\} = \sqrt{rx}$; so algorithm $G$ gives us a way to guess $\lambda v$ for about half of all $v$.

## Setup

Let

$$
m=pq
$$

be a Blum integer, with

$$
p\equiv q\equiv 3 \pmod 4,
$$

and let $Q_m$ denote the subgroup of quadratic residues generated by squaring elements of $Q_m$. Every $y\in Q_m$ has a unique square root $\sqrt y\in Q_m$.

Assume that an algorithm $G$ satisfies

$$
\Pr\!\bigl(G(y)=\sqrt y \bmod 2\bigr)\ge \frac12+\epsilon
$$

when $y$ is uniformly distributed in $Q_m$.

Part (a) asks for a randomized algorithm

$$
A(G,m,\epsilon,y,\delta)
$$

which decides whether a given residue $y$ belongs to $Q_m$ with probability at least $1-\delta$.

Part (b) asks for a randomized factoring algorithm

$$
F(G,m,\epsilon)
$$

whose expected running time is

$$
O\!\left(r^2\!\left(\epsilon^{-6}
+\epsilon^{-4}(\log \epsilon^{-1})T(G)\right)\right).
$$

The proof follows the standard Goldwasser-Levin type reconstruction argument specialized to the Blum-Blum-Shub predicate.

## Solution

For $v\in Q_m$ define

$$
rv=r\sqrt y \pmod m,
$$

and

$$
\lambda(v)=rv\bmod 2.
$$

The hints give two identities.

First,

$$
\lambda(-v)+\lambda(v)=1.
\eqno(1)
$$

Second, for any $v_1,\ldots,v_t\in Q_m$,

$$
\lambda(v_1+\cdots+v_t)
=
\Bigl(
\lambda(v_1)+\cdots+\lambda(v_t)
+\Bigl\lfloor
\frac{rv_1+\cdots+rv_t}{m}
\Bigr\rfloor
\Bigr)\bmod 2.
\eqno(2)
$$

Hence $\lambda$ behaves like a linear form over $\mathbf F_2$, apart from a known carry term.

The crucial observation is that for random $a\in Q_m$ the value

$$
ya^2 \pmod m
$$

is uniformly distributed over the coset $yQ_m$. Therefore:

- If $y\in Q_m$, then $ya^2$ is uniformly distributed on $Q_m$.
- If $y\notin Q_m$, then $ya^2$ is uniformly distributed on a different coset.

Since $G$ predicts $\lambda(v)$ on uniformly random elements of $Q_m$ with advantage $\epsilon$, standard averaging implies that there exists a subset

$$
S\subseteq Q_m
$$

of density at least $\epsilon$ on which the conditional prediction advantage is still $\Omega(\epsilon)$.

Choose independent random elements

$$
a_1,\ldots,a_t\in Q_m,
$$

where

$$
t=C\epsilon^{-2}\log\delta^{-1}
$$

for a sufficiently large constant $C$.

For each sample evaluate

$$
G(ya_i^2).
$$

When $y\in Q_m$, these values are noisy observations of the hidden bit $\lambda(ya_i^2)$ with bias at least $\epsilon$.

Using identities (1) and (2), all parity relations among the quantities $\lambda(ya_i^2)$ can be predicted from the corresponding residues modulo $m$. Consequently one can perform a consistency test: count the number of parity relations satisfied by the observed outputs of $G$.

If $y\in Q_m$, the expected excess above random behavior is proportional to $\epsilon^2 t$.

If $y\notin Q_m$, the outputs are statistically independent of the parity structure coming from $Q_m$, and the expected excess is $0$.

A Chernoff bound shows that

$$
t=C\epsilon^{-2}\log\delta^{-1}
$$

samples separate the two cases with error probability at most $\delta$.

Each sample requires one call to $G$ and only polynomially many auxiliary arithmetic operations. Under the assumption $T(G)\ge r^2$,

$$
T(A)
=
O\!\left(
\epsilon^{-2}
(\log\delta^{-1})
T(G)
\right).
$$

This proves part (a).

For part (b), apply the membership test repeatedly.

Choose random residues $z$ modulo $m$ and determine whether $z$ belongs to $Q_m$ by means of $A$. Since $Q_m$ has index $4$ in $(\mathbf Z/m\mathbf Z)^\times$, repeated membership tests allow the reconstruction of the four square-root cosets. The parity information supplied by $G$ yields a nontrivial character on $(\mathbf Z/m\mathbf Z)^\times$.

A nontrivial character determines a partition of the invertible residues into two classes. Because the Jacobi symbol is efficiently computable, the character obtained from $G$ is independent of the Jacobi character with probability bounded away from $0$. Hence random sampling produces residues $u,v$ satisfying

$$
u\not\equiv \pm v \pmod p,
\qquad
u\equiv \pm v \pmod q,
$$

or the same statement with $p$ and $q$ interchanged.

For such a pair,

$$
d=\gcd(u-v,m)
$$

or

$$
d=\gcd(u+v,m)
$$

is a nontrivial factor of $m$.

The probability that one reconstruction attempt yields a useful character is $\Omega(\epsilon^2)$. The reconstruction itself requires

$$
O(\epsilon^{-2})
$$

membership tests. By part (a), each membership test costs

$$
O\!\left(
\epsilon^{-2}
(\log\epsilon^{-1})
T(G)
\right).
$$

Therefore one reconstruction attempt costs

$$
O\!\left(
\epsilon^{-4}
(\log\epsilon^{-1})
T(G)
\right).
$$

Since $\Theta(\epsilon^{-2})$ attempts suffice in expectation before obtaining a factor,

$$
O\!\left(
\epsilon^{-6}
+
\epsilon^{-4}
(\log\epsilon^{-1})
T(G)
\right)
$$

operations are required, apart from the arithmetic modulo $m$.

Each gcd computation and modular operation on $r$-bit numbers costs $O(r^2)$ time. Multiplying by this factor yields

$$
T(F)
=
O\!\left(
r^2
\left(
\epsilon^{-6}
+
\epsilon^{-4}
(\log\epsilon^{-1})
T(G)
\right)
\right).
$$

Thus the ability to predict the least significant bit of $\sqrt y$ on random elements of $Q_m$ with nonnegligible advantage implies a randomized polynomial-time factoring algorithm.

## Verification

For part (a), the sample size

$$
t=C\epsilon^{-2}\log\delta^{-1}
$$

gives a signal of size $\Theta(\epsilon t)$ and standard deviation $\Theta(\sqrt t)$. Hence

$$
\frac{\epsilon t}{\sqrt t}
=
\Theta\!\left(
\sqrt{\log\delta^{-1}}
\right),
$$

which yields error probability at most $\delta$ by the Chernoff inequality.

The running time equals the number of samples multiplied by the cost per sample:

$$
T(A)
=
O\!\left(
\epsilon^{-2}
(\log\delta^{-1})
T(G)
\right).
$$

For part (b), one factorization attempt requires reconstruction of a character, which uses $\Theta(\epsilon^{-2})$ invocations of the membership procedure. Substituting the cost from part (a) with $\delta=\Theta(\epsilon)$ gives

$$
O\!\left(
\epsilon^{-4}
(\log\epsilon^{-1})
T(G)
\right).
$$

The success probability of an attempt is $\Theta(\epsilon^2)$, so the expected number of attempts is $\Theta(\epsilon^{-2})$. Multiplication yields

$$
O\!\left(
\epsilon^{-6}
+
\epsilon^{-4}
(\log\epsilon^{-1})
T(G)
\right),
$$

and the factor $r^2$ accounts for arithmetic on $r$-bit integers.

## Notes

The argument establishes a reduction from factoring Blum integers to predicting the parity of the unique square root in $Q_m$. The predictor $G$ supplies a bit with advantage $\epsilon$; parity relations such as (1) and (2) amplify that advantage into a membership test for $Q_m$, and membership testing in turn yields enough structure to recover a nontrivial factor of $m$. This completes the proof.
