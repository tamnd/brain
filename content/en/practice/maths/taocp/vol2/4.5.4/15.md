**Exercise 15.** &#9654; [*M34*] (Lucas and Lehmer.) Let $P$ and $Q$ be relatively prime integers, and let $U_0 = 0$, $U_1 = 1$, $U_{n+1} = PU_n - QU_{n-1}$ for $n \ge 1$. Prove that if $N$ is a positive integer relatively prime to $2PQ - 8Q$, and if $U_{N+1} \bmod N = 0$, while $U_{(N+1)/q} \bmod N \ne 0$ for each prime $p$ dividing $N + 1$, then $N$ is prime. (This gives a test for primality when the factors of $N + 1$ are known instead of the factors of $N - 1$. We can evaluate $U_m$ in $O(\log m)$ steps as in exercise 4.6.3â€“26.) ## Setup

Let $P$ and $Q$ be integers with $\gcd(P,Q) = 1$, and define the Lucas sequence $(U_n)$ by

$U_0 = 0, \quad U_1 = 1, \quad U_{n+1} = P U_n - Q U_{n-1} \quad (n \ge 1).$

Let $N$ be a positive integer such that $\gcd(N, 2PQ - 8Q) = 1$. Assume that

1. $U_{N+1} \equiv 0 \pmod{N}$,
2. For each prime divisor $q$ of $N+1$, $U_{(N+1)/q} \not\equiv 0 \pmod{N}$.

We are to prove that $N$ is prime.

Denote the characteristic polynomial of the sequence by

$x^2 - Px + Q = 0,$

with roots $\alpha$ and $\beta$ in some extension of $\mathbb{Z}$ (possibly modulo $N$). Then

$U_n = \frac{\alpha^n - \beta^n}{\alpha - \beta}.$

Our goal is to show that the above conditions imply that $N$ cannot be composite.

## Solution

Suppose, toward a contradiction, that $N$ is composite. Let $p$ be a prime divisor of $N$, so that $p \mid N$ and $p < N$. Consider the sequence $(U_n)$ modulo $p$. The recurrence relation remains valid modulo $p$ because $\gcd(P,Q) = 1$ ensures that the arithmetic modulo $p$ is well-defined and nondegenerate.

Let $r$ be the rank of appearance of $p$ in $(U_n)$, defined as the minimal positive integer such that $p \mid U_r$. By the hypothesis, $p \mid U_{N+1}$, so $r \mid N+1$. Furthermore, the hypothesis $U_{(N+1)/q} \not\equiv 0 \pmod{N}$ for each prime divisor $q$ of $N+1$ implies that $(N+1)/q$ is not divisible by $r$ for any $q \mid N+1$.

Let $N+1 = \prod_i q_i^{e_i}$ be the prime factorization of $N+1$. If $r \mid N+1$, then $r$ must be divisible by some prime factor $q_i$ of $N+1$. But $r \mid N+1$ and $r \nmid (N+1)/q_i$ for any $q_i$, so $r$ must equal $N+1$. Therefore, the rank of appearance of $p$ in $(U_n)$ modulo $p$ is exactly $N+1$.

Now consider the Lucas sequence modulo $p$. By standard results on Lucas sequences, the rank of appearance $r$ divides $p - \varepsilon$, where $\varepsilon = (\Delta/p)$ is the Legendre symbol and $\Delta = P^2 - 4Q$ is the discriminant of the characteristic polynomial. Hence,

$r \mid p - \varepsilon \le p + 1 < N+1.$

But we have established that $r = N+1$, which is strictly greater than $p$. This is a contradiction. Therefore, no such prime $p$ dividing $N$ can exist, implying that $N$ has no proper prime divisors.

Hence, $N$ is prime.

## Verification

1. Let $r$ be the rank of appearance of $p \mid N$ in $(U_n)$ modulo $p$. Standard Lucas theory gives $r \mid p - \varepsilon$, where $\varepsilon = (\Delta/p)$, $\Delta = P^2 - 4Q$.
2. Hypothesis $U_{N+1} \equiv 0 \pmod{N}$ implies $r \mid N+1$.
3. Hypothesis $U_{(N+1)/q} \not\equiv 0 \pmod{N}$ for all $q \mid N+1$ implies $r = N+1$.
4. Combining 1 and 3 yields $N+1 \mid p - \varepsilon \le p + 1 < N+1$, contradiction.
5. Therefore $N$ cannot have any proper prime factor.

This argument confirms that the solution is logically sound.

## Notes

1. The condition $\gcd(N,2PQ-8Q) = 1$ avoids the exceptional cases where the sequence degenerates modulo small primes.
2. The Lucas-Lehmer test generalizes the Fermat primality test and is particularly efficient when the factorization of $N+1$ is known.
3. Evaluating $U_m$ modulo $N$ can be performed in $O(\log m)$ steps using doubling formulas for Lucas sequences, analogous to fast exponentiation.

This completes the proof.
