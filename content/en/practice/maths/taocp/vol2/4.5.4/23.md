[Section 4.5.4: Factoring into Primes](../)

**Exercise 23.** [*M35*] The *Jacobi symbol* $\left(\frac{p}{q}\right)$ is defined to be $-1$, $0$, or $+1$ for all integers $p \ge 0$ and all odd integers $q > 1$ by the rules $\left(\frac{p}{q}\right) \equiv p^{(q-1)/2} \pmod{q}$ when $q$ is prime;

$\left(\frac{p}{q}\right) = \left(\frac{p}{q_1}\right) \cdots \left(\frac{p}{q_s}\right)$ when $q$ is the product $q_1 \ldots q_s$ of $s$ primes (not necessarily distinct). Thus it generalizes the Legendre symbol of exercise 2.4-47.

a) Prove that $\left(\frac{p}{q}\right)$ satisfies the following relationships, hence it can be computed efficiently: $\left(\frac{0}{q}\right) = 0$; $\left(\frac{1}{q}\right) = 1$; $\left(\frac{2}{q}\right) = \left(\frac{2}{q \bmod 8}\right)$; $\left(\frac{p}{q}\right) = (-1)^{(p'-1)/2} \cdot (-1)^{(q'-1)/2} \cdot \left(\frac{q}{p}\right)$; $\left(\frac{p}{q}\right) = \left(\frac{p \bmod q}{q}\right)$ when both $p$ and $q$ are odd. [The latter law, which is a reciprocity relation reducing the evaluation of $\left(\frac{p}{q}\right)$ to the evaluation of $\left(\frac{q}{p}\right)$, has been proved in exercise 2.4-47(d) when both $p$ and $q$ are prime, so you may assume its validity in that special case.]

b) (Solovay and Strassen.) Prove that if $n$ is odd but not prime, the number of integers $x$ such that $1 \le x < n$ and $0 \ne \left(\frac{x}{n}\right) \equiv x^{(n-1)/2} \pmod{n}$ is at most $\frac{1}{2}\varphi(n)$. (Thus, the following testing procedure correctly determines whether or not a given $n$ is prime, with probability at least $1/2$ for all fixed $n$: "Generate $x$ at random with $1 \le x < n$. If $0 \ne \left(\frac{x}{n}\right) \equiv x^{(n-1)/2} \pmod{n}$, say that $n$ is probably prime; otherwise say that $n$ is definitely not prime.")

c) (L. Monier.) Prove that if $n$ and $x$ are numbers for which Algorithm P concludes that "$n$ is probably prime", then $0 \ne \left(\frac{x}{n}\right) \equiv x^{(n-1)/2} \pmod{n}$. [Hence Algorithm P is always superior to the test in (b).]

## Setup

Let $p \ge 0$ be an integer and $q > 1$ an odd integer. The _Jacobi symbol_ $\left(\frac{p}{q}\right)$ is defined recursively as follows: If $q$ is prime, then

$$
\left(\frac{p}{q}\right) \equiv p^{(q-1)/2} \pmod{q},
$$

taking values in ${-1,0,1}$. If $q$ factorizes as $q = q_1 \cdots q_s$ with primes $q_i$, then

$$
\left(\frac{p}{q}\right) = \prod_{i=1}^s \left(\frac{p}{q_i}\right).
$$

We are asked in part (a) to prove the following identities:

1. $\displaystyle \left(\frac{0}{q}\right) = 0$,
2. $\displaystyle \left(\frac{1}{q}\right) = 1$,
3. $\displaystyle \left(\frac{2}{q}\right) = \left(\frac{2}{q \bmod 8}\right)$,
4. $\displaystyle \left(\frac{p}{q}\right) = (-1)^{(p'-1)/2} (-1)^{(q'-1)/2} \left(\frac{q}{p}\right)$ for odd $p,q$,
5. $\displaystyle \left(\frac{p}{q}\right) = \left(\frac{p \bmod q}{q}\right)$ for odd $p,q$.

Parts (b) and (c) concern the Solovay–Strassen primality test. We must show that if $n$ is odd and composite, then at most half of the integers $x$ with $1 \le x < n$ satisfy

$$
0 \ne \left(\frac{x}{n}\right) \equiv x^{(n-1)/2} \pmod{n},
$$

and that Algorithm P always returns "$n$ is probably prime" only for $x$ satisfying the same congruence.

## Solution

### Part (a)

The first two identities follow immediately from the definition of the Jacobi symbol for prime divisors and the multiplicative property:

1. If $p = 0$, then $0 \equiv 0^{(q_i-1)/2} \pmod{q_i}$ for every prime $q_i \mid q$, so $\left(\frac{0}{q_i}\right) = 0$ and thus $\left(\frac{0}{q}\right) = 0$.
2. If $p = 1$, then $1^{(q_i-1)/2} \equiv 1 \pmod{q_i}$ for each prime $q_i \mid q$, hence $\left(\frac{1}{q}\right) = \prod_{i=1}^s 1 = 1$.

The third identity relies on the classical formula for $\left(\frac{2}{q}\right)$ when $q$ is an odd prime $q_i$:

$$
\left(\frac{2}{q_i}\right) = (-1)^{(q_i^2-1)/8}.
$$

Since $(-1)^{(q_i^2-1)/8}$ depends only on $q_i \bmod 8$, we have

$$
\left(\frac{2}{q}\right) = \prod_{i=1}^s (-1)^{(q_i^2-1)/8} = \prod_{i=1}^s \left(\frac{2}{q_i \bmod 8}\right) = \left(\frac{2}{q \bmod 8}\right),
$$

where the last equality uses the multiplicative property of the Jacobi symbol and the fact that the exponent $((q_i^2-1)/8) \bmod 2$ depends only on $q_i \bmod 8$.

The fourth identity is the _reciprocity law_. Let $p$ and $q$ be odd and positive. If $p$ and $q$ are prime, exercise 2.4-47(d) gives

$$
\left(\frac{p}{q}\right) \left(\frac{q}{p}\right) = (-1)^{(p-1)(q-1)/4}.
$$

Write $p = p' \bmod 2$ and $q = q' \bmod 2$; the parity of $(p-1)/2$ and $(q-1)/2$ gives the exponent of $-1$, so that

$$
\left(\frac{p}{q}\right) = (-1)^{(p'-1)/2} (-1)^{(q'-1)/2} \left(\frac{q}{p}\right).
$$

For composite $q$, the multiplicative property reduces the general case to the prime case.

The fifth identity is an immediate consequence of the fact that if $p = k q + r$, then $p \equiv r \pmod{q}$, and hence

$$
\left(\frac{p}{q}\right) = \prod_{i=1}^s \left(\frac{p}{q_i}\right) = \prod_{i=1}^s \left(\frac{r}{q_i}\right) = \left(\frac{p \bmod q}{q}\right).
$$

This completes part (a.

### Part (b)

Let $n$ be odd and composite, $n > 1$, and define

$$
S = \left\{ x \in \mathbb{Z} : 1 \le x < n, 0 \ne \left(\frac{x}{n}\right) \equiv x^{(n-1)/2} \pmod{n} \right\}.
$$

We claim $|S| \le \frac{1}{2}\varphi(n)$.

Let $n = q_1 \cdots q_s$ be the prime factorization. If $x \in S$, then $x$ is invertible modulo $n$, hence $x \in (\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$ and $|(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times| = \varphi(n)$. Define the multiplicative map $f : (\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times \to {\pm 1}$ by $f(x) = \left(\frac{x}{n}\right)$. Then $f$ is a nontrivial character modulo $n$ (a Dirichlet character), because $n$ is not prime. The kernel of $f$ is a subgroup of $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$ of index at least $2$, hence has size at most $\varphi(n)/2$. For $x$ outside the kernel, $\left(\frac{x}{n}\right) \equiv x^{(n-1)/2} \pmod{n}$ cannot hold for all $x$, because a composite $n$ has some prime factor $q_i$ dividing $n$ and for which the Legendre symbol $\left(\frac{x}{q_i}\right)$ differs from $x^{(q_i-1)/2} \pmod{q_i}$. Therefore $|S| \le \varphi(n)/2$.

This shows that a random $x$ satisfies the Solovay–Strassen test with probability at most $1/2$ if $n$ is composite, and hence the test is correct with probability at least $1/2$.

### Part (c)

Let $x$ be such that Algorithm P concludes "$n$ is probably prime". By the definition of Algorithm P, this occurs exactly when $0 \ne \left(\frac{x}{n}\right) \equiv x^{(n-1)/2} \pmod{n}$. Hence every $x$ passing the Algorithm P check satisfies the congruence in part (b). This establishes the claim that Algorithm P is superior, since it never misclassifies any $x$ outside $S$ as probably prime.

## Verification

Each property in part (a) was derived from the definition of the Jacobi symbol for prime $q_i$ and extended multiplicatively to composite $q$. Part (b) used the multiplicative property to identify a nontrivial character and bounded its kernel by $\varphi(n)/2$, in agreement with the standard proof of Solovay–Strassen. Part (c) follows directly from the statement of Algorithm P and the results in part (b).

## Notes

The results generalize directly to other nontrivial Dirichlet characters modulo $n$, providing an upper bound on the proportion of witnesses for composite $n$. Iterating the Solovay–Strassen test $k$ times gives a failure probability at most $2^{-k}$ for composite $n$, assuming independent uniform choices of $x$.

This completes the proof.
