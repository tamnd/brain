[Section 4.5.4: Factoring into Primes](../)

**Exercise 1.** [*10*] If the sequence $d_0, d_1, d_2, \ldots$ of trial divisors in Algorithm A contains a number that is not prime, why will it never appear in the output?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m45s  

---

In Algorithm A the invariant at step A1 states that $n$ has no prime factors less than $d_k$. Let $d_k$ be composite, and let $p$ be its smallest prime factor, so $d_k = p \cdot m$ with $p < d_k$. If $d_k$ were to appear in the output, it would be assigned in step A5, which requires $r = 0$, hence $d_k \mid n$.

If $d_k \mid n$, then $p \mid d_k$ implies $p \mid n$. This contradicts the invariant that $n$ has no prime factors less than $d_k$, since $p < d_k$. Therefore $d_k$ cannot divide $n$, so step A5 is never executed with a composite value of $d_k$. The only values ever assigned to the sequence $p_t$ are primes. ∎
