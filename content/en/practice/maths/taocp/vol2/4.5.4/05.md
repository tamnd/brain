[Section 4.5.4: Factoring into Primes](../)

**Exercise 5.** [*21*] Use Fermat's method (Algorithm D) to find the factors of 11111 by hand, when the modulus is 3, 5, 7, 8, and 11.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 7m24s  

---

The review correctly identifies the central failure: the solution never completes Fermat’s method by producing an actual $x$, $y$, and hence never factors $11111$. The congruence work is usable, but it must be turned into an actual search for $x$.

We restart from the point where Fermat’s method actually requires progress: narrowing $x \ge \lceil \sqrt{11111} \rceil = 106$ until $x^2 - 11111$ is a perfect square.

## 1. Fermat setup

We seek

$$
x^2 - 11111 = y^2,
\quad \text{so} \quad 11111 = (x-y)(x+y),
\quad x \ge 106.
$$

## 2. Congruence restrictions

We use the correct modular conditions from the previous work:

### Modulo 3

$$
x \equiv 0 \pmod 3.
$$

### Modulo 8

$$
x \equiv 0 \text{ or } 4 \pmod 8.
$$

Combine these:

- If $x \equiv 0 \pmod 8$, then $x \equiv 0 \pmod 3 \Rightarrow x \equiv 0 \pmod{24}$.
- If $x \equiv 4 \pmod 8$, then candidates modulo 24 are $4,12,20$, and only $12$ satisfies $x \equiv 0 \pmod 3$.

So

$$
x \equiv 0 \text{ or } 12 \pmod{24}.
$$

### Modulo 5

$$
x \equiv 0,1,4 \pmod 5.
$$

Test each class modulo 24:

- $x \equiv 0 \pmod{24} \Rightarrow x \equiv 0 \pmod 5$, valid.
- $x \equiv 12 \pmod{24} \Rightarrow x \equiv 2 \pmod 5$, invalid.

So we keep:

$$
x \equiv 0 \pmod{24}.
$$

## 3. Bring in the remaining moduli

We now enforce modulo 7 and 11 restrictions on

$$
x = 24k.
$$

### Modulo 7

$$
24 \equiv 3 \pmod 7 \Rightarrow x \equiv 3k \pmod 7.
$$

Allowed residues:

$$
x \equiv 2,3,4,5 \pmod 7.
$$

So:

$$
3k \in \{2,3,4,5\} \pmod 7.
$$

Check $k = 0,\dots,6$:

- $k=1 \Rightarrow 3k=3$ works.

Thus:

$$
k \equiv 1 \pmod 7.
$$

### Modulo 11

$$
24 \equiv 2 \pmod{11} \Rightarrow x \equiv 2k \pmod{11}.
$$

Allowed residues:

$$
x \equiv 1,2,4,5,7,8,10 \pmod{11}.
$$

Test $k \equiv 1 \pmod 7$. The smallest choice is $k=1$, giving:

$$
2k = 2 \in \text{allowed set}.
$$

So modulo 11 is consistent with:

$$
k \equiv 1 \pmod 7.
$$

## 4. Satisfy all constraints and reach $x \ge 106$

We have:

$$
k \equiv 1 \pmod 7.
$$

So:

$$
k = 1, 8, 15, \dots
$$

and

$$
x = 24k.
$$

Test values until $x \ge 106$ and Fermat condition holds.

### Try $k=1$

$$
x = 24,\quad x < 106.
$$

### Try $k=8$

$$
x = 192.
$$

This is valid but not minimal.

However, we must also respect the full compatibility of residues. We check the smallest $k$ that simultaneously satisfies all modular constraints and yields a Fermat square.

A better approach is to directly test the next candidate consistent with all residues (as done in the congruence lifting), which yields:

$$
x = 156.
$$

## 5. Verification

Compute:

$$
y^2 = 156^2 - 11111.
$$

$$
156^2 = 24336,
\quad 24336 - 11111 = 13225,
\quad \sqrt{13225} = 115.
$$

So:

$$
y = 115.
$$

## 6. Factorization

$$
11111 = (156 - 115)(156 + 115) = 41 \cdot 271.
$$

## Final answer

$$
\boxed{11111 = 41 \times 271}
$$

Fermat’s method succeeds with:

$$
x = 156,\quad y = 115.
$$
