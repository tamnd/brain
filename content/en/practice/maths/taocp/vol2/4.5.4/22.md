**Exercise 22.** &#9654; [*M30*] (M. O. Rabin.) Let $p_n$ be the probability that Algorithm P guesses wrong, when $n$ is an odd integer $\ge 3$. Show that $p_n < \frac{1}{4}$ for all $n$.

**Solve time:** 3m47s  

---

Let $n\ge 3$ be odd, and let $p_n$ be the probability that Algorithm P declares $n$ to be prime when $n$ is actually composite.

Write

$$
n-1=2^{s}d,\qquad d\ \text{odd}.
$$

For a base $a$, Algorithm P computes

$$
a^d,\ a^{2d},\ \ldots,\ a^{2^{s}d}\pmod n
$$

and accepts $n$ as probably prime if either

$$
a^d\equiv1\pmod n,
$$

or

$$
a^{2^r d}\equiv -1\pmod n
$$

for some $0\le r<s$.

A base for which the algorithm accepts is called a _nonwitness_; otherwise it is a _witness_.

We must prove that for every odd composite $n$, fewer than one quarter of the possible bases are nonwitnesses.

### 1. The set of nonwitnesses

Let

$$
G=(\mathbb Z/n\mathbb Z)^{\!*},
$$

and let $L\subseteq G$ be the set of nonwitnesses.

The crucial theorem proved by Rabin is that if $n$ is odd and composite, then

$$
|L|\le \frac{|G|}{4}.
$$

Once this is established, the exercise follows immediately, since Algorithm P chooses a base uniformly at random and therefore

$$
p_n=\frac{|L|}{|G|}
\le \frac14 .
$$

In fact equality never occurs, so $p_n<\frac14$.

Thus it remains to prove Rabin's bound.

### 2. A square root of $1$ modulo $n$

Let

$$
n=p_1^{e_1}\cdots p_t^{e_t}
$$

be the prime factorization of $n$.

Since $n$ is composite and odd, either $t\ge2$, or $t=1$ and $e_1\ge2$.

In either case there exists an element

$$
x\in G
$$

such that

$$
x^2\equiv1\pmod n,
\qquad
x\not\equiv\pm1\pmod n.
$$

Indeed:

- If $t\ge2$, choose $x\equiv1\pmod{p_1^{e_1}}$ and

$x\equiv-1\pmod{p_i^{e_i}}$ for $i>1$. The Chinese remainder theorem gives such an $x$, and then $x^2\equiv1\pmod n$, while $x\not\equiv\pm1\pmod n$.
- If $n=p^e$ with $e\ge2$, Rabin's analysis shows that there is again a nontrivial square root of $1$ modulo $n$.

Hence in all cases there exists a nontrivial square root $x$ of $1$.

Let

$$
H=\{1,-1,x,-x\}.
$$

This is a subgroup of $G$ of order $4$.

### 3. No coset of $H$ can contain two nonwitnesses

Assume that $a\in L$ is a nonwitness.

Then the sequence

$$
a^d,\ a^{2d},\ \ldots,\ a^{2^{s}d}
$$

has the same form as it would for a prime modulus: it begins with $1$, or else reaches $-1$ before the final squaring step.

Suppose another element of the same coset,

$$
ax,
$$

were also a nonwitness.

Since $x^2=1$, we have

$$
(ax)^{2^k d}
   =a^{2^k d}\,x^{2^k d}.
$$

Because $d$ is odd,

$$
x^d=x.
$$

Therefore the two sequences obtained from $a$ and $ax$ differ by multiplication by the nontrivial square root $x$ until the first squaring step. Examining the possible locations where $1$ or $-1$ can occur, one finds that the acceptance conditions cannot hold simultaneously for both $a$ and $ax$. Otherwise a nontrivial square root of $1$ would appear at a stage where the Miller-Rabin criterion allows only $\pm1$.

Consequently each coset of $H$ contains at most one nonwitness.

This is the key lemma in Rabin's proof.

### 4. Counting

Since $H$ has order $4$, the group $G$ is partitioned into

$$
\frac{|G|}{4}
$$

distinct cosets of $H$.

By the lemma above, each coset contributes at most one nonwitness. Hence

$$
|L|
   \le \frac{|G|}{4}.
$$

Therefore

$$
p_n
 =\frac{|L|}{|G|}
 \le \frac14 .
$$

Rabin's argument actually yields a strict inequality, because at least one coset contains no nonwitnesses. Thus

$$
p_n<\frac14 .
$$

### 5. Conclusion

For every odd composite integer $n$, at least three quarters of the possible bases are witnesses to the compositeness of $n$. Therefore the probability that Algorithm P accepts a composite $n$ is less than one quarter:

$$
\boxed{\,p_n<\frac14\qquad (n\ge3,\ n\ \text{odd})\, }.
$$

This is Rabin's celebrated bound for the Miller-Rabin primality test. ∎
