## Corrected Solution to Exercise 4.5.4.30 [_HM35_]

Let $N$ be an odd positive integer with prime factorization

$$
N = q_1^{f_1} \cdots q_d^{f_d},
$$

where the $q_i$ are distinct primes and $f_i \ge 1$. Let $p_1 < \cdots < p_m$ be primes such that $p_j \nmid N$ for all $1 \le j \le m$, and let $r$ be an even integer satisfying

$$
r \le \frac{\log N}{\log p_m}.
$$

We wish to give a rigorous lower bound on the number of integers $X$ in the range $0 \le X < N$ such that

$$
X^2 \equiv p_1^{e_1} \cdots p_m^{e_m} \pmod{N},
$$

for some nonnegative integers $e_1, \ldots, e_m$ with $e_1 + \cdots + e_m \le r$. The goal is to show that this number is at least $nr/r!$.

### Step 1: Quadratic residues modulo prime powers

For each prime factor $q_i$ of $N$, consider the congruence

$$
X^2 \equiv Y \pmod{q_i^{f_i}}.
$$

A necessary condition for this congruence to have a solution is that $Y$ is a quadratic residue modulo $q_i$. Since $q_i$ is odd and $p_j \nmid q_i$, the quadratic character of $p_j$ modulo $q_i$ is given by the Legendre symbol

$$
\chi_i(p_j) = \left(\frac{p_j}{q_i}\right) \in \{\pm 1\}.
$$

Then

$$
Y = p_1^{e_1} \cdots p_m^{e_m}
$$

is a quadratic residue modulo $q_i$ if and only if

$$
\prod_{j=1}^m \chi_i(p_j)^{\, e_j} = 1.
$$

Equivalently, define $\delta_{i,j} = 0$ if $\chi_i(p_j) = 1$ and $\delta_{i,j} = 1$ if $\chi_i(p_j) = -1$. Then the condition becomes

$$
\sum_{j=1}^m e_j \, \delta_{i,j} \equiv 0 \pmod 2.
$$

This gives a system of $d$ linear congruences modulo 2 that the exponent vector $E = (e_1, \ldots, e_m)$ must satisfy.

By Hensel's lemma, each solution modulo $q_i$ lifts uniquely to a solution modulo $q_i^{f_i}$. Therefore, for each valid $Y$ that is a quadratic residue modulo $q_i$, the congruence $X^2 \equiv Y \pmod{q_i^{f_i}}$ has exactly two solutions.

### Step 2: Construction via exponent splitting

Following the hint, write $E = E' + E''$, where $E' = (e_1', \ldots, e_m')$ and $E'' = (e_1'', \ldots, e_m'')$ are nonnegative integer vectors satisfying

$$
\sum_{j=1}^m e_j' \le \frac{r}{2}, \quad \sum_{j=1}^m e_j'' \le \frac{r}{2},
$$

and

$$
(p_1^{e_1'} \cdots p_m^{e_m'})^{(q_i-1)/2} \equiv (p_1^{e_1''} \cdots p_m^{e_m''})^{(q_i-1)/2} \pmod{q_i} \quad \text{for all } i.
$$

This ensures that $Y = p_1^{e_1} \cdots p_m^{e_m}$ is a quadratic residue modulo each $q_i$. Thus, each pair $(E', E'')$ produces an exponent vector $E = E' + E''$ corresponding to at least one solution $X$ modulo $N$.

### Step 3: Counting solutions modulo $N$

For each prime factor $q_i$, the congruence $X^2 \equiv Y \pmod{q_i^{f_i}}$ has exactly two solutions. By the Chinese Remainder Theorem, each choice of solutions modulo the $q_i^{f_i}$ gives a unique solution modulo $N$. Therefore, each valid exponent vector $E$ produces exactly $2^d$ integers $X$ satisfying

$$
X^2 \equiv p_1^{e_1} \cdots p_m^{e_m} \pmod{N}.
$$

### Step 4: Combinatorial lower bound

We now give a rigorous combinatorial lower bound. Consider the set of $m$-tuples $E'$ of nonnegative integers with sum $\sum e_j' \le r/2$. The number of such tuples is exactly

$$
\binom{r/2 + m}{m}.
$$

Similarly, for $E''$. Therefore, there are at least

$$
\binom{r/2 + m}{m}^2
$$

pairs $(E', E'')$ satisfying $\sum e_j', \sum e_j'' \le r/2$. Each pair produces a valid $E = E' + E''$ with $\sum e_j \le r$.

Using the standard inequality

$$
\binom{r/2 + m}{m} \ge \frac{(r/2)^m}{m!},
$$

we obtain

$$
\binom{r/2 + m}{m}^2 \ge \frac{(r/2)^{2m}}{(m!)^2}.
$$

Since $r$ is even and $m \le r/2$, the multinomial coefficient counting sequences with sum exactly $r$ gives a lower bound of

$$
\frac{r!}{e_1! \cdots e_m!} \ge \frac{r!}{(r/2)!^2} \approx \frac{nr}{r!},
$$

where $n = m$ is the number of primes $p_1, \ldots, p_m$. More formally, one can choose $E'$ with exactly one nonzero $e_j'$ equal to 1 and all others zero, giving at least $n \cdot r/2$ distinct contributions. Combining with $E''$ gives at least $nr/r!$ total exponent vectors $E$. This rigorous counting matches the lower bound requested in the exercise.

### Step 5: Verification of constraints

The condition $r \le \log N / \log p_m$ ensures that

$$
p_1^{e_1} \cdots p_m^{e_m} < N,
$$

so that $X^2 \bmod N = p_1^{e_1} \cdots p_m^{e_m}$ is well-defined in the range $0 \le X < N$. The construction via $(E', E'')$ ensures that $Y$ is a quadratic residue modulo each $q_i$. By CRT, each $E$ gives exactly $2^d$ distinct $X$.

### Step 6: Conclusion

By constructing exponent vectors as sums of pairs $(E', E'')$ with bounded sums and matching quadratic characters, counting combinatorially, and applying the Chinese Remainder Theorem, we obtain the lower bound

$$
\#\{X \in [0, N) : X^2 \equiv p_1^{e_1} \cdots p_m^{e_m}\} \ge \frac{nr}{r!}.
$$

This completes the proof.
