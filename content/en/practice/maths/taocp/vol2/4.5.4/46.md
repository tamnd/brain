[Section 4.5.4: Factoring into Primes](../)

**Exercise 46.** [*HM30*] (L. Adleman.) Let $p$ be a rather large prime number and let $a$ be a primitive root modulo $p$; thus, all integers $b$ in the range $1 \le b < p$ can be written $b = a^n \bmod p$, for some unique $n$ with $1 \le n \le p$.

Design an algorithm that almost always finds $n$, given $b$, in $O(p^\epsilon)$ steps for all $\epsilon > 0$, using ideas similar to those of Dixon's factoring algorithm. [*Hint:* Start by building a repertoire of numbers $n_i$ such that $a^{n_i} \bmod p$ has only small prime factors.]

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 5m55s

## Setup

Let

$$
G=\langle a\rangle=(\mathbb Z/p\mathbb Z)^\times,
$$

where $p$ is prime and $a$ is a primitive root modulo $p$. For every integer $b$ with

$$
1\le b<p,
$$

there exists a unique exponent

$$
n\in\{0,1,\ldots ,p-2\}
$$

such that

$$
b\equiv a^n \pmod p.
$$

The problem is to determine $n$ from $a$, $b$, and $p$.

The hint suggests an adaptation of Dixon's factoring method. The essential idea is to collect multiplicative relations involving numbers whose prime factors are all small, then solve a linear system modulo $p-1$.

Fix a parameter $y$. Let

$$
\mathcal P=\{q_1,q_2,\ldots ,q_t\}
$$

be the set of all primes not exceeding $y$, and let

$$
t=\pi(y).
$$

An integer is called $y$-smooth if all of its prime factors belong to $\mathcal P$.

The goal is to construct a database of logarithms

$$
L_i=\log_a q_i \pmod{p-1},
\qquad
1\le i\le t,
$$

and then express $\log_a b$ in terms of these values.

## Solution

Choose random integers

$$
r\in\{0,1,\ldots ,p-2\}
$$

and compute

$$
g_r\equiv a^r\pmod p.
$$

Whenever $g_r$ is $y$-smooth, write

$$
g_r=\prod_{i=1}^{t} q_i^{e_i}.
$$

Since

$$
g_r\equiv a^r\pmod p,
$$

taking discrete logarithms gives the congruence

$$
r\equiv \sum_{i=1}^{t} e_iL_i
\pmod{p-1}.
\tag{1}
$$

Each successful smooth factorization therefore yields one linear equation in the unknowns

$$
L_1,\ldots,L_t.
$$

Continue generating relations until at least $t$ independent equations have been obtained. These equations form a linear system modulo $p-1$,

$$
E\,L\equiv R \pmod{p-1},
$$

where $E=(e_{ji})$ is the exponent matrix. With probability tending to $1$ as additional relations are collected, the rows span the full space required to determine the unknown logarithms. Gaussian elimination modulo $p-1$ then yields all values

$$
L_i=\log_a q_i \pmod{p-1}.
$$

After the factor-base logarithms have been computed, determine the logarithm of $b$.

Choose random integers

$$
s\in\{0,1,\ldots,p-2\}
$$

until

$$
c\equiv b\,a^s\pmod p
$$

is $y$-smooth. Write

$$
c=\prod_{i=1}^{t} q_i^{f_i}.
$$

Then

$$
b\,a^s
\equiv
\prod_{i=1}^{t} q_i^{f_i}
\pmod p.
$$

Taking logarithms modulo $p-1$ gives

$$
n+s
\equiv
\sum_{i=1}^{t} f_iL_i
\pmod{p-1}.
$$

Hence

$$
n
\equiv
\sum_{i=1}^{t} f_iL_i-s
\pmod{p-1}.
\tag{2}
$$

Equation (2) determines the required exponent.

The algorithm therefore consists of two stages.

First, collect sufficiently many relations of the form (1) and solve for the logarithms of all primes in the factor base.

Second, find a random shift $s$ for which $b\,a^s\bmod p$ is $y$-smooth and then apply (2).

This is exactly analogous to Dixon's factoring algorithm, where smooth factorizations produce linear relations among exponents.

## Verification

Every relation used in the first stage is valid. If

$$
a^r\equiv\prod_{i=1}^{t}q_i^{e_i}\pmod p,
$$

then

$$
\log_a(a^r)
=
r
\equiv
\sum_{i=1}^{t}e_i\log_a q_i
\pmod{p-1},
$$

which is equation (1).

Suppose the linear system has been solved correctly, yielding

$$
L_i\equiv\log_a q_i\pmod{p-1}.
$$

If

$$
b\,a^s\equiv\prod_{i=1}^{t}q_i^{f_i}\pmod p,
$$

then

$$
n+s
=
\log_a(ba^s)
\equiv
\sum_{i=1}^{t}f_iL_i
\pmod{p-1}.
$$

Subtracting $s$ produces equation (2), so the computed value is indeed the unique discrete logarithm of $b$ modulo $p-1$.

To estimate the running time, choose

$$
y=\exp\!\bigl(\sqrt{\log p\,\log\log p}\bigr).
$$

The standard smooth-number estimates underlying Dixon's method imply that a random residue modulo $p$ is $y$-smooth with probability

$$
\exp\!\bigl(-(1+o(1))
\sqrt{\log p\,\log\log p}\bigr).
$$

The factor base contains

$$
t=\pi(y)
=
\exp\!\bigl((1+o(1))
\sqrt{\log p\,\log\log p}\bigr)
$$

primes, by equation (4). Consequently the total work required to gather enough relations and solve the resulting system is

$$
\exp\!\bigl((1+o(1))
\sqrt{\log p\,\log\log p}\bigr).
$$

For every fixed $\epsilon>0$,

$$
\exp\!\bigl((1+o(1))
\sqrt{\log p\,\log\log p}\bigr)
=
o(p^\epsilon),
$$

because

$$
\sqrt{\log p\,\log\log p}
=
o(\log p).
$$

Therefore the algorithm runs in

$$
O(p^\epsilon)
$$

steps for every $\epsilon>0$, except for a set of exceptional random choices whose probability tends to $0$ as more relations are collected.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Notes

The method described above is the index-calculus algorithm in its original form. Adleman's contribution was the observation that Dixon's smooth-number strategy for factoring can be transferred almost verbatim to discrete logarithms in $(\mathbb Z/p\mathbb Z)^\times$, producing a subexponential running time

$$
\exp\!\bigl((1+o(1))
\sqrt{\log p\,\log\log p}\bigr),
$$

which satisfies the requirement of the exercise.
