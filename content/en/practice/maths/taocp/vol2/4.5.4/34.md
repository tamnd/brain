## Solution to Exercise 4.5.4.34 [_M30_]

Let $N = pq$ be the product of two distinct primes, as in the RSA scheme. We are given an integer $m$ such that the congruence

$$
x^m \equiv 1 \pmod N
$$

holds for at least a fraction $\rho \ge \lfloor m/2 \rfloor^{-1/4}$ of all positive integers $x < N$. The goal is to explain how to factor $N$ efficiently when $m$ is not too large ($m < N^{10}$).

### Step 1: Translate the hypothesis into group-theoretic terms

Let $(\mathbb{Z}/N\mathbb{Z})^*$ denote the multiplicative group modulo $N$. By the Chinese remainder theorem,

$$
(\mathbb{Z}/N\mathbb{Z})^* \simeq (\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^* \times (\mathbb{Z}/q\mathbb{Z})^*.
$$

Write $x \mapsto (x_p, x_q)$ with $x_p = x \bmod p$ and $x_q = x \bmod q$. The congruence $x^m \equiv 1 \pmod N$ holds if and only if

$$
x^m \equiv 1 \pmod p \quad \text{and} \quad x^m \equiv 1 \pmod q.
$$

Let

$$
G_p = \{ y \in (\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^* : y^m \equiv 1 \pmod p \}, \quad
G_q = \{ y \in (\mathbb{Z}/q\mathbb{Z})^* : y^m \equiv 1 \pmod q \}.
$$

Then the number of $x \in (\mathbb{Z}/N\mathbb{Z})^*$ satisfying $x^m \equiv 1 \pmod N$ is

$$
|G_p| \cdot |G_q|.
$$

Since $(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^_$ and $(\mathbb{Z}/q\mathbb{Z})^_$ are cyclic of orders $p-1$ and $q-1$, we have

$$
|G_p| = \gcd(m, p-1), \quad |G_q| = \gcd(m, q-1).
$$

Therefore, the number of solutions modulo $N$ is

$$
\gcd(m, p-1) \cdot \gcd(m, q-1).
$$

### Step 2: Relate the lower bound to divisors of $p-1$ and $q-1$

Let $f$ denote the fraction of integers modulo $N$ satisfying $x^m \equiv 1 \pmod N$. Then

$$
f = \frac{\gcd(m, p-1) \cdot \gcd(m, q-1)}{N-1} \ge \lfloor m/2 \rfloor^{-1/4}.
$$

Since $N \approx pq$, we have

$$
\gcd(m, p-1) \cdot \gcd(m, q-1) \ge \rho (p q - 1) \approx \rho N.
$$

This inequality shows that both $\gcd(m, p-1)$ and $\gcd(m, q-1)$ are unusually large, because if either were small, their product could not reach $\rho N$. Let us denote

$$
M_p := \gcd(m, p-1), \quad M_q := \gcd(m, q-1).
$$

Then

$$
M_p M_q \ge \rho N.
$$

Since $m < N^{10}$, one can factor $m$ completely in polynomial time in $\log N$.

### Step 3: Exploit the structure to factor $N$

The key observation is that knowing $M_p$ and $M_q$ (or multiples thereof) allows one to find nontrivial divisors of $p-1$ and $q-1$, which can be used to split $N$. The procedure is as follows:

1. Factor $m$ completely: $m = \prod_i \ell_i^{a_i}$.
2. For each prime factor $\ell_i$ of $m$, consider the exponent $m / \ell_i$. Choose random integers $x < N$ and compute

$$
d := \gcd(x^{m/\ell_i} - 1, N).
$$

1. If $1 < d < N$, then $d$ is a nontrivial factor of $N$. Otherwise, try a different $\ell_i$ or a different random $x$.

This works because $x^{m} \equiv 1 \pmod N$ for a significant fraction of $x$. For such an $x$, write

$$
x^m - 1 = \prod_{d \mid m} \Phi_d(x),
$$

where $\Phi_d$ is the $d$th cyclotomic polynomial. The nontrivial $\gcd(x^{m/\ell_i} - 1, N)$ reveals a factor of $p$ or $q$, exploiting the large divisibility of $p-1$ or $q-1$ by factors of $m$.

This is essentially a generalized form of the classical Pollard $p-1$ method, with $m$ acting as a known smooth multiple of $p-1$ or $q-1$.

### Step 4: Complexity analysis

- Factoring $m < N^{10}$ can be done in time polynomial in $\log N$.
- Modular exponentiation $x^{m/\ell_i} \bmod N$ can be performed using repeated squaring in $O(\log m \cdot \log^2 N)$ time.
- Each $\gcd(x^{m/\ell_i}-1, N)$ can be computed in $O(\log^2 N)$ time.
- Since a positive fraction $\rho$ of $x$ satisfy $x^m \equiv 1 \pmod N$, a random choice of $x$ yields a nontrivial factor of $N$ with probability at least $\rho$, so the expected number of trials is $O(\rho^{-1})$.

Overall, the procedure runs in expected polynomial time in $\log N$ for $m < N^{10}$.

### Step 5: Conclusion

Knowing an exponent $m$ for which a positive fraction of integers satisfy $x^m \equiv 1 \pmod N$ allows one to factor $N$ efficiently. The method proceeds by factoring $m$, computing $\gcd(x^{m/\ell}-1, N)$ for suitably chosen divisors $\ell$ of $m$, and obtaining a nontrivial factor of $N$. This works because the hypothesis implies that $p-1$ and $q-1$ have unusually large common factors with $m$, which can be exploited to split $N$.

$$
\boxed{\text{Use the known exponent } m \text{ and the high density of solutions of } x^m \equiv 1 \pmod N \text{ to compute } \gcd(x^{m/\ell}-1, N) \text{ and factor } N.}
$$
