**Exercise 32.** &#9654; [*M21*] Show how to modify the RSA encoding scheme so that there is no problem with messages $< \sqrt[3]{N}$, in such a way that the length of messages is not substantially increased.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 5m02s  

---

## Solution

Suppose RSA uses public exponent $e=3$. The difficulty is that if

$$
0 \le m < N^{1/3},
$$

then

$$
c \equiv m^3 \pmod N
$$

is actually equal to $m^3$ as an ordinary integer, because $m^3<N$. Hence anyone can recover $m$ by taking the integer cube root of $c$.

The essential idea is to ensure that the integer fed into RSA is never a small number, even when the message itself is small.

Let

$$
k=\left\lceil \frac13\log_2 N \right\rceil .
$$

Then $2^k > N^{1/3}$.

Given a message block $m$, choose a random $k$-bit string $r$, and form

$$
M = m\,2^k + r.
$$

Equivalently, $M$ is obtained by appending $k$ random bits to the message.

We now encrypt $M$ instead of $m$:

$$
c \equiv M^3 \pmod N.
$$

Since $r$ is a $k$-bit quantity, we may choose it so that its leading bit is $1$. Then

$$
2^{k-1} \le r < 2^k.
$$

Therefore

$$
M \ge r \ge 2^{k-1}.
$$

Because $k=\lceil \tfrac13\log_2 N\rceil$,

$$
2^{k-1} > \frac12 N^{1/3}.
$$

If desired, we can take $k=\lceil \tfrac13\log_2 N\rceil+1$; then

$$
M \ge 2^{k-1} > N^{1/3}.
$$

Hence

$$
M^3>N.
$$

Consequently the ciphertext is no longer equal to the ordinary integer $M^3$. It is the residue of $M^3$ modulo $N$, and the simple cube-root attack is impossible.

Decryption proceeds exactly as in ordinary RSA. The receiver computes

$$
M \equiv c^d \pmod N,
$$

obtaining the padded block $M$. Since the final $k$ bits are the random padding $r$, the original message is recovered by removing those $k$ bits, that is,

$$
m=\left\lfloor \frac{M}{2^k}\right\rfloor .
$$

The increase in message length is only $k$ bits. Since

$$
k \approx \frac13\log_2 N,
$$

this adds only about one third of the modulus length to each block, which is not a substantial increase.

Thus a simple modification is to append $k$ random bits to every message block before RSA encryption. The resulting encoded integer is always larger than $N^{1/3}$, so the small-message attack no longer applies. ∎
