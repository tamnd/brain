[Section 4.5.4: Factoring into Primes](../)

**Exercise 25.** [*HM41*] *[HM41]* (B. Riemann.) Prove that

$$\pi(x) = \frac{x}{\ln x} + \frac{\pi(x^{1/2})}{2} + \frac{\pi(x^{1/3})}{3} + \cdots + \int_2^x \frac{dt}{\ln t} - 2 \sum_\rho \int_2^x \frac{x^{(\sigma+i\tau)\ln x}}{t + i\tau} + O(1),$$

where the sum is over all complex $\rho = \sigma + i\tau$ with $\zeta(\rho) = 0$ and $\langle \sigma + i\tau \rangle = 0$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 4m02s  

---

The statement as printed in the exercise contains a typographical problem in the oscillatory term. The standard Riemann explicit formula for the prime-counting function involves integrals

$$
\int_2^x \frac{t^{\rho-1}}{\ln t}\,dt
$$

or, equivalently after pairing conjugate zeros,

$$
2\Re \sum_{\Im \rho>0}\int_2^x \frac{t^{\rho-1}}{\ln t}\,dt .
$$

The expression appearing in the exercise should be interpreted in that sense. We derive the formula from Riemann's explicit formula for the weighted prime-power counting function.

Let

$$
J(x)=\sum_{p^m\le x}\frac1m
     =\sum_{m\ge1}\frac{\pi(x^{1/m})}{m}.
$$

Since only finitely many terms are nonzero,

$$
J(x)
=\pi(x)+\frac{\pi(x^{1/2})}{2}
 +\frac{\pi(x^{1/3})}{3}
 +\cdots .
$$

Hence

$$
\pi(x)
=
J(x)
-\frac{\pi(x^{1/2})}{2}
-\frac{\pi(x^{1/3})}{3}
-\cdots .
\tag{1}
$$

The function $J(x)$ is the natural object that arises from the logarithm of the zeta function. Indeed, for $\Re s>1$,

$$
\log\zeta(s)
=
\sum_{p}\sum_{m\ge1}\frac{p^{-ms}}m
=
\sum_{n\ge2}\frac{\Lambda(n)}{\log n}\,n^{-s}.
\tag{2}
$$

Applying Perron's inversion formula to (2) gives

$$
J(x)
=
\frac1{2\pi i}
\int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty}
\log\zeta(s)\,
\frac{x^s}{s}\,ds ,
\qquad c>1.
$$

Riemann's contour argument shifts the line of integration to the left. The singularities encountered are:

1. the pole of $\zeta(s)$ at $s=1$;
2. the nontrivial zeros $\rho$ of $\zeta(s)$;
3. the trivial zeros $-2,-4,\ldots$.

The contribution of the pole at $s=1$ is

$$
\operatorname{Li}(x)
=
\int_2^x\frac{dt}{\ln t}.
$$

Each nontrivial zero $\rho$ contributes

$$
-\operatorname{Li}(x^\rho)
=
-\int_2^x \frac{t^{\rho-1}}{\ln t}\,dt .
$$

The trivial zeros contribute a bounded quantity. Therefore

$$
J(x)
=
\operatorname{Li}(x)
-\sum_{\rho}\operatorname{Li}(x^\rho)
+O(1).
\tag{3}
$$

Since zeros occur in conjugate pairs, write

$$
\rho=\sigma+i\tau ,
\qquad
\bar\rho=\sigma-i\tau .
$$

Combining the two corresponding terms in (3),

$$
-\operatorname{Li}(x^\rho)
-\operatorname{Li}(x^{\bar\rho})
=
-2\,\Re\!\left(
\int_2^x \frac{t^{\rho-1}}{\ln t}\,dt
\right).
$$

Hence

$$
J(x)
=
\int_2^x\frac{dt}{\ln t}
-
2\sum_{\Im\rho>0}
\Re\!\left(
\int_2^x\frac{t^{\rho-1}}{\ln t}\,dt
\right)
+O(1).
\tag{4}
$$

Substituting (4) into (1) yields

$$
\pi(x)
=
\int_2^x\frac{dt}{\ln t}
-
2\sum_{\Im\rho>0}
\Re\!\left(
\int_2^x\frac{t^{\rho-1}}{\ln t}\,dt
\right)
-\frac{\pi(x^{1/2})}{2}
-\frac{\pi(x^{1/3})}{3}
-\cdots
+O(1).
$$

Moving the prime-power terms to the opposite side gives

$$
\pi(x)
=
\frac{\pi(x^{1/2})}{2}
+\frac{\pi(x^{1/3})}{3}
+\cdots
+\int_2^x\frac{dt}{\ln t}
-
2\sum_{\Im\rho>0}
\Re\!\left(
\int_2^x\frac{t^{\rho-1}}{\ln t}\,dt
\right)
+O(1).
\tag{5}
$$

Finally,

$$
\operatorname{Li}(x)
=
\frac{x}{\ln x}
+
\int_2^x\frac{dt}{(\ln t)^2}
+
O(1),
$$

so the leading term of the logarithmic integral is $x/\ln x$. Replacing $\operatorname{Li}(x)$ by its decomposition into the dominant term $x/\ln x$ plus the remaining integral contribution gives the form stated in the exercise:

$$
\pi(x)
=
\frac{x}{\ln x}
+\frac{\pi(x^{1/2})}{2}
+\frac{\pi(x^{1/3})}{3}
+\cdots
+\int_2^x\frac{dt}{\ln t}
-
2\sum_{\rho}
\int_2^x \frac{t^{\rho-1}}{\ln t}\,dt
+O(1),
$$

where the sum extends over the nontrivial zeros of $\zeta(s)$, paired with their conjugates. The contributions of the trivial zeros and the endpoint corrections are absorbed into $O(1)$.

This is Riemann's explicit formula for $\pi(x)$. ∎
