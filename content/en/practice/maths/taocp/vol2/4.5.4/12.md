[Section 4.5.4: Factoring into Primes](../)

**Exercise 12.** [*HM25*] (J. D. Dixon.) Prove that whenever the algorithm of exercise 12 is presented with a solution $(x, e_0, \ldots, e_m)$ whose exponents are linearly dependent modulo 2

on the exponents of previous solutions, the probability is $2^{1-d}$ that a factorization will not be found, when $N$ has $d$ distinct prime factors and $x$ is chosen at random.

**Solve time:** 4m32s

## Solution

Let

$$
N=p_1^{a_1}p_2^{a_2}\cdots p_d^{a_d},
$$

where $p_1,\ldots,p_d$ are the distinct prime factors of $N$.

In Dixon's method, a set of relations

$$
x_i^2 \equiv \prod_{j=0}^{m} q_j^{\,e_{ij}} \pmod N
$$

is collected. When a newly obtained exponent vector is linearly dependent modulo $2$ on the previous exponent vectors, a nontrivial binary combination of relations yields

$$
X^2 \equiv Y^2 \pmod N,
$$

where

$$
X=\prod x_i^{c_i}, \qquad
Y=\prod q_j^{\,\frac12\sum_i c_i e_{ij}} .
$$

The algorithm attempts to factor $N$ by computing

$$
\gcd(X-Y,N).
$$

A factorization is not found precisely when

$$
\gcd(X-Y,N)=1
\quad\text{or}\quad
\gcd(X-Y,N)=N.
$$

Since $X^2\equiv Y^2\pmod N$,

$$
(X-Y)(X+Y)\equiv0\pmod N.
$$

For each prime power $p_k^{a_k}$ dividing $N$, this implies

$$
X\equiv \pm Y \pmod{p_k^{a_k}}.
$$

Conversely, every independent choice of signs

$$
(\varepsilon_1,\ldots,\varepsilon_d),
\qquad
\varepsilon_k\in\{\pm1\},
$$

determines, by the Chinese remainder theorem, a unique residue class $X$ modulo $N$ satisfying

$$
X\equiv \varepsilon_k Y \pmod{p_k^{a_k}}
\qquad(1\le k\le d).
$$

Hence the congruence

$$
Z^2\equiv Y^2\pmod N
$$

has exactly $2^d$ square roots modulo $N$.

The factorization attempt fails only in the two cases when all signs are equal:

$$
X\equiv Y \pmod{p_k^{a_k}}
\quad(1\le k\le d),
$$

or

$$
X\equiv -Y \pmod{p_k^{a_k}}
\quad(1\le k\le d).
$$

Indeed, in the first case $X\equiv Y\pmod N$, so $\gcd(X-Y,N)=N$; in the second case $X\equiv -Y\pmod N$, so $\gcd(X-Y,N)=1$.

If the signs are not all equal, then for some indices $r,s$,

$$
X\equiv Y \pmod{p_r^{a_r}},
\qquad
X\equiv -Y \pmod{p_s^{a_s}}.
$$

Therefore $X-Y$ is divisible by some, but not all, prime-power factors of $N$, and

$$
1<\gcd(X-Y,N)<N,
$$

yielding a nontrivial factorization.

The hypothesis that $x$ is chosen at random implies that, among the $2^d$ square roots of $Y^2$ modulo $N$, each sign pattern is equally likely. Exactly two of those $2^d$ patterns correspond to failure. Therefore

$$
\Pr(\text{failure})
=
\frac{2}{2^d}
=
2^{\,1-d}.
$$

Thus, whenever the new relation is linearly dependent modulo $2$ on the previous ones, the probability that no factorization is obtained is

$$
\boxed{2^{\,1-d}}.
$$

This completes the proof.

∎
