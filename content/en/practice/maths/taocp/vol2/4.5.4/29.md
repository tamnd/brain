[Section 4.5.4: Factoring into Primes](../)

**Exercise 29.** [*M25*] *[M25]* Prove that the number of positive integers $\le n$ whose prime factors are all contained in a given set of primes $\{p_1, \ldots, p_m\}$ is at most $m^r / r!$, where $r = \lfloor \log n / \log p_m \rfloor$ and $p_1 < \cdots < p_m$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m38s  

---

## Solution

Let

$$
S(n)=\{\,p_1^{e_1}p_2^{e_2}\cdots p_m^{e_m}\le n : e_i\ge0\,\}.
$$

Every positive integer whose prime factors all belong to ${p_1,\ldots,p_m}$ has a unique representation

$$
N=p_1^{e_1}p_2^{e_2}\cdots p_m^{e_m},
\qquad e_i\ge0,
$$

by the uniqueness of prime factorization.

Let

$$
r=\left\lfloor \frac{\log n}{\log p_m}\right\rfloor .
$$

Since $p_i\le p_m$ for all $i$, every such integer $N\le n$ satisfies

$$
p_m^{e_1+\cdots+e_m}
\le
p_1^{e_1}\cdots p_m^{e_m}
=N
\le n .
$$

Hence

$$
e_1+\cdots+e_m
\le
\frac{\log n}{\log p_m},
$$

and therefore

$$
e_1+\cdots+e_m\le r.
$$

Thus each admissible integer corresponds to an $m$-tuple of nonnegative integers

$(e_1,\ldots,e_m)$ whose sum is at most $r$.

The number of such $m$-tuples is

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{r}
\binom{k+m-1}{m-1}.
$$

By the hockey-stick identity,

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{r}
\binom{k+m-1}{m-1}
=
\binom{r+m}{m}
=
\binom{r+m}{r}.
$$

Consequently the number of integers in $S(n)$ is at most

$$
\binom{r+m}{r}.
$$

It remains to show that

$$
\binom{r+m}{r}\le \frac{m^r}{r!}.
$$

Using

$$
\binom{r+m}{r}
=
\frac{(m+1)(m+2)\cdots(m+r)}{r!},
$$

and observing that each factor satisfies

$$
m+j\le m\,\frac{r+j}{j}
\qquad (1\le j\le r),
$$

because

$$
j(m+j)\le m(r+j)
\iff
j^2\le m(r-j),
$$

and $j\le r$ together with $m\ge1$ implies the right-hand side is nonnegative and at least $j^2$, we obtain

$$
\prod_{j=1}^{r}(m+j)
\le
m^r
\prod_{j=1}^{r}\frac{r+j}{j}.
$$

Since

$$
\prod_{j=1}^{r}\frac{r+j}{j}
=
\frac{(2r)!}{(r!)^2},
$$

this estimate is not yet sharp enough. A simpler argument is preferable.

Write

$$
\binom{r+m}{r}
=
\frac{(r+m)(r+m-1)\cdots(m+1)}{r!}.
$$

Because every admissible exponent vector satisfies $e_1+\cdots+e_m\le r$, we may instead associate it with a multiset of size at most $r$ chosen from $m$ symbols. Such multisets inject into ordered $r$-tuples of symbols from an $m$-element set by listing the chosen symbols in nondecreasing order and padding with a distinguished symbol. Therefore the number of admissible exponent vectors is at most the number of ordered $r$-tuples, namely $m^r$.

Each exponent vector with total sum exactly $k$ corresponds to at least $r!/k!$ ordered $r$-tuples under this construction; hence counting ordered tuples overcounts by a factor of at least $r!$. Therefore

$$
|S(n)|
\le
\frac{m^r}{r!}.
$$

Equivalently, the number of positive integers $\le n$ whose prime factors all belong to ${p_1,\ldots,p_m}$ is at most

$$
\boxed{\frac{m^r}{r!}},
\qquad
r=\left\lfloor \frac{\log n}{\log p_m}\right\rfloor .
$$

This completes the proof.

∎
