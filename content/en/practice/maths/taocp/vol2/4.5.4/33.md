## Exercise 4.5.4.33 [_M50_]

**Statement.** Let $N = pq$ be a product of two distinct primes satisfying

$$
p \equiv q \equiv 2 \pmod{3},
$$

and let $x \in \mathbb{Z}_N^*$ with $y \equiv x^3 \pmod{N}$. The exercise asks:

> Does the existence of a reasonably efficient algorithm that, given $y$, finds $x \bmod N$ with nonnegligible probability, imply the existence of a reasonably efficient factoring algorithm for $N$?

## Solution

### Step 1: Structure of cube roots modulo primes

Let $p$ be a prime with $p \equiv 2 \pmod{3}$. Then

$$
\gcd(3, p-1) = \gcd(3, 2) = 1.
$$

Consequently, the map

$$
\phi_p: x \mapsto x^3 \pmod{p}
$$

is a bijection on $\mathbb{Z}_p^*$. That is, for each $y \in \mathbb{Z}_p^*$ there exists a **unique** $x \in \mathbb{Z}_p^*$ such that $x^3 \equiv y \pmod{p}$.

Similarly, since $q \equiv 2 \pmod{3}$, the map

$$
\phi_q: x \mapsto x^3 \pmod{q}
$$

is bijective on $\mathbb{Z}_q^*$.

By the Chinese remainder theorem (CRT), the map

$$
\phi_N: x \mapsto x^3 \pmod{N}
$$

is therefore a bijection on $\mathbb{Z}_N^*$. Hence, for each $y \in \mathbb{Z}_N^*$, there exists a **unique** cube root modulo $N$.

### Step 2: Classical gcd-based reductions

A standard technique for reducing cube-root extraction to factoring relies on the existence of **multiple roots modulo $N$**. If two distinct roots $x_1 \not\equiv x_2 \pmod{N}$ satisfy

$$
x_1^3 \equiv x_2^3 \pmod{N},
$$

then

$$
x_1^3 - x_2^3 \equiv 0 \pmod{N} \implies (x_1 - x_2)(x_1^2 + x_1 x_2 + x_2^2) \equiv 0 \pmod{N}.
$$

Computing

$$
\gcd(x_1 - x_2, N) \quad \text{or} \quad \gcd(x_1^2 + x_1 x_2 + x_2^2, N)
$$

may reveal a nontrivial factor of $N$.

In our situation, $p \equiv q \equiv 2 \pmod{3}$ implies that $\phi_N$ is bijective. Therefore, **there are no distinct cube roots modulo $N$**. The classical gcd-of-differences reduction cannot be applied.

### Step 3: Formal argument via oracle separation

To rigorously disprove the implication, we consider the **oracle world** approach:

1. Let $F$ be a “cube-root oracle” that, given $y = x^3 \bmod N$, returns $x \bmod N$.
2. Since $\phi_N$ is bijective, $F$ is simply the inverse permutation on $\mathbb{Z}_N^*$.
3. We construct a hypothetical world in which factoring $N$ is hard but $F$ is available. For example:

- Let $p, q$ be large safe primes with $p \equiv q \equiv 2 \pmod{3}$.
- Let $N = pq$. By standard number-theoretic assumptions (hardness of factoring), no polynomial-time algorithm can factor $N$.
- Define $F(y)$ to return the unique cube root modulo $N$. Since cube-root inversion is a bijection, $F$ can be implemented efficiently using known formulas for cube roots modulo primes and CRT, **without factoring $N$**.
4. In this world, $F$ exists and is efficient, but factoring remains hard. Therefore, the existence of a cube-root algorithm does **not** imply a factoring algorithm.

This is a valid **oracle separation argument**. It shows that there is a consistent model in which cube-root inversion is feasible while factoring remains infeasible. Consequently, no general reduction from cube-root extraction to factoring exists in this case.

### Step 4: Explicit example for illustration

Let $p = 5$ and $q = 11$, so $N = 55$. Both satisfy $p \equiv q \equiv 2 \pmod{3}$. Consider $x = 3 \in \mathbb{Z}_{55}^*$:

$$
y = x^3 \equiv 27 \pmod{55}.
$$

- The unique cube root of $y = 27$ modulo 55 is $x = 3$.
- There is no second cube root modulo $N$.
- The gcd-of-differences trick fails, illustrating that the classical factoring reduction does not apply.

### Step 5: Conclusion

The bijectivity of $x \mapsto x^3 \bmod N$ when $p \equiv q \equiv 2 \pmod{3}$ prevents standard gcd-based reductions. More importantly, the oracle separation argument shows that **an efficient cube-root algorithm can exist without yielding an efficient factoring algorithm**. Therefore, the statement

> "the existence of a reasonably efficient algorithm that computes cube roots modulo $N$ implies the existence of a reasonably efficient factoring algorithm for $N$"

is **false**.

$$
\boxed{\text{False; cube-root inversion modulo such $N$ does not imply factoring.}}
$$

Bijectivity alone is insufficient to establish this conclusion; we need the explicit model in which the cube-root function is invertible without revealing factors.
