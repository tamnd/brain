[Section 4.5.4: Factoring into Primes](../)

**Exercise 2.** [*15*] If it is known that the input $N$ to Algorithm A is equal to 3 or more, could step A2 be eliminated?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m57s  

---

## Solution

Step A2 of Algorithm A tests whether the current value of $n$ is equal to $1$, and if so, terminates the algorithm. Its purpose is to detect that all prime factors of the original input $N$ have already been extracted. Suppose that $N \ge 3$. Initially, $n \leftarrow N$, so $n \ge 3 > 1$; hence the test $n = 1$ cannot succeed at the first execution of A2. During the execution of the algorithm, $n$ is replaced by $n \leftarrow n/p_t$ in step A5 whenever a factor $p_t$ is found. Therefore, the only situation in which $n = 1$ occurs is after all prime factors have been divided out.

If $N \ge 3$, then at least one iteration of the divide-and-check steps (A3–A6) must occur to find the smallest prime factor $p_1 \le N$. Once $n$ has been reduced by repeated divisions, eventually one of the following occurs: either $n = 1$, in which case the algorithm would terminate at A2, or $n$ is prime and $q \le d_k$ in step A6, in which case step A7 executes and terminates the algorithm. Consider the case when $n = 1$ would be reached: the algorithm could instead proceed directly from A5 to step A6 after setting $n \leftarrow q$. If $q > d_k$, $k$ is incremented; if $q \le d_k$, then by the argument in Section 4.5.4 we must have $n$ prime and $n \le d_k$, in which case step A7 will correctly assign $p_t \leftarrow n$ and terminate. Therefore, the explicit check $n = 1$ in A2 is not strictly necessary, because the same termination occurs automatically when step A7 is reached.

In other words, for inputs $N \ge 3$, the invariant that $n$ has no prime factors less than $d_k$ ensures that either a factor will be found in step A5, or the remaining $n$ is prime and will be assigned in step A7. The intermediate case $n = 1$ is subsumed by this logic and does not require a separate test. The correctness and completeness of Algorithm A are preserved if step A2 is removed.

This completes the proof.
