**Exercise 16.** [*M50*] Are there infinitely many *Mersenne* primes?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m52s  

---

## Setup

A **Mersenne prime** is a prime number of the form

$M_p = 2^p - 1,$

where $p$ itself is prime. The exercise asks whether there are infinitely many Mersenne primes. Formally, we are asked to determine the truth of the statement:

> "There exists an infinite sequence of primes $p_1 < p_2 < p_3 < \cdots$ such that $2^{p_i} - 1$ is prime for all $i$."

Let $\mathbb{P}$ denote the set of all prime numbers and let $\mathbb{M} \subset \mathbb{P}$ denote the set of Mersenne primes. We are to decide whether $|\mathbb{M}| = \infty$.

## Solution

The existence of Mersenne primes is conditional upon two independent properties: first, that $p$ is prime, and second, that $2^p - 1$ is prime.

It is well known that for $p$ prime, $2^p - 1$ may still be composite. For example, $p = 11$ is prime, but $2^{11} - 1 = 2047 = 23 \cdot 89$ is not prime. Therefore, the infinitude of primes does not automatically imply the infinitude of Mersenne primes.

All currently known Mersenne primes correspond to specific values of $p$ and are sparse in the sequence of all primes. Computational searches have identified only finitely many Mersenne primes as of now (fewer than 60 as of 2023), although larger Mersenne primes continue to be discovered by distributed computing projects such as GIMPS.

There is **no known proof** in analytic or algebraic number theory that guarantees infinitely many primes $p$ such that $2^p - 1$ is prime. While heuristics based on the prime number theorem suggest that the probability that $2^p - 1$ is prime decreases roughly like $1/(p \ln 2)$, these heuristics do not constitute a rigorous proof.

Consequently, we can only state:

- The question of whether there are infinitely many Mersenne primes is an **open problem** in number theory.
- No theorem currently proves or disproves the infinitude of $\mathbb{M}$.

## Verification

We check the known facts:

1. If $2^p - 1$ is prime, then $p$ must be prime. Verified: the contrapositive is classical: if $p$ is composite, $p = ab$, $2^p - 1 = 2^{ab} - 1 = (2^a - 1)(2^{a(b-1)} + \cdots + 2^a + 1)$, which is composite.
2. Not all prime exponents yield Mersenne primes. Examples: $p = 11$, $2^{11} - 1 = 2047$ is composite; $p = 23$, $2^{23} - 1 = 8388607 = 47 \cdot 178481$ is composite.
3. Current computational evidence only confirms finitely many Mersenne primes; no method exists to extend these to an infinite sequence rigorously.

Thus, based on all rigorous arguments and known facts, the claim remains unresolved.

## Notes

- Heuristic models (Bateman–Horn conjecture) predict infinitely many Mersenne primes, but these remain conjectural.
- Lucas–Lehmer tests allow efficient verification of primality for $2^p - 1$ once $p$ is prime, but do not imply infinitude.
- The infinitude of Mersenne primes is analogous in status to the twin prime conjecture: plausible but unproven.

This completes the solution.
