[Section 4.5.4: Factoring into Primes](../)

**Exercise 27.** &#9654; [*M30*] *[M30]* Show that there is a way to test numbers of the form $N = 5 \cdot 2^n + 1$ for primality, using approximately the same number of squarings mod $N$ as the Lucas–Lehmer test for Mersenne numbers described in §4.5.4 (in the previous exercises).

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m24s  

---

## Setup

Let

$$
N=5\cdot 2^n+1,
$$

and let

$$
M=N-1=5\cdot 2^n.
$$

Exercise 26 (Pocklington's theorem) applies to numbers of the form

$$
N=fr+1,
$$

provided the complete factorization of $f$ is known and $0<r\le f+1$.

For the present problem we take

$$
f=2^n,\qquad r=5.
$$

Since $5\le 2^n+1$ whenever $n\ge2$, Pocklington's theorem can be used. The only prime divisor of $f$ is $2$. Therefore it suffices to find an integer $x$ such that

$$
x^{N-1}\equiv1\pmod N,
$$

and

$$
\gcd\!\left(x^{(N-1)/2}-1,N\right)=1.
$$

The task is to obtain such a test using essentially the same number of modular squarings as the Lucas-Lehmer test.

## Solution

Choose

$$
x=5.
$$

For a prime number $N=5\cdot2^n+1$, Euler's criterion gives

$$
5^{(N-1)/2}\equiv
\left(\frac{5}{N}\right)\pmod N,
$$

where $\left(\frac{5}{N}\right)$ is the Legendre symbol.

Because

$$
N\equiv1\pmod5,
$$

quadratic reciprocity yields

$$
\left(\frac{5}{N}\right)
=
\left(\frac{N}{5}\right)
=
\left(\frac{1}{5}\right)
=1.
$$

Hence, for every prime $N$,

$$
5^{2^{\,n-1}\cdot5}\equiv1\pmod N.
$$

Define

$$
u_0=5^5=3125,
\qquad
u_{k+1}\equiv u_k^2\pmod N
\quad(k\ge0).
$$

Then

$$
u_k\equiv 5^{5\cdot2^k}\pmod N.
$$

Therefore a necessary condition for primality is

$$
u_{n-1}\equiv1\pmod N.
$$

To obtain a sufficient condition, apply Pocklington's theorem. Since

$$
\frac{N-1}{2}=5\cdot2^{\,n-1},
$$

the second condition becomes

$$
\gcd(u_{n-1}-1,N)=1.
$$

If $u_{n-1}\equiv1\pmod N$, this gcd equals $N$, so the base $5$ itself cannot satisfy Pocklington's criterion directly.

Instead let

$$
v=u_{n-2}=5^{5\cdot2^{\,n-2}}\pmod N.
$$

Then

$$
v^2\equiv u_{n-1}\pmod N.
$$

For a prime $N$, the congruence $u_{n-1}\equiv1\pmod N$ shows that $v$ is a square root of $1$ modulo $N$. Since

$$
v=5^{(N-1)/4},
$$

its order divides $4$. If $v\equiv1\pmod N$, then

$$
5^{(N-1)/4}\equiv1\pmod N,
$$

which would imply that the order of $5$ modulo $N$ divides

$$
\frac{N-1}{4}.
$$

But Euler's criterion shows that

$$
5^{(N-1)/2}\equiv1\pmod N,
$$

and for a prime $N$ the element $5$ is a quadratic residue. Consequently the order of $5$ modulo $N$ contains the full factor $2^n$ of $N-1$; hence $v\not\equiv1\pmod N$. Since the only square roots of $1$ modulo a prime are $\pm1$,

$$
v\equiv-1\pmod N.
$$

Thus every prime $N=5\cdot2^n+1$ satisfies

$$
5^{5\cdot2^{\,n-2}}\equiv-1\pmod N.
$$

Conversely, suppose

$$
5^{5\cdot2^{\,n-2}}\equiv-1\pmod N.
$$

Squaring gives

$$
5^{N-1}\equiv1\pmod N.
$$

Furthermore,

$$
5^{(N-1)/2}
=
\left(5^{5\cdot2^{\,n-2}}\right)^2
\equiv1\pmod N,
$$

while

$$
5^{(N-1)/4}
\equiv-1\pmod N.
$$

Hence

$$
\gcd\!\left(5^{(N-1)/2}-1,N\right)=N,
$$

and

$$
\gcd\!\left(5^{(N-1)/4}-1,N\right)
=
\gcd(-2,N)
=1.
$$

The order of $5$ modulo $N$ therefore contains the factor $2^n$. Applying Pocklington's theorem with $f=2^n$ and prime divisor $2$, $N$ must be prime.

The resulting test is:

1. Set $u_0=3125\bmod N$.
2. Compute

$$
u_{k+1}\equiv u_k^2\pmod N
$$

for $0\le k\le n-2$.
3. Declare $N$ prime if and only if

$$
u_{n-2}\equiv-1\pmod N.
$$

This requires one initial modular exponentiation to obtain $5^5$, followed by exactly $n-2$ modular squarings. The running time is therefore essentially the same as the Lucas-Lehmer test, whose work is also dominated by a linear number of modular squarings.

## Verification

Assume $u_{n-2}\equiv-1\pmod N$.

Then

$$
u_{n-1}=u_{n-2}^2\equiv1\pmod N,
$$

so

$$
5^{N-1}\equiv1\pmod N.
$$

Also,

$$
5^{(N-1)/4}\equiv-1\pmod N,
$$

hence the order of $5$ modulo $N$ is divisible by $2^n$. Since $2^n$ is the complete factorization of $f=(N-1)/5$, Pocklington's theorem implies that $N$ is prime.

Assume instead that $N$ is prime.

Then $5$ is a quadratic residue modulo $N$, because $N\equiv1\pmod5$. Therefore

$$
5^{(N-1)/2}\equiv1\pmod N.
$$

The element

$$
5^{(N-1)/4}
$$

is a square root of $1$ modulo the prime $N$, hence equals $\pm1$. It cannot equal $1$, since that would force the order of $5$ to divide $(N-1)/4$, contradicting the presence of the full factor $2^n$ required by Pocklington's criterion. Therefore it equals $-1$, which is precisely the stated test condition.

## Notes

This test is the analogue for numbers

$$
N=5\cdot2^n+1
$$

of the Lucas-Lehmer test for Mersenne numbers. The computation consists of a fixed initialization followed by $n-2$ modular squarings, so its asymptotic cost is the same. The primality criterion is

$$
\boxed{\;
5^{\,5\cdot2^{\,n-2}}
\equiv -1 \pmod{\,5\cdot2^n+1\,}
\;}
$$

for $n\ge2$.
