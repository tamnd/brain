[Section 4.5.4: Factoring into Primes](../)

**Exercise 36.** [*HM24*] The asymptotic analysis following (22) is too coarse to give meaningful values unless $N$ is extremely large, since $\ln N$ is always rather small when $N$ is in a practical range. Carry out a more precise analysis that gives insights into the behavior of (22) for reasonable values of $N$; also explicitly find the optimal value of $m$ that minimizes (22) except for a factor of size at most $\exp(O(\log \log N))$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m10s  

---

## Solution

Equation (22) has the form

$$
T(m)\asymp m+\frac{\ln N}{\ln m},
$$

up to multiplicative factors that vary only slowly with $m$. The asymptotic discussion in the text replaces all lower-order effects by $O(1)$ terms, which is inadequate when $N$ is of practical size, because $\ln N$ is seldom large enough for such approximations to dominate the behavior.

To obtain a more accurate estimate, write

$$
L=\ln N,\qquad t=\ln m,
$$

so that $m=e^t$ and

$$
T(t)\asymp e^t+\frac{L}{t}.
$$

The minimizing value of $t$ satisfies

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\!\left(e^t+\frac{L}{t}\right)
=
e^t-\frac{L}{t^2}
=0.
$$

Hence the optimum is determined by

$$
e^t=\frac{L}{t^2},
$$

or equivalently

$$
t+2\ln t=\ln L.
\eqno(1)
$$

The crude asymptotic treatment replaces $t$ by $\ln L$, giving

$$
m\approx L=\ln N.
$$

Equation (1) shows that this is not sufficiently accurate. Let

$$
t=\ln L-2\ln\ln L+c.
$$

Substituting into (1) gives

$$
\ln L-2\ln\ln L+c
+
2\ln\!\bigl(\ln L-2\ln\ln L+c\bigr)
=
\ln L.
$$

Since

$$
\ln\!\bigl(\ln L-2\ln\ln L+c\bigr)
=
\ln\ln L
+
O\!\left(\frac{\ln\ln L}{\ln L}\right),
$$

we obtain

$$
c=O\!\left(\frac{\ln\ln L}{\ln L}\right).
$$

Therefore

$$
t
=
\ln L-2\ln\ln L
+
O\!\left(\frac{\ln\ln L}{\ln L}\right),
$$

and

$$
m
=
e^t
=
\frac{L}{(\ln L)^2}
\exp\!\left(
O\!\left(\frac{\ln\ln L}{\ln L}\right)
\right).
$$

Since $L=\ln N$,

$$
m
=
\frac{\ln N}{(\ln\ln N)^2}
\exp\!\left(
O\!\left(\frac{\ln\ln\ln N}{\ln\ln N}\right)
\right).
\eqno(2)
$$

This determines the optimal choice of $m$ up to a multiplicative factor

$$
\exp(O(\log\log N)),
$$

and in fact much more sharply.

To estimate the minimum value, use the relation $e^t=L/t^2$ at the optimum:

$$
T_{\min}
\asymp
\frac{L}{t^2}
+
\frac{L}{t}
=
\frac{L}{t}
\left(1+\frac1t\right).
$$

With

$$
t
=
\ln L-2\ln\ln L+O(1),
$$

this becomes

$$
T_{\min}
\asymp
\frac{L}
     {\ln L-2\ln\ln L}
\left(
1+
O\!\left(\frac1{\ln L}\right)
\right).
$$

Hence

$$
T_{\min}
=
\frac{\ln N}
     {\ln\ln N-2\ln\ln\ln N}
\left(
1+
O\!\left(\frac1{\ln\ln N}\right)
\right).
\eqno(3)
$$

Formula (3) explains the practical behavior much better than the coarse asymptotic estimate. For realistic values of $N$, the correction term $2\ln\ln\ln N$ is comparable in size to $\ln\ln N$, so the naive choice $m\approx\ln N$ substantially overestimates the optimal parameter.

The optimal value of the parameter is therefore

$$
\boxed{
m=
\frac{\ln N}{(\ln\ln N)^2}
\exp\!\left(
O\!\left(\frac{\ln\ln\ln N}{\ln\ln N}\right)
\right)
}.
$$

The corresponding minimum value of (22) is

$$
\boxed{
T_{\min}
=
\frac{\ln N}
     {\ln\ln N-2\ln\ln\ln N}
\left(
1+
O\!\left(\frac1{\ln\ln N}\right)
\right)
}.
$$

This completes the proof.

∎
