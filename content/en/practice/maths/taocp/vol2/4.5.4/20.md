## Setup

Let $D$ be a given positive integer, and let $p$ range over odd primes. In Exercise 19, we were asked to construct a number $M$ divisible by all odd primes $p$ such that $p-1$ divides $D$. In this exercise, we are asked to consider $p+1$ instead of $p-1$, i.e., to construct a number $M$ divisible by all odd primes $p$ such that $p+1 \mid D$.

Formally, define the set of primes

$\mathcal{P} = \{ p \text{ odd prime} : p + 1 \mid D \}.$

The goal is to produce an integer $M$ such that

$p \mid M \quad \text{for all } p \in \mathcal{P},$

and to describe an effective method to find such $M$, analogous to Pollard's construction in Exercise 19. The hint in Exercise 19 suggests considering numbers of the form $a^k - 1$, which arise naturally from the divisibility condition $p-1 \mid k$; here, we must adapt the idea to $p+1 \mid D$.

## Solution

Let $D$ be given, and write its prime factorization as

$D = 2^{\delta} \cdot \prod_{i=1}^r q_i^{\alpha_i},$

where the $q_i$ are odd primes. Suppose $p$ is an odd prime satisfying $p+1 \mid D$. Then $p+1$ is an even divisor of $D$, so we may write

$p+1 = 2^e \cdot d, \quad \text{with } d \mid \prod_i q_i^{\alpha_i}, \ e \ge 1.$

We wish to find $M$ divisible by all such $p$. Consider the factorization

$p+1 = 2^e \cdot d \implies p = 2^e \cdot d - 1.$

To construct $M$, we follow a strategy analogous to Pollard's: choose a base $a$ such that $\gcd(a, p) = 1$ for all $p$, and use numbers of the form

$a^{2^k \cdot d} - 1.$

If $k = e-1$, then $a^{2^{e-1} \cdot d} \equiv -1 \pmod{p}$, so that

$a^{2^e \cdot d} - 1 \equiv 0 \pmod{p}.$

Hence, every prime $p$ with $p+1 = 2^e \cdot d \mid D$ divides

$M := \prod_{d \mid D/2, \, 1 \le k \le \log_2(D/d)} (a^{2^k \cdot d} - 1).$

This ensures that each $p$ satisfying $p+1 \mid D$ appears as a factor of $M$. In practice, one chooses $a = 2$ or any small integer coprime to all candidate $p$, then multiplies together all such $a^{2^k \cdot d} - 1$ for appropriate $d$ and $k$ as above.

This construction generalizes Pollard's method: the key observation is that $p \mid a^{p+1 - 1} + 1$, and $p+1 \mid D$ allows us to select an exponent of the form $2^k \cdot d$ so that $a^{2^k \cdot d} \equiv -1 \pmod{p}$.

## Verification

Consider $p \in \mathcal{P}$ with $p+1 = 2^e \cdot d \mid D$. Then $a^{2^{e-1} \cdot d} \equiv -1 \pmod{p}$, so

$(a^{2^{e-1} \cdot d} - 1)(a^{2^{e-1} \cdot d} + 1) = a^{2^e \cdot d} - 1 \equiv 0 \pmod{p}.$

Hence $p \mid a^{2^e \cdot d} - 1$, as required. Since $a^{2^e \cdot d} - 1$ divides the product $M$, it follows that $p \mid M$. Every $p$ satisfying $p+1 \mid D$ is included because we have taken all divisors $d$ of $D/2$ and all powers $k$ such that $2^k \cdot d \le D$, ensuring $2^k \cdot d = 2^{e} \cdot d$ occurs for each such $p$.

Overflow or redundancy can be avoided by removing duplicate factors corresponding to equal $2^k \cdot d$. This completes the verification.

## Notes

1. The method is efficient when all prime factors of $p+1$ are small, analogous to the Pollard approach for $p-1$. If $p+1$ contains a single large prime factor, the method may fail with high probability, and more sophisticated algorithms such as elliptic-curve factorization may be required.
2. The choice of $a$ may be optimized to reduce the size of $M$. In practice, small primes such as $a=2$ suffice for general-purpose computation.
3. This method produces a number $M$ divisible by all $p$ with $p+1 \mid D$, but $M$ may contain additional prime factors not of the form $p$ with $p+1 \mid D$. Such extraneous factors do not affect the correctness for factoring purposes.

This completes the proof.

∎

The final construction of $M$ can be explicitly expressed as

$\boxed{M = \prod_{d \mid D/2} \prod_{k=1}^{\lfloor \log_2(D/d) \rfloor} (a^{2^k \cdot d} - 1)},$

where $a$ is any integer coprime to all candidate primes $p$.
