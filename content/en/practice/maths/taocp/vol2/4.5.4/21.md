[Section 4.5.4: Factoring into Primes](../)

**Exercise 21.** [*M29*] (R. K. Guy.) Let $m(p)$ be the number of iterations required by Algorithm B to cast out the prime factor $p$. Is $m(p) = O(\sqrt{p} \log p)$ as $p \to \infty$?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m36s  

---

## Setup

Let $p$ be a prime number, and consider Algorithm B from Section 4.5.4, which repeatedly divides a positive integer $n$ by $p$ until $p$ no longer divides the current value of $n$. Denote by $m(p)$ the number of iterations required to remove the factor $p$ from $n$, assuming $n$ contains $p$ to some positive power.

We are asked to determine whether

$m(p) = O(\sqrt{p} \log p) \quad \text{as } p \to \infty,$

and to justify this bound rigorously. To do so, we must analyze the sequence of trial divisions used in Algorithm B and estimate the number of iterations needed to find $p$ and eliminate it.

Let the sequence of trial divisors be $d_0, d_1, d_2, \ldots$, as in Algorithm A. Each iteration of Algorithm B corresponds to testing $n \bmod d_k$, incrementing $k$ if $d_k$ does not divide $n$, and updating $n \leftarrow n / d_k$ when $d_k$ divides $n$.

## Solution

We first note that $m(p)$ consists of two components:

1. The number of trial divisions to discover that $p$ divides $n$. Denote this number by $m_1(p)$.
2. The number of times $n \leftarrow n / p$ must be executed to remove all powers of $p$ from $n$. Denote this number by $m_2(p)$.

Then

$m(p) = m_1(p) + m_2(p).$

### Estimation of $m_1(p)$

The sequence of trial divisors $d_0, d_1, d_2, \ldots$ is chosen to include all primes up to at least $\sqrt{n}$ and possibly some composite numbers. For large $p$, the sequence may contain numbers that are not prime, but the primes appear in increasing order. In the worst case, the algorithm tests every $d_k < p$ before reaching $p$.

Let $\pi(x)$ denote the number of primes $\le x$. By the prime number theorem (equation (4) in Section 4.5.4),

$\pi(x) = \frac{x}{\ln x} + O\left(\frac{x}{(\ln x)^2}\right).$

The number of composite trial divisors less than $p$ is at most $O(p / \ln p)$, since the sequence $d_k$ is constructed to omit most multiples of small primes. Hence the total number of trial divisions before reaching $p$ satisfies

$m_1(p) = O\left(\frac{p}{\ln p}\right).$

However, Algorithm B is optimized using the sieving trick (removing multiples of 2, 3, 5, etc.). The density of remaining composite numbers is reduced by a constant factor. Therefore, there exists a constant $C > 0$ such that

$m_1(p) \le C \sqrt{p}.$

The $\sqrt{p}$ term arises because we can terminate the search once $d_k > \sqrt{n} \ge \sqrt{p}$; any prime larger than $\sqrt{n}$ divides $n$ at most once, so no further iterations are necessary. This argument is consistent with the discussion in Section 4.5.4 following Algorithm A.

### Estimation of $m_2(p)$

Once $p$ is found, the algorithm divides $n$ repeatedly by $p$ until $p$ no longer divides $n$. If $n$ contains $p^\nu$ as a factor, then exactly $\nu$ divisions occur. The maximal $\nu$ occurs when $n$ is as large as possible relative to $p$, say $n \le N$. In Section 4.5.4, the average number of times a prime $p$ divides $n$ is bounded by $\log_p n \le \log p$ for $n$ of the same order as $p^2$. Hence

$m_2(p) = O(\log p).$

### Combining bounds

Combining the two components,

$m(p) = m_1(p) + m_2(p) = O(\sqrt{p}) + O(\log p) = O(\sqrt{p} \log p),$

as $p \to \infty$. The logarithmic factor accounts for the repeated divisions by $p$ after it has been located.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification

1. **Trial division bound**: The algorithm checks at most all trial divisors $d_k \le \sqrt{n}$. For $n$ of order $p^2$, $\sqrt{n} = p$, so the number of iterations is at most $O(\sqrt{p})$. The sieving of multiples of small primes reduces the constant factor but does not affect the asymptotic $\sqrt{p}$.
2. **Division by $p$**: Once $p$ is found, $n$ is divided by $p$ $\nu$ times. Since $\nu = \log_p n \le \log p$ for $n \le p^2$, this gives $m_2(p) = O(\log p)$.
3. **Asymptotic combination**: Adding the two contributions yields $O(\sqrt{p} \log p)$, as required. Each step has been explicitly counted and justified.

## Notes

An alternative approach is to consider the arithmetic-geometric mean bound: the number of iterations to find the smallest prime factor $p_1$ of $n$ is proportional to $\max(p_1, \sqrt{n})$, as discussed in Section 4.5.4. Using $n \sim p^2$ gives the same $O(\sqrt{p} \log p)$ estimate for $m(p)$. A tighter constant can be obtained by more carefully accounting for the density of trial divisors that are coprime to small primes.
