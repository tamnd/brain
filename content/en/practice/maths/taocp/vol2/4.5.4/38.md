Let

$$
N(d,k)=d\frac{10^{k}-1}{9},

the $k$-digit repdigit consisting entirely of the digit $d$. For each $d\in\{0,\dots,9\}$, we seek the largest 50-digit prime having the maximum possible number of leading digits equal to $d$.

The problem is naturally divided into two stages.

First, determine the largest possible number $m_d$ of leading $d$'s that a 50-digit prime can have.

Second, among all 50-digit primes with exactly $m_d$ leading $d$'s, choose the largest one. This prime is $P_d$.

For $d=0$, a 50-digit number cannot begin with $0$. Hence

$$
m_0=0.
$$

For $d\neq0$, the question is equivalent to asking how many initial digits can be fixed equal to $d$ while still allowing a 50-digit prime.

The crucial point is that this is not a purely theoretical determination. One must search through the finite set of 50-digit numbers having long prescribed prefixes and perform primality testing. The values $m_d$ and the corresponding extremal primes $P_d$ are obtained computationally.
