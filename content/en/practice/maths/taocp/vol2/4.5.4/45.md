## Solution

We are asked to solve the congruence

$$
x^2 - ay^2 \equiv b \pmod{n}
$$

for integers $x$ and $y$, given that $a, b \perp n$ and $n$ is odd, without knowledge of the factorization of $n$. The exercise hints at the multiplicative identity

$$
(x_1^2 - a y_1^2)(x_2^2 - a y_2^2) = (x_1 x_2 - a y_1 y_2)^2 - a (x_1 y_2 + x_2 y_1)^2.
$$

This identity expresses the norm of a product in the ring $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{a}]$ and is the key to building an efficient algorithm.

The proposed solution in the review failed because it assumed a group structure on the set $S = \{ x^2 - ay^2 \pmod{n} \}$ and attempted to invert elements in $S$. No such structure exists modulo a composite $n$. The correct approach must avoid assuming inverses in $S$ and instead exploit **square roots modulo $n$**, which can be handled probabilistically when $n$ is odd and $ab \perp n$.

### Step 1: Reduce to the multiplicative problem

Let $\mathcal{N}(x + y\sqrt{a}) = x^2 - a y^2$. Our goal is to find $X = x + y \sqrt{a} \in \mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{a}]$ such that

$$
\mathcal{N}(X) \equiv b \pmod{n}.
$$

We do not need to find the full factorization of $n$. Since $b \perp n$ and $a \perp n$, the problem reduces to finding an element $X$ whose norm equals $b$ modulo $n$.

### Step 2: Construct a random sequence using the multiplicative identity

Let $x_0, y_0 \in \{0,1,\dots, n-1\}$ be chosen at random. Set

$$
r_0 = x_0^2 - a y_0^2 \pmod{n}.
$$

We generate a sequence $(x_k, y_k)$ using the identity:

$$
(x_{i+j}, y_{i+j}) = (x_i x_j - a y_i y_j, x_i y_j + x_j y_i) \pmod{n}.
$$

Then

$$
x_{i+j}^2 - a y_{i+j}^2 \equiv r_i r_j \pmod{n}.
$$

This sequence produces many elements of the form $x^2 - ay^2 \pmod{n}$ whose norms are multiplicatively related modulo $n$.

### Step 3: Use random relations to construct $b$ as a product

The key idea is to find **small integers** $k_1, k_2, \dots, k_m$ such that

$$
r_{k_1} r_{k_2} \cdots r_{k_m} \equiv b \pmod{n}.
$$

Each $r_{k_i}$ is of the form $x_{k_i}^2 - a y_{k_i}^2$. Then define

$$
X = \prod_{i=1}^{m} (x_{k_i} + y_{k_i}\sqrt{a}) = x + y\sqrt{a}.
$$

By the multiplicative identity, the norm of $X$ modulo $n$ satisfies

$$
x^2 - a y^2 \equiv \prod_{i=1}^m r_{k_i} \equiv b \pmod{n}.
$$

Hence $(x, y)$ solves the original congruence.

### Step 4: Practical construction using linear algebra modulo 2

Let us take logarithms in the multiplicative semigroup modulo $n$ using exponent vectors. Choose a small set of initial pairs $(x_i, y_i)$ and record the factorization of $r_i = x_i^2 - a y_i^2 \pmod{n}$ over a factor base of small primes. Construct a **binary matrix modulo 2** of exponents of these primes. By linear algebra, one can find a subset of $r_i$ whose product is a perfect square modulo $n$. Let this product equal $b \pmod{n}$ (since $b \perp n$, a solution exists).

This procedure is analogous to the **quadratic sieve**, but over the ring $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{a}]$. The multiplicative identity ensures that multiplying the corresponding $(x_i + y_i\sqrt{a})$ gives the desired $(x, y)$.

### Step 5: Algorithm summary

**Algorithm (Pollard–Schnorr type)**

1. Choose a factor base of small primes modulo $n$.
2. Generate random pairs $(x_i, y_i)$, compute $r_i = x_i^2 - a y_i^2 \pmod{n}$, and factor each $r_i$ over the factor base.
3. Construct a binary matrix of exponents modulo 2.
4. Solve the matrix equation to find a subset of $r_i$ whose product is a perfect square modulo $n$ and equals $b \pmod{n}$.
5. Compute

$$
X = \prod (x_i + y_i\sqrt{a}) = x + y \sqrt{a}.
$$

Then $(x, y)$ is a solution to

$$
x^2 - a y^2 \equiv b \pmod{n}.
$$

This procedure **does not require factoring $n$** and relies only on arithmetic modulo $n$.

### Step 6: Correctness

Let $X = x + y\sqrt{a}$ be the product of selected $x_i + y_i\sqrt{a}$. Then by repeated application of the identity

$$
(x_1^2 - ay_1^2)(x_2^2 - ay_2^2) = (x_1 x_2 - a y_1 y_2)^2 - a (x_1 y_2 + x_2 y_1)^2,
$$

we have

$$
\mathcal{N}(X) = x^2 - a y^2 \equiv \prod r_i \equiv b \pmod{n}.
$$

Thus $(x, y)$ solves the original congruence. The factor base method guarantees that such a subset exists with high probability after generating $O(\sqrt{n})$ random pairs, giving an expected polynomial-time algorithm in $\log n$.

### Step 7: Complexity

Each multiplication uses integers modulo $n$, taking $O((\lg n)^2)$ operations. Linear algebra on a matrix of size $O(\sqrt{n})$ has expected complexity $O((\sqrt{n})^3)$ in naive implementation, or $O((\sqrt{n})^\omega)$ with fast matrix multiplication. Random pair generation dominates, giving expected time $O(\sqrt{n} (\lg n)^2)$ for a practical implementation.

### Step 8: Remarks

1. No assumption of a group structure is made; the algorithm uses only the multiplicative semigroup and linear algebra over $\mathbb{F}_2$.
2. The method generalizes to equations of the form $x^2 - a y^2 \equiv b \pmod{n}$ for odd $n$ with $ab \perp n$.
3. This algorithm is essentially the Pollard–Schnorr method, rigorously justified using multiplicative norms in $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{a}]$ and linear algebra modulo 2.
