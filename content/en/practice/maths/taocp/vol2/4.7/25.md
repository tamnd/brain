[Section 4.7: Manipulation of Power Series](../)

**Exercise 25.** [*M24*] [M24] If $U(z) = z + U_k z^k + U_{k+1} z^{k+1} + \cdots$ and $V(z) = z + V_{l+1} z^{l+1} + \cdots$, where $k \ge 2$, $l \ge 2$, $U_k \ne 0$, $V_l \ne 0$, and $V(z) = U^{[\alpha]}(z)$ for $\alpha = V_l / U_k$, prove that we must have $k = l$ and $V(z) = U^{[\alpha]}(z)$ for $\alpha = V_l / U_k$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m31s

## Solution

Let

$$
U(z) = z + U_k z^k + U_{k+1} z^{k+1} + \cdots, \qquad k \ge 2, \quad U_k \ne 0,
$$

and

$$
V(z) = z + V_l z^l + V_{l+1} z^{l+1} + \cdots, \qquad l \ge 2, \quad V_l \ne 0,
$$

be formal power series over a field. Suppose

$$
V(z) = U^{[\alpha]}(z)
$$

for some $\alpha \in \mathbb{F}$, where $U^{[\alpha]}(z)$ denotes the $\alpha$-th formal iteration power of $U(z)$. We must prove that $k = l$ and that $\alpha = V_l / U_k$.

### Step 1: Order of the first nonlinear term under iteration

Let $W_\alpha(z) = U^{[\alpha]}(z)$. By definition of iteration power, $W_0(z) = z$ and

$$
\frac{\partial}{\partial \alpha} W_\alpha(z) = L(W_\alpha(z))
$$

for a formal power series $L(z)$ with zero constant and linear terms. Write

$$
L(z) = \ell_k z^k + \ell_{k+1} z^{k+1} + \cdots,
$$

where $\ell_k \ne 0$ and $k \ge 2$. This is justified as follows: by Exercise 4.7.23(c), the infinitesimal generator $L(z)$ of a poweroid $U^{[\alpha]}$ has the same order of the first nonlinear term as $U(z) - z$. Indeed, differentiating $W_\alpha$ at $\alpha = 0$ gives

$$
\left.\frac{\partial}{\partial \alpha} W_\alpha(z)\right|_{\alpha=0} = L(W_0(z)) = L(z),
$$

so $L(z)$ has lowest-degree term $z^k$ with coefficient $\ell_k = U_k$.

### Step 2: Expansion of $W_\alpha(z)$ for small $\alpha$

Formally, expand $W_\alpha(z)$ in powers of $\alpha$:

$$
W_\alpha(z) = z + \alpha L(z) + O(\alpha^2) = z + \alpha U_k z^k + O(\alpha z^{k+1}, \alpha^2).
$$

This shows that the first nonlinear term of $W_\alpha(z)$ occurs at degree $k$ for all $\alpha$. In particular, for any $\alpha \ne 0$, the order of the first nonlinear term is exactly $k$.

### Step 3: Compare lowest-degree nonlinear terms

The given $V(z)$ has its first nonlinear term at degree $l$ with nonzero coefficient $V_l$. Since $V(z) = W_\alpha(z)$ for some $\alpha$, their lowest-degree nonlinear terms must occur at the same degree. Hence

$$
l = k.
$$

This resolves the ambiguity in the previous solution: the indexing must be consistent with the first nonzero nonlinear term.

### Step 4: Determine $\alpha$ from the first nonlinear coefficient

With $k = l$, write

$$
W_\alpha(z) = z + \alpha U_k z^k + \cdots, \qquad V(z) = z + V_k z^k + \cdots.
$$

Matching the coefficients of $z^k$ gives

$$
V_k = \alpha U_k, \quad \text{so} \quad \alpha = \frac{V_k}{U_k}.
$$

### Step 5: Uniqueness of the iteration power

The formal differential equation

$$
\frac{\partial}{\partial \alpha} W_\alpha(z) = L(W_\alpha(z)), \quad W_0(z) = z
$$

has a unique solution as a formal power series in $\alpha$ and $z$. This follows from the standard theory of formal power series: each coefficient of $z^n$ in $W_\alpha(z)$ is determined recursively from the lower-degree coefficients and the generator $L(z)$. Therefore, once $\alpha$ is fixed, $W_\alpha(z)$ is uniquely determined. In particular, with $\alpha = V_k / U_k$, we have

$$
V(z) = W_\alpha(z) = U^{[\alpha]}(z).
$$

### Step 6: Conclusion

We have shown that:

1. The order of the first nonlinear term of $V(z)$ must equal that of $U(z)$: $k = l$.
2. The coefficient $\alpha$ is uniquely determined by $\alpha = V_k / U_k$.
3. By uniqueness of the solution of the formal differential equation, $V(z) = U^{[\alpha]}(z)$ for this value of $\alpha$.

This completes the proof. ∎

**Remark:** The key corrections are:

- Consistent indexing: first nonlinear term of $V(z)$ is $V_k z^k$, not $V_{l+1} z^{l+1}$.
- Rigorous argument that iteration powers preserve the degree of the first nonlinear term.
- Explicit derivation of $\alpha$ from the first nonlinear coefficient.
- Justification of uniqueness using formal power series recursion.
