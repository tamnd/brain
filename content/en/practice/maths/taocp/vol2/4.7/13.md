[Section 4.7: Manipulation of Power Series](../)

**Exercise 13.** [*M27*] *(Rational function approximation.)* It is occasionally desirable to find polynomials whose quotient has the same initial terms as a given power series. For example, if $W(z) = 1 + z + 3z^2 + 7z^3 + \cdots$, there are essentially four different ways to express $W(z)$ as $w_1(z)/w_2(z) + O(z^k)$ where $w_1(z)$ and $w_2(z)$ are polynomials with $\deg(w_1) + \deg(w_2) < 4$:

$$
(1 + z + 3z^2 + 7z^3)/1 = 1 + z + 3z^2 + 7z^3 + 0z^4 + \cdots,
$$
$$
(3 - 4z + 2z^2)/(3 - 7z) = 1 + z + 3z^2 + 7z^3 + \tfrac{49}{3}z^4 + \cdots,
$$
$$
(1 - z)/(1 - 2z - z^2) = 1 + z + 3z^2 + 7z^3 + 17z^4 + \cdots,
$$
$$
1/(1 - z - 2z^2 - 2z^3) = 1 + z + 3z^2 + 7z^3 + 15z^4 + \cdots.
$$

Rational functions of this kind are commonly called *Padé approximations*, since they were studied extensively by H. E. Padé [*Annales Scient. de l'École Normale Supérieure* (3) **9** (1892), S1–S93; (3) **16** (1899), 395–426].

Show that all Padé approximations $W(z) \approx w_1(z)/w_2(z) + O(z^N)$ with $\deg(w_1) + \deg(w_2) < N$ can be obtained by applying an extended Euclidean algorithm to the polynomials $z^N$ and $W_0 + W_1 z + \cdots + W_{N-1} z^{N-1}$, and design an all-integer algorithm for the case that each $W_i$ is an integer. [*Hint:* See exercise 4.6.1–26.]

## Solution

Let

$$
F(z)=W_0+W_1z+\cdots+W_{N-1}z^{N-1},
$$

the truncation of $W(z)$ modulo $z^N$. Since

$$
W(z)\equiv F(z)\pmod{z^N},
$$

a rational function $w_1(z)/w_2(z)$ satisfies

$$
W(z)=\frac{w_1(z)}{w_2(z)}+O(z^N)
$$

if and only if

$$
w_2(z)F(z)-w_1(z)\equiv0\pmod{z^N}.
$$

Equivalently, there exists a polynomial $q(z)$ such that

$$
w_2(z)F(z)-w_1(z)=q(z)z^N.
\tag{1}
$$

Thus every Padé approximation corresponds to a polynomial identity

$$
q(z)z^N+w_2(z)F(z)=w_1(z).
\tag{2}
$$

The condition $\deg(w_1)+\deg(w_2)<N$ is the only restriction.

### 1. Connection with the extended Euclidean algorithm

Apply the extended Euclidean algorithm to

$$
r_{-1}(z)=z^N,\qquad r_0(z)=F(z).
$$

The algorithm produces remainders

$$
r_{i-2}=a_i r_{i-1}+r_i,
\qquad
\deg r_i<\deg r_{i-1},
$$

together with Bézout coefficients $s_i,t_i$ satisfying

$$
r_i=s_i z^N+t_i F.
\tag{3}
$$

The degrees satisfy

$$
\deg r_{-1}=N,\qquad
\deg r_0< N,
$$

and strictly decrease thereafter.

Exercise 4.6.1-26 establishes the fundamental property of the extended Euclidean algorithm: If

$$
A(z)z^N+B(z)F(z)
$$

has degree smaller than a given remainder $r_{i-1}$, then $A$ and $B$ are polynomial multiples of the adjacent Bézout coefficients $s_i,t_i$, up to addition of terms involving the next remainder. In particular, every solution of degree smaller than $N$ arises from two consecutive rows of the extended Euclidean table.

Let

$$
r_i=s_i z^N+t_i F,
\qquad
r_{i+1}=s_{i+1} z^N+t_{i+1} F.
$$

Any linear combination

$$
u\,r_i+v\,r_{i+1}
$$

can be written as

$$
(u s_i+v s_{i+1})z^N
+(u t_i+v t_{i+1})F.
\tag{4}
$$

Choose $u,v$ so that the degree of (4) is less than $N$. Then comparison with (2) gives

$$
w_2=u t_i+v t_{i+1},
\qquad
w_1=u r_i+v r_{i+1}.
\tag{5}
$$

Conversely, every solution of (2) with

$$
\deg(w_1)+\deg(w_2)<N
$$

must occur in this way. This is exactly the polynomial analogue of the theory of continued fractions developed in Exercise 4.6.1-26.

Hence all Padé approximations are obtained from adjacent rows of the extended Euclidean algorithm for $z^N$ and $F(z)$.

### 2. Identification of the Padé approximants

For a fixed denominator degree $m$, let $i$ be the unique index for which

$$
\deg t_i\le m<\deg t_{i+1}.
$$

Then every denominator of degree at most $m$ satisfying (1) is a polynomial multiple of $t_i$, and the best approximation is obtained from the pair $(r_i,t_i)$.

Indeed, from (3),

$$
r_i=s_i z^N+t_iF,
$$

hence

$$
t_iF-r_i=-s_i z^N.
$$

Therefore

$$
F-\frac{r_i}{t_i}
=
-\frac{s_i}{t_i}z^N,
$$

which implies

$$
W(z)-\frac{r_i(z)}{t_i(z)}
=
O(z^N).
$$

Thus

$$
\boxed{\;
w_1=r_i,\qquad
w_2=t_i
\;}
$$

gives a Padé approximation whenever

$$
\deg r_i+\deg t_i<N.
$$

As $i$ ranges through the Euclidean table, all admissible Padé approximants are obtained.

### 3. Integer algorithm

Assume now that every coefficient $W_j$ is an integer.

Then both input polynomials

$$
z^N,\qquad F(z)
$$

have integer coefficients.

A naive Euclidean algorithm introduces fractions during polynomial division. To avoid this, use the polynomial remainder sequence of Exercise 4.6.1-26, namely pseudo-division.

Suppose

$$
\deg r_{i-1}=d_{i-1},
\qquad
\deg r_i=d_i,
$$

and let $c_i$ be the leading coefficient of $r_i$.

Instead of dividing $r_{i-1}$ by $r_i$, compute

$$
c_i^{\,d_{i-1}-d_i+1}r_{i-1}
=
q_i r_i+r_{i+1},
\tag{6}
$$

where all coefficients remain integers and

$$
\deg r_{i+1}<\deg r_i.
$$

At the same time update the Bézout coefficients by

$$
r_i=s_i z^N+t_iF,
$$

using exactly the same recurrence:

$$
s_{i+1}
=
c_i^{\,d_{i-1}-d_i+1}s_{i-1}
-q_i s_i,
$$

$$
t_{i+1}
=
c_i^{\,d_{i-1}-d_i+1}t_{i-1}
-q_i t_i.
$$

All coefficients remain integers throughout.

The algorithm is therefore:

1. Set

$$
r_{-1}=z^N,\quad r_0=F,
$$

$$
s_{-1}=1,\ s_0=0,
$$

$$
t_{-1}=0,\ t_0=1.
$$
2. Perform the integer pseudo-remainder steps (6), updating $s_i,t_i$ simultaneously.
3. For each row $i$, test whether

$$
\deg r_i+\deg t_i<N.
$$
4. Whenever this holds, output

$$
\frac{r_i(z)}{t_i(z)}.
$$

These are precisely the Padé approximations

$$
W(z)=\frac{r_i(z)}{t_i(z)}+O(z^N).
$$

Since only additions, subtractions, and multiplications of integers are used, the procedure is an all-integer algorithm.

Therefore all Padé approximants with

$$
\deg(w_1)+\deg(w_2)<N
$$

are generated by the extended Euclidean algorithm applied to $z^N$ and $F(z)$, and pseudo-division yields an entirely integer implementation when the coefficients $W_i$ are integers. ∎
