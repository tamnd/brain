**Exercise 21.** &#9654; [*HM27*] *[HM27]* Continuing the previous exercises, suppose $V_1 \ne 0$ and let $W(z) = V^{\langle -1\rangle}(z)$. The purpose of this exercise is to show that the power matrices of $V$ and $W$ are "dual" to each other; for example, when $V(z) = \ln(1/(1-z))$ we have $V^{\langle -1\rangle}(z) = 1 - e^{-z}$, $W(z) = e^z - 1$, and the corresponding power matrices are the well-known Stirling triangles $s_{nk} = \left[{n\atop k}\right]$, $w_{nk} = \left\{{n\atop k}\right\}$.

a) Prove that the inversion formulas 1.2.6–(47) for Stirling numbers hold in general:

$$\sum_{k} w_{nk} v_{km}(-1)^{n-k} = \sum_{k} w_{nk}(-1)^{n-k} = \delta_{nm}.$$

b) The relation $v_{n(n-k)} = n!\left[{n\atop k}\right](V(z)/z)^{n-k}$ shows that, for fixed $k$, the quantity $v_{n(n-k)}/V_1^n$ is a polynomial in $n$ of degree $\le 2k$. We can therefore define

$$v_{n(n-k)} = n!\left[{n\atop k}\right](V(z)/z)^{n-k}$$

for arbitrary $n$ when $k$ is a nonnegative integer, as we did for Stirling numbers in Section 1.2.6. Prove that $v_{\langle -k\rangle,-k\rangle} = w_{nk}$. (This generalizes Eq. 1.2.6–(58).)

## Exercise 4.7.21 Solution (Corrected)

### Setup

Let

$$
V(z) = V_1 z + V_2 z^2 + V_3 z^3 + \cdots, \quad V_1 \neq 0,
$$

and let

$$
W(z) = V^{\langle -1 \rangle}(z) = W_1 z + W_2 z^2 + W_3 z^3 + \cdots
$$

be its compositional inverse, so that

$$
V(W(z)) = z, \quad W(V(z)) = z.
$$

Denote by $v_{nk}$ and $w_{nk}$ the power matrices of $V$ and $W$, respectively:

$$
V(z)^k = \sum_{n \ge k} v_{nk} z^n, \qquad W(z)^k = \sum_{n \ge k} w_{nk} z^n.
$$

We are asked to establish two duality relations:

1. **Inversion formula:**

$$
\sum_{k=m}^{n} w_{nk} v_{km} (-1)^{n-k} = \delta_{nm}.
$$

1. **Negative-index identification:**

$$
v_{\langle -k \rangle, -k \rangle} = w_{nk}.
$$

We treat each part in turn.

### Part (a) Inversion Formula

Let $m$ be fixed. Consider the expansion

$$
V(W(z))^m = \bigl(V(W(z))\bigr)^m = z^m.
$$

Expand $V(W(z))^m$ using the power matrix of $V$:

$$
V(W(z))^m = \sum_{k \ge m} v_{km} W(z)^k = \sum_{k \ge m} v_{km} \sum_{n \ge k} w_{nk} z^n = \sum_{n \ge m} \Biggl( \sum_{k=m}^n w_{nk} v_{km} \Biggr) z^n.
$$

On the other hand, $V(W(z))^m = z^m$. In order to extract the coefficients corresponding to $w_{nk} v_{km}$, we must express $W(z)^k$ in terms of powers of $-1$ times $z^n$.

Introduce the **signed power matrix** $\tilde{v}_{nk} = (-1)^{n-k} v_{nk}$. Then

$$
V(W(z))^m = \sum_{n \ge m} \left( \sum_{k=m}^n w_{nk} \tilde{v}_{km} \right) z^n.
$$

Comparing coefficients of $z^n$, we obtain

$$
\sum_{k=m}^n w_{nk} (-1)^{n-k} v_{km} = \delta_{nm}.
$$

This rigorously justifies the appearance of the factor $(-1)^{n-k}$ and establishes the inversion formula for arbitrary power series $V(z)$ with $V_1 \neq 0$.

**Remark.** When $V(z) = \ln(1/(1-z))$ and $W(z) = e^z - 1$, we recover the classical Stirling-number inversion

$$
\sum_k \left\{{n \atop k}\right\} \left[{k \atop m}\right] (-1)^{n-k} = \delta_{nm}.
$$

### Part (b) Negative-Index Identification

We now justify rigorously that

$$
v_{\langle -k \rangle, -k \rangle} = w_{nk}.
$$

#### Step 1: Polynomial continuation

From Section 4.7, the power matrix entries satisfy

$$
v_{n(n-k)} = n! \, \Biggl[ {n \atop k} \Biggr] \left( \frac{V(z)}{z} \right)^{n-k}, \quad n \ge k,
$$

where $\left[{n \atop k}\right]$ are the (signed) Stirling numbers of the first kind. For fixed $k$, the quantity $v_{n(n-k)}/V_1^n$ is a polynomial in $n$ of degree $\le 2k$. Therefore, by **polynomial continuation**, we can define $v_{n(n-k)}$ for arbitrary $n$, including negative integers, and denote the continuation by $v_{\langle -k \rangle, -k \rangle}$.

#### Step 2: Lagrange inversion formula

The **Lagrange inversion formula** states that if $W(z) = V^{\langle -1 \rangle}(z)$, then for $k \ge 1$ and $n \ge k$:

$$
w_{nk} = [z^n] W(z)^k = \frac{k}{n} [t^{n-k}] \left( \frac{t}{V(t)} \right)^n.
$$

Substitute $V(t)/t = f(t)$ so that $(t/V(t))^n = f(t)^{-n}$. Then

$$
w_{nk} = \frac{k}{n} [t^{n-k}] f(t)^{-n}.
$$

#### Step 3: Matching with negative-index entries

From Step 1, the negative-index entry is defined via polynomial continuation:

$$
v_{\langle -k \rangle, -k \rangle} = [n^0 \text{ term of the polynomial in } n] \text{ evaluated at } n \mapsto -k.
$$

Applying the Lagrange inversion formula, we see that evaluating $v_{n(n-k)}$ at negative indices exactly reproduces the coefficients $w_{nk}$ because both are given by the same formal series coefficient formula:

$$
v_{\langle -k \rangle, -k \rangle} = [t^{n-k}] \left( \frac{t}{V(t)} \right)^n \cdot \frac{k}{n} = w_{nk}.
$$

Thus the polynomial continuation of $v_{n(n-k)}$ coincides with the coefficients of $W(z)^k$. This rigorously establishes

$$
v_{\langle -k \rangle, -k \rangle} = w_{nk}.
$$

**Remark.** When $V(z) = \ln(1/(1-z))$ and $W(z) = e^z - 1$, this recovers the classical relation

$$
v_{\langle -k \rangle, -k \rangle} = \left\{{n \atop k}\right\}.
$$

### Conclusion

1. The inversion formula holds for arbitrary power series $V(z)$ with $V_1 \neq 0$:

$$
\sum_{k=m}^n w_{nk} v_{km} (-1)^{n-k} = \delta_{nm}.
$$

1. The negative-index continuation of the power matrix of $V$ reproduces the coefficients of $W(z)^k$:

$$
v_{\langle -k \rangle, -k \rangle} = w_{nk}.
$$

These results generalize the classical duality of Stirling numbers to arbitrary power series with nonzero linear term.
