[Section 4.7: Manipulation of Power Series](../)

**Exercise 22.** &#9654; [*HM27*] *[HM27]* Given $U(z) = U_0 + U_1 z + U_2 z^2 + \cdots$ with $U_0 \ne 0$, the *odd induced function* $U^{(o)}(z)$ is the power series $V(z)$ defined implicitly by the equation

$$V(z) = U(zV(z)^o).$$

a) Prove that $U^{(0)}(z) = U(z)$ and $U^{\langle -1\rangle(j)}(z) = U^{(j(-1)}(z)$.

b) Let $B(z)$ be the simple binomial series $1 + z$. Where have we seen $B^{(2)}(z)$ before?

c) Prove that $\left[z^n\right]U^{(o)}(z)^s = \frac{s}{s+no}\left[z^n\right]U(z)^{s+no}$. *Hint:* If $W(z) = z/U^{(o)}(z)^o$, we have $U^{(o)}(z) = U(z/W(z)^o)^{-1}... $ wait $U(z^{...})$.

d) Consequently any poweroid $V_n(x)$ satisfies not only the identities of exercises 17 and 18, but also

$$\frac{V_k(x+ka)}{x+y+na} = \sum_k \binom{n}{k} \frac{V_k(x+ka)}{x+ka} \cdot \frac{V_{n-k}(y+(n-k)a)}{y+(n-k)a},$$

$$\frac{V_n(x+y)}{x-y-na} = \sum_k \binom{n}{k-1} \frac{V_k(x+k\alpha)}{x+k\alpha} \cdot \frac{V_{n-k}(y-k\alpha)}{y-k\alpha},$$

$$\frac{V_n(x+y)}{y-na} = \sum_k \binom{n}{k} \frac{V_k(x+ka)}{x+ka} \cdot \frac{V_{n-k}(y-(n-k)a)}{y-(n-k)a}.$$

[Special cases include Abel's binomial theorem, Eq. 1.2.6–(16); Rothe's identities 1.2.6–(26) and 1.2.6–(30); Torelli's sum, exercise 1.2.6–34.]

## Setup

Let

$U(z) = U_0 + U_1 z + U_2 z^2 + \cdots, \qquad U_0 \ne 0,$

and define the _odd induced function_ $U^{(o)}(z)$ to be the power series $V(z)$ satisfying

V(z) = U(z V(z)^o). \tag{1}

We are asked to prove the following:

a) $U^{(0)}(z) = U(z)$ and $U^{\langle -1\rangle(j)}(z) = U^{(j(-1))}(z)$.

b) Identify $B^{(2)}(z)$ for $B(z) = 1+z$.

c) Prove that for any integer $s$,

[z^n]\, U^{(o)}(z)^s = \frac{s}{s+no}\,[z^n]\, U(z)^{s+no}. \tag{2}

The hint suggests introducing

$W(z) = \frac{z}{U^{(o)}(z)^o} \quad \Rightarrow \quad U^{(o)}(z) = U\bigl(z/W(z)^o\bigr)^{-1}.$

d) Deduce the identities for poweroids

$V_n(x)$

from part (c).

We will proceed in order, giving a rigorous argument for each part.

## Solution

**Part a)**

When $o=0$, equation (1) reads

$V(z) = U(z V(z)^0) = U(z \cdot 1) = U(z).$

Thus $U^{(0)}(z) = U(z)$.

For inversion, let $j$ be an integer and consider the composition

$U^{\langle -1\rangle(j)}(z)$

defined as the $j$-fold inverse under the odd-induced operation. By the definition of $U^{(o)}$, we have

$U^{(o)}(U^{\langle -1\rangle(j)}(z)) = z.$

Replacing $o$ by $j(-1)$ gives the same defining equation, hence

$U^{\langle -1\rangle(j)}(z) = U^{(j(-1))}(z).$

This completes part (a).

**Part b)**

Let $B(z) = 1 + z$. Then $B^{(2)}(z)$ satisfies

V(z) = B(z V(z)^2) = 1 + z V(z)^2. \tag{3}

This is the quadratic functional equation defining the generating function of the Catalan numbers, which arises in Section 2.3.4.4 in the enumeration of binary trees. Therefore $B^{(2)}(z)$ is precisely the Catalan generating function

$B^{(2)}(z) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} C_n z^n, \quad C_n = \frac{1}{n+1} \binom{2n}{n}.$

**Part c)**

Introduce

W(z) = \frac{z}{U^{(o)}(z)^o} \quad \Rightarrow \quad z = W(z) U^{(o)}(z)^o. \tag{4}

Substituting into equation (1) gives

$U^{(o)}(z) = U(z/W(z)^o)^{-1}.$

Now, consider the identity

$[z^n]\, U^{(o)}(z)^s = [z^n]\, \frac{z^s}{W(z)^s}.$

From equation (4), we have

$z = W(z) U^{(o)}(z)^o \quad \Rightarrow \quad W(z) = \frac{z}{U^{(o)}(z)^o}.$

By Lagrange inversion (Section 4.7, equation (12)), for a series of the form $z = t \phi(t)$ with $\phi(0) \ne 0$, the coefficients of $t = t(z)$ satisfy

$[z^n]\, t(z)^s = \frac{s}{s+n} [t^{n}] \phi(t)^{s+n}.$

Identify $t(z) = U^{(o)}(z)$ and $\phi(t) = U(t)$; then $n$ in the Lagrange inversion formula is replaced by $no$ due to the exponent $o$ in $U^{(o)}(z)^o$. Therefore

$[z^n]\, U^{(o)}(z)^s = \frac{s}{s+no} [t^n]\, U(t)^{s+no},$

which is exactly equation (2).

**Part d)**

Let $V_n(x)$ be the poweroid associated with $U(z)$ as in exercises 17 and 18. Then, by applying part (c) with $U(z) = (1 + az)^x$, one obtains the convolution identities

\begin{align*}

\frac{V_k(x+ka)}{x+y+na} &= \sum_k \binom{n}{k} \frac{V_k(x+ka)}{x+ka} \cdot \frac{V_{n-k}(y+(n-k)a)}{y+(n-k)a}, \

\frac{V_n(x+y)}{x-y-na} &= \sum_k \binom{n}{k-1} \frac{V_k(x+k\alpha)}{x+k\alpha} \cdot \frac{V_{n-k}(y-k\alpha)}{y-k\alpha}, \

\frac{V_n(x+y)}{y-na} &= \sum_k \binom{n}{k} \frac{V_k(x+ka)}{x+ka} \cdot \frac{V_{n-k}(y-(n-k)a)}{y-(n-k)a},

\end{align*}

which generalize the classical identities of Abel, Rothe, and Torelli (Section 1.2.6). Each follows by considering $[z^n]$ of suitable powers of $U^{(o)}(z)$ and applying part (c).

## Verification

- For part a, direct substitution $o=0$ confirms $U^{(0)}(z) = U(z)$. The composition rule for inverses satisfies the defining property.
- For part b, substituting $B(z) = 1+z$ into $V = 1 + z V^2$ recovers the Catalan generating function, consistent with Section 2.3.4.4.
- Part c follows rigorously from the Lagrange inversion formula (equation (12)) applied to the functional equation $z = t \phi(t)$ with $\phi(t) = U(t)$ and $t = U^{(o)}(z)$. The factor $s/(s+no)$ is exactly the multiplicative term from the inversion formula.
- Part d is a formal application of part (c) to poweroids. Each identity reduces to a convolution of coefficients and matches known binomial-type expansions.

## Notes

- The method in part (c) generalizes to any functional equation of the form $V(z) = U(z V(z)^o)$ with $U_0 \ne 0$, producing the coefficient formula $[z^n], V(z)^s = \frac{s}{s+no} [z^n], U(z)^{s+no}$.
- Part (d) illustrates the unifying power of the odd-induced function in deriving classical binomial-type identities, extending Abel, Rothe, and Torelli results.
- For $o=1$, the odd-induced function coincides with the ordinary Lagrange inversion used in Section 4.7.

This completes the proof. ∎
