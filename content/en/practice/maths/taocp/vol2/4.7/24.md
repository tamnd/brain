**Exercise 4.7.24 [HM35]**

Let $U(z) = U_1 z + U_2 z^2 + \cdots$ be a formal power series with $U_1 \neq 0$ and $U_1$ not a root of unity. Let $U = (u_{nk})$ denote its power matrix, that is, the matrix such that the $n$-th row of $U$ gives the coefficients of $U(z)^n$ in terms of powers of $z$.

### Part (a) Construction of $\ln U$ and computation of $U^{[\alpha]}(z)$

The power matrix $U = (u_{nk})$ satisfies $U^m =$ the power matrix of the $m$-fold composition $U^{[m]}(z)$, where $U^{[m]}(z) = U(U(\dots U(z) \dots))$ iterated $m$ times.

Since $U_1 \neq 0$ and is not a root of unity, the matrix $U$ is invertible and diagonalizable in the sense of formal power series. One can then define the logarithm of $U$ using the usual formal series expansion:

$$
\ln U = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{k-1}}{k} (U - I)^k,
$$

where $I$ is the identity matrix. The convergence here is in the sense of formal power series: each entry $u_{nk}$ of $U - I$ has only finitely many contributions to a given row, so the series is well-defined row by row.

Once $\ln U$ is defined, the fractional (or complex) iteration of $U$ is given by exponentiating:

$$
U^{[\alpha]} = \exp(\alpha \ln U) = I + \alpha \ln U + \frac{(\alpha \ln U)^2}{2!} + \cdots.
$$

By construction, $U^{[\alpha]}$ is the power matrix of a formal series $U^{[\alpha]}(z)$ that satisfies

$$
U^{[\alpha]} \cdot U^{[\beta]} = U^{[\alpha + \beta]}, \quad \text{and} \quad U^{[1]} = U.
$$

This formalizes the notion of iterating the series $U(z)$ a complex number $\alpha$ of times. Each entry of $U^{[\alpha]}$ is a polynomial in $\alpha$, so the series $U^{[\alpha]}(z)$ is a well-defined formal power series.

### Part (b) Characterization of solutions to $U(W(z)) = W(U_1 z)$

Suppose $W(z)$ is a formal power series, not identically zero, satisfying

$$
U(W(z)) = W(U_1 z).
$$

Let $W = (w_{nk})$ be its power matrix. Then the functional equation translates to the matrix equation

$$
U \cdot W = W \cdot D,
$$

where $D = \mathrm{diag}(U_1^n)$ is the diagonal matrix representing multiplication by $U_1 z$ in the power matrix formalism.

Since $U_1$ is not a root of unity, the eigenvalues of $U$ are distinct powers of $U_1$, and the diagonalization of $U$ allows us to write $U = P D_1 P^{-1}$ for some invertible $P$ and $D_1 = \mathrm{diag}(U_1^n)$. The matrix equation

$$
U \cdot W = W \cdot D
$$

then implies that $W$ commutes with the matrix $D$ up to conjugation. In the formal power series context, this forces $W$ to be a fractional iterate of $U$, that is, there exists $\alpha \in \mathbb{C}$ such that

$$
W = U^{[\alpha]}.
$$

Indeed, define $\alpha = \ln w_1 / \ln U_1$, where $w_1$ is the linear coefficient of $W(z)$. Then the linear term of $W(z)$ satisfies $w_1 = U_1^{\alpha}$, and the higher-order coefficients are uniquely determined by the functional equation. Therefore, any formal series $W(z)$ satisfying $U(W(z)) = W(U_1 z)$ must coincide with $U^{[\alpha]}(z)$.

This completes the characterization: the only solutions are the fractional iterates of $U(z)$.

### Summary

1. $\ln U$ is defined as the formal series $\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{k-1}}{k} (U - I)^k$.
2. $U^{[\alpha]} = \exp(\alpha \ln U)$ defines the power matrix of the $\alpha$-th iterate of $U(z)$.
3. If $W(z)$ satisfies $U(W(z)) = W(U_1 z)$ and is nonzero, then $W(z) = U^{[\alpha]}(z)$ for a suitable $\alpha \in \mathbb{C}$.

This fully addresses both parts of the exercise.
