**Exercise 3.** [*M15*] Does formula (9) give the right results when $n = 0$? When $n = 1$?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m16s  

---

Formula (9) expresses $W_n$ for $n \ge 1$ in terms of the coefficients $V_k$ and the previously computed $W_{n-k}$, and it contains an explicit factor $1/n$. We examine the boundary cases $n=0$ and $n=1$ directly from this structure.

For $n=0$, formula (9) is not applicable as written. The presence of the factor $1/n$ makes the expression undefined at $n=0$, so the formula cannot be interpreted arithmetically in this case. Independently of this recurrence, the definition of $W(z)$ as a formal power series implies $W_0 = 1$. Hence formula (9) does not give a meaningful value at $n=0$, but this is consistent with the fact that the constant term is prescribed separately.

For $n=1$, we substitute into the second form of formula (9), which is a sum over indices $k = 1, \dots, n$. When $n=1$, this sum contains exactly one term, namely the term with $k=1$. There are no additional contributions and no possibility of overlap between endpoints, since the summation range collapses to a single index.

Thus the numerator reduces to the single contribution corresponding to $k=1$, which has the form

$$
(\alpha + 1 - 1)V_1 W_{1-1} = \alpha V_1 W_0.
$$

All terms with $k \ge 2$ are absent because they lie outside the summation range, not because of vanishing factors or undefined negative indices.

Dividing by $n=1$ gives

$$
W_1 = \alpha V_1 W_0.
$$

Using $W_0 = 1$, this yields

$$
W_1 = \alpha V_1.
$$

Therefore formula (9) produces the correct result at $n=1$, and there is no doubling or ambiguity in the evaluation when the summation limits are interpreted correctly.

For $n=0$, the formula is not defined but is consistent with the separately given initial condition $W_0 = 1$.
