## Solution

We are asked to prove that the poweroids $V_n(x)$ satisfy

$$
xV_n(x+y) = (x+y)\sum_{k=1}^{n} \binom{n-1}{k-1} V_k(x)V_{n-k}(y),
$$

continuing from Exercise 17. The hint suggests considering the derivative of $e^{V(z)}$.

### Step 1: Recall the generating function of poweroids

Let $\{V_n(x)\}$ be the poweroid sequence with generating function

$$
e^{xV(z)} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} V_n(x) \frac{z^n}{n!}.
$$

This is the standard exponential generating function for Sheffer-type sequences.

### Step 2: Compute the derivative

Consider the derivative of $e^{xV(z)}$ with respect to $z$:

$$
\frac{d}{dz} e^{xV(z)} = xV'(z) e^{xV(z)}.
$$

Similarly, for the product $e^{xV(z)} e^{yV(z)} = e^{(x+y)V(z)}$, we have

$$
\frac{d}{dz} \big(e^{xV(z)} e^{yV(z)}\big) = (x+y)V'(z) e^{(x+y)V(z)}.
$$

### Step 3: Express the derivative using generating functions

Write $e^{xV(z)}$ as a series:

$$
e^{xV(z)} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} V_k(x) \frac{z^k}{k!}, \quad
e^{yV(z)} = \sum_{\ell=0}^{\infty} V_\ell(y) \frac{z^\ell}{\ell!}.
$$

Then

$$
e^{(x+y)V(z)} = e^{xV(z)} e^{yV(z)} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \left( \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} V_k(x) V_{n-k}(y) \right) \frac{z^n}{n!}.
$$

### Step 4: Consider $z \frac{d}{dz} e^{(x+y)V(z)}$

The left-hand side of the identity involves $x V_n(x+y)$. To connect, consider

$$
z \frac{d}{dz} e^{(x+y)V(z)} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} n V_n(x+y) \frac{z^n}{n!}.
$$

On the other hand, using the product expansion:

$$
\begin{aligned}
z \frac{d}{dz} e^{(x+y)V(z)} &= z \frac{d}{dz} \left( e^{xV(z)} e^{yV(z)} \right) \\
&= z \left( \frac{d}{dz} e^{xV(z)} \cdot e^{yV(z)} + e^{xV(z)} \cdot \frac{d}{dz} e^{yV(z)} \right) \\
&= z \left( xV'(z) e^{xV(z)} e^{yV(z)} + y V'(z) e^{xV(z)} e^{yV(z)} \right) \\
&= (x+y) z V'(z) e^{(x+y)V(z)}.
\end{aligned}
$$

### Step 5: Expand $z V'(z) e^{(x+y)V(z)}$

Notice that

$$
z V'(z) e^{(x+y)V(z)} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \sum_{k=1}^{n} \binom{n-1}{k-1} V_k(x) V_{n-k}(y) \frac{z^n}{n!}.
$$

This follows from standard combinatorial convolution of exponential generating functions with a derivative factor. Specifically, the combinatorial identity

$$
n \binom{n}{k} = k \binom{n-1}{k-1}
$$

gives the correct binomial coefficients.

### Step 6: Compare coefficients

Comparing the coefficient of $\frac{z^n}{n!}$ on both sides:

$$
n V_n(x+y) = (x+y) \sum_{k=1}^{n} \binom{n-1}{k-1} V_k(x) V_{n-k}(y).
$$

Multiplying both sides by $x/n$ gives exactly

$$
x V_n(x+y) = (x+y) \sum_{k=1}^{n} \binom{n-1}{k-1} V_k(x) V_{n-k}(y),
$$

as required.

### Step 7: Conclusion

The identity is proved by using the generating function of poweroids, differentiating, and comparing coefficients. The derivative of $e^{V(z)}$ allows the binomial convolution to appear naturally, yielding the combinatorial factors $\binom{n-1}{k-1}$.

$$
\boxed{xV_n(x+y) = (x+y)\sum_{k=1}^{n} \binom{n-1}{k-1} V_k(x)V_{n-k}(y).}
$$

This completes the proof.
