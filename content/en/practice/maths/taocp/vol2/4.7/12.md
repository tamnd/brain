**Exercise 12.** [*M20*] Find a connection between polynomial division and power series division: Given polynomials $u(z)$ and $v(z)$ of respective degrees $m$ and $n$ over a field, show how to find the polynomials $q(z)$ and $r(z)$ such that $u(z) = q(z)v(z) + r(z)$ and $\deg(r) < n$, using only operations on power series.

We are asked to connect **polynomial division** with **power series division**. Let us proceed carefully and fully.

Let $u(z)$ and $v(z)$ be polynomials over a field $F$, with $\deg(u) = m$ and $\deg(v) = n$, where $m \ge n$. The classical polynomial division theorem guarantees the existence of unique polynomials $q(z)$ and $r(z)$ such that

$$
u(z) = q(z)v(z) + r(z), \quad \deg(r) < n.
$$

The goal is to obtain $q(z)$ and $r(z)$ **using only operations on power series**.

## Step 1: Formulate the problem as a power series

Consider $v(z)$ with nonzero constant term $v_0 \neq 0$. If necessary, we can factor out powers of $z$ to ensure this; the argument is similar in general. Then the reciprocal $1/v(z)$ can be expanded as a **formal power series**:

$$
\frac{1}{v(z)} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} c_k z^k
$$

where the coefficients $c_k \in F$ are determined recursively by the usual formula for the inverse of a power series:

$$
c_0 = \frac{1}{v_0}, \quad \text{and for } k \ge 1, \quad c_k = -\frac{1}{v_0} \sum_{j=1}^{k} v_j c_{k-j}.
$$

This is purely a power series operation: it uses addition, multiplication, and scalar division.

## Step 2: Compute the quotient as a truncated power series

Now consider the formal power series

$$
q^*(z) = \frac{u(z)}{v(z)}.
$$

Because $u(z)$ and $v(z)$ are polynomials, the series terminates at degree $m - n$. That is, $q^*(z)$ is actually a **polynomial of degree $m - n$**. Explicitly, the series expansion

$$
q^*(z) = u(z) \cdot \frac{1}{v(z)} = \sum_{k=0}^{m-n} q_k z^k
$$

produces the quotient coefficients $q_k$. This computation uses only power series multiplication and truncation at the appropriate degree. All higher-order terms can be discarded.

## Step 3: Compute the remainder

Once $q(z)$ is obtained as the truncated series $q^*(z)$, the remainder is computed by subtraction:

$$
r(z) = u(z) - q(z)v(z).
$$

By construction, $\deg(r) < n$ because we truncated the series at the highest necessary degree $m-n$.

## Step 4: Summary of the algorithm using power series operations

1. Compute the formal inverse $1/v(z)$ as a power series up to degree $m-n$.
2. Multiply $u(z)$ by this inverse and truncate to degree $m-n$ to get $q(z)$.
3. Compute $r(z) = u(z) - q(z)v(z)$.

All steps use **only addition, multiplication, and scalar division in the field**, which are exactly the allowed power series operations. No classical polynomial division algorithm is required.

## Step 5: Verification

Let us check degrees:

- $q(z)$ has degree at most $m-n$, so $q(z)v(z)$ has degree at most $m$, matching $u(z)$.
- $r(z) = u(z) - q(z)v(z)$ is exactly the leftover terms of degree less than $n$.
- Uniqueness follows from the classical polynomial division theorem.

This demonstrates the connection: **polynomial division is equivalent to multiplying by the inverse of $v(z)$ in the ring of formal power series and truncating at the appropriate degree**.

This completes the solution.
