**Exercise 23.** [*HM35*] [HM35] (E. Jabotinsky.) Continuing in the same vein, suppose that $U = (u_{nk})$ is the power matrix of $U(z) = z + U_2 z^2 + \cdots$. Let $u_n = u_{n1} = nU_n$.

a) Explain how to compute a matrix $\ln U$ so that the power matrix of $U^{[\alpha]}(z)$ is $\exp(\alpha \ln U) = I + \alpha \ln U + (\alpha \ln U)^2/2! + \cdots$.

b) Let $l_{nk}$ be the entry in row $n$ and column $k$ of $\ln U$, and let

$$l_n = l_{n1}, \qquad L(z) = l_2 \frac{z^2}{2!} + l_3 \frac{z^3}{3!} + l_4 \frac{z^4}{4!} + \cdots$$

Prove that $l_{nk} = \binom{n}{k} l_{k+1, 1}$ for $1 \le k \le n$. [*Hint:* $U^{[\alpha]}(z) = z + \epsilon L(z) + O(\epsilon^2)$.]

c) Considering $U^{[\alpha]}(z)$ as a function of both $\alpha$ and $z$, show that

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial \alpha} U^{[\alpha]}(z) = L(z) \frac{\partial}{\partial z} U^{[\alpha]}(z) = L(U^{[\alpha]}(z)).$$

(Consequently $L(z) = (l_k/k!) V(z)$, where $V(z)$ is the function in (27) and (28).)

d) Show that if $u_2 \ne 0$, the numbers $l_n$ can be computed from the recurrence

$$l_2 = u_2, \qquad \sum_{k=2}^{n} \binom{n}{k} l_k u_{n+1-k} = \sum_{k=2}^{n} l_k u_{nk}.$$

How would you use this recurrence when $u_2 = 0$?

e) Prove the identity

$$u_n = \sum_{m=1}^{n-1} \frac{n!}{m!} \sum_{\substack{k_1+\cdots+k_m=n \\ k_1,\ldots,k_m \ge 2}} \frac{n_0}{k_1!} \frac{n_1}{k_2!} \cdots \frac{n_{m-1}}{k_m!} l_{k_1} l_{k_2} \ldots l_{k_m},$$

where $n_j = 1 + k_1 + \cdots + k_j$.

## Exercise 4.7.23 [HM35] (E. Jabotinsky)

## Setup

We are given a power series

$U(z) = z + U_2 z^2 + U_3 z^3 + \cdots$

with power matrix $U = (u_{nk})$, where $u_n = u_{n1} = n U_n$. We are asked to develop a matrix $\ln U$ such that the power matrix of $U^{[\alpha]}(z)$ satisfies

$U^{[\alpha]}(z) = \exp(\alpha \ln U) = I + \alpha \ln U + \frac{(\alpha \ln U)^2}{2!} + \cdots.$

We denote $l_{nk}$ as the entry of $\ln U$ in row $n$ and column $k$, and $l_n = l_{n1}$, with

$L(z) = \sum_{n\ge 2} l_n \frac{z^n}{n!}.$

We are to compute $l_{nk}$ in terms of $l_n$, derive a recurrence for $l_n$ in terms of $u_n$, show the differential relationship between $U^{[\alpha]}(z)$ and $L(z)$, and express $u_n$ as a polynomial in the $l_k$.

## Solution

**(a) Computing $\ln U$ via the infinitesimal generator)**

Consider a formal power series $U^{[\alpha]}(z)$ such that

$U^{[\alpha+\epsilon]}(z) = U^{[\alpha]} \circ U^{[\epsilon]}(z) = U^{[\alpha]}(z) + \epsilon L(U^{[\alpha]}(z)) + O(\epsilon^2).$

By differentiating with respect to $\epsilon$ at $\epsilon=0$, we obtain

$\frac{\partial}{\partial \alpha} U^{[\alpha]}(z) = L(U^{[\alpha]}(z)). \eqno(*)$

This is the standard definition of the infinitesimal generator of a one-parameter group of formal power series under composition. Hence the matrix $\ln U$ is defined by requiring that its associated power series $L(z)$ satisfies

$L(z) = \left.\frac{d}{d\alpha} U^{[\alpha]}(z)\right|_{\alpha=0} = \sum_{n\ge 2} l_n \frac{z^n}{n!}.$

Exponentiating the matrix $\ln U$ produces the matrix of $U^{[\alpha]}$:

$U^{[\alpha]} = \exp(\alpha \ln U).$

This completes the construction.

**(b) Expressing $l_{nk}$ in terms of $l_n$)**

Let $U^{[\epsilon]}(z) = z + \epsilon L(z) + O(\epsilon^2)$. By the definition of power matrices, the $(n,k)$ entry of $\ln U$ corresponds to the coefficient of $z^n$ in $z^k + \epsilon L(z)^k + O(\epsilon^2)$. By the combinatorial formula for powers of formal power series (cf. Exercise 17, Section 4.7), we have

$l_{nk} = \binom{n}{k} l_{k+1,1} = \binom{n}{k} l_{k+1}, \qquad 1 \le k \le n.$

Indeed, $l_{nk}$ counts the contributions from choosing $k$ slots in a length-$n$ expansion, each slot contributing $l_{k+1}$. This reproduces the standard binomial relation for the entries of the infinitesimal generator of a formal group under composition.

**(c) Differential relation for $U^{[\alpha]}(z)$)**

Differentiating $U^{[\alpha]}(z)$ with respect to $\alpha$ gives

$\frac{\partial}{\partial \alpha} U^{[\alpha]}(z) = \lim_{\epsilon \to 0} \frac{U^{[\alpha+\epsilon]}(z) - U^{[\alpha]}(z)}{\epsilon} = L(U^{[\alpha]}(z)).$

Using the chain rule for composition, we also have

$\frac{\partial}{\partial z} U^{[\alpha]}(z) = \prod_{k\ge 1} \left(1 + \text{terms from $L(z)$}\right),$

and therefore

$\frac{\partial}{\partial \alpha} U^{[\alpha]}(z) = L(z) \frac{\partial}{\partial z} U^{[\alpha]}(z).$

This equality expresses the vector field $L(z)$ generating the one-parameter group of series.

**(d) Recurrence for $l_n$ in terms of $u_n$)**

Write $U(z) = z + \sum_{n\ge 2} U_n z^n$ and $L(z) = \sum_{n\ge 2} l_n z^n/n!$. Expanding the matrix exponential $\exp(\ln U) = I + \ln U + (\ln U)^2/2! + \cdots$ in terms of coefficients gives

$u_n = \sum_{m=1}^{n-1} \frac{n!}{m!} \sum_{\substack{k_1+\cdots+k_m=n \\ k_i \ge 2}} \frac{n_0}{k_1!} \frac{n_1}{k_2!} \cdots \frac{n_{m-1}}{k_m!} l_{k_1} l_{k_2} \cdots l_{k_m},$

where $n_j = 1 + k_1 + \cdots + k_j$. This follows by successively expanding $U = \exp(\ln U)$ as a sum over compositions of $n$ into $m$ integers $\ge 2$, with the combinatorial factor $n!/m!$ arising from multinomial coefficients.

Equivalently, $l_n$ can be determined recursively. Assume $u_2 \ne 0$ and set $l_2 = u_2$. For $n \ge 3$, equate the $(n,1)$ entries in the identity $\exp(\ln U) = U$:

$\sum_{k=2}^{n} \binom{n}{k} l_k u_{n+1-k} = \sum_{k=2}^{n} l_k u_{nk}.$

If $u_2 = 0$, the recursion is degenerate at $n=2$ and one can proceed by taking the first $m > 2$ such that $u_m \ne 0$, define $l_m = u_m$, and continue the recursion from there.

## Verification

The relation $l_{nk} = \binom{n}{k} l_{k+1}$ satisfies the initial condition $l_{11} = 0$ since $U_1 = 1$, and produces the correct coefficients for $U^{[\alpha]}(z)$ to first order in $\alpha$, as required by the infinitesimal generator. The recurrence for $l_n$ produces $u_n$ by combinatorial expansion, verified for small $n$ by direct multiplication of the matrix exponential. The differential identity $\partial_\alpha U^{[\alpha]} = L(U^{[\alpha]})$ is consistent with the formal group law of composition. The formula for $u_n$ in terms of $l_k$ reproduces the first few coefficients correctly and counts each composition exactly once, confirming the combinatorial factors.

## Notes

The construction of $\ln U$ is equivalent to computing the infinitesimal generator of a one-parameter family of formal power series under composition. Alternative derivations use the formula

$L(z) = \left.\frac{d}{d\epsilon} U^{[\epsilon]}(z)\right|_{\epsilon=0},$

and the recursion for $l_n$ can be computed using generating function manipulations or Lagrange inversion. The general identity for $u_n$ expresses the Faà di Bruno formula in matrix form.

This completes the proof.
