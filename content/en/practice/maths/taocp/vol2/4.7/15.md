## Solution

We are asked:

> For what functions $U(z)$ does $U^{[n]}(z)$ have the simple form $z^k$ in (27)? What do you deduce about the answers to exercise 8?

### Step 1: General setup

Let $U(z)$ be a formal power series with $U(0)=0$:

$$
U(z) = \sum_{m=1}^{\infty} u_m z^m, \quad u_m \in \mathbb{C}, \ u_m \neq 0 \text{ for at least one } m.
$$

Let the **compositional powers** $U^{[n]}(z)$ be defined recursively by

$$
U^{[1]}(z) = U(z), \qquad U^{[n+1]}(z) = U(U^{[n]}(z)).
$$

We are asked to determine all series $U(z)$ such that for some $n \ge 1$:

$$
U^{[n]}(z) = z^k
$$

for some integer $k \ge 1$.

### Step 2: Monomial structure

Suppose $U^{[n]}(z)$ is a **single monomial**. That is,

$$
U^{[n]}(z) = c_n z^{k}, \quad c_n \neq 0.
$$

We claim that in order for a formal power series to have a compositional iterate that is a single monomial, the series itself must be a monomial:

$$
\boxed{U(z) = c z^m, \quad m \ge 1, \ c \neq 0}.
$$

**Justification:**

- Suppose $U(z)$ contains more than one term:

$$
U(z) = \sum_{i=1}^M c_i z^{m_i}, \quad 1 \le m_1 < m_2 < \dots < m_M, \ M \ge 2.
$$

- Consider $U^{[2]}(z) = U(U(z))$. Expanding the composition, the lowest exponent comes from $c_1 z^{m_1}$, but higher exponents appear as $c_i (c_1 z^{m_1})^{m_i} = c_i c_1^{m_i} z^{m_i m_1}$ and from cross-terms in the multinomial expansion. Therefore $U^{[2]}(z)$ has **at least two distinct exponents**, unless $M=1$.
- By induction, any higher iterate $U^{[n]}(z)$ will also contain at least two distinct exponents. Hence, the only possibility for a monomial iterate is that $U(z)$ itself is a monomial.

Thus we can assume

$$
U(z) = c z^m, \quad m \ge 1, \ c \neq 0.
$$

### Step 3: Iterates of a monomial

Let

$$
U(z) = c z^m.
$$

Then the iterates are:

1. For $n=1$, $U^{[1]}(z) = c z^m$.
2. For $n=2$, $U^{[2]}(z) = U(U(z)) = c (c z^m)^m = c^{1+m} z^{m^2}$.
3. By induction, the general formula is

$$
\boxed{
U^{[n]}(z) = c^{1+m+m^2+\dots + m^{n-1}} z^{m^n} = c^{(m^n-1)/(m-1)} z^{m^n}, \quad m \neq 1
}
$$

and if $m=1$,

$$
U^{[n]}(z) = c^n z.
$$

### Step 4: Condition for a monomial iterate

We require

$$
U^{[n]}(z) = z^k.
$$

Comparing the exponent and coefficient:

1. **Exponent condition:**

$$
m^n = k.
$$

1. **Coefficient condition:**

- If $m = 1$: $c^n = 1 \implies c$ is an $n$-th root of unity.
- If $m \neq 1$: $c^{(m^n-1)/(m-1)} = 1$.

Thus the complete characterization is:

$$
\begin{cases}
U(z) = c z^m, \quad m \ge 1, \ c \neq 0,\\[2mm]
\text{for some } n \ge 1, \quad m^n = k, \quad 
c^{(m^n-1)/(m-1)} = 1 \text{ if } m \neq 1, \quad c^n = 1 \text{ if } m=1.
\end{cases}
$$

This is the full family of solutions.

### Step 5: Deduction for Exercise 8

Exercise 8 concerns **series whose repeated composition produces a simple monomial**. From Step 4 we deduce:

- Only monomial series $U(z) = c z^m$ can produce a monomial under iteration.
- Any nontrivial series with multiple terms (or nonlinear terms with additive contributions) will never reduce to a single monomial under iteration.
- The possible coefficients $c$ must satisfy the root-of-unity condition above for exact equality.

Hence, the answer to exercise 8 is that **all series whose repeated composition reduces to a monomial are exactly the monomials** $c z^m$ with appropriate coefficient constraints.

### Step 6: Examples

1. $U(z) = z \implies U^{[n]}(z) = z$ (identity, any $n$, $k=1$).
2. $U(z) = -z \implies U^{[2]}(z) = z$ ($c^2 = 1$, $m=1$).
3. $U(z) = 2 z^3 \implies U^{[2]}(z) = 2^{1+3} z^9 = 16 z^9$. To get $U^{[2]}(z) = z^9$, need $c = 1$ (or $c^{4} = 1 \implies c = 1$).

### Step 7: Conclusion

**All formal power series $U(z)$ such that an iterate $U^{[n]}(z)$ is a single monomial $z^k$ are of the form**

$$
\boxed{
U(z) = c z^m, \quad m \ge 1, \ c \neq 0, \quad \text{with } m^n = k, \text{ and } c^{(m^n-1)/(m-1)} = 1 \text{ if } m \neq 1, \ c^n=1 \text{ if } m=1
}.
$$

This completely answers Exercise 4.7.15 and provides the correct foundation for Exercise 8.
