**Exercise 4.7.27 [_M22_] (D. Zeilberger)**

Find a recurrence analogous to (9) for computing the coefficients of

$$
W(z) = V(z)\,V(qz)\,V(q^2 z)\cdots V(q^{m-1} z),
$$

where

$$
V(z) = 1 + V_1 z + V_2 z^2 + \cdots,
$$

and assume that $q$ is not a root of unity.

## Solution

Let

$$
W(z) = \sum_{n \ge 0} W_n z^n.
$$

We want a recurrence for the coefficients $W_n$ in terms of the coefficients $V_k$ and powers of $q$.

### Step 1: Express $W(z)$ as a product

By definition,

$$
W(z) = \prod_{j=0}^{m-1} V(q^j z) = \prod_{j=0}^{m-1} \left(1 + \sum_{k \ge 1} V_k (q^j z)^k \right).
$$

Expanding each factor as a formal power series gives

$$
V(q^j z) = 1 + V_1 q^j z + V_2 q^{2j} z^2 + V_3 q^{3j} z^3 + \cdots
$$

for each $j = 0, 1, \dots, m-1$.

### Step 2: Write a general formula for $W_n$

The coefficient $W_n$ of $z^n$ in $W(z)$ is given by collecting all terms of total degree $n$ from the product. For each partition $(k_0, k_1, \dots, k_{m-1})$ of $n$ into non-negative integers, the contribution is

$$
\prod_{j=0}^{m-1} V_{k_j} q^{j k_j},
$$

with the convention $V_0 = 1$. Thus,

$$
W_n = \sum_{k_0 + \cdots + k_{m-1} = n} \prod_{j=0}^{m-1} V_{k_j} q^{j k_j}.
$$

This formula is explicit but involves an exponential number of terms, so we seek a **recurrence**.

### Step 3: Derive a recurrence

We adopt a method analogous to the convolution recurrence in TAOCP (see (9) in §4.7.2).

Write

$$
W(z) = V(z) \cdot \underbrace{V(q z) V(q^2 z) \cdots V(q^{m-1} z)}_{=: W^{(1)}(z)}.
$$

Let $W^{(1)}(z) = \sum_{n \ge 0} W^{(1)}_n z^n$. Then by the Cauchy product,

$$
W_n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} V_k \, W^{(1)}_{n-k} \, q^{(n-k) \cdot 0} = \sum_{k=0}^{n} V_k \, W^{(1)}_{n-k},
$$

because the first factor contributes no $q$-power.

Similarly, for $W^{(1)}(z) = \prod_{j=1}^{m-1} V(q^j z)$, shifting the powers of $q$ gives a general pattern:

$$
W_n^{(r)} = \sum_{k=0}^{n} V_k \, q^{r k} W_{n-k}^{(r+1)}, \quad 0 \le r \le m-1,
$$

with the convention $W_n^{(m)} = \delta_{n0}$ (the empty product).

### Step 4: Explicit recurrence

Setting $r = 0$ for the full product, we have

$$
\boxed{
W_n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} V_k \, q^{0 \cdot k} W_{n-k}^{(1)} = \sum_{k=0}^{n} V_k \, W_{n-k}^{(1)}, \quad n \ge 0,
}
$$

and recursively

$$
\boxed{
W_n^{(r)} = \sum_{k=0}^{n} V_k \, q^{r k} W_{n-k}^{(r+1)}, \quad 0 \le r \le m-1,
}
$$

with $W_0^{(m)} = 1$ and $W_n^{(m)} = 0$ for $n > 0$.

This provides a complete recurrence: for each $n$, compute $W_n^{(m-1)}, W_n^{(m-2)}, \dots, W_n^{(0)} = W_n$ in succession.

### Step 5: Verification

1. **Base case:** $n = 0$ gives $W_0 = 1$, consistent with the constant term of a product of series with constant term 1.
2. **Recursion consistency:** Each coefficient $W_n^{(r)}$ is expressed as a sum over previous coefficients $W_{n-k}^{(r+1)}$ weighted by $V_k q^{r k}$, which mirrors the standard convolution formula in (9).
3. **Termination:** Since $V_0 = 1$, the recurrence bottoms out at $r = m$, yielding $W_n^{(m)} = \delta_{n0}$.

This fully determines all coefficients $W_n$ for any $n \ge 0$.

### Step 6: Example (optional check)

Let $m = 2$, $V(z) = 1 + V_1 z + V_2 z^2$, then

$$
W(z) = V(z) V(q z) \quad \implies \quad W_0 = 1, \quad W_1 = V_1 + V_1 q = V_1 (1+q), \dots
$$

which agrees with the recurrence above:

$$
W_1 = V_0 W_1^{(1)} + V_1 W_0^{(1)} = 1 \cdot V_1 q^0 + V_1 \cdot 1 = V_1 (1+q).
$$

### Step 7: Conclusion

We have derived a complete, recursive formula for $W_n$:

$$
\begin{cases}
W_n^{(m)} = \delta_{n0},\\[2mm]
W_n^{(r)} = \displaystyle \sum_{k=0}^{n} V_k q^{r k} W_{n-k}^{(r+1)}, \quad 0 \le r \le m-1,\\[1mm]
W_n = W_n^{(0)}.
\end{cases}
$$

This recurrence is analogous to (9) in TAOCP, correctly accounts for the powers of $q$, and generates the coefficients of $W(z) = V(z) V(q z) \cdots V(q^{m-1} z)$ for any $n$.

**Answer (Recurrence for $W_n$):**

$$
\displaystyle W_n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} V_k \, q^{0\cdot k} W_{n-k}^{(1)}, \quad 
W_n^{(r)} = \sum_{k=0}^{n} V_k \, q^{r k} W_{n-k}^{(r+1)}, \quad
W_n^{(m)} = \delta_{n0}, \quad W_n = W_n^{(0)}.
$$

This completes the solution.
