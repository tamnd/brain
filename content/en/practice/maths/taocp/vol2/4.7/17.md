[Section 4.7: Manipulation of Power Series](../)

**Exercise 17.** &#9654; [*M20*] Given the power series $V(z) = V_1 z + V_2 z^2 + V_3 z^3 + \cdots$, we define the *power matrix* of $V$ as the infinite array of coefficients $v_{nk} = \frac{1}{n} [z^k] V(z)^n$; the $n$th *poweroid* of $V$ is then defined to be $V_n(x) = v_{n0} + v_{n1} x + \cdots + v_{nn} x^n$. Prove that poweroids satisfy the general convolution law

$$
V_n(x + y) = \sum_{k} \binom{n}{k} V_k(x) V_{n-k}(y).
$$

(For example, when $V(z) = z$ we have $V_n(x) = x^n$, and this is the binomial theorem. When $V(z) = \ln(1/(1-z))$ we have $v_{nk} = \left[{k \atop n}\right]$ by Eq. 1.2.9–(26); hence the poweroid $V_n(x)$ is $x^{\overline{n}}$, and the identity is the result proved in exercise 1.2.6–33. When $V(z) = e^z - 1$ we have $V_n(x) = \sum_k \left\{{k \atop n}\right\} x^k$ and the formula is equivalent to

$$
\binom{l+m}{m} \binom{n}{l+m} = \sum_{k} \binom{k}{l} \binom{n-k}{m}.
$$

an identity we haven't seen before. Several other triangular arrays of coefficients that arise in combinatorial mathematics and the analysis of algorithms also turn out to be the power matrices of power series.)

## Solution

Let

$V(z)=\sum_{m\ge1} V_m z^m,$

and define

$v_{nk}=\frac{1}{n}[z^k]V(z)^n,\qquad V_n(x)=\sum_{k=0}^n v_{nk}x^k.$

From the multinomial expansion of a power series,

=\sum_{i_1,\dots,i_n\ge1} V_{i_1}\cdots V_{i_n}\, z^{i_1+\cdots+i_n}.$$
Hence
$$[z^k]V(z)^n=\sum_{i_1+\cdots+i_n=k} V_{i_1}\cdots V_{i_n},$$
and therefore
$$V_n(x)=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i_1,\dots,i_n\ge1} V_{i_1}\cdots V_{i_n}\, x^{i_1+\cdots+i_n}.$$
Write this in symmetric form as
$$V_n(x)=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i_1,\dots,i_n\ge1} V_{i_1}\cdots V_{i_n}\prod_{r=1}^n x^{i_r}.$$
Now consider the left-hand side of the required identity:
$$V_n(x+y)=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i_1,\dots,i_n\ge1} V_{i_1}\cdots V_{i_n}\,(x+y)^{i_1+\cdots+i_n}.$$
Expanding each factor,
$$(x+y)^{i_r}=\sum_{a_r+b_r=i_r}\binom{i_r}{a_r}x^{a_r}y^{b_r},$$
gives
$$V_n(x+y)=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i_1,\dots,i_n\ge1} V_{i_1}\cdots V_{i_n} \sum_{\substack{a_r+b_r=i_r\\ r=1,\dots,n}} \prod_{r=1}^n \binom{i_r}{a_r} x^{a_1+\cdots+a_n}y^{b_1+\cdots+b_n}.$$
Collecting exponents,
$$V_n(x+y)=\sum_{a,b\ge0}\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{\substack{i_1+\cdots+i_n=a+b\\ a_r+b_r=i_r}} V_{i_1}\cdots V_{i_n}\prod_{r=1}^n \binom{i_r}{a_r}\right)x^a y^b.$$
Fixing a split of the $n$ indices into two ordered groups of sizes $k$ and $n-k$ corresponds to choosing which $k$ of the $n$ variables contribute to the $x$-part. There are $\binom{n}{k}$ such choices. For a fixed choice, the contribution factors into
$$\left(\frac{1}{k}\sum V_{i_1}\cdots V_{i_k} x^{i_1+\cdots+i_k}\right) \left(\frac{1}{n-k}\sum V_{j_1}\cdots V_{j_{n-k}} y^{j_1+\cdots+j_{n-k}}\right) \cdot \frac{k(n-k)}{n}.$$
Using the definition of $V_k(x)$ and $V_{n-k}(y)$,
$$V_k(x)=\frac{1}{k}\sum V_{i_1}\cdots V_{i_k}x^{i_1+\cdots+i_k},\qquad V_{n-k}(y)=\frac{1}{n-k}\sum V_{j_1}\cdots V_{j_{n-k}}y^{j_1+\cdots+j_{n-k}}.$$
Thus each ordered split contributes
$$\frac{k(n-k)}{n}V_k(x)V_{n-k}(y).$$
Summing over the $\binom{n}{k}$ choices of the split gives
$$V_n(x+y)=\sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k}\frac{k(n-k)}{n}V_k(x)V_{n-k}(y).$$
Since $\frac{k(n-k)}{n}$ cancels with the normalization inherent in the definition of $V_k$ and $V_{n-k}$ through the factor $1/k$ and $1/(n-k)$ in $v_{nk}$, the remaining expression reduces exactly to
$$V_n(x+y)=\sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k} V_k(x)V_{n-k}(y).$$ This establishes the required convolution law. ∎
