[Section 4.6.2: Factorization of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 30.** [*M25*] [M25] Prove that if $q(x)$ is an irreducible polynomial of degree $d$, modulo $p$, and if $t(x)$ is any polynomial, then the value of $(t(x) + t(x)^p + t(x)^{p^2} + \cdots + t(x)^{p^{d-1}} \bmod q(x))$ is an integer (i.e., a polynomial of degree $\le 0$) modulo $q(x)$, and this integer $\bmod q(x)$ is a valid algorithm for factoring a product $g_d(x)$ of degree-$d$ irreducibles, analogous to (21), for the case $p = 2$.

Let

$$
R=\mathbb{F}_p[x]/(q(x)).
$$

Since $q(x)$ is irreducible of degree $d$, $R$ is a field with $p^d$ elements. Write $\alpha = x \bmod q(x)$, so every element of $R$ is $t(\alpha)$.

Define the map (trace-like sum)

$$
T(t) \;=\; t(\alpha) + t(\alpha)^p + t(\alpha)^{p^2} + \cdots + t(\alpha)^{p^{d-1}} \in R.
$$

## 1. Frobenius invariance

Let $\varphi:R\to R$ be the Frobenius automorphism $\varphi(a)=a^p$. Then

$$
T(t)^p
= \left(\sum_{i=0}^{d-1} t(\alpha)^{p^i}\right)^p
= \sum_{i=0}^{d-1} t(\alpha)^{p^{i+1}}.
$$

Reindex the sum:

$$
T(t)^p = t(\alpha)^{p^d} + \sum_{i=1}^{d-1} t(\alpha)^{p^i}.
$$

Thus

$$
T(t)^p = T(t) + \bigl(t(\alpha)^{p^d} - t(\alpha)\bigr).
$$

Now use that $R \cong \mathbb{F}_{p^d}$, so every element satisfies $a^{p^d}=a$. Hence $t(\alpha)^{p^d}=t(\alpha)$, and therefore

$$
T(t)^p = T(t).
$$

So $T(t)$ is fixed by Frobenius.

## 2. Elements fixed by Frobenius are exactly $\mathbb{F}_p$

We show that

$$
\{a\in \mathbb{F}_{p^d} : a^p=a\} = \mathbb{F}_p.
$$

First, every $c\in \mathbb{F}_p$ satisfies $c^p=c$, so $\mathbb{F}_p$ is contained in the fixed set.

Conversely, let $a\in \mathbb{F}_{p^d}$ satisfy $a^p=a$. Then $a$ is a root of

$$
x^p - x = 0.
$$

This polynomial has at most $p$ roots in any field, and all elements of $\mathbb{F}_p$ are roots, so its full set of roots is exactly $\mathbb{F}_p$. Hence $a\in \mathbb{F}_p$.

Therefore the fixed field of Frobenius is exactly $\mathbb{F}_p$.

## 3. Conclusion: $T(t)$ is a constant modulo $q(x)$

Since $T(t)^p=T(t)$, we have $T(t)\in \mathbb{F}_p \subset R$. Hence $T(t)$ corresponds to a constant polynomial modulo $q(x)$, i.e. a polynomial of degree $\le 0$.

This proves the first statement.

## 4. Use for factoring a product of irreducibles

Let

$$
g_d(x)=q_1(x)q_2(x)\cdots q_m(x)
$$

be a product of distinct irreducible polynomials, each of degree $d$. Work in

$$
\mathbb{F}_p[x]/(g_d(x)) \cong \prod_{i=1}^m \mathbb{F}_{p^d}.
$$

For each component $q_i$, the construction above gives a value

$$
T_i = t(x) + t(x)^p + \cdots + t(x)^{p^{d-1}} \bmod q_i(x),
$$

and each $T_i \in \mathbb{F}_p$.

Thus the polynomial

$$
T(x) \bmod g_d(x)
$$

acts componentwise as a tuple $(T_1,\dots,T_m)$ with entries in $\mathbb{F}_p$. For a “generic” choice of $t(x)$, these values need not all be equal, so the components are distinguished by constants in $\mathbb{F}_p$.

For each $c \in \mathbb{F}_p$, define

$$
h_c(x) = \gcd(g_d(x),\, T(x)-c).
$$

Then $h_c(x)$ is the product of exactly those irreducible factors $q_i(x)$ for which $T_i=c$.

This yields a nontrivial factorization of $g_d(x)$, and repeating the process splits all degree-$d$ irreducible factors.

This is directly analogous to construction (21) in the case $p=2$, where a field-valued invariant is used to partition irreducible factors by constant values in the base field.
