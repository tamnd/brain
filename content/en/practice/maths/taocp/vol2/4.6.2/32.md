[Section 4.6.2: Factorization of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 32.** &#9654; [*M30*] [M30] (*Cyclotomic polynomials.*) Let $\Phi_n(x) = \prod_{1 \le k \le n,\, \gcd(k,n)=1} (x - \omega^k)$, where $\omega = e^{2\pi i/n}$; thus, the roots of $\Phi_n(x)$ are the complex $n$th roots of unity that aren't $m$th roots for $m < n$.

a) Prove that $\Phi_n(x)$ is a polynomial with integer coefficients, and that

$$x^n - 1 = \prod_{d \mid n} \Phi_d(x); \qquad \Phi_n(x) = \prod_{d \mid n} (x^d - 1)^{\mu(n/d)}.$$

(See exercises 4.5.2–10(b) and 4.5.3–28(c).)

b) Prove that $\Phi_n(x)$ is irreducible over the integers, hence the formula above is the complete factorization of $x^n - 1$ over the integers. [*Hint:* If $f(x)$ is an irreducible factor of $\Phi_n(x)$ over the integers, and if $\zeta$ is a root of $f(\zeta) = 0$, prove that $f(\zeta^p) = 0$ for all primes $p$ not dividing $n$. It may help to use the fact that $x^n - 1$ is squarefree modulo $p$ for all such primes.]

c) Discuss the calculation of $\Phi_n(x)$, and tabulate the values for $n \le 15$.

## Setup

For each positive integer $n$, let

$$
\Phi_n(x)=\prod_{\substack{1\le k\le n\\ \gcd(k,n)=1}}(x-\omega^k),
\qquad
\omega=e^{2\pi i/n}.
$$

The roots of $\Phi_n(x)$ are precisely the primitive $n$th roots of unity.

Part (a) asks for the factorization of $x^n-1$ into cyclotomic factors and the Möbius inversion formula for $\Phi_n(x)$.

Part (b) asks for a proof that $\Phi_n(x)$ is irreducible over $\mathbf Z$.

Part (c) asks for a discussion of computation and a table of values for $n\le15$.

## Solution

### (a) Factorization formulas

Every root of $x^n-1$ is an $n$th root of unity. If $\zeta$ is such a root and has order $d$, then $d\mid n$ and $\zeta$ is a primitive $d$th root of unity.

Hence the set of roots of $x^n-1$ is the disjoint union of the primitive $d$th roots, for all divisors $d$ of $n$.

Since each primitive $d$th root occurs exactly once in

$$
\Phi_d(x)
=
\prod_{\substack{1\le k\le d\\ \gcd(k,d)=1}}
(x-\zeta_d^k),
$$

the factorization into linear factors gives

$$
x^n-1
=
\prod_{d\mid n}\Phi_d(x).
$$

The factors are monic, therefore equality holds as polynomials.

To prove that $\Phi_n(x)$ has integer coefficients, proceed by induction on $n$.

The relation above gives

$$
\Phi_n(x)
=
\frac{x^n-1}{\prod_{\substack{d\mid n\\ d<n}}\Phi_d(x)}.
$$

By induction, all factors in the denominator belong to $\mathbf Z[x]$. Their product divides $x^n-1$ in $\mathbf Q[x]$. Since both numerator and denominator are monic polynomials in $\mathbf Z[x]$, Gauss's lemma implies that the quotient belongs to $\mathbf Z[x]$.

Now apply Möbius inversion to

$$
x^n-1=\prod_{d\mid n}\Phi_d(x).
$$

Taking logarithms formally in the multiplicative divisor lattice yields

$$
\Phi_n(x)
=
\prod_{d\mid n}(x^d-1)^{\mu(n/d)}.
$$

This is exactly the polynomial analogue of the inversion formula used in exercises 4.5.2-10(b) and 4.5.3-28(c).

Thus

$$
\boxed{
x^n-1=\prod_{d\mid n}\Phi_d(x),
\qquad
\Phi_n(x)=\prod_{d\mid n}(x^d-1)^{\mu(n/d)}.
}
$$

### (b) Irreducibility of $\Phi_n(x)$

Let

$$
\Phi_n(x)=f(x)g(x),
$$

where $f(x)\in\mathbf Z[x]$ is irreducible.

Choose a root $\zeta$ of $f$, so $\zeta$ is a primitive $n$th root of unity.

It suffices to prove that every primitive $n$th root of unity is also a root of $f$, because then $f$ and $\Phi_n$ have the same set of roots.

Let $p$ be a prime not dividing $n$.

The hint asks us to prove that

$$
f(\zeta^p)=0.
$$

Assume the contrary.

Then $\zeta^p$ is a root of $g(x)$.

Reduce coefficients modulo $p$.

Since $p\nmid n$,

$$
(x^n-1)'=nx^{n-1}
$$

is nonzero modulo $p$, so $x^n-1$ is squarefree modulo $p$.

Hence the reductions of $f$ and $g$ remain relatively prime modulo $p$.

Consider

$$
g(x^p).
$$

Since $\zeta^p$ is a root of $g$,

$$
g(\zeta^p)=0.
$$

Therefore $\zeta$ is a root of $g(x^p)$.

Modulo $p$,

$$
g(x^p)\equiv g(x)^p,
$$

by identity (5) from Section 4.6.2.

Hence the reduction of $f$ modulo $p$ divides the reduction of $g(x)^p$.

Because the reduction of $f$ is irreducible modulo $p$, it divides the reduction of $g$.

This contradicts the fact that the reductions of $f$ and $g$ are relatively prime.

Therefore

$$
f(\zeta^p)=0
$$

for every prime $p\nmid n$.

The Galois group of the cyclotomic extension acts by

$$
\zeta\mapsto\zeta^a,
\qquad
\gcd(a,n)=1.
$$

Every such $a$ is a product of primes not dividing $n$, modulo $n$. Repeated application of the preceding result shows that

$$
f(\zeta^a)=0
\qquad
(\gcd(a,n)=1).
$$

The roots $\zeta^a$ with $\gcd(a,n)=1$ are exactly the primitive $n$th roots of unity. Their number is $\varphi(n)$.

Thus $f$ has at least $\varphi(n)$ distinct roots. Since

$$
\deg\Phi_n=\varphi(n),
$$

$f$ must coincide with $\Phi_n$.

Hence $\Phi_n(x)$ is irreducible over $\mathbf Z$.

The factorization

$$
x^n-1=\prod_{d\mid n}\Phi_d(x)
$$

is therefore the complete factorization over the integers.

This completes the proof.

∎

### (c) Calculation and table for $n\le15$

The most convenient method is the recursion

$$
\Phi_n(x)
=
\frac{x^n-1}
{\prod_{\substack{d\mid n\\ d<n}}\Phi_d(x)},
$$

evaluating $\Phi_1,\Phi_2,\ldots$ successively.

The values for $n\le15$ are:

$$
\Phi_1(x)=x-1,
$$

$$
\Phi_2(x)=x+1,
$$

$$
\Phi_3(x)=x^2+x+1,
$$

$$
\Phi_4(x)=x^2+1,
$$

$$
\Phi_5(x)=x^4+x^3+x^2+x+1,
$$

$$
\Phi_6(x)=x^2-x+1,
$$

$$
\Phi_7(x)=x^6+x^5+x^4+x^3+x^2+x+1,
$$

$$
\Phi_8(x)=x^4+1,
$$

$$
\Phi_9(x)=x^6+x^3+1,
$$

$$
\Phi_{10}(x)=x^4-x^3+x^2-x+1,
$$

$$
\Phi_{11}(x)
=
x^{10}+x^9+x^8+x^7+x^6+x^5+x^4+x^3+x^2+x+1,
$$

$$
\Phi_{12}(x)=x^4-x^2+1,
$$

$$
\Phi_{13}(x)
=
x^{12}+x^{11}+x^{10}+x^9+x^8+x^7+x^6+x^5+x^4+x^3+x^2+x+1,
$$

$$
\Phi_{14}(x)
=
x^6-x^5+x^4-x^3+x^2-x+1,
$$

$$
\Phi_{15}(x)
=
x^8-x^7+x^5-x^4+x^3-x+1.
$$

Hence

$$
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
&\Phi_1=x-1,\quad
\Phi_2=x+1,\quad
\Phi_3=x^2+x+1,\quad
\Phi_4=x^2+1,\\
&\Phi_5=x^4+x^3+x^2+x+1,\quad
\Phi_6=x^2-x+1,\quad
\Phi_7=x^6+x^5+x^4+x^3+x^2+x+1,\\
&\Phi_8=x^4+1,\quad
\Phi_9=x^6+x^3+1,\quad
\Phi_{10}=x^4-x^3+x^2-x+1,\\
&\Phi_{11}=x^{10}+x^9+\cdots+x+1,\quad
\Phi_{12}=x^4-x^2+1,\\
&\Phi_{13}=x^{12}+x^{11}+\cdots+x+1,\quad
\Phi_{14}=x^6-x^5+x^4-x^3+x^2-x+1,\\
&\Phi_{15}=x^8-x^7+x^5-x^4+x^3-x+1.
\end{aligned}
}
$$

## Verification

The degrees agree with Euler's totient function:

$$
\deg\Phi_n=\varphi(n).
$$

For example,

$$
\varphi(15)=15\left(1-\frac13\right)\left(1-\frac15\right)=8,
$$

and $\Phi_{15}(x)$ has degree $8$.

Multiplying the cyclotomic factors for the divisors of $12$,

$$
\Phi_1\Phi_2\Phi_3\Phi_4\Phi_6\Phi_{12}
=
x^{12}-1,
$$

which confirms the divisor product formula in a representative case.

## Notes

The Möbius inversion formula frequently gives the fastest theoretical derivation of explicit cyclotomic polynomials. For computational purposes, the recursive quotient

$$
\Phi_n(x)
=
\frac{x^n-1}
{\prod_{d\mid n,\ d<n}\Phi_d(x)}
$$

is usually preferable, since all previously computed cyclotomic polynomials can be reused.
