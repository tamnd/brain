[Section 4.6.2: Factorization of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 33.** [*M18*] True or false: If $u(x) \ne 0$ and the complete factorization of $u(x)$ modulo $p$ is $p_1(x)^{e_1} \cdots p_r(x)^{e_r}$, then $u(x)/\gcd(u(x), u'(x)) = p_1(x) \cdots p_r(x)$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 7m09s  

---

The statement is **false in general**.

Let

$$
u(x)=\prod_{i=1}^r p_i(x)^{e_i}
$$

be the complete factorization of $u(x)$ over $\mathbf F_p$, where the $p_i$ are distinct irreducible polynomials.

We analyze

$$
\frac{u(x)}{\gcd(u(x),u'(x))}.
$$

Since the factors $p_i$ are pairwise relatively prime,

$$
u'(x)
=\sum_{i=1}^r e_i p_i(x)^{e_i-1}p_i'(x)
   \prod_{j\ne i} p_j(x)^{e_j}.
$$

Hence the exponent of $p_i$ in $\gcd(u,u')$ depends on whether $p_i'(x)$ vanishes.

### Case 1: $p_i'(x)\neq0$

Then $p_i$ is relatively prime to $p_i'$, so the $i$-th term of $u'$ contains exactly $p_i^{e_i-1}$, while every other term contains $p_i^{e_i}$. Therefore

$$
v_{p_i}(u')=e_i-1,
$$

and consequently

$$
v_{p_i}\!\bigl(\gcd(u,u')\bigr)=e_i-1.
$$

The exponent of $p_i$ in

$$
\frac{u}{\gcd(u,u')}
$$

is then $1$.

### Case 2: $p_i'(x)=0$

In characteristic $p$, this means that $p_i(x)$ is a polynomial in $x^p$. Then every term of $u'$ contains $p_i^{e_i}$, so

$$
v_{p_i}(u')\ge e_i,
$$

hence

$$
v_{p_i}\!\bigl(\gcd(u,u')\bigr)=e_i.
$$

The exponent of $p_i$ in $u/\gcd(u,u')$ is therefore $0$.

Thus

$$
\frac{u}{\gcd(u,u')}
=
\prod_{p_i'\ne0} p_i(x),
$$

not necessarily $\prod_i p_i(x)$.

### Counterexample

Over $\mathbf F_2$, let

$$
u(x)=(x+1)^2=x^2+1.
$$

Then

$$
u'(x)=2(x+1)=0
$$

in characteristic $2$. Hence

$$
\gcd(u,u')=u,
$$

and therefore

$$
\frac{u}{\gcd(u,u')}=1.
$$

But the product of the distinct irreducible factors of $u$ is

$$
x+1.
$$

Since

$$
1\neq x+1,
$$

the stated identity fails.

Therefore the proposition is **false**.

In fact, the correct formula is

$$
\boxed{\frac{u(x)}{\gcd(u(x),u'(x))}
      =\prod_{p_i'(x)\ne0} p_i(x).}
$$

When every irreducible factor satisfies $p_i'(x)\ne0$ (the usual squarefree situation), this reduces to the product of the distinct factors.
