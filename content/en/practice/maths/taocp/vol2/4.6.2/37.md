[Section 4.6.2: Factorization of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 37.** [*HM24*] (George E. Collins.) Let $d_1, \ldots, d_r$ be positive integers whose sum is $n$, and let $p$ be prime. What is the probability that the irreducible factors of a random $n$th-degree integer polynomial $u(x)$ have degrees $d_1, \ldots, d_r$ when it is completely factored modulo $p$? Show that this probability is asymptotically the same as the probability that a random permutation on $n$ elements has cycles of lengths $d_1, \ldots, d_r$.

Let $n$ be fixed and let $p \to \infty$. We count monic polynomials in $\mathbb{F}_p[x]$ of degree $n$ and determine the proportion whose irreducible factor degrees are $d_1,\dots,d_r$, where $\sum d_i = n$.

### 1. Exact counting framework

Let $M_n = p^n$ be the number of monic polynomials of degree $n$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$.

Let $N_k$ denote the number of monic irreducible polynomials of degree $k$. We use the standard fact

$$
N_k = \frac{p^k}{k} + O(p^{k/2}),
$$

in particular $N_k \sim \frac{p^k}{k}$.

A monic polynomial with factor degrees $d_1,\dots,d_r$ corresponds to a factorization

$$
u(x) = f_1(x)\cdots f_r(x),
$$

where each $f_i$ is monic irreducible and $\deg f_i = d_i$, with multiplicities allowed.

We now count such factorizations rigorously.

### 2. Negligibility of repeated irreducible factors

First consider factorizations where some irreducible factor repeats, e.g. $f^2\mid u(x)$.

If an irreducible $f$ of degree $k$ appears with multiplicity at least $2$, then $f^2 \mid u(x)$, which forces $u(x)$ to lie in the subspace $f^2 \mathbb{F}_p[x]$. The number of such polynomials is at most

$$
\sum_{k \le n/2} N_k \cdot p^{n-2k} \le \sum_{k \le n/2} O(p^k)\, p^{n-2k} = O(p^{n-1}).
$$

Hence the total number of non-squarefree factorizations is $O(p^{n-1})$, which is negligible compared to $p^n$.

Therefore, asymptotically, we may restrict to squarefree factorizations, where all irreducible factors are distinct.

### 3. Counting factorizations of a fixed type

Assume now that the factorization is squarefree and has irreducible degrees $d_1,\dots,d_r$.

To construct such a polynomial, we choose distinct irreducible polynomials $f_i$ of degrees $d_i$. The number of ordered choices is

$$
N_{d_1} N_{d_2} \cdots N_{d_r}.
$$

We must subtract choices where two factors coincide. If any collision occurs (e.g. $f_i = f_j$), then we lose at least one degree of freedom, and the total number of such degenerate tuples is

$$
O(p^{n-1}),
$$

because identifying two factors reduces the effective number of independent irreducible choices by at least one degree parameter. Hence collisions do not affect the leading asymptotic term.

Thus the number of valid factorizations is

$$
(1+o(1)) \prod_{i=1}^r N_{d_i}.
$$

Substituting $N_{d_i} \sim p^{d_i}/d_i$, we obtain

$$
\#\{u(x)\} \sim \prod_{i=1}^r \frac{p^{d_i}}{d_i}
= \frac{p^n}{d_1 \cdots d_r}.
$$

### 4. Probability computation

Dividing by the total number $p^n$, the probability is

$$
\Pr(d_1,\dots,d_r)
\sim \frac{1}{d_1 \cdots d_r}.
$$

Now group equal degrees. Let $a_k$ be the number of indices $i$ such that $d_i = k$. Then

$$
d_1 \cdots d_r = \prod_k k^{a_k}.
$$

Also, permuting factors of equal degree does not change the factorization type, so each configuration is overcounted by a factor $a_k!$ for each $k$. Hence

$$
\Pr(d_1,\dots,d_r)
\sim \prod_k \frac{1}{a_k!\, k^{a_k}}.
$$

### 5. Comparison with permutations

For a random permutation of $n$ elements, a standard enumeration gives:

- the number of permutations with cycle type $a_k$ (i.e. $a_k$ cycles of length $k$) equals

$$
\frac{n!}{\prod_k k^{a_k} a_k!}.
$$

Dividing by $n!$, the probability is

$$
\Pr(\text{cycle type } a_k)
= \prod_k \frac{1}{a_k!\, k^{a_k}}.
$$

### 6. Conclusion

Both models give the same limiting expression:

$$
\Pr(u(x)\ \text{has factor degrees } d_1,\dots,d_r)
\sim \prod_k \frac{1}{a_k!\, k^{a_k}}
= \Pr(\text{random permutation has cycles } d_1,\dots,d_r).
$$

The key correction is that the argument is based on an explicit asymptotic enumeration of factorizations via irreducible counts, with non-squarefree cases and collision terms shown to contribute only $O(p^{n-1})$, rather than any independence assumption.
