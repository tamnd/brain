[Section 4.6.2: Factorization of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 18.** &#9654; [*M25*] Let $u(x) = u_n x^n + \cdots + u_0$, $u_n \ne 0$, be a primitive polynomial with integer coefficients, and let $v(x)$ be the monic polynomial defined by

$$v(x) = u_n^{-1} \cdot u(x/u_n) = x^n + u_{n-1} x^{n-1} + u_{n-2} u_n x^{n-2} + \cdots + u_{n-2} u_n^{n-2} x + \cdots + u_0 u_n^{n-1}.$$

(a) Given that each $p_i(x)$ is monic, what is the complete factorization of $u(x)$ over the integers?  
(b) If $u(x) = x^n + u_{n-1} x^{n-1} + \cdots + u_0$ is a factor of $v(x)$, prove that $u_0$ is a multiple of $u^{n-1-k}$ for $0 \le k \le m$.

## Solution

Let $u(x) = u_n x^n + \cdots + u_0$ be a primitive polynomial with integer coefficients, and let

$v(x) = u_n^{-1} \cdot u(x / u_n) = x^n + u_{n-1} u_n^{-1} x^{n-1} + u_{n-2} u_n^{-2} x^{n-2} + \cdots + u_0 u_n^{-n}.$

(a) Since $u(x)$ is primitive, its content is 1; that is, $\gcd(u_0, u_1, \ldots, u_n) = 1$. Let

$u(x) = \prod_{i=1}^r p_i(x)$

be the factorization of $u(x)$ over $\mathbb{Z}$, where each $p_i(x)$ is monic and irreducible. Then by definition, $v(x)$ is obtained from $u(x)$ by the linear change of variable $x \mapsto x/u_n$ and scaling by $u_n^{-1}$. Each factor $p_i(x)$ is mapped to a monic polynomial

$q_i(x) = u_n^{-1} \, p_i(x / u_n),$

so that

$v(x) = \prod_{i=1}^r q_i(x), \quad \text{each $q_i(x)$ monic over $\mathbb{Q}$}.$

Since $u(x)$ is primitive, Gauss's lemma implies that every factorization over $\mathbb{Q}$ corresponds to a factorization over $\mathbb{Z}$ up to multiplication by units. Hence the complete factorization of $u(x)$ over the integers is

$\boxed{u(x) = \prod_{i=1}^r u_n^{\deg(p_i)} q_i(u_n x) = \prod_{i=1}^r p_i(x),}$

with each $p_i(x)$ monic.

(b) Let

$u(x) = x^n + u_{n-1} x^{n-1} + \cdots + u_0$

be a factor of $v(x)$. Write

$v(x) = u(x) \, w(x), \quad w(x) = x^m + w_{m-1} x^{m-1} + \cdots + w_0, \quad \deg(w) = m.$

Then the constant term of $v(x)$ satisfies

$v_0 = u_0 w_0.$

On the other hand, by the definition of $v(x)$, the constant term is

$v_0 = u_0 u_n^{-n}.$

Since $u(x)$ divides $v(x)$ over $\mathbb{Z}$, the product $u_0 w_0$ must equal $v_0$ in $\mathbb{Z}$; therefore

$u_0 w_0 = u_0 u_n^{-n}, \quad \text{so that } w_0 = u_n^{-n}.$

But $w_0$ is an integer because $v(x)$ and $u(x)$ have integer coefficients. Hence $u_n^n$ divides $u_0$, and more generally, considering the factor of $x^k$ in $u(x)$, we have

$u_0 = \text{multiple of } u_n^{n-1-k}, \quad 0 \le k \le m,$

since each coefficient $u_k$ of $x^k$ in $u(x)$ is multiplied by $u_n^{-k}$ in $v(x)$, and $v(x)$ has integer coefficients. This shows that the constant term of $u(x)$ must be divisible by the appropriate power of $u_n$ depending on the degree of the remaining factor $w(x)$. This completes the proof.
