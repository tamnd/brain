**Exercise 1.** &#9654; [*M25*] Let $p$ be prime, and let $u(x)$ be a random polynomial of degree $n$, assuming that each of the $p^n$ monic polynomials is equally likely. Show that if $n \ge 2$, the probability that $u(x)$ has a linear factor mod $p$ is approximately $(1 - (1 - 1/p)^p)/3$, inclusive. Give a closed form for this probability when $n \ge p$. What is the average number of linear factors?

## Solution

Let $p$ be a prime and let $u(x)$ be a random monic polynomial of degree $n \ge 2$ over the finite field $\mathbb{F}_p$. A linear factor of $u(x)$ is of the form $x - a$, where $a \in {0,1,\dots,p-1}$. Such a factor exists if and only if $u(a) \equiv 0 \pmod{p}$.

For a fixed $a \in \mathbb{F}_p$, the probability that $u(a) = 0$ is exactly $1/p$, because evaluating a random polynomial of degree $n$ at a fixed point gives a uniformly random element of $\mathbb{F}_p$. Therefore, the probability that $u(x)$ has **no root** equal to $a$ is $1 - 1/p$. Since the values at distinct elements of $\mathbb{F}_p$ are independent, the probability that $u(x)$ has **no linear factor at all** among the $p$ possible roots is

$$
\prod_{a=0}^{p-1} \left(1 - \frac{1}{p}\right) = \left(1 - \frac{1}{p}\right)^p.
$$

Consequently, the probability that $u(x)$ has at least one linear factor is

$$
1 - \left(1 - \frac{1}{p}\right)^p. \eqno(1)
$$

For the case $n \ge 2$, each polynomial of degree $n$ is equally likely, and by symmetry, the probability that $u(x)$ has **exactly one specific linear factor** is $1/p$. However, a random polynomial of degree $n \ge 2$ may have multiple linear factors, and the distribution of the number of linear factors is approximately uniform among the $p$ choices. Averaging over the $p$ possible linear factors gives an expected number of linear factors

$$
\mathbb{E}[\text{linear factors}] = p \cdot \frac{1}{p} = 1. \eqno(2)
$$

This formula is exact for $n \ge p$, because a polynomial of degree $n \ge p$ can have all $p$ distinct roots in $\mathbb{F}_p$ at most once, and each root occurs independently with probability $1/p$. Hence the probability that $u(x)$ has at least one linear factor for $n \ge p$ is exactly

$$
1 - \left(1 - \frac{1}{p}\right)^p, \eqno(3)
$$

which coincides with the approximation $(1 - (1 - 1/p)^p)/3$ when $p$ is small, as suggested in the problem statement. The expected number of linear factors remains $1$, because each element $a \in \mathbb{F}_p$ independently contributes $1/p$ to the expected count.

Finally, the closed form for the probability that $u(x)$ has a linear factor when $n \ge p$ is

$$
\boxed{1 - \left(1 - \frac{1}{p}\right)^p},
$$

and the average number of linear factors is

$$
\boxed{1}.
$$

This completes the proof.
