[Section 4.6.2: Factorization of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 27.** [*HM30*] [HM30] Prove that a random primitive polynomial over the integers is "almost always" irreducible, in some appropriate sense.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 13m33s  

---

## Setup

Let

$$
f_n(x)=a_nx^n+a_{n-1}x^{n-1}+\cdots+a_0
$$

be a primitive polynomial of degree $n$ with integer coefficients, and let

$$
H(f_n)=\max_{0\le j\le n}|a_j|
$$

denote its height.

The problem asks for a precise sense in which a random primitive polynomial is almost always irreducible. A natural formulation is:

Let

$$
P_n(N)=\#\{f_n:\ H(f_n)\le N,\ f_n\ \text{primitive}\},
$$

and let

$$
R_n(N)=\#\{f_n:\ H(f_n)\le N,\ f_n\ \text{primitive and reducible}\}.
$$

Prove that

$$
\lim_{N\to\infty}\frac{R_n(N)}{P_n(N)}=0.
$$

Equivalently, the probability that a primitive polynomial of degree $n$ and height at most $N$ is irreducible tends to $1$ as $N\to\infty$.

## Solution

Fix $n\ge1$.

The number of integer polynomials of degree exactly $n$ whose coefficients satisfy $|a_j|\le N$ is

$$
(2N+1)^n(2N),
$$

since the leading coefficient may be any nonzero integer in the interval $[-N,N]$.

Among these, a positive proportion are primitive. Indeed, the probability that $n+1$ integers are relatively prime tends to

$$
\frac1{\zeta(n+1)},
$$

hence

$$
P_n(N)\asymp N^{\,n+1}.
$$

Therefore it suffices to prove that

$$
R_n(N)=O(N^n).
$$

Consider a reducible primitive polynomial

$$
f_n(x)=g(x)h(x),
$$

where

$$
\deg g=r,\qquad \deg h=s,\qquad r+s=n,
$$

with $1\le r,s\le n-1$.

By Gauss's lemma, since $f_n$ is primitive, both $g$ and $h$ may be taken primitive.

Fix a decomposition $n=r+s$.

Write

$$
g(x)=b_rx^r+\cdots+b_0,
\qquad
h(x)=c_sx^s+\cdots+c_0.
$$

Since

$$
a_n=b_rc_s,
$$

and $|a_n|\le N$, there are at most

$$
O(N\log N)
$$

possible pairs $(b_r,c_s)$, because the number of factorizations of an integer $m$ is $d(m)$ and

$$
\sum_{|m|\le N}d(|m|)=O(N\log N).
$$

Similarly,

$$
a_0=b_0c_0,
$$

so there are at most

$$
O(N\log N)
$$

possible pairs $(b_0,c_0)$.

Once the four coefficients

$$
b_r,\ c_s,\ b_0,\ c_0
$$

are fixed, the remaining coefficients of $g$ and $h$ determine a unique product. There are

$$
r-1+s-1=n-2
$$

remaining free coefficients. Each lies in an interval of length $O(N)$, hence the number of possibilities is

$$
O(N^{\,n-2}).
$$

Therefore the number of reducible primitive polynomials corresponding to the fixed decomposition $n=r+s$ is at most

$$
O(N\log N)\cdot O(N\log N)\cdot O(N^{\,n-2})
=
O(N^n(\log N)^2).
$$

A sharper counting argument due to van der Waerden shows that the divisor-function factors can be absorbed, yielding

$$
O(N^n)
$$

reducible polynomials for each decomposition $n=r+s$.

Since there are only finitely many decompositions,

$$
R_n(N)=O(N^n).
$$

Consequently,

$$
\frac{R_n(N)}{P_n(N)}
=
O\!\left(\frac{N^n}{N^{\,n+1}}\right)
=
O\!\left(\frac1N\right),
$$

and therefore

$$
\lim_{N\to\infty}\frac{R_n(N)}{P_n(N)}=0.
$$

Hence the proportion of primitive polynomials of degree $n$ that are reducible tends to zero as the height bound tends to infinity.

Thus a random primitive polynomial is almost always irreducible.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification

The total number of primitive degree-$n$ polynomials of height at most $N$ grows on the order of $N^{n+1}$.

The set of reducible primitive polynomials is contained in a union over the finitely many splittings $n=r+s$. For each splitting, reducibility imposes one algebraic relation between the coefficients, reducing the order of growth by one power of $N$. Hence the reducible set has size $O(N^n)$.

Therefore

$$
\frac{\#\{\text{reducible primitive polynomials}\}}
{\#\{\text{primitive polynomials}\}}
\to0,
$$

which is exactly the required notion of "almost always."

## Notes

More precise results are known. For fixed degree $n$, the number of reducible integer polynomials of height at most $N$ is asymptotic to a constant multiple of $N^n$, while the total number of primitive polynomials is asymptotic to

$$
\frac{2^{n+1}}{\zeta(n+1)}\,N^{n+1}.
$$

Hence the probability of reducibility is actually of order $1/N$.
