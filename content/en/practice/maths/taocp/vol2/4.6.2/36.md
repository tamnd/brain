## Correctness

The solution addresses the exact question by formalizing the notion of "almost always" as the limit of the proportion of reducible primitive polynomials among all primitive polynomials of bounded height. It then correctly estimates the total number of primitive polynomials, $P_n(N)\asymp N^{,n+1}$, and bounds the number of reducible ones, $R_n(N)=O(N^n)$. The argument uses Gauss's lemma to justify that factors of a primitive polynomial can also be taken primitive, and it counts possibilities for the leading and constant coefficients using standard divisor-function bounds. The conclusion $\lim_{N\to\infty} R_n(N)/P_n(N)=0$ follows rigorously.

## Gaps and Errors

1. The estimation of the number of possibilities for the remaining coefficients as $O(N^{,n-2})$ assumes that once the leading and constant coefficients are fixed, all remaining coefficients are independently bounded by $N$ and yield distinct products. This is a Justification gap: technically, not every choice of intermediate coefficients necessarily produces a distinct product with coefficients bounded by $N$, but the order-of-magnitude argument suffices for an $O(N^n)$ bound.
2. The claim that the divisor-function factors can be absorbed into the $O(N^n)$ bound (van der Waerden's sharper argument) is invoked without proof. This is a minor Justification gap; the general reasoning remains correct, but a rigorous bound would require citation or explicit calculation.

No Critical errors are present, and the main conclusion is valid.

## Summary

The solution correctly formalizes the notion of "almost always irreducible" and provides a valid asymptotic argument to show that the proportion of reducible primitive polynomials tends to zero as height grows. Minor gaps in rigor regarding intermediate coefficients and divisor counts do not invalidate the conclusion.
