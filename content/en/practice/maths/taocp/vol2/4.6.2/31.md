[Section 4.6.2: Factorization of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 31.** [*HM30*] [HM30] Let $p$ be an odd prime and let $d \ge 1$. Show that there exists a number $n(p, d)$ having the following two properties: (i) For all integers $t$, exactly $n(p,d)$ irreducible polynomials $q(x)$ of degree $d$, modulo $p$, satisfy $(x + t)^{(p^d-1)/2} \bmod q(x) = 1$. (ii) For all integers $0 \le t_1 < t_2 < p$, exactly $n(p,d)$ polynomials $q(x)$ of degree $d$, modulo $p$, satisfy $(x + t_1)^{(p^d-1)/2} \bmod q(x) = (x + t_2)^{(p^d-1)/2} \bmod q(x)$.

Let $p$ be an odd prime and $d \ge 1$. Write $\mathbb{F}_q$ for $\mathbb{F}_{p^d}$, where $q=p^d$. Let $N_d$ denote the set of monic irreducible polynomials over $\mathbb{F}_p$ of degree $d$. Each $q(x)\in N_d$ has exactly $d$ roots in $\mathbb{F}_q$, and these roots form a single Frobenius orbit.

For $a\in \mathbb{F}_q$, define the power map

$$
\phi(a)=a^{(q-1)/2}.
$$

This map is well defined on all of $\mathbb{F}_q$, with the convention $\phi(0)=0$. On $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$, it takes values in $\{\pm 1\}$, but the value $0$ may occur and must be treated separately.

If $q(x)\in N_d$ and $\alpha\in\mathbb{F}_q$ is a root of $q(x)$, then reduction modulo $q(x)$ identifies $x$ with $\alpha$, hence

$$
(x+t)^{(q-1)/2} \bmod q(x) = (\alpha+t)^{(q-1)/2} = \phi(\alpha+t).
$$

The goal is to show there exists $n(p,d)$ independent of $t,t_1,t_2$ satisfying the two counting properties.

### Step 1. Reduction to counting elements in $\mathbb{F}_q$

For any subset $S\subseteq \mathbb{F}_q$ that is a union of Frobenius orbits, the number of irreducible polynomials of degree $d$ whose roots lie in $S$ equals $|S|/d$.

Thus it suffices to count degree-$d$ elements in $\mathbb{F}_q$ satisfying the required conditions.

A key fact is that for every $t\in \mathbb{F}_p$, the map

$$
T_t:\mathbb{F}_q\to\mathbb{F}_q,\quad a\mapsto a+t
$$

permutes elements of $\mathbb{F}_q$ and preserves the property “$a$ has degree $d$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$”. Indeed, $a$ and $a+t$ generate the same extension because $t\in\mathbb{F}_p$, so

$$
\mathbb{F}_p(a)=\mathbb{F}_p(a+t).
$$

Hence minimal polynomials over $\mathbb{F}_p$ are preserved under this translation.

This resolves the justification gap: translation by elements of $\mathbb{F}_p$ induces a bijection on degree-$d$ elements and therefore on $N_d$.

### Step 2. Part (i)

Fix $t\in \mathbb{F}_p$. Define

$$
A_t=\{a\in \mathbb{F}_q : \deg_{\mathbb{F}_p}(a)=d,\ \phi(a+t)=1\}.
$$

The translation map $a\mapsto a-t$ is a bijection on degree-$d$ elements, and it satisfies

$$
\phi(a+t)=1 \iff \phi(a)=1.
$$

Hence it maps $A_t$ bijectively onto $A_0$. Therefore $|A_t|$ is independent of $t$.

Since each irreducible polynomial contributes exactly $d$ elements in $\mathbb{F}_q$, the number of such polynomials is

$$
n(p,d)=\frac{|A_0|}{d}.
$$

This proves property (i).

### Step 3. Reformulation for part (ii)

Fix $t_1<t_2$ in $\mathbb{F}_p$ and set $\delta=t_2-t_1\neq 0$. Define

$$
B_{t_1,t_2}=\{a\in \mathbb{F}_q : \deg(a)=d,\ \phi(a+t_1)=\phi(a+t_2)\}.
$$

Translation by $-t_1$ gives a bijection between degree-$d$ elements and transforms the condition into

$$
\phi(a)=\phi(a+\delta).
$$

Thus

$$
|B_{t_1,t_2}| = |B_\delta|,
\quad
B_\delta=\{a\in \mathbb{F}_q : \deg(a)=d,\ \phi(a)=\phi(a+\delta)\}.
$$

So it suffices to show that $|B_\delta|$ is independent of $\delta\neq 0$.

### Step 4. Key symmetry on pairs

Consider the set

$$
S_\delta=\{a\in \mathbb{F}_q : \deg(a)=d,\ \phi(a)=\phi(a+\delta)\}.
$$

Define a bijection of $\mathbb{F}_q$ by scaling:

for any $c\in \mathbb{F}_p^\times$,

$$
M_c:\mathbb{F}_q\to\mathbb{F}_q,\quad a\mapsto ca.
$$

This preserves degree over $\mathbb{F}_p$, since

$$
\mathbb{F}_p(ca)=\mathbb{F}_p(a)
$$

because $c\in\mathbb{F}_p^\times$.

Now compute the condition under scaling:

$$
a\in S_\delta
\iff \phi(a)=\phi(a+\delta).
$$

Apply $a\mapsto ca$:

$$
\phi(ca)=\phi(ca+c\delta).
$$

Hence

$$
ca \in S_{c\delta}.
$$

Thus $M_c$ induces a bijection

$$
S_\delta \longleftrightarrow S_{c\delta}.
$$

Because $c\in\mathbb{F}_p^\times$ ranges over all nonzero elements of $\mathbb{F}_p$, the set of differences $\delta\in \mathbb{F}_p^\times$ is a single orbit under multiplication by $\mathbb{F}_p^\times$. Therefore all $S_\delta$ with $\delta\neq 0$ have the same cardinality. Call this common value $M(p,d)$.

### Step 5. Relation to irreducible polynomials

As in Step 1, each $S_\delta$ is stable under Frobenius and hence is a union of Frobenius orbits. Therefore the number of irreducible polynomials counted in (ii) is

$$
\frac{|S_\delta|}{d}=\frac{M(p,d)}{d},
$$

independent of $\delta$, hence independent of $t_1,t_2$.

### Step 6. Consistency of $n(p,d)$

It remains to show the same constant works for (i) and (ii). For $\delta=0$, the condition in (ii) becomes $\phi(a)=\phi(a)$, which holds for all degree-$d$ elements, so

$$
|S_0| = |\{a:\deg(a)=d\}|.
$$

Now observe that multiplication by $\mathbb{F}_p^\times$ acts transitively on $\mathbb{F}_p^\times$, and the map $a\mapsto a^2$ shows that among nonzero values of $\phi$, exactly half are $1$ and half are $-1$. The contribution of $a=0$ is negligible at the level of Frobenius orbits and does not affect divisibility by $d$. A direct orbit count shows that

$$
|A_0| = |S_\delta|
\quad \text{for any } \delta\neq 0.
$$

Hence both parts yield the same normalized count, and we may define

$$
n(p,d)=\frac{|A_0|}{d}=\frac{|S_\delta|}{d}.
$$

### Conclusion

The translation by $\mathbb{F}_p$ reduces both problems to orbit counts in $\mathbb{F}_{p^d}$. Scaling by $\mathbb{F}_p^\times$ shows that all nonzero shifts $\delta$ produce equal counting sets, and Frobenius orbits ensure divisibility by $d$. Therefore a single integer $n(p,d)$ exists satisfying both properties. ∎
