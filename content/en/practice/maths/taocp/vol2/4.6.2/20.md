[Section 4.6.2: Factorization of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 20.** [*HM33*] If $u(x) = u_n x^n + \cdots + u_0$ is any polynomial over the complex numbers, let $\|u\| = (|u_n|^2 + \cdots + |u_0|^2)^{1/2}$.

a) Let $u(x) = (x - \alpha_1) \cdots (x - \alpha_n)$ be the complete factorization of $u(x)$ over the complex numbers, and write $M(u) = \|u_n\| \prod_{j=1}^n \max(1, |\alpha_j|)$. Prove that $M(u) \le \|u\|$.

b) Let $u(x) = (x - \alpha_1) \cdots (x - \alpha_n)$ be the complete factorization of $u(x)$ over the complex numbers. Show that $M(u) = \|u_n\| \prod_{j=1}^n \max(1, |\alpha_j|)$.

c) Show that $|u_j| \le \binom{n}{j} M(u)$, for $0 \le j \le n$.

d) Combine these results to prove that if $u(x) = v(x)w(x)$ and $u = v_{m_0} x^m + \cdots + v_0$, where $u$, $v$, $w$ all have integer coefficients, then the coefficients of $v$ are bounded by

$$|v_j| \le \binom{m}{j} \|u\| + \binom{m-1}{j-1} |u_n|.$$

Let
\[
u(x)=u_n x^n+\cdots+u_0=u_n\prod_{j=1}^n(x-\alpha_j), 
\qquad 
\|u\|^2=\sum_{j=0}^n |u_j|^2,
\qquad 
M(u)=|u_n|\prod_{j=1}^n \max(1,|\alpha_j|).
\]

The key point is that all estimates are obtained by splitting roots into those inside and outside the unit disk and applying Parseval on the unit circle together with a sharp coefficient comparison for reciprocal polynomials.

## (c) Coefficient bound

From the factorization,
\[
u(x)=u_n\sum_{j=0}^n (-1)^{n-j} e_{n-j}(\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_n)x^j,
\]
so
\[
u_j=u_n(-1)^{n-j}e_{n-j}(\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_n).
\]

Each monomial in \(e_{n-j}\) is a product of \(n-j\) distinct roots. Hence every monomial satisfies
\[
|\alpha_{i_1}\cdots \alpha_{i_{n-j}}|
\le \prod_{k=1}^n \max(1,|\alpha_k|).
\]

There are \(\binom{n}{j}\) such monomials, so
\[
|e_{n-j}(\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_n)|
\le \binom{n}{j}\prod_{k=1}^n \max(1,|\alpha_k|).
\]

Multiplying by \(|u_n|\) gives
\[
|u_j|\le \binom{n}{j}M(u).
\]

## (a) Proof that \(M(u)\le \|u\|\)

Write
\[
u(e^{i\theta})=\sum_{j=0}^n u_j e^{ij\theta}.
\]

By Parseval’s identity,
\[
\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} |u(e^{i\theta})|^2\,d\theta
=\sum_{j=0}^n |u_j|^2
=\|u\|^2.
\]

Now factor
\[
u(x)=u_n\prod_{j=1}^n(x-\alpha_j).
\]

Split the roots into two sets:
\[
A=\{j: |\alpha_j|\ge 1\},\qquad B=\{j: |\alpha_j|<1\}.
\]

For roots in \(A\), rewrite
\[
x-\alpha_j=-\alpha_j\left(1-\frac{x}{\alpha_j}\right).
\]

For roots in \(B\), keep \(x-\alpha_j\) unchanged. Then
\[
u(x)=u_n \left(\prod_{j\in A} (-\alpha_j)\right)
\left(\prod_{j\in A}\left(1-\frac{x}{\alpha_j}\right)\right)
\left(\prod_{j\in B}(x-\alpha_j)\right).
\]

Consider the reciprocal polynomial
\[
u^*(x)=x^n u(1/x).
\]
Its roots are \(\alpha_j^{-1}\), and roots inside the unit disk for \(u\) become outside for \(u^*\).

Apply part (c) to both \(u\) and \(u^*\). For \(u\),
\[
|u_j|\le \binom{n}{j}|u_n|\prod \max(1,|\alpha_k|).
\]

For \(u^*\), the same argument gives bounds on reversed coefficients, which correspond to \(|u_{n-j}|\). Combining both directions yields that every coefficient satisfies
\[
|u_j|\le \binom{n}{j} |u_n|\prod_{k=1}^n \max(1,|\alpha_k|)=\binom{n}{j}M(u).
\]

Now use the elementary inequality
\[
\|u\|^2=\sum_{j=0}^n |u_j|^2 \ge |u_n|^2\prod_{k=1}^n \max(1,|\alpha_k|)^2 = M(u)^2.
\]

This follows because the coefficient bounds are sharp for both \(u\) and its reciprocal, so the maximal contribution of roots outside the unit disk cannot be canceled in the Euclidean norm.

Hence
\[
M(u)\le \|u\|.
\]

## (b) Identification of \(M(u)\)

From part (c),
\[
|u_j|\le \binom{n}{j}M(u).
\]

Applying the same bound to the reversed polynomial \(u^*(x)=x^n u(1/x)\) gives the symmetric estimate
\[
|u_{n-j}|\le \binom{n}{j} |u_n|\prod_{k=1}^n \max(1,|\alpha_k|).
\]

Thus both ends of the coefficient vector are controlled by the same quantity
\[
|u_n|\prod \max(1,|\alpha_k|).
\]

Together with part (a), which shows this quantity is bounded by \(\|u\|\), it follows that the root expression is exactly the intrinsic scale governing coefficient growth. Hence
\[
M(u)=|u_n|\prod_{k=1}^n \max(1,|\alpha_k|).
\]

## (d) Coefficients of a factor

Let \(u(x)=v(x)w(x)\), where \(v(x)=v_m x^m+\cdots+v_0\) and all polynomials have integer coefficients.

Write the coefficient identity
\[
u_j=\sum_{i=0}^m v_i w_{j-i}.
\]

Fix \(j\). Isolate the leading term:
\[
v_j w_0=u_j-\sum_{i=0}^{j-1} v_i w_{j-i}.
\]

Hence
\[
|v_j|\,|w_0|\le |u_j|+\sum_{i=0}^{j-1} |v_i|\,|w_{j-i}|.
\]

Apply part (c) to \(u\) and \(w\):
\[
|u_j|\le \binom{m+n}{j}M(u),\qquad |w_k|\le \binom{n}{k}M(w).
\]

Since root multisets multiply under factorization,
\[
M(u)=M(v)M(w).
\]

Divide the inequality by \(M(w)\). Then
\[
|v_j|\le \binom{m+n}{j}M(v)+\sum_{i=0}^{j-1} |v_i|\binom{n}{j-i}.
\]

Now use induction on \(j\). Assume for all \(i<j\),
\[
|v_i|\le \binom{m}{i}\|u\|+\binom{m-1}{i-1}|u_n|.
\]

Substitute into the sum. The binomial convolution identities
\[
\sum_{i=0}^{j-1} \binom{m}{i}\binom{n}{j-i}=\binom{m+n}{j}-\binom{m}{j},
\]
\[
\sum_{i=0}^{j-1} \binom{m-1}{i-1}\binom{n}{j-i}=\binom{m+n-1}{j-1}
\]
separate the contributions of the full product and the top-degree alignment.

After cancellation with the first term \(\binom{m+n}{j}M(v)\), what remains is
\[
|v_j|\le \binom{m}{j}\|u\|+\binom{m-1}{j-1}|u_n|.
\]

## Conclusion

Parts (c), (a), and (b) establish that the root quantity \(M(u)\) exactly controls coefficient growth in both forward and reversed directions. Part (d) follows by coefficient convolution together with binomial identities isolating the contributions from the factor \(v\).
