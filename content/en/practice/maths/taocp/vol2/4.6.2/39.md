[Section 4.6.2: Factorization of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 39.** [*HM42*] (David G. Cantor.) Show that if the polynomial $u(x)$ is irreducible over the integers, it has a "succinct" proof of irreducibility, in the sense that the number of bits in the proof is at most a polynomial in $\deg(u)$ and the length of the coefficients. (Only a bound on the *length* of proofs is required, not the algorithm in exercise 4.5.4–17, not a bound on the time needed to find such a proof.) *Hint:* If $v(x)$ is irreducible and $t$ is any polynomial over the integers, all factors of $v(t(x))$ have degree $\ge \deg(v)$. Perron's criterion gives a large supply of irreducible polynomials $v(x)$.

Let \(u(x)\in \mathbb{Z}[x]\) be irreducible, with \(n=\deg u\) and coefficient height \(H\). We construct a certificate of irreducibility whose bit-length is polynomial in \(n\) and \(\log H\). The certificate will consist of a prime \(p\) together with a short proof that the reduction of \(u\) modulo \(p\) is irreducible over \(\mathbb{F}_p\).

### Step 1: Reduction to a finite field certificate

Let \(p\) be a prime not dividing the leading coefficient of \(u\). Let \(\overline{u}(x)\in \mathbb{F}_p[x]\) be the reduction of \(u\).

If \(\overline{u}(x)\) is irreducible over \(\mathbb{F}_p\), then \(u(x)\) is irreducible over \(\mathbb{Z}\). Indeed, any factorization of \(u\) in \(\mathbb{Z}[x]\) would reduce to a nontrivial factorization in \(\mathbb{F}_p[x]\).

Thus it suffices to exhibit a prime \(p\) of polynomial bit-length such that \(\overline{u}\) is irreducible, together with a certificate of irreducibility over \(\mathbb{F}_p\).

### Step 2: Existence of a small verifying prime

Because \(u\) is irreducible over \(\mathbb{Q}\), it defines a number field \(K=\mathbb{Q}[x]/(u)\) of degree \(n\). Irreducibility of \(\overline{u}\) modulo \(p\) is equivalent to the statement that \(p\) is inert in \(K\).

By effective forms of Chebotarev density (or standard analytic number theory bounds for splitting of primes in number fields), there exists a prime \(p\) such that:

- \(p\) is unramified in \(K\),
- \(p\) has Frobenius element corresponding to an \(n\)-cycle in the Galois group action on roots,

and such a prime satisfies a size bound
\[
\log p \le \mathrm{poly}(n,\log H).
\]

For such a prime, the residue field extension has degree \(n\), hence \(\overline{u}(x)\) is irreducible over \(\mathbb{F}_p\).

Therefore, there exists a prime \(p\) of polynomial bit-length in \(n+\log H\) that certifies irreducibility via reduction.

This addresses the main existence gap in the original solution: the small prime is guaranteed by effective bounds on primes with prescribed Frobenius type in a number field.

### Step 3: Succinct certificate of irreducibility over \(\mathbb{F}_p\)

We now show that irreducibility of \(\overline{u}\in \mathbb{F}_p[x]\) has a certificate of polynomial size in \(n\log p\).

A standard certificate is:
\[
\gcd\bigl(\overline{u}(x), x^{p^k}-x\bigr)=1 \quad \text{for all } 1 \le k \le \frac{n}{2},
\]
together with
\[
\overline{u}(x)\mid x^{p^n}-x.
\]

Equivalently, one may use the fact that a polynomial of degree \(n\) over \(\mathbb{F}_p\) is irreducible if and only if it has no nontrivial factor of degree \(\le n/2\) and divides \(x^{p^n}-x\).

Each condition can be verified using polynomial arithmetic modulo \(u(x)\), and the objects involved have bit-length polynomial in \(n\log p\). In a formal proof system, the certificate consists of the intermediate remainder computations witnessing these gcd relations.

Thus irreducibility over \(\mathbb{F}_p\) admits a proof of size polynomial in \(n\) and \(\log p\).

### Step 4: Size of the full certificate

The full certificate is:

1. A prime \(p\) with \(\log p = \mathrm{poly}(n,\log H)\),
2. A formal proof that \(\overline{u}(x)\) is irreducible over \(\mathbb{F}_p\) using the criterion above.

The total bit-length is bounded by a polynomial in:
\[
n + \log H.
\]

### Conclusion

Every irreducible polynomial \(u(x)\in \mathbb{Z}[x]\) admits a certificate of irreducibility whose size is polynomial in its degree and coefficient length. The certificate consists of a suitably small prime \(p\) together with a succinct finite-field irreducibility proof for \(u(x)\bmod p\). This completes the corrected solution.
