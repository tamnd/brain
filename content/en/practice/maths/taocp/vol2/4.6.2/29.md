[Section 4.6.2: Factorization of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 29.** [*M22*] [M22] Let $g(x)$ be a product of two or more distinct irreducible polynomials of degree $d$, modulo an odd prime $p$. Prove that $\gcd(g(x), t(x)^{(p^d-1)/2} - 1)$ will be a proper factor of $g(x)$ with probability $\ge 1/2 - 1/(2p^d)$, for any fixed $g(x)$, when $t(x)$ is selected at random from among the $p^{nd}$ polynomials of degree $< 2d$ modulo $p$.

## Correctness

The solution is essentially correct and follows a standard and valid strategy for this theorem. The key idea is to use the resultant of $u$ and its derivative $u'$ to detect when squarefreeness fails after reduction modulo $p$. This is exactly the right invariant: squarefreeness over a field is equivalent to $\gcd(u,u') = 1$, and failure of this condition is captured by the vanishing of the resultant.

The argument correctly establishes that since $u$ is squarefree over $\mathbb{Z}$, we have $\gcd(u,u') = 1$ in $\mathbb{Z}[x]$, hence the resultant $R = \mathrm{Res}(u,u')$ is a nonzero integer. It then correctly uses the fact that primes $p$ for which $u(x)$ becomes non-squarefree modulo $p$ are exactly those dividing $R$. Since a nonzero integer has finitely many prime divisors, the conclusion follows.

All logical steps are aligned with standard algebraic number theory and elimination theory facts.

## Gaps and Errors

The proof is complete in structure, but there are two minor justification gaps in the way equivalences are stated.

The first is the claim that

$$
\overline{u}(x) \text{ is not squarefree over } \mathbb{F}_p \iff p \mid \mathrm{Res}(u,u').
$$

This is stated without an explicit argument connecting reduction modulo $p$ with the behavior of the resultant. The missing step is the standard fact that the resultant is a polynomial expression in the coefficients of $u$ and $u'$, hence reduces modulo $p$, and that

$$
\mathrm{Res}(\overline{u}, \overline{u}') \equiv \mathrm{Res}(u,u') \pmod p,
$$

so vanishing of the resultant in $\mathbb{F}_p$ is equivalent to $p\mid R$. This is a Justification gap.

The second is implicit use of the equivalence between squarefreeness and $\gcd(u,u')=1$ over a field. This is standard and correct, but it is assumed rather than explicitly justified. This is a minor Justification gap, not affecting correctness.

No circular reasoning or incorrect mathematical claims appear.

## Summary

The solution is correct in its main argument: it reduces the problem to the nonzero resultant and uses finiteness of prime divisors. The only issue is missing explicit justification of the compatibility of the resultant with reduction modulo $p$, which is standard but should be stated.
