[Section 4.6.2: Factorization of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 28.** [*M25*] [M25] The distinct-degree factorization procedure is "lucky" when there is at most one irreducible polynomial of each degree $d$; then $g(x)$ never needs to be broken into factors. What is the probability of such a lucky circumstance, when factoring a random polynomial of degree $n$, modulo $p$, for fixed $n$ as $p \to \infty$?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m46s

## Solution

Let $u(x)$ be a random monic polynomial of degree $n$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$. Factorization into monic irreducible polynomials over $\mathbb{F}_p$ is written

$u(x)=\prod_{j=1}^r p_j(x),$

where the $p_j(x)$ are distinct monic irreducibles with degrees $d_j$.

Fix $n$ and let $p\to\infty$. In this regime, the standard correspondence between factorization types of random polynomials over $\mathbb{F}_p$ and cycle structure of random permutations on $n$ elements applies: the probability that a random monic polynomial of degree $n$ has a given pattern of irreducible factor degrees converges to the probability that a random permutation of $n$ has the corresponding cycle type.

Under this correspondence, each irreducible factor of degree $d$ plays the role of a cycle of length $d$. The “lucky” condition is that there is at most one irreducible factor of each degree $d$, which translates exactly to the condition that the associated permutation has no repeated cycle lengths.

Let a permutation of $n$ elements have cycle-type with multiplicities $m_k$, where $m_k$ is the number of cycles of length $k$. The probability of a fixed cycle-type is

$\frac{1}{\prod_{k} k^{m_k} m_k!}.$

The lucky condition requires $m_k \in {0,1}$ for all $k$, so $m_k! = 1$ whenever $m_k=1$, and $k^{m_k}$ contributes only when $m_k=1$. Hence the probability of a given admissible cycle structure is

$\frac{1}{\prod_{k: m_k=1} k}.$

Summing over all permutations with distinct cycle lengths is equivalent to summing over all partitions of $n$ into distinct parts $\lambda = (\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_t)$, giving total probability

$\sum_{\substack{\lambda \vdash n \\ \lambda_i \text{ distinct}}} \frac{1}{\lambda_1 \lambda_2 \cdots \lambda_t}.$

Since the polynomial model converges to the permutation model as $p\to\infty$, this sum is the limiting probability of the lucky circumstance.

Therefore,

$\boxed{\mathbb{P}(\text{lucky}) = \sum_{\substack{\lambda \vdash n \\ \lambda \text{ has distinct parts}}} \frac{1}{\prod_{d\in\lambda} d}}.$

This completes the solution. ∎
