## Solution

We are asked to prove the congruence

$$
x^p - x \equiv (x - 0)(x - 1) \cdots (x - (p-1)) \pmod{p}, \eqno(9)
$$

where $p$ is a prime number. All arithmetic is performed in the field $\mathbb{F}_p$ of $p$ elements.  

Let us denote by $\mathbb{F}_p = \{0, 1, \ldots, p-1\}$ the integers modulo $p$, with addition and multiplication modulo $p$. Consider the polynomial

$$
f(x) = x^p - x \in \mathbb{F}_p[x].
$$

We claim that $f(x)$ has **exactly the $p$ elements of $\mathbb{F}_p$ as roots**, and that each root is simple.  

First, for any $a \in \mathbb{F}_p$, Fermat's little theorem asserts

$$
a^p \equiv a \pmod{p},
$$

so that $f(a) = a^p - a \equiv 0 \pmod{p}$. Therefore each $a \in \mathbb{F}_p$ is a root of $f(x)$. Since there are $p$ elements in $\mathbb{F}_p$, we have at least $p$ roots.  

Next, $f(x)$ is a polynomial of degree $p$. A polynomial over a field cannot have more roots than its degree. Therefore $f(x)$ has exactly $p$ roots, and they are precisely the elements of $\mathbb{F}_p$.  

Consequently, $f(x)$ is divisible by $(x - a)$ for each $a \in \mathbb{F}_p$. Since there are exactly $p$ roots, we may write

$$
x^p - x = \prod_{a \in \mathbb{F}_p} (x - a) = (x - 0)(x - 1) \cdots (x - (p-1))
$$

in $\mathbb{F}_p[x]$, as claimed. This proves congruence (9) modulo $p$.  

This completes the proof. ∎
