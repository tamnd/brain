## Solution

Let

$$
u(x)=u_nx^n+\cdots+u_0
$$

be reducible over the integers, and let

$$
u(x)=g(x)h(x),
\qquad
g(x),h(x)\in\mathbf Z[x],
$$

where neither factor is constant. Assume that every coefficient of every factor of $u(x)$ is bounded in absolute value by $B$.

The method discussed in the text reconstructs factors from a factorization modulo $p^k$. To guarantee uniqueness it requires

$$
p^k>2|u_n|B.
$$

The reason is that if a factor $g(x)$ is known modulo $p^k$, then its leading coefficient is known only modulo $p^k$. Since the leading coefficient of a factor may be as large as $|u_n|$, the reconstruction argument uses the bound $|u_n|B$.

A sharper procedure is obtained by removing the leading coefficient first.

Let

$$
v(x)=u_n^{\,n-1}u\!\left(\frac{x}{u_n}\right).
$$

Then $v(x)$ is a primitive polynomial with integer coefficients. If

$$
u(x)=g(x)h(x),
$$

where

$$
\deg g=r,\qquad \deg h=s,\qquad r+s=n,
$$

define

$$
G(x)=u_n^{\,r-1}g\!\left(\frac{x}{u_n}\right),
\qquad
H(x)=u_n^{\,s-1}h\!\left(\frac{x}{u_n}\right).
$$

The coefficients of $G$ and $H$ are integers, and

$$
v(x)=G(x)H(x).
$$

The leading coefficients of $G$ and $H$ divide the leading coefficient of $v$, which is $1$. Hence $G$ and $H$ are monic.

Every coefficient of $G$ and $H$ is bounded in absolute value by $B$. Therefore two distinct monic polynomials satisfying this coefficient bound cannot be congruent modulo $p^k$ whenever

$$
p^k>2B.
$$

Indeed, if

$$
G_1(x)\equiv G_2(x)\pmod{p^k},
$$

then every coefficient difference is a multiple of $p^k$. Since each coefficient difference has absolute value less than

$$
2B<p^k,
$$

all coefficient differences are zero; hence

$$
G_1(x)=G_2(x).
$$

Consequently, if a factorization of $v(x)$ is known modulo $p^k$ with

$$
p^k>2B,
$$

the monic factors lift uniquely to integer factors. After obtaining

$$
v(x)=G(x)H(x),
$$

the original factorization of $u(x)$ is recovered from

$$
g(x)=u_n^{\,1-r}G(u_nx),
\qquad
h(x)=u_n^{\,1-s}H(u_nx).
$$

Thus the modulus need only satisfy

$$
\boxed{p^k>2B},
$$

instead of

$$
p^k>2|u_n|B.
$$

This improvement is effective precisely when $|u_n|>1$, and in particular when $|u_n|$ is much larger than the coefficient bound $B$ obtained from exercise 21.

This completes the proof.
