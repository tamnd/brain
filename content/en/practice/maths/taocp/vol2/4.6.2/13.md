## Corrected Solution for Exercise 4.6.2.13

We work modulo an odd prime $p$ and aim to factor

$$
x^8 + 1 \in \mathbb{F}_p[x]
$$

in terms of the radicals $\sqrt{-1}$, $\sqrt{2}$, $\sqrt{-2}$ when they exist. We proceed systematically, using cyclotomic factorization and classical quadratic techniques.

### Step 1: Cyclotomic decomposition

The polynomial $x^8 + 1$ is the $16$th cyclotomic polynomial times $x + 1$ or equivalently

$$
x^8 + 1 = \prod_{k=0}^{7} (x - \zeta^{2k+1}),
$$

where $\zeta$ is a primitive $16$th root of unity in an algebraic closure of $\mathbb{F}_p$. We seek factorizations over $\mathbb{F}_p$, so the goal is to express pairs of roots as quadratics or quartics using available square roots in $\mathbb{F}_p$.

### Step 2: Existence of $\sqrt{-1}$

If $\sqrt{-1} \in \mathbb{F}_p$, then $-1$ is a square modulo $p$, i.e., $p \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$. Set $i = \sqrt{-1}$. Then we have the identity in $\mathbb{F}_p[x]$:

$$
x^8 + 1 = (x^4 + i)(x^4 - i).
$$

Each quartic can now be factored as a quadratic over $\mathbb{F}_p$ if $\sqrt{2} \in \mathbb{F}_p$. Indeed, we use the standard factorization formula for quartics of the form $x^4 + a^2$:

$$
x^4 + i = \left(x^2 + \sqrt{2}\, x + i^{1/2}\right)\left(x^2 - \sqrt{2}\, x + i^{1/2}\right),
$$

where $i^{1/2}$ exists in $\mathbb{F}_p$ whenever $i$ is a fourth power. For clarity, the four quadratic factors are

$$
x^2 \pm \sqrt{2}\, x \pm i,
$$

with all four sign combinations. Therefore, when both $\sqrt{-1}$ and $\sqrt{2}$ exist, the complete factorization is

$$
x^8 + 1 = \prod_{\varepsilon_1, \varepsilon_2 \in \{\pm 1\}} \left(x^2 + \varepsilon_1 \sqrt{2}\, x + \varepsilon_2 i\right).
$$

If $\sqrt{-1} \in \mathbb{F}_p$ but $\sqrt{2} \notin \mathbb{F}_p$, each quartic remains irreducible over $\mathbb{F}_p$, so

$$
x^8 + 1 = (x^4 + i)(x^4 - i)
$$

is the maximal factorization over $\mathbb{F}_p$.

### Step 3: $\sqrt{-1} \notin \mathbb{F}_p$, $\sqrt{-2}$ exists

Suppose $\sqrt{-1} \notin \mathbb{F}_p$ but $\sqrt{-2} \in \mathbb{F}_p$. Let $j = \sqrt{-2}$. Then $j^2 = -2$, and we can factor $x^8 + 1$ as a product of quadratics in $x^2$:

$$
x^8 + 1 = (x^4 + j x^2 + 1)(x^4 - j x^2 + 1).
$$

Set $y = x^2$ to reduce each quartic to quadratics:

$$
y^2 \pm j y + 1 \in \mathbb{F}_p[y].
$$

These quadratics split in $\mathbb{F}_p[y]$ if the discriminant $j^2 - 4 = -2 - 4 = -6$ is a square. If $-6$ is not a square, each quartic is irreducible in $x$. The maximal factorization over $\mathbb{F}_p$ is therefore

$$
x^8 + 1 = (x^4 + j x^2 + 1)(x^4 - j x^2 + 1),
$$

with $j = \sqrt{-2}$.

### Step 4: $\sqrt{-1}, \sqrt{2}, \sqrt{-2}$ absent

If none of the radicals exist modulo $p$, the polynomial is irreducible over $\mathbb{F}_p$ as a product of two quartics. This can be seen from the identity

$$
x^8 + 1 = (x^4 + 2 x^2 + 1)^2 - 4 x^4,
$$

which yields the formal factorization

$$
x^8 + 1 = (x^4 + \sqrt{-2} x^2 + 1)(x^4 - \sqrt{-2} x^2 + 1),
$$

but since $\sqrt{-2} \notin \mathbb{F}_p$, these quartics remain irreducible over $\mathbb{F}_p$.

### Step 5: Summary of explicit factors

Let $i = \sqrt{-1}$, $s_2 = \sqrt{2}$, $s_{-2} = \sqrt{-2}$ when they exist. Then the factorization of $x^8 + 1$ modulo an odd prime $p$ is:

1. If $\sqrt{-1}, \sqrt{2} \in \mathbb{F}_p$:

$$
x^8 + 1 = \prod_{\varepsilon_1, \varepsilon_2 \in \{\pm 1\}} \left(x^2 + \varepsilon_1 s_2\, x + \varepsilon_2 i\right).
$$

1. If $\sqrt{-1} \in \mathbb{F}_p$, $\sqrt{2} \notin \mathbb{F}_p$:

$$
x^8 + 1 = (x^4 + i)(x^4 - i).
$$

1. If $\sqrt{-1} \notin \mathbb{F}_p$, $\sqrt{-2} \in \mathbb{F}_p$:

$$
x^8 + 1 = (x^4 + s_{-2} x^2 + 1)(x^4 - s_{-2} x^2 + 1),
$$

with each quartic possibly irreducible depending on whether $-6$ is a square.

1. If none of $\sqrt{-1}, \sqrt{2}, \sqrt{-2}$ exist:

$$
x^8 + 1
$$

is irreducible over $\mathbb{F}_p$.

### Justification

- The existence of $\sqrt{-1}$, $\sqrt{2}$, $\sqrt{-2}$ classifies odd primes via quadratic residue conditions modulo $p$.
- When the necessary radicals exist, quartic polynomials of the form $x^4 \pm i$ or $x^4 \pm \sqrt{-2} x^2 + 1$ can be factored into quadratics by completing the square and solving $b^2 = \pm i$ or $b^2 = \pm \sqrt{-2}$, as shown.
- If the radicals do not exist, the formal quartics are irreducible in $\mathbb{F}_p[x]$ because no square roots are available to split them further. Quadratic reciprocity confirms which primes allow which radicals.
- This treatment exhausts all possibilities for an odd prime $p$, providing a complete and rigorous factorization scheme in terms of the allowed radicals.
