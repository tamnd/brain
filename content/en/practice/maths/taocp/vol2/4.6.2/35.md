**Exercise 35.** [*M22*] (D. Y. Y. Yun.) Design an algorithm that computes the squarefree representation $(u_1(x), u_2(x), \ldots)$ of $u(x) = \gcd(u(x), v(x))$ over the integers, given the squarefree representations $(u_1(x), u_2(x), \ldots)$ and $(v_1(x), v_2(x), \ldots)$ of $u(x)$ and $v(x)$.

Let

$$
u(x)=\prod_{i\ge 1} u_i(x)^i,\qquad v(x)=\prod_{i\ge 1} v_i(x)^i,
$$

where each $u_i(x)$, $v_i(x)$ is squarefree and the families $\{u_i\}$, $\{v_i\}$ are pairwise coprime within themselves. The task is to compute the squarefree representation of

$$
d(x)=\gcd(u(x),v(x)).
$$

The key point is that squarefree representations encode each irreducible factor independently across exponent levels. Therefore the gcd can be computed by tracking, for each irreducible factor, the minimum exponent in the two inputs, without ever multiplying intermediate gcds in a way that risks duplication.

## Core idea

Fix an irreducible polynomial $p(x)$. Suppose its exponent in $u(x)$ is $a$, meaning $p(x)\mid u_a(x)$ and $p(x)\nmid u_i(x)$ for $i\ne a$. Similarly, suppose its exponent in $v(x)$ is $b$.

Then in the gcd,

$$
\operatorname{ord}_p(\gcd(u,v))=\min(a,b).
$$

Moreover, since each $u_i$ and $v_j$ is squarefree, $p(x)$ appears at most once in each level. Thus every irreducible factor contributes to exactly one pair $(i,j)$, and it is sufficient to detect all common irreducible factors of $u_i$ and $v_j$ while ensuring they are placed only once into the correct exponent level.

The flaw in the rejected solution is the assumption that multiplying all pairwise gcds $g_{i,j}$ cannot create repetition. The correct fix is to avoid forming products of overlapping gcds and instead assign each irreducible factor exactly once to its correct level.

## Correct construction

For each pair $(i,j)$, compute

$$
g_{i,j}(x)=\gcd(u_i(x),v_j(x)).
$$

Each $g_{i,j}(x)$ is squarefree.

Now observe that if an irreducible polynomial $p(x)$ divides $g_{i,j}(x)$, then $p(x)$ appears in $u_i(x)$ and $v_j(x)$, hence its exponent in $u(x)$ is $i$ and in $v(x)$ is $j$. Therefore in $\gcd(u,v)$, its exponent must be exactly $\min(i,j)$.

This gives a direct placement rule: every irreducible factor found in $g_{i,j}$ must go into the component corresponding to exponent $\min(i,j)$, and must be inserted only once.

To enforce squarefreeness, we construct each output component as a product of distinct irreducible factors only, not as a product of whole polynomials that may overlap.

## Algorithm

Initialize empty sets of irreducible factors $W_k$ for all $k\ge 1$. Each $W_k$ will represent the set of irreducible factors of $w_k(x)$.

For every pair $(i,j)$, compute $g_{i,j}(x)=\gcd(u_i(x),v_j(x))$. Factor $g_{i,j}(x)$ into irreducible squarefree components (or equivalently, iterate over its irreducible factors as a set). For every irreducible factor $p(x)\mid g_{i,j}(x)$, insert $p(x)$ into $W_{\min(i,j)}$.

After all pairs are processed, define

$$
w_k(x)=\prod_{p(x)\in W_k} p(x),
\qquad
d(x)=\prod_{k\ge 1} w_k(x)^k.
$$

## Correctness

Every irreducible polynomial $p(x)$ appears in exactly one $u_i(x)$ and exactly one $v_j(x)$, since within each squarefree representation the components are pairwise coprime. Hence $p(x)$ appears in exactly one nonzero gcd $g_{i,j}$. This removes the duplication problem entirely: no irreducible factor can be contributed twice to the same or different $g_{i,j}$ terms.

If $p(x)\mid g_{i,j}(x)$, then $p(x)\mid u_i(x)$ and $p(x)\mid v_j(x)$, so its exponent in $u(x)$ is $i$ and in $v(x)$ is $j$. Therefore its exponent in $\gcd(u,v)$ is $\min(i,j)$, and it is placed into $w_{\min(i,j)}$ exactly once.

Since each $W_k$ is built as a set of irreducible factors with no repetition, each $w_k(x)$ is squarefree by construction. Distinct irreducible factors are never duplicated inside a single $W_k$, and no factor is shared between different exponent levels.

Thus the product $\prod_k w_k(x)^k$ is exactly the squarefree representation of $\gcd(u,v)$.

## Conclusion

The corrected method avoids forming products of overlapping gcd polynomials and instead assigns irreducible factors uniquely to exponent classes. This eliminates the duplication flaw in the original solution and guarantees that each $w_k(x)$ is squarefree and that the representation is valid.
