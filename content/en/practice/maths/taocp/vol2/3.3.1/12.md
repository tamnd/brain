**Exercise 12.** [*M28*] [M28] Suppose a chi-square test is made on a set of $n$ observations, assuming that $p_s$ is the probability that each observation falls into category $s$; but suppose that in actual fact the observations have probability $q_s \ne p_s$ of falling into category $s$. (See exercise 3.) We would, of course, like the chi-square test to detect the fact that the $p_s$ assumption was incorrect. Show that this *will* happen, if $n$ is large enough. Prove also the analogous result for the KS test.

**Solution to Exercise 3.3.1.12 [_M28_]**

Let $n$ be the number of independent observations, and suppose there are $k$ categories labeled $s = 1,2,\dots,k$. Let $p_s$ denote the assumed probability of an observation falling into category $s$, and let $q_s$ denote the true probability, with $q_s \ne p_s$ for at least one $s$. Let $N_s$ be the number of observations observed in category $s$.

**Chi-square test**

The chi-square statistic is defined as

$$
X^2 = \sum_{s=1}^{k} \frac{(N_s - n p_s)^2}{n p_s}.
$$

Since the $N_s$ are counts of independent observations with true probabilities $q_s$, we have

$$
\mathbb{E}[N_s] = n q_s, \quad \operatorname{Var}(N_s) = n q_s (1 - q_s).
$$

Consider the decomposition

$$
N_s - n p_s = (N_s - n q_s) + n (q_s - p_s).
$$

Squaring both sides and dividing by $n p_s$ gives

$$
\frac{(N_s - n p_s)^2}{n p_s} = \frac{(N_s - n q_s)^2}{n p_s} + \frac{2 (N_s - n q_s) n (q_s - p_s)}{n p_s} + \frac{n^2 (q_s - p_s)^2}{n p_s}.
$$

Taking expectations, the first term contributes

$$
\frac{\operatorname{Var}(N_s)}{n p_s} = \frac{n q_s (1 - q_s)}{n p_s} = \frac{q_s (1 - q_s)}{p_s},
$$

the second term has expectation zero, and the third term contributes

$$
\frac{n (q_s - p_s)^2}{p_s}.
$$

Thus

$$
\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \sum_{s=1}^{k} \frac{q_s (1 - q_s)}{p_s} + n \sum_{s=1}^{k} \frac{(q_s - p_s)^2}{p_s}.
$$

The second sum is strictly positive since $q_s \ne p_s$ for some $s$. Consequently, as $n \to \infty$, the expected value of $X^2$ grows without bound:

$$
\mathbb{E}[X^2] \sim n \sum_{s=1}^{k} \frac{(q_s - p_s)^2}{p_s} \to \infty.
$$

Since $X^2$ is a sum of independent random variables and its variance grows only linearly in $n$, by Chebyshev's inequality it follows that $X^2 \to \infty$ in probability. Therefore, for any fixed significance level $\alpha$, the probability that the chi-square test rejects the null hypothesis $H_0: p_s$ tends to 1 as $n \to \infty$. This shows that the chi-square test will detect the difference between $p_s$ and $q_s$ for sufficiently large $n$.

**Kolmogorov–Smirnov (KS) test**

Let $F(x)$ denote the assumed cumulative distribution function (CDF) under the $p_s$ probabilities and let $G(x)$ denote the true CDF under the $q_s$. Let $F_n(x)$ be the empirical CDF of the $n$ observations. The KS statistic is

$$
D_n = \sup_x |F_n(x) - F(x)|.
$$

By the law of large numbers, $F_n(x) \to G(x)$ almost surely uniformly in $x$. Since $G(x) \ne F(x)$ at some $x$ (because $q_s \ne p_s$ for some $s$), there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $|G(x) - F(x)| \ge \delta$ for some $x$. Therefore, for sufficiently large $n$, $|F_n(x) - F(x)|$ will exceed $\delta/2$ with high probability, and

$$
D_n \ge |F_n(x) - F(x)| \to \sup_x |G(x) - F(x)| > 0.
$$

Thus the KS statistic eventually exceeds any fixed critical value corresponding to a given significance level $\alpha$, implying that the KS test will also detect the discrepancy between $F$ and $G$ with probability tending to 1 as $n \to \infty$.

**Conclusion**

Both the chi-square test and the KS test are consistent against fixed alternatives: if the true probabilities $q_s$ differ from the assumed probabilities $p_s$, then as the sample size $n$ becomes large, the tests will detect the discrepancy with probability approaching 1.

$$
\boxed{\text{Both tests detect $p_s \ne q_s$ for large $n$.}}
$$
