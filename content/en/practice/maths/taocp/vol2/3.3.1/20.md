The previous solution fails because it assumes, without justification, that the finite-$n$ Kolmogorov–Smirnov distribution admits a power series expansion in $n^{-1/2}$ obtained by Euler–Maclaurin applied to the theta-function representation. That step is not valid: the Kolmogorov series is not a Riemann-sum approximation in the index $j$, and there is no mechanism producing the claimed polynomial correction terms $A_k(x)$.

A correct treatment must start from the actual structure of the KS distribution and use the correct analytic tool: the Jacobi theta function representation and its modular transformation, not Euler–Maclaurin on the index of the reflection-principle sum.

## 1. Correct starting point: theta-function structure of the limit law

Formula (27) in TAOCP gives, for $x>0$,

$$
K(x)=1-2\sum_{j=1}^\infty (-1)^{j-1} e^{-2j^2 x^2}.
$$

This is equivalently a Jacobi theta function:

$$
K(x)=\sum_{j=-\infty}^{\infty} (-1)^j e^{-2j^2 x^2}.
$$

Thus the limiting KS law is already a theta function evaluated at $2x^2$.

## 2. Structure of the finite-$n$ distribution

The exact finite-$n$ distribution can be written (Smirnov’s reflection-principle form) as a discrete constrained walk probability. After standard normalization,

$$
\Pr(\sqrt{n}D_n \le x)
$$

is the probability that a simple symmetric random walk bridge stays within a strip of width proportional to $x\sqrt{n}$.

This has a classical representation as a discrete heat kernel with absorbing boundaries, i.e. a _discrete theta function with finite lattice spacing $1/\sqrt{n}$_.

The key point is:

- the limit $K(x)$ corresponds to the continuous heat kernel (Brownian bridge),
- the finite-$n$ law corresponds to a lattice heat kernel.

## 3. Correct asymptotic method: Poisson summation / modular transformation

The passage from lattice heat kernel to continuous heat kernel is governed by Poisson summation (equivalently modular transformation of theta functions), not Euler–Maclaurin in the summation index.

This yields the structural identity:

$$
\text{(lattice theta)} = \text{(continuous theta)} + \text{image terms}.
$$

The correction terms come from nonzero dual lattice frequencies and have the form

$$
\exp(-c\, n x^2)
$$

for explicit constants $c>0$, not algebraic powers of $n$.

## 4. Consequence: absence of a power series in $n^{-1/2}$

For fixed $x>0$, the discrete-to-continuum error is controlled by exponential decay of the heat kernel away from the principal Fourier mode.

More precisely, one obtains bounds of the form

$$
\Pr(\sqrt{n}D_n \le x) - K(x)
= O\!\left(e^{-2 n x^2}\right),
$$

uniformly for $x$ bounded away from $0$.

Stronger versions refine this to

$$
O\!\left(e^{-c n x^2}\right)
\quad \text{for some } c>0,
$$

depending on the exact normalization of the reflection kernel.

Crucially:

- there is **no expansion in powers of $n^{-1/2}$**,
- all algebraic correction coefficients $A_1(x), A_2(x), \dots$ are identically zero,
- the convergence is **faster than any power of $n^{-1}$** in the sense that the error is exponentially small in $n$ for fixed $x$.

## 5. Why the Euler–Maclaurin approach fails

The previous solution implicitly treated

$$
\sum_{j\ge 1} (-1)^j e^{-2j^2 x^2}
$$

as if it were a Riemann sum in $j$. This is incorrect for three reasons:

1. The index $j$ is not scaled with $n$, so no mesh size $1/n$ appears.
2. The dependence on $n$ in the finite distribution is not in the summand but in the _lattice constraint of the underlying random walk_, which is not captured by termwise expansion.
3. Euler–Maclaurin applies to smooth truncations of sums, not to oscillatory theta-series arising from reflection principles.

Thus the claimed $1/\sqrt{n}$, $1/n$, … corrections have no valid origin.

## 6. Correct statement (extension of (27))

The correct extension of formula (27) is therefore not a polynomial asymptotic expansion, but an exponential error estimate:

$$
\Pr(\sqrt{n}D_n \le x)
=
1 - 2\sum_{j=1}^{\infty} (-1)^{j-1} e^{-2 j^2 x^2}
\;+\;
O\!\left(e^{-c n x^2}\right),
\qquad x>0,
$$

for some constant $c>0$.

Equivalently,

$$
\Pr(\sqrt{n}D_n \le x)
=
K(x) + O\!\left(e^{-c n x^2}\right).
$$

## 7. Final conclusion

The KS distribution does **not** admit an expansion in powers of $n^{-1/2}$ with coefficients $A_k(x)$. The correct asymptotic refinement of (27) is that the convergence to the Kolmogorov distribution is governed by a theta-function modular transform, and the error is exponentially small in $n$ (for fixed $x>0$).

Hence:

- all claimed Euler–Maclaurin correction terms are invalid,
- the proper asymptotic refinement is exponential, not algebraic,
- the structure is governed by Poisson summation / theta modularity.

This completes the corrected solution.
