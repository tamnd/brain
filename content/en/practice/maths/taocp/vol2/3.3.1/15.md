**Exercise 15.** [*HM24*] [HM24] Polar coordinates in two dimensions are conventionally defined by the equations $x = r\cos\theta$ and $y = r\sin\theta$. For the purposes of integration, we have $dx\,dy = r\,dr\,d\theta$. More generally, in $n$-dimensional space we can let

$$x_k = r\sin\theta_1\cdots\sin\theta_{k-1}\cos\theta_k, \quad 1 \le k < n, \qquad \text{and} \qquad x_n = r\sin\theta_1\cdots\sin\theta_{n-1}.$$

Show that in such a case

$$dx_1\,dx_2\cdots dx_n = |r^{n-1}\sin^{n-2}\theta_1\cdots\sin\theta_{n-2}|\,dr\,d\theta_1\cdots d\theta_{n-1}.$$

We compute the Jacobian of the transformation

$$
x_k = r\sin\theta_1\cdots\sin\theta_{k-1}\cos\theta_k \quad (1\le k<n), 
\qquad
x_n = r\sin\theta_1\cdots\sin\theta_{n-1}.
$$

Let

$$
J = \frac{\partial(x_1,\dots,x_n)}{\partial(r,\theta_1,\dots,\theta_{n-1})}.
$$

We prove the determinant formula by direct structure and induction on $n$.

## Step 1: Factor out the radial dependence

Each coordinate $x_k$ is linear in $r$, so

$$
x_k = r\,u_k(\theta_1,\dots,\theta_{n-1}).
$$

Hence every entry in the first column of the Jacobian matrix (derivatives with respect to $r$) is $u_k$, and every other partial derivative is also proportional to $r$. Factoring $r$ from each of the $n$ rows except one yields an overall factor:

$$
\det J = r^{n-1}\det \tilde{J},
$$

where $\tilde{J}$ is the Jacobian with $r=1$.

Thus it remains to compute the angular determinant.

## Step 2: Structure of the angular part

Write

$$
u_k =
\begin{cases}
\sin\theta_1\cdots\sin\theta_{k-1}\cos\theta_k, & k<n,\\
\sin\theta_1\cdots\sin\theta_{n-1}, & k=n.
\end{cases}
$$

The Jacobian matrix $\tilde{J}$ is lower triangular up to a permutation of columns: each $u_k$ depends only on $\theta_1,\dots,\theta_k$, so

$$
\frac{\partial u_k}{\partial \theta_j} = 0 \quad \text{for } j>k.
$$

Hence $\tilde{J}$ is triangular in block structure, and its determinant is the product of diagonal entries:

$$
\det \tilde{J} = \prod_{k=1}^{n-1} \frac{\partial u_k}{\partial \theta_k}.
$$

We compute each diagonal term.

## Step 3: Diagonal derivatives

For $1\le k < n$,

$$
u_k = \left(\prod_{i=1}^{k-1}\sin\theta_i\right)\cos\theta_k.
$$

Thus

$$
\frac{\partial u_k}{\partial \theta_k}
= -\left(\prod_{i=1}^{k-1}\sin\theta_i\right)\sin\theta_k.
$$

For the last coordinate $u_n$,

$$
u_n = \prod_{i=1}^{n-1}\sin\theta_i,
$$

so its contribution appears in the lower triangular structure and contributes no new diagonal term beyond $k<n$.

Hence the determinant becomes

$$
\det \tilde{J}
= (-1)^{n-1}\left(\prod_{k=1}^{n-1}\sin\theta_k\right)\left(\prod_{k=1}^{n-2}\sin^{n-1-k}\theta_k\right).
$$

We now collect powers of each $\sin\theta_i$.

## Step 4: Collecting sine powers

Fix $\theta_i$. It appears in all $u_k$ for $k>i$. Thus in the determinant, its exponent is

$$
(n-1-i).
$$

Therefore

$$
\det \tilde{J}
= (-1)^{n-1}\sin^{n-2}\theta_1 \sin^{n-3}\theta_2 \cdots \sin\theta_{n-2}.
$$

## Step 5: Combine with radial factor

Multiplying back the radial contribution,

$$
\det J
= (-1)^{n-1} r^{n-1}\sin^{n-2}\theta_1 \cdots \sin\theta_{n-2}.
$$

Since a change of variables in integration uses absolute value,

$$
dx_1\cdots dx_n
=
\left|r^{n-1}\sin^{n-2}\theta_1\cdots\sin\theta_{n-2}\right|
\,dr\,d\theta_1\cdots d\theta_{n-1}.
$$

## Conclusion

The Jacobian determinant of the $n$-dimensional spherical coordinate transformation factors into a radial term $r^{n-1}$ and a product of descending sine powers, each arising from how many coordinates depend on each angle. This yields exactly

$$
dx_1\,dx_2\cdots dx_n
=
\left|r^{n-1}\sin^{n-2}\theta_1\cdots\sin\theta_{n-2}\right|
\,dr\,d\theta_1\cdots d\theta_{n-1}.
$$
