## Exercise 3.3.1.22 [_HM46_]

Investigate the "improved" KS test suggested in the answer to exercise 6.

## Setup

Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n$ be independent observations from a continuous distribution function $F(x)$, and let $F_n(x)$ denote the empirical distribution function:

$$
F_n(x) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \mathbf{1}_{\{X_i \le x\}}.
$$

The classical Kolmogorov–Smirnov statistics are

$$
K_n^+ = \sup_x (F_n(x) - F(x)), \qquad K_n^- = \sup_x (F(x) - F_n(x)),
$$

and the two-sided statistic is

$$
K_n = \max(K_n^+, K_n^-).
$$

The "improved" KS statistic modifies $K_n$ by standardizing each deviation by its pointwise standard deviation. Define

$$
K_n^\ast = \sup_{x \in (a,b)} \frac{|F_n(x) - F(x)|}{\sqrt{F(x)(1-F(x))}}, \tag{22.1}
$$

where $(a,b) \subset (0,1)$ to avoid division by zero at the boundaries. The goal is to investigate the limiting distribution, well-posedness, and practical implications of $K_n^\ast$.

## Standardization and limiting process

For each fixed $x$, $n F_n(x)$ has a binomial distribution with mean $n F(x)$ and variance $n F(x)(1-F(x))$. Hence

$$
\operatorname{Var}(F_n(x)) = \frac{F(x)(1-F(x))}{n}.
$$

Define the standardized empirical process

$$
G_n(x) = \frac{F_n(x) - F(x)}{\sqrt{F(x)(1-F(x))/n}} = \sqrt{n}\,\frac{F_n(x) - F(x)}{\sqrt{F(x)(1-F(x))}}.
$$

By Donsker's theorem, the classical empirical process $\sqrt{n}(F_n(x) - F(x))$ converges weakly in the Skorokhod space $D[0,1]$ to a Brownian bridge $B(F(x))$, a mean-zero Gaussian process with covariance

$$
\operatorname{Cov}(B(F(x)), B(F(y))) = F(x \wedge y) - F(x) F(y).
$$

Hence, for any fixed interval $[a,b] \subset (0,1)$,

$$
G_n(x) \Rightarrow \frac{B(F(x))}{\sqrt{F(x)(1-F(x))}}, \qquad x \in [a,b]. \tag{22.2}
$$

This defines a Gaussian process with mean zero and pointwise variance $1$.

## Boundary behavior

Near the endpoints $t=F(x) \to 0$ or $t \to 1$, the pointwise variance remains finite ($\operatorname{Var}(B(t)/\sqrt{t(1-t)}) = 1$), but the process is highly irregular due to the vanishing of $t(1-t)$ in the denominator. To determine whether the supremum is almost surely finite, consider the law of the iterated logarithm for Brownian bridge:

$$
\limsup_{t \to 0+} \frac{|B(t)|}{\sqrt{2 t \log\log(1/t)}} = 1, \qquad \limsup_{t \to 1-} \frac{|B(t)|}{\sqrt{2 (1-t) \log\log(1/(1-t))}} = 1 \quad \text{a.s.}
$$

Hence, for small $t$,

$$
\frac{|B(t)|}{\sqrt{t(1-t)}} \sim \frac{|B(t)|}{\sqrt{t}} \sim \sqrt{2 \log\log(1/t)} \quad \text{a.s.},
$$

which diverges as $t \to 0$ (and similarly as $t \to 1$). Therefore, the supremum

$$
\sup_{0 < t < 1} \frac{|B(t)|}{\sqrt{t(1-t)}}
$$

is **not finite almost surely**. This rigorously justifies that the untruncated statistic $K_n^\ast$ over $(0,1)$ is ill-posed.

## Truncated statistic

To obtain a well-defined, usable limiting distribution, introduce a small truncation $\varepsilon>0$:

$$
K_{n,\varepsilon}^\ast = \sup_{x \in F^{-1}([\varepsilon, 1-\varepsilon])} \frac{|F_n(x)-F(x)|}{\sqrt{F(x)(1-F(x))}}.
$$

For any fixed $\varepsilon>0$, the limiting process

$$
K_\varepsilon^\ast = \sup_{t \in [\varepsilon,1-\varepsilon]} \frac{|B(t)|}{\sqrt{t(1-t)}}
$$

is finite almost surely and defines a valid Gaussian supremum. Hence,

$$
K_{n,\varepsilon}^\ast \Rightarrow K_\varepsilon^\ast \quad \text{as } n \to \infty.
$$

As $\varepsilon \to 0$, $K_\varepsilon^\ast \to \infty$ almost surely, reflecting the extreme sensitivity near the boundaries.

## Practical implications

Let $t = F(x)$. Then

$$
K_{n,\varepsilon}^\ast = \sup_{t \in [\varepsilon, 1-\varepsilon]} \frac{|F_n(F^{-1}(t)) - t|}{\sqrt{t(1-t)}}.
$$

- The variance stabilization gives equal relative weight to fluctuations across the interior interval $[\varepsilon, 1-\varepsilon]$.
- Deviations near $t \approx \varepsilon$ or $t \approx 1-\varepsilon$ that are small in absolute magnitude may dominate the supremum, increasing sensitivity in the tails.
- The classical KS statistic underweights these deviations, making the improved statistic more responsive to tail departures.

The limiting distribution of $K_{n,\varepsilon}^\ast$ depends on the Gaussian correlation structure of the Brownian bridge over the compact interval. It cannot be expressed in closed form. Approximate critical values can be obtained using Monte Carlo simulation. For continuous $F$, the test remains approximately distribution-free in the sense that the limiting law of the standardized process does not depend on $F$.

## Statistical investigation

1. **Distribution-free property**: For large $n$, the standardized empirical process converges to a Brownian bridge divided by $\sqrt{t(1-t)}$, independent of the underlying $F$. Hence, the test is asymptotically distribution-free within $[\varepsilon, 1-\varepsilon]$.
2. **Power considerations**: The weighting increases sensitivity to deviations near the boundaries, which the classical KS test underestimates. For detecting tail anomalies, $K_{n,\varepsilon}^\ast$ is more powerful.
3. **Pathologies**: Without truncation, the supremum diverges almost surely. Hence, untruncated $K_n^\ast$ is not usable, and truncation is mandatory.
4. **Practical use**: Choose $\varepsilon$ small relative to $1/n$ to capture most data while avoiding divergence. Alternative weightings $w(t)$ with bounded interior and $w(t) \sim (t(1-t))^{-1/2}$ near the endpoints can regularize the process.

## Verification

- The truncation ensures the supremum is finite almost surely.
- The variance-standardization matches the binomial variance of $F_n(x)$.
- Donsker's theorem applies to the standardized process over compact $[\varepsilon,1-\varepsilon]$.
- The divergence as $\varepsilon \to 0$ is rigorously justified using the law of iterated logarithm for Brownian bridge.
- Increased sensitivity in the tails is consistent with the variance structure.

This completes the investigation.

$\displaystyle \blacksquare$
