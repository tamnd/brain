**Exercise 9.** &#9654; [*28*] [28] The experiment described in the text consisted of plotting 20 values of $K_{10}^+$, computed from the maximum-of-5 test applied to different parts of a random sequence. We could have computed also the corresponding 20 values of $K_{10}^-$; since $K_{10}^-$ has the same distribution as $K_{10}^+$, we could lump together the 40 values thus obtained (that is, 20 of the $K_{10}^+$'s and 20 of the $K_{10}^-$'s), and a KS test could be applied so that we would get new values $K_{40}^+$, $K_{40}^-$. Discuss the merits of this idea.

Let the 20 values of $K_{10}^+$ be

$$
X_1,\dots,X_{20},
$$

and let the corresponding 20 values of $K_{10}^-$ be

$$
Y_1,\dots,Y_{20}.
$$

Since $K_{10}^-$ has the same distribution as $K_{10}^+$, it is tempting to regard the combined sample

$$
X_1,\dots,X_{20},Y_1,\dots,Y_{20}
$$

as 40 observations from the common distribution of $K_{10}^+$, and then apply a second-level KS test to obtain $K_{40}^+$ and $K_{40}^-$.

The apparent advantage is clear. A KS test based on $40$ observations is more sensitive than one based on $20$ observations. If the 40 observations were independent, the empirical distribution would fluctuate less, and departures from the theoretical distribution would be easier to detect. Thus one might hope for a more powerful test.

However, the crucial issue is independence. The values $X_i$ and $Y_i$ are computed from the same underlying block of random numbers. In fact, for a fixed sample,

$$
K_n^+ = \max_x \bigl(F_n(x)-F(x)\bigr), \qquad
K_n^- = \max_x \bigl(F(x)-F_n(x)\bigr),
$$

so $K_n^+$ and $K_n^-$ are not independent random variables. They are two statistics extracted from the same empirical distribution function. Knowledge of one gives information about the other.

Therefore the 40 observations are not 40 independent draws from the common distribution. Rather, they come in 20 correlated pairs

$$
(X_i,Y_i).
$$

The standard KS distribution for $K_{40}^\pm$ assumes 40 independent observations. Applying that distribution to the lumped sample would overstate the effective sample size and yield significance levels that are not correct.

There is still some value in examining both $K_{10}^+$ and $K_{10}^-$. Since they probe deviations in opposite directions, using both statistics extracts more information from each experiment than using $K_{10}^+$ alone. But they should not simply be counted as 40 independent observations in a second KS test. Any valid combined analysis would have to take the dependence between $K_{10}^+$ and $K_{10}^-$ into account.

Hence the merit of the idea is limited. It gains additional information by using both one-sided KS statistics, but the naive procedure of lumping the 20 values of $K_{10}^+$ and the 20 values of $K_{10}^-$ into a sample of size 40 is statistically questionable because the paired observations are dependent. The resulting $K_{40}^+$ and $K_{40}^-$ would not have the usual KS null distribution.
