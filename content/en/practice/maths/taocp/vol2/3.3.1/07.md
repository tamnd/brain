**Exercise 7.** [*M16*] Prove that $K_n^+$ and $K_n^-$ can never be negative. What is the largest possible value $K_n^-$ can have?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 5m42s  

---

## Solution

In Section 3.3.1 the Kolmogorov-Smirnov statistics are defined by

$$
K_n^+ = \sqrt n \max_{1\le j\le n}
\left(\frac{j}{n}-F(X_{(j)})\right),
$$

$$
K_n^- = \sqrt n \max_{1\le j\le n}
\left(F(X_{(j)})-\frac{j-1}{n}\right),
$$

where

$$
X_{(1)}\le X_{(2)}\le \cdots \le X_{(n)}
$$

are the observations arranged in increasing order.

To prove that $K_n^+$ and $K_n^-$ cannot be negative, consider the quantities whose maxima define them.

For $K_n^+$, taking $j=n$ gives

$$
\frac{n}{n}-F(X_{(n)})
=
1-F(X_{(n)}).
$$

Since every distribution function satisfies $F(x)\le 1$,

$$
1-F(X_{(n)})\ge 0.
$$

Therefore the maximum over all $j$ is at least $0$, hence

$$
K_n^+\ge 0.
$$

For $K_n^-$, taking $j=1$ gives

$$
F(X_{(1)})-\frac{0}{n}
=
F(X_{(1)}).
$$

Since $F(x)\ge 0$ for every $x$,

$$
F(X_{(1)})\ge 0.
$$

Therefore the maximum over all $j$ is at least $0$, hence

$$
K_n^-\ge 0.
$$

Thus neither statistic can be negative.

To determine the largest possible value of $K_n^-$, observe that for every $j$,

$$
F(X_{(j)})-\frac{j-1}{n}
\le
1-\frac{j-1}{n},
$$

because $F(X_{(j)})\le 1$.

Since

$$
1-\frac{j-1}{n}
=
\frac{n-j+1}{n},
$$

its largest value occurs when $j=1$, giving

$$
F(X_{(j)})-\frac{j-1}{n}\le 1.
$$

Hence

$$
K_n^-
=
\sqrt n
\max_j\left(F(X_{(j)})-\frac{j-1}{n}\right)
\le
\sqrt n.
$$

This upper bound is attainable. Choose all observations equal to a point $x_0$ for which

$$
F(x_0)=1.
$$

Then

$$
F(X_{(1)})-\frac{0}{n}=1,
$$

so the maximum in the definition of $K_n^-$ equals $1$. Therefore

$$
K_n^-=\sqrt n.
$$

Consequently the largest possible value of $K_n^-$ is

$$
\boxed{\sqrt n}.
$$

This completes the proof.

∎
