Let $X_1,\ldots,X_n$ be independent observations from a distribution function $F$, and let

$$
F_n(x)=\frac1n\#\{j:X_j\le x\}.
$$

The one-sided Kolmogorov statistics are

$$
K_n^+=\sqrt n\,\sup_x\bigl(F_n(x)-F(x)\bigr),
\qquad
K_n^-=\sqrt n\,\sup_x\bigl(F(x)-F_n(x)\bigr).
$$

When $F$ is continuous, $K_n^+$ and $K_n^-$ have asymptotically distribution-free limits. The purpose of this exercise is to investigate what happens when $F$ has jumps.

## 1. General behavior at a jump

Suppose $F$ has a jump of size

$$
p=F(a)-F(a^-)>0
$$

at the point $a$. Let

$$
N_a=\#\{j:X_j=a\}.
$$

Then

$$
N_a\sim\operatorname{Bin}(n,p),
$$

and

$$
F_n(a)-F_n(a^-)=\frac{N_a}{n}.
$$

Since

$$
\frac{N_a}{n}=p+O_p(n^{-1/2}),
$$

the empirical jump converges to the true jump with fluctuations of order

$n^{-1/2}$.

At a discontinuity the quantity $F_n(x)-F(x)$ changes abruptly as $x$

passes through $a$. Writing

$$
\Delta_n=\frac{N_a}{n}-p,
$$

we obtain

$$
\sqrt n\,\Delta_n
\Longrightarrow N(0,p(1-p)).
$$

Hence the dominant contribution to $K_n^+$ and $K_n^-$ near a jump is a normal fluctuation whose variance depends on the jump size $p$.

Therefore the limiting distributions of $K_n^+$ and $K_n^-$ are no longer universal. They depend on the locations and sizes of the jumps of $F$.

This explains why the usual KS critical values are not valid for discontinuous distributions.

## 2. Purely discrete distributions

Consider first a distribution supported on finitely many points

$$
a_1<a_2<\cdots<a_m,
$$

with probabilities

$$
p_i=P(X=a_i).
$$

Define

$$
S_i=\sum_{j\le i}p_j,
\qquad
N_i=\#\{X_r=a_i\}.
$$

Then

$$
F(a_i)=S_i,
$$

and

$$
F_n(a_i)=\frac1n\sum_{j\le i}N_j.
$$

Consequently

$$
\sqrt n\bigl(F_n(a_i)-F(a_i)\bigr)
=
\frac{\sum_{j\le i}(N_j-np_j)}{\sqrt n}.
$$

The vector of these quantities converges to a multivariate normal distribution determined by $p_1,\ldots,p_m$.

Hence

$$
K_n^+
$$

converges to the maximum of finitely many correlated normal variables, and similarly for

$$
K_n^-.
$$

The limiting law depends explicitly on the probabilities $p_i$.

For example, if

$$
P(X=0)=P(X=1)=\tfrac12,
$$

then

$$
F(x)=
\begin{cases}
0,&x<0,\\[2mm]
\tfrac12,&0\le x<1,\\[2mm]
1,&x\ge1.
\end{cases}
$$

Let $N$ be the number of zeros. Then

$$
N\sim\operatorname{Bin}(n,\tfrac12),
$$

and

$$
K_n^+=K_n^-=
\frac{|N-n/2|}{\sqrt n}.
$$

Therefore

$$
K_n^\pm
\Longrightarrow
\frac12|Z|,
$$

where $Z\sim N(0,1)$.

This limit is completely different from the usual Kolmogorov distribution.

## 3. Mixed continuous-discrete distributions

Suppose

$$
F(x)=q\,G(x)+(1-q)\,H(x),
$$

where $G$ is continuous and $H$ is discrete.

The empirical process then has two components.

1. A Brownian-bridge type contribution coming from the continuous part.
2. Normal fluctuations attached to the discrete jumps.

The supremum defining $K_n^\pm$ is governed by whichever component is larger.

Large jumps tend to dominate. If a jump of size $p$ is substantial, its fluctuation has standard deviation

$$
\sqrt{p(1-p)},
$$

and the maximum discrepancy often occurs at that jump rather than in the continuous regions.

Thus the asymptotic law depends on the entire jump structure of $F$.

## 4. Consequences for testing

Suppose the null hypothesis is that the observations follow a specified discontinuous distribution $F$.

If one nevertheless applies the ordinary KS test using the continuous-distribution critical values, the nominal significance level is generally incorrect.

The reason is that the true distribution of $K_n^\pm$ is no longer the Kolmogorov distribution.

Depending on the jump pattern, the test may be either conservative or liberal.

Therefore a KS test for a discrete null distribution requires either:

1. exact computation of the null distribution;
2. Monte Carlo calibration; or
3. modified critical values.

## 5. Comparison with the chi-square test

For a discrete distribution with categories $a_1,\ldots,a_m$, the Pearson statistic is

$$
\chi^2
=
\sum_{i=1}^m
\frac{(N_i-np_i)^2}{np_i}.
$$

The chi-square test uses all category counts simultaneously.

By contrast, $K_n^+$ and $K_n^-$ depend only on the largest cumulative discrepancy.

Hence the two procedures respond differently to alternatives.

### Localized alternatives

Suppose one category probability is substantially wrong while the others are nearly correct.

Then the KS statistics detect the resulting large cumulative jump efficiently.

In such cases the KS and chi-square tests have comparable power.

### Oscillatory alternatives

Suppose the probabilities alternate above and below their null values:

$$
p_i' = p_i+\delta_i,
\qquad
\sum_i\delta_i=0,
$$

with successive $\delta_i$ having alternating signs.

The cumulative errors partially cancel, so the KS statistics may remain small.

The chi-square statistic, however, accumulates the squared deviations and is usually more sensitive.

Therefore the chi-square test is generally superior for discrete multinomial alternatives.

### Extreme concentration alternatives

If excess probability mass is concentrated in one tail, the cumulative discrepancy becomes large and the KS statistics can be quite effective.

In this situation the KS test may perform nearly as well as the chi-square test.

## 6. Conclusions

For discontinuous $F$, the statistics $K_n^+$ and $K_n^-$ remain well defined, but their distribution is no longer distribution-free. The asymptotic law depends on the sizes and locations of the jumps of $F$.

For a purely discrete distribution, $K_n^+$ and $K_n^-$ converge to maxima of correlated normal variables determined by the category probabilities. In mixed distributions, both Brownian-bridge fluctuations and jump fluctuations contribute.

Consequently the standard KS critical values are invalid. When testing a discrete null distribution, the chi-square test is usually preferable because it exploits all cell counts and generally has greater power against multinomial alternatives. The KS statistics are most competitive when the alternative produces a pronounced cumulative discrepancy, especially in one tail.
