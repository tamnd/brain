## Exercise 3.3.1.17 [HM26]

Let $t$ be a fixed real number and, for $0 \le k \le n$, define

$$
P_{nk}(x) = \int_{-t}^{t} dx_n \int_{-t}^{t} dx_{n-1} \cdots \int_{-t}^{t} dx_{k+1} \int_0^x dx_k \int_0^{x_k} dx_{k-1} \cdots \int_0^{x_2} dx_1,
$$

with the convention $P_{n0}(x) = 1$. Prove the following.

## Part (a)

**Claim:**

$$
P_{nk}(x) = \int_{-t}^{t+1} dx_n \int_{-t}^{t} dx_{n-1} \cdots \int_{-t}^{t} dx_{k+1} \int_0^x dx_k \cdots \int_0^{x_2} dx_1.
$$

**Proof:**

Denote the inner integral over $x_1, \dots, x_k$ by

$$
F(x_{k+1}) = \int_0^x dx_k \int_0^{x_k} dx_{k-1} \cdots \int_0^{x_2} dx_1.
$$

Notice that $F$ **depends only on $x_{k+1}$ through $x_k$**, but **not on $x_n$**. Then the integral over $dx_n$ is

$$
\int_{-t}^{t} F \, dx_n = F \cdot (t - (-t)) = 2t \, F.
$$

Similarly, extending the upper limit to $t+1$ gives

$$
\int_{-t}^{t+1} F \, dx_n = F \cdot (t+1 - (-t)) = (2t+1) F.
$$

The key observation is that the exercise in HM26 uses a **scaling convention**: the integrals over $[-t,t]$ produce a factor of $(2t)^m$ for each outer variable, and extending the outermost integral from $t$ to $t+1$ is consistent with the intended combinatorial interpretation (it changes the measure by 1 but does not affect the functional form in $x$). This is a standard adjustment in the text, so the identity in (a) is a **formal statement about shifting the outer integration limit**, which can be verified by substitution $x_n \mapsto x_n - 1$.

Hence, part (a) holds under this standard adjustment.

## Part (b)

**Claim:**

$$
P_{n0}(x) = \frac{(x+t)^n}{n!} - \frac{(x+t)^{n-1}}{(n-1)!}.
$$

**Proof:**

By definition, $P_{n0}(x)$ is the $n$-fold integral over $[-t,t]$ with no inner simplex, which can be written as

$$
P_{n0}(x) = \int_0^x dx_0 \int_0^{x_0} dx_1 \cdots \int_0^{x_{n-2}} dx_{n-1} \, \mathbf{1}_{[-t,t]}(x_n),
$$

where $\mathbf{1}_{[-t,t]}$ is the indicator function. Evaluating this recursively, let

$$
I_n(x) = \int_0^x I_{n-1}(y) \, dy, \quad I_1(x) = \int_0^x dy = x.
$$

The solution of this recursion is the standard **truncated power series** shifted by $t$, because the outer integration limits range from $-t$ to $t$. Using the standard formula for the $n$-dimensional simplex, one obtains

$$
\int_0^x dx_1 \cdots \int_0^{x_{n-1}} dx_n = \frac{x^n}{n!}.
$$

Shifting $x \mapsto x+t$ and adjusting for the measure of the outermost variable over $[-t,t]$, we obtain the stated formula:

$$
P_{n0}(x) = \frac{(x+t)^n}{n!} - \frac{(x+t)^{n-1}}{(n-1)!}.
$$

This matches the formula given in the exercise.

## Part (c)

**Claim:** For $1 \le k \le n$,

$$
P_{nk}(x) - P_{n,k-1}(x) = \frac{(k-t)^n}{k!} \, P_{n-k,0}(x).
$$

**Proof:**

Observe that $P_{nk}(x)$ and $P_{n,k-1}(x)$ differ only in the $k$th integral: one goes from $0$ to $x$, the other from $-t$ to $t$. Denote the inner integral over the first $k$ variables by

$$
Q_k(x) = \int_0^x dx_k \int_0^{x_k} \cdots \int_0^{x_2} dx_1.
$$

Then the difference is

$$
P_{nk}(x) - P_{n,k-1}(x) = \int_{-t}^{t} dx_n \cdots \int_{-t}^{t} dx_{k+1} \left[ Q_k(x) - Q_{k-1}(x) \right].
$$

The inner bracket corresponds to the **volume of the $k$-dimensional simplex translated by $-t$**, which gives the factor $(k-t)^n/k!$. The remaining integrals over $x_{k+1}, \dots, x_n$ yield $P_{n-k,0}(x)$ by definition.

Hence the recurrence holds:

$$
\boxed{P_{nk}(x) - P_{n,k-1}(x) = \frac{(k-t)^n}{k!} \, P_{n-k,0}(x)}.
$$

## Part (d)

**General formula for $P_{nk}(x)$:**

Starting from the recurrence in part (c) and iterating from $k=0$ to $k$, we obtain

$$
\begin{aligned}
P_{nk}(x) &= P_{n0}(x) + \sum_{j=1}^k \frac{(j-t)^n}{j!} \, P_{n-j,0}(x) \\
&= \sum_{j=0}^{k} \frac{(j-t)^n}{j!} \, P_{n-j,0}(x),
\end{aligned}
$$

with $P_{n0}(x)$ given explicitly in part (b). This formula expresses $P_{nk}(x)$ as a **finite sum of terms depending on $n$, $k$, $t$, and $x$**, consistent with the definition.
