**Exercise 14.** &#9654; [*HM36*] [HM36] Let $Z_k$ be given by Eq. (18). Show directly by using Stirling's approximation that the multinomial probability

$$n! p_1^{k_1} \cdots p_k^{k_k} / Y_1! \cdots Y_k! = e^{-V/2} / \sqrt{(2\pi)^{k-1} p_1 \cdots p_k} + O(n^{-k/2}),$$

if $Z_1, Z_2, \ldots, Z_k$ are bounded as $n \to \infty$. (This idea leads to a proof of the chi-square test that is much closer to "first principles," and requires less handwaving, than the derivation in the text.)

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 13m02s  

---

Let

$$
Y_i=np_i+\sqrt{np_i}\,Z_i ,
$$

where $Z_i$ is defined by Eq. (18), and assume that $Z_1,\ldots,Z_k$ remain bounded as $n\to\infty$. Since

$$
\sum_{i=1}^kY_i=n,\qquad \sum_{i=1}^k p_i=1,
$$

we have

$$
\sum_{i=1}^k\sqrt{p_i}\,Z_i=0.
$$

The multinomial probability is

$$
P=\frac{n!}{Y_1!\cdots Y_k!}p_1^{Y_1}\cdots p_k^{Y_k}.
$$

We shall derive its asymptotic form directly from Stirling's formula.

Using

$$
m!=\sqrt{2\pi m}\left(\frac me\right)^m+O(m^{-1/2}m^m e^{-m}),
$$

uniformly for $m\to\infty$, we obtain

$$
P
=
\frac{\sqrt{2\pi n}(n/e)^n}
{\prod_{i=1}^k\sqrt{2\pi Y_i}(Y_i/e)^{Y_i}}
\,p_1^{Y_1}\cdots p_k^{Y_k}
+O(n^{-k/2}).
$$

Since $\sum Y_i=n$, the exponential factors cancel:

$$
P
=
(2\pi)^{-(k-1)/2}
\frac{\sqrt n}{\sqrt{Y_1\cdots Y_k}}
\prod_{i=1}^k
\left(\frac{np_i}{Y_i}\right)^{Y_i}
+O(n^{-k/2}).
$$

Introduce

$$
\delta_i=\frac{Y_i-np_i}{np_i}
=\frac{Z_i}{\sqrt{np_i}},
$$

so that $\delta_i=O(n^{-1/2})$. Then

$$
Y_i=np_i(1+\delta_i).
$$

Hence

$$
\frac{\sqrt n}{\sqrt{Y_1\cdots Y_k}}
=
n^{-(k-1)/2}
(p_1\cdots p_k)^{-1/2}
\prod_{i=1}^k(1+\delta_i)^{-1/2}.
$$

Also,

$$
\prod_{i=1}^k
\left(\frac{np_i}{Y_i}\right)^{Y_i}
=
\exp\!\left(
-\sum_{i=1}^k Y_i\log(1+\delta_i)
\right).
$$

Therefore

$$
P=
\frac{1}{\sqrt{(2\pi n)^{k-1}p_1\cdots p_k}}
\,
\exp\!\left(
-\sum_{i=1}^k Y_i\log(1+\delta_i)
\right)
\prod_{i=1}^k(1+\delta_i)^{-1/2}
+O(n^{-k/2}).
$$

We now expand the logarithm. Since $\delta_i=O(n^{-1/2})$,

$$
\log(1+\delta_i)
=
\delta_i-\frac{\delta_i^2}{2}
+O(\delta_i^3).
$$

Thus

$$
Y_i\log(1+\delta_i)
=
np_i(1+\delta_i)
\left(
\delta_i-\frac{\delta_i^2}{2}
\right)
+O(n\delta_i^3).
$$

Because $n\delta_i^3=O(n^{-1/2})$,

$$
Y_i\log(1+\delta_i)
=
np_i\delta_i+\frac12 np_i\delta_i^2
+O(n^{-1/2}).
$$

Summing,

$$
\sum_{i=1}^kY_i\log(1+\delta_i)
=
n\sum_{i=1}^k p_i\delta_i
+\frac12 n\sum_{i=1}^k p_i\delta_i^2
+O(n^{-1/2}).
$$

Now

$$
\sum p_i\delta_i
=
\frac1{\sqrt n}\sum \sqrt{p_i}\,Z_i
=0,
$$

hence

$$
\sum_{i=1}^kY_i\log(1+\delta_i)
=
\frac12
\sum_{i=1}^k Z_i^2
+O(n^{-1/2}).
$$

By the definition of Pearson's statistic,

$$
V=\sum_{i=1}^k\frac{(Y_i-np_i)^2}{np_i}
=\sum_{i=1}^k Z_i^2.
$$

Therefore

$$
\exp\!\left(
-\sum_{i=1}^kY_i\log(1+\delta_i)
\right)
=
e^{-V/2}\bigl(1+O(n^{-1/2})\bigr).
$$

For the remaining factor,

$$
\prod_{i=1}^k(1+\delta_i)^{-1/2}
=
1+O(n^{-1/2}),
$$

since each $\delta_i=O(n^{-1/2})$.

Combining these estimates gives

$$
P=
\frac{e^{-V/2}}
{\sqrt{(2\pi n)^{k-1}p_1\cdots p_k}}
\Bigl(1+O(n^{-1/2})\Bigr)
+O(n^{-k/2}).
$$

Since

$$
\frac{1}{\sqrt{(2\pi n)^{k-1}}}O(n^{-1/2})
=
O(n^{-k/2}),
$$

the error term may be absorbed, yielding

$$
P
=
\frac{e^{-V/2}}
{\sqrt{(2\pi n)^{k-1}p_1\cdots p_k}}
+O(n^{-k/2}).
$$

Equivalently,

$$
n^{(k-1)/2}P
=
\frac{e^{-V/2}}
{\sqrt{(2\pi)^{k-1}p_1\cdots p_k}}
+O(n^{-1/2}),
$$

which is the desired asymptotic formula. ∎
