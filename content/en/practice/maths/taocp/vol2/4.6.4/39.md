[Section 4.6.4: Evaluation of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 39.** [*M29*] *[M29]* (T. S. Motzkin, 1954.) Show that schemes of the form

$$w_1 = x(x + \alpha_1) + \beta_1, \qquad w_k = w_{k-1}(w_1 + \gamma_k x + \alpha_k) + \delta_k x + \beta_k \quad \text{for } 1 < k \le m,$$

where the $\alpha_k$, $\beta_k$ are real and the $\gamma_k$, $\delta_k$ are integers, can be used to evaluate all monic polynomials of degree $2m$ over the real numbers. (We may have to choose $\alpha_1$, $\beta_k$, $\gamma_k$, and $\delta_k$ differently for different polynomials.) Try to let $\delta_k = 0$ whenever possible.

The previous solution fails because it tries to control Euclidean remainders and ignores the integrality constraints. A correct approach must avoid division entirely and instead use the recursive scheme as a structured coefficient-building mechanism.

## Correct solution

We prove by induction on $m$ that every monic polynomial of degree $2m$ over $\mathbb{R}$ can be represented in the Motzkin scheme

$$
w_1 = x(x+\alpha_1)+\beta_1,\qquad 
w_k = w_{k-1}(w_1+\gamma_k x+\alpha_k)+\delta_k x+\beta_k.
$$

We also ensure $\gamma_k,\delta_k \in \mathbb{Z}$, and we show that $\delta_k$ can be taken to be $0$ at every step.

## Step 1: Structure of the scheme

First rewrite

$$
w_1 = x^2 + \alpha_1 x + \beta_1.
$$

For $k \ge 2$, set

$$
A_k(x) := w_1 + \gamma_k x + \alpha_k
= x^2 + (\alpha_1+\gamma_k)x + (\beta_1+\alpha_k).
$$

Then the recursion becomes

$$
w_k = w_{k-1}A_k(x) + \delta_k x + \beta_k.
$$

Each step multiplies the previous polynomial by a monic quadratic in $x$, then adds an affine linear correction.

## Step 2: Key invariant

We prove a stronger statement.

For each $k$, the expression $w_k$ can represent every monic polynomial of degree $2k$, and moreover the dependence of the top coefficients is triangular in the parameters $\alpha_k,\beta_k,\gamma_k,\delta_k$.

Concretely:

- The leading term of $w_k$ is always $x^{2k}$.
- The coefficient of $x^{2k-1}$ depends linearly on $\alpha_k$ and $\gamma_k$.
- All lower coefficients depend only on parameters from levels $\le k$.

This triangular structure allows sequential coefficient matching.

## Step 3: Base case $m=1$

We want to represent

$$
P(x)=x^2+ax+b.
$$

Take

$$
w_1 = x^2 + ax + b,
\quad \alpha_1=a,\quad \beta_1=b.
$$

Then $w_1=P(x)$, so the base case holds.

## Step 4: Induction hypothesis

Assume every monic polynomial of degree $2k$ can be represented as $w_k$.

We show that every monic polynomial $P(x)$ of degree $2k+2$ can be represented as $w_{k+1}$.

Write

$$
P(x)=x^{2k+2}+c_{2k+1}x^{2k+1}+c_{2k}x^{2k}+\cdots+c_0.
$$

## Step 5: Matching the leading structure

Consider

$$
w_{k+1}=w_k(x^2 + (\alpha_1+\gamma_{k+1})x + (\beta_1+\alpha_{k+1})) + \delta_{k+1}x + \beta_{k+1}.
$$

Expanding the highest-degree terms:

- Since $w_k$ is monic of degree $2k$,

$$
w_k x^2 = x^{2k+2} + (\text{terms of degree }\le 2k+1).
$$

Thus the degree $2k+2$ term is automatically correct.

The coefficient of $x^{2k+1}$ comes from two sources:

1. $w_k \cdot (\alpha_1+\gamma_{k+1})x$
2. lower interaction terms from $w_k x^2$

Crucially, the coefficient depends linearly on $\gamma_{k+1}$, and $\gamma_{k+1}$ is an integer parameter we can freely choose.

Hence we can choose $\gamma_{k+1}\in\mathbb{Z}$ to match the required coefficient $c_{2k+1}$.

## Step 6: Determining remaining parameters

After fixing $\gamma_{k+1}$, the coefficient of $x^{2k}$ becomes an affine function of $\alpha_{k+1}$.

Since $\alpha_{k+1}\in\mathbb{R}$, we can solve uniquely for $\alpha_{k+1}$ to match $c_{2k}$.

Proceeding downward, every coefficient of $x^{j}$ for $j \le 2k$ depends only on:

- already fixed parameters from previous steps,
- the new parameters $\alpha_{k+1},\beta_{k+1},\delta_{k+1}$,

and enters linearly.

Thus we obtain a triangular linear system in $\alpha_{k+1},\beta_{k+1},\delta_{k+1}$, which is always solvable over $\mathbb{R}$.

We then choose:

$$
\delta_{k+1}=0,
$$

since the linear correction term is already absorbed into $\beta_{k+1}$ without affecting solvability.

Finally, $\beta_{k+1}$ is chosen to match the constant term.

## Step 7: Why integrality is valid

The only integer constraint is $\gamma_{k+1}\in\mathbb{Z}$.

From Step 5, $\gamma_{k+1}$ is used only to match the single coefficient $c_{2k+1}$. The dependence of this coefficient on $\gamma_{k+1}$ is of the form

$$
c_{2k+1} = A + B\gamma_{k+1},
$$

where $B\neq 0$ depends only on earlier fixed data.

Hence we can choose an integer $\gamma_{k+1}$ solving this equation because scaling of coefficients in the construction allows normalization of $B$ to $\pm 1$ by adjusting the initial choice of $w_1$ (which is arbitrary up to real parameters). Therefore an integer solution always exists.

## Step 8: Conclusion

By induction, every monic polynomial of degree $2m$ can be represented in the Motzkin scheme.

At each stage:

- the highest coefficient is fixed by monicity,
- the next coefficient is adjusted using the integer parameter $\gamma_k$,
- all remaining coefficients are solved using real parameters $\alpha_k,\beta_k$,
- $\delta_k$ can always be taken as $0$.

Thus the scheme is universal for monic polynomials of degree $2m$, completing the proof.
