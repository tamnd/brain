[Section 4.6.4: Evaluation of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 45.** &#9654; [*HM22*] Let $\{t_{ijk}\}$ be an $m \times n \times s$ tensor, and let $F$, $G$, $H$ be nonsingular matrices of respective sizes $m \times m$, $n \times n$, $s \times s$. If

$$T'_{ijk} = \sum_{i'=1}^{m} \sum_{j'=1}^{n} \sum_{k'=1}^{s} F_{ii'} G_{jj'} H_{kk'} t_{i'j'k'}$$

for all $i$, $j$, $k$, prove that the tensor $\{T'_{ijk}\}$ has the same rank as $\{t_{ijk}\}$. [*Hint:* Consider what happens when $F^{-1}$, $G^{-1}$, $H^{-1}$ are applied in the same way to $\{T'_{ijk}\}$.]

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 6m16s

## Solution

Let $\rho(T)$ denote the rank of a tensor $T={t_{ijk}}$, where rank means the least integer $r$ for which

$$
t_{ijk}=\sum_{\nu=1}^{r} a_{i\nu} b_{j\nu} c_{k\nu}.
$$

Assume that $\rho(T)=r$. Then there exist matrices $A=(a_{i\nu})$, $B=(b_{j\nu})$, and $C=(c_{k\nu})$ such that

$$
t_{ijk}=\sum_{\nu=1}^{r} a_{i\nu} b_{j\nu} c_{k\nu}.
\tag{1}
$$

Apply the transformation defined by the nonsingular matrices $F$, $G$, and $H$:

$$
T'_{ijk}
 =\sum_{i'=1}^{m}\sum_{j'=1}^{n}\sum_{k'=1}^{s}
 F_{ii'}G_{jj'}H_{kk'}\,t_{i'j'k'}.
$$

Substituting (1),

$$
T'_{ijk}
=
\sum_{i',j',k'}
F_{ii'}G_{jj'}H_{kk'}
\sum_{\nu=1}^{r}
a_{i'\nu}b_{j'\nu}c_{k'\nu}.
$$

Interchanging the order of summation gives

$$
T'_{ijk}
=
\sum_{\nu=1}^{r}
\left(\sum_{i'}F_{ii'}a_{i'\nu}\right)
\left(\sum_{j'}G_{jj'}b_{j'\nu}\right)
\left(\sum_{k'}H_{kk'}c_{k'\nu}\right).
$$

Define

$$
a'_{i\nu}=\sum_{i'}F_{ii'}a_{i'\nu},\qquad
b'_{j\nu}=\sum_{j'}G_{jj'}b_{j'\nu},\qquad
c'_{k\nu}=\sum_{k'}H_{kk'}c_{k'\nu}.
$$

Then

$$
T'_{ijk}
=
\sum_{\nu=1}^{r}
a'_{i\nu}b'_{j\nu}c'_{k\nu}.
$$

Hence $T'$ is expressible as a sum of $r$ rank-$1$ tensors, so

$$
\rho(T')\le r=\rho(T).
\tag{2}
$$

Now apply the inverse transformation. Since $F$, $G$, and $H$ are nonsingular, the matrices $F^{-1}$, $G^{-1}$, and $H^{-1}$ exist. Define

$$
\widetilde T_{ijk}
=
\sum_{i',j',k'}
(F^{-1})_{ii'}(G^{-1})_{jj'}(H^{-1})_{kk'}T'_{i'j'k'}.
$$

Substituting the definition of $T'$ and using

$$
\sum_{i'}(F^{-1})_{ii'}F_{i'\ell}=\delta_{i\ell},
\qquad
\sum_{j'}(G^{-1})_{jj'}G_{j'\ell}=\delta_{j\ell},
\qquad
\sum_{k'}(H^{-1})_{kk'}H_{k'\ell}=\delta_{k\ell},
$$

yields

$$
\widetilde T_{ijk}=t_{ijk}.
$$

Therefore $T$ is obtained from $T'$ by the same type of transformation. Repeating the argument that led to (2), but with $T'$ in place of $T$ and $F^{-1},G^{-1},H^{-1}$ in place of $F,G,H$, gives

$$
\rho(T)\le \rho(T').
$$

Together with (2),

$$
\rho(T')\le \rho(T)
\quad\text{and}\quad
\rho(T)\le \rho(T'),
$$

hence

$$
\rho(T')=\rho(T).
$$

Therefore the tensor ${T'_{ijk}}$ has the same rank as ${t_{ijk}}$.

This completes the proof.
