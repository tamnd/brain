[Section 4.6.4: Evaluation of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 73.** [*HM25*] (J. Morgenstern, 1973.) Prove that any polynomial chain for the discrete Fourier transform (§3.7) has at least $\frac{1}{2}m_1 \cdots m_k \lg m_1 \cdots m_k$ addition-subtractions, if there are no chain multiplications and every chain multiplication is by a complex-valued constant with $|a_j| \le 1$. *Hint:* Consider the matrices of the linear transformations computed by the first $k$ steps. How big can their determinants be?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m46s  

---

## Solution

Let $N = m_1 \cdots m_k$ and consider a polynomial chain computing the discrete Fourier transform as a linear transformation

$y = Fx,$

where $F$ is the $N \times N$ Fourier matrix with entries $\omega^{ij}$, $\omega = e^{2\pi i/N}$.

The computation starts from the input variables $x_1,\ldots,x_N$. At every step of the chain, a new variable is formed from previously constructed variables by either an addition, subtraction, or multiplication by a constant $a$ with $|a| \le 1$. Each intermediate variable is therefore a complex linear form in the original inputs.

Let $v^{(t)}_1,\ldots,v^{(t)}_N \in \mathbb{C}^N$ denote the coefficient vectors of the $N$ variables available after $t$ steps, written in the basis $x_1,\ldots,x_N$. Each $v^{(t)}_j$ records the linear form represented by the $j$-th variable.

Initially,

$v^{(0)}_j = e_j,$

so every column vector has Euclidean norm $1$.

Each chain operation modifies one vector:

If $v \leftarrow a v$ with $|a| \le 1$, then $|v|$ does not increase.

If $v \leftarrow v_i \pm v_j$, then

$\|v\| \le \|v_i\| + \|v_j\| \le 2 \max(\|v_i\|,\|v_j\|).$

Define

$M_t = \max_j \|v^{(t)}_j\|.$

Then the previous inequalities give

$M_{t+1} \le 2 M_t,$

since only one column changes per step and its norm increases by at most a factor $2$.

With $M_0 = 1$, induction yields

$M_t \le 2^t.$

Now form the $N \times N$ matrix $A_t$ whose columns are any $N$ of the vectors $v^{(t)}_j$. In particular, at the end of the computation, one such matrix is the Fourier matrix $F$ itself, since the output variables are linear forms in the inputs.

By Hadamard’s inequality,

$|\det A_t| \le \prod_{j=1}^N \| \text{column}_j(A_t)\| \le (2^t)^N = 2^{tN}.$

For the Fourier matrix,

$|\det F| = N^{N/2},$

a standard evaluation of the determinant of the discrete Fourier matrix.

Therefore,

$N^{N/2} \le 2^{tN}.$

Taking binary logarithms,

$\frac{N}{2} \log_2 N \le tN,$

hence

$t \ge \frac{1}{2} N \log_2 N.$

Since every chain addition or subtraction corresponds to one step of the process, any such polynomial chain computing the discrete Fourier transform requires at least

$\frac{1}{2} m_1 \cdots m_k \log_2(m_1 \cdots m_k)$

addition-subtractions.

This completes the proof. ∎
