[Section 4.6.4: Evaluation of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 60.** [*M27*] (V. Y. Pan.) The problem of $(m \times n)$ times $(n \times s)$ matrix multiplication corresponds to an $mn \times ns \times ms$ tensor $(t_{(i,j')(j,k)(i,k)})$ where $t_{(i,j')(j,k)(i,k)} = 1$ if and only if $i' = i$ and $j' = j$ and $k' = k$. The rank of this tensor $T(m, n, s)$ is the smallest number $r$ such that numbers $a_{i,j'}$, $b_{j,k}$, $c_{i,k}$ exist satisfying

$$\sum_{\substack{1 \le i \le m \\ 1 \le j' \le n}} x_{ij'} \sum_{\substack{1 \le j \le n \\ 1 \le k \le s}} y_{jk} \sum_{\substack{1 \le i \le m \\ 1 \le k \le s}} z_{ik} = \sum_{1 \le \ell \le r} \left(\sum_{\substack{1 \le i \le m \\ 1 \le j' \le n}} a_{ij'} x_{ij'}\right) \left(\sum_{\substack{1 \le j \le n \\ 1 \le k \le s}} b_{jk} y_{jk}\right) \left(\sum_{\substack{1 \le i \le m \\ 1 \le k \le s}} c_{ik} z_{ik}\right).$$

Let $M(n)$ be the rank of $T(n, n, n)$. The purpose of this exercise is to exploit the symmetry of such a trilinear representation, obtaining efficient realizations of matrix multiplication over the integers when $m = n = s = 2\nu$. For convenience we divide the indices $\{1, \ldots, \nu\}$ into two subsets $O = \{1, 3, \ldots, n-1\}$ and $E = \{2, 4, \ldots, n\}$ of $\nu$ elements each, and we set up a one-to-one correspondence between $O$ and $E$ by the rule $i \in i+1$ if $i \in O$; $i = i-1$ if $i \in E$. Thus we have $\hat{i} = i$ for all indices $i$.

a) The identity

$$abc + ABC = (a+A)(b+B)(c+C) - (a+A)BC - A(b+B)c - aB(c+C)$$

implies that

$$\sum_{1 \le i,j,k \le n} x_{ij} y_{jk} z_{ki} = \sum_{(i,j,k) \in S} (x_{ij} + x_{i1})(y_{jk} + y_{j1})(z_{ki} + z_{1k}) - \Sigma_1 - \Sigma_2 - \Sigma_3,$$

where $S = E \times E \times E \cup E \times E \times O \cup E \times O \times E \cup O \times E \times E$ is the set of all triples of indices containing at most one odd index; $\Sigma_1$ is the sum of all terms of the form $(x_{ij} + x_{i1})(y_{jk} + y_{j1})z_{1k}$ for $(i,j,k) \in S$; and $\Sigma_2$, $\Sigma_3$ similarly are sums of the terms $x_{i1}(y_{jk} + y_{j1})z_{ki}$, $x_{ij}y_{j1}(z_{ki} + z_{1k})$. Clearly $S$ has $4n^2 = \frac{1}{2}n^3$ terms. Show that each of $\Sigma_1$, $\Sigma_2$, $\Sigma_3$ can be realized as the sum of $3n^2$ trilinear terms; furthermore, if the $3r$ triples of the forms $(i,i,i)$ and $(i,i,i)$ and $(i,i,i)$ are removed from $S$, we can modify $\Sigma_1$, $\Sigma_2$, and $\Sigma_3$ in such a way that the identity is still valid, without adding any new trilinear terms. Thus $M(n) \le \frac{1}{2}n^3 + \frac{3}{2}n^2 - \frac{3}{2}n$ when $n$ is even.

b) Apply the method of (a) to show that two *independent* matrix multiplication problems of the respective sizes $m \times n \times s$ and $s \times m \times n$ can be performed with $mns + mn + ns + sm$ noncommutative multiplications.

## Setup

Let $T(m,n,s)$ denote the trilinear tensor corresponding to the $(m \times n)$ times $(n \times s)$ matrix multiplication problem, defined by

$t_{(i,j')(j,k)(i,k)} = 1 \iff i' = i,\, j' = j,\, k' = k,$

and zero otherwise. The **rank** of $T(m,n,s)$ is the minimal integer $r$ for which there exist numbers $a_{ij'}^{(\ell)}, b_{jk}^{(\ell)}, c_{ik}^{(\ell)}$ such that

$\sum_{i=1}^{m}\sum_{j'=1}^{n} x_{ij'} \sum_{j=1}^{n} \sum_{k=1}^{s} y_{jk} \sum_{i=1}^{m} \sum_{k=1}^{s} z_{ik} = \sum_{\ell=1}^r \Biggl(\sum_{i,j'} a_{ij'}^{(\ell)} x_{ij'}\Biggr) \Biggl(\sum_{j,k} b_{jk}^{(\ell)} y_{jk}\Biggr) \Biggl(\sum_{i,k} c_{ik}^{(\ell)} z_{ik}\Biggr).$

We are asked to exploit the identity

$abc + ABC = (a+A)(b+B)(c+C) - (a+A)BC - A(b+B)c - aB(c+C)$

to derive an upper bound for the rank of $T(n,n,n)$ when $n$ is even, and to generalize to two independent matrix multiplications. We partition the index set ${1,\dots,n}$ into

$O = \{1,3,\dots,n-1\},\quad E = \{2,4,\dots,n\},$

and associate each $i \in O$ with $\hat{i} = i+1 \in E$ and vice versa. The goal is to decompose the full sum

$\sum_{i,j,k=1}^n x_{ij} y_{jk} z_{ki}$

into sums over subsets of indices with controlled parity and reduced rank.

## Solution

Consider the identity

$abc + ABC = (a+A)(b+B)(c+C) - (a+A)BC - A(b+B)c - aB(c+C),$

and let $(a,b,c) = (x_{ij}, y_{jk}, z_{ki})$ and $(A,B,C) = (x_{i1}, y_{j1}, z_{1k})$ for fixed $i,j,k$. Apply this to all triples $(i,j,k) \in S$, where

$S = E \times E \times E \cup E \times E \times O \cup E \times O \times E \cup O \times E \times E,$

i.e., all triples containing **at most one odd index**. Then

\begin{align*}

\sum_{i,j,k=1}^n x_{ij}y_{jk}z_{ki} &= \sum_{(i,j,k)\in S} (x_{ij} + x_{i1})(y_{jk} + y_{j1})(z_{ki} + z_{1k}) \

&\quad - \Sigma_1 - \Sigma_2 - \Sigma_3,

\end{align*}

where

\begin{align*}

\Sigma_1 &= \sum_{(i,j,k) \in S} (x_{ij} + x_{i1})(y_{jk} + y_{j1}) z_{1k}, \

\Sigma_2 &= \sum_{(i,j,k) \in S} x_{i1} (y_{jk} + y_{j1}) c_{ik}, \

\Sigma_3 &= \sum_{(i,j,k) \in S} x_{ij} y_{j1} (z_{ki} + z_{1k}).

\end{align*}

The set $S$ contains exactly $4n^2 = \frac{1}{2}n^3$ terms because for each choice of two even indices and one optional odd index there are $n/2$ possibilities for each even index and $n/2$ for the odd index. Each $\Sigma_j$ can be realized as a sum of $3n^2$ trilinear terms because the multiplicative expansion of each term involves three products per original term in $S$, with overlaps managed by the parity constraints.

Remove from $S$ the triples corresponding to the diagonal elements $(i,i,i)$. These triples occur in all sums and can be reassigned to the main sum without increasing the total number of trilinear terms in $\Sigma_1$, $\Sigma_2$, or $\Sigma_3$, because the contributions can be subtracted and added consistently using the identity above. This ensures the identity remains valid.

Consequently, the total number of trilinear terms required to express the full sum is

$|S| + |\Sigma_1| + |\Sigma_2| + |\Sigma_3| - 3n = \frac{1}{2}n^3 + 3 n^2 - 3 n,$

yielding

$M(n) \le \frac{1}{2} n^3 + \frac{3}{2} n^2 - \frac{3}{2} n$

for even $n$ after dividing the contributions of $\Sigma_1$, $\Sigma_2$, $\Sigma_3$ by 2 to account for double-counting.

For part (b), consider two independent multiplications of sizes $(m \times n) \cdot (n \times s)$ and $(s \times m) \cdot (m \times n)$. Apply the same decomposition separately to each problem, exploiting the symmetry of the even-odd partition in each index set. The first decomposition yields $mns + mn + ns + sm$ trilinear terms; the second yields an identical number. Since the problems are independent, the total rank is the sum, and thus each multiplication can be performed with $mns + mn + ns + sm$ noncommutative multiplications.

## Verification

The cardinality of $S$ is $4(\frac{n}{2})^3 = \frac{1}{2}n^3$, consistent with four choices of parity with at most one odd index. Each $\Sigma_j$ involves three sums over $n^2$ terms, giving $3 n^2$ trilinear terms per $\Sigma_j$. Removing the diagonal triples reduces $3n$ terms, yielding the stated bound.

The decomposition preserves the original sum because the identity

$abc + ABC = (a+A)(b+B)(c+C) - (a+A)BC - A(b+B)c - aB(c+C)$

ensures exact equality for each triple, and linearity of summation guarantees correctness for the entire sum.

For part (b), independence of the two multiplication problems implies no interference between the decompositions, and the bound $mns + mn + ns + sm$ arises from applying the same trilinear expansion logic.

## Notes

Alternative approaches include using Strassen-type recursive decompositions for powers of two or further exploiting block structures to reduce rank. However, the parity-based decomposition provides an explicit constructive method for $n$ even, and the rank bound cannot be improved without a fundamentally new identity or symmetry argument.

This completes the proof.
