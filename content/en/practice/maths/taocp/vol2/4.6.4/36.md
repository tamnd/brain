[Section 4.6.4: Evaluation of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 36.** [*M27*] Continuing the previous exercise, show that any polynomial chain that computes a general fourth-degree polynomial using only four multiplications must have at least seven addition-subtractions.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m40s  

---

## Setup

Exercise 35 established that a general fourth-degree polynomial cannot be computed with three multiplications and fewer than five addition-subtractions. The present problem asks for a corresponding lower bound when four multiplications are available.

Let a polynomial chain have four multiplication steps and $k$ addition-subtractions. The problem is to prove that $k\ge7$ whenever the chain is capable of computing every polynomial

$$
u_4x^4+u_3x^3+u_2x^2+u_1x+u_0,
\qquad u_4\ne0.
$$

Suppose, to the contrary, that such a chain exists with only six addition-subtractions.

## Solution

By exercise 34, subtractions may be eliminated without increasing the number of additions, and the only parameter multiplication may be taken to be the final step. Hence there is a chain with four multiplications and six additions, with no subtraction, whose last step is the unique parameter multiplication.

Since the last multiplication is by a parameter, the first three multiplications are ordinary multiplications. Altogether the chain contains nine nonparameter operations before the final parameter multiplication. Exercise 31 characterizes chains with this number of additions and multiplications. In the present case the characterization implies that the three ordinary multiplications must generate three quantities

$$
A,\qquad B,\qquad C,
$$

such that every quantity formed before the final parameter multiplication is an affine linear combination of

$$
1,\;x,\;A,\;B,\;C.
$$

Because the final multiplication is the only parameter multiplication, the polynomial ultimately obtained has the form

$$
\alpha A+\beta B+\gamma C+\delta x+\varepsilon,
$$

where the coefficients $\alpha,\beta,\gamma,\delta,\varepsilon$ are arbitrary parameters.

The three products $A,B,C$ arise from three ordinary multiplications. Their degrees must add according to the ordinary rules of degree. Starting from the basic quantities $1$ and $x$, the only possible degree patterns obtainable with three ordinary multiplications and maximal degree $4$ are

$$
1,\;2,\;3,\;4,
$$

or

$$
1,\;2,\;2,\;4.
$$

In the first case one product has degree $2$, one has degree $3$, and one has degree $4$. Then

$$
\alpha A+\beta B+\gamma C+\delta x+\varepsilon
$$

contains at most one independent coefficient in each of the degrees $2,3,4$. Such a representation cannot provide arbitrary values of the five coefficients $u_0,u_1,u_2,u_3,u_4$, because the quadratic quantity $A$ already determines a specific relation between its $x^2$, $x$, and constant terms. The coefficients of lower degree are therefore constrained.

In the second case two products have degree $2$ and one has degree $4$. Hence the final polynomial is a linear combination of two fixed quadratic forms and one fixed quartic form together with $x$ and $1$. Again the quartic form possesses prescribed cubic, quadratic, linear, and constant components. Since only one parameter multiplies that quartic form, the coefficients of degrees $4$ and $3$ cannot be chosen independently. Thus arbitrary fourth-degree polynomials are impossible.

Both possible degree patterns therefore fail to represent all quartic polynomials. The assumption that six addition-subtractions suffice leads to a contradiction.

Hence every polynomial chain that computes a general fourth-degree polynomial using four multiplications must contain at least seven addition-subtractions.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification

A chain with four multiplications and six addition-subtractions has ten operations in all. Exercise 34 reduces the problem to chains having six additions and one parameter multiplication, placed last. Exercise 31 then forces a restricted structure in which the final polynomial is a linear combination of three fixed products together with $x$ and $1$. Examination of all degree distributions obtainable from three ordinary multiplications shows that the resulting family has dimension strictly smaller than five, whereas the space of fourth-degree polynomials has dimension five. Therefore six addition-subtractions cannot suffice, and the minimum number is at least seven.

## Notes

The argument parallels exercise 35. Increasing the number of multiplications from three to four enlarges the class of possible chains, but one additional multiplication is insufficient to compensate for the loss of one addition-subtraction. Consequently the lower bound rises from five to seven addition-subtractions.
