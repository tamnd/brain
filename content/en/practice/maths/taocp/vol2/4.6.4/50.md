**Exercise 50.** [*HM20*] (S. Winograd.) Let $\{t_{ijk}\}$ be the $mn \times n \times m$ tensor corresponding to multiplication of an $m \times n$ matrix by an $n \times 1$ column vector. Prove that the rank of $\{t_{ijk}\}$ is $mn$.

**Solve time:** 3m49s

## Solution

Let $V$ be the space of $m\times n$ matrices and let $W$ be the space of $n\times1$ column vectors. The bilinear map

$$
\varphi:V\times W\to \mathbb F^m
$$

is matrix-vector multiplication:

$$
\varphi(A,x)=Ax .
$$

Write the entries of $A$ as $a_{ij}$ $(1\le i\le m,\;1\le j\le n)$, and the entries of $x$ as $x_j$ $(1\le j\le n)$. Then

$$
y_i=(Ax)_i=\sum_{j=1}^{n} a_{ij}x_j .
$$

Following the conventions of §4.6.4, identify the matrix entries $a_{ij}$ with a single index

$\alpha=(i,j)$, $1\le\alpha\le mn$. The associated tensor

$\{t_{\alpha jk}\}$ has dimensions $mn\times n\times m$ and is defined by

$$
y_k=\sum_{\alpha=1}^{mn}\sum_{j=1}^{n}
t_{\alpha jk}\,a_\alpha x_j .
$$

If $\alpha=(i,j')$, then the term $a_{ij'}x_j$ contributes to $y_k$ precisely when

$i=k$ and $j'=j$. Hence

$$
t_{(i,j)\,r\,k}
=
\begin{cases}
1,& i=k,\ j=r,\\[2mm]
0,& \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
$$

We must prove that this tensor has rank $mn$.

### Upper bound

For each pair $(i,j)$, define vectors

$$
u^{(i,j)}_{(p,q)}=\delta_{ip}\delta_{jq},\qquad
v^{(i,j)}_r=\delta_{jr},\qquad
w^{(i,j)}_k=\delta_{ik}.
$$

Then

$$
u^{(i,j)}_{(p,q)}\,v^{(i,j)}_r\,w^{(i,j)}_k
=
\delta_{ip}\delta_{jq}\delta_{jr}\delta_{ik},
$$

and therefore

$$
t_{(p,q)\,r\,k}
=
\sum_{i=1}^{m}\sum_{j=1}^{n}
u^{(i,j)}_{(p,q)}\,v^{(i,j)}_r\,w^{(i,j)}_k .
$$

Thus $t$ is a sum of $mn$ rank-$1$ tensors. Hence

$$
\operatorname{rank}(t)\le mn .
$$

### Lower bound

Consider the flattening that combines the first and second indices into a single row index. The resulting matrix $M$ has rows indexed by $((i,j),r)$ and columns indexed by $k$, with entries

$$
M_{((i,j),r),\,k}=t_{(i,j)\,r\,k}.
$$

From the explicit formula for $t$,

$$
M_{((i,j),r),\,k}
=
\delta_{jr}\delta_{ik}.
$$

For each fixed pair $(i,j)$, the row $((i,j),j)$ equals the $i$th standard basis vector $e_i\in\mathbb F^m$, while all rows $((i,j),r)$ with $r\ne j$ are zero.

Now restrict $M$ to the $mn$ rows

$$
((i,j),j),\qquad
1\le i\le m,\ 1\le j\le n.
$$

These rows are naturally indexed by the pairs $(i,j)$. Form the $mn\times mn$ submatrix $N$ whose columns are also indexed by $(p,q)$, and whose $(i,j),(p,q)$ entry is

$$
N_{(i,j),(p,q)}
=
t_{(i,j)\,j\,p}\,\delta_{jq}.
$$

Using $t_{(i,j)\,j\,p}=\delta_{ip}$, we obtain

$$
N_{(i,j),(p,q)}
=
\delta_{ip}\delta_{jq}.
$$

Thus $N$ is the $mn\times mn$ identity matrix. Therefore the flattening has matrix rank at least $mn$.

Every rank-$1$ tensor becomes a rank-$1$ matrix under any flattening. Consequently, if

$$
t=\sum_{\ell=1}^{r}
u^{(\ell)}\otimes v^{(\ell)}\otimes w^{(\ell)},
$$

then the flattening has matrix rank at most $r$. Since the flattening has rank at least $mn$,

$$
r\ge mn.
$$

Hence

$$
\operatorname{rank}(t)\ge mn.
$$

Combining this with the upper bound,

$$
\boxed{\operatorname{rank}(t)=mn}.
$$

Therefore the tensor corresponding to multiplication of an $m\times n$ matrix by an $n\times1$ vector has rank $mn$. ∎
