**Exercise 24.** &#9654; [*M24*] [M24] Find values of $c$ and $\alpha_1$, $\beta_1$ satisfying the conditions of Theorem E, for the polynomial $u(x) = (x+7)(x^2+6x+4)(x^2+4x+5)(x+1)$. Choose these values so that $\beta_2 = 0$. Give two different solutions.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 4m03s  

---

We restart from the structural requirement of Theorem E.

In Theorem E, the polynomial is evaluated after a shift $x = y - c$, so that the evaluation is performed at $y = 0$. The condition $\beta_2 = 0$ means that the final quadratic block reduces to a linear factor $y$, i.e., one of the original linear factors of $u(x)$ must become $y$ after the shift. Hence $c$ must be chosen so that $x+c$ is one of the linear factors of $u(x)$.

The polynomial

$$
u(x) = (x+7)(x^2+6x+4)(x^2+4x+5)(x+1)
$$

has linear factors $x+7$ and $x+1$. Therefore the only possible choices are

$$
c = -7 \quad \text{or} \quad c = -1.
$$

## Case 1: $c = -7$

Let $y = x+7$, so $x = y-7$. Then $x+7 = y$, giving the required $\beta_2 = 0$.

Rewrite the remaining factors in terms of $y$:

$$
x^2+6x+4 = (y-7)^2 + 6(y-7) + 4 = y^2 - 8y + 11,
$$

$$
x^2+4x+5 = (y-7)^2 + 4(y-7) + 5 = y^2 - 10y + 26,
$$

$$
x+1 = y-6.
$$

Thus

$$
u(x) = y (y^2 - 8y + 11)(y^2 - 10y + 26)(y-6).
$$

To fit Theorem E, we take the first quadratic block as one of the transformed quadratics.

### First solution

Choose

$$
y^2 + \alpha_1 y + \beta_1 = y^2 - 8y + 11.
$$

Hence

$$
\alpha_1 = -8,\quad \beta_1 = 11,\quad c = -7.
$$

This gives a valid decomposition with $\beta_2 = 0$ coming from the factor $y$.

### Second solution (same $c$, different block choice)

Choose instead

$$
y^2 + \alpha_1 y + \beta_1 = y^2 - 10y + 26,
$$

so

$$
\alpha_1 = -10,\quad \beta_1 = 26,\quad c = -7.
$$

## Case 2: $c = -1$

Let $y = x+1$, so $x = y-1$. Then $x+1 = y$, giving $\beta_2 = 0$.

Rewrite the remaining factors:

$$
x^2+6x+4 = (y-1)^2 + 6(y-1) + 4 = y^2 + 4y - 1,
$$

$$
x^2+4x+5 = (y-1)^2 + 4(y-1) + 5 = y^2 + 2y + 2,
$$

$$
x+7 = y+6.
$$

Thus

$$
u(x) = y (y+6)(y^2+4y-1)(y^2+2y+2).
$$

Again, we select one quadratic block.

### Third valid solution

$$
y^2 + \alpha_1 y + \beta_1 = y^2 + 4y - 1,
$$

so

$$
\alpha_1 = 4,\quad \beta_1 = -1,\quad c = -1.
$$

### Fourth valid solution

$$
y^2 + \alpha_1 y + \beta_1 = y^2 + 2y + 2,
$$

so

$$
\alpha_1 = 2,\quad \beta_1 = 2,\quad c = -1.
$$

## Final answer

Two valid schemes (as requested) are, for example:

$$
(c,\alpha_1,\beta_1) = (-7,-8,11),
$$

$$
(c,\alpha_1,\beta_1) = (-1,2,2),
$$

with $\beta_2 = 0$ in each case arising from the shifted linear factor $x+7$ or $x+1$, respectively.

These satisfy Theorem E because in each case the shift makes one linear factor become $y$, and the remaining quadratic block is taken directly from the transformed factorization.
