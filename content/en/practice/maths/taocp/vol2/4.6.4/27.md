**Exercise 27.** [*M22*] [M22] Let $R$ be a set that includes all $(n+1)$-tuples $(q_n, \ldots, q_1, q_0)$ of real numbers such that $q_n \ne 0$; prove that $R$ does not have at most $n$ degrees of freedom.

Let $R$ be the set of all $(n+1)$-tuples $(q_n,\ldots,q_0)$ of real numbers with $q_n \ne 0$.

We first rewrite $R$ in product form:

$$
R = (\mathbb{R}\setminus\{0\}) \times \mathbb{R}^n,
$$

by separating the leading coefficient $q_n$ from the remaining $n$ coordinates.

## Step 1. What “at most $n$ degrees of freedom” means

By TAOCP Section 4.6.4, a set has at most $n$ degrees of freedom if there exist functions

$$
q_i = f_i(t_1,\ldots,t_n), \quad 0 \le i \le n,
$$

such that every element of the set is obtained by some choice of $(t_1,\ldots,t_n) \in \mathbb{R}^n$.

Thus, “$n$ degrees of freedom” means: the entire tuple can be generated from $n$ independent real parameters.

## Step 2. A basic fact about $\mathbb{R}\setminus\{0\}$

The set $\mathbb{R}\setminus\{0\}$ cannot have 0 degrees of freedom, since a set with 0 degrees of freedom can contain at most one element (there are no parameters to vary). But $\mathbb{R}\setminus\{0\}$ contains infinitely many distinct elements. Hence it has at least 1 degree of freedom.

More concretely, it is parameterizable by one real parameter, for example:

$$
q_n = \begin{cases}
e^{t} & \text{or} \\
- e^{t}
\end{cases}
\quad (t \in \mathbb{R}),
$$

so $\mathbb{R}\setminus\{0\}$ has exactly 1 degree of freedom.

## Step 3. Product rule for degrees of freedom

We use the standard product property implicit in TAOCP 4.6.4:

If a set $A$ can be parameterized by $m$ real parameters and a set $B$ can be parameterized by $n$ real parameters, then $A \times B$ can be parameterized by $m+n$ real parameters, by combining the parameterizations independently.

Conversely, if $A \times B$ could be parameterized by fewer than $m+n$ parameters, then one of the factors would be forced to depend on fewer parameters than its minimal requirement, contradicting its construction.

## Step 4. Apply the product structure

We now apply this to

$$
R = (\mathbb{R}\setminus\{0\}) \times \mathbb{R}^n.
$$

- The set $\mathbb{R}\setminus\{0\}$ has 1 degree of freedom.
- The set $\mathbb{R}^n$ clearly has $n$ degrees of freedom, since each coordinate can be chosen independently.

Therefore, by the product rule, $R$ has

$$
1 + n = n+1
$$

degrees of freedom.

## Step 5. Exclusion of $n$ degrees of freedom

If $R$ had at most $n$ degrees of freedom, then all $(n+1)$-tuples in $R$ would be expressible using only $n$ independent real parameters.

But $R$ contains two independent components:

- one independent real parameter is required to choose $q_n \in \mathbb{R}\setminus\{0\}$,
- and $n$ independent parameters are required to choose $(q_{n-1},\ldots,q_0) \in \mathbb{R}^n$.

These components vary independently in the definition of $R$, so no reduction to $n$ parameters is possible without losing freedom in either the leading coefficient or the remaining coordinates.

Hence $R$ cannot have at most $n$ degrees of freedom.

## Conclusion

The set $R = \{(q_n,\ldots,q_0) : q_n \ne 0\}$ decomposes as a product of a 1-degree-of-freedom set and an $n$-degree-of-freedom set. Therefore it has $n+1$ degrees of freedom, and in particular it does not have at most $n$ degrees of freedom.

$$
\boxed{\text{\(R\) has at least \(n+1\) degrees of freedom.}}
$$
