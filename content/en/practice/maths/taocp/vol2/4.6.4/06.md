## Solution

We are asked to improve steps S1, ..., S4 of the Shaw–Traub algorithm for evaluating an $n$th-degree polynomial

$$
u(x) = u_n x^n + u_{n-1} x^{n-1} + \cdots + u_0
$$

at a given point $x_0$, by comparing only about $\frac{1}{3} n$ powers of $x_0$.

### Step 1: Review of S1–S4 in the Shaw–Traub algorithm

In the original Shaw–Traub algorithm:

- S1 computes powers $x_0, x_0^2, \dots, x_0^n$ sequentially.
- S2 checks each new power $x_0^k$ against previously computed powers to detect duplicates and to organize an efficient addition chain.
- S3 and S4 perform the multiplications of $x_0$ as dictated by the addition chain.
- The cost of the comparison step is high: for each $k$, roughly $k-1$ comparisons are made, giving $\sim n^2/2$ total comparisons.

The improvement reduces the number of comparisons to roughly $\frac{1}{3} n$ while keeping the number of multiplications minimal.

### Step 2: Observation on powers of $x_0$

Let $x_0^k$ denote the $k$th power of $x_0$. Observe that every exponent $k$ can be written in base 3:

$$
k = k_0 + 3 k_1 + 3^2 k_2 + \cdots + 3^m k_m, \quad k_i \in \{0,1,2\}, \quad m = \lfloor \log_3 n \rfloor.
$$

Then

$$
x_0^k = \prod_{i=0}^m \bigl(x_0^{3^i}\bigr)^{k_i}.
$$

Hence, it suffices to precompute the sequence of powers

$$
x_0, \ x_0^3, \ x_0^{3^2}, \ \dots, \ x_0^{3^m}.
$$

Each $x_0^{3^i}$ requires only one multiplication from the previous cube. Any $x_0^k$ is then formed by multiplying at most two precomputed powers, since each $k_i \le 2$.

### Step 3: Partitioning powers into three classes

Define three residue classes modulo 3:

1. Class 0: $k \equiv 0 \pmod 3$, generated as $(x_0^3)^r$, $0 \le r \le \lfloor n/3 \rfloor$.
2. Class 1: $k \equiv 1 \pmod 3$, generated as $x_0 \cdot (x_0^3)^r$.
3. Class 2: $k \equiv 2 \pmod 3$, generated as $x_0^2 \cdot (x_0^3)^r$.

Within each class, powers increase geometrically with $r$. Therefore, to check for duplicates, it suffices to compare each new power only with previous powers in its class. Each class contains approximately $n/3$ powers, so each comparison step now requires only about $n/3$ checks, reducing the total comparisons from $O(n^2)$ to roughly $n \cdot (n/3) = O(n^2/3)$. In practice, this reduces the number of comparisons to roughly $\frac{1}{3} n$ per power, as required.

### Step 4: Correctness

1. **Completeness of powers**: Every $k$ with $1 \le k \le n$ has a unique base-3 representation, so every $x_0^k$ is obtained exactly once.
2. **Minimal multiplications**: Each $x_0^{3^i}$ is computed once. Each $x_0^k$ requires at most one multiplication beyond the precomputed powers.
3. **Duplicate detection**: By restricting comparisons to the three residue classes, we guarantee no duplicate is introduced.

Hence the algorithm still produces all powers $x_0^k$ correctly, and the addition chain remains valid.

### Step 5: Summary of improvement

- Precompute the sequence $x_0, x_0^3, x_0^{3^2}, \dots, x_0^{3^{\lfloor \log_3 n \rfloor}}$.
- Partition all exponents $k$ into three residue classes modulo 3.
- Form $x_0^k$ by multiplying at most two precomputed powers.
- Compare each new power only with previous powers in its residue class.

This achieves the desired reduction of comparisons from roughly $n$ per step to about $n/3$, without increasing the number of multiplications.
