Restart from the goal: construct a Pan-style evaluation scheme (16), meaning a straight-line program that minimizes multiplications by first generating needed powers of $x$ via a short addition chain, then forming the polynomial by reusing these intermediate results.

We must evaluate

$$
P(x)=x^9 - 3x^5 + x^4 - 2x^3 + x^2 - 3x - 1.
$$

### 1. Construct the addition chain for the exponents

The relevant exponents are $1,2,3,4,5,9$. A minimal reuse chain is:

$$
2=1+1,\quad 3=2+1,\quad 4=2+2,\quad 5=3+2,\quad 9=5+4.
$$

Translate this into multiplications:

Let $x_1=x$.

1. $x_2 = x_1 \cdot x_1 = x^2$
2. $x_3 = x_2 \cdot x_1 = x^3$
3. $x_4 = x_2 \cdot x_2 = x^4$
4. $x_5 = x_3 \cdot x_2 = x^5$
5. $x_9 = x_5 \cdot x_4 = x^9$

This uses 5 multiplications for all required powers.

### 2. Form the polynomial using the computed powers

Now express the polynomial in terms of the intermediates:

$$
P(x)=x_9 - 3x_5 + x_4 - 2x_3 + x_2 - 3x_1 - 1.
$$

This part uses only additions and multiplications by small constants, which are not counted as main multiplications in Pan’s scheme.

### 3. Straight-line evaluation scheme

We combine both parts into one evaluation program:

Start:

$$
x_1 = x
$$

Power construction:

$$
\begin{aligned}
x_2 &= x_1 x_1,\\
x_3 &= x_2 x_1,\\
x_4 &= x_2 x_2,\\
x_5 &= x_3 x_2,\\
x_9 &= x_5 x_4.
\end{aligned}
$$

Polynomial accumulation:

$$
\begin{aligned}
y &= x_9,\\
y &= y - 3x_5,\\
y &= y + x_4,\\
y &= y - 2x_3,\\
y &= y + x_2,\\
y &= y - 3x_1,\\
y &= y - 1.
\end{aligned}
$$

### 4. Resulting scheme

The scheme evaluates $P(x)$ using:

- 5 multiplications to generate all required powers,
- only additions and scalar multiplications afterward.

Thus, Pan’s method yields an efficient evaluation scheme with the multiplication structure determined by the addition chain

$$
1 \to 2 \to 3 \to 4 \to 5 \to 9,
$$

and direct reuse of intermediates for the final linear combination.
