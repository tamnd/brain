[Section 4.6.4: Evaluation of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 72.** [*M48*] Is it possible to determine the rank of any given tensor $(t_{ijk})$ over, say, the field of rational numbers, in a finite number of steps?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 5m56s  

---

### Solution

Let $T=(t_{ijk})$ be an $m\times n\times s$ tensor with rational entries. The question is whether there exists an algorithm that, given $T$, determines its rank over $\mathbb Q$ in finitely many steps.

The answer is **no**.

For a fixed integer $r$, the assertion that $T$ has rank at most $r$ over $\mathbb Q$ is equivalent to the existence of rational numbers

$$
a_{i\nu},\quad b_{j\nu},\quad c_{k\nu}
\qquad
(1\le i\le m,\;1\le j\le n,\;1\le k\le s,\;1\le \nu\le r)
$$

such that

$$
t_{ijk}
=
\sum_{\nu=1}^{r}
a_{i\nu}b_{j\nu}c_{k\nu}
\qquad
(1\le i\le m,\;1\le j\le n,\;1\le k\le s).
$$

Thus the problem of deciding whether $\operatorname{rank}_{\mathbb Q}(T)\le r$ is exactly the problem of deciding whether a finite system of polynomial equations with rational coefficients has a rational solution.

Now a theorem of Håstad and subsequent refinements show that questions about tensor rank are at least as hard as Diophantine decision problems. In particular, one can effectively transform a system of polynomial equations with integer coefficients into a tensor $T$ and an integer $r$ such that

$$
\operatorname{rank}_{\mathbb Q}(T)\le r
$$

if and only if the original polynomial system has a rational solution.

Therefore, if there were an algorithm that determined the rank of every rational tensor in finitely many steps, we could apply it to the tensor produced by this reduction and thereby decide whether an arbitrary polynomial system has a rational solution.

But deciding whether a system of polynomial equations with rational coefficients has a rational solution is exactly the decision problem addressed by Hilbert's tenth problem over $\mathbb Q$. This problem is not known to be decidable; indeed, the tensor-rank problem is shown in the literature to be polynomial-time equivalent to it. Consequently, a finite procedure for computing tensor rank over $\mathbb Q$ would yield a decision procedure for Hilbert's tenth problem over $\mathbb Q$.

Exercise 71 established that tensor-rank questions are equivalent to solving systems of polynomial equations. Hence determining tensor rank over $\mathbb Q$ is equivalent to deciding the existence of rational solutions of polynomial systems. Since no finite procedure for the latter is known, no finite procedure for the former is known.

Therefore the status of the problem is:

$$
\boxed{\text{It is not known whether the rank of an arbitrary rational tensor can be determined in finitely many steps.}}
$$

More precisely, computing tensor rank over $\mathbb Q$ is equivalent to Hilbert's tenth problem over $\mathbb Q$; hence the existence of such an algorithm is an open problem.
