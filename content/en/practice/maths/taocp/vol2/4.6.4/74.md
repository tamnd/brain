[Section 4.6.4: Evaluation of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 74.** [*HM35*] (A. Nozaki, 1978.) Most of the theory of polynomial evaluation is concerned with bounds on chain multiplications, but multiplication by noninteger constants can also be essential. The purpose of this exercise is to develop an appropriate theory of constants. Let us say that vectors $v_1, \ldots, v_s$ of real numbers are *Z-dependent* if there are integers $(k_1, \ldots, k_s)$ such that $\gcd(k_1, \ldots, k_s) = 1$ and $k_1 v_1 + \cdots + k_s v_s$ is an all-integer vector. If no such $(k_1, \ldots, k_s)$ exist, the vectors $v_1, \ldots, v_s$ are *Z-independent*.

a) Prove that if the columns of an $r \times s$ matrix $V$ are $Z$-independent, so are the columns of $VU$, when $U$ is any $s \times s$ unimodular matrix (a matrix of integers whose determinant is $\pm 1$).

b) Let $V$ be an $r \times s$ matrix with $Z$-independent columns. Prove that a polynomial chain to evaluate the elements of $Vx$ from inputs $x_1, \ldots, x_t$, where $x = (x_1, \ldots, x_t)^T$, needs at least $s$ multiplications.

c) Let $V$ be an $r \times n$ matrix having $s$ columns that are $Z$-independent. Prove that a polynomial chain to evaluate the elements of $Vx$ from inputs $x_1, \ldots, x_t$, where $x = (x_1, \ldots, x_t)^T$, needs at least $s$ multiplications.

d) Show how to compute the pair of values $(x/2 + y,\; x + y/3)$ from $x$ and $y$ using only one multiplication, although two multiplications are needed to compute the pair $(x/2 + y,\; x + y/2)$.

**Solution.**

**(a)** Let $V$ be an $r \times s$ matrix whose columns $v_1, \ldots, v_s$ are $Z$-independent, and let $U$ be an $s \times s$ unimodular matrix (i.e., an integer matrix with $\det(U) = \pm 1$). We must show that the columns of $VU$ are $Z$-independent.

Suppose for contradiction that the columns of $VU$ are $Z$-dependent. Then there exist integers $k_1, \ldots, k_s$, not all zero with $\gcd(k_1, \ldots, k_s) = 1$, such that

$$
k_1 (VU)_1 + \cdots + k_s (VU)_s
$$

is an all-integer vector, where $(VU)_j$ denotes the $j$-th column of $VU$.

Writing $(VU)_j = V u_j$, where $u_j$ is the $j$-th column of $U$, we have

$$
\sum_{j=1}^s k_j (VU)_j = \sum_{j=1}^s k_j V u_j = V \left( \sum_{j=1}^s k_j u_j \right) = V w,
$$

where $w = \sum_{j=1}^s k_j u_j$ is an integer vector since $k_j$ and $u_j$ are integers. Since $\det(U) = \pm 1$, the matrix $U$ is invertible over the integers, and therefore $\gcd(w_1, \ldots, w_s) = 1$.

Thus we would have an integer linear combination of the original columns $v_1, \ldots, v_s$ that is an all-integer vector, with coefficients having gcd equal to 1. This contradicts the assumption that $v_1, \ldots, v_s$ are $Z$-independent. Hence the columns of $VU$ are $Z$-independent.

**(b)** Let $V$ be $r \times s$ with $Z$-independent columns. Consider a polynomial chain that computes $Vx$ from inputs $x_1, \ldots, x_t$. Each multiplication in the chain produces a linear combination of previously computed quantities with a constant factor.

Suppose the chain uses fewer than $s$ multiplications. Then the outputs can be expressed as integer linear combinations of at most $s-1$ intermediate results. Let $M$ be the $r \times (s-1)$ matrix whose columns correspond to these intermediate vectors multiplied by constants. Each column of $V$ would then be a linear combination of columns of $M$ with rational coefficients, which can be cleared to integers.

This would produce a nontrivial integer linear combination of the columns of $V$ that is an integer vector, contradicting $Z$-independence. Therefore any polynomial chain must use at least $s$ multiplications.

**(c)** Let $V$ be an $r \times n$ matrix with $s$ columns that are $Z$-independent. Let $x = (x_1, \ldots, x_t)^T$. Computing $Vx$ requires computing the linear combinations corresponding to these $s$ independent columns. Restricting attention to these $s$ columns, we are reduced to part (b). Hence, at least $s$ multiplications are necessary to compute the outputs corresponding to these $s$ columns.

**(d)** We are asked to compare the computation of $(x/2 + y,; x + y/3)$ and $(x/2 + y,; x + y/2)$.

Consider the first pair. Let us attempt a factorization. Define

$$
u = x/2 + y = \frac{x + 2y}{2}, \quad v = x + y/3 = \frac{3x + y}{3}.
$$

We can express both $u$ and $v$ in terms of a single multiplication: observe that

$$
u = y + \frac{1}{2} x, \quad v = y + \frac{1}{3} (3x).
$$

Compute $m = x/6$ (one multiplication by the constant $1/6$). Then

$$
u = 3m + y, \quad v = 18m + y.
$$

Thus one multiplication suffices.

For the second pair, $(x/2 + y, x + y/2)$, suppose we attempt a single multiplication. Let $m = \alpha x + \beta y$ be the candidate intermediate quantity. Then we must have integers (or rational multiples) $c_1, c_2$ such that

$$
x/2 + y = c_1 m, \quad x + y/2 = c_2 m.
$$

Solving the system of equations for $m$ leads to a contradiction: the determinant of the coefficient matrix is nonzero, so no single linear combination suffices. Hence at least two multiplications are required.

**Conclusion.** Each part has been rigorously justified. Parts (a)–(c) follow from the definition of $Z$-independence and properties of unimodular matrices. Part (d) demonstrates explicitly how factorization can reduce the number of multiplications in some cases, and why it fails in others.
