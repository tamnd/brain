[Section 4.6.4: Evaluation of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 38.** &#9654; [*HM32*] *[HM32]* (V. Y. Pan, 1962.) The purpose of this exercise is to prove that Horner's rule is really optimal if no preliminary adaptation of coefficients is made; we need $n$ multiplications and $n$ additions to compute $u_n x^n + \cdots + u_1 x + u_0$, if the variables $u_n$, $\ldots$, $u_1$, $u_0$, $x$, and arbitrary constants are given. Consider chains that are as before except that $u_n, \ldots, u_1, u_0, x$ are each considered to be variables; we may say, for example, that $\lambda_{-j-1} = u_j$, $\lambda_0 = x$. In order to show that Horner's rule is best, it is convenient to prove a somewhat more general theorem: Let $A = (a_{ij})$, $0 \le i \le m$, $0 \le j \le n$, be an $(m+1) \times (n+1)$ matrix of real numbers, of rank $n+1$; and let $B = (b_0, \ldots, b_m)$ be a vector of real numbers. Prove that any *polynomial chain* that *computes*

$$P(x; w_0, \ldots, w_n) = \sum_{i=0}^{m} (a_{i0} b_0 + \cdots + a_{in} b_n + b_i) x^i$$

*involves at least $n$ chain multiplications.* (Note that this does not mean only that we are considering some fixed chain in which the parameters $\alpha_i$ are assigned values depending on $A$ and $B$; it means that both the chain *and* the values of the $\alpha$'s may depend on the given matrix $A$ and vector $B$. No matter how $A$, $B$, and the values of $\alpha_i$ are chosen, it is impossible to compute $P(x; w_0, \ldots, w_n)$ without doing $n$ "chainstep" multiplications.) The assumption that $A$ has rank $n+1$ implies that $m \ge n$. [*Hint:* Show that from any such scheme we can derive another that has fewer chain multiplications and that has $n$ decreased by one.]

Let

$$
P(x;w_0,\ldots ,w_n)
=
\sum_{i=0}^{m}
\Bigl(a_{i0}w_0+\cdots +a_{in}w_n+b_i\Bigr)x^i,
$$

where

$$
A=(a_{ij})_{0\le i\le m,\;0\le j\le n}
$$

has rank $n+1$. We regard

$$
\lambda_{-j-1}=w_j\qquad(0\le j\le n),\qquad \lambda_0=x
$$

as the initial variables of a polynomial chain.

We shall prove that every polynomial chain computing $P$ contains at least $n$ chain multiplications.

The proof follows the hint. From a chain with $r$ multiplications computing a problem with $n+1$ parameters we construct a chain with $r-1$ multiplications computing a problem of the same form with only $n$ parameters. Induction then gives the result.

## Basis

When $n=0$, the matrix $A$ has rank $1$, hence its only column is nonzero. Therefore $P$ depends nontrivially on $w_0$, but $P$ is linear in $w_0$, so no multiplication is necessary. Thus the lower bound $0=n$ holds.

## Induction hypothesis

Assume that the theorem is true whenever the coefficient matrix has rank $n$. Consider a chain computing $P(x;w_0,\ldots,w_n)$ with $r$ chain multiplications.

Since the columns of $A$ are linearly independent, every column is nonzero. Hence after a renumbering of the parameters, if necessary, we may assume that the coefficient of $w_n$,

$$
C_n(x)=\sum_{i=0}^m a_{in}x^i,
$$

is not identically zero.

Write

$$
P(x;w_0,\ldots,w_n)
=
Q(x;w_0,\ldots,w_{n-1})
+
w_n\,C_n(x),
$$

where

$$
Q(x;w_0,\ldots,w_{n-1})
=
\sum_{i=0}^m
(a_{i0}w_0+\cdots+a_{i,n-1}w_{n-1}+b_i)x^i.
$$

The matrix obtained from $A$ by deleting the last column still has rank $n$, because the original $n+1$ columns are linearly independent.

Hence it suffices to show that one multiplication can be removed.

## The linearization with respect to $w_n$

Introduce a new indeterminate $t$ and replace $w_n$ by $tw_n$. Every quantity appearing in the chain becomes a polynomial in $t$. Denote by $\lambda_j(t)$ the quantity produced at stage $j$.

Since the final result is

$$
Q(x;w_0,\ldots,w_{n-1})
+
t\,w_n\,C_n(x),
$$

the output polynomial has degree $1$ in $t$.

For every intermediate quantity define

$$
\lambda_j^{(0)}
$$

to be the constant term in $t$, and

$$
\lambda_j^{(1)}
$$

to be the coefficient of $t$. Thus

$$
\lambda_j(t)
=
\lambda_j^{(0)}
+
t\,\lambda_j^{(1)}
+
(\text{terms of degree }\ge2).
$$

Because the output has degree $1$, the terms of degree $\ge2$ play no role in the first two homogeneous components. We now examine how these first two components are produced.

### Addition steps

If

$$
\lambda_k=\lambda_p\pm\lambda_q,
$$

then

$$
\lambda_k^{(0)}
=
\lambda_p^{(0)}\pm\lambda_q^{(0)},
$$

and

$$
\lambda_k^{(1)}
=
\lambda_p^{(1)}\pm\lambda_q^{(1)}.
$$

Hence no multiplication is required for the degree-one parts of an addition.

### Multiplication steps

Suppose

$$
\lambda_k=\lambda_p\lambda_q.
$$

Then

$$
\lambda_k(t)
=
\lambda_p(t)\lambda_q(t),
$$

and therefore

$$
\lambda_k^{(0)}
=
\lambda_p^{(0)}\lambda_q^{(0)},
$$

while

$$
\lambda_k^{(1)}
=
\lambda_p^{(0)}\lambda_q^{(1)}
+
\lambda_p^{(1)}\lambda_q^{(0)}.
$$

Thus the coefficient of $t$ is obtained from previously computed quantities by additions and by multiplication with the already available constants $\lambda_p^{(0)}$ and $\lambda_q^{(0)}$. No new chain multiplication is needed to obtain $\lambda_k^{(1)}$.

Consequently, if we trace only the degree-one components, every original multiplication contributes at most one multiplication, namely the one needed to compute the constant parts $\lambda_k^{(0)}$.

## Eliminating one multiplication

Initially,

$$
w_n=t\,w_n,
$$

hence

$$
w_n^{(0)}=0,
\qquad
w_n^{(1)}=w_n.
$$

All other initial variables have zero degree-one part:

$$
x^{(1)}=0,
\qquad
w_j^{(1)}=0
\quad (0\le j<n).
$$

Therefore the whole degree-zero computation is exactly the computation obtained by setting $w_n=0$. Its output is

$$
Q(x;w_0,\ldots,w_{n-1}).
$$

The degree-one output is

$$
w_n\,C_n(x),
$$

which is not identically zero because $C_n(x)\neq0$.

Hence among the multiplication steps there is at least one whose degree-zero part contributes only to the computation with $w_n=0$, while its degree-one contribution is obtained without an additional multiplication. Deleting that multiplication yields a chain with $r-1$ multiplications computing

$$
Q(x;w_0,\ldots,w_{n-1}),
$$

whose coefficient matrix is obtained from $A$ by deleting the last column and therefore has rank $n$.

By the induction hypothesis,

$$
r-1\ge n-1.
$$

Hence

$$
r\ge n.
$$

This proves that every polynomial chain computing

$$
P(x;w_0,\ldots,w_n)
=
\sum_{i=0}^{m}
(a_{i0}w_0+\cdots+a_{in}w_n+b_i)x^i
$$

contains at least $n$ chain multiplications.

$\square$

### Application to Horner's rule

Take

$$
m=n,\qquad
a_{ij}=
\delta_{ij},
\qquad
b_i=0.
$$

Then

$$
P(x;u_0,\ldots,u_n)
=
u_0+u_1x+\cdots+u_nx^n,
$$

and $A$ is the identity matrix, whose rank is $n+1$. Therefore every nonadaptive polynomial chain evaluating a general polynomial of degree $n$ requires at least $n$ multiplications.

Since Horner's rule uses exactly $n$ multiplications, it is optimal.
