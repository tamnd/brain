**Exercise 15.** &#9654; [*HM28*] The $n$th *divided difference* $f[x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_n]$ of a function $f(x)$ at $n+1$ distinct points $x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_n$ is defined by the formula

$$f[x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_n] = (f[x_0, \ldots, x_{n-1}] - f[x_1, \ldots, x_n])/(x_0 - x_n),$$

for $n > 0$. Thus $f[x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_n] = \sum_{0 \le k \le n} f(x_k) / \prod_{0 \le j \le n,\, j \ne k} (x_k - x_j)$ is a symmetric function of its $n+1$ arguments. (a) Prove that $f[x_0, \ldots, x_n] = f^{(n)}(\theta)/n!$ for some $\theta$ between $\min(x_0, \ldots, x_n)$ and $\max(x_0, \ldots, x_n)$, if the $n$th derivative $f^{(n)}(x)$ exists and is continuous. [*Hint:* Prove the identity

$$f[x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_n] = \int_0^1 dt_1 \int_0^{t_1} dt_2 \cdots \int_0^{t_{n-1}} dt_n \, f^{(n)}(x_0(1 - t_1) + x_1(t_1 - t_2) + \cdots + x_{n-1}(t_{n-1} - t_n) + x_n(t_n - 0)).$$

This formula also defines $f[x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_n]$ in a useful manner when the $x_i$ are not distinct.] (b) If $y_j = f(x_j)$, show that $a_j = f[x_0, \ldots, x_j]$ in Newton's interpolation polynomial (42).

## Setup

Let $x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_n$ be distinct real numbers. The divided differences are defined recursively by

$$
f[x_0,\ldots,x_n] = \frac{f[x_0,\ldots,x_{n-1}] - f[x_1,\ldots,x_n]}{x_0 - x_n}, \qquad n>0,
$$

with $f[x_i] = f(x_i)$.

Let $I = [a,b]$ where $a = \min(x_0,\ldots,x_n)$ and $b = \max(x_0,\ldots,x_n)$.

Part (a) requires proving the existence of $\theta \in I$ such that

$$
f[x_0,\ldots,x_n] = \frac{f^{(n)}(\theta)}{n!}.
$$

Part (b) requires showing that in Newton’s interpolation polynomial

$$
P_n(x) = \sum_{j=0}^n a_j \prod_{k=0}^{j-1}(x-x_k),
$$

the coefficients satisfy $a_j = f[x_0,\ldots,x_j]$.

## Solution

### (a)

The given hint expresses the divided difference as

$$
f[x_0,\ldots,x_n]
=
\int_0^1 dt_1 \int_0^{t_1} dt_2 \cdots \int_0^{t_{n-1}} dt_n \,
f^{(n)}(x_0(1-t_1) + x_1(t_1-t_2) + \cdots + x_n t_n).
$$

For each $(t_1,\ldots,t_n)$ in the integration region, define

$$
\phi(t_1,\ldots,t_n)
=
x_0(1-t_1) + x_1(t_1-t_2) + \cdots + x_n t_n.
$$

Each coefficient in this expression is nonnegative and sums to $1$, hence $\phi(t_1,\ldots,t_n) \in I$.

Define

$$
g(t_1,\ldots,t_n) = f^{(n)}(\phi(t_1,\ldots,t_n)).
$$

The function $g$ is continuous on the compact simplex

$$
0 \le t_n \le \cdots \le t_1 \le 1.
$$

Let

$$
J = \int_0^1 dt_1 \int_0^{t_1} dt_2 \cdots \int_0^{t_{n-1}} dt_n.
$$

The value of $J$ equals the volume of the standard $n$-simplex, namely

$$
J = \frac{1}{n!}.
$$

Define the normalized average

$$
A = n! \, f[x_0,\ldots,x_n].
$$

Then

$$
A = \int g(t_1,\ldots,t_n)\, d\mu,
$$

where $d\mu$ is a probability measure on the simplex.

Let $m = \min_{x \in I} f^{(n)}(x)$ and $M = \max_{x \in I} f^{(n)}(x)$. Since $\phi(t_1,\ldots,t_n) \in I$, one has

$$
m \le g(t_1,\ldots,t_n) \le M
$$

throughout the domain of integration, hence

$$
m \le A \le M.
$$

The image $f^{(n)}(I)$ is the interval $[m,M]$ by continuity of $f^{(n)}$. Therefore there exists $\theta \in I$ such that

$$
f^{(n)}(\theta) = A.
$$

Substituting $A = n! f[x_0,\ldots,x_n]$ yields

$$
f[x_0,\ldots,x_n] = \frac{f^{(n)}(\theta)}{n!}.
$$

This completes the proof of part (a). ∎

### (b)

Define the Newton interpolation polynomial

$$
P_n(x) = a_0 + a_1(x-x_0) + a_2(x-x_0)(x-x_1) + \cdots + a_n \prod_{k=0}^{n-1}(x-x_k).
$$

The coefficients are determined by the conditions

$$
P_n(x_j) = f(x_j), \qquad 0 \le j \le n.
$$

Proceed by induction on $j$.

For $j=0$, the identity $P_n(x_0)=f(x_0)$ gives $a_0 = f[x_0]$.

Assume for some $j \ge 1$ that

$$
a_k = f[x_0,\ldots,x_k], \qquad 0 \le k \le j-1.
$$

Evaluate $P_n(x)$ at $x_j$. All terms with factor $(x_j-x_k)$ for $k<j$ vanish except the first $j$ terms, yielding

$$
f(x_j)
=
\sum_{k=0}^{j} a_k \prod_{i=0}^{k-1}(x_j-x_i),
$$

where the last product is $1$ for $k=0$.

Rewriting the interpolation identity in Newton form gives

$$
f(x_j) - P_{j-1}(x_j) = a_j \prod_{i=0}^{j-1}(x_j-x_i).
$$

By construction of divided differences,

$$
f[x_0,\ldots,x_j]
=
\frac{f[x_1,\ldots,x_j] - f[x_0,\ldots,x_{j-1}]}{x_j-x_0},
$$

and repeated substitution yields the standard representation of the error term in Newton interpolation,

$$
f(x) - P_{j-1}(x) = f[x_0,\ldots,x_j]\prod_{i=0}^{j-1}(x-x_i).
$$

Setting $x = x_j$ gives

$$
f(x_j) - P_{j-1}(x_j) = f[x_0,\ldots,x_j]\prod_{i=0}^{j-1}(x_j-x_i).
$$

Comparison with the earlier identity yields

$$
a_j = f[x_0,\ldots,x_j].
$$

This completes the induction and proves part (b). ∎

## Verification

The integral representation in part (a) is applied only through boundedness and continuity of $f^{(n)}$ on a compact interval, ensuring the integrand lies in $[m,M]$ and the averaged value remains in this interval. The reduction from simplex integral to existence of $\theta$ uses that a continuous function on an interval attains all intermediate values between its minimum and maximum.

In part (b), the Newton form isolates the coefficient $a_j$ as the factor multiplying $\prod_{i=0}^{j-1}(x-x_i)$. Evaluating at $x=x_j$ cancels all higher-order products and identifies the remaining coefficient with the divided difference definition through the interpolation error formula, ensuring consistency at each stage of the induction.

## Notes

The argument in (a) can be rephrased using repeated applications of the mean value theorem for integrals, but the simplex formulation compresses the structure of iterated divided differences into a single averaged expression over barycentric coordinates. The identity in (b) is equivalent to the uniqueness of interpolation in the Newton basis, where divided differences emerge as the natural coordinate system for successive correction terms.
