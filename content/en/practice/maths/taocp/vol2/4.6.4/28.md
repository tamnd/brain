[Section 4.6.4: Evaluation of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 28.** [*HM20*] Show that if $f_0(\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_s), \ldots, f_r(\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_s)$ are multivariate polynomials with integer coefficients, then there is a nonzero polynomial $g(x_0, \ldots, x_r)$ with integer coefficients such that $g(f_0(\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_s), \ldots, f_r(\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_s)) = 0$ for all real $\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_s$. (Hence any polynomial chain with $s$ parameters has at most $s$ degrees of freedom.) *Hint:* Use the theorems about "algebraic dependence" that are found, for example, in B. L. van der Waerden's *Modern Algebra*, translated by Fred Blum (New York: Ungar, 1949), Section 64.

## Solution

Let $f_0, \ldots, f_r$ be multivariate polynomials with integer coefficients in the variables $\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_s$. We wish to show that there exists a nonzero polynomial $g(x_0, \ldots, x_r)$, also with integer coefficients, such that

$g(f_0(\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_s), \ldots, f_r(\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_s)) = 0$

for all real $\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_s$.

The statement is equivalent to asserting that the $r+1$ polynomials $f_0, \ldots, f_r$ are algebraically dependent over the integers. By the argument in Section 4.6.4 and the references cited, any collection of more polynomials than variables is necessarily algebraically dependent.

We proceed as follows. Consider the polynomial ring $\mathbb{Z}[\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_s]$. Each $f_j \in \mathbb{Z}[\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_s]$, and hence every monomial of $f_j$ is of the form $c \alpha_1^{k_1} \cdots \alpha_s^{k_s}$ with $c \in \mathbb{Z}$ and $k_i \ge 0$. The number of distinct monomials of total degree at most $d$ in $s$ variables is finite, namely

$\binom{d+s}{s}.$

Choose a sufficiently large degree $D$ such that every $f_j$ has total degree at most $D$. Consider the set of all monomials in $x_0, \ldots, x_r$ of total degree at most $N$, where $N$ will be determined shortly. When we evaluate these monomials at $(f_0, \ldots, f_r)$, we obtain polynomials in $\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_s$ whose total degrees are bounded by $DN$.

Let $M = \binom{DN+s}{s}$ be the number of distinct monomials of total degree at most $DN$ in $\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_s$. Let $K = \binom{N + r}{r}$ be the number of monomials of total degree at most $N$ in $x_0, \ldots, x_r$. For $N$ sufficiently large, we have $K > M$. Then the set of polynomials

$\{x_0^{i_0} x_1^{i_1} \cdots x_r^{i_r} \mid i_0 + \cdots + i_r \le N\}$

evaluated at $(f_0, \ldots, f_r)$ produces more polynomials in $\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_s$ than there are distinct monomials of degree at most $DN$. Therefore, these evaluated polynomials are linearly dependent over $\mathbb{Z}$. Explicitly, there exist integers $c_{i_0, \ldots, i_r}$, not all zero, such that

$\sum_{i_0 + \cdots + i_r \le N} c_{i_0, \ldots, i_r} f_0^{i_0} \cdots f_r^{i_r} = 0.$

Define

$g(x_0, \ldots, x_r) = \sum_{i_0 + \cdots + i_r \le N} c_{i_0, \ldots, i_r} x_0^{i_0} \cdots x_r^{i_r}.$

Then $g$ is a nonzero polynomial with integer coefficients satisfying

$g(f_0(\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_s), \ldots, f_r(\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_s)) = 0$

for all real $\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_s$.

Consequently, any polynomial chain with $s$ parameters cannot have more than $s$ degrees of freedom, because any set of $r+1 > s$ polynomial outputs is algebraically dependent on the $s$ input parameters. This completes the proof.
