[Section 4.6.4: Evaluation of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 4.** [*M20*] The text shows that scheme (3) is superior to Horner's rule when we are evaluating a polynomial with real coefficients at a complex point $z$. Compare (3) to Horner's rule when *both* the coefficients and the variable $z$ are complex numbers; how many (real) multiplications and addition-subtractions are required by each method?

## Solution

Let

$u(z) = u_n z^n + u_{n-1} z^{n-1} + \cdots + u_1 z + u_0$

be a polynomial of degree $n$, where each coefficient $u_k$ is complex and $z = x + iy$ is a complex variable. Denote $x = \operatorname{Re}(z)$ and $y = \operatorname{Im}(z)$, and write $u_k = a_k + ib_k$ with $a_k, b_k \in \mathbb{R}$. We compare two evaluation schemes: Horner's rule (2) applied directly to complex arithmetic, and the method (3) generalized to complex coefficients.

### Horner's rule for complex coefficients

Applying (2) directly to complex coefficients, we compute

$$
u(z) = (\ldots((u_n z + u_{n-1}) z + u_{n-2}) z + \cdots + u_1) z + u_0.
$$

At each step, we perform one complex multiplication and one complex addition. Using the standard formula for multiplication of complex numbers:

$$
(a + ib)(c + id) = (ac - bd) + i(ad + bc),
$$

each complex multiplication requires $4$ real multiplications and $2$ real additions. Each complex addition requires $2$ real additions. Therefore, evaluating $u(z)$ of degree $n$ requires exactly $n$ complex multiplications and $n$ complex additions, which corresponds to

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{real multiplications} &= 4n,\\
\text{real additions} &= 2n \text{ (from multiplications) } + 2n \text{ (from additions) } = 4n.
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, Horner's rule for complex coefficients requires $4n$ real multiplications and $4n$ real additions.

### Method (3) generalized to complex coefficients

Method (3) in the text is

$$
\begin{cases}
a_1 = u_n, \quad b_1 = u_{n-1}, \quad r = 2x, \quad s = x^2 + y^2,\\
a_j = b_{j-1} + r a_{j-1}, \quad b_j = u_{n-j} - a_{j-2} - a_{j-1}, \quad 1 < j \le n.
\end{cases}
$$

We generalize it to complex coefficients by letting $a_j, b_j \in \mathbb{C}$ and defining $r = 2 \operatorname{Re}(z)$, $s = |z|^2 = x^2 + y^2$, as before. Each update of $a_j$ involves one complex multiplication by $r$ (a real number) and one complex addition. Each update of $b_j$ involves two complex subtractions (equivalent to two complex additions). Counting real operations:

- Multiplication of a complex number by a real number requires $2$ real multiplications and $0$ real additions (or $1$ addition if one counts accumulating).
- Complex addition or subtraction requires $2$ real additions.

For each $j = 2, \dots, n$, we perform:

1. $a_j = b_{j-1} + r a_{j-1}$: one complex × real multiplication ($2$ real multiplications) plus one complex addition ($2$ real additions).
2. $b_j = u_{n-j} - a_{j-2} - a_{j-1}$: two complex additions/subtractions ($4$ real additions).

The initial step $a_1 = u_n, b_1 = u_{n-1}$ involves no multiplications and one complex addition if we compute $r a_1$ at $j=2$. Finally, the value $u(z) = z a_n + b_n$ requires one complex multiplication and one complex addition, totaling $4$ real multiplications and $4$ real additions.

Adding contributions for all $n$ steps:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{real multiplications} &= 2(n-1) + 4 = 2n + 2,\\
\text{real additions} &= 2(n-1) + 4(n-1) + 4 = 6n - 2.
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus the generalized method (3) requires $2n + 2$ real multiplications and $6n - 2$ real additions.

### Comparison

- Horner's rule: $4n$ real multiplications, $4n$ real additions.
- Generalized method (3): $2n + 2$ real multiplications, $6n - 2$ real additions.

Hence, the generalized method reduces the number of multiplications nearly by a factor of $2$ at the expense of increasing the number of additions. When multiplications are significantly more costly than additions, method (3) is superior, particularly for large $n$.

This completes the proof.
