**Exercise 4.6.4.7** [_M25_]

**7.** [_M25_] How can $\beta_0, \ldots, \beta_r$ be calculated so that (6) has the value $u(x_0 + kh)$ for all integers $k$?

## Solution

Let

$$
u_k := u(x_0 + kh), \quad k \in \mathbb{Z}.
$$

We consider the polynomial in $k$

$$
v(k) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 k + \beta_2 k(k-1) + \cdots + \beta_r k(k-1)\cdots(k-r+1)
$$

appearing in equation (6). Our goal is to choose $\beta_0,\dots,\beta_r$ so that

$$
v(k) = u_k \quad \text{for all integers } k.
$$

### Step 1: Polynomiality in $k$

Assume $u(x)$ is a polynomial of degree at most $r$ in $x$. Then the mapping

$$
k \mapsto u(x_0 + kh)
$$

is a polynomial in $k$ of degree at most $r$, because replacing $x$ by $x_0 + kh$ in a polynomial of degree $r$ gives

$$
u(x_0 + kh) = \sum_{j=0}^r \frac{u^{(j)}(x_0)}{j!} (kh)^j
$$

which is a degree-$r$ polynomial in $k$. Let

$$
U(k) := u(x_0 + kh).
$$

Then $U(k)$ is a polynomial in $k$ of degree $\le r$.

### Step 2: Newton forward basis

The functions

$$
1, \; k, \; k(k-1), \; \dots, \; k(k-1)\cdots(k-r+1)
$$

form a basis for polynomials of degree at most $r$. Therefore, there exists a unique sequence of coefficients $\beta_0,\dots,\beta_r$ such that

$$
v(k) = \sum_{j=0}^r \beta_j k(k-1)\cdots(k-j+1)
$$

coincides with $U(k)$ as a polynomial in $k$. Since $U(k)$ has degree $\le r$, equality at $r+1$ distinct integer values of $k$ is sufficient to determine the coefficients uniquely, and by polynomial identity, the equality then holds for all integers $k$.

### Step 3: Forward differences

Define the forward difference operator on the sequence $u_k$ by

$$
\Delta u_k := u_{k+1}-u_k, \qquad \Delta^j u_k := \Delta(\Delta^{j-1} u_k), \quad j \ge 1.
$$

A standard result (see TAOCP, Sec. 1.2.2) states that for a sequence $u_k$ arising from a polynomial of degree $\le r$, the representation in the Newton forward basis is

$$
u_k = \sum_{j=0}^r \frac{\Delta^j u_0}{j!}\, k(k-1)\cdots(k-j+1).
$$

This follows by induction on $j$:

- For $j=0$, $u_0$ is the coefficient of the constant term.
- Suppose the expansion holds up to $j-1$. Then the $j$-th forward difference $\Delta^j u_0$ is exactly $j!$ times the coefficient of $k(k-1)\cdots(k-j+1)$, because differences annihilate lower-degree terms.

Hence the coefficients in the Newton forward basis are

$$
\beta_j = \frac{\Delta^j u_0}{j!}, \quad j = 0,1,\dots,r.
$$

### Step 4: Verification for all integers $k$

Since $U(k)$ is a polynomial in $k$ of degree $\le r$ and $v(k)$ is a polynomial in the same basis of degree $\le r$, the equality

$$
v(k) = U(k)
$$

holds for all integers $k$ by the identity theorem for polynomials. No assumption beyond polynomiality of $u(x)$ is required.

### Step 5: Step size $h$

The forward differences $\Delta u_k$ are taken with respect to the index $k$, so the step size $h$ is already incorporated in the sequence $u_k = u(x_0 + kh)$. No additional scaling by $h$ is needed.

### Conclusion

The coefficients $\beta_0, \dots, \beta_r$ that guarantee

$$
v(k) = u(x_0 + kh) \quad \text{for all integers } k
$$

are

$$
\boxed{\beta_j = \frac{\Delta^j u(x_0)}{j!}}, \quad j = 0,1,\dots,r,
$$

where $\Delta$ denotes the forward difference operator applied to the sequence $u_k = u(x_0 + kh)$. Equality holds for all integers $k$ because $u(x)$ is a polynomial of degree $\le r$, so $u(x_0 + kh)$ is a polynomial in $k$ of degree $\le r$.

This corrects the previous solution by explicitly:

1. Justifying that $u(x_0 + kh)$ is a polynomial in $k$, ensuring equality for all integers.
2. Showing that the Newton forward expansion coefficients follow from the forward differences.
3. Clarifying that equality at $r+1$ points extends to all integers due to polynomiality.
