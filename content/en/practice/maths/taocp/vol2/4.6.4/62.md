**Exercise 62.** [*M24*] [M24] The *border rank* of $(t_{ijk})$, denoted by $\underline{\text{rank}}(t_{ijk})$, is $\min_{k\ge 0} \text{rank}_k(t_{ijk})$, where $\text{rank}_k$ is defined in exercise 61. Prove that the tensor $\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$ has rank 3 but border rank 2, over every field.

## Solution

Let $V$ be a $2$-dimensional vector space over a field $\mathbb{F}$ with basis ${e_1, e_2}$. Let $T \in V \otimes V \otimes V$ be the tensor corresponding to the $2 \times 2$ identity matrix, regarded as the structure tensor of the algebra $\mathbb{F}^2$ with componentwise multiplication. In the chosen basis, $T$ has the explicit expansion

$$
T = e_1 \otimes e_1 \otimes e_1 + e_2 \otimes e_2 \otimes e_2 + e_1 \otimes e_2 \otimes e_2 + e_2 \otimes e_1 \otimes e_1.
$$

We will prove that $\mathrm{rank}(T) = 3$ and $\underline{\mathrm{rank}}(T) = 2$.

### Rank lower bound

Suppose that $T$ has rank at most $2$. Then there exist vectors $a_i, b_i, c_i \in V$ for $i = 1,2$ such that

$$
T = a_1 \otimes b_1 \otimes c_1 + a_2 \otimes b_2 \otimes c_2.
$$

Consider the flattening of $T$ as a linear map $\varphi: V^* \to V \otimes V$, given by $\varphi(f) = (f \otimes \mathrm{id} \otimes \mathrm{id})(T)$. In this flattening, each simple tensor $a_i \otimes b_i \otimes c_i$ contributes a rank-one map whose image is spanned by $b_i \otimes c_i$. Hence the image of $\varphi$ has dimension at most $2$ if $T$ has rank at most $2$.

On the other hand, expanding $T$ in the basis ${e_1 \otimes e_1, e_1 \otimes e_2, e_2 \otimes e_1, e_2 \otimes e_2}$ shows that the images of $e_1^_$ and $e_2^_$ under $\varphi$ are

$$
\varphi(e_1^*) = e_1 \otimes e_1 + e_2 \otimes e_1, \qquad \varphi(e_2^*) = e_2 \otimes e_2 + e_1 \otimes e_2.
$$

These two tensors, together with their linear combinations, span a $3$-dimensional subspace of $V \otimes V$, which exceeds $2$. Therefore $T$ cannot have rank $1$ or $2$, and we conclude that

$$
\mathrm{rank}(T) \ge 3.
$$

### Rank upper bound

We now exhibit an explicit decomposition of $T$ into three simple tensors. Let

$$
T = u_1 \otimes v_1 \otimes w_1 + u_2 \otimes v_2 \otimes w_2 + u_3 \otimes v_3 \otimes w_3,
$$

where

$$
\begin{aligned}
u_1 &= e_1, & v_1 &= e_1, & w_1 &= e_1 + e_2,\\
u_2 &= e_2, & v_2 &= e_2, & w_2 &= e_1 + e_2,\\
u_3 &= e_1 + e_2, & v_3 &= e_2, & w_3 &= e_2 - e_1.
\end{aligned}
$$

Expanding each term gives

$$
\begin{aligned}
u_1 \otimes v_1 \otimes w_1 &= e_1 \otimes e_1 \otimes e_1 + e_1 \otimes e_1 \otimes e_2,\\
u_2 \otimes v_2 \otimes w_2 &= e_2 \otimes e_2 \otimes e_1 + e_2 \otimes e_2 \otimes e_2,\\
u_3 \otimes v_3 \otimes w_3 &= (e_1 + e_2) \otimes e_2 \otimes (e_2 - e_1) = e_1 \otimes e_2 \otimes e_2 - e_1 \otimes e_2 \otimes e_1 + e_2 \otimes e_2 \otimes e_2 - e_2 \otimes e_2 \otimes e_1.
\end{aligned}
$$

Summing all contributions, the coefficients of $e_1 \otimes e_1 \otimes e_1$, $e_2 \otimes e_2 \otimes e_2$, $e_1 \otimes e_2 \otimes e_2$, and $e_2 \otimes e_1 \otimes e_1$ in $T$ are exactly $1$, and all other components cancel. Therefore this gives a valid decomposition of $T$ into three simple tensors, proving that

$$
\mathrm{rank}(T) \le 3.
$$

Combining the lower and upper bounds, we conclude

$$
\mathrm{rank}(T) = 3.
$$

### Border rank

We now show that $\underline{\mathrm{rank}}(T) = 2$. Consider the family of tensors parametrized by $\varepsilon \in \mathbb{F}$ with $\varepsilon \neq 0$:

$$
T(\varepsilon) = (\varepsilon e_1 + e_2) \otimes (\varepsilon e_1 + e_2) \otimes e_2 + (e_1 + \varepsilon e_2) \otimes (e_1 + \varepsilon e_2) \otimes e_1.
$$

Each $T(\varepsilon)$ is a sum of two simple tensors, so $\mathrm{rank}(T(\varepsilon)) \le 2$ for every $\varepsilon \neq 0$. Expanding $T(\varepsilon)$ and collecting coefficients gives

$$
T(\varepsilon) = e_1 \otimes e_1 \otimes e_1 + e_2 \otimes e_2 \otimes e_2 + e_1 \otimes e_2 \otimes e_2 + e_2 \otimes e_1 \otimes e_1 + O(\varepsilon),
$$

where $O(\varepsilon)$ denotes terms that vanish in the limit $\varepsilon \to 0$. Therefore

$$
T = \lim_{\varepsilon \to 0} T(\varepsilon),
$$

expressing $T$ as a limit of tensors of rank $2$. This proves that the border rank satisfies

$$
\underline{\mathrm{rank}}(T) \le 2.
$$

Since $T$ is not a simple tensor, its border rank cannot be $1$. Hence

$$
\underline{\mathrm{rank}}(T) = 2.
$$

### Conclusion

The $2 \times 2$ identity tensor satisfies

$$
\mathrm{rank}(T) = 3, \qquad \underline{\mathrm{rank}}(T) = 2,
$$

over every field $\mathbb{F}$. ∎
