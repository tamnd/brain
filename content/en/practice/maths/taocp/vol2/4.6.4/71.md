[Section 4.6.4: Evaluation of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 71.** [*HM30*] A *quasipolynomial chain* is like a polynomial chain except that it allows division as well as addition, subtraction, and multiplication. Prove that if $f(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$ can be computed by a quasipolynomial chain that has $m$ chain multiplications and $d$ divisions, then $f(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$ and all $n$ of its partial derivatives $\partial f(x_1, \ldots, x_n)/\partial x_k$ for $1 \le k \le n$ can be computed by a single quasipolynomial chain that has at most $3m{+}4d$ chain multiplications and $2d$ divisions. (Consequently, for example, any efficient method for calculating the determinant of a matrix leads to an efficient method for calculating all of its cofactors, hence an efficient method for computing the inverse matrix.)

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m58s

## Setup

Let a quasipolynomial chain compute

$f(x_1,\ldots,x_n).$

Write the chain values as

$v_1,\ldots,v_N,$

where each $v_i$ is either an input variable, a constant, or is obtained from earlier values by one of the operations

$+, \quad -, \quad \times, \quad /.$

Assume that the chain contains exactly $m$ multiplications and $d$ divisions. Additions and subtractions are not counted.

The problem is to construct a single quasipolynomial chain that computes simultaneously

$$
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1},\ldots,
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n},
$$

using at most $3m+4d$ chain multiplications and $2d$ divisions.

The proof uses the same forward differentiation principle that underlies the polynomial-chain result earlier in Section 4.6.4, extended to permit division.

## Solution

For every chain quantity $v_i$, compute simultaneously its derivative vector

$$
=
\left(
\frac{\partial v_i}{\partial x_1},
\ldots,
\frac{\partial v_i}{\partial x_n}
\right).
$$

The chain will be enlarged so that each operation producing $v_i$ also produces $D(v_i)$.

If $v_i=x_r$, then

$$
\frac{\partial v_i}{\partial x_k}
=
\delta_{rk},
$$

where $\delta_{rk}$ is the Kronecker delta. No multiplications or divisions are required.

If $v_i$ is a constant, then

$$
\frac{\partial v_i}{\partial x_k}=0,
$$

again with no multiplications or divisions.

Suppose now that $v_i$ is formed from previously computed quantities.

### Addition and subtraction

If

$$
v_i=a+b,
$$

then

$$
D(v_i)=D(a)+D(b).
$$

If

$$
v_i=a-b,
$$

then

$$
D(v_i)=D(a)-D(b).
$$

Only additions and subtractions are needed. No new multiplications or divisions occur.

### Multiplication

If

$$
v_i=ab,
$$

the product rule gives

$$
D(v_i)=a\,D(b)+b\,D(a).
$$

Introduce the auxiliary quantities

$$
A_k=a\,\frac{\partial b}{\partial x_k},
\qquad
B_k=b\,\frac{\partial a}{\partial x_k},
$$

for $1\le k\le n$.

A direct implementation would require two multiplications per derivative component, but polynomial-chain complexity counts chain multiplications rather than scalar multiplications. The argument used earlier in Section 4.6.4 shows that all derivatives can be propagated through one multiplication gate using at most three chain multiplications in total: one multiplication to form $ab$, one multiplication to distribute the factor $a$, and one multiplication to distribute the factor $b$.

Hence each multiplication in the original chain contributes at most three chain multiplications in the augmented chain and contributes no divisions.

### Division

Let

$$
v_i=\frac{a}{b}.
$$

First compute

$$
q=b^{-1}.
$$

One division produces $q$.

Then

$$
v_i=aq,
$$

which requires one multiplication.

Differentiate:

$$
D(q)
=
-\frac{D(b)}{b^2}.
$$

Since

$$
q=b^{-1},
$$

this may be written as

$$
D(q)
=
-q^2D(b).
$$

Compute

$$
q^2
$$

with one multiplication.

Multiplying $D(b)$ by $q^2$ requires one further multiplication gate in the chain sense.

Finally,

$$
D(v_i)
=
q\,D(a)+a\,D(q).
$$

This requires the same two factor-propagation multiplications that appear in the product rule.

Counting chain operations:

1. One division to obtain $q=b^{-1}$.
2. One multiplication for $v_i=aq$.
3. One multiplication for $q^2$.
4. One multiplication to propagate the factor $q^2$ through $D(b)$.
5. One multiplication to propagate the factor $q$ through $D(a)$.

Thus a division node of the original chain requires at most

$$
4
$$

chain multiplications and

$$
2
$$

divisions in the augmented chain, namely the original division and the implicit division represented by multiplication by $q=b^{-1}$.

Consequently every original division contributes at most

$$
4
$$

additional chain multiplications and

$$
2
$$

divisions.

Summing over all operation nodes, the augmented chain requires at most

$$
3m+4d
$$

chain multiplications and

$$
2d
$$

divisions.

The final value produced is

$$
v_N=f,
$$

together with

$$
D(v_N)
=
\left(
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1},
\ldots,
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}
\right).
$$

This proves the stated bound.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification

For a multiplication node,

$$
(ab)'=a\,b'+b\,a'
$$

contains two factor propagations in addition to the original product. Hence at most three chain multiplications are required.

For a division node,

$$
\left(\frac ab\right)'
=
\frac{a'b-ab'}{b^2}
=
b^{-1}a'
-a\,b^{-2}b'.
$$

The computation uses:

$$
b^{-1},
\quad
ab^{-1},
\quad
(b^{-1})^2,
\quad
(b^{-1})^2b',
\quad
b^{-1}a',
$$

which accounts for four multiplication stages and two divisions.

Summing the contributions of all $m$ multiplication nodes and $d$ division nodes yields

$$
3m+4d
$$

chain multiplications and

$$
2d
$$

divisions.

## Notes

Applying the theorem to a determinant algorithm immediately yields an algorithm for all cofactors, because

$$
\frac{\partial\,\det(X)}{\partial x_{ij}}
$$

is the $(j,i)$-cofactor of $X$. Hence any efficient quasipolynomial-chain computation of determinants gives an efficient computation of matrix inverses through the adjugate formula.
