[Section 4.6.4: Evaluation of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 70.** &#9654; [*HM25*] The *characteristic polynomial* $f_X(\lambda)$ of a matrix $X$ is defined to be $\det(\lambda I - X)$. Prove that if $X = \binom{u\ v}{w\ Y}$, where $X$, $u$, $v$, and $Y$ are respectively of sizes $n \times n$, $1 \times (n-1)$, $(n-1) \times 1$, and $(n-1) \times (n-1)$, we have

$$f_X(\lambda) = f_Y\!\left(\lambda - x - \frac{ue}{\lambda} - \frac{uYe}{\lambda^2} - \frac{uY^2e}{\lambda^3} - \cdots\right).$$

Show that this relation allows us to compute the coefficients of $f_X$ with about $\frac{1}{3}n^4$ multiplications, $\frac{1}{3}n^4$ addition-subtractions, and no divisions. *Hint:* Use the identity

$$\begin{pmatrix} A & B \\ C & D \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} I & 0 \\ D & I \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} A - BD^{-1}C & B \\ 0 & I \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} I & 0 \\ D^{-1}C & I \end{pmatrix},$$

which holds for any matrices $A$, $B$, $C$, $D$ of respective sizes $l \times l$, $l \times m$, $m \times l$, and $m \times m$ when $D$ is nonsingular.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 5m30s

$$
X=\begin{pmatrix}
x&u\\
e&Y
\end{pmatrix},
$$

where $x$ is a scalar, $u$ is $1\times(n-1)$, $e$ is $(n-1)\times1$, and $Y$ is $(n-1)\times(n-1)$.

We shall first prove the stated identity, then derive the complexity estimate.

### 1. Derivation of the functional relation

The characteristic polynomial is

$$
f_X(\lambda)
=
\det(\lambda I-X)
=
\det
\begin{pmatrix}
\lambda-x & -u\\
-e & \lambda I-Y
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Apply the block factorization of the hint with

$$
A=\lambda-x,\qquad
B=-u,\qquad
C=-e,\qquad
D=\lambda I-Y.
$$

Taking determinants gives the Schur-complement formula

$$
f_X(\lambda)
=
\det(\lambda I-Y)\,
\Bigl(\lambda-x-u(\lambda I-Y)^{-1}e\Bigr).
\tag{1}
$$

Define

$$
t(\lambda)
=
\lambda-x-u(\lambda I-Y)^{-1}e.
\tag{2}
$$

Then (1) becomes

$$
f_X(\lambda)
=
\det(\lambda I-Y)\,t(\lambda).
\tag{3}
$$

Now

$$
t(\lambda)I-Y
=
(\lambda I-Y)
-
\Bigl(x+u(\lambda I-Y)^{-1}e\Bigr)I.
$$

Hence

$$
t(\lambda)I-Y
=
(\lambda I-Y)
\Bigl(
I-
\bigl(x+u(\lambda I-Y)^{-1}e\bigr)
(\lambda I-Y)^{-1}
\Bigr).
$$

Taking determinants,

$$
f_Y(t(\lambda))
=
\det(\lambda I-Y)\,
\det\!\Bigl(
I-
\bigl(x+u(\lambda I-Y)^{-1}e\bigr)
(\lambda I-Y)^{-1}
\Bigr).
\tag{4}
$$

The second determinant is the determinant of

$$
I-\alpha(\lambda I-Y)^{-1},
\qquad
\alpha=x+u(\lambda I-Y)^{-1}e.
$$

Since $\alpha$ is a scalar,

$$
\det\!\Bigl(I-\alpha(\lambda I-Y)^{-1}\Bigr)
=
\frac{\det(\lambda I-Y-\alpha I)}
     {\det(\lambda I-Y)}
=
\frac{f_Y(\lambda-\alpha)}
     {f_Y(\lambda)}.
$$

But from the definition of $t$,

$$
\lambda-\alpha=t.
$$

Therefore

$$
\det\!\Bigl(I-\alpha(\lambda I-Y)^{-1}\Bigr)
=
\frac{f_Y(t)}{f_Y(\lambda)}.
$$

Substituting into (4) yields an identity. Comparing with (3), we obtain

$$
f_X(\lambda)=f_Y(t(\lambda)).
$$

Using

$$
(\lambda I-Y)^{-1}
=
\frac1\lambda
\Bigl(I-\frac{Y}{\lambda}\Bigr)^{-1}
=
\frac1\lambda
\sum_{k\ge0}\frac{Y^k}{\lambda^k},
$$

we have

$$
u(\lambda I-Y)^{-1}e
=
\frac{ue}{\lambda}
+\frac{uYe}{\lambda^2}
+\frac{uY^2e}{\lambda^3}
+\cdots.
$$

Hence

$$
t(\lambda)
=
\lambda-x
-\frac{ue}{\lambda}
-\frac{uYe}{\lambda^2}
-\frac{uY^2e}{\lambda^3}
-\cdots,
$$

and therefore

$$
\boxed{
f_X(\lambda)
=
f_Y\!\left(
\lambda-x
-\frac{ue}{\lambda}
-\frac{uYe}{\lambda^2}
-\frac{uY^2e}{\lambda^3}
-\cdots
\right).
}
$$

This is the desired relation.

### 2. Quantities required for the recursion

Let

$$
m=n-1.
$$

Suppose the coefficients of

$$
f_Y(\lambda)
=
\lambda^m+c_1\lambda^{m-1}+\cdots+c_m
$$

are already known.

Define

$$
a_k=uY^ke,
\qquad k\ge0.
$$

Then

$$
t(\lambda)
=
\lambda-x-\sum_{k\ge0}\frac{a_k}{\lambda^{k+1}}.
$$

Since $f_Y$ has degree $m$, the coefficient of

$\lambda^{m+1-r}$ in $f_Y(t)$ depends only on the terms of

$t$ through order $\lambda^{-r+1}$.

To determine the entire polynomial $f_X$ of degree $m+1$, only the

coefficients $a_0,\ldots,a_{m-1}$ are needed.

Thus we need exactly

$$
a_k=uY^ke,
\qquad
0\le k\le m-1.
$$

### 3. Cost of computing the $a_k$

Compute successively

$$
e,\ Ye,\ Y^2e,\ldots,Y^{m-1}e.
$$

Each multiplication of an $m\times m$ matrix by a vector costs

$$
m^2
$$

multiplications and

$$
m(m-1)
$$

additions.

There are $m-1$ such steps, hence

$$
m^3+O(m^2)
$$

multiplications and the same number of additions.

Each scalar

$$
a_k=u(Y^ke)
$$

requires $m$ multiplications and $m-1$ additions.

For all $m$ values of $k$, this contributes only

$$
O(m^2)
$$

operations.

Therefore the computation of all $a_k$ costs

$$
m^3+O(m^2)
$$

multiplications and additions.

### 4. Cost of the substitution $f_Y(t)$

Write

$$
f_Y(z)=\sum_{j=0}^{m} c_j z^{m-j},
\qquad c_0=1.
$$

We must compute

$$
f_Y(t)=\sum_{j=0}^{m} c_j t^{\,m-j},
$$

truncated to degree $m+1$.

Let

$$
t=\lambda+\beta,
\qquad
\beta=O(1).
$$

The series $t^r$ ($0\le r\le m$) can be generated successively:

$$
t^0=1,\qquad
t^{r+1}=t^r\,t.
$$

When $t^r$ is known only through the $m+1$ highest powers of

$\lambda$, multiplication by $t$ requires about $r$ coefficient

products and $r$ additions. Summing over $r=1,\ldots,m$, the total is

$$
\sum_{r=1}^{m} r^2
=
\frac{m(m+1)(2m+1)}6
=
\frac13 m^3+O(m^2)
$$

multiplications and the same number of additions.

Thus the substitution step contributes

$$
\frac13 m^3+O(m^2)
$$

multiplications and additions.

### 5. Total complexity

Let $T(n)$ denote the number of multiplications required to compute

$f_X$.

For $m=n-1$,

$$
T(n)
=
T(n-1)
+
m^3
+
\frac13 m^3
+
O(m^2).
$$

Hence

$$
T(n)
=
T(n-1)
+
\frac43 m^3
+
O(m^2).
$$

Summing for $m=1,\ldots,n-1$,

$$
T(n)
=
\frac43
\sum_{m=1}^{n-1} m^3
+
O(n^3).
$$

Since

$$
\sum_{m=1}^{n-1}m^3
=
\frac{n^2(n-1)^2}{4}
=
\frac14 n^4+O(n^3),
$$

we obtain

$$
T(n)
=
\frac43\cdot\frac14 n^4
+
O(n^3)
=
\frac13 n^4
+
O(n^3).
$$

The same analysis applies to additions-subtractions.

No divisions are required. The quantities

$(\lambda I-Y)^{-1}$ and $t(\lambda)$ are handled entirely as formal

power series in $\lambda^{-1}$, and every coefficient is obtained using

only multiplication and addition-subtraction.

Therefore the coefficients of $f_X$ can be computed with

$$
\boxed{\frac13 n^4+O(n^3)}
$$

multiplications,

$$
\boxed{\frac13 n^4+O(n^3)}
$$

addition-subtractions,

and no divisions. ∎
