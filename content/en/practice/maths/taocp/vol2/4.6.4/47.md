[Section 4.6.4: Evaluation of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 47.** [*M25*] Prove that for all $m$, $n$, and $s$ there exists an $m \times n \times s$ tensor whose rank is at least $\lfloor mns/(m + n + s) \rfloor$. Conversely, show that every $m \times n \times s$ tensor has rank at most $\min(mns/(m, n, s))$.

**Solve time:** 6m30s  

---

## Solution

Let $V=F^{m}\otimes F^{n}\otimes F^{s}$, the vector space of all $m\times n\times s$ tensors over a field $F$. Then

$$
\dim V=mns.
$$

A tensor has rank $1$ if it is of the form

$$
a\otimes b\otimes c,
$$

with $a\in F^m$, $b\in F^n$, $c\in F^s$. Its rank is the least $r$ for which it can be written as a sum of $r$ rank-one tensors.

### Existence of a tensor of rank at least $\left\lfloor \dfrac{mns}{m+n+s}\right\rfloor$

Fix $r\ge0$. Consider the polynomial map

$$
\Phi_r:
(F^m\times F^n\times F^s)^r \longrightarrow V,
$$

defined by

$$
\Phi_r\!\bigl((a^{(\nu)},b^{(\nu)},c^{(\nu)})_{1\le \nu\le r}\bigr)
=
\sum_{\nu=1}^{r}
a^{(\nu)}\otimes b^{(\nu)}\otimes c^{(\nu)}.
$$

Its image is precisely the set $W_r$ of tensors of rank at most $r$.

The domain has dimension

$$
r(m+n+s).
$$

A polynomial image cannot have dimension larger than its domain, hence

$$
\dim W_r \le r(m+n+s).
$$

Now let

$$
r_0=\left\lfloor\frac{mns}{m+n+s}\right\rfloor-1.
$$

Then

$$
r_0(m+n+s)<mns=\dim V.
$$

Therefore $W_{r_0}$ has dimension strictly smaller than $V$, so $W_{r_0}\neq V$. Hence some tensor is not of rank at most $r_0$, and therefore has rank at least

$$
r_0+1
=
\left\lfloor\frac{mns}{m+n+s}\right\rfloor.
$$

This proves the first assertion.

### Universal upper bound

Let

$$
d=(m,n,s).
$$

Every tensor $T=(t_{ijk})$ can be written as

$$
T
=
\sum_{i=1}^{m}\sum_{j=1}^{n}
e_i\otimes f_j\otimes
\Bigl(\sum_{k=1}^{s} t_{ijk}g_k\Bigr),
$$

where $e_i,f_j,g_k$ are the standard basis vectors.

Each summand

$$
e_i\otimes f_j\otimes
\Bigl(\sum_{k=1}^{s} t_{ijk}g_k\Bigr)
$$

has rank $1$. Since there are $mn$ such summands,

$$
\operatorname{rank}(T)\le mn.
$$

By symmetry,

$$
\operatorname{rank}(T)\le ms,
\qquad
\operatorname{rank}(T)\le ns.
$$

Hence

$$
\operatorname{rank}(T)
\le
\min(mn,ms,ns).
$$

Since $d=(m,n,s)$ divides each of $m,n,s$,

$$
d\le m,\qquad d\le n,\qquad d\le s.
$$

Therefore

$$
mn \le \frac{mns}{d},\qquad
ms \le \frac{mns}{d},\qquad
ns \le \frac{mns}{d}.
$$

Taking the minimum gives

$$
\min(mn,ms,ns)
\le
\frac{mns}{d}.
$$

Combining this with the previous inequality,

$$
\operatorname{rank}(T)
\le
\min(mn,ms,ns)
\le
\frac{mns}{(m,n,s)}.
$$

Thus every $m\times n\times s$ tensor satisfies

$$
\operatorname{rank}(T)\le \frac{mns}{(m,n,s)}.
$$

Hence:

$$
\exists\,T \text{ with }
\operatorname{rank}(T)\ge
\left\lfloor\frac{mns}{m+n+s}\right\rfloor,
$$

and every $m\times n\times s$ tensor satisfies

$$
\operatorname{rank}(T)\le
\frac{mns}{(m,n,s)}.
$$

$\square$
