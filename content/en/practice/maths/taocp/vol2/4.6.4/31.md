[Section 4.6.4: Evaluation of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 31.** [*M23*] Prove that a polynomial chain capable of computing all *monic* polynomials of degree $n$ must have $\lfloor n/2 \rfloor$ multiplications and at least $n$ addition-subtractions.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m07s  

---

## Solution

A polynomial chain computes expressions from the variable $x$ and parameters using additions, subtractions, and multiplications. A monic polynomial of degree $n$ has the form

$u(x) = x^n + u_{n-1}x^{n-1} + \cdots + u_1 x + u_0,$

and therefore requires the independent specification of the $n$ coefficients $u_0,\ldots,u_{n-1}$.

Each addition or subtraction in the chain is linear in previously constructed expressions, so it can introduce at most one new independent parameter. After $m_p$ additions-subtractions, at most $m_p$ independent parameters can enter the final expression. Since the coefficients $u_0,\ldots,u_{n-1}$ are independent, the chain must supply at least $n$ independent parameters. This yields

$m_p \ge n.$

To analyze multiplications, group every expression in the chain according to its degree in $x$. Each expression can be written uniquely as a finite sum of terms, each term being a parameter-dependent coefficient times a power of $x$. Write the result of the chain in the form

$u(x) = \sum_{k=0}^n a_k x^k,$

where each $a_k$ is a polynomial in the parameters introduced by the chain.

Consider how a multiplication affects the structure of these coefficient expressions. If two previously constructed expressions are multiplied, then each coefficient in the product is a sum of products of coefficients from the two factors. Each such multiplication couples two previously independent parameter expressions, and therefore each chain multiplication can introduce dependence between at most two previously independent parameter contributions into new coefficient positions. Consequently, each multiplication can increase the number of coefficient positions that can carry independent parameter information by at most $2$.

Initially, the chain contains only the single expression $x$, which provides at most one coefficient position in the sense of $x^1$. After $m_n$ chain multiplications, the total number of coefficient positions that can be independently controlled is therefore at most $2m_n + 1$.

A monic polynomial of degree $n$ has $n+1$ coefficient positions $x^n, x^{n-1}, \ldots, x^0$, but the leading coefficient is fixed equal to $1$, so only $n$ coefficient positions remain free. Therefore the chain must be capable of supporting at least $n$ independent coefficient positions, which implies

$2m_n + 1 \ge n.$

Solving for $m_n$ gives

$m_n \ge \frac{n-1}{2},$

hence

$m_n \ge \lfloor n/2 \rfloor.$

The two bounds together yield the required necessary conditions:

$m_n \ge \lfloor n/2 \rfloor, \qquad m_p \ge n.$

This completes the proof. ∎
