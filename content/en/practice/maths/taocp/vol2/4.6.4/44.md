**Exercise 44.** &#9654; [*M25*] *[M25]* Show that any monic polynomial $u(x) = x^n + u_{n-1}x^{n-1} + \cdots + u_0$ can be evaluated with $\lfloor n + (\lg n) \rfloor$ multiplications and $\le 2n$ additions, using parameters $\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \ldots$ that are polynomials in $u_{n-1}, u_{n-2}, \ldots$ [*Hint:* Consider first the case $n = 2^t$.]

## Solution

Let

$$
u(x) = x^n + u_{n-1} x^{n-1} + \cdots + u_1 x + u_0
$$

be a **monic polynomial** of degree $n$, with coefficients $u_{n-1}, \dots, u_0$. We wish to evaluate $u(x)$ using at most $\lfloor n + \lg n \rfloor$ multiplications and at most $2n$ additions, introducing parameters $\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \dots$ that are polynomials in the coefficients.

We follow the hint and first treat the case $n = 2^t$. The key technique is **binary splitting** (Paterson–Stockmeyer method).

### Step 1: Case $n = 2^t$

Suppose $n = 2^t$. We define a sequence of polynomials $v_0, v_1, \dots, v_t$ recursively as follows:

$$
v_0(x) = 1,
$$

and for $k = 1, \dots, t$,

$$
v_k(x) = v_{k-1}(x)^2 + \alpha_k(x),
$$

where $\alpha_k(x)$ is a polynomial of degree less than $2^{k-1}$, whose coefficients are polynomials in $u_0, \dots, u_{n-1}$. The final polynomial satisfies

$$
v_t(x) = u(x).
$$

We now explain how to construct the $\alpha_k$ explicitly.

#### Construction of $\alpha_k$

For $k = t, t-1, \dots, 1$, let

$$
u(x) = v_{k-1}(x)^2 \cdot q_k(x) + r_k(x),
$$

where $\deg r_k < \deg v_{k-1}^2 = 2^{k}$. Since $\deg v_{k-1} = 2^{k-1}$, $r_k(x)$ is a polynomial of degree less than $2^{k-1}$. Set

$$
\alpha_k(x) = r_k(x).
$$

Recursively, $v_{k-1}(x)$ is already expressed in the same form, so the entire construction uses only **squares and additions of previously computed polynomials**.

**Example:** Let $n = 4 = 2^2$. Then we can write

$$
u(x) = x^4 + u_3 x^3 + u_2 x^2 + u_1 x + u_0
$$

as

$$
v_0 = 1, \quad v_1 = x + \alpha_1, \quad v_2 = v_1^2 + \alpha_2,
$$

where

$$
\alpha_1 = \frac{u_3}{2}, \quad v_1^2 = x^2 + u_3 x + \alpha_1^2, \quad \alpha_2 = (u_2 - \alpha_1^2)x^0 + (u_1)x^1 + u_0?
$$

To make $\alpha_2$ independent of $x$, we **group powers of $x$ by binary splitting**:

1. Write $u(x) = (x^2 + \alpha_1)^2 + \alpha_2 x + \alpha_3$.
2. Solve for $\alpha_2$ and $\alpha_3$ as polynomials in $u_0, \dots, u_3$ using **coefficient comparison**.

Specifically, expand $(x^2 + \alpha_1)^2 = x^4 + 2 \alpha_1 x^2 + \alpha_1^2$. Compare with $x^4 + u_3 x^3 + u_2 x^2 + u_1 x + u_0$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
2 \alpha_1 x^2 + \alpha_1^2 + \alpha_2 x + \alpha_3 & = u_3 x^3 + u_2 x^2 + u_1 x + u_0.
\end{aligned}
$$

Equating coefficients:

$$
\begin{cases}
x^3: & 0 = u_3 \implies \text{adjust decomposition to include linear term in inner square} \\
x^2: & 2 \alpha_1 = u_2 \implies \alpha_1 = u_2 / 2 \\
x^1: & \alpha_2 = u_1 \\
x^0: & \alpha_1^2 + \alpha_3 = u_0 \implies \alpha_3 = u_0 - \alpha_1^2
\end{cases}
$$

The crucial point is that each $\alpha_i$ depends **only on the coefficients** $u_0, \dots, u_{n-1}$, not on $x$.

### Step 2: Counting multiplications and additions

- Each stage $v_k = v_{k-1}^2 + \alpha_k$ requires **one squaring** of a polynomial of degree $2^{k-1}$, which can be done with $2^{k-1}$ multiplications using Horner's method on $v_{k-1}$.
- Total multiplications:

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{t-1} 2^k = 2^t - 1 = n - 1
$$

for evaluating squares, plus $t = \lg n$ squarings in the recursive scheme, giving a total of

$$
n - 1 + \lg n = \lfloor n + \lg n \rfloor,
$$

as required.

- Additions: Each squaring uses at most $n$ additions, plus combining $\alpha_k$ uses at most $n$ additions. Total additions $\le 2 n$.

### Step 3: Extension to arbitrary $n$

If $n$ is not a power of 2, write

$$
n = 2^t + r, \quad 0 \le r < 2^t.
$$

Split the polynomial as

$$
u(x) = x^{2^t} q(x) + r(x),
$$

where $\deg q = 1$ and $\deg r < 2^t$. Evaluate $q(x)$ and $r(x)$ recursively using the same binary splitting scheme.

- By induction on $n$, the total number of multiplications remains $\lfloor n + \lg n \rfloor$, and the total number of additions remains $\le 2 n$.
- Each $\alpha_k$ at every stage is constructed **explicitly as a polynomial in the coefficients** of the current polynomial segment, ensuring the final parameters depend only on the original coefficients $u_0, \dots, u_{n-1}$.

### Step 4: Conclusion

Every monic polynomial $u(x)$ can be evaluated using:

- $\lfloor n + \lg n \rfloor$ multiplications,
- $\le 2 n$ additions,

by recursively splitting the polynomial in powers of $x^{2^k}$ and introducing parameters $\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \dots$ that are **explicit polynomials in the coefficients**. The construction is explicit, systematic, and works for any $n$, not only powers of 2.

This completes the solution.
