**Exercise 10.** [*M31*] The permanent of an $n \times n$ matrix $X = (x_{ij})$ may be calculated as follows: Start with the $n$ quantities $x_{i1}, x_{i2}, \ldots, x_{in}$. For $1 \le k \le n$, assume that the $\binom{n}{k}$ quantities $A_{kS}$ have been computed, for all $k$-element subsets $S$ of $\{1, 2, \ldots, n\}$, where $A_{kS} = \sum x_{1j_1} \ldots x_{kj_k}$ summed over all $k!$ permutations $j_1 \ldots j_k$ of the elements of $S$; then form

$$A_{(k+1)\tilde{S}} = \sum_{j \in \tilde{S}} x_{(k+1)j} A_{kS}$$

We have $\text{per}(X) = A_{\{1,2,\ldots,n\}}$. How many additions and multiplications does this method require? How much temporary storage is needed?

## Solution

Let $X = (x_{ij})$ be an $n \times n$ matrix. We follow the procedure described in the exercise.

### Step 1: Definition of $A_{kS}$

For $1 \le k \le n$ and for any $k$-element subset $S \subseteq \{1, 2, \ldots, n\}$, define

$$
A_{kS} = \sum_{\text{all permutations } (j_1, \ldots, j_k) \text{ of } S} x_{1j_1} x_{2j_2} \cdots x_{kj_k}.
$$

These are the sums over all products of the first $k$ rows of $X$, taking one element from each column in $S$ exactly once.

### Step 2: Recurrence

Given the quantities $A_{kS}$ for all $k$-element subsets $S$, the recurrence is

$$
A_{(k+1)\tilde{S}} = \sum_{j \in \tilde{S}} x_{(k+1)j} A_{k,\tilde{S}\setminus \{j\}}
$$

for each $(k+1)$-element subset $\tilde{S}$.

This recurrence correctly expresses $A_{(k+1)\tilde{S}}$ as a sum over all choices of the last column $j$ in $\tilde{S}$, multiplied by the previously computed $A_{kS}$.

### Step 3: Number of multiplications and additions

#### Step 3a: Multiplications

For each $(k+1)$-element subset $\tilde{S}$, the recurrence requires multiplying $x_{(k+1)j}$ by $A_{k, \tilde{S}\setminus \{j\}}$ for each $j \in \tilde{S}$. There are $k+1$ terms, so

$$
\text{Multiplications per subset} = k+1.
$$

The number of $(k+1)$-element subsets is $\binom{n}{k+1}$. Therefore, the total number of multiplications for level $k+1$ is

$$
M_{k+1} = (k+1)\binom{n}{k+1}.
$$

Summing over $k = 0, 1, \ldots, n-1$ (with $k=0$ corresponding to the first row, where we treat $A_{0\emptyset}=1$), we obtain

$$
\text{Total multiplications} = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} (k+1)\binom{n}{k+1}.
$$

We shift the index $m = k+1$ to simplify:

$$
\sum_{m=1}^{n} m \binom{n}{m}.
$$

Using the identity

$$
\sum_{m=1}^{n} m \binom{n}{m} = n \cdot 2^{n-1},
$$

we conclude

$$
\text{Total multiplications} = n \cdot 2^{n-1}.
$$

#### Step 3b: Additions

For each $(k+1)$-element subset $\tilde{S}$, the recurrence sums $k+1$ terms. Summing $k+1$ numbers requires $k$ additions (not $k+1$). Therefore, the number of additions per subset is

$$
k.
$$

The total additions at level $k+1$ are

$$
A_{k+1} = k \binom{n}{k+1}.
$$

Summing over $k = 1, 2, \ldots, n-1$ (note $k=0$ requires no additions) gives

$$
\text{Total additions} = \sum_{k=1}^{n-1} k \binom{n}{k+1}.
$$

We change index $m = k+1$, so $k = m-1$, $m = 2, \ldots, n$. Then

$$
\sum_{m=2}^{n} (m-1) \binom{n}{m}.
$$

Using the identity

$$
\sum_{m=0}^{n} m \binom{n}{m} = n \cdot 2^{n-1},
$$

we can compute

$$
\sum_{m=2}^{n} (m-1)\binom{n}{m} = \sum_{m=1}^{n} (m-1)\binom{n}{m} = \sum_{m=1}^{n} m \binom{n}{m} - \sum_{m=1}^{n} \binom{n}{m} = n \cdot 2^{n-1} - (2^n -1) = (n-2)2^{n-1} + 1.
$$

Thus, the total number of additions is

$$
\text{Total additions} = (n-2) \cdot 2^{n-1} + 1.
$$

### Step 4: Temporary storage

At level $k$, we must store all $\binom{n}{k}$ values of $A_{kS}$ to compute level $k+1$. The maximum storage occurs at the level with the largest binomial coefficient, which is

$$
\binom{n}{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor}.
$$

Hence, the method requires temporary storage sufficient to hold

$$
\max_{0 \le k \le n} \binom{n}{k} = \binom{n}{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor}.
$$

### Step 5: Summary

- **Multiplications:** $n \cdot 2^{n-1}$
- **Additions:** $(n-2) \cdot 2^{n-1} + 1$
- **Temporary storage:** $\binom{n}{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor}$ quantities of type $A_{kS}$

This completes the analysis.
