**Exercise 29.** &#9654; [*M20*] Let $R_1, R_2, \ldots, R_m$ all be sets of $(n+1)$-tuples of real numbers having at most $t$ degrees of freedom. Show that the union $R_1 \cup R_2 \cup \cdots \cup R_m$ also has at most $t$ degrees of freedom.

## Solution

Let $R_1, \dots, R_m \subset \mathbb{R}^{n+1}$, and assume each $R_i$ has at most $t$ degrees of freedom. That is, for any choice of $t+1$ indices $0 \le j_1 < \cdots < j_{t+1} \le n$, there exists a nonzero polynomial $g_i$ in the variables $x_{j_1}, \dots, x_{j_{t+1}}$ such that

$$
g_i(x_{j_1}, \dots, x_{j_{t+1}}) = 0 \quad \text{for all } (x_0, \dots, x_n) \in R_i.
$$

We wish to show that the union

$$
R = R_1 \cup \cdots \cup R_m
$$

also has at most $t$ degrees of freedom.

### Step 1: Induction on the number of sets

We proceed by induction on $m$.

**Base case ($m = 1$):** Trivial, since $R = R_1$ and $R_1$ has at most $t$ degrees of freedom by assumption.

**Inductive step:** Assume that the union of any $m-1$ sets each with at most $t$ degrees of freedom has at most $t$ degrees of freedom. Consider $R_1 \cup \cdots \cup R_m$. Let $S = R_1 \cup \cdots \cup R_{m-1}$. By the induction hypothesis, $S$ has at most $t$ degrees of freedom.

We now show that $S \cup R_m$ has at most $t$ degrees of freedom.

### Step 2: Fix $t+1$ coordinate indices

Fix arbitrary indices $0 \le j_1 < \cdots < j_{t+1} \le n$. By the definition of degrees of freedom:

- There exists a nonzero polynomial $g_S(x_{j_1}, \dots, x_{j_{t+1}})$ vanishing on $S$.
- There exists a nonzero polynomial $g_m(x_{j_1}, \dots, x_{j_{t+1}})$ vanishing on $R_m$.

### Step 3: Construct a polynomial for the union

Consider the polynomial

$$
g(x_{j_1}, \dots, x_{j_{t+1}}) = g_S(x_{j_1}, \dots, x_{j_{t+1}}) \cdot g_m(x_{j_1}, \dots, x_{j_{t+1}}).
$$

This is a polynomial in the same $t+1$ variables. It satisfies

$$
g(x_{j_1}, \dots, x_{j_{t+1}}) = 0 \quad \text{for all points in } S \cup R_m
$$

because any point belongs either to $S$ or to $R_m$, and in either case one factor vanishes.

### Step 4: Verify that the polynomial is nonzero

Since $g_S$ and $g_m$ are nonzero polynomials in $t+1$ variables, their product is also nonzero. To justify this:

- If $g_S$ and $g_m$ were polynomials in the same set of $t+1$ variables, then $g_S \cdot g_m$ is nonzero as an element of $\mathbb{R}[x_{j_1}, \dots, x_{j_{t+1}}]$.
- This holds because a product of two nonzero polynomials in a polynomial ring over a field cannot vanish identically.

Hence $g$ is a valid nonzero polynomial vanishing on $S \cup R_m$.

### Step 5: Conclude the inductive argument

Since $j_1, \dots, j_{t+1}$ were arbitrary, we have found for every choice of $t+1$ coordinates a nonzero polynomial vanishing on $R_1 \cup \cdots \cup R_m$. Therefore, the union has at most $t$ degrees of freedom.

Induction completes the argument. ∎

### Remarks

- This corrects the flaw in the previous solution by using induction to ensure that a single polynomial can be constructed for the union without assuming simultaneously that all $g_i$ can be multiplied without risk.
- Each step explicitly verifies that the polynomial for the union is nonzero and works for an arbitrary choice of $t+1$ coordinates.

This argument rigorously establishes that a finite union of sets with at most $t$ degrees of freedom also has at most $t$ degrees of freedom.
