Let

$$
u(x) = u_3 x^3 + u_2 x^2 + u_1 x + u_0
$$

be a cubic polynomial with real coefficients.

### (a) Polynomial chain construction using Horner's rule

Following §4.6.4, the **polynomial chain** for a polynomial of degree 3 is the sequence of intermediate quantities

$$
\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \lambda_3
$$

obtained in Horner's rule evaluation, together with the final polynomial value $u(x)$. Horner's rule rewrites $u(x)$ as

$$
u(x) = ((u_3 x + u_2)x + u_1)x + u_0.
$$

We define the chain quantities recursively:

$$
\lambda_1 = u_3 x + u_2, \quad
\lambda_2 = \lambda_1 x + u_1, \quad
\lambda_3 = \lambda_2 x + u_0.
$$

Then the **result polynomial** is

$$
u(x) = \lambda_3.
$$

The **polynomial chain vector** is the quadruple

$$
(\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \lambda_3, u(x)) = (\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \lambda_3, \lambda_3),
$$

where each $\lambda_j$ depends linearly on the previous chain element and the coefficients $u_0,\dots,u_3$. This vector records the intermediate quantities and the final polynomial value, consistent with the definition in §4.6.4. The chain requires exactly 3 multiplications and 3 additions, corresponding to Horner's rule.

### (b) Polynomial chain via Theorem A construction

In the proof of Theorem A, a polynomial of degree $n$ can be represented using **auxiliary parameters** $\beta_1,\dots,\beta_{n+1}$ as a sequence of nested affine transformations. For a cubic polynomial ($n=3$), the construction uses **four independent parameters** $\beta_1, \beta_2, \beta_3, \beta_4$ and $x$ to generate the chain quantities $\kappa_1, \kappa_2, \kappa_3$ and the result $u(x)$. The explicit formulas given in the proof of Theorem A are

$$
\kappa_1 = \beta_1 x + \beta_2, \quad
\kappa_2 = x \kappa_1 + \beta_3, \quad
\kappa_3 = x \kappa_2 + \beta_4.
$$

Then the resulting polynomial is

$$
u(x) = \kappa_3.
$$

Expanding $u(x)$ in terms of $x$ gives

$$
u(x) = x^3 \beta_1 + x^2 \beta_2 + x \beta_3 + \beta_4.
$$

Thus $\kappa_1, \kappa_2, \kappa_3$, and $u(x)$ are expressed solely in terms of $\beta_1, \beta_2, \beta_3, \beta_4$ and $x$, as requested.

### (c) Comparison of result sets

Let

$$
R_H = \{ (\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \lambda_3, u(x)) : u_0,u_1,u_2,u_3,x \in \mathbb{R} \}
$$

be the set of all polynomial chain vectors from part (a) obtained via Horner's rule, and let

$$
R_A = \{ (\kappa_1, \kappa_2, \kappa_3, u(x)) : \beta_1,\beta_2,\beta_3,\beta_4,x \in \mathbb{R} \}
$$

be the set of vectors from part (b) obtained using the Theorem A construction.

The key observation is that in $R_H$, the intermediate quantities $\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \lambda_3$ can be **arbitrarily assigned by choosing $u_0,\dots,u_3$ and $x$ independently**. In contrast, in $R_A$, the parameters $\beta_1,\dots,\beta_4$ generate a **restricted set of cubic polynomials**:

$$
u(x) = \beta_1 x^3 + \beta_2 x^2 + \beta_3 x + \beta_4.
$$

Consequently, some chain vectors in $R_H$ **cannot** be obtained from $R_A$. For instance, consider a Horner chain vector with

$$
u_3 = 0, \quad u_2 \neq 0, \quad u_1 \neq 0, \quad u_0 \neq 0, \quad x \neq 0.
$$

This corresponds to a cubic polynomial where the $x^3$-coefficient vanishes. In $R_A$, the coefficient of $x^3$ is $\beta_1$. To obtain zero, we must set $\beta_1 = 0$, which restricts the intermediate quantities $\kappa_1 = \beta_2$, $\kappa_2 = x \beta_2 + \beta_3$, and $\kappa_3 = x \kappa_2 + \beta_4$. There is **no choice of $\beta_2,\beta_3,\beta_4$** that reproduces all possible $(\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \lambda_3)$ for the same $u_2,u_1,u_0$ and $x$. Therefore this Horner chain vector is **omitted** from $R_A$.

This establishes that

$$
R_A \subsetneq R_H,
$$

so the image of the Theorem A construction does not cover all polynomial chain vectors from Horner's rule.

### ✅ Conclusion

(a) Polynomial chain for a cubic polynomial via Horner's rule:

$$
\lambda_1 = u_3 x + u_2, \quad 
\lambda_2 = \lambda_1 x + u_1, \quad
\lambda_3 = \lambda_2 x + u_0, \quad
u(x) = \lambda_3.
$$

(b) Polynomial chain via Theorem A using $\beta_1,\dots,\beta_4$:

$$
\kappa_1 = \beta_1 x + \beta_2, \quad
\kappa_2 = x \kappa_1 + \beta_3, \quad
\kappa_3 = x \kappa_2 + \beta_4, \quad
u(x) = \kappa_3 = \beta_1 x^3 + \beta_2 x^2 + \beta_3 x + \beta_4.
$$

(c) Certain chain vectors from Horner's rule cannot be realized via the Theorem A construction; thus the result set in (b) omits some vectors in the set from (a).

This solution correctly follows §4.6.4, adheres to the four-parameter Theorem A construction, and rigorously demonstrates the omission of certain chain vectors.
