**Exercise 35.** &#9654; [*M25*] Show that any polynomial chain that computes a general fourth-degree polynomial using three multiplications must have at least five addition-subtractions. [*Hint:* Assume that there are only four addition-subtractions, and show that exercise 31 applies; therefore the scheme must have a particular form that is incapable of representing all fourth-degree polynomials.]

## Solution

Assume that a polynomial chain computes a general fourth-degree polynomial with three multiplications and four addition-subtractions. Let the total number of steps be $r$, so that

$$
r=3+4=7.
$$

Exercise 31 classifies chains having three multiplications and four addition-subtractions. The argument there shows that every such chain is equivalent to one in which the three multiplication steps occur consecutively, and each multiplication has the form

$$
\lambda_i=\lambda_j\lambda_k
$$

with previously computed quantities $\lambda_j,\lambda_k$, while the four addition-subtractions are parameter steps.

By exercise 34, subtractions may be replaced by additions, and the unique parameter multiplication may be taken as the final operation. Since there are only three multiplications altogether, the chain must therefore have the structure

$$
\begin{aligned}
\lambda_1&=a_1x+b_1,\\
\lambda_2&=a_2x+b_2,\\
\lambda_3&=\lambda_1\lambda_2,\\
\lambda_4&=a_4x+b_4,\\
\lambda_5&=\lambda_3\lambda_4,\\
\lambda_6&=a_6x+b_6,\\
\lambda_7&=\lambda_5\lambda_6,
\end{aligned}
$$

where the coefficients $a_i,b_i$ are parameters.

Each factor is linear in $x$. Hence

$$
\lambda_3=(a_1x+b_1)(a_2x+b_2)
$$

has degree $2$,

$$
\lambda_5=\lambda_3(a_4x+b_4)
$$

has degree $3$, and

$$
\lambda_7=\lambda_5(a_6x+b_6)
$$

has degree $4$.

Expanding,

$$
\lambda_7
=(a_1x+b_1)(a_2x+b_2)(a_4x+b_4)(a_6x+b_6).
$$

Therefore the coefficients of the resulting quartic satisfy

$$
\begin{aligned}
u_4&=a_1a_2a_4a_6,\\
u_3&=a_1a_2a_4b_6+a_1a_2b_4a_6+a_1b_2a_4a_6+b_1a_2a_4a_6,\\
u_2&=a_1a_2b_4b_6+a_1b_2a_4b_6+a_1b_2b_4a_6\\
&\qquad +b_1a_2a_4b_6+b_1a_2b_4a_6+b_1b_2a_4a_6,\\
u_1&=a_1b_2b_4b_6+b_1a_2b_4b_6+b_1b_2a_4b_6+b_1b_2b_4a_6,\\
u_0&=b_1b_2b_4b_6.
\end{aligned}
$$

These five coefficients depend on only eight parameters through the coefficient relations of a completely factorized quartic. Consequently they are not independent. For example,

$$
u(x)=x^4+x+1
$$

is irreducible over the rationals and cannot be represented as a product of four linear factors with parameter coefficients. Hence the above form does not represent all fourth-degree polynomials.

The assumption that four addition-subtractions suffice therefore leads to a contradiction. Consequently any polynomial chain with three multiplications that computes a general fourth-degree polynomial must contain at least five addition-subtractions.

Thus the minimum number of addition-subtractions is

$$
\boxed{5}.
$$

This completes the proof.
