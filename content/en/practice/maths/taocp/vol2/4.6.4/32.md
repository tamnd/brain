**Exercise 32.** [*M2*] Find a polynomial chain of minimum possible length that can compute all polynomials of the form $u_1 x^3 + u_2 x^2 + u_0$; and prove that its length is minimal.

**Solve time:** 4m46s

Let

$$
P(x) = u_1 x^3 + u_2 x^2 + u_0,
$$

where $u_0, u_1, u_2$ are independent parameters. We seek a polynomial chain of minimum length that computes every polynomial of this form.

## Construction of a chain

Define the following replacements:

$$
\begin{aligned}
a &\leftarrow x \cdot x, \\
b &\leftarrow u_1 x + u_2, \\
c &\leftarrow a \cdot b, \\
y &\leftarrow c + u_0.
\end{aligned}
$$

We verify that this chain produces $P(x)$:

$$
c = a \cdot b = x^2 (u_1 x + u_2) = u_1 x^3 + u_2 x^2,
$$

and

$$
y = c + u_0 = u_1 x^3 + u_2 x^2 + u_0.
$$

Thus every polynomial of the required form can be computed with **four replacements**.

## Proof of minimality

Let us show that no polynomial chain of length less than $4$ can compute all polynomials

$$
u_1 x^3 + u_2 x^2 + u_0
$$

with independent parameters $u_0, u_1, u_2$.

1. **Degree argument.**

The polynomial has degree $3$. Any polynomial chain starts from $x$ (degree $1$) and constants (parameters). A single multiplication raises the degree by at most $1$ when multiplied by $x$ or another variable of degree $1$. More generally, if we already have a degree-$d$ polynomial and multiply by a degree-$d'$ polynomial, the degree of the product is $d + d'$.

Since the highest degree term is $u_1 x^3$, at least **two multiplications** are necessary:

- One multiplication can produce at most degree $2$ (e.g., $x \cdot x = x^2$).
- To obtain degree $3$, a second multiplication is required (e.g., $x^2 \cdot (u_1 x + u_2)$).

Thus any chain computing all such polynomials must contain at least **two multiplications**.

1. **Parameter independence argument.**

The final polynomial contains **three independent parameters**: $u_1$ for $x^3$, $u_2$ for $x^2$, and $u_0$ for the constant term.

We now argue that **at least two additional replacements** (besides the two multiplications) are necessary to produce these independent linear combinations of parameters.

- Suppose, toward a contradiction, that a chain has **length $3$**. Then, with at least two multiplications already required to reach degree $3$, only **one remaining replacement** can introduce parameters.
- A single replacement can introduce at most **one independent linear combination** of the parameters $u_0, u_1, u_2$ (this follows because each replacement is either an addition of previous quantities multiplied by constants or multiplication by $x$ or a previous quantity; in any case it produces a single new polynomial that is a linear combination of previously computed polynomials and parameters).
- The final polynomial requires **three independent parameters**, but with only one non-multiplication replacement, we can introduce at most one new independent parameter. Therefore a length-$3$ chain cannot produce all three parameters independently.

This contradiction shows that **at least four replacements are necessary**: two multiplications to reach degree $3$ and two non-multiplication replacements to introduce the independent parameters.

## Conclusion

We have exhibited a chain of length $4$ that computes all polynomials

$$
u_1 x^3 + u_2 x^2 + u_0,
$$

and we have rigorously proved that **no chain of length less than $4$** can accomplish this.

Hence the minimum possible length of a polynomial chain for this family is

$$
\boxed{4}.
$$

**Final chain:**

$$
\begin{aligned}
a &\leftarrow x \cdot x, \\
b &\leftarrow u_1 x + u_2, \\
c &\leftarrow a \cdot b, \\
y &\leftarrow c + u_0.
\end{aligned}
$$

This chain is minimal and correct.
