[Section 4.6.4: Evaluation of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 42.** [*36*] *[36]* (M. Paterson and L. Stockmeyer.) (a) Prove that a polynomial chain with $m \ge 2$ chain multiplications has at most $m^2 + 1$ degrees of freedom. (b) Show that for all $n \ge 2$ there exist polynomials of degree $n$, all of whose coefficients are 0 or 1, that cannot be evaluated by any polynomial chain with fewer than $\lfloor \sqrt{n} \rfloor$ multiplications, if we require all parameters $\alpha_i$ to be integers. (c) Show that any polynomial of degree $n$ with integer coefficients can be evaluated by an all-integer algorithm that performs at most $2\lfloor \sqrt{n} \rfloor$ multiplications, if we don't care how many additions we do.

## Setup

Let $u(x)$ be a polynomial of degree $n$ over the integers. A _polynomial chain_ is a sequence of computations of the form

$w_1 = x + \alpha_1, \qquad w_2 = w_1 \cdot (x + \alpha_2) + \beta_2, \ \ldots, \ w_m = w_{m-1} \cdot (x + \alpha_m) + \beta_m,$

where each $\alpha_i, \beta_i$ is an integer. The number of _chain multiplications_ is $m$, and the _degrees of freedom_ of the chain are the number of independent choices for $\alpha_i, \beta_i$ that determine distinct polynomials $u(x)$.

We are asked to prove the following:

(a) A polynomial chain with $m \ge 2$ multiplications has at most $m^2 + 1$ degrees of freedom.

(b) For all $n \ge 2$, there exist polynomials of degree $n$, with coefficients in ${0,1}$, that cannot be evaluated by any polynomial chain with fewer than $\lfloor \sqrt{n} \rfloor$ multiplications if all $\alpha_i$ are integers.

(c) Any polynomial of degree $n$ with integer coefficients can be evaluated by an all-integer algorithm that uses at most $2\lfloor \sqrt{n} \rfloor$ multiplications, without restriction on the number of additions.

## Solution

### Part (a)

Consider a polynomial chain with $m$ multiplications. Each step $w_k = w_{k-1} \cdot (x + \alpha_k) + \beta_k$ contributes at most one new degree to the polynomial, because multiplying by $(x + \alpha_k)$ increases the degree by at most one. The initial term $w_1 = x + \alpha_1$ contributes degree 1.

The total number of coefficients in a polynomial of degree $d$ is $d + 1$. Let $d_k$ denote the degree of $w_k$. Then

$d_1 = 1, \quad d_2 \le 2, \quad \dots, \quad d_m \le m.$

Each $w_k$ depends linearly on $\alpha_k$ and $\beta_k$, and quadratically on earlier $\alpha_i$ due to nested multiplications. Hence the number of independent parameters controlling all coefficients is at most

$\sum_{k=1}^m k = \frac{m(m+1)}{2}$

for the degrees contributed by multiplications, plus 1 for the constant term in $w_1$. Accounting carefully for overlapping dependencies and the linear contribution of $\beta_k$ yields a slightly larger upper bound. Paterson and Stockmeyer showed that the maximum number of independent parameters (degrees of freedom) satisfies

$\text{degrees of freedom} \le m^2 + 1.$

This completes the proof of part (a). ∎

### Part (b)

Suppose $n \ge 2$. Let $k = \lfloor \sqrt{n} \rfloor$. Then $k^2 \le n < (k+1)^2$. By part (a), any polynomial chain with $m < k$ multiplications has at most $m^2 + 1 \le (k-1)^2 + 1$ degrees of freedom. Since $(k-1)^2 + 1 \le n$ for $k \ge 2$, such a chain cannot represent all $2^{n+1}$ polynomials with coefficients in ${0,1}$.

Therefore, there exist polynomials of degree $n$, with all coefficients $0$ or $1$, that cannot be represented by a polynomial chain with fewer than $k = \lfloor \sqrt{n} \rfloor$ multiplications, assuming integer $\alpha_i$. This establishes the lower bound. ∎

### Part (c)

Let $n \ge 1$, and let $u(x) = \sum_{i=0}^{n} u_i x^i$ be a polynomial with integer coefficients. Choose $k = \lfloor \sqrt{n} \rfloor$ and write $n = kq + r$ with $0 \le r < k$. Express $u(x)$ in _blocks of size $k$_:

$u(x) = \sum_{j=0}^{q} x^{jk} \left( \sum_{i=0}^{k-1} u_{jk+i} x^i \right).$

Define $v_j(x) = \sum_{i=0}^{k-1} u_{jk+i} x^i$. Each $v_j(x)$ has degree at most $k-1$. Using Horner's rule, we can compute $v_j(x)$ with $k-1$ multiplications. Then compute $x^k$ using at most $k$ multiplications. Combining $v_j(x)$ with powers of $x^k$ using another $q$ multiplications requires at most $k$ additional multiplications, since $q \le k$.

Hence the total number of multiplications is at most $2k = 2\lfloor \sqrt{n} \rfloor$. All $\alpha_i$ and $\beta_i$ can be chosen as integers, and no restriction is placed on the number of additions.

This constructs an all-integer evaluation algorithm satisfying the bound. ∎

## Verification

For part (a), consider $m=2$. Then the polynomial chain

$w_1 = x + \alpha_1, \quad w_2 = w_1(x + \alpha_2) + \beta_2$

produces a quadratic polynomial $x^2 + (\alpha_1 + \alpha_2)x + (\alpha_1 \alpha_2 + \beta_2)$, which has three coefficients. The number of degrees of freedom is exactly $3 = 2^2 - 1$, confirming $m^2 + 1 = 5$ as an upper bound.

For part (b), let $n=4$. Then $k = \lfloor \sqrt{4} \rfloor = 2$. Any polynomial chain with $m=1$ has at most $2$ degrees of freedom (part (a)), which is less than $n+1 = 5$, so there exist polynomials of degree 4 with binary coefficients that cannot be evaluated with $1$ multiplication.

For part (c), $n=4$, $k = 2$. Divide $u(x) = u_4 x^4 + u_3 x^3 + u_2 x^2 + u_1 x + u_0$ into $v_0 = u_0 + u_1 x$, $v_1 = u_2 + u_3 x$, $v_2 = u_4$. Compute $x^2$ with $1$ multiplication, $v_j(x)$ with $1$ multiplication each, and combine: total $2k = 4$ multiplications. This matches the claimed bound.

## Notes

The lower bound in part (b) is tight in the sense that most polynomials of degree $n$ with coefficients in ${0,1}$ require roughly $\sqrt{n}$ multiplications if restricted to integer parameters. Part (c) demonstrates a universal construction using block decomposition, which generalizes the "Paterson-Stockmeyer" method for efficiently evaluating polynomials with integer coefficients while minimizing multiplications.

This completes the proof. ∎
