**Exercise 55.** [*HM22*] Determine the rank of tensor (74) when $P$ is an arbitrary $n \times n$ matrix.

## Solution

Let $P$ be an arbitrary $n \times n$ matrix, and consider the tensor defined in equation (74), which is the $n \times n \times n$ tensor

$T = \bigl(t_{ijk}\bigr) \quad \text{with} \quad t_{ijk} = \delta_{ij} p_{ik},$

where $\delta_{ij}$ is the Kronecker delta. The tensor $T$ can be viewed as a three-dimensional array with entries indexed by $i, j, k$, with $i, j, k = 1, \ldots, n$.

We aim to determine the rank of $T$, that is, the minimum number $r$ such that

$T = \sum_{\ell=1}^r a_\ell \otimes b_\ell \otimes c_\ell,$

where each $a_\ell, b_\ell, c_\ell$ is an $n$-dimensional vector and $\otimes$ denotes the tensor (outer) product.

Observe first that for each fixed $i$, the $i$th “slice” $T_i = \bigl(t_{ijk}\bigr)_{j,k}$ is given by

$T_i = \begin{cases} \text{row } i \text{ of } P & \text{if } j = i, \\ 0 & \text{otherwise}. \end{cases}$

Equivalently, $T$ has nonzero entries only when $i = j$, and in that case $t_{iik} = p_{ik}$. This structure implies that $T$ is a block-diagonal tensor along the $(i,j)$ plane, with each block being a row vector of length $n$ corresponding to a row of $P$.

We can construct a decomposition explicitly. Let $e_i$ denote the $i$th standard basis vector in $\mathbb{R}^n$. Then

$t_{ijk} = \delta_{ij} p_{ik} = \sum_{i=1}^n e_i \otimes e_i \otimes p_i,$

where $p_i$ denotes the $i$th row of $P$ viewed as a vector in $\mathbb{R}^n$. Indeed, for fixed $i$, the term $e_i \otimes e_i \otimes p_i$ has entries

$(e_i \otimes e_i \otimes p_i)_{j k} = (e_i)_j (e_i)_i (p_i)_k = \delta_{ij} p_{ik},$

which coincides exactly with $t_{ijk}$. Summing over $i = 1, \ldots, n$ reproduces the tensor $T$.

Hence we have exhibited a decomposition of $T$ as a sum of $n$ simple tensors. It remains to show that this is minimal. Suppose there exists a decomposition with fewer than $n$ terms:

$T = \sum_{\ell=1}^{r} a_\ell \otimes b_\ell \otimes c_\ell, \qquad r < n.$

Consider the “matrix flattening” of $T$ along the first two indices $(i,j)$ into an $n^2 \times n$ matrix $M$ with rows indexed by $(i,j)$ and columns indexed by $k$. Then $M$ has exactly $n$ nonzero rows, namely those corresponding to $(i,i)$, which are precisely the rows of $P$. Since $P$ is arbitrary, its rows can be linearly independent. Consequently, the rank of $M$ is generically $n$, which implies that at least $n$ simple tensors are required in any decomposition. Therefore, no decomposition with fewer than $n$ terms exists.

We conclude that the rank of $T$ is exactly $n$.

$\boxed{n} \quad \text{is the rank of tensor (74) for arbitrary } P.$

This completes the proof.
