**Exercise 12.** [*M50*] What is the minimum number of multiplications required to form the product of two $n \times n$ matrices? What is the smallest exponent $\omega$ such that $O(n^{\omega+\epsilon})$ multiplications are sufficient for all $\epsilon > 0$? (Find good upper and lower bounds for small $n$ as well as large $n$.)

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m51s

Let $A,B \in F^{n\times n}$ and let $C=AB$. We view matrix multiplication as a bilinear map

$$
\mu_n : U \times V \to W,
$$

where $U=V=W=F^{n\times n}$ with $\dim W = n^2$. The number of scalar multiplications in an algorithm corresponds to the bilinear rank of $\mu_n$, i.e., the smallest $r$ such that

$$
\mu_n(A,B) = \sum_{s=1}^r f_s(A)\, g_s(B)\, W_s,
$$

where $f_s,g_s$ are linear forms and $W_s \in W$ are fixed matrices.

Let $M(n)$ denote this minimum rank.

## Small values of $n$

For $n=1$, clearly $M(1)=1$.

For $n=2$, Strassen exhibited a decomposition of rank $7$, hence

$$
M(2) \le 7.
$$

It is also known that $M(2)\ge 7$, so

$$
M(2)=7.
$$

For $n=3$, Laderman constructed a scheme using $23$ multiplications, so

$$
M(3) \le 23,
$$

while the trivial bound is $M(3)\ge 19$ (and in particular $M(3)> 9$). No exact value is known.

## Block recursion and asymptotic upper bounds

If there exists an algorithm multiplying $k \times k$ matrices using $r$ scalar multiplications, then partitioning $n\times n$ matrices into $(n/k)\times (n/k)$ blocks yields

$$
M(n) = O\!\left(n^{\log_k r}\right).
$$

Applying Strassenâ€™s construction recursively gives

$$
M(n) = O\!\left(n^{\log_2 7}\right), \qquad \log_2 7 \approx 2.807.
$$

More advanced tensor constructions improve the exponent. The best known results give

$$
M(n) = O\!\left(n^{2.3728596\ldots}\right).
$$

Hence any $\omega > 2.3728596\ldots$ satisfies the definition that $M(n)=O(n^{\omega+\epsilon})$ for all $\epsilon>0$.

## Correct lower bound for $\omega$

We now justify rigorously that $\omega \ge 2$.

Assume a bilinear algorithm of rank $r$ computes matrix multiplication:

$$
AB = \sum_{s=1}^r f_s(A)\, g_s(B)\, W_s.
$$

Fixing $B$, this expresses the map $A \mapsto AB$ as a linear combination of the fixed matrices $W_s$ with coefficients depending linearly on $A$. Hence the image of the map $\mu_n$ lies in

$$
\operatorname{span}\{W_1,\dots,W_r\},
$$

so

$$
\dim(\operatorname{Im}\mu_n) \le r.
$$

We now compute $\operatorname{Im}\mu_n$. For any $C \in F^{n\times n}$, we have

$$
C = CI,
$$

so every matrix is realizable as a product. Hence $\mu_n$ is surjective, and

$$
\operatorname{Im}\mu_n = F^{n\times n},
\quad \dim(\operatorname{Im}\mu_n)=n^2.
$$

Therefore,

$$
r \ge n^2,
\quad \text{so } M(n) \ge n^2.
$$

This establishes the fundamental lower bound.

## Consequence for the exponent $\omega$

By definition, $\omega$ is the infimum of real numbers such that

$$
M(n) = O(n^{\omega+\varepsilon}) \quad \text{for all } \varepsilon > 0.
$$

Since $M(n) \ge n^2$, any such exponent must satisfy

$$
\omega \ge 2.
$$

Combined with the best known upper bound, we obtain

$$
2 \le \omega \le 2.3728596\ldots.
$$

## Final summary

For small dimensions:

$$
M(1)=1,\quad M(2)=7,\quad M(3)\le 23.
$$

For large $n$:

$$
n^2 \le M(n) \le O(n^{2.3728596\ldots}).
$$

Thus the exponent of matrix multiplication satisfies

$$
2 \le \omega \le 2.3728596\ldots,
$$

and no method achieving $\omega=2$ is known.
