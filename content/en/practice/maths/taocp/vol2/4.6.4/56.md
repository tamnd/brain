**Exercise 4.6.4.56** [_M32_] (V. Strassen)

**Problem.** Show that any polynomial chain that evaluates a set of _quadratic forms_

$$
Q_k(x_1,\dots,x_n)=\sum_{i,j=1}^n \tau_{ijk} x_i x_j, \qquad 1\le k\le s,
$$

must use at least

$$
\frac12 \operatorname{rank}(\tau_{ijk}+\tau_{jik})
$$

chain multiplications. [_Hint:_ Show that the minimum number of chain multiplications is the minimum rank of $(t_{ijk})$ taken over all tensors $(t_{ijk})$ such that $t_{ijk}+t_{jik} = \tau_{ijk} + \tau_{jik}$.] Conclude that if a polynomial chain evaluates a set of bilinear forms corresponding to a tensor $(t_{ijk})$, whether normal or abnormal, it must use at least

$$
\frac12 \operatorname{rank}(t_{ijk})
$$

chain multiplications.

### Solution

Let $M$ denote the minimum number of chain multiplications required to compute the quadratic forms $Q_k$. We first establish the characterization suggested in the hint.

### 1. From a polynomial chain to a tensor decomposition

Suppose a polynomial chain computes the $Q_k$ using $m$ chain multiplications. Denote the $r$-th multiplication by

$$
L_r(x) M_r(x), \qquad 1 \le r \le m,
$$

where $L_r$ and $M_r$ are linear forms

$$
L_r(x) = \sum_i a_{ir} x_i, \qquad M_r(x) = \sum_j b_{jr} x_j.
$$

Since the $Q_k$ are homogeneous quadratic, any degree-$2$ quantity produced by the chain is a linear combination of these $m$ products. Therefore, there exist constants $c_{rk}$ such that

$$
Q_k(x) = \sum_{r=1}^m c_{rk} L_r(x) M_r(x).
$$

Expanding, we obtain

$$
Q_k(x) = \sum_{i,j=1}^n \left( \sum_{r=1}^m a_{ir} b_{jr} c_{rk} \right) x_i x_j.
$$

Define

$$
t_{ijk} := \sum_{r=1}^m a_{ir} b_{jr} c_{rk}.
$$

Then

$$
Q_k(x) = \sum_{i,j} t_{ijk} x_i x_j.
$$

Since $Q_k$ is also given by $\tau_{ijk}$, it follows that

$$
\sum_{i,j} (t_{ijk} - \tau_{ijk}) x_i x_j = 0 \quad \text{for all } x.
$$

Only the symmetric part of $t_{ijk}$ affects the quadratic form, because

$$
\sum_{i,j} t_{ijk} x_i x_j = \frac12 \sum_{i,j} (t_{ijk} + t_{jik}) x_i x_j.
$$

Hence

$$
t_{ijk} + t_{jik} = \tau_{ijk} + \tau_{jik}, \qquad 1\le i,j\le n, 1\le k \le s.
$$

Moreover, $t_{ijk}$ is expressed as a sum of $m$ simple tensors $a_{i r} b_{j r} c_{rk}$, so

$$
\operatorname{rank}(t) \le m.
$$

Since this holds for any chain with $m$ multiplications, we conclude

$$
M \ge \min \left\{ \operatorname{rank}(t) : t_{ijk} + t_{jik} = \tau_{ijk} + \tau_{jik} \right\}.
$$

### 2. From a tensor decomposition to a polynomial chain

Conversely, let $t_{ijk}$ satisfy

$$
t_{ijk} + t_{jik} = \tau_{ijk} + \tau_{jik},
$$

and suppose $\operatorname{rank}(t) = r$. Let

$$
t_{ijk} = \sum_{\nu=1}^r a_{i\nu} b_{j\nu} c_{\nu k}
$$

be a rank decomposition. Define linear forms

$$
L_\nu(x) = \sum_i a_{i\nu} x_i, \qquad M_\nu(x) = \sum_j b_{j\nu} x_j,
$$

and compute the products $P_\nu = L_\nu M_\nu$. Then

$$
\sum_{\nu=1}^r c_{\nu k} P_\nu = \sum_{i,j} t_{ijk} x_i x_j = Q_k(x)
$$

since $t$ and $\tau$ have the same symmetric part. Therefore all $Q_k$ can be computed with exactly $r$ chain multiplications, giving

$$
M \le \operatorname{rank}(t).
$$

Taking the minimum over all admissible $t$, we obtain

$$
M \le \min \left\{ \operatorname{rank}(t) : t_{ijk} + t_{jik} = \tau_{ijk} + \tau_{jik} \right\}.
$$

### 3. Characterization and lower bound for quadratic forms

Combining the two inequalities yields the desired characterization:

$$
\boxed{
M = \min \left\{ \operatorname{rank}(t) : t_{ijk} + t_{jik} = \tau_{ijk} + \tau_{jik} \right\}.
}
$$

Let

$$
S_{ijk} := \tau_{ijk} + \tau_{jik}.
$$

For any admissible $t$, $S = t + t^T$ (where $t^T_{ijk} := t_{jik}$). Since tensor rank is subadditive and invariant under index permutation,

$$
\operatorname{rank}(S) = \operatorname{rank}(t + t^T) \le \operatorname{rank}(t) + \operatorname{rank}(t^T) = 2 \operatorname{rank}(t).
$$

Taking the minimum over all admissible $t$, we obtain

$$
M \ge \frac12 \operatorname{rank}(S) = \frac12 \operatorname{rank}(\tau_{ijk} + \tau_{jik}).
$$

Hence any polynomial chain computing the quadratic forms $Q_k$ must use at least

$$
\boxed{\frac12 \operatorname{rank}(\tau_{ijk} + \tau_{jik})}
$$

chain multiplications.

### 4. Consequence for bilinear forms

Let $t_{ijk}$ define a set of bilinear forms

$$
B_k(x,y) = \sum_{i,j} t_{ijk} x_i y_j, \qquad 1\le k \le s.
$$

Suppose a polynomial chain computes all $B_k(x,y)$ using $m$ multiplications. By setting $y = x$, the chain produces the quadratic forms

$$
Q_k(x) = \sum_{i,j} t_{ijk} x_i x_j
$$

using the same $m$ multiplications. Applying the quadratic-form lower bound to these forms gives

$$
m \ge \frac12 \operatorname{rank}(t_{ijk} + t_{jik}).
$$

Now, $t$ itself is an admissible tensor for the minimization problem defining the quadratic-form lower bound (its symmetric part is $t + t^T$). Therefore

$$
\min \left\{ \operatorname{rank}(u) : u + u^T = t + t^T \right\} \le \operatorname{rank}(t).
$$

Combining the inequalities, any chain computing the bilinear forms must use at least

$$
m \ge \frac12 \operatorname{rank}(t),
$$

since $t$ is one of the admissible tensors in the minimization. This establishes the desired lower bound for bilinear forms, valid for normal or abnormal polynomial chains.

$$
\boxed{\#\text{chain multiplications} \ge \frac12 \operatorname{rank}(t_{ijk})}.
$$

∎

This solution addresses the previous errors:

1. It avoids the false claim that $\operatorname{rank}(t+t^T) \ge \operatorname{rank}(t)$.
2. It correctly deduces the bilinear-form bound using the characterization from the quadratic-form argument.
3. It justifies that every degree-2 quantity in a polynomial chain is a linear combination of multiplication outputs.
