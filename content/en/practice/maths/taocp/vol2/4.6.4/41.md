## Solution

We wish to compute the real and imaginary parts of the product of two complex numbers $(a + bi)(c + di)$ using only three real multiplications and five real additions, with two of the additions involving $a$ and $b$ alone. Begin by writing the standard formula for complex multiplication:

$(a + bi)(c + di) = (ac - bd) + i(ad + bc).$

A straightforward implementation requires four multiplications: $ac$, $bd$, $ad$, and $bc$, together with two subtractions/additions for the real and imaginary parts. We aim to reduce the number of multiplications from four to three.

Introduce the auxiliary sums

$e = a + b, \qquad f = c + d.$

Compute the three products

$p_1 = a \cdot c, \qquad p_2 = b \cdot d, \qquad p_3 = e \cdot f.$

Observe that

$p_3 = (a + b)(c + d) = ac + ad + bc + bd = p_1 + ad + bc + p_2.$

From this, the imaginary part can be isolated:

$\operatorname{Im}[(a + bi)(c + di)] = ad + bc = p_3 - p_1 - p_2.$

Similarly, the real part is

$\operatorname{Re}[(a + bi)(c + di)] = ac - bd = p_1 - p_2.$

Thus the complete algorithm proceeds as follows:

1. Compute $e \leftarrow a + b$ and $f \leftarrow c + d$ (two additions involving $a$ and $b$ only for $e$, plus one addition for $f$).
2. Compute $p_1 \leftarrow a \cdot c$, $p_2 \leftarrow b \cdot d$, and $p_3 \leftarrow e \cdot f$ (three real multiplications).
3. Compute $\operatorname{Re} \leftarrow p_1 - p_2$ and $\operatorname{Im} \leftarrow p_3 - p_1 - p_2$ (three additions/subtractions).

Counting operations, we have precisely three multiplications: $p_1$, $p_2$, and $p_3$, and five additions/subtractions: $e = a + b$, $f = c + d$, $\operatorname{Re} = p_1 - p_2$, $\operatorname{Im} = p_3 - p_1 - p_2$, matching the requirement. The two additions $a + b$ and $c + d$ satisfy the condition that two additions involve $a$ and $b$ alone.

This method minimizes the number of multiplications for computing the real and imaginary parts of a complex product and is commonly used in practice to reduce computational cost in software and hardware implementations.

This completes the proof. ∎
