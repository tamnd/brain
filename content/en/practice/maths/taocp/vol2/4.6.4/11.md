**Exercise 11.** [*M46*] Is there any way to evaluate the permanent of a general $n \times n$ matrix using fewer than $2^n$ arithmetic operations?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m33s  

---

## Setup

Let $X = (x_{ij})$ be an $n \times n$ matrix. The permanent is

$$
\operatorname{per}(X) = \sum_{\sigma \in S_n} \prod_{i=1}^n x_{i,\sigma(i)}.
$$

We consider computation in the arithmetic model where each addition or multiplication counts as one operation. The problem is whether there exists an algorithm that evaluates $\operatorname{per}(X)$ for arbitrary $X$ using fewer than $2^n$ arithmetic operations.

## Solution

Ryser’s inclusion–exclusion identity expresses the permanent as

$$
\operatorname{per}(X)
= (-1)^n \sum_{\epsilon_1,\ldots,\epsilon_n \in \{0,1\}}
(-1)^{\epsilon_1+\cdots+\epsilon_n}
\prod_{i=1}^n \sum_{j=1}^n \epsilon_j x_{ij}.
$$

The outer summation ranges over all $2^n$ binary vectors $(\epsilon_1,\ldots,\epsilon_n)$. Any direct evaluation of this expression requires at least one contribution per subset of ${1,\ldots,n}$, so the natural structure of this identity already involves $2^n$ terms.

Ryser’s algorithm evaluates the same quantity by dynamic reuse of partial sums over subsets, reducing repeated multiplication structure. This yields a computation requiring on the order of $n2^n$ arithmetic operations, and no known rearrangement reduces the dependence on $2^n$.

Any attempt to reduce the number of arithmetic operations below $2^n$ would require eliminating the contribution of exponentially many distinct subset-dependent terms without explicitly or implicitly accounting for their interactions. No such cancellation scheme is known that applies uniformly to arbitrary matrices.

No general lower bound excluding all computations with fewer than $2^n$ operations is known in the arithmetic circuit model. Known complexity results for the permanent establish exponential hardness in various restricted settings, but they do not yield a tight lower bound of $2^n$ arithmetic operations in this unrestricted setting.

Hence the existence of a method computing $\operatorname{per}(X)$ for all $n \times n$ matrices using fewer than $2^n$ arithmetic operations is not ruled out by known theory, and no such method is currently known.

## Verification

Ryser’s formula involves $2^n$ subsets of ${1,\ldots,n}$, and each subset contributes a distinct signed term determined by the vector $(\epsilon_1,\ldots,\epsilon_n)$. The dependence on all subsets is structural, since each coordinate $\epsilon_j$ independently affects every row sum $\sum_{j=1}^n \epsilon_j x_{ij}$. Any evaluation must propagate information from each subset configuration into the final value.

No argument in the current theory provides a transformation that compresses all $2^n$ subset contributions into fewer than $2^n$ arithmetic contributions while preserving correctness for all matrices $X$. Known algorithms, including Ryser’s method, still enumerate or aggregate over all subsets, confirming the absence of a sub-$2^n$ operation bound.

This completes the verification that no known improvement below $2^n$ operations exists, and that no lower bound is established to forbid such an improvement.
