**Exercise 54.** [*M23*] Theorem W refers to an infinite field. How many elements must a finite field have in order for the proof of Theorem W to be valid?

**Verified:** no

## Solution

In §4.6.4, Theorem W is proved under the assumption that the underlying field $F$ is infinite. The exercise asks not whether the theorem itself remains true over finite fields, but how large a finite field must be for **the proof** to remain valid.

The proof uses the standard fact that a nonzero polynomial of degree $d$ cannot have more than $d$ roots. Equivalently, if a polynomial of degree at most $d$ vanishes at more than $d$ distinct field elements, it must be the zero polynomial.

When the field is infinite, one can always choose enough distinct evaluation points, so this argument causes no difficulty.

Let $D$ be the largest degree that appears in the proof of Theorem W. The proof remains valid over a finite field $F$ provided that there are more than $D$ distinct field elements available. Then every polynomial that occurs in the argument can still be tested at $D+1$ distinct points, and the root-counting argument goes through exactly as in the infinite-field case.

If $|F| \le D$, the proof breaks down, because a nonzero polynomial of degree $D$ may vanish at every element of the field. For example,

$$
x^{|F|}-x
$$

is a nonzero polynomial of degree $|F|$ that vanishes at all elements of $F$. Thus the key step of the proof is no longer justified.

In the proof of Theorem W, the relevant degree bound is $D=2n$. Therefore the proof works unchanged whenever

$$
|F| > 2n.
$$

Since the number of field elements is an integer, the smallest possible size is

$$
\boxed{2n+1}.
$$

Hence a finite field must contain at least

$$
\boxed{2n+1}
$$

elements for the proof of Theorem W to remain valid. ∎
