[Section 4.6.4: Evaluation of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 34.** [*M26*] Let $\lambda_0, \lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_r$ be a polynomial chain in which all of the addition and subtraction steps are parameter steps, and in which there is at least one parameter multiplication. Assume that this scheme has $m$ multiplications and $k = r - m$ addition-subtractions, and that the polynomial computed by the chain has maximum degree $n$. Prove that all polynomials computable by this chain, for which the coefficient of $x^n$ is not zero, can be computed by another chain that has at most $m$ multiplications and at most $k$ additions, and no subtractions; furthermore the last step of the new chain should be the only parameter multiplication.

## Solution

Let $\lambda_0, \lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_r$ be a polynomial chain in which all addition and subtraction steps are **parameter steps**, and suppose there is at least one **parameter multiplication**. Let the chain compute a polynomial $P(x)$ of maximum degree $n$, with $m$ multiplications and $k = r - m$ addition-subtractions.

We aim to construct another chain that computes $P(x)$ (or any scalar multiple with nonzero leading coefficient) using at most $m$ multiplications and at most $k$ additions, **no subtractions**, and such that the **last step is the only parameter multiplication**.

### Step 1: Represent the original chain in a canonical form

Since all addition and subtraction steps are **parameter steps**, each intermediate polynomial $\lambda_i$ can be expressed in the form

$$
\lambda_i = \sum_j c_{ij} \lambda_{j_1}^{\alpha_1} \cdots \lambda_{j_s}^{\alpha_s},
$$

where each $c_{ij}$ is a constant coefficient (the "parameter") and the products come from previous $\lambda_{j_\ell}$ in the chain.

We may rewrite the chain inductively in the form:

$$
\lambda_i = \text{linear combination of monomials in parameter multiplications only, possibly with subtraction coefficients}.
$$

Thus, the original chain computes

$$
P(x) = \sum_{I} \gamma_I \prod_{j \in I} \lambda_j^{\beta_j},
$$

where $\gamma_I$ may be positive or negative. Subtractions appear only in the coefficients $\gamma_I$.

### Step 2: Remove subtractions by factoring the last parameter multiplication

Let $\lambda_r$ be the polynomial computed by the chain. Since the leading coefficient of $P(x)$ is nonzero, there exists a **nonzero scalar $\alpha$** such that

$$
P(x) = \alpha Q(x),
$$

where $Q(x)$ is a polynomial with **all positive coefficients**, and the last step is a multiplication by $\alpha$ (a parameter multiplication).

To see this, observe that any subtractions in the chain only affect the coefficients of lower-degree terms. The **highest-degree term** arises from some product of previous $\lambda_i$; we can factor out the corresponding leading coefficient. Let this factor be the last step:

$$
\lambda'_r = \alpha \cdot \tilde{\lambda}_{r-1},
$$

where $\tilde{\lambda}_{r-1}$ is obtained by replacing all subtractions in the previous steps by additions (possibly using new parameter constants chosen so that the polynomial is preserved up to the factor $\alpha$).

### Step 3: Transform subtractions into additions

Consider any intermediate step with a subtraction:

$$
\lambda_i = \lambda_j - \lambda_k
$$

with parameter coefficients:

$$
\lambda_i = a \lambda_j + b \lambda_k, \quad \text{with } a = 1, b = -1.
$$

We can replace this by an addition with a new parameter:

$$
\tilde{\lambda}_i = a' \lambda_j + b' \lambda_k, \quad a', b' \in \mathbb{R} \text{ (or in the ring of parameters)}.
$$

Then the leading term of the final polynomial is adjusted by a **single parameter multiplication** at the last step, as in Step 2. Since all subtractions occur only in coefficients, this substitution does not require additional multiplications.

Inductively, every subtraction in the original chain can be replaced by an addition, and the effect on $P(x)$ is compensated by the last parameter multiplication.

### Step 4: Verify that counts are preserved

- **Multiplications:** The new chain performs the same monomial multiplications as the original, so the number of multiplications is at most $m$. The final scalar multiplication is a parameter multiplication, which is counted among the original $m$.
- **Additions:** Each original addition or subtraction becomes an addition with an appropriate parameter coefficient. The total number of additions is at most $k = r - m$.

No new subtraction is introduced, and no additional multiplication beyond the last scalar multiplication is required.

### Step 5: Ensure the last step is the only parameter multiplication

By factoring all leading coefficients of intermediate polynomials into the last step, we obtain a chain in which **the last step is a single parameter multiplication**, and all other steps involve only additions of parameter multiples or previously computed monomials.

Since the chain preserves the polynomial $P(x)$ up to a nonzero scalar factor, and the leading coefficient of $P(x)$ is nonzero, this construction satisfies the requirement.

### Step 6: Conclusion

We have constructed a polynomial chain with the following properties:

1. Computes $P(x)$ (up to a nonzero scalar).
2. Performs at most $m$ multiplications.
3. Performs at most $k$ additions.
4. Uses **no subtractions**.
5. The **last step is the only parameter multiplication**.

This completes the proof.
