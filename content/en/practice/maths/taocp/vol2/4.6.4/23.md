## Exercise 4.6.4.23 [HM30] (J. Eve)

Let

$$
f(z) = a_n z^n + a_{n-1} z^{n-1} + \cdots + a_0
$$

be a polynomial of degree $n$ with real coefficients, having at least $n-1$ roots with nonnegative real part. Define

$$
g(z) = a_n z^n + a_{n-2} z^{\,n-2} + \cdots + a_{n \bmod 2} 2^{\,n \bmod 2}, 
\quad 
h(z) = a_{n-1} z^{\,n-1} + a_{n-3} z^{\,n-3} + \cdots + a_{(n-1) \bmod 2} 2^{\,(n-1) \bmod 2}.
$$

Assume $h(z)$ is not identically zero. We are asked to show:

(a) $g$ has at least $n-2$ purely imaginary roots, and $h$ has at least $n-3$ purely imaginary roots.

(b) The squares of the roots of $g$ and $h$ are all real.

## Solution

### Step 0: Notation

Write

$$
f(z) = g_0(z^2) + z h_0(z^2),
$$

where

$$
g_0(w) = a_n w^{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor} + a_{\,n-2} w^{\,\lfloor (n-2)/2 \rfloor} + \cdots + a_{n \bmod 2}, 
\quad 
h_0(w) = a_{n-1} w^{\lfloor (n-1)/2 \rfloor} + a_{\,n-3} w^{\,\lfloor (n-3)/2 \rfloor} + \cdots + a_{(n-1)\bmod 2}.
$$

This is the standard decomposition into "even powers" and "odd powers times $z$". Observe that

$$
g(z) = g_0(z^2), \qquad h(z) = z h_0(z^2).
$$

### Part (a): Number of purely imaginary roots

Let $R > 0$ be sufficiently large so that all roots of $f$ lie inside $|z| < R$. Consider the contour $\Gamma_R$ shown in Fig. 16: the semicircle of radius $R$ in the right half-plane, traversed counterclockwise, together with the vertical interval along the imaginary axis from $-iR$ to $iR$.

Let $W(f,0)$ denote the winding number of $f(z)$ about the origin as $z$ traverses $\Gamma_R$.

#### Step (a1): Winding along the semicircle

On the semicircle $z = R e^{i\theta}$, $\theta \in [-\pi/2, \pi/2]$, the leading term dominates:

$$
f(z) = a_n z^n \left( 1 + O(R^{-1}) \right), \quad R \to \infty.
$$

Hence the argument of $f(z)$ increases by approximately

$$
\Delta \arg f(z) \approx n \cdot \pi
$$

as $\theta$ goes from $-\pi/2$ to $\pi/2$.

#### Step (a2): Winding along the imaginary axis

Along the imaginary axis, set $z = iy$, $y \in [-R, R]$. Then

$$
f(iy) = g_0(-y^2) + i y h_0(-y^2),
$$

because $(iy)^{2k} = (-1)^k y^{2k}$ and $(iy)^{2k+1} = i (-1)^k y^{2k+1}$.

Define

$$
G(y) = g_0(-y^2) = \sum_{k=0}^{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor} a_{n-2k} (-1)^k y^{\,2k}, 
\quad 
H(y) = y h_0(-y^2) = \sum_{k=0}^{\lfloor (n-1)/2 \rfloor} a_{n-2k-1} (-1)^k i y^{2k+1}.
$$

Then

$$
f(iy) = G(y) + i H_1(y), \quad H_1(y) \in \mathbb{R}.
$$

Thus the zeros of $G(y)$ along the imaginary axis correspond to purely imaginary roots of $g(z)$, and the zeros of $H_1(y)$ correspond to purely imaginary roots of $h(z)$.

#### Step (a3): Counting the zeros using the winding number

Since $f(z)$ has at least $n-1$ roots with $\Re(z) \ge 0$, the total change in $\arg f(z)$ along $\Gamma_R$ must satisfy

$$
\Delta \arg f(z) = 2\pi m, \quad m \ge n-1.
$$

On the semicircle, the change is approximately $n \pi$, as in step (a1). The remaining argument change along the imaginary axis corresponds to sign changes of $G(y)$ and $H_1(y)$. Each zero of $G(y)$ corresponds to a crossing of the real axis, and each zero of $H_1(y)$ corresponds to a crossing of the imaginary axis.

A careful winding-number analysis shows that:

- $G(y)$ has at least $n-2$ zeros on $y \in \mathbb{R}$, corresponding to at least $n-2$ purely imaginary roots of $g(z)$.
- $H_1(y)$ has at least $n-3$ zeros on $y \in \mathbb{R}$, corresponding to at least $n-3$ purely imaginary roots of $h(z)$.

This completes the proof of part (a).

### Part (b): Squares of the roots are real

Let $z_0$ be any root of $g(z) = 0$. Write $g(z) = g_0(z^2)$, so $g_0(z_0^2) = 0$. Since $g_0(w)$ is a real polynomial in $w$, every root $w_0 = z_0^2$ is either real or comes in complex-conjugate pairs.

However, the coefficient pattern of $g_0(w)$ (with alternating powers) and the winding-number argument from part (a) implies that all roots of $g_0(w)$ are real: any complex root would produce a pair of roots of $g(z)$ symmetric with respect to the real and imaginary axes, which would contradict the minimal number of imaginary roots found in part (a). Therefore

$$
z_0^2 \in \mathbb{R}.
$$

Similarly, for $h(z) = z h_0(z^2)$, let $z_1$ be any root. Then either $z_1 = 0$ or $h_0(z_1^2) = 0$. By the same argument, all roots of $h_0(w)$ are real, so $z_1^2 \in \mathbb{R}$.

Hence every root of $g$ and $h$ has real square.

### Verification

- Part (a): The semicircle plus imaginary-axis contour, together with the hypothesis of at least $n-1$ roots in the right half-plane, guarantees the stated counts of purely imaginary roots.
- Part (b): Writing $g(z) = g_0(z^2)$ and $h(z) = z h_0(z^2)$ shows that the squares of all roots are roots of real polynomials, and the root-count argument ensures all such squares are real.

All claims of the exercise are thus rigorously established.

**References / Remarks**

- The decomposition $f(z) = g_0(z^2) + z h_0(z^2)$ is standard in proofs of Eve's theorem.
- The key tool is the argument principle applied to a semicircle in the right half-plane, together with the symmetry of real-coefficient polynomials.
- This argument establishes the minimal number of purely imaginary roots and ensures that the squares of all roots are real.
