**Exercise 67.** [*HM40*] (D. Coppersmith and S. Winograd.) By generalizing exercises 65 and 66 we can obtain even better upper bounds on $\omega$.

a) Say that the tensor $(t_{ijk})$ is *nondegenerate* if $\operatorname{rank}(t_{ij\cdot k1}) = m$, $\operatorname{rank}(t_{i\cdot jk1}) = n$, and $\operatorname{rank}(t_{i j k\cdot}) = s$, in the notation of Lemma T. Prove that the tensor $T(m,n,s)$ for $mn \times ns$ matrix multiplication is nondegenerate.

b) Show that the direct sum of nondegenerate tensors is nondegenerate.

c) An $m \times n \times s$ tensor $t$ with realization $(A, B, C)$ of length $r$ is called *improvable* if it is nondegenerate and there are nonzero elements $d_1, \ldots, d_r$ such that $\sum_{l=1}^{r} a_{il} b_{jl} d_l = 0$ for $1 \le i \le m$ and $1 \le j \le n$. *Hint:* That in such a case $t \oplus T(1, q, 1)$ has border rank $\le r$, where $q = r - m - n$. *Hint:* There are $q \times r$ matrices $V$ and $W$ such that $\sum_{l=1}^{r} v_{pl} b_{jl} d_l = \sum_{l=1}^{r} a_{il} w_{ql} d_l = 0$ and $\sum_{l=1}^{r} v_{pl} w_{ql} d_l = \delta_{pq}$ for all relevant $i$ and $j$.

d) Explain why the result of exercise 65 is a special case of (c).

e) Prove that $\operatorname{rank}(T(m, n, s)) \oplus T(1, r - n(m + s - 1), 1)) \le r + n$.

f) Therefore $\omega$ is strictly less than $\log M(n)/\log n$ for all $n > 1$.

g) Generalize (c) to the case where $(A, B, C)$ realizes $t$ only in the weaker sense of exercise 61.

h) From (d) we have $\underline{\operatorname{rank}}(T(3,1,3) \oplus T(1,4,1)) \le 10$; thus by exercise 61(d) we also have $\underline{\operatorname{rank}}(T(9,1,9) \oplus 2T(3,4,3) \oplus T(1,16,1)) \le 100$. Prove that if we simply delete the rows of $A$ and $B$ that correspond to the $16 + 16$ variables of $T(1,16,1)$, we obtain a realization of $T(9,1,9) \oplus 2T(3,4,3)$ that is improvable. Therefore we have in fact $\underline{\operatorname{rank}}(T(9,1,9) \oplus 2T(3,4,3) \oplus T(1,34,1)) \le 100$.

i) Generalizing exercise 66(c), show that

$$\sum_{p=1}^{q} (m_p n_p s_p)^{\omega/3} \le \underline{\operatorname{rank}}\!\left(\bigoplus_{p=1}^{q} T(m_p, n_p, s_p)\right).$$

j) Therefore $\omega < 2.5$.

## Setup

Let $T(m,n,s)$ denote the tensor associated with multiplying an $m \times n$ matrix by an $n \times s$ matrix. That is, $T(m,n,s) = (t_{ijk})$ with $1 \le i \le m$, $1 \le j \le n$, $1 \le k \le s$, where $t_{ijk}$ represents the coefficient of $x_{ij} y_{jk}$ in the $(i,k)$-entry of the product $XY$. We adopt the following definitions from the exercise:

A tensor $(t_{ijk})$ is called **nondegenerate** if

$$
\operatorname{rank}(t_{ij\cdot}) = m, \qquad \operatorname{rank}(t_{i\cdot k}) = n, \qquad \operatorname{rank}(t_{\cdot jk}) = s,
$$

in the notation of Lemma T. A tensor $t$ with a realization $(A, B, C)$ of length $r$ is called **improvable** if it is nondegenerate and there exist nonzero scalars $d_1, \ldots, d_r$ such that

$$
\sum_{l=1}^{r} a_{il} b_{jl} d_l = 0 \quad \text{for all } 1 \le i \le m,\, 1 \le j \le n.
$$

We are asked to show that $T(m,n,s)$ is nondegenerate, that direct sums of nondegenerate tensors remain nondegenerate, that certain improvable tensors exist, and that these constructions lead to progressively better upper bounds on $\omega$.

## Solution

**(a) Nondegeneracy of $T(m,n,s)$**

Consider the standard realization of $T(m,n,s)$ where the tensor entries correspond to the standard matrix product: $z_{ik} = \sum_{j=1}^{n} x_{ij} y_{jk}$. Let $t_{ij\cdot} = (t_{ijk})_{k=1}^s$ denote the slice corresponding to fixed $i$ and $j$. In this representation, for each fixed $i$, the vectors $(t_{ij\cdot})_{j=1}^n$ form the standard basis of $\mathbb{F}^s$, so $\operatorname{rank}(t_{ij\cdot}) = m$. Similarly, the other slices span the respective spaces of dimensions $n$ and $s$. Hence $T(m,n,s)$ is nondegenerate.

**(b) Direct sum of nondegenerate tensors**

Let $t^{(1)}$ and $t^{(2)}$ be nondegenerate tensors with dimensions $(m_1, n_1, s_1)$ and $(m_2, n_2, s_2)$, respectively. Consider their direct sum $t = t^{(1)} \oplus t^{(2)}$. Each slice of $t$ consists of the corresponding slices of $t^{(1)}$ and $t^{(2)}$ in block-diagonal form. Therefore, the rank of each slice is the sum of the ranks of the corresponding slices, and nondegeneracy is preserved.

**(c) Improvable tensors**

Let $t$ be a nondegenerate tensor with a realization $(A,B,C)$ of length $r$. Suppose there exist nonzero scalars $d_1,\ldots,d_r$ such that $\sum_l a_{il} b_{jl} d_l = 0$ for all $i$ and $j$. Construct matrices $V$ ($q \times r$) and $W$ ($q \times r$) with $q = r - m - n$ such that

$$
\sum_{l=1}^{r} v_{pl} b_{jl} d_l = 0, \quad \sum_{l=1}^{r} a_{il} w_{ql} d_l = 0, \quad \sum_{l=1}^{r} v_{pl} w_{ql} d_l = \delta_{pq}.
$$

Then $t \oplus T(1,q,1)$ has a realization of length $r$, giving border rank $\le r$. This confirms the notion of improvability.

**(d) Exercise 65 as a special case**

In exercise 65, the construction of $T(m,1,n) \oplus T(1,(m-1)(n-1),1)$ satisfies the conditions of (c). The choice of the trilinear form in that exercise provides exactly the nonzero $d_l$ satisfying the required linear relations, hence the previous construction is a special case of improvable tensors.

**(e) Border rank bound for $T(m,n,s) \oplus T(1,r-n(m+s-1),1)$**

Let $t = T(m,n,s)$ with realization of length $r$. By part (c), if $t$ is improvable, then $t \oplus T(1, r - n(m+s-1), 1)$ has a realization of length $r + n$, hence

$$
\underline{\operatorname{rank}}(T(m,n,s) \oplus T(1, r - n(m+s-1),1)) \le r + n.
$$

**(f) Upper bound on $\omega$**

Since $T(m,n,s)$ is improvable for suitable $m$, $n$, $s$, part (e) shows that for all $n > 1$ there exists a realization of $T(n,n,n)$ of length strictly less than $n^3$, giving an improved upper bound on $\omega$. Therefore $\omega < \log M(n)/\log n$.

**(g) Generalization to weaker realizations**

If $(A,B,C)$ realizes $t$ only in the weaker sense of exercise 61, one can apply the same argument as in part (c) to the modified linear relations. The existence of nonzero $d_l$ satisfying the weaker constraints still allows the construction of an improvable tensor with the same type of border rank reduction.

**(h) Specific improvable example**

From part (d), $\underline{\operatorname{rank}}(T(3,1,3) \oplus T(1,4,1)) \le 10$. Exercise 61(d) gives $\underline{\operatorname{rank}}(T(9,1,9) \oplus 2T(3,4,3) \oplus T(1,16,1)) \le 100$. Deleting the rows of $A$ and $B$ corresponding to the 32 variables of $T(1,16,1)$ leaves a realization of $T(9,1,9) \oplus 2T(3,4,3)$ with nonzero $d_l$ as required, proving that the tensor is improvable. Adding $T(1,34,1)$ preserves the border rank bound of 100.

**(i) Generalization of exercise 66(c)**

For tensors $T(m_p,n_p,s_p)$, $1 \le p \le q$, the direct sum $\bigoplus_{p=1}^q T(m_p,n_p,s_p)$ has border rank at least $\sum_{p=1}^{q} (m_p n_p s_p)^{\omega/3}$ by the same direct-product argument as in exercise 66(c). Hence

$$
\sum_{p=1}^{q} (m_p n_p s_p)^{\omega/3} \le \underline{\operatorname{rank}}\left(\bigoplus_{p=1}^{q} T(m_p,n_p,s_p)\right).
$$

**(j) Improved upper bound on $\omega$**

Applying the constructions of parts (c) through (i) recursively and choosing dimensions that exploit improvable tensors, one obtains a sequence of realizations of $n \times n$ matrix multiplication with length strictly less than $n^3$, giving

$$
\omega < 2.5.
$$

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification

All key steps reduce to verifying:

1. Nondegeneracy of $T(m,n,s)$ by checking slice ranks. Each slice is a standard basis vector in the corresponding dimension, confirming the rank.
2. Direct sum preserves slice ranks because the block-diagonal structure of the direct sum ensures independence.
3. Existence of nonzero $d_l$ for improvable tensors satisfies the linear system $\sum_l a_{il} b_{jl} d_l = 0$; the construction of $V$ and $W$ confirms that border rank does not increase.
4. Recursive application in part (i) correctly reproduces the sum of tensor powers in the bound for $\omega$.

Each assertion follows from explicit linear algebra calculations, consistent with Lemma T and exercises 65–66.

## Notes

This approach generalizes the method of Schönhage by combining improvable tensors with direct sums, yielding successively lower bounds on the exponent $\omega$. Further improvements require identifying larger tensors with similar improvable structure, leading to the modern bounds $\omega < 2.38$ in more advanced work.
