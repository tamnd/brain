[Section 4.6.4: Evaluation of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 57.** [*M20*] Show that fast Fourier transforms can be used to compute the coefficients of the product $x(u)y(u)$ of two given polynomials of degree $n$, using $O(n \log n)$ operations of (exact) addition and multiplication of complex numbers. [*Hint:* Consider the product of Fourier transforms of the coefficients.]

## Solution

Let

$u(x)=\sum_{i=0}^{n} a_i x^i,\qquad y(x)=\sum_{j=0}^{n} b_j x^j.$

Their product is

$z(x)=u(x)y(x)=\sum_{k=0}^{2n} c_k x^k,$

where

$c_k=\sum_{i+j=k} a_i b_j.$

Choose an integer $m$ such that $m=2^r$ and $m\ge 2n+1$. Let $\omega=e^{2\pi i/m}$ be a primitive $m$th root of unity. For a polynomial $p(x)=\sum_{k=0}^{m-1} p_k x^k$, define its values at the $m$th roots of unity by

$P(\omega^t)=\sum_{k=0}^{m-1} p_k \omega^{tk},\qquad 0\le t<m.$

Applying this to $u$ and $y$ yields

$U_t=u(\omega^t),\qquad V_t=y(\omega^t),\qquad 0\le t<m.$

For each $t$,

$U_tV_t=u(\omega^t)y(\omega^t)=z(\omega^t).$

Hence the sequence $(U_tV_t)_{t=0}^{m-1}$ consists of the values of $z(x)$ at the $m$ distinct points $\omega^t$.

Since $\deg z\le 2n<m$, a polynomial of degree at most $2n$ is uniquely determined by its values at $m$ distinct points. Therefore $z(x)$ is uniquely determined by the values $z(\omega^t)$.

The coefficients $(c_0,\ldots,c_{m-1})$ of $z(x)$ are obtained from the values $z(\omega^t)$ by the inverse discrete Fourier transform,

$c_k=\frac{1}{m}\sum_{t=0}^{m-1} z(\omega^t)\,\omega^{-kt},\qquad 0\le k<m.$

The computation proceeds by three stages.

The first stage computes $(U_t)$ from $(a_k)$ and $(V_t)$ from $(b_k)$ using two discrete Fourier transforms of length $m$. Each transform is computed by the fast Fourier transform algorithm using $O(m\log m)$ arithmetic operations in $\mathbb{C}$.

The second stage forms $W_t=U_tV_t$ for $0\le t<m$, requiring $m$ multiplications.

The third stage applies the inverse fast Fourier transform to $(W_t)$ to obtain $(c_k)$, again in $O(m\log m)$ operations.

The total number of operations is therefore bounded by a constant multiple of $m\log m$. Since $m\le 4n+2$, this bound is $O(n\log n)$.

The correctness follows from the identity $z(\omega^t)=u(\omega^t)y(\omega^t)$ and from the uniqueness of interpolation of a polynomial of degree at most $2n$ from its values at $m>2n$ distinct points. This completes the proof. âˆŽ
