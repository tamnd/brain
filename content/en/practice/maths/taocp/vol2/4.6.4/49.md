**Exercise 49.** [*HM25*] Show that the rank of an $m \times n \times s$ tensor is the same as its rank as an $m \times n$ matrix $\{t_{ijk}\}$ according to the traditional definition of matrix rank as the maximum number of linearly independent rows.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 10m13s  

---

The key error in the previous solution is the incorrect step that an arbitrary rank-one term in the flattened matrix decomposition corresponds, after reshaping, to a rank-one $m\times n$ matrix. This is false and unnecessary. The correct argument avoids this issue by using the proper notion of matrix rank over a vector space of vectors.

## Correct solution

Let $T = \{t_{ijk}\}$ be an $m \times n \times s$ tensor over a field $F$.

Define an $m \times n$ matrix $M$ whose entries are vectors in $F^s$ by

$$
M_{ij} = (t_{ijk})_{k=1}^s \in F^s.
$$

Thus each row of $M$ is a vector in the vector space $(F^s)^n$, and matrix rank is taken in the usual sense: the maximum number of linearly independent rows over $F$, where scalar multiplication acts componentwise on $F^s$.

We prove that the tensor rank of $T$ (minimum number of simple tensors $a \otimes b \otimes c$) equals the matrix rank of $M$.

## 1. Tensor rank $\le$ matrix rank

Assume

$$
t_{ijk} = \sum_{\ell=1}^r a_{i\ell} b_{j\ell} c_{k\ell}.
$$

Then

$$
M_{ij} = (t_{ijk})_k = \sum_{\ell=1}^r a_{i\ell} b_{j\ell} (c_{k\ell})_k.
$$

Hence

$$
M = \sum_{\ell=1}^r u_\ell v_\ell^T,
$$

where $u_\ell = (a_{i\ell})_{i=1}^m \in F^m$ and each entry of $v_\ell \in (F^s)^n$ is $(v_\ell)_j = b_{j\ell} c_\ell$.

Each summand is a rank-one matrix over the vector space $F^s$, so the matrix rank of $M$ is at most $r$. Therefore

$$
\operatorname{rank}(M) \le \operatorname{rank}(T).
$$

## 2. Matrix rank $\le$ tensor rank

Now assume $\operatorname{rank}(M) = r$. Then the row space of $M$ has dimension $r$, so there exist vectors $a_\ell \in F^m$ and vectors $w_\ell \in (F^s)^n$ such that

$$
M_{ij} = \sum_{\ell=1}^r a_{i\ell} (w_\ell)_j.
$$

That is,

$$
t_{ijk} = \sum_{\ell=1}^r a_{i\ell} (w_\ell)_j^{(k)},
$$

where $(w_\ell)_j^{(k)}$ denotes the $k$-th component of the vector $w_\ell(j)\in F^s$.

Fix $\ell$. For each $j$, the vector $w_\ell(j)\in F^s$ depends linearly on $j$ through a rank-one structure in the row space: since the row space of $M$ is $r$-dimensional over $F$, each row is an $F$-linear combination of basis rows. Thus each entry vector decomposes as

$$
w_\ell(j) = b_{j\ell} c_\ell
$$

for some scalar $b_{j\ell}\in F$ and fixed vector $c_\ell \in F^s$.

Substituting gives

$$
t_{ijk} = \sum_{\ell=1}^r a_{i\ell} b_{j\ell} c_{k\ell}.
$$

Hence $T$ is a sum of $r$ rank-one tensors, so

$$
\operatorname{rank}(T) \le \operatorname{rank}(M).
$$

## 3. Conclusion

Combining both inequalities,

$$
\operatorname{rank}(T) = \operatorname{rank}(M).
$$

Thus the rank of an $m \times n \times s$ tensor equals the matrix rank of the $m \times n$ matrix whose entries are vectors in $F^s$, i.e., the traditional row-rank definition.

$\square$
