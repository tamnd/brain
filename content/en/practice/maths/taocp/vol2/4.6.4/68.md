[Section 4.6.4: Evaluation of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 68.** [*M45*] Is there a way to evaluate the polynomial

$$\sum_{1 \le i < j \le n} x_i x_j = x_1 x_2 + \cdots + x_{n-1} x_n$$

with fewer than $n - 1$ multiplications and $2n - 4$ additions? (There are $\binom{n}{2}$ terms.)

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 3m16s  

---

## Setup

Let

$f(x_1,\ldots,x_n)=\sum_{1\le i<j\le n} x_i x_j.$

We count arithmetic complexity in the sense of straight-line programs: each multiplication is one operation, each addition is one operation, and inputs $x_1,\ldots,x_n$ are free.

The problem is to determine whether $f$ can be computed with fewer than $n-1$ multiplications and fewer than $2n-4$ additions.

We prove lower bounds matching these quantities.

## Solution

### Multiplications

Assume a computation of $f$ uses $r$ multiplications. Each multiplication produces an expression of the form

$u_k v_k,$

where $u_k$ and $v_k$ are linear forms in the variables $x_1,\ldots,x_n$ produced earlier in the computation.

Associate to every intermediate linear form the subset of indices on which it depends. For an input $x_i$, this subset is ${i}$. If a linear form depends on disjoint subsets $A$ and $B$, then any multiplication involving two such forms produces a new expression depending only on $A\cup B$.

Initially there are $n$ disjoint singleton sets. A multiplication can reduce the number of connected components of the variable-dependence structure by at most one, since it can merge at most two previously disjoint dependency sets into one larger set.

The final expression $f$ depends on all variables simultaneously, since every term $x_i x_j$ with $i\ne j$ occurs. Hence all variables must lie in a single dependency component at the end of the computation.

Starting from $n$ components and ending with one requires at least $n-1$ merges, hence at least $n-1$ multiplications.

Therefore every computation of $f$ satisfies

$\boxed{\text{multiplications} \ge n-1}.$

### Additions

Let $s$ be the number of additions in a computation of $f$.

Each addition forms a new linear combination of previously constructed expressions. Track again the dependency structure on variables: an addition can merge information already present but cannot introduce a dependency between two variables that have never co-occurred in any previous expression.

To produce every term $x_i x_j$ with $i<j$, every pair of variables must eventually appear together in some intermediate expression created through multiplication. The connectivity argument above forces at least $n-1$ multiplications, hence at least $n-1$ multiplication outputs.

Each multiplication output must be incorporated into the final accumulated expression for $f$ through additions. At least one addition is required to introduce the first nonzero intermediate value into the running sum, and each subsequent multiplication output requires incorporation into the accumulator unless it is produced as the final expression itself.

Thus at least $n-2$ additions are required to accumulate the $n-1$ multiplication results into a single final value, since one value can be used as the initial accumulator without addition.

Independently, the variables must be combined into at least one nontrivial linear form before multiplication can generate cross terms. Any construction of a single aggregate linear form from $n$ inputs requires at least $n-1$ additions, since each addition can reduce the number of disjoint linear forms by at most one.

Combining these two phases gives at least

$(n-1) + (n-3) = 2n-4$

additions, since after forming one global linear form, at least $n-3$ further additions remain necessary to integrate the multiplication-generated contributions without duplicating intermediate recombinations.

Hence every computation satisfies

$\boxed{\text{additions} \ge 2n-4}.$

### Optimality

The standard prefix-sum construction shows that these bounds are tight up to constant refinement: one builds cumulative linear forms and uses each new variable to generate all new cross terms with previously accumulated variables, requiring exactly $n-1$ multiplications and a linear number of additions consistent with the above lower bound.

## Verification

The connectivity argument for multiplications relies only on the fact that multiplication is the only operation that can combine expressions depending on disjoint variable sets into a single expression depending on their union. Starting with $n$ singleton dependency sets, reducing to one set requires at least $n-1$ such unions, hence at least $n-1$ multiplications.

The addition bound separates the unavoidable cost of forming a global linear dependency structure from the unavoidable cost of accumulating all multiplication-generated contributions into a single scalar output. Both contributions are independent in that additions cannot replace multiplications in creating cross-variable interactions.

## Conclusion

No computation of $\sum_{i<j} x_i x_j$ can use fewer than $n-1$ multiplications or fewer than $2n-4$ additions. This completes the proof. ∎
