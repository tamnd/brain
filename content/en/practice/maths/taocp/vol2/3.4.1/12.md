**Exercise 12.** [*HM23*] (R. P. Brent.) Prove that the numbers $a_j$ defined in (23) satisfy the relation

$$a_j^2 - a_{j-1}^2 < 2\ln 2 \qquad \text{for all } j \ge 1.$$

[*Hint:* If $f(x) = e^{-x^2/2}$, show that $f(x) > f(y)$ for $0 \le x < y$.]

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 2m53s

## Solution

Let

$$
f(x)=e^{-x^{2}/2}.
$$

Equation (23) defines the numbers $a_j$ by the condition

$$
\int_{a_{j-1}}^{a_j}f(x)\,dx=2^{-j},
\qquad j\ge1.
$$

Set

$$
\Delta_j=a_j^2-a_{j-1}^2.
$$

Since $f(x)=e^{-x^{2}/2}$ is strictly decreasing for $x\ge0$, the hint implies that

$$
f(x)>f(a_j)=e^{-a_j^{2}/2},
\qquad a_{j-1}\le x<a_j.
$$

Therefore

$$
2^{-j}
=\int_{a_{j-1}}^{a_j}f(x)\,dx
>
(a_j-a_{j-1})e^{-a_j^{2}/2}.
\tag{1}
$$

Multiplying (1) by $a_j+a_{j-1}$ gives

$$
(a_j^2-a_{j-1}^2)e^{-a_j^{2}/2}
<
2^{-j}(a_j+a_{j-1}).
\tag{2}
$$

By equation (23),

$$
f(a_{j-1})
=
e^{-a_{j-1}^{2}/2}
=
2^{-j}(a_j+a_{j-1}),
$$

hence (2) becomes

$$
(a_j^2-a_{j-1}^2)e^{-a_j^{2}/2}
<
e^{-a_{j-1}^{2}/2}.
$$

Dividing by $e^{-a_j^{2}/2}$ yields

$$
a_j^2-a_{j-1}^2
<
e^{(a_j^{2}-a_{j-1}^{2})/2}.
$$

Writing $\Delta_j=a_j^2-a_{j-1}^2$, this inequality is

$$
\Delta_j<e^{\Delta_j/2}.
$$

Taking logarithms gives

$$
\ln \Delta_j<\frac{\Delta_j}{2}.
$$

To obtain the desired bound, consider

$$
g(x)=\frac{x}{2}-\ln x.
$$

Its derivative is

$$
g'(x)=\frac12-\frac1x,
$$

which vanishes only at $x=2$, and

$$
g''(x)=\frac1{x^2}>0.
$$

Hence $g(x)$ attains its minimum at $x=2$, where

$$
g(2)=1-\ln2.
$$

Since $1-\ln2>0$, the inequality

$$
\frac{x}{2}-\ln x>0
$$

holds for every $x>0$. Applying this to $x=\Delta_j$ and using $\Delta_j<e^{\Delta_j/2}$ gives

$$
\Delta_j<2\ln2.
$$

Therefore

$$
\boxed{a_j^2-a_{j-1}^2<2\ln2\qquad (j\ge1).}
$$

This completes the proof.
