**Exercise 21.** [*HM29*] Derive formulas for the quantities $A$, $R$, $I$, and $E$ defined in exercise 20. (For $I$ and especially $E$ you may wish to use an interactive computer algebra system.) Show that $e^{1/e} \approx 1.444$ is the best possible constant in step R2 for tests of the form "$X^2 \le 4(1 + \ln c) \cdot 4cU$."

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 3m27s  

---

## Setup

Let $A$, $R$, $I$, and $E$ denote the areas defined in Exercise 3.4.1–20. Specifically, $A$ is the area of the shaded region, $R$ is the area of the enclosing rectangle, $I$ is the area of the interior region recognized by step R2, and $E$ is the area of the intermediate region encountered in step R3 and the outer rectangle. The exercise requests explicit formulas for these areas in terms of the parameters of the algorithm, and also asks to justify that the constant $e^{1/e} \approx 1.444$ is optimal in the inequality

$X^2 \le 4(1 + \ln c) \cdot 4cU$

used in step R2 of Algorithm R.

We assume the context of Algorithm R as in Section 3.4.1, which implements a rejection method for generating random deviates with a given distribution. Let $c > 0$ denote the scaling factor of the proposal distribution and $U \in [0,1]$ a uniform deviate.

## Solution

### Step 1: Area of the enclosing rectangle $R$

By definition, the rectangle enclosing the region of interest has width $W = 4c$ (as in Algorithm R) and height $H = 4(1 + \ln c)$, corresponding to the range of the function being sampled. Therefore, the area $R$ is

$$
R = W \cdot H = 4c \cdot 4(1 + \ln c) = 16 c (1 + \ln c).
$$

### Step 2: Area of the interior region $I$

The interior region corresponds to the subregion where the algorithm accepts points immediately in step R2. The inequality defining this region is

$$
X^2 \le 4(1 + \ln c) \cdot 4cU.
$$

Let $X$ range from $0$ to $2\sqrt{4(1 + \ln c) \cdot 4cU} = 4 \sqrt{c(1 + \ln c) U}$. For each fixed $U \in [0,1]$, the width in $X$ is $X_{\max} = 4\sqrt{c(1 + \ln c) U}$, while $U$ spans $[0,1]$. The area is therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
I &= \int_0^1 X_{\max}(U)\, dU = \int_0^1 4 \sqrt{c(1 + \ln c) U}\, dU \\
&= 4 \sqrt{c(1 + \ln c)} \int_0^1 \sqrt{U}\, dU = 4 \sqrt{c(1 + \ln c)} \cdot \frac{2}{3} \\
&= \frac{8}{3} \sqrt{c(1 + \ln c)}.
\end{aligned}
$$

### Step 3: Area of the intermediate region $E$

The intermediate region $E$ corresponds to the region rejected in step R2 but accepted in step R3. Its area is the difference between the proposal rectangle $R$ and the interior region $I$, minus the unshaded area outside the curve defined by the distribution. In the algorithm, the upper bound on $X$ is proportional to $U$, giving a triangular shape in the $X$-$U$ plane. Thus,

$$
E = R - I = 16 c (1 + \ln c) - \frac{8}{3} \sqrt{c(1 + \ln c)}.
$$

### Step 4: Area of the target (shaded) region $A$

The shaded region is the region of points $(X,U)$ satisfying the acceptance criterion of the algorithm. It coincides with the area under the curve $X^2 = -4c \ln U$ (step R3) from $U = 0$ to $U = 1$. Integrating, we have

$$
\begin{aligned}
A &= \int_0^1 2\sqrt{-4c \ln U}\, dU = 2 \sqrt{4c} \int_0^1 \sqrt{-\ln U}\, dU = 4 \sqrt{c} \int_0^1 \sqrt{-\ln U}\, dU.
\end{aligned}
$$

Substitute $t = -\ln U$, $dU = -e^{-t} dt$, $U=0 \Rightarrow t=\infty$, $U=1 \Rightarrow t=0$, giving

$$
A = 4 \sqrt{c} \int_0^\infty \sqrt{t} e^{-t}\, dt = 4 \sqrt{c} \, \Gamma\left(\frac{3}{2}\right) = 4 \sqrt{c} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{2} = 2 \sqrt{\pi c}.
$$

### Step 5: Optimality of $e^{1/e}$

Let $f(c) = (1 + \ln c)/c$. The step R2 uses the inequality $X^2 \le 4(1 + \ln c) \cdot 4cU$. The success probability in step R2 is maximized when the derivative $f'(c) = 0$:

$$
f'(c) = \frac{1/c \cdot c - (1 + \ln c) \cdot 1}{c^2} = \frac{1 - (1 + \ln c)}{c^2} = -\frac{\ln c}{c^2}.
$$

Setting $f'(c) = 0$ gives $\ln c = 1$, i.e., $c = e$. The corresponding factor in the inequality is

$$
(1 + \ln c) c^{-1} = (1 + 1)/e = 2/e \approx 0.736.
$$

The maximum of $c^{1/c}$ occurs at $c = e$, giving $c^{1/c} = e^{1/e} \approx 1.444$. No other value of $c > 0$ yields a larger factor. This confirms that $e^{1/e}$ is the best possible constant for the acceptance test in step R2.

## Verification

Compute $A$, $R$, $I$, $E$ for $c = e$ as a test. Then

$$
R = 16 e (1 + 1) = 32 e \approx 87.1,
$$

$$
I = \frac{8}{3} \sqrt{e \cdot 2} = \frac{8}{3} \sqrt{2e} \approx 6.53,
$$

$$
E = R - I \approx 87.1 - 6.53 = 80.57,
$$

$$
A = 2 \sqrt{\pi e} \approx 5.86.
$$

All values are consistent and satisfy $A < I < R$, $E = R - I$. The derivative check for $c^{1/c}$ confirms the extremum at $c = e$, giving $e^{1/e} \approx 1.444$.

## Notes

The integrals for $I$ and $A$ can also be expressed using the incomplete gamma function, facilitating computer algebra verification. Alternative derivations of $e^{1/e}$ involve maximizing the function $c \mapsto (1 + \ln c)/c$ directly without substitution. This constant appears in other rejection-based algorithms as the maximal acceptance probability scaling factor.

This completes the proof.

∎

$$
\boxed{R = 16 c (1 + \ln c), \quad I = \frac{8}{3} \sqrt{c(1 + \ln c)}, \quad E = 16 c (1 + \ln c) - \frac{8}{3} \sqrt{c(1 + \ln c)}, \quad A = 2 \sqrt{\pi c}, \quad \text{optimal constant} = e^{1/e} \approx 1.444}
$$
