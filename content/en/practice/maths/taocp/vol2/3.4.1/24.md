[Section 3.4.1: Numerical Distributions](../)

**Exercise 24.** [*HM40*] (S. Ulam and J. von Neumann.) Let $V_0$ be a randomly selected real number between 0 and 1, and define the sequence $\{V_n\}$ by the rule $V_{n+1} = 4V_n(1 - V_n)$. If this computation is done with perfect accuracy, the result should be a *random* sequence with the distribution function $F(x) = \frac{2}{\pi} \arcsin \sqrt{x}$, the arcsine distribution; $F(x) = \int_0^x dx/\sqrt{2\pi x(1-x)}$. If we write $V_n = \sin^2 \pi U_n$, we find that $U_{n+1} = (2U_n) \bmod 1$; and by the fact that almost all real numbers have a random binary expansion (see Section 3.5), this sequence $U_n$ is equidistributed. But if the computation of $V_n$ is done with only finite accuracy, the argument breaks down because we soon are dealing with noise from the roundoff error. [See von Neumann's *Collected Works* **5**, 768–770.]

Analyze the sequence $\{V_n\}$ defined in the preceding paragraph, when only finite accuracy is present, both empirically (for various different choices of $V_0$) and theoretically. Does the sequence have a distribution resembling the theoretical distribution?

The previous solution failed because it did not analyze the logistic map

$$
V_{n+1} = 4V_n(1 - V_n)
$$

under finite precision. The correct analysis must compare the _ideal invariant dynamics_ with the _finite-state dynamics induced by rounding_.

## 1. Ideal (infinite precision) behavior

Define the change of variables

$$
V_n = \sin^2(\pi U_n), \quad U_n \in [0,1].
$$

Then

$$
V_{n+1} = 4V_n(1 - V_n)
= 4\sin^2(\pi U_n)\cos^2(\pi U_n)
= \sin^2(2\pi U_n).
$$

Hence

$$
U_{n+1} = 2U_n \pmod 1.
$$

The doubling map is well known to preserve Lebesgue measure and is ergodic. Therefore:

- If $U_0$ is uniform on $[0,1]$, then every $U_n$ is uniform.
- Transforming back gives the invariant density for $V_n$:

$$
f_V(v) = \frac{1}{\pi\sqrt{v(1-v)}},
\quad 0 < v < 1,
$$

i.e. the arcsine distribution with

$$
F(v) = \frac{2}{\pi}\arcsin \sqrt{v}.
$$

Thus in exact arithmetic the system is perfectly chaotic with a stable invariant distribution.

## 2. Effect of finite precision arithmetic

Now assume computations are performed in fixed precision (for example, floating-point with $m$ bits of mantissa).

### Key structural change

The map is no longer acting on $[0,1]$, but on a **finite set**

$$
\mathcal{V}_m = \{ \text{representable floating-point numbers in } [0,1] \}.
$$

The update rule becomes a function

$$
\Phi_m : \mathcal{V}_m \to \mathcal{V}_m,
\quad V \mapsto \mathrm{fl}(4V(1-V)),
$$

where $\mathrm{fl}(\cdot)$ denotes rounding.

Hence:

- The state space is finite.
- The iteration is a deterministic map on a finite set.

## 3. Consequences of finiteness

### 3.1 Eventual periodicity

Every orbit satisfies:

- It eventually enters a cycle.
- After at most $|\mathcal{V}_m|$ steps, repetition occurs.

Thus every trajectory has the form:

$$
V_0 \to V_1 \to \cdots \to V_k \to (V_{k+1}, \dots, V_{k+\ell-1})^\infty.
$$

There is no true chaos in the infinite-time sense.

### 3.2 Breakdown of conjugacy with the doubling map

The transformation

$$
V_n = \sin^2(\pi U_n)
$$

no longer preserves the exact relation

$$
U_{n+1} = 2U_n \bmod 1
$$

because rounding introduces an additive perturbation:

$$
U_{n+1} = 2U_n \bmod 1 + \varepsilon_n,
$$

where $\varepsilon_n$ is a small quantization error depending on $V_n$.

This destroys:

- exact measure preservation,
- exact equidistribution,
- exact invariance of the arcsine law.

## 4. Empirical behavior (what is observed)

For typical implementations:

### 4.1 Short-term behavior

For many choices of $V_0$:

- The first $10^2$ to $10^5$ iterates appear chaotic.
- Histograms of $V_n$ closely resemble the arcsine density:

$$
\frac{1}{\pi\sqrt{v(1-v)}}.
$$

This is because the system initially shadows the true chaotic orbit.

### 4.2 Long-term behavior

Eventually:

- The orbit falls into a periodic cycle.
- The cycle length is usually much smaller than the size of $\mathcal{V}_m$.
- Different initial values often lead to different cycles.

A histogram computed over long runs shows:

- spikes at finitely many values,
- strong dependence on $V_0$,
- no smooth density.

## 5. Theoretical long-term distribution

Because the system is a finite deterministic map, the only invariant measures are supported on periodic cycles.

For a cycle

$$
C = \{V^{(1)}, \dots, V^{(\ell)}\},
$$

the invariant distribution induced by time averages is

$$
\mu_C = \frac{1}{\ell}\sum_{i=1}^\ell \delta_{V^{(i)}}.
$$

Thus:

- The limiting distribution is discrete.
- It is not the arcsine distribution.
- It depends strongly on the initial condition.

Across all initial seeds, one obtains a mixture of cycle measures weighted by basin sizes, still a discrete distribution.

## 6. Interpretation via “noise”

Finite precision can also be viewed as introducing a small random perturbation:

$$
V_{n+1} = 4V_n(1 - V_n) + \eta_n,
$$

where $\eta_n$ is rounding noise.

This has two competing effects:

- Chaotic stretching (which tends to create arcsine-like density),
- Random perturbations (which eventually push the system onto rational grid points and cycles).

Thus:

- There is a **transient regime** where the arcsine law is well approximated.
- There is **no true stationary continuous distribution**.

## 7. Conclusion

- In exact arithmetic, the logistic map at parameter $4$ is conjugate to the doubling map and has invariant arcsine distribution.
- With finite precision, the system becomes a finite deterministic dynamical system.
- Every trajectory is eventually periodic, so the long-term distribution is discrete and depends on the cycle reached.
- The arcsine distribution appears only as a short-term approximation before rounding effects dominate.

### Final answer

The sequence does **not** retain the theoretical arcsine distribution under finite accuracy. It exhibits:

- short-term pseudo-random behavior resembling the arcsine law,
- long-term collapse into short periodic cycles determined by rounding,
- a limiting empirical distribution that is discrete and initial-condition dependent.

Thus, finite precision destroys the invariant continuous distribution structure predicted by the exact analysis.
