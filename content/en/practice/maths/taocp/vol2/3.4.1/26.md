**Exercise 26.** [*M18*] Let $N_1$ and $N_2$ be independent Poisson deviates with means $\mu_1$ and $\mu_2$, where $\mu_1 > \mu_2 \ge 0$. Prove or disprove: (a) $N_1 + N_2$ has the Poisson distribution with mean $\mu_1 + \mu_2$. (b) $N_1 - N_2$ has the Poisson distribution with mean $\mu_1 - \mu_2$.

Let $N_1$ and $N_2$ be independent Poisson random variables with parameters $\mu_1$ and $\mu_2$, where $\mu_1 > \mu_2 \ge 0$. Thus, for $n \ge 0$,

$$
\Pr\{N_i = n\} = e^{-\mu_i}\frac{\mu_i^n}{n!}, \quad i=1,2.
$$

## (a) Distribution of $N_1 + N_2$

Let $N = N_1 + N_2$. Since $N_1$ and $N_2$ are independent, we compute the distribution of $N$ by convolution:

$$
\Pr\{N = n\} = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \Pr\{N_1 = k\}\Pr\{N_2 = n-k\}.
$$

Substituting the Poisson mass functions,

$$
\Pr\{N = n\} = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \left(e^{-\mu_1}\frac{\mu_1^k}{k!}\right)\left(e^{-\mu_2}\frac{\mu_2^{n-k}}{(n-k)!}\right).
$$

Factoring out constants,

$$
\Pr\{N = n\} = e^{-(\mu_1+\mu_2)} \sum_{k=0}^{n} \frac{\mu_1^k \mu_2^{n-k}}{k!(n-k)!}.
$$

Rewrite each term using binomial coefficients:

$$
\frac{1}{k!(n-k)!} = \frac{1}{n!}\binom{n}{k}.
$$

Hence,

$$
\Pr\{N = n\} = e^{-(\mu_1+\mu_2)} \frac{1}{n!} \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k}\mu_1^k \mu_2^{n-k}.
$$

By the binomial theorem,

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k}\mu_1^k \mu_2^{n-k} = (\mu_1 + \mu_2)^n.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\Pr\{N = n\} = e^{-(\mu_1+\mu_2)} \frac{(\mu_1+\mu_2)^n}{n!},
$$

so $N_1 + N_2$ is Poisson with mean $\mu_1 + \mu_2$. This proves part (a).

## (b) Distribution of $N_1 - N_2$

Let $D = N_1 - N_2$. The support of $D$ is all integers, since $N_2$ can exceed $N_1$, so $D$ can be negative. A Poisson distribution, however, is supported only on $\{0,1,2,\dots\}$. This already rules out the possibility that $D$ is Poisson whenever $\mu_2 > 0$.

To be explicit, consider any $k < 0$. Then

$$
\Pr\{D = k\} = \Pr\{N_1 - N_2 = k\} > 0
$$

because there exist pairs $(i, i-k)$ with $i \ge 0$ such that $N_1 = i$ and $N_2 = i-k \ge 0$. Hence $D$ assigns positive probability to negative integers, which is impossible for a Poisson random variable.

Thus $N_1 - N_2$ is not Poisson whenever $\mu_2 > 0$.

For completeness, we identify its actual distribution. For any integer $k$,

$$
\Pr\{D = k\} = \sum_{j=\max(0,-k)}^{\infty} \Pr\{N_1 = j+k,\, N_2 = j\}.
$$

Using independence,

$$
\Pr\{D = k\} = \sum_{j=\max(0,-k)}^{\infty} e^{-\mu_1}\frac{\mu_1^{j+k}}{(j+k)!}\; e^{-\mu_2}\frac{\mu_2^j}{j!}.
$$

So

$$
\Pr\{D = k\} = e^{-(\mu_1+\mu_2)} \sum_{j=\max(0,-k)}^{\infty} \frac{\mu_1^{j+k}\mu_2^j}{(j+k)!\,j!}.
$$

This is the Skellam distribution with parameters $(\mu_1,\mu_2)$, not a Poisson distribution.

Finally, the condition $\mu_1 > \mu_2$ does not change this conclusion. Even when $\mu_1 > \mu_2$, the difference still has full integer support and cannot be Poisson.

## Conclusion

Part (a): $N_1 + N_2 \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(\mu_1 + \mu_2)$.

Part (b): $N_1 - N_2$ is not Poisson for $\mu_2 > 0$; it follows the Skellam distribution.
