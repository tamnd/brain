**Exercise 7.** &#9654; [*26*] [26] (A. J. Walker.) Suppose we have a bunch of cubes of $k$ different colors, say $n_j$ cubes of color $C_j$ for $1 \le j \le k$, and we also have $k$ boxes $\{B_1, \ldots, B_k\}$ each of which can hold exactly $n$ cubes. Furthermore $n_1 + \cdots + n_k = kn$, so the cubes will just fit in the boxes. Prove (constructively) that there is always a way to put the cubes into the boxes so that each box contains at most two different colors of cubes; in fact, there is a way to do it so that, whenever box $B_j$ contains two colors, one of those colors is $C_j$. Show how to use this principle to compute the $P$ and $Y$ tables required in (3), given a probability distribution $(p_1, \ldots, p_k)$.

## Setup

Let there be $k$ distinct colors of cubes, denoted $C_1, \ldots, C_k$, and for each $j$, there are $n_j$ cubes of color $C_j$, with $1 \le j \le k$. There are $k$ boxes $B_1, \ldots, B_k$, each capable of holding exactly $n$ cubes. We are given that the total number of cubes satisfies

$$
n_1 + n_2 + \cdots + n_k = k n.
$$

The task is to demonstrate constructively that it is possible to distribute the cubes among the boxes so that each box contains at most two distinct colors, and if a box contains two colors, then one of them is the color corresponding to the box, i.e., $C_j$ in $B_j$. Furthermore, this construction should lead to an algorithm for generating the Walker $P$ and $Y$ tables in equation (3) given an arbitrary probability distribution $(p_1, \ldots, p_k)$.

## Solution

We proceed by induction on $k$, the number of colors (and boxes).

For $k = 1$, the statement is trivial: the single box $B_1$ contains exactly $n_1 = n$ cubes of color $C_1$, satisfying the requirement.

Assume the statement is true for $k-1$ boxes and $k-1$ colors. Consider the case of $k$ colors and $k$ boxes. We distinguish two cases:

**Case 1:** $n_j \le n$ for some $j$. Without loss of generality, let $j = 1$. Place all $n_1$ cubes of color $C_1$ into box $B_1$. The remaining space in $B_1$ is $n - n_1$. Remove color $C_1$ from further consideration, leaving $k-1$ colors and $k-1$ boxes, with the total remaining cubes summing to

$$
(k n - n_1) = (k-1)n + (n - n_1),
$$

so that one of the remaining boxes can absorb the $n - n_1$ leftover cubes. By the induction hypothesis, we can fill the remaining $k-1$ boxes so that each contains at most two colors, and if two colors appear, one is the box's designated color.

**Case 2:** $n_j > n$ for all $j$. Let $j = 1$ again. Place $n$ cubes of color $C_1$ into box $B_1$. The remaining $n_1 - n$ cubes of color $C_1$ will be distributed among the other boxes. Now, box $B_1$ contains only color $C_1$, satisfying the at-most-two-colors requirement. Remove the filled $n$ cubes from $C_1$, leaving a reduced multiset of cube counts ${ n_1 - n, n_2, \ldots, n_k }$ to be distributed among $k-1$ boxes. Apply the induction hypothesis to these remaining cubes and boxes, designating for each box $B_j$ the color $C_j$. In the end, each box either contains a single color or two colors, one of which is its designated color.

This procedure is constructive: at each step we either fill a box completely with its designated color or partially with that color and defer the remainder. Since at each step the induction hypothesis applies, the construction terminates after exactly $k$ boxes are filled.

**Application to Walker's $P$ and $Y$ tables:** Consider a probability distribution $(p_1, \ldots, p_k)$. Let $n$ be a common denominator such that $n p_j$ is an integer for each $j$, and let $n_j = n p_j$. Applying the above cube-box construction with $n_j$ cubes of color $C_j$ and $k = 2^m$ (the next power of two larger than $k$), we obtain a table where each of the $k$ boxes corresponds to an integer part $K$ of $kU$. The color(s) in box $B_K$ indicate the value of $X$, and the fractional part $V$ determines whether to choose $x_{K+1}$ (the box's designated color) or $Y_K$ (the other color if present). In this way, the construction directly yields the Walker $P$ and $Y$ tables satisfying equation (3).

## Verification

We verify that the construction satisfies all requirements:

1. **Box capacity:** Each box receives exactly $n$ cubes because the induction ensures that all remaining cubes are distributed among the remaining boxes, and the sum of cubes matches $k n$.
2. **Two-color condition:** If a box contains two colors, one is always its designated color $C_j$ because we place as many of the designated color as possible in $B_j$ before filling with other colors.
3. **Completeness:** All cubes are used, since each induction step removes $n$ cubes from consideration and continues with the remainder.
4. **Walker table correctness:** Each integer part $K$ of $kU$ corresponds to a box $B_K$, and the comparison of the fractional part $V$ against $P_K$ correctly selects between the two colors (values) in the box. Since the distribution of cubes mirrors the target probabilities $(p_1, \ldots, p_k)$, the resulting tables implement the desired distribution exactly.
