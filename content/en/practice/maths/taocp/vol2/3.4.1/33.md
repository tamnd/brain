**Exercise 33.** [*29*] [29] Algorithms P, M, F, and R generate normal deviates by consuming an unknown number of uniform random variables $U_1, U_2, \ldots$ How can they be modified so that the output is a function of just one $U$?

## Solution

Algorithms P, M, F, and R generate a normal deviate by reading a sequence of independent uniform random variables

$$
U_1,U_2,U_3,\ldots,
$$

stopping after a random number of them has been examined. Let the output be

$$
X=f(U_1,U_2,\ldots).
$$

Although only finitely many $U_i$ are actually used on any particular run, the output is nevertheless a measurable function of the entire sequence.

The problem is to replace the independent sequence $(U_1,U_2,\ldots)$ by a single uniform random variable $U$.

Let

$$
U=0.b_1b_2b_3\cdots
$$

be the binary expansion of a uniform random variable on $[0,1)$, using the nonterminating expansion whenever two representations are possible. The bits $b_i$ are independent Bernoulli variables with parameter $1/2$.

Partition the positive integers into infinitely many disjoint infinite sets. For example,

$$
A_1=\{1,3,5,7,\ldots\},
$$

$$
A_2=\{2,6,10,14,\ldots\},
$$

$$
A_3=\{4,12,20,28,\ldots\},
$$

and so on. Write the elements of $A_j$ in increasing order,

$$
A_j=\{a_{j1},a_{j2},a_{j3},\ldots\}.
$$

Define

$$
U_j
=
0.b_{a_{j1}}\,b_{a_{j2}}\,b_{a_{j3}}\cdots .
$$

Since each $U_j$ is formed from an infinite collection of independent unbiased bits, each $U_j$ is uniformly distributed on $[0,1)$. Since the sets $A_j$ are disjoint, the collections of bits used for different $U_j$ are independent. Hence

$$
U_1,U_2,U_3,\ldots
$$

is a sequence of independent uniform random variables having exactly the same joint distribution as the sequence required by the original algorithm.

Therefore Algorithm P, M, F, or R may be modified as follows. Generate one uniform random variable $U$, recover from its binary expansion the sequence $U_1,U_2,\ldots$ defined above, and then run the original algorithm exactly as before. The output becomes

$$
X=f(U_1,U_2,\ldots)=g(U),
$$

for a suitable function $g$.

Because the reconstructed sequence $(U_1,U_2,\ldots)$ has the same distribution as the original independent sequence, the distribution of $X$ is unchanged. Thus the normal deviate produced by Algorithms P, M, F, and R can be regarded as a function of a single uniform random variable $U$.

This construction is an explicit measure-preserving correspondence between one uniform random variable and a countably infinite sequence of independent uniform random variables, which is exactly what the exercise requires.
