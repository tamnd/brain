**Exercise 31.** [*HM39*] (*Direct generation of normal deviates.*)

a) Prove that if $a_1^2 + \cdots + a_n^2 = 1$ and if $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are independent normal deviates with mean 0 and variance 1, then $a_1 X_1 + \cdots + a_n X_n$ is a normal deviate with mean 0 and variance 1.

b) The result of (a) suggests that we can generate new normal deviates from old ones, just as we obtain new uniform deviates from old ones. For example, we might use the idea of 3.2.2–(7), but with a recurrence like

$$X_n = (X_{n-24} + X_{n-55})/\sqrt{2} \qquad \text{or} \qquad X_n = \tfrac{1}{2}X_{n-21} + \tfrac{1}{2}X_{n-55},$$

after a set of normal deviates $X_1, \ldots, X_{54}$ has been initialized. Explain why this is *not* a good idea.

c) Show, however, that there is a suitable way to generate normal deviates quickly from other normal deviates, by using a refinement of the idea in (a) and (b). [*Hint:* If $X$ and $Y$ are independent normal deviates, so are $X' = X\cos\theta + Y\sin\theta$ and $Y' = -X\sin\theta + Y\cos\theta$, for any angle $\theta$.]

## Setup

Let $N(0,1)$ denote the normal distribution with mean $0$ and variance $1$. Part (a) asks for a proof that every linear combination

$$
Y=a_1X_1+\cdots+a_nX_n,
$$

with

$$
a_1^2+\cdots+a_n^2=1,
$$

is again distributed as $N(0,1)$ when $X_1,\ldots,X_n$ are independent $N(0,1)$ variables.

Part (b) asks why recurrences such as

$$
X_n=\frac{X_{n-24}+X_{n-55}}{\sqrt2}
$$

or

$$
X_n=\frac12X_{n-21}+\frac12X_{n-55}
$$

do not provide a satisfactory random number generator for normal deviates.

Part (c) asks for a suitable method that does generate new normal deviates rapidly from old ones, using the rotational transformation

$$
X'=X\cos\theta+Y\sin\theta,\qquad
Y'=-X\sin\theta+Y\cos\theta.
$$

## Solution

### (a)

Consider

$$
Y=a_1X_1+\cdots+a_nX_n.
$$

Since $E(X_i)=0$,

$$
E(Y)=a_1E(X_1)+\cdots+a_nE(X_n)=0.
$$

Because the $X_i$ are independent and $\operatorname{Var}(X_i)=1$,

$$
\operatorname{Var}(Y)
=\sum_{i=1}^{n}a_i^2\operatorname{Var}(X_i)
=\sum_{i=1}^{n}a_i^2
=1.
$$

To determine the distribution of $Y$, compute its moment generating function:

$$
M_Y(t)
=E(e^{tY})
=E\!\left(
e^{t(a_1X_1+\cdots+a_nX_n)}
\right).
$$

Independence gives

$$
M_Y(t)
=\prod_{i=1}^{n}E(e^{ta_iX_i}).
$$

Since each $X_i$ is $N(0,1)$,

$$
E(e^{sa_iX_i})=e^{s^2a_i^2/2},
$$

hence

$$
M_Y(t)
=\prod_{i=1}^{n}e^{t^2a_i^2/2}
=e^{(t^2/2)\sum a_i^2}
=e^{t^2/2}.
$$

This is the moment generating function of $N(0,1)$. Therefore $Y$ is a normal deviate with mean $0$ and variance $1$.

### (b)

The recurrence

$$
X_n=\frac{X_{n-24}+X_{n-55}}{\sqrt2}
$$

preserves the mean and variance because

$$
\frac1{2}+\frac1{2}=1.
$$

Similarly,

$$
X_n=\frac12X_{n-21}+\frac12X_{n-55}
$$

has variance

$$
\frac14+\frac14=\frac12,
$$

so the variables would have to be rescaled; nevertheless, the essential difficulty is the same.

The problem is that part (a) guarantees only the marginal distribution of each newly produced variable. It does not guarantee independence.

Indeed, every value produced by the recurrence is a linear combination of the initial values

$$
X_1,\ldots,X_{55}.
$$

Consequently, all later variables lie in the $55$-dimensional vector space spanned by those initial quantities. After sufficiently many steps there are exact linear relations among the generated values. The sequence therefore cannot behave like an infinite sequence of independent normal deviates.

A second defect is analogous to the weakness of additive random number generators discussed in Section 3.2.2. Correlations are propagated by the recurrence. Although each individual variable has the correct normal distribution, collections of generated variables do not have the joint distribution that independent normal deviates should possess.

Hence these recurrences produce the correct one-dimensional distribution but fail to produce satisfactory independence properties.

### (c)

Let $X$ and $Y$ be independent $N(0,1)$ variables. Define

$$
X'=X\cos\theta+Y\sin\theta,
$$

$$
Y'=-X\sin\theta+Y\cos\theta.
$$

The transformation matrix is

$$
A=
\begin{pmatrix}
\cos\theta & \sin\theta\\
-\sin\theta & \cos\theta
\end{pmatrix},
$$

and

$$
AA^{T}=I.
$$

For any real numbers $u,v$,

$$
uX'+vY'
=(u\cos\theta-v\sin\theta)X
+(u\sin\theta+v\cos\theta)Y.
$$

By part (a), this linear combination is normal with variance

$$
(u\cos\theta-v\sin\theta)^2
+(u\sin\theta+v\cos\theta)^2
=u^2+v^2.
$$

Therefore the joint distribution of $(X',Y')$ is the same as that of $(X,Y)$. In particular, $X'$ and $Y'$ are independent $N(0,1)$ variables.

This suggests the following method. Maintain a pool of independent normal deviates. Select two of them, say $X$ and $Y$. Generate a random angle $\theta$, independent of $X$ and $Y$, and replace the pair by

$$
X'=X\cos\theta+Y\sin\theta,
\qquad
Y'=-X\sin\theta+Y\cos\theta.
$$

Since the transformation is orthogonal, the pair remains independent and normally distributed. Repeated random rotations of selected pairs preserve the entire joint normal distribution while continually producing fresh values. Unlike the recurrences of part (b), orthogonal transformations preserve all covariance relations and do not collapse the sequence into a low-dimensional linear recurrence structure.

Thus a suitable fast generator is obtained by storing a collection of normal deviates and repeatedly applying random orthogonal rotations to pairs of them.

## Verification

For part (a),

$$
M_Y(t)
=\prod_{i=1}^{n}e^{t^2a_i^2/2}
=e^{t^2/2},
$$

which is exactly the moment generating function of $N(0,1)$.

For part (b), every generated value is a linear combination of finitely many initial variables, hence the entire infinite sequence is confined to a finite-dimensional vector space and cannot consist of independent normal deviates.

For part (c), the rotation matrix satisfies

$$
AA^{T}=I,
$$

so it preserves Euclidean length. Therefore the covariance matrix remains the identity matrix, and a multivariate normal vector remains a vector of independent $N(0,1)$ variables after the transformation.

This completes the proof.

∎
