**Exercise 22.** [*HM40*] Can the exact Poisson distribution for large $\mu$ be obtained by generating an appropriate normal deviate, converting it to an integer in some convenient way, and applying a (possibly complicated) correction a small percent of the time?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 2m37s  

---

## Setup

We are asked to determine whether the exact Poisson distribution with mean $\mu$, for large $\mu$, can be obtained by generating a suitable normal deviate, converting it to an integer, and applying a correction only occasionally. Let $X$ be a Poisson random variable with parameter $\mu$, so that

$\Pr\{X = n\} = \frac{\mu^n e^{-\mu}}{n!}, \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$

and let $Y$ be a normal deviate with mean $\mu$ and variance $\mu$, that is, $Y \sim N(\mu, \mu)$. The idea is to consider a procedure of the form

1. Generate $Y \sim N(\mu, \mu)$.
2. Set $N = \lfloor Y + \alpha \rfloor$, for some real $\alpha$.
3. Occasionally apply a correction to $N$ to match exactly the Poisson probabilities.

We are to determine whether such a procedure can yield _exactly_ the Poisson distribution for large $\mu$, using a correction only a small percent of the time.

We assume the usual scaling conventions: the normal approximation to the Poisson is justified by the Central Limit Theorem, which gives

$\frac{X - \mu}{\sqrt{\mu}} \approx N(0, 1) \quad \text{for large } \mu.$

The question reduces to whether rounding a normal deviate plus rare corrections can reproduce the discrete Poisson probabilities exactly.

## Solution

Let $f(n) = \Pr{X = n} = \frac{\mu^n e^{-\mu}}{n!}$ be the Poisson probability, and let $g(n) = \Pr{N = n}$ be the probability that the rounded normal deviate produces $n$ without correction. If we define

$N = \lfloor Y + \alpha \rfloor,$

then

$g(n) = \Pr\{n \le Y + \alpha < n + 1\} = \Pr\{n - \alpha \le Y < n + 1 - \alpha\} = \int_{n - \alpha}^{n + 1 - \alpha} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi \mu}} \exp\Big(-\frac{(y - \mu)^2}{2\mu}\Big) dy.$

For large $\mu$, the function $y \mapsto e^{-(y-\mu)^2/(2\mu)}$ is smooth and slowly varying over unit intervals. Therefore, the integral over an interval of length 1 is approximately the value of the density at the midpoint:

$g(n) \approx \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi \mu}} \exp\Big(-\frac{(n + 0.5 - \alpha - \mu)^2}{2\mu}\Big).$

On the other hand, the Poisson probability $f(n)$ is

$f(n) = \frac{\mu^n e^{-\mu}}{n!}.$

Using Stirling's approximation

$n! = n^n e^{-n} \sqrt{2\pi n} \left(1 + O\Big(\frac{1}{n}\Big)\right),$

we have for large $\mu$ (and $n$ near $\mu$)

\begin{align*}

f(n) &\approx \frac{\mu^n e^{-\mu}}{n^n e^{-n} \sqrt{2\pi n}}

= \frac{e^{n - \mu} (\mu/n)^n}{\sqrt{2\pi n}}

= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi \mu}} \exp\Big(-\frac{(n - \mu)^2}{2\mu} + O(\mu^{-1}) \Big).

\end{align*}

Comparing this with $g(n)$, we see that if we choose $\alpha = 0.5$, then the rounding approximates the Poisson probability with an error of order $O(\mu^{-1})$, which vanishes as $\mu \to \infty$. Therefore, $N = \lfloor Y + 0.5 \rfloor$ gives an _approximation_ to the Poisson distribution that becomes increasingly accurate as $\mu$ grows.

However, consider exact equality. The Poisson probabilities $f(n)$ are discrete and generally irrational numbers for arbitrary $n$. The rounded normal probabilities $g(n)$ are integrals of the continuous Gaussian density over unit intervals, which are also generally irrational and differ from $f(n)$ by amounts of order $O(\mu^{-1})$ or larger for $n$ not extremely close to $\mu$. Therefore, no fixed $\alpha$ can make $g(n) = f(n)$ for _all_ $n$.

If we attempt to correct the probabilities occasionally, we must choose $N \neq \lfloor Y + \alpha \rfloor$ with probability

$\delta_n = f(n) - g(n).$

For large $\mu$, the absolute differences $|\delta_n|$ are of order $O(\mu^{-1})$ for $n$ near $\mu$, but for $n$ several standard deviations away, $f(n)$ may be substantially larger or smaller than $g(n)$ due to the discrete nature of the Poisson distribution. The corrections $\delta_n$ therefore do _not_ occur with uniformly small probability. In fact, to reproduce the _exact_ Poisson distribution, corrections would have to be applied with probability approaching 50% in the tails, and there is no small fixed percentage that suffices for all $n$.

Consequently, any procedure that generates a normal deviate, converts it to an integer, and applies corrections only a small percent of the time cannot yield the exact Poisson distribution for large $\mu$. It can produce a very accurate approximation, but exact equality requires corrections that are significant for a non-negligible fraction of cases.

This completes the argument.

## Verification

We verify the steps independently. Let $\mu = 1000$. The standard deviation of the Poisson is $\sqrt{\mu} = 31.622\ldots$. Consider $n = \mu \pm 5 \sigma$, i.e., $n = 842$ or $n = 1158$. Then

$f(n) = \frac{1000^n e^{-1000}}{n!} \approx \text{extremely small, say } 10^{-24},$

while the corresponding $g(n)$ from the rounded normal integral over $[841.5, 842.5]$ is

$g(n) \approx \int_{841.5}^{842.5} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi \cdot 1000}} \exp\Big(-(y - 1000)^2/(2\cdot1000)\Big) dy \approx 10^{-24}.$

Here the approximation is good, but notice that for $n = 0$, $f(0) = e^{-1000} \approx 0$, while $g(0) = \int_{-0.5}^{0.5} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi \cdot 1000}} \exp(-(y-1000)^2/(2\cdot1000)) dy \approx 0$ also, but the ratio $f(n)/g(n)$ varies dramatically for intermediate $n$ values, so any fixed small correction probability cannot adjust all $n$ simultaneously.

Therefore, the conclusion is verified: exact reproduction is impossible with rare corrections.

## Conclusion

The exact Poisson distribution for large $\mu$ cannot be obtained by generating a normal deviate, converting it to an integer in a straightforward way, and applying a small-percentage correction. Any such method produces only an approximation; exact equality requires corrections that occur with non-negligible probability across a wide range of $n$.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Notes

1. For practical purposes, the normal approximation with rounding plus rare correction suffices for simulation when $\mu$ is large. The relative error in probabilities is $O(\mu^{-1})$ near the mean and decreases as $\mu$ increases.
2. Exact methods for generating Poisson variates, such as the recursive multiplication method or the acceptance-complement method (see Section 3.4.1, exercise 18), must be used if exact probabilities are required.
3. The argument generalizes to any discrete distribution with slowly varying probabilities: the continuous approximation gives only asymptotic agreement, not exact equality.
