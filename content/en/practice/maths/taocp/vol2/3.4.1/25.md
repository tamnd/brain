[Section 3.4.1: Numerical Distributions](../)

**Exercise 25.** [*M35*] Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_t$ be binary words each of whose bits is independently 0 or 1 with probability $\frac{1}{2}$. What is the probability that a given bit position of $X_1 \mid (X_2 \mathbin{\&} (X_3 \mid (X_4 \mathbin{\&} X_5)))$ contains a 1? Generalize.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m58s  

---

## Setup

Let

$$
E_t=X_1\mid\bigl(X_2\mathbin{\&}(X_3\mid(X_4\mathbin{\&}X_5)\cdots )\bigr)
$$

denote the given bitwise expression. Since all bit positions are independent and identically distributed, it suffices to study a single fixed bit position.

Let $p_t$ be the probability that this bit equals $1$ in the value of the alternating expression beginning with $X_1$ and ending with $X_t$. Thus

$$
p_1=\Pr\{X_1=1\}=\frac12.
$$

We seek $p_5$ for

$$
X_1\mid\bigl(X_2\mathbin{\&}(X_3\mid(X_4\mathbin{\&}X_5))\bigr),
$$

and then a general formula for $p_t$.

For independent binary variables $A,B$ with

$$
\Pr\{A=1\}=a,\qquad \Pr\{B=1\}=b,
$$

the bitwise operations satisfy

$$
\Pr\{A\mid B=1\}=a+b-ab,
\tag{1}
$$

because $A\mid B=0$ exactly when $A=B=0$, and

$$
\Pr\{A\mathbin{\&}B=1\}=ab.
\tag{2}
$$

## Solution

Define recursively, working from the innermost term outward,

$$
q_5=\Pr\{X_5=1\}=\frac12.
$$

Since $X_4$ is independent of $X_5$,

$$
q_4=\Pr\{X_4\mathbin{\&}X_5=1\}
     =\frac12\,q_5
     =\frac14.
$$

Next,

$$
q_3=\Pr\{X_3\mid(X_4\mathbin{\&}X_5)=1\}
     =\frac12+q_4-\frac12 q_4
     =\frac12+\frac18
     =\frac58.
$$

Then

$$
q_2=\Pr\{X_2\mathbin{\&}(X_3\mid(X_4\mathbin{\&}X_5))=1\}
     =\frac12\,q_3
     =\frac{5}{16}.
$$

Finally,

$$
p_5=\Pr\{X_1\mid(X_2\mathbin{\&}(X_3\mid(X_4\mathbin{\&}X_5)))=1\}
    =\frac12+q_2-\frac12 q_2
    =\frac12+\frac{5}{32}
    =\frac{21}{32}.
$$

Hence the required probability is

$$
\boxed{\frac{21}{32}}.
$$

For the general case, let $p_t$ denote the probability that the alternating expression ending with $X_t$ equals $1$.

Introduce

$$
r_t=1-p_t.
$$

If the outermost operation is $\mid$, formula (1) gives

$$
p_t=\frac12+\frac12 p_{t-1},
$$

hence

$$
r_t=\frac12 r_{t-1}.
\tag{3}
$$

If the outermost operation is $\mathbin{&}$, formula (2) gives

$$
p_t=\frac12 p_{t-1},
$$

hence

$$
r_t=1-\frac12p_{t-1}
    =\frac12+\frac12r_{t-1}.
\tag{4}
$$

For the alternating expression beginning with $X_1$, equations (3) and (4) combine over two successive levels:

$$
r_t=\frac12\!\left(\frac12+\frac12 r_{t-2}\right)
    =\frac14+\frac14 r_{t-2}.
\tag{5}
$$

Let

$$
s_n=r_{2n+1}.
$$

Then (5) becomes

$$
s_n=\frac14+\frac14 s_{n-1},
\qquad
s_0=r_1=\frac12.
$$

The fixed point satisfies

$$
s=\frac14+\frac14 s,
$$

so

$$
s=\frac13.
$$

Therefore

$$
s_n-\frac13
=\frac14\left(s_{n-1}-\frac13\right),
$$

and hence

$$
s_n
=\frac13+\left(\frac12-\frac13\right)\!\left(\frac14\right)^n
=\frac13+\frac16\left(\frac14\right)^n.
$$

Thus

$$
p_{2n+1}
=1-s_n
=\frac23-\frac16\left(\frac14\right)^n.
\tag{6}
$$

For even length,

$$
p_{2n}
=\frac12\,p_{2n-1}
=\frac13-\frac1{12}\left(\frac14\right)^{n-1}.
\tag{7}
$$

Equations (6) and (7) give the probability that a fixed bit position is $1$ in the alternating expression

$$
X_1\mid(X_2\mathbin{\&}(X_3\mid\cdots)).
$$

For $n=2$, equation (6) yields

$$
p_5
=\frac23-\frac16\left(\frac14\right)^2
=\frac23-\frac1{96}
=\frac{21}{32},
$$

agreeing with the direct computation.

## Verification

The direct calculation gives

$$
\frac12
\;\xrightarrow{\mathbin{\&}\,\frac12}\;
\frac14
\;\xrightarrow{\mid\,\frac12}\;
\frac58
\;\xrightarrow{\mathbin{\&}\,\frac12}\;
\frac5{16}
\;\xrightarrow{\mid\,\frac12}\;
\frac{21}{32}.
$$

The closed formula gives

$$
p_5=\frac23-\frac1{96}=\frac{63}{96}-\frac1{96}
=\frac{62}{96}
=\frac{21}{32}.
$$

The two computations coincide.

## Notes

As the length of the alternating expression tends to infinity,

$$
p_{2n+1}\to\frac23,
\qquad
p_{2n}\to\frac13.
$$

Thus the probability oscillates between limits $\frac23$ and $\frac13$ according to the parity of the number of variables.
