## Solution

We are asked to generate a random integer $N$ with distribution

$\Pr\{N = n\} = n p^2 (1-p)^{n-1}, \qquad n \ge 0, \; 0 < p \le 1.$

We first verify that this is a proper probability distribution. The sum over all $n$ is

$$
\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} n p^2 (1-p)^{n-1} = p^2 \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n (1-p)^{n-1}.
$$

Using the standard series formula

$$
\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n q^{n-1} = \frac{1}{(1-q)^2}, \quad |q|<1,
$$

we set $q = 1-p$, giving

$$
\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n (1-p)^{n-1} = \frac{1}{p^2}.
$$

Thus the total probability is

$$
p^2 \cdot \frac{1}{p^2} = 1,
$$

confirming that the distribution is correctly normalized.

Next, we express the distribution in a form suitable for simulation. Observe that

$$
\Pr\{N = n\} = n p^2 (1-p)^{n-1} = p \cdot \underbrace{p (1-p)^{n-1}}_{\text{geometric term}} \cdot n.
$$

Let $G$ be a geometric random variable with

$$
\Pr\{G = n\} = p (1-p)^{n-1}, \quad n \ge 1.
$$

Then

$$
\Pr\{N = n\} = p \cdot n \cdot \Pr\{G = n\}.
$$

We recognize this as a _size-biased_ version of a geometric distribution: $N$ equals $n$ with probability proportional to $n$ times the geometric probability.
