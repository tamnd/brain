**Exercise 15.** [*HM21*] If $X_1$ and $X_2$ are independent random variables with the respective distributions $F_1(x)$ and $F_2(x)$, and with densities $f_1(x) = F_1'(x)$, $f_2(x) = F_2'(x)$, what are the distribution and density functions of the quantity $X_1 + X_2$?

## Solution

Let $X_1$ and $X_2$ be independent random variables with distribution functions $F_1(x)$ and $F_2(x)$, and densities $f_1(x) = F_1'(x)$ and $f_2(x) = F_2'(x)$. We seek the distribution function $F_{X_1 + X_2}(z)$ and the corresponding density $f_{X_1 + X_2}(z)$ for the sum $X_1 + X_2$.

By definition, the distribution function of $X_1 + X_2$ is

$$
F_{X_1 + X_2}(z) = \Pr\{ X_1 + X_2 \le z \} = \iint_{x_1 + x_2 \le z} f_1(x_1) f_2(x_2) \, dx_1 dx_2,
$$

where the integrals extend over the entire real line for $x_1$ and $x_2$. Since $X_1$ and $X_2$ are independent, the joint density factorizes as $f_1(x_1) f_2(x_2)$. We can evaluate the double integral by first integrating over $x_2$ for a fixed $x_1$:

$$
F_{X_1 + X_2}(z) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_1(x_1) \left( \int_{-\infty}^{z - x_1} f_2(x_2) \, dx_2 \right) dx_1.
$$

The inner integral is precisely $F_2(z - x_1)$, so we obtain

$$
F_{X_1 + X_2}(z) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_1(x_1) F_2(z - x_1) \, dx_1. \eqno(1)
$$

This formula gives the distribution function of the sum in terms of the individual density and distribution functions.

To obtain the density function, we differentiate (1) with respect to $z$. The Leibniz integral rule applies because $f_1$ and $f_2$ are continuous. Differentiating under the integral sign yields

$$
f_{X_1 + X_2}(z) = \frac{d}{dz} F_{X_1 + X_2}(z) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_1(x_1) \frac{d}{dz} F_2(z - x_1) \, dx_1.
$$

Since $F_2'(x) = f_2(x)$, we have $\frac{d}{dz} F_2(z - x_1) = f_2(z - x_1)$. Therefore,

$$
f_{X_1 + X_2}(z) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_1(x_1) f_2(z - x_1) \, dx_1. \eqno(2)
$$

Equation (2) is the **convolution formula** for the sum of two independent continuous random variables. It expresses the density of the sum as the integral over all possible decompositions $z = x_1 + x_2$ weighted by the corresponding densities of $X_1$ and $X_2$.

Both formulas are valid for all $z \in \mathbb{R}$. They reduce to simpler expressions in special cases. For example, if $X_1$ and $X_2$ are uniform on $[0,1]$, then

$$
f_{X_1 + X_2}(z) = \int_0^1 1 \cdot f_2(z - x_1) \, dx_1 = \int_0^1 f_2(z - x_1) \, dx_1,
$$

which reproduces the triangular distribution as expected.

This completes the proof.
