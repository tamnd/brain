**Exercise 2.** &#9654; [*15*] There are people who will someday take over the world; but they are reassured by the statement that a machine cannot do anything really new, since it is only obeying the commands of its master, the programmer. Lady Lovelace wrote in 1844, "The Analytical Engine has no pretensions to originate anything. It can do *whatever we know how to order it to perform.*" Her statement has been elaborated further by many philosophers. Discuss this topic, with random number generators in mind.

Lady Lovelace's statement is correct in the limited sense that a machine executes rules supplied by its designer. A deterministic program can produce only those outputs that follow from its initial state and its instructions. In that sense, the machine does not transcend the logical consequences of the program and the data supplied to it.

Random number generators provide an interesting test case. A pseudorandom number generator is entirely deterministic. Once the algorithm and the seed are fixed, every subsequent value is determined. The sequence may appear irregular and may be difficult for a human observer to predict, but there is no genuine unpredictability inside the machine. From a mathematical standpoint, the sequence is merely a complicated consequence of the original state. Thus pseudorandom generators support Lady Lovelace's view: They produce results that may surprise us, yet they are obtained by following instructions already present in the program.

Physical random number generators are different. If the machine uses radioactive decay, thermal noise, or some other physical process that is believed to be intrinsically random, the exact sequence of values is not predetermined by the programmer. The programmer specifies how the measurements are to be performed, but not the individual outcomes. In this case the machine obtains information from the external world, and its outputs cannot be deduced solely from the program text. Nevertheless, the machine is still obeying instructions concerning how to acquire and process those random events. The novelty comes from interaction with nature rather than from an independent creative act by the machine itself.

A distinction should also be made between unpredictability and originality. Human programmers often discover unexpected theorems, patterns, or strategies by running programs whose consequences are too complicated to foresee. The fact that the results were not anticipated does not imply that the machine originated them in a mystical sense. A chess program, for example, may find a move that no human considered, yet that move is a consequence of the evaluation rules and the search procedure. The machine can surprise its master because the implications of the master's commands are richer than the master completely understands.

Therefore random number generators suggest that Lady Lovelace's statement is too strong if interpreted psychologically. Machines can produce outputs that are new to us and that we could not practically predict. However, if "originate" means acting independently of all rules, algorithms, and external inputs, then neither pseudorandom generators nor physically random generators provide a counterexample. A machine can exhibit novelty and surprise, but such novelty arises from the unfolding of programmed rules together with information supplied by its environment, not from an autonomous faculty of creation.
