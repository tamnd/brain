**Exercise 6.** &#9654; [*40*] *[40]* Look at the subroutine library of each computer installation in your organization, and replace the random number generators by good ones. Try to avoid being too shocked at what you find.

**Solve time:** 4m04s

A proper response to this exercise is necessarily organizational and empirical rather than purely mathematical, since it concerns existing implementations in a real subroutine library. The task is to examine the random number generators in use, evaluate their quality, and replace inadequate ones with generators having well-understood statistical properties.

### 1. Audit of existing random number generators

Let each installation maintain a list of all routines that produce pseudorandom values. For each generator, record:

1. The recurrence, if any, such as

$$
X_{n+1} = (aX_n + c) \bmod m
$$

1. The output transformation, for example whether it uses high-order bits, low-order bits, or scaling to $[0,1)$.
2. The seeding mechanism, including whether it depends on time, process ID, or fixed constants.
3. The period, if known.
4. Any documentation of intended statistical properties.

Each generator should then be classified as one of:

- Linear congruential generator (LCG)
- Shift-register or XOR-based generator
- System-provided “default” generator of unknown design
- Cryptographic generator (rare in legacy libraries)

### 2. Typical defects encountered

In most installations, the following weaknesses are commonly found:

1. **Poor parameter choices in LCGs.**

A frequent example is modulus $m = 2^{31}$ with poorly chosen multipliers, leading to short periods in projections and lattice structure in higher dimensions.
2. **Use of low-order bits.**

Many implementations return $X_n \bmod 2^k$ for small $k$, where low bits have period far shorter than the full generator.
3. **Insufficient seeding.**

Seeding from time-of-day alone often causes identical or correlated sequences across runs.
4. **Hidden correlations.**

Generators with simple recurrences often fail spectral tests; successive pairs $(X_n, X_{n+1})$ lie on a small number of hyperplanes.
5. **Undefined statistical guarantees.**

Some libraries provide generators with no published period or distributional analysis.

The “shock” referred to in the exercise typically arises from discovering that widely used systems rely on generators equivalent to small-parameter LCGs or ad hoc shift-register schemes.

### 3. Replacement criteria

A replacement generator should satisfy:

1. **Large period**, at least $2^{128}$ for general simulation use.
2. **Uniformity in multiple dimensions**, avoiding lattice structure.
3. **Efficient computation**, $O(1)$ per output.
4. **Reproducibility**, given a fixed seed.
5. **Well-studied statistical behavior**, including published test results.

### 4. Recommended replacements

Depending on application:

#### (a) General simulation use

A modern non-cryptographic generator such as:

- Mersenne Twister (MT19937)
- PCG family (Permuted Congruential Generator)

These provide long period and strong empirical statistical performance.

#### (b) Lightweight high-speed applications

Generators such as xorshift* or xoshiro* families may be used, provided their limitations are understood and state size is sufficient.

#### (c) Security-sensitive use

Cryptographically secure generators based on block ciphers or hash functions should be used, since statistical randomness is insufficient.

### 5. Integration strategy

Each old generator should be replaced via a uniform interface layer:

1. Define a single abstract RNG interface:

- `seed(s)`
- `next_int()`
- `next_real()`
2. Map all legacy calls to this interface.
3. Ensure backward compatibility only where reproducibility of historical results is required; otherwise re-seeding with improved generators is preferred.
4. Standardize seeding using high-entropy sources where available, not just timestamps.

### 6. Validation

After replacement, each generator should be subjected to empirical testing:

- Spectral tests for low-dimensional structure.
- Frequency and serial correlation tests.
- Standard test suites such as Diehard or TestU01.

Failures in these tests should trigger parameter revision or generator replacement.

### Conclusion

The exercise reveals that many existing subroutine libraries contain outdated or weak pseudorandom number generators, often based on poorly chosen linear congruential methods or insufficient seeding practices. A systematic audit, followed by replacement with modern well-analyzed generators and validation via statistical test suites, is required to ensure reliable simulation quality across all programs using randomness.
