A careful reading of these scripts reveals the highly intense drama present here. The computer program was careful to keep track of the locations of each player, the contents of his hands, etc. Actions taken by the players were random, governed by certain probabilities; the probability of a firefight or a miss depended depending on the positions of the players and other factors that affected the probability of a clean shot. The reader will be able to deduce further properties of the program by studying the sample scripts.

### Solution

This exercise is intentionally open-ended. Its purpose is not to prove a theorem but to extract ideas about how a computer might generate stories and other forms of creative writing. The examples show several mechanisms that are already visible in the generated scripts and letters, and they suggest natural extensions.

The television scripts show that the program maintained an explicit description of the world. Each object and character possessed attributes such as location, possessions, and status. Facts like

- robber at window;
- gun in right hand;
- glass on table;
- sheriff at door;

were part of the current state. Every action modified that state. Thus, after "pick up gun with right hand," the gun is no longer on the table, and after "go to door," the character's location changes. Consistency of this kind explains why the scripts are awkward but usually physically possible.

Action selection was constrained by the current state. Only actions compatible with the circumstances were available. A character could not fire a gun that he did not possess, and movement could only occur between locations that existed in the setting. Among the admissible actions, the choice was made probabilistically. The problem statement explicitly indicates that probabilities depended on such factors as relative positions and the likelihood of obtaining a clear shot. Hence randomness supplied variety, while the state description supplied coherence.

The examples also illustrate how large-scale events can arise from many small actions. A sequence such as

- move to window;
- aim;
- fire;
- opponent wounded;

is interpreted by the audience as a gunfight. Likewise, repeated actions involving money, doors, and whiskey naturally suggest a robbery scene. Thus high-level dramatic situations emerge from combinations of lower-level operations together with suitable probabilities. Human editors then condensed and rearranged the machine's output into the final skits used on television.

Strachey's love letter demonstrates another technique. Instead of manipulating physical objects, the program manipulated words and phrases. Templates such as

> adjective + noun

or

> adverb + verb + object

could be filled by random choices from vocabularies grouped by grammatical category. Because the syntax is controlled, the resulting sentences are understandable even though the specific combinations are unpredictable.

These examples suggest several ways to teach a computer to perform creative writing.

First, one may represent a story world by a collection of facts describing characters, objects, locations, and relationships. A database of such facts serves as the memory of the story.

Second, one may provide a repertoire of actions together with preconditions and consequences. Each action is a rule of the form

> if certain conditions hold, then the action may occur and changes the state in specified ways.

This gives the system the ability to simulate events.

Third, probabilities may be attached to actions. Randomness prevents every story from being identical and provides the Monte Carlo aspect mentioned in the text. Different probability tables can create different styles of stories.

Fourth, one may introduce higher-level plot structures. For example, a western story might contain stages such as

1. introduction;
2. conflict;
3. pursuit;
4. confrontation;
5. resolution.

Within each stage, random actions are selected subject to the current circumstances. Such a hierarchy helps explain how coherent narratives can emerge from stochastic low-level behavior.

Fifth, linguistic generation can be separated from semantic content. Once the underlying events are known, they may be expressed by grammatical templates, phrase-structure rules, or collections of stereotyped sentences. The same sequence of events could therefore be narrated in different styles.

Sixth, a separate editing stage may revise the raw output. The television examples show that human editors transformed lengthy and sometimes absurd machine scripts into short, dramatic scenes. An automated editor could similarly remove repetitions, merge actions into larger events, or choose the most interesting episodes.

Finally, style itself can be controlled statistically. Different vocabularies, sentence patterns, and probability distributions could imitate various genres or authors. Strachey's letter already demonstrates how a simple collection of romantic adjectives and nouns can create prose with a recognizable tone.

Thus the examples in the exercise suggest a general architecture for computer creative writing:

1. Maintain a symbolic model of the world.
2. Define actions with preconditions and effects.
3. Choose among admissible actions probabilistically.
4. Organize actions into larger plot structures.
5. Translate events into language by grammatical rules or templates.
6. Apply an editing stage to improve the result.

The scripts and the love letter show that even relatively simple combinations of state tracking, random choice, and linguistic templates can produce outputs that are recognizable as stories or prose. The exercise's main lesson is that creativity need not arise from mysterious mechanisms. It can emerge from the interaction of representation, rules, probability, and selection.
