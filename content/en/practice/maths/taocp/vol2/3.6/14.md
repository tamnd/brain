**Exercise 14.** &#9654; [*M35*] *[M35]* *(The future versus the past.)* Let $X_n = (X_{n-37} + X_{n-100}) \bmod 2$ and consider the sequences

$$(Y_0, Y_1, \ldots) = (X_0, X_1, \ldots, X_{99}, X_{200}, X_{201}, \ldots, X_{299}, X_{400}, X_{401}, \ldots, X_{499}, X_{600}, \ldots).$$

(Only the local digits at the least significant bits, at the discarding of many elements.) By applying *run_array*(a,200) at the (least significant bits, after discarding half of the elements.) The following experiment was repeated one hundred times using the sequence $\langle Y_n \rangle$: "Generate 100 random bits; then if 60 or more of them were 0, generate one more bit and print it." The result was to print 14527 0s and 13955 1s; but the probability that 28482 random bits contain at most 13955 1s is only about .000358.

Give a mathematical explanation why so many 0s were output.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m54s

## Setup

Let

$$
X_n=(X_{n-37}+X_{n-100})\bmod 2
$$

and define

$$
Y_j=X_{200m+r}
\qquad (j=100m+r,\ 0\le r<100).
$$

Thus the sequence $\langle Y_j\rangle$ consists of blocks of $100$ consecutive $X$'s, followed by the next $100$ values discarded, then another $100$ kept, and so on.

The experiment generates $100$ consecutive bits of $\langle Y_j\rangle$. If at least $60$ of those bits are $0$, one more bit is generated and printed. The observed output contains substantially more $0$s than $1$s. We must explain this bias mathematically.

## Solution

The recurrence is linear over $\mathrm{GF}(2)$. Since

$$
200=2\cdot100,
$$

the subsequence obtained by keeping every other block of length $100$ inherits a simpler recurrence.

Let

$$
Z_m=(X_{200m},X_{200m+1},\ldots,X_{200m+99})
$$

be the $m$th retained block. For $0\le r<100$,

$$
X_{200m+r}
=
X_{200m+r-37}+X_{200m+r-100}.
\tag{1}
$$

Applying the same recurrence repeatedly and using arithmetic modulo $2$, the transformation from one retained block to the next is linear. In particular, the characteristic polynomial of the original recurrence is

$$
f(z)=z^{100}+z^{37}+1.
$$

Over $\mathrm{GF}(2)$,

$$
f(z)^2=z^{200}+z^{74}+1,
$$

because all cross terms disappear. Therefore every coordinate sequence

$$
X_r,\ X_{r+200},\ X_{r+400},\ldots
$$

satisfies

$$
X_{n}=X_{n-74}+X_{n-200}.
\tag{2}
$$

When only the retained blocks are viewed, (2) becomes

$$
Y_j=Y_{j-37}+Y_{j-100}.
\tag{3}
$$

Hence the sequence $\langle Y_j\rangle$ obeys exactly the same lagged-Fibonacci relation as the original least-significant-bit sequence.

Consider a window of $101$ consecutive values

$$
Y_t,Y_{t+1},\ldots,Y_{t+100}.
$$

Equation (3) gives

$$
Y_{t+100}=Y_t+Y_{t+63}.
\tag{4}
$$

Thus the $101$st bit is not independent of the preceding $100$ bits. It is completely determined by two of them.

Let

$$
N_0=\#\{\,0\le i<100: Y_{t+i}=0\,\}
$$

be the number of zeros among the first $100$ bits.

The experiment prints $Y_{t+100}$ only when

$$
N_0\ge 60.
\tag{5}
$$

Condition (5) means that the selected $100$-bit blocks have an unusually large number of zeros. Under this condition, each individual position is more likely to contain $0$ than $1$. In particular,

$$
\Pr(Y_t=0\mid N_0\ge60)
>
\frac12,
$$

and likewise

$$
\Pr(Y_{t+63}=0\mid N_0\ge60)
>
\frac12.
$$

By (4), the printed bit equals

$$
Y_{t+100}=Y_t+Y_{t+63}\pmod2.
$$

A sum modulo $2$ is $0$ precisely when the two summands are equal. Under condition (5), both summands are biased toward $0$, so the events

$$
Y_t=0,\qquad Y_{t+63}=0
$$

occur together more often than they would for independent fair bits. Consequently,

$$
\Pr(Y_{t+100}=0\mid N_0\ge60)
>
\frac12.
$$

Therefore the selection criterion favors windows whose forced successor bit is $0$. The printed bits are not unbiased; they are conditioned on a property of the preceding $100$ bits, and the linear relation (4) transfers that conditioning to the next bit.

This explains why the experiment produced substantially more $0$s than $1$s.

## Verification

Equation (3) implies

$$
Y_{100}=Y_0+Y_{63}.
$$

Among all $100$-bit blocks having at least $60$ zeros, the coordinates $Y_0$ and $Y_{63}$ are each more likely to be $0$ than $1$. Therefore the parity

$$
Y_0+Y_{63}
$$

is more likely to be $0$ than $1$. Since the printed bit is exactly this parity, a surplus of printed $0$s is expected.

The observed counts,

$$
14527\text{ zeros},\qquad 13955\text{ ones},
$$

show a bias of

$$
14527-13955=572
$$

toward $0$, which is consistent with the qualitative prediction obtained from the deterministic relation (4).

## Notes

The phenomenon is an instance of the warning stated in Section 3.6(viii): after enough values have been generated, "the future behaves like the past." The test explicitly selects portions of the sequence according to a property of the past $100$ bits, while the recurrence forces the next bit to depend on those same bits. The resulting conditioning creates a systematic bias toward $0$.

The explanation is therefore the linear dependence

$$
\boxed{Y_{n}=Y_{n-37}+Y_{n-100}\pmod2,}
$$

together with the selection condition that favors blocks containing many zeros. ∎
