Let $H(a,b)$ denote the number of iterations of Harris's algorithm. Harris's modification may be stated as follows for odd integers $a>b>0$:

1. Write

$$
a=bq+r,
$$

where $q$ is chosen so that $r$ is even and $|r|<b$.

Equivalently, $q$ is the nearest integer to $a/b$, with the parity condition forcing $r$ to be even. The remainder is therefore a least absolute even remainder.
2. If $r=0$, terminate.
3. If $r\neq0$, write

$$
r=2^t c,
\qquad c\ \text{odd},
$$

and replace $(a,b)$ by $(b,|c|)$. Repeat.

The exercise asks for an analysis of this algorithm.

### Correctness

At each step,

$$
a=bq+r.
$$

Hence

$$
\gcd(a,b)=\gcd(b,r).
$$

Since $r=2^t c$ and $b$ is odd, every common divisor of $b$ and $r$ is also a divisor of $c$, and conversely. Therefore

$$
\gcd(b,r)=\gcd(b,c).
$$

Thus the transformation

$$
(a,b)\longmapsto (b,|c|)
$$

preserves the greatest common divisor:

$$
\gcd(a,b)=\gcd(b,|c|).
$$

The algorithm therefore maintains the invariant that the gcd of the current pair is the same as the gcd of the original pair.

If $r=0$, then $a=bq$, so

$$
\gcd(a,b)=b,
$$

and the algorithm correctly terminates.

### Termination

Because $|r|<b$, we have

$$
|c|=\frac{|r|}{2^t}\le |r|<b.
$$

Thus after one iteration the second component strictly decreases:

$$
0<|c|<b.
$$

The successive second arguments form a strictly decreasing sequence of positive odd integers. Hence only finitely many iterations are possible. Eventually $r=0$, and the algorithm terminates.

### Size reduction

The essential feature of Harris's method is that it combines two ideas:

- Euclid's algorithm uses a remainder $r$.
- Stein's binary algorithm immediately removes all powers of $2$ from that remainder.

Since $r$ is even whenever $r\neq0$, one always divides by at least one factor $2$. Therefore

$$
|c| \le \frac{|r|}{2}.
$$

Because $r$ is chosen as a least absolute remainder,

$$
|r|\le \frac b2.
$$

Consequently

$$
|c|\le \frac b4.
$$

Thus, except for the terminating case $r=0$, each iteration reduces the smaller argument by at least a factor of $4$.

### Complexity

Let $b_k$ denote the smaller argument at the beginning of the $k$-th iteration. From the previous estimate,

$$
b_{k+1}\le \frac{b_k}{4}.
$$

Inductively,

$$
b_k\le \frac{b_0}{4^k}.
$$

The process must stop once $b_k=1$, so

$$
4^k\le b_0,
$$

hence

$$
k\le \log_4 b_0 + O(1).
$$

Therefore

$$
H(a,b)=O(\log b).
$$

In fact, Harris's method requires at most about half as many iterations as the ordinary Euclidean algorithm in the worst case, because each nonterminal step reduces the smaller argument by a factor at least $4$, whereas Euclid guarantees only a factor $2$ reduction in the worst case.

### Conclusion

Harris's algorithm is correct because each transformation preserves the gcd:

$$
\gcd(a,b)=\gcd(b,|c|).
$$

It terminates because the second argument decreases strictly through positive odd integers. The use of a least absolute even remainder followed by removal of all powers of $2$ guarantees

$$
b_{k+1}\le \frac{b_k}{4},
$$

so the number of iterations is

$$
H(a,b)=O(\log b).
$$

Thus Harris's procedure is a hybrid of Euclid's remainder method and Stein's binary reduction, achieving a stronger reduction per iteration than either method alone.
