**Exercise 41.** [*M22*] Use Euclid's algorithm to find a simple formula for $\gcd(10^m - 1, 10^n - 1)$ when $m$ and $n$ are nonnegative integers.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m21s  

---

## Solution

Let $m$ and $n$ be nonnegative integers. We wish to compute

$\gcd(10^m - 1, 10^n - 1).$

Without loss of generality, assume $m \ge n$. Euclid's algorithm gives the identity

$\gcd(a, b) = \gcd(b, a \bmod b).$

We apply this to $a = 10^m - 1$ and $b = 10^n - 1$. Consider the remainder of $10^m - 1$ upon division by $10^n - 1$. Since $10^m - 1$ can be written as

$10^m - 1 = 10^{m-n} \cdot 10^n - 1 = 10^{m-n}(10^n - 1) + (10^{m-n} - 1),$

we have

$(10^m - 1) \bmod (10^n - 1) = 10^{m-n} - 1.$

Hence, by Euclid's algorithm,

$\gcd(10^m - 1, 10^n - 1) = \gcd(10^n - 1, 10^{m-n} - 1).$

Repeating this argument, we subtract multiples of the smaller exponent from the larger. Let $r$ be the remainder of $m$ upon division by $n$, so that $m = qn + r$ with $0 \le r < n$. Then

$10^m - 1 = 10^{qn+r} - 1 = 10^r \cdot (10^{qn} - 1) + (10^r - 1).$

Since $10^n - 1$ divides $10^{qn} - 1$, we again have

$(10^m - 1) \bmod (10^n - 1) = 10^r - 1.$

Thus, the gcd reduces to

$\gcd(10^m - 1, 10^n - 1) = \gcd(10^n - 1, 10^r - 1), \quad r = m \bmod n.$

Iterating this procedure according to Euclid's algorithm, we eventually reduce to the case where one exponent is zero, giving $10^0 - 1 = 0$. Therefore, the algorithm terminates with

$\gcd(10^m - 1, 10^n - 1) = 10^{\gcd(m, n)} - 1.$

This formula is valid even if $m$ or $n$ is zero, because $10^0 - 1 = 0$ and $\gcd(m, 0) = m$, consistent with the general definition of gcd in equation (1).

Hence the desired simple formula is

$\boxed{\gcd(10^m - 1, 10^n - 1) = 10^{\gcd(m, n)} - 1}.$

This completes the proof.
