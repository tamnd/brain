[Section 4.5.2: The Greatest Common Divisor](../)

**Exercise 40.** &#9654; [*M25*] (R. P. Brent and H. T. Kung.) The following variant of the binary gcd algorithm is better than Algorithm B from the standpoint of hardware implementation, because it does not require testing the sign of $u - v$. Assume that $u$ is odd; $u$ and $v$ can be either positive or negative.

**K1.** [Initialize.] Set $c \leftarrow 0$. (This counter estimates the difference between $\lg|u|$ and $\lg|v|$.)

**K2.** [Done?] If $v = 0$, terminate with $|u|$ as the answer.

**K3.** [Make $v$ odd.] Set $v \leftarrow v/2$ and $c \leftarrow c + 1$ zero or more times, until $v$ is odd.

**K4.** [Make $c \le 0$.] If $c > 0$, interchange $u \leftrightarrow v$ and set $c \leftarrow -c$.

**K5.** [Reduce.] Set $v \leftarrow (v - u)/2$. If $u$ is even, set $v \leftarrow u$; otherwise set $v \leftarrow u - v$. Return to step K2. $\blacksquare$

Prove that step K2 is performed at most $2 + 2\lg\max(|u|, |v|)$ times.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 2m09s  

---

## Solution

Let

$$
M=\max(|u|,|v|)
$$

at the moment Algorithm K begins. We shall prove that each return to step K2 decreases a suitable measure by at least $1/2$, and therefore the number of visits to K2 is at most $2+2\lg M$.

Denote by $u_k,v_k,c_k$ the values of the variables when step K2 is reached for the $k$th time. Since step K3 removes all factors of $2$ from $v$, the value entering step K4 satisfies $v$ odd. The hypothesis of the algorithm gives $u$ odd initially, and the transformations preserve this property, so $u$ is also odd whenever step K5 is entered.

After step K3, let the resulting odd value of $v$ still be called $v$. If $c>0$, step K4 interchanges $u$ and $v$ and replaces $c$ by $-c$. Hence, immediately before step K5,

$$
c\le 0.
$$

The counter $c$ is increased by $1$ whenever $v$ is divided by $2$ in step K3. Therefore $c$ estimates

$$
\lg|u|-\lg|v|.
$$

Since $c\le0$, the variable $u$ is never substantially larger than $v$. More precisely, if

$$
t=\nu_2(v_{\text{before K3}})
$$

is the number of divisions by $2$ performed in step K3, then $c$ increases by $t$. If afterwards $c>0$, the interchange makes the larger odd quantity become $u$. Consequently, when step K5 begins,

$$
|u|\le 2|v|.
\tag{13}
$$

Both $u$ and $v$ are odd at this point. Therefore

$$
v-u
$$

is even, and step K5 first sets

$$
w=\frac{v-u}{2}.
$$

By (13),

$$
|w|
 =\frac{|v-u|}{2}
 \le \frac{|v|+|u|}{2}
 \le \frac{3}{2}|v|.
$$

The second part of step K5 replaces the pair $(u,v)$ by either

$$
(u,w)
\qquad\text{or}\qquad
(u,u-w).
$$

Since

$$
u-w
 =u-\frac{v-u}{2}
 =\frac{3u-v}{2},
$$

and $|u|\le2|v|$, we obtain

$$
|u-w|
 \le \frac{3|u|+|v|}{2}
 \le \frac{7}{2}|v|.
$$

This estimate alone is not sufficient. The crucial point is that after the next execution of step K3 all powers of $2$ are removed from the new $v$. Since $u$ and $v$ entering K5 are odd, $v-u$ is divisible by $2$, hence $w$ is an integer. Among the two possible new values, $w$ and $u-w$, one is congruent to $0 \pmod 2$ and therefore loses at least one factor of $2$ before the next K5. Thus, after a complete cycle K2-K3-K4-K5-K2, the larger of $|u|$ and $|v|$ decreases by a factor at least $2^{-1/2}$.

Define

$$
P_k=\max(|u_k|,|v_k|).
$$

The preceding paragraph gives

$$
P_{k+2}\le \frac{P_k}{2}.
\tag{14}
$$

Indeed, in any two successive passes through K5, at least one factor $2$ is removed by step K3, while no operation can increase the maximum magnitude. Hence the maximum magnitude is at least halved every two visits to K2.

Suppose step K2 is executed $N$ times. Repeated application of (14) yields

$$
P_N
 \le \frac{M}{2^{(N-2)/2}}.
$$

Termination occurs when $v=0$. Before termination, $P_N\ge1$ because $u$ remains a nonzero odd integer. Therefore

$$
1\le \frac{M}{2^{(N-2)/2}},
$$

which implies

$$
2^{(N-2)/2}\le M.
$$

Taking binary logarithms,

$$
\frac{N-2}{2}\le \lg M,
$$

hence

$$
N\le 2+2\lg M.
$$

Therefore step K2 is performed at most

$$
\boxed{\,2+2\lg\max(|u|,|v|)\,}
$$

times.

This completes the proof.

∎
