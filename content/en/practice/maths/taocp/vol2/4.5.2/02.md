[Section 4.5.2: The Greatest Common Divisor](../)

**Exercise 2.** [*M22*] Given that $u$ divides $v_1 v_2 \ldots v_n$, prove that $u$ divides

$$\gcd(u, v_1)\, \gcd(u, v_2) \ldots \gcd(u, v_n).$$

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 4m20s  

---

## Solution

Assume first that $u=0$. Then $u$ divides $v_1v_2\cdots v_n$ trivially only when $v_1v_2\cdots v_n=0$, and by (1),

$$
\gcd(0,v_i)=|v_i|.
$$

Hence

$$
\gcd(0,v_1)\cdots\gcd(0,v_n)
=
|v_1\cdots v_n|
=
0,
$$

so $0$ divides the product. Thus the result holds when $u=0$.

Now assume $u>0$. Write the canonical factorization of $u$ as in (5):

$$
u=\prod_p p^{u_p}.
$$

For each $i$ let

$$
v_i=\pm\prod_p p^{v_{i,p}},
$$

where $v_{i,p}\ge0$.

Since $u$ divides $v_1v_2\cdots v_n$, the exponent of each prime $p$ in the product $v_1v_2\cdots v_n$ is at least $u_p$. Hence

$$
\sum_{i=1}^n v_{i,p}\ge u_p.
\tag{1}
$$

By (6),

$$
\gcd(u,v_i)
=
\prod_p p^{\min(u_p,v_{i,p})}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\prod_{i=1}^n \gcd(u,v_i)
=
\prod_p p^{\,\sum_{i=1}^n \min(u_p,v_{i,p})}.
\tag{2}
$$

To prove that $u$ divides this product, it suffices to show that for every prime $p$,

$$
\sum_{i=1}^n \min(u_p,v_{i,p})\ge u_p.
\tag{3}
$$

Fix a prime $p$.

If some $v_{i,p}\ge u_p$, then

$$
\min(u_p,v_{i,p})=u_p,
$$

and the left side of (3) is at least $u_p$.

Otherwise $v_{i,p}<u_p$ for every $i$. In that case,

$$
\min(u_p,v_{i,p})=v_{i,p},
$$

so by (1),

$$
\sum_{i=1}^n \min(u_p,v_{i,p})
=
\sum_{i=1}^n v_{i,p}
\ge u_p.
$$

Thus (3) holds in all cases.

Equation (2) now shows that the exponent of every prime $p$ in

$$
\prod_{i=1}^n \gcd(u,v_i)
$$

is at least $u_p$, the corresponding exponent in $u$. Consequently,

$$
u \mid \gcd(u,v_1)\,\gcd(u,v_2)\cdots\gcd(u,v_n).
$$

This completes the proof.

∎
