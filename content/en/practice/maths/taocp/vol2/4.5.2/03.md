**Exercise 3.** [*M23*] Show that the number of ordered pairs of positive integers $(u, v)$ such that $\text{lcm}(u, v) = n$ is the number of divisors of $n^2$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m56s  

---

## Solution

Let

$$
n = \prod_{p} p^{a_p}
$$

be the canonical prime factorization of $n$, where each $a_p \ge 0$ and all but finitely many $a_p$ are zero.

Let $(u,v)$ be an ordered pair of positive integers with

$$
\operatorname{lcm}(u,v)=n.
$$

Write

$$
u=\prod_p p^{i_p}, \quad v=\prod_p p^{j_p},
$$

with $i_p, j_p \ge 0$. From the definition of least common multiple,

$$
\operatorname{lcm}(u,v)=\prod_p p^{\max(i_p,j_p)}.
$$

Hence the condition $\operatorname{lcm}(u,v)=n$ is equivalent to the system of constraints

$$
\max(i_p,j_p)=a_p \quad \text{for every prime } p.
$$

For a fixed prime $p$, consider the number of pairs $(i_p,j_p)$ of nonnegative integers satisfying $0 \le i_p \le a_p$, $0 \le j_p \le a_p$, and $\max(i_p,j_p)=a_p$. The total number of pairs with $0 \le i_p,j_p \le a_p$ is $(a_p+1)^2$. The pairs with $\max(i_p,j_p) \le a_p-1$ are exactly those with $0 \le i_p,j_p \le a_p-1$, and their number is $a_p^2$. Therefore the number of pairs with $\max(i_p,j_p)=a_p$ is

$$
(a_p+1)^2 - a_p^2 = 2a_p+1.
$$

Since the constraints for distinct primes are independent, the total number of ordered pairs $(u,v)$ is

$$
\prod_p (2a_p+1).
$$

Now consider $n^2$. Its prime factorization is

$$
n^2 = \prod_p p^{2a_p}.
$$

The number of positive divisors of $n^2$ equals

$$
\prod_p (2a_p+1),
$$

since each exponent $2a_p$ contributes $(2a_p+1)$ choices for the exponent of $p$ in a divisor.

The two expressions coincide, so the number of ordered pairs $(u,v)$ with $\operatorname{lcm}(u,v)=n$ equals the number of positive divisors of $n^2$.

$$
\boxed{\text{number of such ordered pairs } = d(n^2)}
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
