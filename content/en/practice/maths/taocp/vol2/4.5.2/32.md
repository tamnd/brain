[Section 4.5.2: The Greatest Common Divisor](../)

**Exercise 32.** [*M42*] *[M42]* Is there a unique continuous function $G(x)$ that satisfies (36) and (37)?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 2m30s  

---

## Setup

Let $G$ be a continuous function on $[0,1]$ satisfying equations (36) and (37). In the notation of the section, equation (36) is

$$
G(x)=\sum_{k\ge1}2^{-k}
\left(
G\!\left(\frac{x}{x+2^k}\right)
-
G\!\left(\frac1{1+2^k x}\right)
\right),
\qquad 0\le x\le1,
$$

and equation (37) gives the boundary conditions

$$
G(0)=1,\qquad G(1)=0.
$$

The problem asks whether there is a unique continuous function satisfying these conditions.

To prove uniqueness, assume that $G_1$ and $G_2$ are two continuous solutions. Define

$$
H(x)=G_1(x)-G_2(x).
$$

Then $H$ is continuous, satisfies

$$
H(0)=H(1)=0,
$$

and, by subtraction of the two copies of (36),

$$
H(x)=\sum_{k\ge1}2^{-k}
\left(
H\!\left(\frac{x}{x+2^k}\right)
-
H\!\left(\frac1{1+2^k x}\right)
\right).
\eqno(*)
$$

It suffices to prove that $H\equiv0$.

## Solution

Let

$$
M=\max_{0\le x\le1}|H(x)|.
$$

Assume that $M>0$. Since $H$ is continuous on the compact interval $[0,1]$, there exists $x_0\in[0,1]$ such that $|H(x_0)|=M$.

Replacing $H$ by $-H$ if necessary, we may assume

$$
H(x_0)=M.
$$

From $(*)$,

$$
M
=
\sum_{k\ge1}2^{-k}
\left(
H\!\left(\frac{x_0}{x_0+2^k}\right)
-
H\!\left(\frac1{1+2^k x_0}\right)
\right).
$$

For every $k$,

$$
H\!\left(\frac{x_0}{x_0+2^k}\right)\le M,
\qquad
-H\!\left(\frac1{1+2^k x_0}\right)\le M,
$$

hence

$$
H\!\left(\frac{x_0}{x_0+2^k}\right)
-
H\!\left(\frac1{1+2^k x_0}\right)
\le 2M .
$$

The coefficients $2^{-k}$ are positive and satisfy

$$
\sum_{k\ge1}2^{-k}=1.
$$

Since the weighted average equals $M$, every term contributing to it must attain its maximal possible value. Therefore, for every $k\ge1$,

$$
H\!\left(\frac{x_0}{x_0+2^k}\right)=M,
\qquad
H\!\left(\frac1{1+2^k x_0}\right)=0.
\tag{1}
$$

Choose $k=1$. Then

$$
x_1=\frac{x_0}{x_0+2}
$$

satisfies $0\le x_1<x_0$ whenever $x_0>0$, and by (1),

$$
H(x_1)=M.
$$

Applying the same argument at $x_1$ yields

$$
x_2=\frac{x_1}{x_1+2},
\qquad
H(x_2)=M.
$$

Continuing inductively,

$$
x_{n+1}=\frac{x_n}{x_n+2},
\qquad
H(x_n)=M
\quad(n\ge0).
$$

Since

$$
0\le x_{n+1}\le \frac{x_n}{2},
$$

we have $x_n\to0$.

By continuity of $H$,

$$
H(0)=\lim_{n\to\infty}H(x_n)=M.
$$

But $H(0)=0$. Hence $M=0$, contradicting the assumption $M>0$.

Therefore

$$
\max_{0\le x\le1}|H(x)|=0,
$$

so $H(x)\equiv0$ on $[0,1]$. Consequently,

$$
G_1(x)=G_2(x)
\qquad(0\le x\le1).
$$

Thus there is at most one continuous solution of (36) and (37).

Existence was the issue underlying Brent's heuristic analysis. The uniqueness argument above shows that if a continuous solution exists, it is uniquely determined by (36) and (37). Hence the answer to the exercise is affirmative.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification

Let $H$ be the difference of two continuous solutions.

Equation (36) is linear, therefore $H$ satisfies the homogeneous fixed-point equation $(*)$.

If $M=\max|H|>0$, a point $x_0$ with $H(x_0)=M$ exists by continuity and compactness.

The positivity of the weights $2^{-k}$ forces equality in every summand, yielding

$$
H\!\left(\frac{x_0}{x_0+2^k}\right)=M.
$$

Iterating the map $x\mapsto x/(x+2)$ produces a sequence tending to $0$ on which $H$ is constantly $M$.

Continuity then gives $H(0)=M$, contradicting $H(0)=0$.

Therefore $M=0$, so $H\equiv0$.

## Notes

The argument proves a stronger statement: the homogeneous form of equation (36) with boundary conditions

$$
H(0)=H(1)=0
$$

has only the trivial continuous solution. Hence the fixed-point equation determines at most one continuous distribution function. This is precisely the uniqueness question raised in Exercise 32.
