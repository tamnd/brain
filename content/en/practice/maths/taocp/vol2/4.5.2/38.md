## Setup

Exercise 4.5.2.38 asks to modify Algorithm B, the subtractive Euclidean algorithm, for large numbers using ideas analogous to Algorithm L. Algorithm B performs the Euclidean algorithm by repeated subtractions and shifts: given positive integers $u$ and $v$, it repeatedly subtracts the smaller from the larger and reduces by powers of two when possible. Algorithm L improves the efficiency of Euclidean computations on large numbers by performing _binary long division_ or _multi-bit shifts_ instead of single-bit or single-subtraction steps, effectively compressing multiple iterations of Algorithm B into fewer steps.

Let $u$ and $v$ be positive integers with $\ell = \lfloor \lg u \rfloor$ and $m = \lfloor \lg v \rfloor$, and assume $u \ge v > 0$. The goal is to construct a modified version of Algorithm B, call it **Algorithm B'**, which replaces sequences of repeated subtractions by operations that reduce $u$ by a multiple of $v$ or by a power-of-two multiple, analogous to the shift-and-subtract technique in Algorithm L. The modification should correctly compute $\gcd(u, v)$ while handling large integers efficiently.

## Solution

The original Algorithm B consists of repeated applications of:

1. Compute $r \leftarrow u - v$ if $u \ge v$, else $r \leftarrow v - u$.
2. Replace $(u, v)$ with $(\max(u, v), r)$.
3. Repeat until $v = 0$, returning $u$.

This is inefficient for large $u$ and $v$ because each subtraction reduces the larger number by only a single smaller number. Algorithm L addresses this by using _shifted multiples_ of the smaller number to accelerate convergence.

We construct **Algorithm B'** as follows:

**Algorithm B' (Modified for large numbers).** Given $u \ge v > 0$, compute $\gcd(u, v)$.

**B'1.** Determine $\ell = \lfloor \lg u \rfloor$, $m = \lfloor \lg v \rfloor$. If $u = v$, terminate with $u$ as the gcd.

**B'2.** Compute the largest integer $k = \ell - m \ge 0$ such that $v \cdot 2^k \le u$.

**B'3.** Replace $u \leftarrow u - v \cdot 2^k$. Count this as a single subtraction step (or "compressed subtraction").

**B'4.** If $u = 0$, terminate with $v$ as the gcd. Otherwise, swap $u \leftrightarrow v$ if $u < v$.

**B'5.** Return to **B'1**.

The key idea is that, instead of subtracting $v$ from $u$ repeatedly, we compute the largest power-of-two multiple of $v$ that does not exceed $u$ and subtract it in one step. This reduces the number of iterations roughly to the number of bits in the smaller integer, rather than its full value. Each step preserves the invariant:

$\gcd(u, v) = \gcd(u - v \cdot 2^k, v).$

This invariant follows because any integer that divides both $u - v \cdot 2^k$ and $v$ also divides $u = (u - v \cdot 2^k) + v \cdot 2^k$, and vice versa.

The shift $k$ can be determined efficiently by computing $\ell - m$ using the binary lengths of $u$ and $v$, analogous to the leading-bit estimate in Algorithm L. Each subtraction is then a single multiple-subtract operation rather than $2^k$ individual subtractions.

### Step Counting

Let $N = \max(u, v)$. In the original Algorithm B, the maximum number of subtractions is bounded by $1 + \lfloor \lg N \rfloor$ per Gosper's argument (exercise 37). In Algorithm B', each "compressed subtraction" reduces the leading bit of the larger number by at least one, so the total number of steps is bounded by the number of bits in the initial larger number:

\text{#steps} \le 1 + \lfloor \lg N \rfloor.

Each step may involve computing a binary shift and one subtraction of a multiple, but this is efficient in terms of large-number arithmetic.

## Verification

Let $d = \gcd(u, v)$. Suppose after B'3 we replace $u \leftarrow u - v \cdot 2^k = u'$. Then $d \mid v$ and $d \mid u$, so $d \mid u'$. Similarly, if $d \mid u'$ and $d \mid v$, then $d \mid u = u' + v \cdot 2^k$. Therefore the invariant $\gcd(u, v) = \gcd(u', v)$ holds.

Termination occurs when $u = 0$ or $u = v$, in which case the returned value is clearly $\gcd(u, v)$. The replacement of repeated subtractions by a power-of-two multiple does not skip any possible common divisor. Swapping $u$ and $v$ maintains $u \ge v$ for the next iteration.

Thus, Algorithm B' correctly computes the greatest common divisor of $u$ and $v$.

Step counting aligns with the argument in Section 4.5.2: each iteration reduces the binary length of the larger number, guaranteeing termination in at most $1 + \lfloor \lg \max(u, v) \rfloor$ compressed subtraction steps.

## Notes

Algorithm B' generalizes the "shift-and-subtract" optimization of Algorithm L to the subtractive Euclidean algorithm. One may further improve efficiency by replacing the binary shift with full division, computing $q = \lfloor u/v \rfloor$ and subtracting $q \cdot v$, which reduces the number of steps to $O(\lg \min(u, v))$ rather than $O(\lg \max(u, v))$. This leads directly to the classical division-based Euclidean algorithm.

This approach is particularly useful in software implementing multi-precision arithmetic or when $u$ and $v$ have hundreds of bits, because it converts many small subtractions into a single arithmetic operation involving a precomputed multiple.

This completes the proof.
