[Section 4.5.2: The Greatest Common Divisor](../)

**Exercise 11.** [*M22*] [M22] What is the probability that $\gcd(u, v) \le 3$? (See Theorem D.) What is the *average* value of $\gcd(u, v)$?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m55s  

---

We correct both parts, and in particular replace the unjustified interchange of limit and infinite sum by a derivation that keeps all sums finite until the final asymptotic step.

## 1. Probability that $\gcd(u,v)\le 3$

Let $1 \le u,v \le n$. Write $q_n(k)$ for the number of pairs with $\gcd(u,v)=k$. As in the flawed solution, every such pair corresponds uniquely to $(u,v)=(ka,kb)$ with $1\le a,b \le \lfloor n/k\rfloor$ and $\gcd(a,b)=1$. Hence

$$
q_n(k)=q_{\lfloor n/k\rfloor},
$$

where $q_m$ denotes the number of coprime pairs in $[1,m]^2$.

By Theorem D,

$$
\frac{q_m}{m^2} \to \frac{6}{\pi^2}.
$$

Thus

$$
\frac{q_n(k)}{n^2}
= \frac{q_{\lfloor n/k\rfloor}}{\lfloor n/k\rfloor^2}\cdot \frac{\lfloor n/k\rfloor^2}{n^2}
\to \frac{6}{\pi^2}\cdot \frac{1}{k^2}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\mathbb{P}(\gcd(u,v)=k)=\frac{6}{\pi^2 k^2}.
$$

Now sum over $k\le 3$:

$$
\mathbb{P}(\gcd(u,v)\le 3)
= \frac{6}{\pi^2}\left(1+\frac14+\frac19\right)
= \frac{6}{\pi^2}\cdot \frac{49}{36}
= \frac{49}{6\pi^2}.
$$

## 2. Average value of $\gcd(u,v)$

Let

$$
E_n = \frac{1}{n^2}\sum_{1\le u,v\le n} \gcd(u,v).
$$

### Step 1: exact arithmetic identity

Use the standard identity

$$
\gcd(u,v)=\sum_{d\mid u,\, d\mid v} \varphi(d).
$$

Substitute into the sum:

$$
E_n
= \frac{1}{n^2}\sum_{d\le n} \varphi(d)\,\#\{(u,v): d\mid u,\, d\mid v\}.
$$

For fixed $d$, the number of such pairs is $\lfloor n/d\rfloor^2$. Hence

$$
E_n = \sum_{d\le n} \varphi(d)\frac{\lfloor n/d\rfloor^2}{n^2}.
$$

This expression is completely finite, so no interchange-of-limit issues occur.

### Step 2: asymptotic reduction

We estimate

$$
\frac{\lfloor n/d\rfloor^2}{n^2} = \frac{1}{d^2} + O\!\left(\frac{1}{nd}\right).
$$

Thus

$$
E_n
= \sum_{d\le n} \frac{\varphi(d)}{d^2}
+ O\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{d\le n}\frac{\varphi(d)}{d}\right).
$$

We use the standard bounds (from TAOCP results on $\varphi$):

$$
\sum_{d\le n}\varphi(d)=\frac{3}{\pi^2}n^2+O(n\log n),
$$

which implies by partial summation that

$$
\sum_{d\le n}\frac{\varphi(d)}{d} = O(n).
$$

Hence the error term is $O(1)$, and

$$
E_n = \sum_{d\le n} \frac{\varphi(d)}{d^2} + O(1).
$$

### Step 3: growth of the main sum

We now analyze

$$
S_n = \sum_{d\le n} \frac{\varphi(d)}{d^2}.
$$

Use the average order $\varphi(d) \sim \frac{6}{\pi^2}d$, in the summatory sense:

$$
\sum_{d\le n} \varphi(d) = \frac{3}{\pi^2}n^2 + O(n\log n).
$$

Apply partial summation with $a_d=\varphi(d)$, $f(d)=1/d^2$. This yields

$$
S_n = \frac{6}{\pi^2}\log n + O(1).
$$

### Step 4: conclusion

Therefore

$$
E_n = \frac{6}{\pi^2}\log n + O(1),
$$

so $E_n \to \infty$.

## Final answers

$$
\mathbb{P}(\gcd(u,v)\le 3)=\frac{49}{6\pi^2},
$$

$$
\mathbb{E}[\gcd(u,v)] \text{ diverges, with } E_n \sim \frac{6}{\pi^2}\log n.
$$
