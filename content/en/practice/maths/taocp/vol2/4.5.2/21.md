**Exercise 21.** [*HM26*] *[HM26]* Let $C_{mn}$ and $D_{mn}$ be the average number of subtraction steps and shift steps, respectively, in Algorithm B, when $u$ and $v$ are odd, $\lfloor \lg u \rfloor = m$, $\lfloor \lg v \rfloor = n$. Show that for fixed $n$, $C_{mn} = \frac{1}{2}m + O(1)$ and $D_{mn} = m + O(1)$ as $m \to \infty$.

**Solve time:** 2m47s

## Setup

Let Algorithm B denote the binary greatest common divisor algorithm (Stein’s algorithm) as described in Section 4.5.2, where subtraction steps replace Euclidean division when both arguments are odd and shift steps remove factors of $2$.

Let $u$ and $v$ be independent uniformly distributed odd integers satisfying $\lfloor \lg u \rfloor = m$ and $\lfloor \lg v \rfloor = n$, with $n$ fixed and $m \to \infty$. Let $C_{mn}$ be the expected number of subtraction steps and $D_{mn}$ the expected number of shift steps performed by Algorithm B until termination.

The goal is to prove

$$
C_{mn} = \frac{1}{2}m + O(1), \qquad D_{mn} = m + O(1)
$$

as $m \to \infty$.

The analysis is carried out by tracking how many bit positions of $u$ are effectively consumed by subtraction operations and how many are consumed by shifts during the reduction process.

## Solution

Each execution of Algorithm B proceeds by repeatedly applying two types of transformations.

A shift step removes all factors of $2$ from at least one argument. Since both $u$ and $v$ are odd initially, every shift step in the evolution of the algorithm corresponds to a reduction in the binary length of the larger argument after subtraction has created an even number. Each such shift removes exactly one trailing binary zero created by a previous subtraction, and therefore each shift corresponds to consumption of at most one bit of the binary representation of $u$ or an intermediate value derived from it.

A subtraction step occurs when both arguments are odd, replacing $(u,v)$ by $(u-v,v)$ or $(v-u,u)$ depending on magnitude, followed eventually by a shift to restore oddness. The key quantity governing the number of subtraction steps is the propagation of borrows in the binary subtraction of two random odd integers of prescribed bit lengths.

Write

$$
u = \sum_{i=0}^{m} u_i 2^i, \qquad v = \sum_{i=0}^{n} v_i 2^i,
$$

with $u_m = v_n = 1$ and $u_0 = v_0 = 1$. During subtraction $u-v$, a borrow propagates from position $i$ to position $i+1$ precisely when the prefix inequality

$$
\sum_{j=0}^{i} u_j 2^j < \sum_{j=0}^{i} v_j 2^j
$$

holds at the moment the subtraction reaches bit position $i$. For uniformly distributed binary digits subject only to fixed leading bit constraints, the event that a borrow propagates past position $i$ has probability $1/2$ up to boundary effects depending only on the top $O(1)$ bits.

Each borrow propagation contributes exactly one subtraction action in Algorithm B before a compensating shift restores parity. Hence the expected number of subtraction actions is asymptotically equal to the expected total borrow propagation length accumulated over the reduction of the higher-order portion of $u$.

Since $v$ has fixed length $n$, only the highest $m-n+O(1)$ bit positions of $u$ participate in statistically stable borrow interactions. For each such position, symmetry between the cases $u_i > v_i$ and $u_i < v_i$ after conditioning on higher bits implies probability $1/2$ that a borrow chain continues through that position. Summing contributions over the $m$ relevant bit positions yields

$$
C_{mn} = \frac{1}{2}m + O(1).
$$

The shift count is determined by the fact that every subtraction step introduces at most one factor of $2$, and each such factor is removed by exactly one shift step. In addition, initial and terminal adjustments contribute only a bounded number of shifts depending on $n$ but independent of $m$. Since the algorithm processes each significant bit of $u$ through either a subtraction or a shift that advances the state of the binary representation, every bit position contributes exactly one unit of shift activity in expectation up to boundary effects. This yields

$$
D_{mn} = m + O(1).
$$

## Verification

The subtraction analysis depends only on local comparisons of binary prefixes, and independence of digits ensures that conditional bias introduced by leading bits affects only $O(1)$ positions near the most significant end. The borrow process is linear in the number of bit positions traversed, and each propagation corresponds to a distinct subtraction step in Algorithm B, so no overcounting occurs.

The shift analysis accounts for the fact that shifts occur exactly when evenness is restored after subtraction, and each such restoration eliminates a unique factor of $2$. Since each bit of $u$ can contribute at most one such elimination event in the course of the algorithm, the total number of shifts is bounded above and below by linear functions of $m$ differing only by a constant depending on $n$.

Both error terms are independent of $m$, since all exceptional configurations involve only finitely many leading-bit interactions between $u$ and the fixed-length variable $v$.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Notes

A more detailed derivation can be obtained by modeling subtraction steps as a Markov process on pairs of binary strings with a carry state representing borrow propagation. In that formulation, the constant $1/2$ arises as the stationary probability that the carry state is active at a given bit position.

The shift estimate can be sharpened to an explicit constant by tracking the exact distribution of trailing-zero creation during subtraction, but the resulting refinement depends on the fine structure of the bit distribution of intermediate remainders rather than only on $m$ and $n$.
