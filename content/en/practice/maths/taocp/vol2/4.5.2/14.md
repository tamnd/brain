**Exercise 14.** &#9654; [*HM25*] [HM25] What is the expected value of $\lg \gcd(u, v)$ when $u$ and $v$ are (a) random positive integers; (b) random positive odd integers?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m12s  

---

## Solution

Let $u$ and $v$ be random positive integers. We are asked to find the expected value of $\lg \gcd(u, v)$, where $\lg$ denotes the binary logarithm. Let us denote the expected value by $E[\lg \gcd(u, v)]$.  

By definition of expectation for nonnegative integer-valued random variables, we have

$$
E[\lg \gcd(u, v)] = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \Pr\bigl(\lfloor \lg \gcd(u, v) \rfloor \ge k \bigr),
$$

or equivalently, using the summation over prime factorizations, it is more convenient to consider the multiplicative structure of $\gcd(u, v)$. Let $p$ be a prime and let $u_p$ and $v_p$ denote the exponents of $p$ in the canonical prime factorizations of $u$ and $v$, respectively, as in equation (5) of Section 4.5.2. Then by equation (6),

$$
\gcd(u, v) = \prod_{p \text{ prime}} p^{\min(u_p, v_p)}.
$$

Hence we can write

$$
\lg \gcd(u, v) = \sum_{p \text{ prime}} \min(u_p, v_p) \lg p.
$$

Since $u$ and $v$ are independent random positive integers, the exponents $u_p$ and $v_p$ are independent geometric-like random variables with

$$
\Pr(u_p \ge k) = \frac{1}{p^k}, \quad k \ge 0,
$$

because a randomly chosen positive integer is divisible by $p^k$ with probability $1/p^k$. Therefore, for each prime $p$, the expected value of $\min(u_p, v_p)$ is

\[
\begin{aligned}
E[\min(u_p, v_p)] &= \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \Pr(\min(u_p, v_p) \ge k) \\
&= \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \Pr(u_p \ge k \text{ and } v_p \ge k) \\
&= \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \Pr(u_p \ge k) \Pr(v_p \ge k) \\
&= \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{p^k} \cdot \frac{1}{p^k} \\
&= \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{p^{2k}} \\
&= \frac{1/p^2}{1 - 1/p^2} = \frac{1}{p^2 - 1}.
\end{aligned}
\]

Consequently, by linearity of expectation, we have

\[
\begin{aligned}
E[\lg \gcd(u, v)] &= \sum_{p \text{ prime}} (\lg p) \, E[\min(u_p, v_p)] \\
&= \sum_{p \text{ prime}} \frac{\lg p}{p^2 - 1}.
\end{aligned}
\]

This gives the expected value of $\lg \gcd(u, v)$ for two random positive integers:

\[
\boxed{E[\lg \gcd(u, v)] = \sum_{p \text{ prime}} \frac{\lg p}{p^2 - 1}}.
\]

For part (b), let $u$ and $v$ be random positive odd integers. In this case, $2$ never divides $u$ or $v$, so $u_2 = v_2 = 0$ and the contribution of $p = 2$ vanishes. Therefore the expected value becomes

\[
\begin{aligned}
E[\lg \gcd(u, v)] &= \sum_{\substack{p \text{ prime} \\ p > 2}} \frac{\lg p}{p^2 - 1}.
\end{aligned}
\]

This completes the derivation.  

∎
