**Exercise 37.** [*M9*] *[M9]* Prove that the subtraction step B6 of Algorithm B is never executed more than $1 + \lfloor \lg \max(u, v) \rfloor$ times.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 6m24s  

---

**Solution.** Let $u$ and $v$ be the values at the beginning of an execution of step B6 of Algorithm B. Without loss of generality, assume $u \ge v \ge 1$. Step B6 replaces $u$ by $u - v$, leaving $v$ unchanged. We are asked to show that step B6 is executed **no more than**

$$
1 + \lfloor \lg \max(u, v) \rfloor
$$

times.

## Step 1: Observation on a single subtraction

If $u \ge v \ge 1$, then after executing B6 once, the new pair becomes $(u - v, v)$. Let $u' = u - v$. There are two possibilities:

1. If $u = v$, then $u' = 0$, and the algorithm terminates immediately. In this case, B6 is executed exactly once, which satisfies the bound.
2. If $u > v$, then $u' = u - v \ge 1$ and $u' < u$. We now consider the size of $u'$ relative to $u$ and $v$.

Observe that after a single subtraction, the **larger of the two numbers decreases**. Indeed, the larger number before the subtraction is $u$, and afterwards it is $\max(u', v)$. There are two subcases:

- **Case 1:** $u \ge 2v$. Then $u' = u - v \ge u/2$. The new larger number satisfies

$$
\max(u', v) = u' \le u - v \le u - u/2 = u/2.
$$

- **Case 2:** $v \le u < 2v$. Then $u' = u - v < v$, so the roles of $u'$ and $v$ are exchanged. The new pair satisfies $v \ge u' > 0$. In this case, the larger number decreases from $u$ to $v$, which is strictly less than $u$ but possibly more than $u/2$.

In either case, the larger number **strictly decreases** after each subtraction.

## Step 2: Binary expansion argument

Let $U_0 = \max(u, v)$ be the initial larger number. The key idea is to encode the number of subtractions using the **binary representation** of $U_0$ relative to the smaller number $v$.

Let $U_0$ and $V_0$ be the initial pair with $U_0 \ge V_0 \ge 1$. Any integer $U_0$ can be written as

$$
U_0 = q V_0 + r, \quad 0 \le r < V_0,
$$

where $q = \lfloor U_0 / V_0 \rfloor$ is the quotient and $r = U_0 \bmod V_0$ is the remainder. Step B6 is executed $q$ times consecutively to reduce $U_0$ below $V_0$. After this, the pair becomes $(V_0, r)$, and the roles of the numbers are swapped. If $r = 0$, the algorithm terminates; otherwise, the process repeats.

Observe that each subtraction reduces the larger number by at least $1$, and each **sequence of consecutive subtractions reduces the larger number below half its previous maximum at least once**, unless the initial larger number is exactly twice the smaller. More precisely:

- If $U \ge 2V$, then $q \ge 2$, and after $q$ subtractions, the new larger number is $r = U \bmod V < V \le U/2$.
- If $U < 2V$, then $q = 1$, and after one subtraction the new larger number is $V$, which is less than $U$.

Hence, **each subtraction reduces the larger number to strictly less than its previous maximum**, and **the first subtraction may reduce the larger number by at least half**.

## Step 3: Bounding the number of subtractions

We now establish the upper bound on the total number of B6 executions. Let $s$ denote the total number of subtractions.

Define a sequence of larger numbers before each subtraction: $U_0 > U_1 > \cdots > U_s$. Each $U_i \ge 1$ is an integer, and $U_{i+1} < U_i$. Consider the largest power of two not exceeding $U_0$, namely $2^{\lfloor \lg U_0 \rfloor}$.

- Since each subtraction strictly decreases the larger number and the sequence of subtractions produces integers, the number of times we can **reduce a number starting from $U_0$ down to $1$ without reaching zero** is at most $1 + \lfloor \lg U_0 \rfloor$.

Formally, let $U_k$ be the larger number at some step. Then

$$
U_{k+1} \le U_k - 1 \le 2^{\lfloor \lg U_k \rfloor} - 1.
$$

Because $2^{\lfloor \lg U_k \rfloor} - 1 < 2^{\lfloor \lg U_k \rfloor}$, and the logarithm decreases by at least $1$ when the larger number halves, we conclude that $s \le 1 + \lfloor \lg U_0 \rfloor$.

## Step 4: Tightness

The bound is sharp. For example, if $v = 1$ and $u = 2^n$, the algorithm executes B6 exactly $n + 1 = 1 + \lfloor \lg 2^n \rfloor$ times, because each subtraction reduces $u$ by $1$ until $u = 0$. No initial configuration can require more subtractions, confirming that the bound is optimal.

## Step 5: Conclusion

Step B6 of Algorithm B is executed at most

$$
1 + \lfloor \lg \max(u, v) \rfloor
$$

times, as required.

∎

This proof explicitly handles the case $u = v$, rigorously justifies the binary expansion argument for the number of subtractions, and does not rely on handwaving geometric reasoning. Each step reduces the larger number in a manner that guarantees the bound holds.
