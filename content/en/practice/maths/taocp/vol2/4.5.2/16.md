[Section 4.5.2: The Greatest Common Divisor](../)

**Exercise 16.** [*M22*] [M22] Design an algorithm to *divide $u$ by $v$ modulo $m$*, given positive integers $u$, $v$, and $m$, with $v$ relatively prime to $m$. In other words, your algorithm should find $w$, in the range $0 \le w < m$, such that $u \equiv vw \pmod{m}$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m15s  

---

## Solution

We are asked to find an integer $w$ satisfying

$$
u \equiv v w \pmod{m}, \quad 0 \le w < m,
$$

given positive integers $u$, $v$, $m$, with $v \perp m$. Since $\gcd(v, m) = 1$, $v$ has a multiplicative inverse modulo $m$; denote this inverse by $v^{-1}$, so that

$$
v v^{-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{m}. \eqno(1)
$$

Multiplying both sides of $u \equiv v w \pmod{m}$ by $v^{-1}$ gives

$$
v^{-1} u \equiv w \pmod{m}. \eqno(2)
$$

Hence $w = v^{-1} u \bmod m$ satisfies the required congruence. The task reduces to computing $v^{-1} \bmod m$.

### Step 1. Compute the inverse of $v$ modulo $m$ using the extended Euclidean algorithm

By the extended Euclidean algorithm, there exist integers $x$ and $y$ such that

$$
v x + m y = \gcd(v, m) = 1. \eqno(3)
$$

Since $v \perp m$, this equation has a solution with integer $x$, and $x \bmod m$ gives $v^{-1} \bmod m$. Explicitly, the algorithm proceeds as follows:

Let $A \leftarrow m$, $C \leftarrow v$. We iterate the standard Euclidean algorithm:

**E1.** Compute $r \leftarrow A \bmod C$.

**E2.** Set $A \leftarrow C$, $C \leftarrow r$.

**E3.** Repeat until $C = 0$.

Tracking coefficients via back-substitution produces integers $x$ and $y$ such that $v x + m y = 1$. Then $x \bmod m$ is the multiplicative inverse $v^{-1}$.

### Step 2. Compute $w = v^{-1} u \bmod m$

Once $v^{-1}$ is known, compute

$$
w \leftarrow (v^{-1} \cdot u) \bmod m.
$$

By equation (2), this guarantees $v w \equiv u \pmod{m}$. The resulting $w$ automatically satisfies $0 \le w < m$ by taking the remainder modulo $m$.

### Step 3. Algorithm summary

We may summarize the algorithm as a single procedure:

**Input:** Positive integers $u, v, m$ with $v \perp m$.

**Output:** Integer $w$, $0 \le w < m$, satisfying $v w \equiv u \pmod{m}$.

1. Compute integers $x, y$ such that $v x + m y = 1$ using the extended Euclidean algorithm.
2. Set $v^{-1} \leftarrow x \bmod m$.
3. Set $w \leftarrow (v^{-1} \cdot u) \bmod m$.
4. Return $w$.

### Correctness

By construction, step 1 ensures $v v^{-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{m}$. Multiplying both sides of the desired congruence $u \equiv v w \pmod{m}$ by $v^{-1}$ gives $w \equiv v^{-1} u \pmod{m}$. Step 3 reduces this congruence to the canonical representative $0 \le w < m$. This completes the proof.

∎

### Complexity

The extended Euclidean algorithm uses $O(\log m)$ steps, each step involving integer division with remainder. The final multiplication and modulo operation require one integer multiplication modulo $m$. Therefore the algorithm is efficient and requires at most $O(\log^2 m)$ bit operations if implemented with standard arithmetic.
