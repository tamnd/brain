**Exercise 18.** [*M24*] [M24] Show how Algorithm L can be extended (as Algorithm A was extended to Algorithm X) to obtain solutions of (15) when $u$ and $v$ are large.

## Solution

Algorithm L, as presented in Section 4.5.2, solves a linear Diophantine equation of the form

u x + v y = \gcd(u, v), \tag{15}

for integers $x$ and $y$ when $u$ and $v$ are small enough that all intermediate calculations fit within the word length of the machine. To extend Algorithm L for large $u$ and $v$, we mimic the approach used to extend Algorithm A to Algorithm X in Section 4.3.3, which allows Euclidean-style computations with arbitrarily large integers.

Let $U$ and $V$ be the given large integers with $U, V > 0$. Introduce local variables $(A, C, X, Y)$ corresponding to Algorithm L, where $A$ and $C$ are remainders in Euclid's algorithm and $X$ and $Y$ accumulate the coefficients of $U$ and $V$ in the identity

$A = U X + V Y.$

Initialize $A \leftarrow U$, $C \leftarrow V$, $X \leftarrow 1$, $Y \leftarrow 0$, $X' \leftarrow 0$, $Y' \leftarrow 1$. The invariants of Algorithm L hold at each step: $A = U X + V Y$, $C = U X' + V Y'$, and $0 \le C < A$.

Proceed with the Euclidean reduction using **quotient-and-remainder** form rather than repeated subtraction, to minimize the number of replacements and handle large integers efficiently. Let

$Q \leftarrow \lfloor A / C \rfloor, \quad R \leftarrow A - Q C.$

Then set

$A \leftarrow C, \quad C \leftarrow R,$

$X \leftarrow X' , \quad Y \leftarrow Y', \quad X' \leftarrow X - Q X', \quad Y' \leftarrow Y - Q Y'.$

Return to the reduction step until $C = 0$. At termination, $A = \gcd(U, V)$, and the integers $(X, Y)$ satisfy

$U X + V Y = \gcd(U, V).$

This procedure generalizes Algorithm L to arbitrarily large integers without exceeding machine word length in intermediate steps, provided that arithmetic on the multiword integers is supported. Each iteration strictly decreases $C$ because $0 \le R < C$, guaranteeing termination.

If one requires a bound on the number of steps, the argument in Section 4.5.2 shows that the number of quotient-remainder replacements is at most $2 \log_2(\min(U, V))$, by the standard Fibonacci-based analysis of Euclidean algorithm progress.

This completes the proof.
