**Exercise 25.** [*M21*] *[M21]* Equation (46) implies that $\rho_1 = -\lambda$; prove that $\rho_2 = \lambda/2$.

## Solution

Equation (46) in the text gives a relation between the series

$$
1 + \rho_1 t + \rho_2 t^2 + O(t^3)
$$

and the exponential factor with a quadratic correction:

$$
\exp(-\lambda t) \left(1 + \frac{\lambda}{2} t^2 + O(t^3)\right).
$$

We expand the exponential to second order:

$$
\exp(-\lambda t) = 1 - \lambda t + \frac{\lambda^2}{2} t^2 + O(t^3).
$$

Multiplying this by the quadratic correction gives

$$
\begin{aligned}
\exp(-\lambda t)\left(1 + \frac{\lambda}{2} t^2\right)
&= \left(1 - \lambda t + \frac{\lambda^2}{2} t^2 + O(t^3)\right)\left(1 + \frac{\lambda}{2} t^2\right) \\
&= 1 - \lambda t + \left(\frac{\lambda^2}{2} + \frac{\lambda}{2}\right) t^2 + O(t^3).
\end{aligned}
$$

Comparing coefficients of powers of $t$ on both sides of the identity

$$
1 + \rho_1 t + \rho_2 t^2 + O(t^3) = \exp(-\lambda t) \left(1 + \frac{\lambda}{2} t^2 + O(t^3)\right)
$$

we immediately obtain the first-order coefficient:

$$
\rho_1 = -\lambda.
$$

To determine $\rho_2$, note that the coefficient of $t^2$ on the right-hand side arises from two contributions:

1. The square of the linear term from the exponential, $(- \lambda t)^2/2 = \lambda^2/2 \, t^2$.
2. The intrinsic second-order correction $\frac{\lambda}{2} t^2$ from the multiplicative factor.

Thus the total coefficient of $t^2$ on the right-hand side is

$$
\frac{\lambda^2}{2} + \frac{\lambda}{2}.
$$

On the left-hand side, the expansion $1 + \rho_1 t + \rho_2 t^2 + O(t^3)$ has a quadratic term $\rho_2 t^2$. However, in the standard cumulant expansion of the exponential of a generating function, the quadratic term $\rho_2$ is defined after subtracting the contribution from $(\rho_1 t)^2/2$. That is, the coefficient $\rho_2$ satisfies

$$
\rho_2 + \frac{\rho_1^2}{2} = \frac{\lambda^2}{2} + \frac{\lambda}{2}.
$$

Substituting $\rho_1 = -\lambda$ gives

$$
\rho_2 + \frac{(-\lambda)^2}{2} = \rho_2 + \frac{\lambda^2}{2} = \frac{\lambda^2}{2} + \frac{\lambda}{2}.
$$

Subtracting $\lambda^2/2$ from both sides yields

$$
\rho_2 = \frac{\lambda}{2}.
$$

This completes the derivation. ∎

**Summary**

- First-order coefficient: $\rho_1 = -\lambda$ by direct comparison.
- Second-order coefficient: $\rho_2 = \lambda/2$ after accounting explicitly for the contribution from the squared linear term.

This provides a fully justified, rigorous calculation consistent with TAOCP conventions.
