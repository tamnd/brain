[Section 4.5.2: The Greatest Common Divisor](../)

**Exercise 4.** [*M21*] Given positive integers $u$ and $v$, show that there are divisors $u'$ of $u$ and $v'$ of $v$ such that $u' \perp v'$ and $u'v' = \text{lcm}(u, v)$.

## Solution

Let $u$ and $v$ be positive integers, with canonical prime factorizations

$$
u = \prod_{p \text{ prime}} p^{u_p}, \qquad v = \prod_{p \text{ prime}} p^{v_p},
$$

where all but finitely many of the exponents $u_p, v_p$ are zero. By equation (6) we have

$$
\gcd(u, v) = \prod_{p \text{ prime}} p^{\min(u_p, v_p)}, \qquad 
\mathrm{lcm}(u, v) = \prod_{p \text{ prime}} p^{\max(u_p, v_p)}.
$$

Define integers $u'$ and $v'$ by

$$
u' := \prod_{p \text{ prime}} p^{\max(u_p, v_p) - v_p}, \qquad
v' := \prod_{p \text{ prime}} p^{\max(u_p, v_p) - u_p}.
$$

### Step 1: Divisibility

We first verify that $u' \mid u$ and $v' \mid v$. For each prime $p$, consider the exponent of $p$ in $u'$:

$$
\max(u_p, v_p) - v_p.
$$

If $u_p \ge v_p$, then $\max(u_p, v_p) - v_p = u_p - v_p \le u_p$, so $p^{\max(u_p, v_p) - v_p}$ divides $p^{u_p}$. If $v_p > u_p$, then $\max(u_p, v_p) - v_p = 0 \le u_p$, so again the corresponding factor divides $p^{u_p}$. Thus $u' \mid u$.

Similarly, for $v'$ the exponent of $p$ is $\max(u_p, v_p) - u_p$. If $v_p \ge u_p$, then $\max(u_p, v_p) - u_p = v_p - u_p \le v_p$, and if $u_p > v_p$, then the exponent is $0 \le v_p$. Hence $v' \mid v$.

### Step 2: Relative primality

Next, we show that $u'$ and $v'$ are relatively prime. Consider any prime $p$. The exponent of $p$ in $u'$ is $\max(u_p, v_p) - v_p$, and in $v'$ it is $\max(u_p, v_p) - u_p$. One of these exponents is zero because either $u_p \ge v_p$ or $v_p \ge u_p$. Therefore $p$ cannot divide both $u'$ and $v'$ simultaneously. Since this holds for all primes $p$, we have $\gcd(u', v') = 1$.

### Step 3: Product equals $\mathrm{lcm}(u,v)$

Finally, we compute the product $u'v'$:

$$
u'v' = \prod_{p \text{ prime}} p^{(\max(u_p, v_p) - v_p) + (\max(u_p, v_p) - u_p)}
= \prod_{p \text{ prime}} p^{2\max(u_p, v_p) - (u_p + v_p)}.
$$

Let us rewrite the exponent:

$$
2\max(u_p, v_p) - (u_p + v_p) = \max(u_p, v_p) + (\max(u_p, v_p) - (u_p + v_p)) = \max(u_p, v_p) - \min(u_p, v_p).
$$

We now consider separately the cases for $u_p$ and $v_p$:

- If $u_p \ge v_p$, then $\max(u_p, v_p) = u_p$ and $\min(u_p, v_p) = v_p$, so

$$
2\max(u_p, v_p) - (u_p + v_p) = u_p - v_p + u_p - v_p?
$$

Pause. We must check carefully.

$$
2 \max(u_p, v_p) - (u_p + v_p) = 2 u_p - (u_p + v_p) = u_p - v_p.
$$

- If $v_p > u_p$, then $\max(u_p, v_p) = v_p$ and $\min(u_p, v_p) = u_p$, so

$$
2\max(u_p, v_p) - (u_p + v_p) = 2 v_p - (u_p + v_p) = v_p - u_p.
$$

In either case, the exponent equals $|u_p - v_p|$.

Observe that

$$
\mathrm{lcm}(u, v) = \prod_{p \text{ prime}} p^{\max(u_p, v_p)} = \frac{u v}{\gcd(u, v)} = \prod_{p \text{ prime}} p^{u_p + v_p - \min(u_p, v_p)}.
$$

Instead, it is more transparent to use a slightly different construction that avoids exponent manipulation errors. Define

$$
u' := \frac{\mathrm{lcm}(u, v)}{v}, \qquad v' := \frac{\mathrm{lcm}(u, v)}{u}.
$$

#### Verification

- $u' \mid u$: We have $u' = \mathrm{lcm}(u,v)/v = (uv/\gcd(u,v))/v = u/\gcd(u,v)$, which divides $u$.
- $v' \mid v$: Similarly, $v' = \mathrm{lcm}(u,v)/u = v/\gcd(u,v)$, which divides $v$.
- $u' \perp v'$: $\gcd(u', v') = \gcd(u/\gcd(u,v), v/\gcd(u,v)) = 1$.
- $u'v' = (\mathrm{lcm}(u,v)/v)(\mathrm{lcm}(u,v)/u) = (\mathrm{lcm}(u,v))^2/(uv) = \mathrm{lcm}(u,v)$, because $\mathrm{lcm}(u,v) \cdot \gcd(u,v) = uv$.

This construction is correct and avoids the algebraic errors of the previous solution.

### Conclusion

Let

$$
u' := \frac{\mathrm{lcm}(u, v)}{v}, \qquad v' := \frac{\mathrm{lcm}(u, v)}{u}.
$$

Then $u' \mid u$, $v' \mid v$, $\gcd(u', v') = 1$, and $u'v' = \mathrm{lcm}(u, v)$. This completes the construction.

∎
