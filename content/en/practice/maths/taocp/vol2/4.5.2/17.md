**Exercise 17.** &#9654; [*M20*] [M20] Given two integers $u$ and $v$ such that $u \equiv 1 \pmod{2^e}$, explain how to compute an integer $u'$ such that $u u' \equiv 1 \pmod{2^e}$. [This leads to a fast algorithm for computing the reciprocal of an odd number modulo a power of 2, since we can start with a table of all such reciprocals for $e = 8$ or $e = 16$.]

## Solution

We are asked to compute an integer $u'$ such that

$u u' \equiv 1 \pmod{2^e},$

given that $u$ is odd, i.e., $u \equiv 1 \pmod{2}$. Since $u$ is odd, $\gcd(u, 2^e) = 1$; hence a modular inverse exists and is unique modulo $2^e$.

We construct $u'$ by _Hensel lifting_, starting from the base case modulo $2$ and iteratively doubling the modulus.

**Step 1: Base case modulo 2.**

Since $u$ is odd, $u \equiv 1 \pmod{2}$. Let

$u_1 \equiv u^{-1} \pmod{2}.$

Then $u_1 = 1$ satisfies

$u u_1 \equiv 1 \pmod{2}.$

**Step 2: Iterative doubling.**

Suppose we have computed $u_k$ such that

$u u_k \equiv 1 \pmod{2^k}, \quad 1 \le k < e.$

We seek $u_{k+1} = u_k + t \cdot 2^k$ such that

$u u_{k+1} \equiv 1 \pmod{2^{k+1}}.$

Substitute $u_{k+1}$:

$u (u_k + t \cdot 2^k) = u u_k + t u 2^k \equiv 1 \pmod{2^{k+1}}.$

Since $u u_k \equiv 1 \pmod{2^k}$, write $u u_k = 1 + s 2^k$ for some integer $s$. Then

$1 + s 2^k + t u 2^k \equiv 1 \pmod{2^{k+1}} \implies (s + t u) 2^k \equiv 0 \pmod{2^{k+1}}.$

Divide both sides by $2^k$:

$s + t u \equiv 0 \pmod{2}.$

Since $u$ is odd, $u \equiv 1 \pmod{2}$, so

$t \equiv -s \pmod{2}.$

Choose $t = -s \bmod 2$. Then

$u_{k+1} = u_k + t \cdot 2^k \equiv u_k - s 2^k \pmod{2^{k+1}},$

and we have

$u u_{k+1} \equiv 1 \pmod{2^{k+1}}.$

**Step 3: Iteration to $2^e$.**

Starting with $u_1 = 1$, repeat the above step for $k = 1, 2, \dots, e-1$. Each step doubles the number of correct bits of the inverse. After $e-1$ iterations, we obtain $u_e$ such that

$u u_e \equiv 1 \pmod{2^e}.$

**Step 4: Explicit formula for implementation.**

If $x_1 = u_1 = 1$, then for $k = 1$ to $e-1$, set

$x_{k+1} = x_k (2 - u x_k) \pmod{2^{k+1}}.$

This follows because $s = (u x_k - 1)/2^k$, so

$x_{k+1} = x_k - s 2^k = x_k - \frac{u x_k - 1}{2^k} 2^k = x_k (2 - u x_k).$

This is a standard doubling iteration for modular inverses modulo powers of 2.

**Example.** Let $u = 5$, $e = 4$. Then

- $x_1 = 1$
- $x_2 = 1 \cdot (2 - 5 \cdot 1) \bmod 4 = 1 \cdot (2 - 5) \bmod 4 = 1 \cdot (-3) \bmod 4 = 1$
- $x_3 = 1 \cdot (2 - 5 \cdot 1) \bmod 8 = 1 \cdot (-3) \bmod 8 = 5$
- $x_4 = 5 \cdot (2 - 5 \cdot 5) \bmod 16 = 5 \cdot (2 - 25) \bmod 16 = 5 \cdot (-23) \bmod 16 = 5 \cdot 9 \bmod 16 = 13$

Check: $5 \cdot 13 = 65 \equiv 1 \pmod{16}$.

This completes the construction.

This algorithm requires exactly $e-1$ doubling steps after the initial base case. Each step preserves the invariant

$u x_k \equiv 1 \pmod{2^k},$

and $x_e$ is the desired inverse modulo $2^e$.

This completes the proof.
