## Setup

Exercise 4.5.2.34 asks for a rigorous proof that Brent's model gives the asymptotic behavior of Algorithm B. Let $T(n)$ denote the expected cost of Algorithm B when the input integers are chosen uniformly from the set of positive integers less than $2^n$. Brent's model predicts that

$$
T(n)\sim \beta n
\qquad (n\to\infty),
$$

where the constant $\beta$ is obtained from the invariant density associated with the transformation underlying Algorithm B. The problem asks for a proof that the actual average cost and the value predicted by Brent's model have the same asymptotic behavior.

## Solution

Exercise 34 is marked [HM49]. The notation refers to an open research problem. The text preceding the exercise develops Brent's heuristic model for Algorithm B and derives an asymptotic average behavior from a transfer operator acting on probability densities. A rigorous proof requires showing that the sequence of probability distributions generated by the actual execution of Algorithm B converges to the invariant distribution assumed in Brent's analysis, together with quantitative estimates strong enough to justify interchange of limits and averaging.

Such a proof must establish the following assertions.

First, the sequence of normalized states occurring during Algorithm B determines a dynamical system whose transfer operator has a unique invariant probability density.

Second, repeated application of the transfer operator to the distributions arising from uniformly distributed inputs converges to that invariant density.

Third, the convergence is sufficiently rapid to imply that the mean number of iterations satisfies

$$
T(n)=\beta n+o(n),
$$

where $\beta$ is the constant obtained from Brent's model.

No proof of these assertions is supplied in the text, and the exercise explicitly designates the problem as one whose solution was unknown. A complete proof therefore lies beyond the material developed in Section 4.5.2.

Consequently the appropriate answer is that Exercise 34 was an open problem when the section was written. A rigorous proof was not known, and the exercise asks the reader to supply one if possible.

## Verification

Brent's model produces a specific asymptotic constant by assuming convergence to an invariant distribution. A rigorous derivation requires proving existence of the invariant measure, proving convergence toward it, and proving that the expected cost obtained from that measure agrees asymptotically with the average running time of Algorithm B. None of these ingredients is established in Section 4.5.2.

Therefore no solution can be obtained solely from the methods developed in the section.
