[Section 4.5.2: The Greatest Common Divisor](../)

**Exercise 10.** &#9654; [*HM24*] [HM24] Let $q_n$ be the number of ordered pairs of integers $(u, v)$ lying in the range $1 \le u, v \le n$ such that $u \perp v$. The object of this exercise is to prove that we have $\lim_{n\to\infty} q_n/n^2 = 6/\pi^2$, thereby establishing Theorem D.

a) Use the principle of inclusion and exclusion (Section 1.3.3) to show that

$$q_n = n^2 - \sum_{p_1} \lfloor n/p_1 \rfloor^2 + \sum_{p_1 < p_2} \lfloor n/p_1 p_2 \rfloor^2 - \cdots,$$

where the sums are taken over all prime numbers $p_i$.

b) The *Möbius function* $\mu(n)$ is defined by the rules $\mu(1) = 1$, $\mu(p_1 p_2 \cdots p_r) = (-1)^r$ if $p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_r$ are distinct primes, and $\mu(n) = 0$ if $n$ is divisible by the square of a prime. Show that $q_n = \sum_{k \ge 1} \mu(k) \lfloor n/k \rfloor^2$.

c) As a consequence of (b), prove that $\lim_{n\to\infty} q_n/n^2 = \sum_{k \ge 1} \mu(k)/k^2$.

d) Prove that $\left(\sum_{k \ge 1} \mu(k)/k^2\right)\left(\sum_{n \ge 1} 1/n^2\right) = 1$. *Hint:* When the series are absolutely convergent we have

$$\left(\sum_n a_n/k^2\right)\left(\sum_m b_m/m^2\right) = \sum_n \left(\sum_{d \mid n} a_d b_{n/d}\right) / n^2.$$

## Solution

Let $q_n$ denote the number of ordered pairs $(u,v)$ with $1 \le u,v \le n$ and $\gcd(u,v)=1$.

### (a) Inclusion–exclusion formula

For each prime $p$, let $A_p$ be the set of pairs $(u,v)$ such that $p \mid u$ and $p \mid v$. Then $(u,v)$ is counted in $A_{p_1} \cap \cdots \cap A_{p_r}$ exactly when $p_1 \cdots p_r \mid u$ and $p_1 \cdots p_r \mid v$, which is equivalent to $p_1 \cdots p_r \mid u$ and $p_1 \cdots p_r \mid v$. The number of such pairs is $\lfloor n/(p_1 \cdots p_r) \rfloor^2$.

A pair is coprime if and only if it belongs to none of the sets $A_p$. By inclusion–exclusion,

$$
q_n
= n^2
- \sum_{p_1} \left\lfloor \frac{n}{p_1} \right\rfloor^2
+ \sum_{p_1 < p_2} \left\lfloor \frac{n}{p_1 p_2} \right\rfloor^2
- \cdots,
$$

where each term corresponds to a squarefree product of distinct primes.

### (b) Möbius form

Let $\mu(k)$ be defined as in the exercise. Each squarefree integer $k = p_1 \cdots p_r$ contributes exactly one term in the inclusion–exclusion expansion with coefficient $(-1)^r = \mu(k)$, and every non-squarefree integer contributes no term.

Thus the previous expression can be rewritten as

$$
q_n = \sum_{k \ge 1} \mu(k) \left\lfloor \frac{n}{k} \right\rfloor^2.
$$

All terms with $k > n$ vanish since $\lfloor n/k \rfloor = 0$.

### (c) Passage to the limit

Divide by $n^2$:

$$
\frac{q_n}{n^2}
= \sum_{k \ge 1} \mu(k)\left(\frac{\lfloor n/k \rfloor}{n}\right)^2.
$$

For each fixed $k$,

$$
\frac{\lfloor n/k \rfloor}{n} = \frac{1}{k} + O\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\right),
$$

so

$$
\left(\frac{\lfloor n/k \rfloor}{n}\right)^2 \to \frac{1}{k^2}.
$$

Also,

$$
0 \le \left(\frac{\lfloor n/k \rfloor}{n}\right)^2 \le \frac{1}{k^2}.
$$

Since $\sum_{k \ge 1} 1/k^2$ converges, dominated convergence applies to the absolutely convergent series $\sum_{k \ge 1} \mu(k)\left(\frac{\lfloor n/k \rfloor}{n}\right)^2$, giving

$$
\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{q_n}{n^2}
= \sum_{k \ge 1} \frac{\mu(k)}{k^2}.
$$

### (d) Evaluation of the series

Using the Euler product for the zeta function,

$$
\sum_{n \ge 1} \frac{1}{n^2}
= \prod_{p} \left(1 - \frac{1}{p^2}\right)^{-1}.
$$

The Dirichlet series for the Möbius function satisfies

$$
\sum_{k \ge 1} \frac{\mu(k)}{k^s}
= \prod_{p} (1 - p^{-s}),
\quad \Re(s) > 1.
$$

For $s=2$,

$$
\sum_{k \ge 1} \frac{\mu(k)}{k^2}
= \prod_p \left(1 - \frac{1}{p^2}\right).
$$

Multiplying the two Euler products gives

$$
\left(\sum_{k \ge 1} \frac{\mu(k)}{k^2}\right)
\left(\sum_{n \ge 1} \frac{1}{n^2}\right)
= \prod_p \left(1 - \frac{1}{p^2}\right)\left(1 - \frac{1}{p^2}\right)^{-1}
= 1.
$$

Hence

$$
\sum_{k \ge 1} \frac{\mu(k)}{k^2}
= \frac{1}{\zeta(2)}
= \frac{6}{\pi^2}.
$$

Combining with part (c) yields

$$
\boxed{\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{q_n}{n^2} = \frac{6}{\pi^2}}.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
