**Exercise 6.** [*30*] Discuss using Methods 1a, 1b, 2a, and 2b when $b$ or $B$ is $-2$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m49s

## Setup

Methods 1a, 1b, 2a, and 2b are stated for positive radices $b$ and $B$. The exercise asks how these methods behave when either the input radix or the output radix is $-2$.

A radix-$(-2)$ representation of an integer has the form

$$
u=\sum_{k=0}^{m}u_k(-2)^k,
\qquad
u_k\in\{0,1\}.
$$

Every integer possesses a unique finite representation of this type. We examine each method separately and determine which modifications are necessary.

## Solution

Method 1b presents no essential difficulty when $b=-2$. The method evaluates

$$
u=u_m(-2)^m+\cdots+u_1(-2)+u_0
$$

by the nested form

$$
\bigl(\cdots((u_m(-2)+u_{m-1})(-2)+u_{m-2})\cdots\bigr)(-2)+u_0.
$$

Since the digits satisfy $u_k\in{0,1}$, the computation requires only multiplication by $-2$ and addition of $0$ or $1$. Therefore Method 1b remains valid without alteration whenever the source radix is $-2$.

Method 2b likewise remains valid when $b=-2$. If

$$
u=(0.u_{-1}u_{-2}\ldots u_{-m})_{-2},
$$

then

$$
u=u_{-1}(-2)^{-1}+u_{-2}(-2)^{-2}+\cdots+u_{-m}(-2)^{-m},
$$

and the nested evaluation becomes

$$
\bigl((\cdots(u_{-m}/(-2)+u_{1-m})/(-2)+\cdots+u_{-2})/(-2)+u_{-1}\bigr)/(-2).
$$

The alternating signs cause no conceptual difficulty. Method 2b therefore applies directly.

The difficulties occur in Methods 1a and 2a, because these methods depend on repeated division by the target radix or repeated multiplication by the target radix.

Consider Method 1a with $B=-2$. The ordinary formulas

$$
U_0=u\bmod B,
\qquad
u=B\lfloor u/B\rfloor+U_0
$$

must be replaced by a division algorithm appropriate to a negative divisor. For every integer $u$ there exist unique integers $q$ and $r$ such that

$$
u=(-2)q+r,
\qquad
0\le r<2.
$$

Thus $r\in{0,1}$.

The least significant radix-$(-2)$ digit is

$$
U_0=r.
$$

The quotient $q$ is then subjected to the same operation. Repeated application yields

$$
u=U_0+(-2)\bigl(U_1+(-2)(U_2+\cdots)\bigr),
$$

hence

$$
u=\sum_{k\ge0}U_k(-2)^k.
$$

Therefore Method 1a remains valid if division by $-2$ is interpreted as producing the unique quotient and remainder satisfying

$$
u=(-2)q+r,
\qquad
r\in\{0,1\}.
$$

For example,

$$
-3=(-2)\cdot2+1,
$$

so the first digit is $1$ and the next quotient is $2$.

Now consider Method 2a with $B=-2$. The original method extracts digits by

$$
U_{-1}=\lfloor uB\rfloor,
\qquad
U_{-2}=\lfloor\{uB\}B\rfloor,
\qquad \ldots
$$

This argument depends on the fact that $0\le u<1$ implies $0\le uB<B$, so that the digit is obtained by taking an ordinary integer part. When $B=-2$,

$$
-2<uB\le0,
$$

and the digits are no longer obtained from the floor function.

Let

$$
u=\frac{d+v}{-2},
\qquad
d\in\{0,1\}.
$$

Then

$$
-2u=d+v,
$$

where $v$ is the remaining fractional part. Since $0\le v<1$, the quantity $-2u$ lies in an interval of length $1$ whose integer endpoint determines $d$.

The digit extraction rule becomes

$$
d=
\begin{cases}
0,& -1<-2u\le0,\\[2mm]
1,& 0\le -2u<1,
\end{cases}
$$

and after choosing $d$ the new fraction is

$$
v=-2u-d.
$$

Repeated application produces the radix-$(-2)$ digits to the right of the radix point. Thus Method 2a survives, but the simple floor formulas of the positive-radix case must be replaced by a digit-selection rule adapted to the intervals generated by multiplication by $-2$.

When the source radix is $-2$, Methods 1b and 2b are the natural choices because they use only multiplication or division by the source radix inside a radix-$B$ computation. When the target radix is $-2$, Methods 1a and 2a require modified quotient-remainder or digit-selection rules so that every extracted digit belongs to ${0,1}$.

## Verification

For Method 1a with $B=-2$, suppose

$$
u=(-2)q+r,
\qquad
r\in\{0,1\}.
$$

Applying the same decomposition to $q$ gives

$$
q=(-2)q_1+r_1,
\qquad
r_1\in\{0,1\}.
$$

Substitution yields

$$
u=r+(-2)r_1+(-2)^2q_1.
$$

Continuing until the quotient becomes $0$ produces

$$
u=\sum_{k=0}^{m}r_k(-2)^k.
$$

Hence the extracted remainders are exactly the radix-$(-2)$ digits.

For Method 1b with $b=-2$, the recurrence

$$
x_k=(-2)x_{k+1}+u_k
$$

satisfies

$$
x_k=u_k+(-2)u_{k+1}+\cdots+(-2)^{m-k}u_m.
$$

Induction on $k$ establishes this identity. Taking $k=0$ gives $x_0=u$, so the method evaluates the represented number correctly.

For Method 2b with $b=-2$, repeated substitution of

$$
x\mapsto \frac{x}{-2}+u_j
$$

expands into

$$
u_{-1}(-2)^{-1}+u_{-2}(-2)^{-2}+\cdots+u_{-m}(-2)^{-m},
$$

which is the value represented by the fractional digits.

## Notes

The negative radix changes only the extraction procedures. Polynomial evaluation, which underlies Methods 1b and 2b, is independent of the sign of the radix. The principal adjustment is that division by $-2$ must always produce remainders $0$ or $1$, and fractional digit extraction must be formulated so that the resulting digits remain in the set ${0,1}$.
