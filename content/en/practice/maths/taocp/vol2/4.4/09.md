[Section 4.4: Radix Conversion](../)

**Exercise 9.** &#9654; [*M29*] The purpose of this exercise is to compute $\lfloor u/10 \rfloor$ with binary shifting and addition operations only, when $u$ is a nonnegative integer. Let $v_0(u) = 3\lfloor u/2 \rfloor + 3$ and

$$v_{k+1}(u) = v_k(u) + \lfloor v_k(u)/2^{k+2} \rfloor \quad \text{for } k \ge 0.$$

Given $k$, what is the smallest nonnegative integer $u$ such that $\lfloor v_k(u)/16 \rfloor \ne \lfloor u/10 \rfloor$?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m13s  

---

## Setup

Define

$$
v_0(u)=3\lfloor u/2\rfloor+3,
$$

and

$$
v_{k+1}(u)=v_k(u)+\left\lfloor \frac{v_k(u)}{2^{k+2}}\right\rfloor
\qquad (k\ge0).
$$

For a fixed $k$, let

$$
F_k(u)=\left\lfloor \frac{v_k(u)}{16}\right\rfloor .
$$

The problem asks for the smallest nonnegative integer $u$ such that

$$
F_k(u)\ne \left\lfloor \frac{u}{10}\right\rfloor .
$$

The answer depends on $k$.

## Solution

### Case $k=0$

Write

$$
u=2n \quad\text{or}\quad u=2n+1,
$$

so that $\lfloor u/2\rfloor=n$. Then

$$
F_0(u)
=
\left\lfloor \frac{3n+3}{16}\right\rfloor .
$$

Also,

$$
\left\lfloor \frac{u}{10}\right\rfloor
=
\left\lfloor \frac{n}{5}\right\rfloor ,
$$

because $u=2n$ or $2n+1$.

Let

$$
n=5q+r,\qquad 0\le r<5.
$$

For $n<20$ we have $q\in{0,1,2,3}$, and

$$
\left\lfloor \frac{3n+3}{16}\right\rfloor
=
\left\lfloor \frac{15q+3r+3}{16}\right\rfloor .
$$

If $q=0$, then $3\le15q+3r+3\le15$, hence the floor is $0=q$.

If $q=1$, then $18\le15q+3r+3\le30$, hence the floor is $1=q$.

If $q=2$, then $33\le15q+3r+3\le45$, hence the floor is $2=q$.

If $q=3$, then $48\le15q+3r+3\le60$, hence the floor is $3=q$.

Therefore

$$
F_0(u)=\left\lfloor \frac{u}{10}\right\rfloor
\qquad (u<40).
$$

For $u=40$, $n=20$, and

$$
F_0(40)
=
\left\lfloor \frac{63}{16}\right\rfloor
=
3,
$$

while

$$
\left\lfloor \frac{40}{10}\right\rfloor=4.
$$

Hence the smallest counterexample is

$$
u=40.
$$

### Case $k=1$

Let

$$
m=\lfloor u/2\rfloor+1.
$$

Then $v_0(u)=3m$, and

$$
v_1(u)
=
3m+\left\lfloor\frac{3m}{4}\right\rfloor
=
\left\lfloor\frac{15m}{4}\right\rfloor .
$$

Therefore

$$
F_1(u)
=
\left\lfloor
\frac{\lfloor 15m/4\rfloor}{16}
\right\rfloor
=
\left\lfloor \frac{15m}{64}\right\rfloor .
$$

For $u<8$, we have $m\le4$, hence

$$
0\le \frac{15m}{64}<1,
$$

so $F_1(u)=0$. Since $u<10$,

$$
\left\lfloor \frac{u}{10}\right\rfloor =0.
$$

Thus equality holds for all $u<8$.

For $u=8$,

$$
m=5,
$$

and

$$
F_1(8)
=
\left\lfloor \frac{75}{64}\right\rfloor
=
1,
$$

while

$$
\left\lfloor \frac{8}{10}\right\rfloor =0.
$$

Hence the smallest counterexample is

$$
u=8.
$$

### Case $k\ge2$

For $u<6$ we have $\lfloor u/2\rfloor\le2$, so

$$
v_0(u)\le 3\cdot2+3=9.
$$

Consequently

$$
v_1(u)\le 9+\left\lfloor\frac94\right\rfloor=11,
$$

and

$$
v_2(u)\le 11+\left\lfloor\frac{11}{8}\right\rfloor=12.
$$

For every $j\ge2$,

$$
2^{j+2}\ge16,
$$

while $v_j(u)\le12$, hence

$$
\left\lfloor \frac{v_j(u)}{2^{j+2}}\right\rfloor =0.
$$

Therefore

$$
v_k(u)\le12
\qquad (u<6,\ k\ge2),
$$

and so

$$
F_k(u)=0.
$$

Since $u<10$,

$$
\left\lfloor \frac{u}{10}\right\rfloor =0.
$$

Thus equality holds for every $u<6$.

Now take $u=6$. Then

$$
v_0(6)=12,
$$

$$
v_1(6)=12+\left\lfloor\frac{12}{4}\right\rfloor=15,
$$

$$
v_2(6)=15+\left\lfloor\frac{15}{8}\right\rfloor=16.
$$

Hence

$$
F_k(6)\ge F_2(6)
=
\left\lfloor \frac{16}{16}\right\rfloor
=
1
\qquad (k\ge2),
$$

while

$$
\left\lfloor \frac{6}{10}\right\rfloor =0.
$$

Therefore the smallest counterexample is

$$
u=6.
$$

## Verification

For the first counterexamples:

$$
\left\lfloor \frac{v_0(40)}{16}\right\rfloor
=
\left\lfloor \frac{63}{16}\right\rfloor
=
3
\ne
4
=
\left\lfloor \frac{40}{10}\right\rfloor ,
$$

$$
\left\lfloor \frac{v_1(8)}{16}\right\rfloor
=
\left\lfloor \frac{18}{16}\right\rfloor
=
1
\ne
0
=
\left\lfloor \frac{8}{10}\right\rfloor ,
$$

and for every $k\ge2$,

$$
\left\lfloor \frac{v_k(6)}{16}\right\rfloor
\ge
\left\lfloor \frac{16}{16}\right\rfloor
=
1
\ne
0
=
\left\lfloor \frac{6}{10}\right\rfloor .
$$

## Notes

The smallest counterexample is

$$
\boxed{
u=
\begin{cases}
40, & k=0,\\[4pt]
8, & k=1,\\[4pt]
6, & k\ge2.
\end{cases}
}
$$
