**Exercise 14.** [*M27*] (A. Schönhage.) The text's method of converting multiple-precision integers requires an execution time of order $n^2$ to convert an $n$-place integer, when $n$ is large. Show that it is possible to convert $n$-digit decimal integers into binary notation in $O(M(n) \log n)$ steps, where $M(n)$ is an upper bound on the number of steps needed to multiply $n$-bit binary numbers that satisfies the "smoothness condition" $M(2n) \ge 2M(n)$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 5m06s  

---

Let the input be an $n$-digit decimal integer

$$
N=\sum_{i=0}^{n-1} a_i 10^i,
\qquad 0\le a_i\le 9.
$$

The usual left-to-right conversion repeatedly computes $x\leftarrow 10x+a_i$, which requires $\Theta(n^2)$ bit operations. We seek a divide-and-conquer method whose cost is $O(M(n)\log n)$.

Let $L=\lceil \log_2 10^n\rceil=\Theta(n)$ be the number of bits in the final binary result.

## Divide-and-conquer evaluation

Write the decimal digit string as a polynomial evaluated at $10$:

$$
N=a_0+a_1 10+\cdots+a_{n-1}10^{n-1}.
$$

Define a recursive procedure $V(S)$ that converts a block $S$ of consecutive decimal digits into the corresponding binary integer.

If $S$ consists of a single digit, return that digit.

Otherwise let $m=|S|$, split $S$ into a lower half $S_0$ and an upper half $S_1$, each of length about $m/2$:

$$
N(S)=N(S_0)+10^{m_0}N(S_1),
$$

where $m_0=|S_0|$.

Recursively compute

$$
A=V(S_0), \qquad B=V(S_1),
$$

then return

$$
A+10^{m_0}B.
$$

Thus the decimal number is evaluated by a balanced binary tree rather than sequentially.

To implement this efficiently, precompute the powers

$$
10,\;10^2,\;10^4,\;10^8,\ldots
$$

by repeated squaring. Since $10^{2^k}$ has $\Theta(2^k)$ bits, the total cost of this precomputation is

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{\lfloor\log n\rfloor} M(2^k)
=O(M(n)),
$$

because $M(2n)\ge 2M(n)$ implies

$$
M(2^k)\le 2^{k-\ell}M(2^\ell)
$$

for $k\le \ell$, so the above sum is dominated by its last term.

Hence the powers needed at all recursion levels are available at cost $O(M(n))$.

## Complexity analysis

Let $T(m)$ denote the cost of converting an $m$-digit decimal block.

At a node handling $m$ digits, the quantity represented has $\Theta(m)$ bits. The combination step computes

$$
A+10^{m_0}B.
$$

The addition costs $O(m)$, which is dominated by multiplication. The multiplication involves operands of $\Theta(m)$ bits, so it costs $O(M(m))$.

Therefore

$$
T(m)\le 2T(m/2)+c\,M(m)
$$

for some constant $c$.

We now solve this recurrence.

At level $j$ of the recursion tree there are $2^j$ subproblems of size $m/2^j$. The work on that level is

$$
2^j M\!\left(\frac{m}{2^j}\right).
$$

From the smoothness condition $M(2x)\ge 2M(x)$, applied repeatedly,

$$
M\!\left(\frac{m}{2^j}\right)
\le \frac{M(m)}{2^j}.
$$

Hence the total work on every level is at most

$$
2^j\cdot \frac{M(m)}{2^j}
= M(m).
$$

The recursion has $\lceil\log_2 m\rceil+1$ levels, so

$$
T(m)
=O\!\left(M(m)\log m\right).
$$

Taking $m=n$,

$$
T(n)=O\!\left(M(n)\log n\right).
$$

## Correctness

The recursion is based on the identity

$$
N(S)=N(S_0)+10^{m_0}N(S_1),
$$

which simply separates the decimal expansion into lower and higher digit blocks. The base case is correct for a single digit. By induction on the block length, each recursive call returns the exact binary value of its digit block, and the combination step reconstructs the original integer. Therefore the algorithm returns the correct binary representation of the input decimal integer.

Thus an $n$-digit decimal integer can be converted to binary notation in

$$
O(M(n)\log n)
$$

bit operations.
