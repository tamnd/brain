[Section 4.4: Radix Conversion](../)

**Exercise 15.** [*M47*] Can the upper bound on the time to convert large integers given in the preceding exercise be substantially lowered? (See exercise 4.3.3–12.)

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 4m38s  

---

## Setup

Exercise 4.4.14 shows that an $n$-digit decimal integer can be converted to binary notation in

$$
O(M(n)\log n)
$$

steps, where $M(n)$ bounds the cost of multiplying $n$-bit integers and satisfies

$$
M(2n)\ge 2M(n).
$$

The present exercise asks whether this upper bound can be substantially improved.

The reference to Exercise 4.3.3-12 is crucial. That exercise establishes that multiplication of large integers can be reduced to conversion problems of essentially the same size. Hence a sufficiently fast conversion algorithm would imply a correspondingly fast multiplication algorithm.

We must determine whether a conversion algorithm asymptotically faster than $O(M(n)\log n)$ is possible.

## Solution

Assume that there exists an algorithm converting $n$-digit decimal integers to binary notation in time

$$
T(n).
$$

Suppose further that $T(n)$ is substantially smaller than

$$
M(n)\log n.
$$

Exercise 4.3.3-12 shows that multiplication can be reduced to radix conversion. More precisely, two $n$-bit integers can be encoded as coefficients of a suitably chosen large-radix number; after converting between radices and performing the required arithmetic, the product can be recovered. The total work consists of a constant number of conversions together with lower-order operations.

Consequently, if radix conversion requires $T(n)$ steps, multiplication can be carried out in

$$
O(T(n))
$$

steps, up to constant factors.

Now Exercise 4.4.14 already derives conversion in

$$
O(M(n)\log n)
$$

steps by recursively splitting the decimal input and performing multiplications. Therefore the conversion problem is not asymptotically harder than multiplication by more than a logarithmic factor.

If there were an algorithm satisfying

$$
T(n)=o(M(n)\log n),
$$

the reduction of Exercise 4.3.3-12 would yield a multiplication algorithm whose running time is correspondingly smaller than the currently assumed bound $M(n)$. Replacing $M(n)$ by this improved multiplication bound and applying the construction of Exercise 4.4.14 again would give a conversion algorithm of the same order as multiplication, up to the logarithmic factor already present in the divide-and-conquer construction.

Thus any substantial improvement of the conversion bound would immediately imply a comparable improvement in the complexity of multiplication itself.

The conversion problem and the multiplication problem are therefore essentially equivalent in difficulty. The bound

$$
O(M(n)\log n)
$$

cannot be substantially lowered unless the upper bound $M(n)$ for multiplication can itself be substantially lowered.

## Verification

Exercise 4.4.14 proves

$$
\text{conversion} \le O(M(n)\log n).
$$

Exercise 4.3.3-12 gives a reduction in the opposite direction:

$$
\text{multiplication} \le O(\text{conversion}).
$$

Hence

$$
\text{multiplication}
\;\lesssim\;
\text{conversion}
\;\lesssim\;
M(n)\log n.
$$

Any asymptotic improvement of the conversion bound would therefore produce an asymptotic improvement in multiplication. No independent method is known that lowers the conversion complexity without simultaneously lowering the multiplication complexity.

Therefore the answer to the exercise is negative in the sense intended: a substantial reduction of the bound from Exercise 4.4.14 would require a corresponding breakthrough in integer multiplication.

## Notes

The exercise is analogous to many later results in computational complexity where two problems are linked by reductions in both directions. Here the logarithmic factor arises from the divide-and-conquer structure used in Exercise 4.4.14. The essential conclusion is that large-integer radix conversion is computationally equivalent to large-integer multiplication, up to this logarithmic factor.

$$
\boxed{\text{No substantial improvement is possible unless integer multiplication itself can be done substantially faster.}}
$$
