**Exercise 3.** &#9654; [*25*] [25] (D. Taranto.) When fractions are being converted, there is no obvious way to decide how many digits to give in the answer. Design a simple generalization of Method 2a that, given two positive radix-$b$ fractions $u$ and $v$ between 0 and 1, converts $u$ to a rounded radix-$B$ equivalent $U$ that has just enough places $M$ to the right of the radix

point to ensure that $|U - u| < \epsilon$. (In particular if $u$ is a multiple of $b^{-m}$ and $v = b^{-m}/2$, the value of $U$ will have just enough digits so that $u$ can be recomputed exactly, given $U$ and $m$. Note that $M$ might be zero; for example, if $\epsilon \le \frac{1}{2}$ and $u > 1 - r$, the proper answer is $U = 1$.)

### ## Solution

The statement uses the symbol $\epsilon$ but the data are $u$ and $v$; the intended meaning is that $v$ is the allowed error bound. We therefore require

$$
|U-u|<v.
$$

Write Method 2a in the usual recursive form. Define

$$
r_0=u,\qquad U_{-k}=\lfloor Br_{k-1}
floor,\qquad r_k=\{Br_{k-1}\}\qquad(k\ge1).
$$

Then

$$
u=\sum_{i=1}^{k}U_{-i}B^{-i}+r_kB^{-k},\qquad 0\le r_k<1. 	ag{1}
$$

The radix-$B$ number with the first $k$ digits is

$$
T_k=\sum_{i=1}^{k}U_{-i}B^{-i}.
$$

By (1),

$$
u-T_k=r_kB^{-k},\qquad 0\le u-T_k<B^{-k}. 	ag{2}
$$

### Generalized Method 2a (variable length)

1. Initialize $r_0\leftarrow u$, $k\leftarrow0$.
2. Repeat:

1. $k\leftarrow k+1$.
2. $U_{-k}\leftarrow\lfloor Br_{k-1}
floor$.
3. $r_k\leftarrow\{Br_{k-1}\}$.
4. If $r_kB^{-k}<v$, stop; otherwise continue.
3. Output $U=T_k=(0.U_{-1}\ldots U_{-k})_B$ and $M=k$.

### Correctness

At termination we have $r_MB^{-M}<v$, hence by (2),

$$
0\le u-U=r_MB^{-M}<v,
$$

so $|U-u|<v$ as required.

### Minimality of the number of digits

For any $k$, every radix-$B$ number with exactly $k$ fractional digits is an integer multiple of $B^{-k}$. The nearest such number to $u$ is obtained by rounding $u$ to $k$ places, and its error is at least

$$
\delta_k=\min\!\bigl(r_kB^{-k},\,B^{-k}-r_kB^{-k}\bigr). 	ag{3}
$$

If $r_kB^{-k}\ge v$, then (3) shows that truncation does not yet meet the tolerance. If also $B^{-k}-r_kB^{-k}\ge v$, then no radix-$B$ number with only $k$ fractional digits can satisfy the bound $|U-u|<v$. Therefore the first index $M$ for which either side of (3) drops below $v$ is the smallest possible number of fractional digits.

A symmetric version that rounds instead of truncates is therefore:

Minimal-digit rounding rule

After computing $U_{-1},\ldots,U_{-k}$ and $r_k$ by Method 2a, stop at the first $k$ for which

$$
\min\!\bigl(r_kB^{-k},\,B^{-k}-r_kB^{-k}\bigr)<v.
$$

If $r_kB^{-k}<v$, output the truncation $T_k$. If $B^{-k}-r_kB^{-k}<v$, output $T_k+B^{-k}$, propagating carries in radix $B$ if necessary. This produces a radix-$B$ number with the smallest possible number $M=k$ of fractional digits satisfying $|U-u|<v$.

### Special case mentioned in the exercise

Suppose $u$ is a multiple of $b^{-m}$ and $v=b^{-m}/2$. Let $M$ be the least integer with $B^{-M}\le b^{-m}$. Then every two distinct radix-$B$ numbers having at most $M$ fractional digits differ by at least $B^{-M}\ge b^{-m}/2=v$. Hence there is at most one such number within distance $v$ of $u$, namely the exact value of $u$. The algorithm therefore stops exactly when enough radix-$B$ digits have been produced to determine $u$ uniquely, and $u$ can be recomputed exactly from $U$ and $m$.

### The case M = 0

The rule naturally allows $M=0$. If $u>1-v$, then the radix-$B$ integer $1$ satisfies $|1-u|=1-u<v$. No fractional digits are needed, so the proper output is $U=1$.

This completes the proof.
