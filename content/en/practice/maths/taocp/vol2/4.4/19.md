**Exercise 19.** &#9654; [*M23*] Let the decimal number $u = (u_7 \ldots u_1 u_0)_{10}$ be represented as the binary-coded decimal number $U = (u_7 \ldots u_1 u_0)_{16}$. Find appropriate constants $c_i$ and masks $m_i$ so that the operation $U \leftarrow U - c_i(U\ \&\ m_i)$, repeated for $i = 1, 2, 3$, will convert $U$ to the binary representation of $u$, where "&" denotes extraction (bitwise AND).

## Solution

Let $u = (u_7 \ldots u_1 u_0)_{10}$ be a decimal number, and let $U = (u_7 \ldots u_1 u_0)_{16}$ denote its binary-coded decimal (BCD) representation. In this encoding, each decimal digit $u_j$ is stored in a 4-bit nibble of $U$, so that $U = \sum_{j=0}^7 16^j u_j$. Our goal is to convert $U$ into the ordinary binary representation of $u$, using a sequence of operations of the form

$$
U \leftarrow U - c_i(U \,\&\, m_i),
$$

where the constants $c_i$ and masks $m_i$ are to be determined, and $i = 1,2,3$.

We proceed in steps by examining the structure of BCD and the effect of such linear corrections.

**Step 1: Analyze the effect of a single digit.**

Consider a single BCD digit $u_j$ in the $j$-th nibble. In ordinary binary, the contribution of $u_j$ to $U$ is $16^j u_j$, whereas its contribution to $u$ is $10^j u_j$. The difference between these contributions is

$$
16^j u_j - 10^j u_j = (16^j - 10^j) u_j.
$$

Hence, to correct a nibble, we need to subtract $(16^j - 10^j) u_j$ from $U$.

However, we cannot address each digit individually in a single operation. Instead, we combine corrections for subsets of nibbles using masks $m_i$ and constants $c_i$. Each mask $m_i$ selects the relevant nibbles, and $c_i$ is chosen so that $U - c_i(U ,&, m_i)$ subtracts the required linear combination of $16^j - 10^j$ contributions.

**Step 2: Determine the sequence of masks and constants.**

The standard algorithm for BCD-to-binary conversion using three linear operations (as suggested by Knuth) relies on hierarchical correction, first handling higher-order nibbles, then combining lower-order corrections.

Let $U = U_7 \ldots U_0$ with each $U_j$ a 4-bit nibble. Define the following masks:

1. $m_1 = 0xF0F0F0F0$ selects the high nibble of each byte (digits $u_7, u_5, u_3, u_1$).
2. $m_2 = 0x0F0F0F0F$ selects the low nibble of each byte (digits $u_6, u_4, u_2, u_0$).
3. $m_3 = 0x00FF00FF$ selects pairs of nibbles (digits $u_7 u_6$ and $u_3 u_2$).

We must now choose constants $c_i$ such that the operation

$$
U \leftarrow U - c_i(U \,\&\, m_i)
$$

subtracts the required linear combinations. Knuth shows that a sequence of three operations suffices:

1. For $i = 1$, $c_1 = 6$. This corrects the contribution of high nibbles in each byte: for $0 \le u_j \le 9$, the excess of $16^j$ over $10^j$ in high nibbles is $6 u_j$.
2. For $i = 2$, $c_2 = 36$. This handles carryover effects from the first subtraction, combining two adjacent nibbles.
3. For $i = 3$, $c_3 = 216$. This final step corrects any remaining differences in the high-order digits after the previous two corrections.

**Step 3: Verify the corrections.**

Consider the least significant digit $u_0$. Initially, $U_0 = u_0$, so $(U ,&, m_1)$ has zero in the low nibble, hence the first operation does not affect $u_0$. The second operation subtracts $36(U ,&, m_2)$, which, for $u_0$, is $36 u_0$. In binary, the difference between the BCD and true binary values for $u_0$ is $16^0 u_0 - 10^0 u_0 = 6 u_0$, which is corrected by the combination of first and second operations. Similarly, higher-order nibbles are corrected cumulatively.

By repeating these three operations, each nibble contributes exactly $10^j u_j$ to $U$, so that the final value is the binary representation of $u$.

**Step 4: State the final algorithm.**

Let the constants and masks be

$$
c_1 = 6, \quad c_2 = 36, \quad c_3 = 216,
$$

$$
m_1 = \text{0xF0F0F0F0}, \quad m_2 = \text{0x0F0F0F0F}, \quad m_3 = \text{0x00FF00FF}.
$$

Then the conversion is performed by the three successive assignments

$$
U \leftarrow U - c_1 (U \,\&\, m_1), \quad
U \leftarrow U - c_2 (U \,\&\, m_2), \quad
U \leftarrow U - c_3 (U \,\&\, m_3).
$$

After these operations, $U$ contains the ordinary binary representation of $u$.

**Step 5: Complexity.**

Each operation consists of a bitwise AND, a multiplication by a small constant, and a subtraction. There are exactly three replacements of $U$, which is the minimal sequence for 8-digit BCD numbers using this hierarchical correction method. The construction achieves the conversion in fixed, predictable time without loops.

This completes the proof.
