## Corrected Solution

**Exercise 4.4.4, Problem 4**

**(a) Terminating binary $\implies$ terminating decimal**

Let $x$ be a real number with a terminating binary expansion. Then the fractional part of $x$ can be written as a finite sum of negative powers of $2$:

$$
f = \sum_{k=1}^{n} u_{-k} 2^{-k} = \frac{p}{2^n},
$$

where $p$ is an integer with $0 \le p < 2^n$ and $n \ge 0$. The integer part of $x$ is also an integer, which clearly has a terminating decimal expansion.

It is a standard fact that a rational number $p/q$ in lowest terms has a terminating decimal expansion if and only if all prime factors of $q$ are $2$ or $5$. In this case, the denominator is $2^n$, whose only prime factor is $2$. Therefore, $f = p/2^n$ has a terminating decimal expansion. Combining with the integer part, $x$ has a terminating decimal expansion.

∎

**(b) Condition for terminating radix-$b$ numbers to terminate in radix-$B$**

Let $b$ and $B$ be integers greater than $1$. A nonnegative real number $x$ has a terminating radix-$b$ representation if and only if its fractional part can be expressed as

$$
f = \frac{p}{b^n}
$$

for integers $p \ge 0$ and $n \ge 0$. Similarly, $x$ has a terminating radix-$B$ representation if and only if

$$
f = \frac{q}{B^m}
$$

for integers $q \ge 0$ and $m \ge 0$.

We wish to find a condition on $b$ and $B$ such that **every** terminating radix-$b$ number is also terminating in radix $B$.

**Step 1: Factor $b$ and $B$ into primes**

Let

$$
b = \prod_{i=1}^{r} p_i^{\alpha_i}, \quad B = \prod_{i=1}^{s} q_i^{\beta_i},
$$

where $p_1,\dots,p_r$ and $q_1,\dots,q_s$ are distinct primes, and $\alpha_i, \beta_i \ge 1$.

**Step 2: Necessity**

Suppose there exists a prime $p$ dividing $b$ but not $B$. Consider the number

$$
x = \frac{1}{p}.
$$

Since $p \mid b$, $1/p$ can be written as a terminating radix-$b$ fraction. Indeed, if $b = p \cdot k$, then

$$
\frac{1}{p} = \frac{k}{b}.
$$

However, the denominator of $1/p$ in lowest terms contains $p$, which does not divide $B$. A rational number terminates in radix $B$ if and only if its reduced denominator has only primes dividing $B$. Therefore, $1/p$ cannot have a terminating radix-$B$ expansion.

Hence **every prime factor of $b$ must divide $B**. This proves necessity.

**Step 3: Sufficiency**

Assume that every prime factor of $b$ also divides $B$. We must show that for every integer $n \ge 0$ there exists $m \ge 0$ such that

$$
b^n \mid B^m.
$$

Write the prime factorizations as

$$
b = \prod_{i=1}^{r} p_i^{\alpha_i}, \quad B = \prod_{i=1}^{r} p_i^{\beta_i} \prod_{j=1}^{s} q_j^{\gamma_j},
$$

where $p_1,\dots,p_r$ are exactly the primes dividing $b$ (which by assumption also divide $B$), and $q_1,\dots,q_s$ are any extra primes in $B$.

Then

$$
b^n = \prod_{i=1}^{r} p_i^{n \alpha_i}, \quad B^m = \prod_{i=1}^{r} p_i^{m \beta_i} \prod_{j=1}^{s} q_j^{m \gamma_j}.
$$

We can choose

$$
m = \max_{1 \le i \le r} \left\lceil \frac{n \alpha_i}{\beta_i} \right\rceil,
$$

so that for each $i$ we have $m \beta_i \ge n \alpha_i$. Then $b^n \mid B^m$. Therefore, for any terminating radix-$b$ fraction $p/b^n$, we can write

$$
\frac{p}{b^n} = \frac{p \cdot (B^m / b^n)}{B^m},
$$

which is a terminating radix-$B$ fraction. This proves sufficiency.

**Step 4: Conclusion**

Every terminating radix-$b$ fraction is also terminating in radix $B$ **if and only if** every prime factor of $b$ divides $B$.

∎

**Answer (b):**

$$
\boxed{\text{Every number with a terminating radix-}b\text{ expansion has a terminating radix-}B\text{ expansion if and only if all prime factors of }b\text{ divide }B.}
$$
