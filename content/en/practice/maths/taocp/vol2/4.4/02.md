Let the mixed-radix system have radices

$$
r_0,r_1,\ldots,r_{n-1},
$$

so that a number is represented in the form

$$
(a_n,a_{n-1},\ldots,a_0),
$$

with

$$
0\le a_i<r_i \qquad (0\le i<n),
$$

and value

$$
N
=
a_0
+a_1r_0
+a_2r_0r_1
+\cdots
+a_n r_0r_1\cdots r_{n-1}.
$$

Method 1a for an ordinary radix $r$ repeatedly divides by $r$, recording the remainders. The mixed-radix generalization is obtained by dividing successively by the varying radices.

Given $N$, define $N_0=N$. For $i=0,1,\ldots$, perform Euclidean division

$$
N_i=q_i r_i+a_i,
\qquad 0\le a_i<r_i .
$$

Then set

$$
N_{i+1}=q_i.
$$

When the quotient becomes $0$, the digits are

$$
a_0,a_1,\ldots .
$$

The correctness follows exactly as in Method 1a. Since

$$
N_i=q_i r_i+a_i,
$$

the remainder $a_i$ is uniquely determined. Repeated substitution gives

$$
N
=
a_0
+r_0\Bigl(
a_1
+r_1\bigl(
a_2+\cdots
\bigr)
\Bigr),
$$

which expands to the mixed-radix representation above. Therefore the successive remainders produced by the algorithm are precisely the mixed-radix digits.

To illustrate the method, consider the mixed-radix system used for days, hours, minutes, and seconds, whose radices are

$$
24,\ 60,\ 60.
$$

Suppose the conversion problem of Exercise 1 is to convert $93784$ seconds into this mixed-radix notation.

Begin with $N_0=93784$.

First divide by $60$:

$$
93784 = 1563\cdot 60 + 4.
$$

Hence

$$
a_0=4.
$$

Next divide $1563$ by $60$:

$$
1563 = 26\cdot 60 + 3.
$$

Hence

$$
a_1=3.
$$

Next divide $26$ by $24$:

$$
26 = 1\cdot 24 + 2.
$$

Hence

$$
a_2=2.
$$

Finally,

$$
1=0\cdot 24+1,
$$

so

$$
a_3=1.
$$

Reading the remainders from last to first gives

$$
(1,2,3,4),
$$

that is,

$$
1\ \text{day},\ 2\ \text{hours},\ 3\ \text{minutes},\ 4\ \text{seconds}.
$$

Indeed,

$$
1(24\cdot60\cdot60)
+2(60\cdot60)
+3(60)
+4
=
86400+7200+180+4
=
93784.
$$

Thus Method 1a generalizes to mixed-radix notation by replacing repeated division by a fixed radix with successive division by the sequence of radices $r_0,r_1,\ldots$, and the resulting remainders are the mixed-radix digits.
