**Exercise 3.1.18** [**]

**18.** _[M20]_ Invent a method analogous to that of exercise 7 for finding cycles in the general form of random number generator discussed in exercise 17.

**Solution.**

Let the general order-$k$ random number generator be

$$
X_{n+1} = f(X_n, X_{n-1}, \dots, X_{n-k+1}),
$$

where each $X_n$ takes values in $\{0,1,\dots,m-1\}$.

**Step 1: Reformulate as a first-order iteration on a finite state space.**

Define the **state** of the generator at time $n$ as the ordered $k$-tuple

$$
S_n = (X_n, X_{n-1}, \dots, X_{n-k+1}) \in \{0,1,\dots,m-1\}^k.
$$

Since each component of $S_n$ has $m$ possible values, the total number of possible states is

$$
|\{0,1,\dots,m-1\}^k| = m^k < \infty.
$$

Define the **state-transition map** $T:\{0,1,\dots,m-1\}^k \to \{0,1,\dots,m-1\}^k$ by

$$
T(x_0,x_1,\dots,x_{k-1}) = (f(x_0,x_1,\dots,x_{k-1}), x_0, x_1, \dots, x_{k-2}).
$$

Then the sequence of states satisfies

$$
S_{n+1} = T(S_n), \quad n \ge 0.
$$

This reduces the order-$k$ generator to a **first-order deterministic iteration** on a finite set of size $m^k$. By the Pigeonhole Principle, there exist integers $\mu \ge 0$ and $\lambda \ge 1$ such that

$$
S_\mu = S_{\mu+\lambda},
$$

and $\lambda$ is minimal with this property. The integer $\mu$ is the **preperiod**, and $\lambda$ is the **period** of the generator.

**Step 2: Adapt Floyd's cycle-finding method to the state-transition map.**

Floyd's algorithm, described in exercise 7 for first-order sequences, finds $\mu$ and $\lambda$ efficiently without storing all states. Here, we apply it to the map $T$.

**Algorithm:**

1. **Initialization:**

$$
\text{tortoise} := S_0, \quad \text{hare} := S_0.
$$

1. **Phase 1: Detect a cycle**

Advance the tortoise one step and the hare two steps under $T$:

$$
\text{tortoise} := T(\text{tortoise}), \quad \text{hare} := T(T(\text{hare})).
$$

Repeat until $\text{tortoise} = \text{hare}$. Let the number of steps taken by the tortoise be $t_1$. Since there are only $m^k$ states, and the sequence is deterministic, this process is guaranteed to terminate in at most $m^k$ steps.

1. **Phase 2: Find the start of the cycle ($\mu$)**

Reset the tortoise to $S_0$, keeping the hare at the meeting point from Phase 1. Advance both one step at a time:

$$
\text{tortoise} := T(\text{tortoise}), \quad \text{hare} := T(\text{hare}).
$$

Let $\mu$ be the number of steps from $S_0$ until they meet. Then $S_\mu$ is the first state in the cycle.

**Justification:** Let the cycle start at $S_\mu$ with length $\lambda$. If the tortoise is at $S_0$ and the hare is at $S_{\mu+r\lambda}$ (from Phase 1), then after $k$ steps both move forward in lockstep modulo $\lambda$. They will meet after exactly $\mu$ steps at $S_\mu$. This is the standard correctness proof of Floyd's algorithm.

1. **Phase 3: Determine the cycle length ($\lambda$)**

Keep the tortoise fixed at $S_\mu$. Advance the hare one step at a time:

$$
\text{hare} := T(\text{hare}),
$$

counting the number of steps until $\text{hare} = \text{tortoise}$. This number is $\lambda$, the period of the generator.

**Justification:** Once the start of the cycle is identified, the first return of the hare to the tortoise occurs exactly after $\lambda$ iterations of $T$.

**Step 3: Remarks**

- Each step applies $T$ to a $k$-tuple, which consists of computing $f$ once and shifting the remaining $k-1$ components.
- The total number of steps in Phase 1 is at most $m^k$. Phase 2 requires at most $\mu\le m^k$ steps, and Phase 3 at most $\lambda \le m^k$ steps. Therefore the algorithm terminates.
- This method is a direct generalization of Floyd's cycle-finding algorithm (exercise 7) from first-order sequences ($k=1$) to sequences of order $k>1$.

**Conclusion**

By defining the state-transition map

$$
T(x_0,\dots,x_{k-1}) = (f(x_0,\dots,x_{k-1}),x_0,\dots,x_{k-2}),
$$

and applying Floyd's tortoise-and-hare method to the sequence $(S_n)$, we obtain a mathematically well-defined, efficient procedure to determine both the preperiod $\mu$ and the period $\lambda$ of a general order-$k$ random number generator.

This solution corrects the previous flaws: the iteration is applied to the proper state-transition map $T$, each step is mathematically justified, and the connection to exercise 7 is explicitly explained.
