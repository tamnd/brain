**Exercise 7.** &#9654; [**] [M21] (R. P. Brent, 1977.) Let $\ell(n)$ be the greatest power of 2 that is less than or equal to $n$; thus, for example, $\ell(15) = 8$ and $\ell(\ell(n)) = \ell(n)$.

a) Show that, in terms of the notation in exercise 6, there exists an $n > 0$ such that $X_n = X_{\ell(n)-1}$. Find a formula that expresses the least such $n$ in terms of the periodicity numbers $\mu$ and $\lambda$.
b) Apply this result to design an algorithm that can be used in conjunction with any random number generator of the type $X_{n+1} = f(X_n)$, to prevent it from cycling indefinitely. Your algorithm should calculate the period length $\lambda$, and it should use only a small amount of memory space, you must not simply store all of the computed sequence values!

### a)

Let

$$
X_0,X_1,X_2,\ldots
$$

be a sequence generated by

$$
X_{n+1}=f(X_n),
$$

and let $\mu$ and $\lambda$ be the periodicity parameters of Exercise 6. Thus

$$
X_i\neq X_j \qquad (0\le i<j<\mu+\lambda,\ i\ne j),
$$

and

$$
X_{\mu+t+\lambda}=X_{\mu+t}\qquad (t\ge0).
$$

Define

$$
\ell(n)=2^{\lfloor \log_2 n\rfloor},
$$

the largest power of $2$ not exceeding $n$.

We seek the least $n>0$ such that

$$
X_n=X_{\ell(n)-1}.
$$

Let

$$
k=\ell(n)-1.
$$

Since $\ell(n)\le n<2\ell(n)$, we have

$$
k<n\le 2k+1.
$$

If $X_n=X_k$ with $n>k$, then $k$ and $n$ must both lie in the periodic part of the sequence, because all values before index $\mu$ are distinct. Hence

$$
k\ge\mu,
$$

and periodicity implies

$$
n\equiv k \pmod{\lambda}.
$$

Since $n>k$, write

$$
n=k+r\lambda,
\qquad r\ge1.
$$

Because $n\le 2k+1$,

$$
k+r\lambda\le 2k+1,
$$

hence

$$
r\lambda\le k+1.
$$

Since $r\ge1$, a necessary condition is

$$
k+1\ge\lambda.
$$

Conversely, if $k\ge\mu$ and $k+1\ge\lambda$, then $n=k+\lambda$ satisfies

$$
k<n\le 2k+1,
$$

and therefore lies in the interval

$$
\ell(n)-1=k.
$$

Since $n\equiv k\pmod\lambda$, we obtain

$$
X_n=X_k=X_{\ell(n)-1}.
$$

Thus such an $n$ exists exactly when $k$ is a power of two minus one satisfying

$$
k\ge\mu,\qquad k+1\ge\lambda.
$$

Let

$$
L=\max(\mu+1,\lambda).
$$

Let $2^m$ be the least power of $2$ such that

$$
2^m\ge L.
$$

Then

$$
k=2^m-1
$$

is the least possible value of $k=\ell(n)-1$ for which a solution can occur. For this $k$, the smallest admissible $n$ is

$$
n=k+\lambda.
$$

Therefore the least $n>0$ satisfying

$$
X_n=X_{\ell(n)-1}
$$

is

$$
\boxed{\;
n=\lambda+\ell\!\bigl(\max(\mu+1,\lambda)\bigr)^{\uparrow}-1
\;}
$$

where $\ell(\max(\mu+1,\lambda))^{\uparrow}$ denotes the least power of $2$ greater than or equal to $\max(\mu+1,\lambda)$.

Equivalently, if

$$
2^m=\min\{2^t:2^t\ge \max(\mu+1,\lambda)\},
$$

then

$$
\boxed{\;n=2^m+\lambda-1.\;}
$$

### b)

The result suggests a cycle-finding method based on powers of two.

Maintain two indices:

- a stationary index $s=2^m-1$,
- a moving index $t>s$.

At stage $m$, keep $X_s$ fixed and generate successive values

$$
X_{s+1},X_{s+2},\ldots,X_{2^{m+1}-1}.
$$

Whenever a new value is produced, compare it with $X_s$.

If no equality occurs before $t=2^{m+1}-1$, replace $s$ by

$$
2^{m+1}-1
$$

and begin the next stage.

By part (a), when $2^m\ge\max(\mu+1,\lambda)$, the first equality

$$
X_t=X_s
$$

must occur with

$$
s=2^m-1,\qquad t=s+\lambda.
$$

Hence at the first collision,

$$
\lambda=t-s.
$$

This yields Brent's algorithm:

1. Set

$$
\text{power}=1,\qquad \lambda=1.
$$

Let

$$
\text{tortoise}=X_0,\qquad
\text{hare}=X_1.
$$
2. While

$$
\text{tortoise}\ne\text{hare},
$$

do:

- If $\lambda=\text{power}$, set

$$
\text{tortoise}:=\text{hare},
\qquad
\text{power}:=2\,\text{power},
\qquad
\lambda:=0.
$$
- Advance

$$
\text{hare}:=f(\text{hare}),
$$

and increment $\lambda$.
3. When equality is found, the value of $\lambda$ is the period length.

The algorithm stores only a constant number of sequence values and counters. No previously generated sequence values need be retained. The memory requirement is therefore $O(1)$.

To see that the computed $\lambda$ is correct, observe that during a stage with stationary point $X_{2^m-1}$, the counter $\lambda$ measures the distance from that point. The first stage for which

$$
2^m\ge\max(\mu+1,\lambda)
$$

contains an index

$$
(2^m-1)+\lambda
$$

having the same value as the stationary point. The detected distance is exactly the cycle length.

Thus Brent's power-of-two method computes the period length $\lambda$ using only a small, constant amount of memory. ∎
