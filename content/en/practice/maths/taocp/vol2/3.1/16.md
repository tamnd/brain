**Exercise 16.** [**] *[15]* A sequence generated as in exercise 6 must begin to repeat after at most $m$ values have been generated. Suppose we generalize the method so that $X_{n+1}$ depends on $X_{n-1}$ as well as on $X_n$; formally, let $f(x, y)$ be a function such that $0 \le x, y < m$ implies $0 \le f(x, y) < m$. The sequence is constructed by selecting $X_0$ and $X_1$ arbitrarily, and then letting

$$X_{n+1} = f(X_n, X_{n-1}), \qquad \text{for } n > 0.$$

What is the maximum period conceivably attainable in this case?

**Solution to Exercise 3.1.16**

We are asked to determine the maximum period attainable for a sequence defined by

$$
X_{n+1} = f(X_n, X_{n-1}), \qquad n > 0,
$$

where $0 \le X_n < m$ and $f$ maps pairs $(x,y)$ with $0 \le x,y < m$ into the same range.

**Step 1: Restate as a first-order sequence on pairs**

Define the **state sequence**

$$
S_n = (X_{n-1}, X_n).
$$

Then the recurrence gives

$$
S_{n+1} = (X_n, X_{n+1}) = (X_n, f(X_n, X_{n-1})) = T(S_n),
$$

where

$$
T(x,y) = (y, f(y,x)).
$$

Thus, the second-order recurrence on $\{X_n\}$ reduces to a first-order iteration on the set of pairs

$$
\{ (x,y) \mid 0 \le x,y < m \}.
$$

Since there are exactly $m^2$ such pairs, every orbit under $T$ eventually repeats, and no cycle of $T$ can exceed $m^2$ in length. Therefore the period of the original sequence $\{X_n\}$ satisfies

$$
\text{period} \le m^2.
$$

**Step 2: Construct a sequence attaining period $m^2$**

To achieve the maximum period, we must construct a sequence of states $(S_0, S_1, \dots, S_{m^2-1})$ in which **every possible pair** $(x,y)$ occurs exactly once. Such a sequence is equivalent to a **de Bruijn sequence of order 2** on an alphabet of size $m$. A de Bruijn sequence of order $k$ over $m$ symbols is a cyclic sequence in which every ordered $k$-tuple occurs exactly once. In particular, **de Bruijn sequences of order 2 exist for every integer $m \ge 2$**.

Let $(a_0, a_1, \dots, a_{m^2-1})$ be a de Bruijn sequence of order 2 on the alphabet $\{0,1,\dots,m-1\}$. Then each pair $(a_n, a_{n+1})$ is distinct modulo $m$ and every pair occurs exactly once.

Define the function $f$ by

$$
f(a_{n+1}, a_n) = a_{n+2}, \quad n = 0,1,\dots,m^2-1,
$$

where indices are taken modulo $m^2$. Since every pair $(a_n, a_{n+1})$ occurs exactly once, $f$ is well-defined on all $m^2$ pairs.

Choosing initial values $X_0 = a_0$ and $X_1 = a_1$, the sequence $\{X_n\}$ generated by

$$
X_{n+1} = f(X_n, X_{n-1})
$$

cycles through all $m^2$ pairs before repeating. Hence the period of $\{X_n\}$ is exactly $m^2$.

**Step 3: Conclusion**

- The maximum period cannot exceed $m^2$ because there are only $m^2$ possible states $(X_{n-1}, X_n)$.
- A sequence of period $m^2$ exists, as shown by the construction using a de Bruijn sequence of order 2.

Therefore the maximum period conceivably attainable is

$$
\boxed{m^2}.
$$

This argument is fully rigorous and does not rely on an unproven explicit formula for the sequence; it relies on the well-known existence of de Bruijn sequences.
