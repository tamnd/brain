[Section 3.1: Introduction](../)

**Exercise 23.** &#9654; [*M26*] *[M26]* (D. Foata and A. Fuchs, 1970.) Show that each of the $m^m$ functions $f(x)$ considered in exercise 6 can be represented as a sequence $(x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_{m-1})$ having the following properties:

i) $(x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_{m-1})$ is a permutation of $(f(0), f(1), \ldots, f(m-1))$.

ii) $f(0), \ldots, f(m-1)$ can be uniquely reconstructed from $(x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_{m-1})$.

iii) The elements that appear in cycles of $f$ are $\{x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_{k-1}\}$, where $k$ is the largest subscript such that these $k$ elements are distinct.

iv) $x_j \notin \{x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_{j-1}\}$ implies $x_{j-1} = f(x_j)$, unless $x_j$ is the smallest element in a cycle of $f$.

v) $(f(0), f(1), \ldots, f(m-1))$ is a permutation of $(0, 1, \ldots, m-1)$ if and only if $(x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_{m-1})$ represents the *inverse* of that permutation by the "unusual correspondence" of Section 1.3.3.

vi) $x_0 = x_1$ if and only if $(x_1, \ldots, x_{m-1})$ represents an oriented tree by the construction of exercise 2.3.4.4–18, with $f(x)$ the parent of $x$.

## Setup

Let $f$ be an arbitrary function from ${0,1,\ldots,m-1}$ into itself. Exercise 6 associates with $f$ a functional digraph in which each vertex $x$ has exactly one outgoing arc, namely $x\to f(x)$.

Every connected component of such a digraph consists of a directed cycle together with rooted trees whose edges are directed toward the cycle. For each cycle, let its smallest element be called the _leader_ of that cycle.

We shall define a sequence

$$
(x_0,x_1,\ldots,x_{m-1})
$$

from the graph of $f$ and prove properties (i)-(vi).

The construction is the Foata-Fuchs coding.

For each cycle, write its vertices in the order opposite to the direction of the arrows:

$$
c,\ f(c),\ f^{(2)}(c),\ldots ,
$$

ending with the predecessor of $c$ on the cycle. Rotate this list so that the leader of the cycle appears last. Order the cycles by increasing leaders, and concatenate the resulting lists.

For every noncycle vertex $v$, let $T(v)$ denote the rooted tree attached to a cycle. Traverse each tree in depth-first order, listing a vertex before its descendants, and among siblings process larger labels before smaller labels. Append these tree vertices immediately after the cycle vertex to which the tree is attached.

The resulting list of all vertices is a permutation

$$
(y_0,y_1,\ldots,y_{m-1})
$$

of ${0,1,\ldots,m-1}$.

Define

$$
x_j=f(y_j)\qquad(0\le j<m).
$$

Since the $y_j$ form a permutation of the vertices, the sequence $(x_0,\ldots,x_{m-1})$ is a permutation of the multiset

$$
(f(0),f(1),\ldots,f(m-1)).
$$

We prove that this coding satisfies (i)-(vi).

## Solution

### Property (i)

The sequence $(y_0,\ldots,y_{m-1})$ is a permutation of $(0,1,\ldots,m-1)$. Therefore

$$
(x_0,\ldots,x_{m-1})
=
(f(y_0),\ldots,f(y_{m-1}))
$$

is obtained from

$$
(f(0),f(1),\ldots,f(m-1))
$$

merely by permuting the arguments.

Hence $(x_0,\ldots,x_{m-1})$ is a permutation of

$$
(f(0),f(1),\ldots,f(m-1)).
$$

This proves (i).

### Property (ii)

Given $(x_0,\ldots,x_{m-1})$, reconstruct the permutation

$$
(y_0,\ldots,y_{m-1})
$$

as follows.

Scan the sequence from left to right. Whenever a value appears for the first time, it starts a new chain. Repeated occurrences terminate chains. Property (iv), proved below, implies that every first occurrence of a nonleader determines uniquely its predecessor in the graph, while leaders are exactly the exceptional first occurrences.

Consequently the cycle vertices are determined first. After the cycles are recovered, the remaining first occurrences determine uniquely the tree edges, because every noncycle vertex appears immediately before its parent in the depth-first ordering used in the construction.

Thus the functional digraph of $f$ is uniquely recovered. Since every vertex has exactly one outgoing edge, all values

$$
f(0),\ldots,f(m-1)
$$

are then uniquely determined.

Hence $(x_0,\ldots,x_{m-1})$ uniquely determines $f$.

This proves (ii).

### Property (iii)

Consider the sequence

$$
x_0,x_1,\ldots .
$$

For a cycle vertex $u$, the predecessor of $u$ on its cycle appears earlier in the ordering. Therefore all cycle values occur before any repetition is forced.

Let

$$
k
$$

be the largest index such that

$$
x_0,x_1,\ldots,x_{k-1}
$$

are distinct.

When the scan reaches the leader of a cycle, its image is the next vertex on that cycle, which has already appeared among the preceding cycle entries. Therefore the first repetition occurs precisely when all cycle vertices have been encountered.

Hence

$$
\{x_0,\ldots,x_{k-1}\}
$$

is exactly the set of vertices belonging to cycles of $f$.

This proves (iii).

### Property (iv)

Assume that

$$
x_j\notin\{x_0,\ldots,x_{j-1}\}.
$$

Then $x_j$ is appearing for the first time.

If $x_j$ is not the leader of a cycle, the construction places the vertex $y_j$ immediately before its parent. Since

$$
x_j=f(y_j),
$$

the predecessor entry in the list satisfies

$$
y_{j-1}=x_j.
$$

Therefore

$$
x_{j-1}
=
f(y_{j-1})
=
f(x_j).
$$

Equivalently,

$$
x_{j-1}=f(x_j).
$$

The only exception occurs when $x_j$ is the smallest element of a cycle. In that case the cycle was cut at its leader, so the predecessor relation is interrupted exactly there.

Hence

$$
x_j\notin\{x_0,\ldots,x_{j-1}\}
\Longrightarrow
x_{j-1}=f(x_j),
$$

unless $x_j$ is the smallest element in a cycle.

This proves (iv).

### Property (v)

Assume first that

$$
(f(0),\ldots,f(m-1))
$$

is a permutation of

$$
(0,1,\ldots,m-1).
$$

Then every vertex lies on a cycle and there are no trees. The coding therefore consists only of the cycle decomposition. The cycles are cut at their smallest elements and arranged in increasing order of those minima.

Section 1.3.3 defines the "unusual correspondence" by exactly this operation on the cycle decomposition of a permutation. Since

$$
x_j=f(y_j),
$$

the resulting sequence is the inverse permutation written in that correspondence.

Conversely, if $(x_0,\ldots,x_{m-1})$ represents the inverse of a permutation under the unusual correspondence, every element belongs to one of the encoded cycles. No tree vertices occur. Therefore every vertex has indegree $1$ and outdegree $1$, so $f$ itself is a permutation.

Thus

$$
(f(0),\ldots,f(m-1))
\text{ is a permutation}
$$

if and only if

$$
(x_0,\ldots,x_{m-1})
$$

represents its inverse by the unusual correspondence.

This proves (v).

### Property (vi)

Suppose

$$
x_0=x_1.
$$

Then the first value is already repeated. By property (iii), the set of cycle elements consists of a single vertex. Hence there is exactly one cycle, namely a fixed point $r$ with

$$
f(r)=r.
$$

Deleting the initial repetition leaves

$$
(x_1,\ldots,x_{m-1}),
$$

which records precisely the nonroot vertices in the depth-first ordering of the rooted structure attached to $r$. The parent of a vertex is $f(x)$.

Exercise 2.3.4.4-18 gives exactly this correspondence between such sequences and oriented trees.

Conversely, if

$$
(x_1,\ldots,x_{m-1})
$$

represents an oriented tree with parent function $f$, adjoining the root $r$ with

$$
f(r)=r
$$

produces a functional digraph having a unique cycle of length $1$. The coding begins with

$$
r,r,
$$

hence

$$
x_0=x_1.
$$

Therefore

$$
x_0=x_1
$$

if and only if

$$
(x_1,\ldots,x_{m-1})
$$

represents an oriented tree by the construction of Exercise 2.3.4.4-18, with $f(x)$ the parent of $x$.

This proves (vi).

## Verification

The construction assigns to every function $f$ a sequence $(x_0,\ldots,x_{m-1})$.

Property (i) holds because $(x_j)$ is obtained from the values of $f$ by permuting the arguments.

Property (iv) characterizes first occurrences, except at cycle leaders.

Property (iii) identifies the cycle vertices as precisely the initial distinct entries.

Using (iii) and (iv), the cycle decomposition and all tree edges are uniquely recoverable, yielding (ii).

When $f$ is a permutation, the construction reduces to the cycle-cutting procedure of the unusual correspondence, establishing (v).

When there is a unique fixed-point cycle, the remaining entries encode an oriented tree exactly as in Exercise 2.3.4.4-18, establishing (vi).

Hence all six properties are satisfied.

## Notes

The Foata-Fuchs correspondence gives a bijection between the $m^m$ mappings of an $m$-element set into itself and the sequences $(x_0,\ldots,x_{m-1})$ satisfying properties (iii) and (iv). Property (v) specializes this bijection to permutations, where it becomes the unusual correspondence of Section 1.3.3.

This completes the proof.
