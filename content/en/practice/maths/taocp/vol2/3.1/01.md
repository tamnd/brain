**Exercise 1.** &#9654; [*20*] Suppose that you wish to obtain a decimal digit at random, not using a computer. Which of the following methods would be suitable?

a) Open a telephone directory to a random place by sticking your finger in it somewhere, and use the units digit of the first number found on the selected page.
b) Same as (a), but use the units digit of the *page* number.
c) Roll a die that is in the shape of a regular icosahedron, whose twenty faces have been labeled with the digits 0, 0, 1, 1, …, 9, 9. Use the digit that appears on top, when the die comes to rest. (A felt-covered table with a hard surface is recommended for rolling dice.)
d) Expose a geiger counter to a source of radioactivity for one minute (shielding yourself) and use the units digit of the resulting count. Assume that the geiger counter displays the number of counts in decimal notation, and that the count is initially zero.
e) Glance at your wristwatch; and if the position of the second-hand is between $6n$ and $6(n+1)$ seconds, choose the digit $n$.
f) Ask a friend to think of a random digit, and use the digit he names.
g) Ask an enemy to think of a random digit, and use the digit he names.
h) Assume that 10 horses are entered in a race and that you know nothing whatever about their qualifications. Assign to these horses the digits 0 to 9, in arbitrary fashion, and after the race use the winner's digit.

## Solution

The desired outcome is a digit distributed as uniformly as possible on the set ${0,1,\ldots,9}$. Each method must therefore be judged according to whether the resulting digit is plausibly unbiased and not systematically influenced by nonrandom factors.

For (a), the units digit of a telephone number is generally suitable. Telephone numbers are assigned for administrative reasons that have little relation to the selection procedure, and the final digit tends to be approximately uniformly distributed. Randomly opening the directory effectively samples from a large collection of such numbers. Hence this method is suitable.

For (b), the units digit of a page number is not suitable. Consecutive pages have consecutive page numbers, so the final digits occur in a highly structured periodic pattern. Furthermore, the procedure of opening a book at random does not generally make all pages equally likely. The resulting distribution need not be uniform.

For (c), the method is suitable provided that the icosahedral die is fair. There are twenty faces, and each digit occurs on exactly two faces. Since each face has probability $\frac1{20}$,

$$
P(\text{digit }d)=\frac2{20}=\frac1{10},
$$

for every $d\in{0,1,\ldots,9}$. Thus the distribution is exactly uniform.

For (d), the method is suitable. Radioactive decay is a standard physical source of randomness. If the count after one minute is large, its units digit is typically very close to uniformly distributed among the ten possibilities. No deterministic bias toward particular digits is expected.

For (e), the method is generally unsuitable. The position of the second hand when one chooses to look at the watch is influenced by human behavior and is not known to be uniformly distributed over the minute. A person tends to glance at a watch at particular times rather than at random instants, introducing possible bias.

For (f), the method is unsuitable. People do not choose digits uniformly at random. Experiments repeatedly show strong preferences for certain digits and avoidance of others. The resulting distribution is biased.

For (g), the method is also unsuitable. An enemy may intentionally choose digits nonuniformly in order to influence the result. Even if no deliberate manipulation occurs, the same psychological biases as in part (f) remain.

For (h), the method is unsuitable. The winner of a horse race is not uniformly distributed among the ten horses unless the horses have exactly equal chances of winning. Ignorance of their qualifications does not imply equal probabilities. Assigning digits to the horses therefore does not produce a uniform random digit.

Hence the suitable methods are (a), (c), and (d).

$$
\boxed{\text{Suitable methods: (a), (c), and (d).}}
$$
