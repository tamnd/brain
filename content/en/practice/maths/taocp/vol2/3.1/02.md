**Exercise 2.** [*M22*] In a random sequence of a million decimal digits, what is the probability that there are exactly 100,000 of each possible digit?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 32m01s  

---

## Solution

Let $X_i$ denote the number of occurrences of digit $i$ in a random sequence of $1{,}000{,}000$ decimal digits. The total number of possible sequences is

$$
10^{1{,}000{,}000},
$$

since each position can contain any of the ten digits.

The event in question is

$$
X_0=X_1=\cdots=X_9=100{,}000.
$$

To count the favorable sequences, choose the positions of the digits.

First choose the $100{,}000$ positions occupied by digit $0$; then choose $100{,}000$ of the remaining positions for digit $1$; continue similarly through digit $9$. The number of favorable sequences is therefore

$$
\binom{1{,}000{,}000}{100{,}000}
\binom{900{,}000}{100{,}000}
\cdots
\binom{100{,}000}{100{,}000}
=
\frac{(1{,}000{,}000)!}{(100{,}000!)^{10}}.
$$

Hence the required probability is

$$
P=
\frac{(1{,}000{,}000)!}
     {(100{,}000!)^{10}\,10^{1{,}000{,}000}}.
$$

Using Stirling's formula,

$$
n!\sim \sqrt{2\pi n}\left(\frac{n}{e}\right)^n,
$$

we obtain

$$
\begin{aligned}
P
&\sim
\frac{
\sqrt{2\pi\cdot10^6}\,(10^6/e)^{10^6}
}
{
\left[
\sqrt{2\pi\cdot10^5}\,(10^5/e)^{10^5}
\right]^{10}
10^{10^6}
}.
\end{aligned}
$$

The factors involving $e$ cancel. Since

$$
(10^5)^{10^5\cdot10}=(10^5)^{10^6},
$$

and

$$
\frac{(10^6)^{10^6}}
     {(10^5)^{10^6}10^{10^6}}
=
1,
$$

the exponential terms also cancel. Thus

$$
\begin{aligned}
P
&\sim
\frac{\sqrt{2\pi\cdot10^6}}
     {(2\pi\cdot10^5)^5}  \\
&=
\frac{10^3\sqrt{2\pi}}
     {10^{25}(2\pi)^5} \\
&=
\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{9/2}\,10^{22}}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Numerically,

$$
(2\pi)^{9/2}\approx 3906.99,
$$

so

$$
P\approx 2.56\times10^{-26}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\boxed{
P=
\frac{(1{,}000{,}000)!}
     {(100{,}000!)^{10}\,10^{1{,}000{,}000}}
\approx 2.56\times10^{-26}
}.
$$
