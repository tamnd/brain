**Exercise 3.1.15** [M15] If $f(x)$ is chosen at random in the sense of exercise 11, what is the probability that none of the final cycles has length 1, regardless of the choice of $X_0$?

**Solution.**

We consider a random mapping $f: \{0,1,\dots,n-1\} \to \{0,1,\dots,n-1\}$. By "chosen at random in the sense of exercise 11", this means that each of the $n^n$ functions is equally likely. We are asked for the probability that _none of the final cycles has length 1_, regardless of the choice of starting value $X_0$.

### Step 1: Translate the problem

A _final cycle_ is a cycle reached after following $f$ repeatedly from some $X_0$. A final cycle of length 1 is a fixed point. The problem asks for the probability that _no final cycle has length 1_. Equivalently:

- Every element of the domain eventually reaches a cycle of length at least 2.
- There are no fixed points in the mapping.

Thus, the event of interest is exactly the event that $f$ has **no fixed points**.

### Step 2: Count the number of mappings with no fixed points

The total number of functions $f: \{0,1,\dots,n-1\} \to \{0,1,\dots,n-1\}$ is

$$
n^n.
$$

We now count the number of functions with **no fixed points**, that is, functions such that $f(x) \neq x$ for all $x$.

- For each element $x \in \{0,\dots,n-1\}$, there are $n-1$ choices for $f(x)$ (any value except $x$).
- These choices are independent for each $x$, so the total number of functions with no fixed points is

$$
(n-1)^n.
$$

### Step 3: Compute the probability

The probability that a randomly chosen function has no fixed points is

$$
\frac{(n-1)^n}{n^n} = \left(\frac{n-1}{n}\right)^n.
$$

### Step 4: Justification regarding final cycles

Since every sequence of iterates eventually reaches a cycle (this is a standard fact for finite mappings), the absence of fixed points ensures that all final cycles have length at least 2. Therefore, requiring that _no final cycle has length 1_ is exactly equivalent to requiring that the function has no fixed points. There is no dependence on the choice of starting value $X_0$, so the probability computed above applies.

### Step 5: Conclusion

$$
\boxed{\left(\frac{n-1}{n}\right)^n}
$$

is the probability that a randomly chosen mapping $f: \{0,1,\dots,n-1\} \to \{0,1,\dots,n-1\}$ has no final cycles of length 1.

**Answer:**

$$
\displaystyle \left(\frac{n-1}{n}\right)^n
$$

This formula is exact and valid for all $n \ge 1$.
