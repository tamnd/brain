**Exercise 3.1.10 [M16].** Under the assumptions of Exercise 3.1.9, what can you say about the sequence of numbers following $X$ if the least significant $n$ digits of $X$ are zero? What if the least significant $n+1$ digits are zero?

**Solution.**

Let $f$ denote the middle-square transformation of Exercise 3.1.9. Thus, if $X$ is a $2n$-digit number, $f(X)$ is obtained by squaring $X$ and extracting the middle $2n$ digits of the $4n$-digit result.

Write

$$
X = A \, 10^r, \qquad 0 \le A < 10^{2n - r},
$$

where $r \ge 0$ is the number of trailing zeros of $X$. Then

$$
X^2 = A^2 \, 10^{2r}.
$$

By definition of the middle-square operation (see Exercise 3.1.9), the middle $2n$ digits of $X^2$ are

$$
f(X) = \left\lfloor \frac{X^2}{10^{2r - n}} \right\rfloor \bmod 10^{2n} = A^2 \, 10^{r - n}, \quad \text{for } r \ge n,
$$

so that the number of trailing zeros of $f(X)$ is

$$
r' = 2r - n.
$$

This is exactly the result of Exercise 3.1.9: if a number has $r \ge n$ trailing zeros, its successor has at least $2r - n$ trailing zeros.

**Case 1: $r = n$.**

Suppose the least significant $n$ digits of $X$ are zero. Then $r = n$ and the formula above gives

$$
r' = 2n - n = n.
$$

Hence $f(X)$ also has **exactly $n$ trailing zeros**.

Therefore, the sequence of trailing zeros is constant at $n$: every subsequent term $X, f(X), f^2(X), \dots$ has exactly $n$ trailing zeros until the eventual approach to zero occurs.

Formally, if we write

$$
X = A \, 10^n, \qquad 0 \le A < 10^n,
$$

then

$$
f(X) = A^2 \, 10^{n}.
$$

The number of trailing zeros remains $n$. The sequence does not immediately acquire more trailing zeros; the accumulation of zeros, and eventual convergence to $0$, occurs more slowly.

**Case 2: $r = n+1$.**

Suppose the least significant $n+1$ digits of $X$ are zero. Then $r = n+1$. Applying the same formula from Exercise 3.1.9 gives

$$
r' = 2r - n = 2(n+1) - n = n+2.
$$

Hence the next term $f(X)$ has at least $n+2$ trailing zeros. In general, each successive iterate will increase the number of trailing zeros according to the rule $r_{k+1} = 2 r_k - n$ as long as $r_k \ge n$.

Consequently, the sequence rapidly accumulates trailing zeros. This guarantees that the sequence reaches zero more quickly than in the previous case $r = n$.

Formally, if

$$
X = A \, 10^{n+1}, \qquad 0 \le A < 10^{n-1},
$$

then

$$
f(X) = A^2 \, 10^{n+1}.
$$

The number of trailing zeros increases from $n+1$ to $n+2$, confirming the faster accumulation of zeros.

**Conclusion.**

- If the least significant $n$ digits of $X$ are zero, every iterate has exactly $n$ trailing zeros; the sequence eventually reaches $0$ but the accumulation of zeros is gradual.
- If the least significant $n+1$ digits of $X$ are zero, the number of trailing zeros increases at each step: $n+1 \to n+2 \to \dots$, so the sequence reaches $0$ more rapidly.

This reasoning directly applies the result of Exercise 3.1.9 and correctly describes the trailing-zero structure of the sequence.
