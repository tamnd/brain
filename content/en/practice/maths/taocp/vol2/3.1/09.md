**Exercise 9.** [*M14*] [M14] Prove that the middle-square method using $2n$-digit numbers to the base $b$ has the following disadvantage: If the sequence includes any number whose most significant $n$ digits are zero, the succeeding numbers will get smaller and smaller until zero occurs repeatedly.

**Solve time:** 32m42s

## Solution

Let a number in the middle-square method have $2n$ digits in base $b$, and let $X_k$ denote the $k$th number in the sequence. Denote the digits of $X_k$ as

$X_k = d_{2n-1}d_{2n-2}\dots d_1 d_0$

where $d_{2n-1}$ is the most significant digit and $d_0$ the least significant digit. The middle-square method proceeds by squaring $X_k$ and then selecting the middle $2n$ digits of the resulting $4n$-digit number to obtain $X_{k+1}$.

Suppose that, for some $k$, the most significant $n$ digits of $X_k$ are all zero. Write

$X_k = 0 \cdot b^n + r_k,$

where $0 \le r_k < b^n$ represents the lower $n$ digits of $X_k$. Then

$X_k^2 = (0 \cdot b^n + r_k)^2 = r_k^2 < b^{2n}.$

Thus, $X_k^2$ has at most $2n$ digits; in particular, its $n$ most significant digits are zero. When the middle $2n$ digits are extracted to form $X_{k+1}$, these digits consist of the most significant $n$ digits of $r_k^2$ followed by the least significant $n$ digits of $r_k^2$. Both are strictly less than $r_k$, because $r_k^2 < b^{2n}$. Consequently,

$X_{k+1} < X_k.$

Iterating this argument, if $X_{k+1}$ again has most significant $n$ digits equal to zero, the next number $X_{k+2}$ satisfies

$X_{k+2} < X_{k+1} < X_k.$

Since the sequence is nonnegative and strictly decreasing whenever the most significant $n$ digits are zero, the sequence must eventually reach zero. Once $X_m = 0$, the next number is

$X_{m+1} = 0^2 = 0,$

and the sequence remains at zero indefinitely. Therefore, any occurrence of a number whose $n$ most significant digits are zero inevitably causes the sequence to decrease until it reaches the absorbing value $0$, which then repeats forever.

This completes the proof.
