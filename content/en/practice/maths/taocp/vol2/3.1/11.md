**Exercise 11.** &#9654; [**] [M26] Consider sequences of random number generators having the form described in exercise 6. If we choose $f(x)$ and $X_0$ at random, in other words, if we assume that each of the $m^m$ possible functions $f(x)$ is equally probable and that each of the $m$ possible values of $X_0$ is equally probable, what is the probability that the sequence will eventually degenerate into a cycle of length $\lambda = 1$? [*Note:* The assumptions of this problem give a natural way to think of a "random" random number generator; and Algorithm K may be expected to behave somewhat like the generator considered here; the answer to this problem gives a measure of how colossal the coincidence of Table 1 really is.]

Let

$$
X_{n+1}=f(X_n),\qquad X_n\in\{1,\ldots,m\},
$$

where $f$ is chosen uniformly from the $m^m$ mappings of $\{1,\ldots,m\}$ into itself, and $X_0$ is chosen uniformly from the $m$ possible starting values.

We must determine the probability that the eventual cycle reached by the orbit of $X_0$ has length $1$.

A cycle of length $1$ is a fixed point. Thus we count pairs $(f,X_0)$ for which the connected component of $X_0$ in the functional digraph of $f$ is a rooted tree whose root is a fixed point.

## Counting

For every such pair $(f,X_0)$, let $a$ be the fixed point eventually reached, and let $S$ be the set of vertices whose iterates reach $a$. Put

$$
|S|=t.
$$

Then:

1. $a\in S$, and $S$ is exactly the connected component containing $a$.
2. On $S$, the mapping $f$ is a rooted tree directed toward $a$, together with the loop $a\mapsto a$.
3. No vertex outside $S$ may map into $S$, otherwise it would also belong to $S$.

Hence the complement $S^c$ is closed under $f$.

Fix $t$.

### Choice of $a$ and $S$

There are

$$
m\binom{m-1}{t-1}
$$

ways to choose the fixed point $a$ and the set $S$ of size $t$.

### Mapping on $S$

A rooted tree on the $t$ labeled vertices of $S$ with prescribed root $a$ is obtained from an ordinary labeled tree by orienting every edge toward $a$.

By Cayley's formula, the number of labeled trees on $t$ vertices is

$$
t^{\,t-2}.
$$

Therefore the number of possible restrictions $f|_S$ is $t^{t-2}$.

### Mapping on the complement

Since $S^c$ must be closed under $f$, every vertex of $S^c$ must map into $S^c$.

Thus the restriction $f|_{S^c}$ is an arbitrary mapping of a set of size $m-t$ into itself, giving

$$
(m-t)^{\,m-t}
$$

possibilities.

### Choice of $X_0$

Any of the $t$ vertices of $S$ may serve as $X_0$.

Therefore the number of triples $(f,a,X_0)$ with $|S|=t$ is

$$
m\binom{m-1}{t-1}
t^{\,t-2}
(m-t)^{\,m-t}
t.
$$

Since $a$ is uniquely determined by $(f,X_0)$, this is also the number of pairs $(f,X_0)$ having $|S|=t$.

Summing over $t$ gives

$$
N
=
m\sum_{t=1}^{m}
\binom{m-1}{t-1}
t^{\,t-1}
(m-t)^{\,m-t}.
$$

The total number of pairs $(f,X_0)$ is

$$
m^{m+1}.
$$

Hence

$$
\boxed{
P(\lambda=1)
=
\frac1{m^m}
\sum_{t=1}^{m}
\binom{m-1}{t-1}
t^{\,t-1}
(m-t)^{\,m-t}
}.
$$

This is the exact probability.

## Asymptotics

For $t=o(m)$,

$$
\binom{m-1}{t-1}
=
\frac{m^{t-1}}{(t-1)!}
\left(1+O\!\left(\frac{t^2}{m}\right)\right),
$$

and

$$
\frac{(m-t)^{m-t}}{m^m}
=
m^{-t}e^{-t}
\left(1+O\!\left(\frac{t^2}{m}\right)\right).
$$

Therefore

$$
\frac1{m^m}
\binom{m-1}{t-1}
t^{t-1}
(m-t)^{m-t}
=
\frac1m
\frac{t^{t-1}e^{-t}}{(t-1)!}
\left(1+O\!\left(\frac{t^2}{m}\right)\right).
$$

Using Stirling's formula,

$$
\frac{t^{t-1}e^{-t}}{(t-1)!}
\sim
\frac1{\sqrt{2\pi t}}.
$$

A standard singularity-analysis argument for this series, equivalently the classical asymptotics for rooted labeled trees, yields

$$
\sum_{t=1}^{m}
\frac{t^{t-1}e^{-t}}{(t-1)!}
\sim
\sqrt{\frac{\pi m}{2}}.
$$

Substituting into the exact formula gives

$$
\boxed{
P(\lambda=1)
\sim
\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2m}}
\qquad (m\to\infty).
}
$$

Thus a randomly chosen mapping and starting value lead to a fixed point with probability asymptotic to $\sqrt{\pi/(2m)}$. This is the quantity requested in the exercise.
