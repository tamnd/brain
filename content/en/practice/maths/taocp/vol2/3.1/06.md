**Exercise 6.** &#9654; [*M21*] Suppose that we want to generate a sequence of integers $X_0, X_1, X_2, \ldots$ in the range $0 \le X_n < m$, using a function such that $0 \le x < m$ implies $0 \le f(x) < m$. Consider a sequence formed by the rule $X_{n+1} = f(X_n)$. (Examples are the middle-square method and Algorithm K.)

a) Show that the sequence is ultimately periodic, in the sense there exist numbers $\lambda$ and $\mu$ for which the values

$$X_0, \ X_1, \ \ldots, \ X_\mu, \ \ldots, \ X_{\lambda+\mu-1}$$

are distinct, but $X_{\lambda+\mu} = X_\mu$ when $n \ge \mu$. Find the maximum and minimum possible values of $\mu$ and $\lambda$.

b) (R. W. Floyd.) Show that there exists an $n > 0$ such that $X_n = X_{2n}$; and the smallest such value of $n$ lies in the range $\mu \le n \le \lambda + \mu$. Furthermore the value of $X_n$ is unique in the sense that if $X_n = X_{2n'}$ and $X_{n'} = X_{2n'}$, then $X_{n'} = X_n$.

c) Use the ideas of (b) to design an algorithm that calculates $\mu$ and $\lambda$ for any given function $f$ and any given $X_0$, using only $O(\mu + \lambda)$ steps and only a bounded number of memory locations.

## Solution

### a)

The sequence takes its values from the finite set

$$
\{0,1,\ldots,m-1\},
$$

which contains exactly $m$ elements. Hence among the $m+1$ terms

$$
X_0,X_1,\ldots,X_m
$$

there must be two equal values. Let $j<k$ be the first pair of indices for which

$$
X_j=X_k.
$$

Since $X_{n+1}=f(X_n)$, induction on $r\ge0$ gives

$$
X_{j+r}=X_{k+r}.
$$

Let

$$
\mu=j,\qquad \lambda=k-j.
$$

We claim that

$$
X_0,X_1,\ldots,X_{\mu+\lambda-1}
$$

are distinct. For if $0\le a<b<\mu+\lambda=k$ and $X_a=X_b$, then $(a,b)$ would be a repeated pair with $b<k$, contradicting the choice of $(j,k)$ as the first repeated pair.

Furthermore,

$$
X_{\mu+\lambda}=X_k=X_j=X_\mu.
$$

Applying $f$ repeatedly yields

$$
X_{\mu+\lambda+r}=X_{\mu+r}\qquad(r\ge0).
$$

Thus the sequence is ultimately periodic, with a tail of length $\mu$ followed by a cycle of length $\lambda$.

Since the $\mu+\lambda$ values

$$
X_0,X_1,\ldots,X_{\mu+\lambda-1}
$$

are distinct and all lie in a set of size $m$,

$$
\mu+\lambda\le m.
$$

Because $\lambda\ge1$,

$$
0\le\mu\le m-1,
\qquad
1\le\lambda\le m.
$$

These bounds are attainable.

- Minimum $\mu$: Take $f(x)=x$. Then $\mu=0$, $\lambda=1$.
- Maximum $\mu$: Let

$$
X_0\to X_1\to\cdots\to X_{m-1},
$$

with $X_{m-1}$ a fixed point. Then $\mu=m-1$, $\lambda=1$.

- Minimum $\lambda$: Any fixed point cycle gives $\lambda=1$.
- Maximum $\lambda$: Take

$$
f(x)\equiv x+1 \pmod m.
$$

Then every state lies on one cycle, so $\mu=0$ and $\lambda=m$.

Hence

$$
0\le\mu\le m-1,
\qquad
1\le\lambda\le m.
$$

### b)

Let $\mu$ and $\lambda$ be as in part (a). Then

$$
X_{\mu+r+\lambda}=X_{\mu+r}
\qquad(r\ge0),
$$

so for all indices $s,t\ge\mu$,

$$
X_s=X_t
\iff
s\equiv t\pmod\lambda.
$$

We first show that there exists $n>0$ with $X_n=X_{2n}$.

Choose $n$ to be the smallest positive multiple of $\lambda$ satisfying

$$
n\ge\mu.
$$

Then

$$
\mu\le n\le \mu+\lambda-1.
$$

Since $n\ge\mu$, both $n$ and $2n$ lie on the cycle. Therefore

$$
X_n=X_{2n}
\iff
n\equiv 2n \pmod\lambda.
$$

But $n\equiv0\pmod\lambda$, so indeed

$$
X_n=X_{2n}.
$$

Thus such an $n$ exists.

Now let $n_0$ be the smallest positive integer satisfying

$$
X_{n_0}=X_{2n_0}.
$$

We prove that

$$
\mu\le n_0\le\mu+\lambda.
$$

First, $n_0\ge\mu$. Suppose instead that $n_0<\mu$. Since $2n_0>\!n_0$ and

$$
2n_0\le2(\mu-1)<\mu+\lambda
$$

because $\lambda\ge1$, both indices $n_0$ and $2n_0$ lie in the range

$$
0,\ldots,\mu+\lambda-1.
$$

But the values

$$
X_0,X_1,\ldots,X_{\mu+\lambda-1}
$$

are distinct, contradicting $X_{n_0}=X_{2n_0}$. Hence $n_0\ge\mu$.

Since $n_0\ge\mu$, both $n_0$ and $2n_0$ are on the cycle. Therefore

$$
X_{n_0}=X_{2n_0}
\iff
n_0\equiv2n_0\pmod\lambda,
$$

hence

$$
n_0\equiv0\pmod\lambda.
$$

Thus every solution is a positive multiple of $\lambda$ that is at least $\mu$. The smallest such multiple is exactly the $n$ constructed above. Therefore

$$
\mu\le n_0\le\mu+\lambda-1<\mu+\lambda.
$$

In particular,

$$
\mu\le n_0\le\mu+\lambda.
$$

Finally, suppose

$$
X_n=X_{2n},
\qquad
X_{n'}=X_{2n'}.
$$

From the preceding argument, both $n$ and $n'$ are multiples of $\lambda$ and satisfy

$$
n,n'\ge\mu.
$$

Hence

$$
n\equiv n'\equiv0\pmod\lambda.
$$

Since both indices lie on the cycle,

$$
X_n=X_\mu,
\qquad
X_{n'}=X_\mu.
$$

Therefore

$$
X_n=X_{n'}.
$$

Thus the value $X_n$ is unique.

### c)

Use two copies of the sequence, one advancing at twice the speed of the other.

#### Phase 1: Find a point on the cycle

Set

$$
T\leftarrow f(X_0),\qquad H\leftarrow f(f(X_0)).
$$

Repeatedly perform

$$
T\leftarrow f(T),\qquad
H\leftarrow f(f(H))
$$

until $T=H$.

After $k$ iterations,

$$
T=X_k,\qquad H=X_{2k}.
$$

Once $k\ge\mu$, both pointers are on the cycle. Their positions on the cycle differ by $k$ steps. At each iteration this difference increases by $1$ modulo $\lambda$. Hence within at most $\lambda$ additional iterations the difference becomes $0$, and the pointers meet.

Therefore a meeting occurs after at most $\mu+\lambda$ iterations.

Let the common value at the meeting be $Y$.

#### Phase 2: Determine $\mu$

Set

$$
T\leftarrow X_0,\qquad
H\leftarrow Y,\qquad
\mu\leftarrow0.
$$

Repeatedly perform

$$
T\leftarrow f(T),\qquad
H\leftarrow f(H),\qquad
\mu\leftarrow\mu+1
$$

until $T=H$.

The pointer $H$ starts on the cycle. The pointer $T$ reaches the cycle entrance after exactly $\mu$ steps. At that moment both pointers are at $X_\mu$. No earlier equality is possible because

$$
X_0,\ldots,X_{\mu+\lambda-1}
$$

are distinct before the cycle entrance is reached.

Hence the resulting count is the desired value $\mu$.

#### Phase 3: Determine $\lambda$

Keep

$$
T=X_\mu.
$$

Set

$$
H=f(T),\qquad \lambda\leftarrow1.
$$

Repeatedly perform

$$
H\leftarrow f(H),\qquad
\lambda\leftarrow\lambda+1
$$

until $H=T$.

Since $T$ lies on a cycle of length $\lambda$, the first return to $T$ occurs after exactly $\lambda$ applications of $f$. Therefore the resulting count is the period length.

#### Complexity

Phase 1 requires $O(\mu+\lambda)$ iterations.

Phase 2 requires exactly $\mu$ iterations.

Phase 3 requires exactly $\lambda$ iterations.

Hence the total work is

$$
O(\mu+\lambda).
$$

Only a constant number of variables are stored, namely the two pointers and the counters $\mu$ and $\lambda$. Thus the algorithm uses a bounded number of memory locations.

Therefore $\mu$ and $\lambda$ can be computed in $O(\mu+\lambda)$ time and $O(1)$ memory. ∎
