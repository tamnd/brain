## Exercise 4.6.1.6 [_M29_]

## Setup

We are asked whether the _binary gcd algorithm_ (Algorithm 4.5.2B), originally formulated for integers, can be adapted to compute the greatest common divisor of polynomials over a field or, more generally, over a unique factorization domain.

Let $u(x)$ and $v(x)$ be nonzero polynomials over a unique factorization domain $S$, with $\deg(u) = m$, $\deg(v) = n$, and assume $m \ge n$. Denote by $\gcd(u, v)$ a greatest common divisor of $u(x)$ and $v(x)$ in the sense of Section 4.6.1. The binary gcd algorithm for integers relies on three key operations:

1. Division by $2$, equivalently extracting powers of $2$ from both integers.
2. Subtraction of the smaller integer from the larger.
3. Repetition until equality or zero is reached.

We must determine whether analogous operations exist for polynomials and whether an iterative procedure, similar to the binary gcd algorithm, can correctly compute $\gcd(u, v)$.

We also note that over polynomials, Algorithm D provides standard Euclidean division: for $u(x)$ and $v(x) \ne 0$, there exist $q(x)$ and $r(x)$ such that

$u(x) = q(x) v(x) + r(x), \qquad \deg(r) < \deg(v).$

Euclid's algorithm (4.5.2A) uses successive remainders to compute $\gcd(u, v)$.

## Solution

The binary gcd algorithm for integers is based on exploiting the factorization

$\gcd(u, v) = 2^{\min(\nu_2(u), \nu_2(v))} \gcd(u/2^{\nu_2(u)}, v/2^{\nu_2(v)}),$

where $\nu_2(w)$ denotes the exponent of $2$ in $w$. Subtraction of integers then replaces division by general integers.

For polynomials over a field $S$, there is no distinguished prime analogous to $2$ in the integers. One could attempt to define an analogous operation by factoring out powers of a fixed irreducible polynomial $p(x)$. Let $\nu_{p(x)}(u)$ denote the largest $k$ such that $p(x)^k$ divides $u(x)$. Then

$\gcd(u, v) = p(x)^{\min(\nu_{p(x)}(u), \nu_{p(x)}(v))} \gcd(u/p(x)^{\nu_{p(x)}(u)}, v/p(x)^{\nu_{p(x)}(v)}).$

This is formally analogous to the integer case, but crucially, there is no _single distinguished irreducible polynomial_ that divides all polynomials as $2$ divides even integers. Polynomials over a field have infinitely many irreducible factors, and there is no canonical choice. Any attempt to extract powers of "some polynomial" requires prior factorization, which is generally as hard as computing the gcd itself.

The second operation of integer subtraction, $|u - v|$, is well-defined for polynomials as $u(x) - v(x)$. However, in the integer binary gcd algorithm, repeated subtraction eventually reduces the larger integer below the smaller one, relying on the fact that integers are totally ordered.
