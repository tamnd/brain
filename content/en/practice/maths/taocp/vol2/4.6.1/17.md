**Exercise 17.** [*M32*] *(P. M. Cohn's algorithm for division of string polynomials.)* Let $A$ be an *alphabet*, that is, a set of symbols. A *string* $\alpha$ on $A$ is a sequence of $n \ge 0$ symbols, $\alpha = a_1 \ldots a_n$, where each $a_j$ is in $A$. The *length* of $\alpha$, denoted by $|\alpha|$, is the number $n$ of symbols. A *string polynomial* over $A$ is a finite sum $U = \sum_k r_k \alpha_k$, where each $r_k$ is a nonzero rational number and each $\alpha_k$ is a string on $A$. The *degree* of $U$, $\deg(U)$, is defined to be $-\infty$ if $U$ is empty (that is, $U$ has no terms), and $\max_k |\alpha_k|$ otherwise; thus $j \ne k$. The *degree* of $U$, $\deg(U)$, is defined to be $-\infty$ if $U$ is empty (that is, $U$ has no terms), and $\max_k |\alpha_k|$ otherwise. We assume that $j \ne k$ implies that $\alpha_j \ne \alpha_k$, so we can say that $\deg(U) = \max\{|\alpha_k|\}$. The *leading degree* of $U$, $\text{ldeg}(U)$, is defined to be $-\infty$ if $U$ is empty (that is, $U$ has no terms), and $\max_k |\alpha_k|$ otherwise. We assume that $j \ne k$ implies that $\alpha_j \ne \alpha_k$, so we can say that $\text{ldeg}(U) = \max\{|\alpha_k|\}$ with equality in the definition. For example, if $A = \{a, b\}$, $U = ab + ba - 2a - 2b$, then $\deg(U) = 2$, $\deg(V) = 1$, $V^2 = 1$ (strings have length 1), $V^2 = ab + ba + bb - 2a - 2b + 1$. Clearly $\deg(UV) = \deg(U) + \deg(V)$, and $\deg(U + V) \le \max(\deg(U), \deg(V))$, with equality in the definition when $\deg(U) \ne \deg(V)$. (String polynomials may be regarded as ordinary multivariate polynomials over the field of rational numbers, except that the variables are *not commutative* under multiplication. In the conventional language of

pure mathematics, the set of string polynomials with the operations defined here is the "free associative algebra" generated by $A$ over the rationals.)

a) Let $Q_1, Q_2, U$, and $V$ be string polynomials with $\deg(U) \ge \deg(V)$ and such that $\deg(Q_1U - Q_2V) < \deg(Q_1U)$. Give an algorithm to find a string polynomial $Q$ such that $\deg(Q - QV) < \deg(Q_1U)$. (Thus if we are given $U$ and $V$ such that $Q_1U = Q_2V + R$ and $\deg(R) < \deg(Q_1U)$, for some $Q_1$ and $Q_2$, then there is a solution to these conditions with $Q_2 = 1$.)

b) Given that $U$ and $V$ are string polynomials with $\deg(V) > \deg(Q_1U - Q_2V)$ for some $Q_1$ and $Q_2$, show that the result of (a) can be improved to find a quotient $Q$ such that $U = QV + R$, $\deg(R) < \deg(V)$. (This is the analog of (1) for string polynomials; part (a) showed that we can make $\deg(R) < \deg(U)$, under weaker hypotheses.)

c) A *homogeneous polynomial* is one whose terms all have the same degree (length). If $U_1, U_2, V_1, V_2$ are homogeneous string polynomials with $U_1V_1 = U_2V_2$ and $\deg(V_1) \ge \deg(V_2)$, show that there is a homogeneous string polynomial $U$ such that $U_2 = U_1U$ and $V_1 = UV_2$.

d) Given that $U$ and $V$ are homogeneous string polynomials with $UV = VU$, prove that there is a homogeneous string polynomial $W$ such that $U = zW^m$, $V = zW^n$ for some integers $m$, $n$ and rational numbers $r$, $s$. Give an algorithm to compute such a $W$ having the largest possible degree. (This algorithm is of interest, for example, when $U = \alpha$ and $V = \beta$ are strings satisfying $\alpha\beta = \beta\alpha$; then $W$ is simply a string $\gamma$. When $U = z^m$ and $V = z^n$, the solution of largest degree is the string $W = z^{\gcd(m,n)}$, so this algorithm includes a gcd algorithm for integers as a special case.)

The central object is a formal sum of words over an alphabet with rational coefficients. Multiplication is concatenation of words extended bilinearly. The key structural fact is that the degree is the maximum word length appearing in the support, and multiplication is compatible with degree additively on leading terms. ## Problem Understanding

We are working in an algebra where elements are finite linear combinations of words over a fixed alphabet, and multiplication is concatenation of words, not commutative. The â€śsizeâ€ť of an element is determined only by the longest word appearing in it.

The key operation in the exercise is controlled cancellation of leading words. When two string polynomials are multiplied, the highest degree terms come only from concatenating longest words, so degree behaves predictably. This makes it possible to imitate division: we can attempt to eliminate leading words of a polynomial by subtracting suitable shifted copies of another polynomial.

The input to each part is not numeric data but algebraic expressions, and the output is either a quotient-like object or a structural decomposition. The constraints are implicit: everything is finite, so only finitely many words appear, and every operation reduces either degree or number of leading terms.

A naive approach would attempt to treat these objects as commutative polynomials, but that immediately breaks because concatenation depends on order. For example, even if two expressions share the same degree pattern, their leading words may be different strings, so cancellation must happen word-by-word, not degree-by-degree alone.

A second failure mode is assuming uniqueness of factorization at the coefficient level. In this algebra, cancellation depends on exact word matches, so one must track leading words explicitly.

## Approaches

The correct perspective is that Cohnâ€™s algorithm is a noncommutative analogue of long division. Instead of cancelling highest powers of a variable, we cancel highest-length words, one word at a time, by matching prefixes.

In part (a), we are given a relation where a leading cancellation already occurs between two products $Q_1U$ and $Q_2V$. The condition $\deg(U) \ge \deg(V)$ ensures that when aligning leading words, copies of $V$ can be used to eliminate leading contributions of $U$ after suitable shifts. The goal is to reconstruct a single quotient $Q$ that achieves the same effect without the auxiliary multiplier $Q_1$.

The key idea is that the difference $Q_1U - Q_2V$ having strictly smaller degree means the leading words of $Q_1U$ are fully canceled by those of $Q_2V$. This forces a rigid alignment between leading words of $U$ and shifted copies of $V$. From this alignment, one can reconstruct $Q$ inductively by matching leading words of $Q_1U$ with those of $V$, peeling off contributions one by one. Each cancellation step reduces the maximal word length, guaranteeing termination.

Part (b) strengthens this into full division with remainder. The assumption that the remainder has degree strictly less than the cancellation threshold ensures that all leading words of $U$ can be eliminated using shifts of $V$. The construction from (a) can be iterated until all words of maximal degree are removed, producing a quotient $Q$ and remainder $R$ with $\deg(R) < \deg(V)$. The difference from (a) is that now the process continues until the structural obstruction disappears completely.

Part (c) concerns homogeneous elements, meaning all words have the same length. In this case multiplication preserves a rigid grading. If $U_1V_1 = U_2V_2$ and both factors are homogeneous, then every word on the left must arise uniquely as a concatenation of a word from $U_1$ and one from $V_1$, and similarly on the right. Equality forces a unique factor alignment: every word in $U_2$ must be obtained by extending words of $U_1$ by a fixed common prefix, and similarly $V_1$ must be obtained by extending $V_2$ by a fixed suffix. This common structure defines a homogeneous polynomial $U$ such that $U_2 = U_1U$ and $V_1 = UV_2$. The argument is essentially uniqueness of factorization of words of fixed length under concatenation.

Part (d) is a commutativity rigidity statement. If two homogeneous polynomials commute, then their word structures must be compatible under concatenation in both orders. This forces both to be powers of a common primitive homogeneous element. The construction of $W$ is obtained by iteratively extracting maximal common homogeneous prefixes: start from leading words of $U$ and $V$, find their maximal common root word, and reduce both by factoring out this root. Repeating this process produces a smallest building block $W$ such that both $U$ and $V$ are powers of $W$ up to scalar coefficients. The scalars arise because coefficients multiply independently of word structure.

## Algorithm Walkthrough

For part (a), the construction proceeds as follows.

1. Identify all words of maximal length in $Q_1U$. These determine the leading structure of the expression.
2. Use the assumption $\deg(Q_1U - Q_2V) < \deg(Q_1U)$ to conclude that every such leading word must also appear in $Q_2V$ with matching coefficient sum zero. This enforces exact cancellation at the top degree.
3. Match each leading word of $U$ against a shifted occurrence inside $V$, determining how $Q$ must begin.
4. Define the leading part of $Q$ so that multiplying it by $V$ reproduces the leading cancellation pattern.
5. Subtract this contribution and repeat on the reduced-degree remainder.
6. Continue until all contributions above the target bound are eliminated, yielding the desired $Q$.

The reason this works is that degree strictly decreases at each cancellation step, and leading words uniquely determine how copies of $V$ must be aligned.

For part (b), the steps are extended until all words of degree at least $\deg(V)$ are eliminated.

1. Start with $U$.
2. While $\deg(U) \ge \deg(V)$, identify leading words of $U$.
3. For each leading word, find the corresponding occurrence in $V$ that can cancel it.
4. Subtract the corresponding shifted multiple of $V$.
5. Accumulate these shifts into the quotient $Q$.
6. Terminate when the remainder has degree strictly less than $\deg(V)$.

The invariant is that at each step, all words of maximal length in the current remainder are exactly those that must be cancelled by a corresponding prefix of $V$, ensuring correctness of the quotient extraction.

For part (c), the procedure is structural rather than iterative.

1. Fix homogeneous $U_1, U_2, V_1, V_2$ with $U_1V_1 = U_2V_2$.
2. Compare sets of words in the product, which all have identical total length.
3. For each word in $U_1V_1$, decompose it uniquely into a prefix from $U_1$ and suffix from $V_1$.
4. Use equality with $U_2V_2$ to show the same word must decompose in the other factorization.
5. This forces a consistent splitting point across all words.
6. Define $U$ as the factor induced by this consistent splitting, giving $U_2 = U_1U$ and $V_1 = UV_2$.

The key invariant is uniqueness of concatenation decomposition for fixed-length words under equality of formal sums.

For part (d), commutativity forces a shared primitive structure.

1. Take leading words of $U$ and $V$.
2. Find the longest word $W$ such that both leading words are powers of $W$.
3. Factor $U$ and $V$ by removing occurrences of $W$ from all words.
4. Repeat the process on reduced expressions.
5. Continue until no further nontrivial common factor exists.
6. The result is a primitive homogeneous $W$ such that both $U$ and $V$ are powers of $W$ up to scalar coefficients.

The invariant is that every factorization step preserves commutativity and reduces word length strictly, so the process terminates at a primitive root.

## Edge Cases

A key edge case is when two polynomials have identical degree but disjoint leading word sets. In that situation, naive cancellation fails because no single shift of $V$ can cancel all leading words of $U$, and the algorithm correctly produces a nontrivial remainder immediately.

Another case is when all terms have the same length but appear in different orderings. The homogeneous assumption ensures that factorization must respect exact word decomposition, and any mismatch forces the intermediate factor $U$ to be uniquely determined by the common splitting position across all terms.

A third case is full commutativity where one polynomial is a repeated concatenation of a primitive word. The algorithm repeatedly strips the primitive block without ambiguity because every leading word aligns perfectly under concatenation, producing a clean power structure with no residual remainder.
