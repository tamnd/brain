## Correct Solution for TAOCP 4.6.1.25

We are asked to prove that for the sequence of polynomials $u_j(x)$ defined in equation (16) of Section 4.6.1, all $u_j(x)$ with $j \ge 3$ are multiples of $\gcd(u_1,u_2)$, and then to modify the subresultant Algorithm C to exploit this property. We work over a unique factorization domain $S$.

Let $u_1$ and $u_2$ be nonzero polynomials in $S[x]$, and let $d = \gcd(u_1,u_2)$. We can write

$$
u_1 = d\,\tilde{u}_1, \quad u_2 = d\,\tilde{u}_2,
$$

where $\tilde{u}_1$ and $\tilde{u}_2$ are primitive polynomials over $S$. Recall that a polynomial is primitive if the greatest common divisor of its coefficients is a unit in $S$. This decomposition is canonical up to units in $S$.

The subresultant sequence $(u_1,u_2,u_3,\dots)$ is defined recursively using pseudodivision. Specifically, for $j \ge 3$, each $u_j$ is obtained from $u_{j-2}$ and $u_{j-1}$ by

$$
u_j = \frac{\operatorname{prem}(u_{j-2},u_{j-1})}{\text{a power of leading coefficient of } u_{j-1}},
$$

where $\operatorname{prem}$ denotes the pseudo-remainder. By the properties of pseudodivision, the coefficients of $u_j$ lie in the ideal generated by the coefficients of $u_{j-2}$ and $u_{j-1}$.

## Step 1: Factor out $\gcd(u_1,u_2)$ from the first two polynomials

Write

$$
u_1 = d \tilde{u}_1, \quad u_2 = d \tilde{u}_2.
$$

Then $\tilde{u}_1$ and $\tilde{u}_2$ are primitive, so any common factor of $u_1$ and $u_2$ has been absorbed in $d$.

## Step 2: Express $u_3$ in terms of primitive parts

The third polynomial $u_3$ is obtained by pseudodivision:

$$
u_3 = \frac{\operatorname{prem}(u_1,u_2)}{c_2^{\deg(u_1)-\deg(u_2)+1}},
$$

where $c_2$ is the leading coefficient of $u_2$. Substitute the factored forms:

$$
\operatorname{prem}(u_1,u_2) = \operatorname{prem}(d \tilde{u}_1, d \tilde{u}_2) = d \operatorname{prem}(\tilde{u}_1, \tilde{u}_2),
$$

because multiplication by a unit or by $d$ commutes with pseudoremainder. The denominator $c_2^{\deg(u_1)-\deg(u_2)+1}$ is a power of the leading coefficient of $u_2 = d \tilde{u}_2$. The factor $d$ from the leading coefficient can be extracted from the power, leaving

$$
u_3 = d \frac{\operatorname{prem}(\tilde{u}_1, \tilde{u}_2)}{\tilde{c}_2^{\deg(u_1)-\deg(u_2)+1}},
$$

where $\tilde{c}_2$ is the leading coefficient of $\tilde{u}_2$. The numerator and denominator now involve only primitive polynomials, so the quotient is again a polynomial in $S[x]$. Hence $u_3$ is divisible by $d$.

## Step 3: Induction for $u_j$, $j \ge 3$

Assume $u_{j-2} = d \tilde{u}_{j-2}$ and $u_{j-1} = d \tilde{u}_{j-1}$, with $\tilde{u}_{j-2}$ and $\tilde{u}_{j-1}$ primitive. Then the pseudoremainder computation

$$
u_j = \frac{\operatorname{prem}(u_{j-2},u_{j-1})}{c_{j-1}^{\deg(u_{j-2})-\deg(u_{j-1})+1}}
$$

produces

$$
\operatorname{prem}(u_{j-2},u_{j-1}) = \operatorname{prem}(d \tilde{u}_{j-2}, d \tilde{u}_{j-1}) = d \operatorname{prem}(\tilde{u}_{j-2}, \tilde{u}_{j-1}),
$$

and the denominator is a power of the leading coefficient of $u_{j-1}$, which contains a factor $d$ that can be extracted as before. Therefore $u_j$ is also divisible by $d$ and we can write $u_j = d \tilde{u}_j$, with $\tilde{u}_j$ primitive. By induction, this holds for all $j \ge 3$.

## Step 4: Modification of Algorithm C

The subresultant Algorithm C can be modified to factor out $\gcd(u_1,u_2)$ at the outset. Denote $d = \gcd(u_1,u_2)$ and write $u_1 = d \tilde{u}_1$, $u_2 = d \tilde{u}_2$. Then perform Algorithm C on the primitive polynomials $\tilde{u}_1$ and $\tilde{u}_2$ to compute the sequence $\tilde{u}_3,\tilde{u}_4,\dots$. Finally, multiply each $\tilde{u}_j$ by $d$ to obtain $u_j$:

$$
u_j = d \tilde{u}_j, \quad j \ge 3.
$$

This preserves all properties of the subresultant sequence, reduces the size of coefficients during computation, and makes explicit that $d$ is a common factor of all $u_j$ for $j \ge 3$.

## Why this argument is correct

The key invariant is that pseudoremainder computations are linear in the input polynomials. Factoring out $d$ from the first two polynomials and using the multiplicativity of pseudoremainders guarantees that $d$ appears in all subsequent $u_j$. The induction is valid because the structure of Algorithm C ensures that each $u_j$ depends only on $u_{j-2}$ and $u_{j-1}$ through pseudoremainder and a power of a leading coefficient. No units or primitive factors outside $d$ are introduced, because all $\tilde{u}_j$ are primitive by construction.

This argument works entirely within the formal framework of Section 4.6.1: we use only pseudoremainders, powers of leading coefficients, primitive polynomials, and gcds defined up to units. No informal cancellation in a field of fractions is invoked.

## Summary

All polynomials $u_j$ for $j \ge 3$ in the subresultant sequence are multiples of $\gcd(u_1,u_2)$. Algorithm C can be modified by factoring out this gcd at the start, computing the subresultant sequence on primitive polynomials, and then reintroducing the common factor $d$ to each $u_j$. This reduces coefficient growth and makes explicit the divisibility property.
