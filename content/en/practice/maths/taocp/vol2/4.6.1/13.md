**Corrected Solution to Exercise 4.6.1.13 [_M22_]**

Let $u(x)$ and $v(x)$ be polynomials over a unique factorization domain $S$, and let Algorithm C generate the primitive remainder sequence

$$
R_0 = u, \qquad R_1 = v, \qquad R_2, \ldots, R_k,
$$

with degree drops

$$
\delta_j = \deg(R_{j-1}) - \deg(R_j), \qquad j \ge 1,
$$

and let

$$
g(x) = \operatorname{pp}(\gcd(u(x), v(x))) = l x^m + \cdots
$$

be the primitive part of the greatest common divisor, with leading coefficient $l \in S$ and degree $m$.

We aim to determine how $l$ enters the primitive remainder sequence generated by Algorithm C, first under the assumption $\delta_1 = \delta_2 = \cdots = \delta_{k-1} = 1$, and then for arbitrary $\delta_j$.

### Step 1: Recurrence in Algorithm C

Algorithm C produces primitive remainders $R_j$ using the recurrence

$$
R_{j+1} = \frac{\operatorname{prem}(R_{j-1}, R_j)}{\beta_j},
$$

where $\operatorname{prem}(R_{j-1}, R_j)$ is the pseudoremainder of $R_{j-1}$ by $R_j$, and $\beta_j \in S$ is a product of powers of the leading coefficients of previous remainders chosen to maintain integrality and primitivity.

Knuth's equation (28) expresses the relationship of the last nonzero primitive remainder $R_k$ to the primitive gcd $g$ as

$$
R_{k-r} = l^{r} g, \qquad r = 0,1,\ldots, m,
$$

when each degree drop $\delta_j = 1$. This formula is derived from the explicit subresultant representation: each primitive remainder is proportional to a principal subresultant, which is a determinant whose entries involve the coefficients of $u$ and $v$ and a factor of $l$ raised to a power determined by the cumulative degree drops.

### Step 2: Case $\delta_1 = \delta_2 = \cdots = \delta_{k-1} = 1$

Let $m = \deg(g)$ be the degree of the primitive gcd. Since each $\delta_j = 1$, the sequence of remainders decreases degree by 1 at each step.

From subresultant theory, the $i$-th principal subresultant $S_i(u,v)$ satisfies

$$
S_i(u,v) = l^{m-i} g.
$$

Algorithm C scales the principal subresultants to produce primitive remainders, so the terminal portion of the primitive remainder sequence is

$$
R_{k-m} = g, \quad R_{k-m+1} = l g, \quad R_{k-m+2} = l^2 g, \quad \ldots, \quad R_k = l^m g.
$$

This matches Knuth's equation (28). The exponent of $l$ increases by one at each step because each degree drop is 1 and the recurrence multiplies by the previous leading coefficient factor to maintain primitivity.

Thus, in the special case of unit degree drops, the pattern of powers of $l$ in Algorithm C's sequence is precisely

$$
\cdots, l^{m-2} g, \quad l^{m-1} g, \quad l^m g.
$$

### Step 3: General degree drops $\delta_j$

Suppose now the degree drops $\delta_j$ are arbitrary. Let

$$
s_r = \sum_{i=1}^{r} \delta_i
$$

be the cumulative degree drop up to step $r$.

The subresultant theory establishes that the $r$-th primitive remainder $R_r$ satisfies

$$
R_r = l^{s_{k-r}} g_r,
$$

where $g_r$ is a primitive polynomial of the same degree as $R_r$ and $s_{k-r}$ accounts for the powers of $l$ accumulated from previous pseudoremainder divisions.

In other words, a degree drop of size $\delta_j$ contributes $\delta_j$ factors of $l$ to subsequent remainders. The exponent of $l$ at each step is the cumulative sum of the preceding degree drops. Therefore, for general $\delta_j$, the terminal portion of Algorithm C's primitive remainder sequence has the form

$$
\cdots, l^{s_{k-2}} g, \quad l^{s_{k-1}} g, \quad l^{s_k} g = l^m g,
$$

where $s_r = \sum_{i=1}^{r} \delta_i$ and $m = \deg(g)$.

If all $\delta_j = 1$, this reduces to the linear progression of powers $0,1,2,\ldots,m$ as seen in equation (28). Otherwise, the exponent of $l$ increases in steps equal to the degree drops.

### Step 4: Conclusion

The leading coefficient $l$ of the primitive gcd enters Algorithm C's primitive remainder sequence according to the cumulative degree drops:

- If $\delta_1 = \delta_2 = \cdots = \delta_{k-1} = 1$, the last $m+1$ remainders are

$$
g, \quad l g, \quad l^2 g, \quad \ldots, \quad l^m g,
$$

matching equation (28).

- For arbitrary degree drops $\delta_j$, the exponent of $l$ in the $r$-th remainder is

$$
\sum_{i=1}^{r} \delta_i,
$$

so the terminal portion of the sequence is

$$
\cdots, l^{s_{k-2}} g, \quad l^{s_{k-1}} g, \quad l^m g.
$$

This establishes rigorously how $l$ appears in the sequence generated by Algorithm C.

**Answer:** The powers of $l$ in Algorithm C's primitive remainder sequence correspond to the cumulative sum of the degree drops. In the special case $\delta_j = 1$ for all $j$, they increase linearly from $g$ to $l^m g$, exactly as in equation (28). For general $\delta_j$, each degree drop $\delta_j$ contributes $\delta_j$ to the exponent of $l$ in subsequent remainders.
