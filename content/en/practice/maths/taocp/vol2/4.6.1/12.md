A correct solution is as follows.

## Solution

Let

$$
u_1(x)=u(x),\qquad u_2(x)=v(x),
$$

and let

$$
u_j=q_j u_{j+1}+u_{j+2},
\qquad
\deg u_{j+2}<\deg u_{j+1},
$$

be the sequence generated by Algorithm C. Write

$$
m_j=\deg u_j,\qquad
a_j=l(u_j),
$$

where $l(f)$ denotes the leading coefficient of $f$.

Let $S(u,v)$ denote Sylvester's matrix. Its determinant is the resultant of $u$ and $v$.

We shall derive the determinant directly from the Euclidean algorithm.

### 1. If $u$ and $v$ have a common factor, then $\det S(u,v)=0$

Suppose that $d(x)$ is a common factor of positive degree:

$$
u=d\,u^*,\qquad v=d\,v^*.
$$

Let $r=\deg d>0$.

Choose

$$
A(x)=v^*(x),\qquad B(x)=-u^*(x).
$$

Then

$$
uA+vB
=d\,u^*v^*-d\,v^*u^*=0.
$$

Since

$$
\deg A=\deg v-r<\deg v,
\qquad
\deg B=\deg u-r<\deg u,
$$

the coefficient vectors of $A$ and $B$ give a nonzero vector in the nullspace of Sylvester's matrix. Hence

$$
\det S(u,v)=0.
$$

This proves one direction.

### 2. Reduction formula for the determinant

Let

$$
m=m_1,\qquad n=m_2.
$$

The first division step is

$$
u_1=q_1u_2+u_3,
$$

with

$$
m_3<m_2=n.
$$

Consider the Sylvester matrix of $u_1$ and $u_2$.

Subtract suitable multiples of rows corresponding to shifts of $u_2$ from the rows corresponding to shifts of $u_1$. These elementary row operations do not change the determinant. The rows coming from $u_1$ are thereby replaced by rows coming from $u_3$.

After a permutation of rows and columns, the transformed matrix becomes block triangular. The upper-left block is an identity-type triangular block of size $m-m_3$, whose diagonal entries are all $a_2=l(u_2)$. Therefore its determinant is

$$
a_2^{\,m-m_3}.
$$

The remaining block is exactly the Sylvester matrix of $u_2$ and $u_3$.

The row and column permutations contribute the sign

$$
(-1)^{mn}.
$$

Hence

$$
\det S(u_1,u_2)
=
(-1)^{m_1m_2}
a_2^{\,m_1-m_3}
\det S(u_2,u_3).
\tag{1}
$$

This is the basic reduction formula.

### 3. Iteration of the reduction formula

Apply (1) repeatedly:

$$
\det S(u_j,u_{j+1})
=
(-1)^{m_jm_{j+1}}
a_{j+1}^{\,m_j-m_{j+2}}
\det S(u_{j+1},u_{j+2}).
$$

Assume that for some $k$,

$$
m_k=0.
$$

Then $u_k$ is a nonzero constant.

The Sylvester matrix of $u_{k-1}$ and $u_k$ is triangular, since every row coming from $u_k$ contains only the constant coefficient $a_k$. Its determinant is

$$
\det S(u_{k-1},u_k)
=
\pm a_k^{\,m_{k-1}}.
\tag{2}
$$

Combining (1) repeatedly with (2) yields

$$
\det S(u_1,u_2)
=
\pm
\prod_{j=2}^{k}
a_j^{\,m_{j-1}-m_{j+1}},
$$

where $m_{k+1}=-1$ is understood so that the last factor contributes

$$
a_k^{m_{k-1}}.
$$

Taking absolute values removes the sign:

$$
\boxed{
\left|\det S(u,v)\right|
=
\prod_{j=2}^{k}
l(u_j)^{\,\deg(u_{j-1})-\deg(u_{j+1})}.
}
\tag{3}
$$

This is the required formula in terms of the leading coefficients and degrees of the polynomials generated by Algorithm C.

### 4. Converse

Suppose that $\deg(u_k)=0$ for some $k$.

Then every leading coefficient $l(u_j)$ is nonzero, and every exponent in (3) is a nonnegative integer. Therefore

$$
\left|\det S(u,v)\right|
=
\prod_{j=2}^{k}
l(u_j)^{\,\deg(u_{j-1})-\deg(u_{j+1})}
\neq 0.
$$

Hence

$$
\det S(u,v)\neq 0.
$$

Therefore the Sylvester matrix is nonsingular.

We have proved:

1. If $u$ and $v$ have a common factor of positive degree, then $\det S(u,v)=0$.
2. If Algorithm C reaches a constant remainder $u_k$, then

$$
\boxed{
\left|\det S(u,v)\right|
=
\prod_{j=2}^{k}
l(u_j)^{\,\deg(u_{j-1})-\deg(u_{j+1})}
}
$$

and consequently $\det S(u,v)\neq0$.

Thus the determinant of Sylvester's matrix vanishes exactly when $u$ and $v$ have a common factor of positive degree. ∎
