[Section 4.6.1: Division of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 16.** &#9654; [*M22*] Let $f(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$ be a multivariate polynomial that is not identically zero, and let $r(S_1, \ldots, S_n)$ be the set of roots $(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$ of $f(x_1, \ldots, x_n) = 0$ such that $x_j \in S_j$ for all $j$. If the degree of $f$ is at most $d_j \le |S_j|$ in the variable $x_j$, prove that

$$|r(S_1, \ldots, S_n)| \le |S_1| \cdot |S_2| \cdots |S_n| - (|S_1| - d_1) \cdots (|S_n| - d_n).$$

Therefore the probability of finding a root, by randomly choosing $x_j \in S_j$, approaches zero as the sets $S_j$ get bigger. [This inequality has many applications in the design of randomized algorithms, because it provides a good way to test whether a complicated sum of products of sums is identically zero without expanding out all the terms.]

## Solution

Let

$$
N(d_1,\ldots,d_n;S_1,\ldots,S_n)
=
|S_1|\cdots |S_n|
-
(|S_1|-d_1)\cdots(|S_n|-d_n).
$$

We shall prove that every nonzero polynomial

$$
f(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
$$

whose degree in the variable $x_j$ is at most $d_j\le |S_j|$ satisfies

$$
|r(S_1,\ldots,S_n)|\le N(d_1,\ldots,d_n;S_1,\ldots,S_n).
$$

The proof is by induction on $n$.

For $n=1$, the statement reduces to the familiar fact that a nonzero polynomial of degree at most $d_1$ has at most $d_1$ roots. Since

$$
N(d_1;S_1)
=
|S_1|-(|S_1|-d_1)
=
d_1,
$$

the result is true.

Assume now that the theorem holds for $n-1$ variables. Let

$$
m=|S_n|.
$$

Write $f$ as a polynomial in $x_n$:

$$
f(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=
\sum_{k=0}^{d_n}
g_k(x_1,\ldots,x_{n-1})x_n^k,
$$

where each $g_k$ is a polynomial in $x_1,\ldots,x_{n-1}$ and the degree of $g_k$ in $x_j$ is at most $d_j$.

Let $t$ be the largest index such that $g_t\not\equiv0$. Then $0\le t\le d_n$.

Define

$$
A=\{(x_1,\ldots,x_{n-1})\in S_1\times\cdots\times S_{n-1}
      : g_t(x_1,\ldots,x_{n-1})\ne0\}.
$$

For each point of $A$, the polynomial

$$
f(x_1,\ldots,x_{n-1},x_n)
$$

is a nonzero polynomial in the single variable $x_n$ of degree $t$. Hence it has at most $t$ roots in $S_n$. Therefore the number of roots of $f$ whose first $n-1$ coordinates lie in $A$ is at most

$$
t|A|.
$$

Next consider

$$
B=(S_1\times\cdots\times S_{n-1})\setminus A.
$$

For points of $B$ we have

$$
g_t(x_1,\ldots,x_{n-1})=0.
$$

Since $g_t$ is a nonzero polynomial whose degree in $x_j$ is at most $d_j$, the induction hypothesis gives

$$
|B|
\le
|S_1|\cdots|S_{n-1}|
-
(|S_1|-d_1)\cdots(|S_{n-1}|-d_{n-1}).
$$

For each point of $B$, all $m$ values of $x_n\in S_n$ are possible when counting roots, so the number of roots of $f$ with first $n-1$ coordinates in $B$ is at most

$$
m|B|.
$$

Hence

$$
|r(S_1,\ldots,S_n)|
\le
t|A|+m|B|.
$$

Since

$$
|A|+|B|=|S_1|\cdots|S_{n-1}|,
$$

we obtain

$$
|r(S_1,\ldots,S_n)|
\le
t|S_1|\cdots|S_{n-1}|+(m-t)|B|.
$$

Substituting the bound for $|B|$ yields

$$
\begin{aligned}
|r(S_1,\ldots,S_n)|
&\le
tP
+(m-t)\bigl(P-Q\bigr) \\
&=
mP-(m-t)Q,
\end{aligned}
$$

where

$$
P=|S_1|\cdots|S_{n-1}|,
\qquad
Q=(|S_1|-d_1)\cdots(|S_{n-1}|-d_{n-1}).
$$

Since $t\le d_n$,

$$
m-t\ge m-d_n,
$$

therefore

$$
|r(S_1,\ldots,S_n)|
\le
mP-(m-d_n)Q.
$$

Using the definitions of $P$ and $Q$,

$$
\begin{aligned}
|r(S_1,\ldots,S_n)|
&\le
|S_1|\cdots|S_n| \\
&\qquad
-
(|S_n|-d_n)
(|S_1|-d_1)\cdots(|S_{n-1}|-d_{n-1}) \\
&=
|S_1|\cdots|S_n|
-
(|S_1|-d_1)\cdots(|S_n|-d_n).
\end{aligned}
$$

This is the desired inequality.

Therefore

$$
|r(S_1,\ldots,S_n)|
\le
|S_1|\cdots|S_n|
-
(|S_1|-d_1)\cdots(|S_n|-d_n).
$$

This completes the proof.

## Notes

If the sets $S_j$ grow while the degrees $d_j$ remain fixed, the probability that a uniformly random point of $S_1\times\cdots\times S_n$ is a root satisfies

$$
\Pr(f=0)
\le
1-
\prod_{j=1}^n
\left(1-\frac{d_j}{|S_j|}\right).
$$

As $|S_j|\to\infty$ for all $j$, each factor tends to $1$, hence the right-hand side tends to $0$. Thus a random evaluation detects a nonzero polynomial with probability approaching $1$.
