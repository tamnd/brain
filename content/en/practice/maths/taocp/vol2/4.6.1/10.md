**Exercise 10.** [*M28*] Prove that the polynomials over a unique factorization domain form a unique factorization domain. [*Hint:* Use the result of exercise 9 to help show that there is at most one kind of factorization possible.]

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 11m37s

## Setup

Let $S$ be a unique factorization domain, and let $S[x]$ denote the ring of polynomials in one indeterminate $x$ with coefficients in $S$.

The exercise asks us to prove that $S[x]$ is itself a unique factorization domain. By definition, two properties must be established.

First, every nonzero nonunit polynomial of $S[x]$ must admit a factorization into irreducible polynomials.

Second, such a factorization must be unique up to multiplication by units and permutation of the irreducible factors.

Exercise 7 determines the units of $S[x]$: they are precisely the units of $S$. Lemmas G and H, together with equation (3), will be used repeatedly.

Let $K$ be the field of fractions of $S$. Since $K$ is a field, Algorithm D applies to polynomials over $K$, and the Euclidean algorithm gives unique factorization in $K[x]$.

The proof is divided into existence and uniqueness.

## Solution

Let $f(x)$ be a nonzero polynomial of $S[x]$.

By Lemma H,

$$
f(x)=c\,p(x),
$$

where $c=\operatorname{cont}(f)\in S$ and $p(x)=\operatorname{pp}(f(x))$ is primitive.

Since $S$ is a unique factorization domain, $c$ can be written as a product of primes of $S$,

$$
c=u\,\pi_1\pi_2\cdots\pi_r,
$$

where $u$ is a unit.

Thus the problem reduces to factoring the primitive polynomial $p(x)$.

Consider $p(x)$ as an element of $K[x]$. Since $K[x]$ is a Euclidean domain, it is a unique factorization domain. Hence

$$
p(x)=q_1(x)q_2(x)\cdots q_t(x),
$$

where each $q_i(x)$ is irreducible in $K[x]$.

Multiplying each $q_i(x)$ by a suitable nonzero element of $K$, we may write

$$
q_i(x)=a_i\,r_i(x),
$$

where $a_i\in K^\times$ and $r_i(x)\in S[x]$ is primitive.

Then

$$
p(x)
=
\left(a_1a_2\cdots a_t\right)
r_1(x)\cdots r_t(x).
$$

Since $p(x)$ is primitive, equation (4) and Lemma G imply that the product

$$
r_1(x)\cdots r_t(x)
$$

is primitive. Hence the scalar factor

$$
a_1a_2\cdots a_t
$$

must be a unit of $S$; otherwise the content of the right-hand side would not be a unit.

Therefore

$$
p(x)=u_0\,r_1(x)\cdots r_t(x)
$$

for some unit $u_0\in S$.

Each $r_i(x)$ is irreducible in $S[x]$. For suppose

$$
r_i(x)=g(x)h(x)
$$

with neither factor a unit of $S[x]$. The same equation holds in $K[x]$, contradicting the irreducibility of $q_i(x)$ in $K[x]$.

Thus

$$
f(x)
=
u\,\pi_1\cdots\pi_r\,r_1(x)\cdots r_t(x)
$$

is a factorization of $f(x)$ into irreducibles of $S[x]$.

This proves existence.

To prove uniqueness, suppose

$$
f(x)
=
u\,p_1(x)\cdots p_m(x)
=
v\,q_1(x)\cdots q_n(x),
$$

where $u$ and $v$ are units of $S$, and all $p_i(x)$ and $q_j(x)$ are irreducible in $S[x]$.

Each irreducible polynomial of $S[x]$ is primitive up to multiplication by a unit. Indeed, if

$$
p(x)=c\,w(x)
$$

with $c$ nonunit in $S$ and $\deg w\ge0$, then $p(x)$ would be reducible.

Hence all $p_i(x)$ and $q_j(x)$ may be chosen primitive.

Viewing the factorization in $K[x]$, the same equation holds in the unique factorization domain $K[x]$. Consequently,

$$
m=n,
$$

and after a permutation of the indices,

$$
p_i(x)=\alpha_i\,q_i(x),
\qquad
\alpha_i\in K^\times .
$$

Since both $p_i(x)$ and $q_i(x)$ are primitive polynomials over $S$, Lemma H applies to the equality

$$
p_i(x)=\alpha_i q_i(x).
$$

Lemma H implies that $\alpha_i$ is a unit of $S$.

Therefore every factor on one side differs from the corresponding factor on the other side only by multiplication by a unit of $S$.

The factorization is unique up to units and order of the factors.

Hence $S[x]$ satisfies both defining properties of a unique factorization domain.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification

Existence rests on three facts.

The content of a polynomial factors uniquely in $S$ by hypothesis.

Every primitive polynomial factors into irreducibles in $K[x]$ because $K[x]$ is Euclidean.

Lemma G and equation (4) force a factorization of a primitive polynomial in $K[x]$ to be representable, up to a unit, by primitive factors lying in $S[x]$.

Uniqueness rests on exercise 9 and Lemma H. The passage to $K[x]$ gives uniqueness there. Lemma H converts equality up to a scalar from $K$ into equality up to a unit of $S$. Hence no new factorizations appear when one restricts coefficients from $K$ to $S$.

Thus both requirements in the definition of a unique factorization domain are satisfied.

## Notes

The essential mechanism is Gauss's Lemma. It allows factorization questions in $S[x]$ to be transferred to the field of fractions $K[x]$, where the Euclidean algorithm supplies unique factorization. The primitive-part decomposition (3) then transports the result back to $S[x]$. This argument extends inductively and yields unique factorization for multivariate polynomial rings over a unique factorization domain.
