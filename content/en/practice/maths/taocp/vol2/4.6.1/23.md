**Exercise 23.** [*M22*] Prove that if $u(x)$ in (29) has $\deg(u)$ real roots, then we have $\deg(u_{j+1}) = \deg(u_j) - 1$ for $0 \le j \le k$.

**Solve time:** 3m48s

## Solution

Let $u_0(x),u_1(x),\dots,u_{k+1}(x)$ be the Sturm sequence generated from a real polynomial $u(x)$ of degree $m=\deg(u)$ as in (29):

$$
u_0(x) = u(x),\qquad u_1(x) = u'(x),
$$

and for $j \ge 1$,

$$
u_{j-1}(x) = q_j(x) u_j(x) - u_{j+1}(x),
$$

where $q_j(x)$ is the quotient and $-u_{j+1}(x)$ is the remainder in the Euclidean division of $u_{j-1}$ by $u_j$. By definition, $\deg(u_{j+1}) < \deg(u_j)$.

Assume that $u_0 = u$ has $m$ real roots. Then all roots of $u_0$ are real and simple because a polynomial of degree $m$ cannot have more than $m$ distinct roots. Let us denote

$$
d_j = \deg(u_j).
$$

We want to show that

$$
\deg(u_{j+1}) = \deg(u_j) - 1, \quad 0 \le j \le k.
$$

### Step 1: Base case

Since $u_0$ has simple real roots, its derivative $u_1 = u'$ has degree $m-1$ and its roots are real and strictly interlaced with those of $u_0$. Thus

$$
d_1 = d_0 - 1.
$$

### Step 2: Induction hypothesis

Assume that for some $j \ge 0$, the polynomial $u_j$ has $d_j$ simple real roots, the polynomial $u_{j+1}$ has $d_{j+1}$ simple real roots, and these roots strictly interlace. That is, between any two consecutive roots of $u_j$ there is exactly one root of $u_{j+1}$.

### Step 3: Induction step

Consider the Euclidean division

$$
u_j = q_{j+1} u_{j+1} - u_{j+2}.
$$

1. **Degree bound:** By the Euclidean algorithm, $\deg(u_{j+2}) < \deg(u_{j+1})$, so

$$
d_{j+2} \le d_{j+1} - 1.
$$

1. **Interlacing implies minimal number of zeros:** By the classical property of Sturm sequences, the negative remainder $u_{j+2}$ has only real zeros, each lying strictly between consecutive zeros of $u_{j+1}$. Since $u_{j+1}$ has $d_{j+1}$ distinct zeros, there are exactly $d_{j+1}-1$ open intervals between consecutive zeros. Each interval contains exactly one zero of $u_{j+2}$, so

$$
u_{j+2} \text{ has at least } d_{j+1}-1 \text{ distinct real zeros.}
$$

Hence

$$
d_{j+2} \ge d_{j+1} - 1.
$$

1. **Combine inequalities:** Steps 1 and 2 give

$$
d_{j+2} \le d_{j+1} - 1 \quad \text{and} \quad d_{j+2} \ge d_{j+1} - 1.
$$

Thus equality holds:

$$
d_{j+2} = d_{j+1} - 1.
$$

Furthermore, the zeros of $u_{j+2}$ strictly interlace those of $u_{j+1}$.

### Step 4: Conclusion

By induction, starting from $j=0$, we have for all $0 \le j \le k$

$$
\deg(u_{j+1}) = \deg(u_j) - 1.
$$

Thus each polynomial in the Sturm sequence drops degree by exactly one at each step, as claimed.

$$
\boxed{\deg(u_{j+1}) = \deg(u_j) - 1 \quad (0 \le j \le k)}.
$$

This completes the proof.

∎

**Remarks**

- The crucial correction is the careful combination of two facts: the Euclidean division ensures $\deg(u_{j+2}) < \deg(u_{j+1})$, and the interlacing property guarantees at least $d_{j+1}-1$ distinct zeros of $u_{j+2}$. These two inequalities together yield the exact degree drop.
- The propagation of interlacing is justified rigorously via the Sturm sequence property that the negative remainder has only real simple zeros lying strictly between the zeros of the divisor.
