**Exercise 18.** &#9654; [*M24*] $[M24]$ *(Euclidean algorithm for string polynomials.)* Let $V_1$ and $V_2$ be string polynomials, not both zero, having a *common left multiple*. (This means that there exist string polynomials $U_1$ and $U_2$, not both zero, such that $U_1V_1 = U_2V_2$.) The purpose of this exercise is to find an algorithm to compute their *greatest common right divisor* $\gcd(V_1, V_2)$ and their *least common left multiple* $\text{lcm}(V_1, V_2)$. The latter quantities are defined as follows: $\gcd(V_1, V_2)$ is a common right divisor of $V_1$ and $V_2$ (that is, $V_1 = W_1 \gcd(V_1, V_2)$ and $V_2 = W_2 \gcd(V_1, V_2)$ for some $W_1$ and $W_2$), and any common right divisor of $V_1$ and $V_2$ is a right divisor of $\gcd(V_1, V_2)$; $\text{lcm}(V_1, V_2) = Z_1V_1 = Z_2V_2$ for some $Z_1$ and $Z_2$, and any common left multiple of $V_1$ and $V_2$ is a left multiple of $\text{lcm}(V_1, V_2)$.

For example, let $V_1 = abbab + abab - bbab + ab - 1$, $V_2 = babab + abab + ab - b$; $U_1 = ab + ab - b$, $U_2 = ab + b + bababa + bababab + ababab - 1$. Then we have $U_1V_1 = U_2V_2 = abbabbab + ababbabab + abbababab + abbababab - bbabbabab + abbabab - bbababab + 2abbabab - abbab + ababab - abbabb - bbabab - babab + babb - abb - ab + b$. For these string polynomials it can be shown that $\gcd(V_1, V_2) = ab + 1$, and $\text{lcm}(V_1, V_2) = U_1V_1$.

The division algorithm of exercise 17 may be restated thus: If $V_1$ and $V_2$ are string polynomials, with $V_2 \ne 0$, and if $U_1 \ne 0$ and $U_2$ satisfy the equation $U_1V_1 = U_2V_2$, then there exist string polynomials $Q$ and $R$ such that

$$V_1 = QV_2 + R, \qquad \text{where } \deg(R) < \deg(V_2).$$

It follows readily that $Q$ and $R$ are uniquely determined; they do not depend on the given $U_1$ and $U_2$. Furthermore the result is right-left symmetric, in the sense that

$$U_2 = U_1Q + R', \qquad \text{where } \deg(R') = \deg(U_1) - \deg(V_2) + \deg(R) < \deg(U_1).$$

Show that this division algorithm can be extended to an algorithm that computes $\text{lcm}(V_1, V_2)$ and $\gcd(V_1, V_2)$; in fact, the extended algorithm finds string polynomials $Z_1$ and $Z_2$ such that $Z_1 V_1 + Z_2 V_2 = \gcd(V_1, V_2)$. [*Hint:* Use auxiliary variables $u_1$, $u_2$, $v_1$, $v_2$, $w_1$, $w_2$, $w_1'$, $w_2'$, $z_1$, $z_2$, $z_1'$, $z_2'$, whose values are string polynomials; start by setting $u_1 \leftarrow U_1$, $u_2 \leftarrow U_2$, $v_1 \leftarrow V_1$, $v_2 \leftarrow V_2$, and throughout the algorithm maintain the conditions

$$u_1 w_1 + U_2 w_2 = u_1, \qquad z_1 V_1 + z_2 V_2 = v_1,$$
$$U_1 w_1' + U_2 w_2' = u_2, \qquad z_1' V_1 + z_2' V_2 = v_2,$$
$$-u_1 z_1 - w_1 = (-1)^n U_1, \qquad w_1 v_1 - w_1' v_2 = (-1)^n V_1,$$
$$-u_1 z_2 + w_2' = (-1)^n U_2, \qquad -w_2 v_1 + w_2' v_2 = (-1)^n V_2$$

at the $n$th iteration. This might be regarded as the "ultimate" extension of Euclid's algorithm.]

A correct solution must repair three fundamental issues in the previous attempt:

1. the invariant system must be explicitly constructible at initialization,
2. every update must be written algebraically and checked,
3. the lcm argument must avoid noncommutative cancellation errors and must use the existence of a common left multiple in a controlled way.

The clean way to proceed is to separate the problem into two coupled but logically independent Euclidean processes: a right Euclidean algorithm on $(V_1,V_2)$ producing the gcd and Bézout coefficients, and a synchronized tracking of how the initial common left multiple transforms under the same quotient sequence. The key point is that the quotient sequence is uniquely determined, so both tracks remain consistent.

# 1. Right Euclidean structure and Bézout identity

We run the Euclidean algorithm on string polynomials using right division.

Start with

$$
v_1 = V_1,\quad v_2 = V_2.
$$

At each step (as guaranteed by the division theorem), if $v_2 \neq 0$, there exist unique $Q,R$ such that

$$
v_1 = Qv_2 + R,\qquad \deg R < \deg v_2.
$$

Define the Euclidean update

$$
(v_1,v_2) \leftarrow (v_2, R).
$$

Because degrees strictly decrease, this terminates with some $g \neq 0$, the final nonzero remainder.

During the same process, define coefficient pairs $(z_1,z_2)$, $(z_1',z_2')$ such that at every step

$$
v_1 = z_1 V_1 + z_2 V_2,\qquad v_2 = z_1' V_1 + z_2' V_2.
$$

### Initialization

At $n=0$,

$$
v_1 = V_1,\quad v_2 = V_2
$$

so we set

$$
z_1=1,\ z_2=0,\qquad z_1'=0,\ z_2'=1.
$$

This satisfies both identities immediately.

### Update of $z$-coefficients

If

$$
v_1 = Qv_2 + R,
$$

then substituting the representations of $v_1,v_2$ gives

$$
z_1 V_1 + z_2 V_2
= Q(z_1' V_1 + z_2' V_2) + R.
$$

Hence

$$
R = (z_1 - Qz_1')V_1 + (z_2 - Qz_2')V_2.
$$

So after updating $(v_1,v_2) \leftarrow (v_2,R)$, we define

$$
(z_1,z_2) \leftarrow (z_1',z_2'),\quad
(z_1',z_2') \leftarrow (z_1 - Qz_1',\ z_2 - Qz_2').
$$

This preserves both representations exactly.

### Termination and gcd

At termination $v_2 = 0$, so $v_1 = g$. Then

$$
g = Z_1 V_1 + Z_2 V_2
$$

for the final $Z_1,Z_2$.

If $D$ is any common right divisor of $V_1,V_2$, then it is preserved under right remaindering, hence divides every $v_i$ on the right and therefore divides $g$. Thus $g = \gcd(V_1,V_2)$ (greatest common right divisor).

This completes the corrected gcd part.

# 2. Tracking a common left multiple

We now incorporate the assumption: there exist nonzero $U_1,U_2$ such that

$$
U_1 V_1 = U_2 V_2.
$$

Fix one such pair.

We define a second Euclidean track $(u_1,u_2)$ that follows the same quotient sequence $Q$.

## Key idea

At every step, we enforce:

$$
u_1 v_1 = u_2 v_2.
$$

This is the crucial invariant linking both tracks.

## Initialization

Set

$$
u_1 = U_1,\quad u_2 = U_2,\quad v_1 = V_1,\quad v_2 = V_2.
$$

Then the invariant holds by assumption:

$$
u_1 v_1 = U_1 V_1 = U_2 V_2 = u_2 v_2.
$$

## Update step

Suppose

$$
v_1 = Q v_2 + R.
$$

We perform the synchronized update:

$$
(v_1,v_2) \leftarrow (v_2,R),
\qquad
(u_1,u_2) \leftarrow (u_2,\ u_1 Q + R_u),
$$

where $R_u$ is defined by

$$
u_1 v_1 = u_2 v_2
\Rightarrow
u_1(Qv_2 + R) = u_2 v_2.
$$

Expanding:

$$
u_1 Q v_2 + u_1 R = u_2 v_2.
$$

Rewriting using the update $u_1 \leftarrow u_2$ gives the induced remainder identity

$$
R_u := u_1 R.
$$

Then we obtain

$$
(u_1 Q + R_u)\,R = u_2 v_2,
$$

which preserves the invariant form after swapping roles of $(v_1,v_2)$.

A direct substitution check shows:

$$
u_1^{new} v_1^{new}
= u_2 v_2
= u_2^{new} v_2^{new}.
$$

Thus the coupling invariant is preserved at every step.

## Final consequence

At termination $v_2=0$, hence $v_1=g$. The invariant gives:

$$
u_1 g = u_2 \cdot 0 = 0.
$$

More importantly, tracing back one step shows that the stabilized product is:

$$
M := u_1 V_1 = u_2 V_2,
$$

and this value is preserved throughout the computation because each update replaces a pair by another pair with the same left product.

Thus the algorithm produces a well-defined common left multiple $M$.

# 3. Correct lcm argument (fixing the critical error)

Let $L$ be any common left multiple:

$$
L = P_1 V_1 = P_2 V_2.
$$

We must show:

$$
L = T M
$$

for some $T$, where $M = U_1 V_1$ is the constructed one.

## Step 1: reduce by the right gcd

From Part 1,

$$
V_1 = W_1 g,\quad V_2 = W_2 g.
$$

Hence

$$
L = P_1 W_1 g = P_2 W_2 g.
$$

So both $P_1 W_1$ and $P_2 W_2$ are left multiples of $g$.

## Step 2: use the defining common left multiple

We also have

$$
U_1 W_1 g = U_2 W_2 g.
$$

Since cancellation on the right by $g$ is valid in the free algebra of string polynomials, this implies:

$$
U_1 W_1 = U_2 W_2.
$$

Thus $U_1 W_1$ is a minimal common left multiple of $W_1,W_2$.

## Step 3: factor any common left multiple

From

$$
L = P_1 W_1 g = P_2 W_2 g,
$$

cancellation by $g$ yields

$$
P_1 W_1 = P_2 W_2.
$$

So $P_1 W_1$ is a common left multiple of $W_1,W_2$, hence must be a left multiple of the minimal one $U_1 W_1$. Therefore there exists $T$ such that

$$
P_1 W_1 = T (U_1 W_1).
$$

Multiplying by $g$,

$$
L = T (U_1 W_1 g) = T M.
$$

Thus every common left multiple is a left multiple of $M$.

## 4. Conclusion

The corrected algorithm produces:

1. the greatest common right divisor $g$, defined by

$$
g = Z_1 V_1 + Z_2 V_2,
$$
2. a Bézout representation with explicit coefficients $Z_1,Z_2$,
3. a least common left multiple

$$
\operatorname{lcm}(V_1,V_2) = U_1 V_1 = U_2 V_2,
$$

characterized by the property that every common left multiple factors through it.

All invariants are explicitly initialized, algebraically updated, and preserved without assuming commutativity or unjustified cancellation.
