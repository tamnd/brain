[Section 4.6.1: Division of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 15.** [*M26*] Prove Hadamard's inequality (25). [*Hint:* Consider the matrix $AA^T$.]

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m33s  

---

## Setup

Let $A=(a_{ij})$ be an $n\times n$ real matrix, and let

$r_i=(a_{i1},a_{i2},\ldots,a_{in})$

denote its $i$th row. Let

$\|r_i\|=\sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^{n}a_{ij}^2}.$

Hadamard's inequality (25) asserts that

$|\det A|\le \prod_{i=1}^{n}\|r_i\|.$

The hint suggests considering the matrix $AA^T$. Since

$\det(AA^T)=(\det A)^2,$

it suffices to prove

$\det(AA^T)\le \prod_{i=1}^{n}\|r_i\|^2.$

## Solution

Set

$B=AA^T.$

Then $B$ is a symmetric matrix whose entries are

$b_{ij}=r_i\cdot r_j.$

In particular,

$$$$

Since $B=AA^T$, for every vector $x\in\mathbf R^n$,

$$
xBx^T=xAA^Tx^T=(xA)(xA)^T
=\sum_{k=1}^{n}(xA)_k^2\ge0.
$$

Hence $B$ is positive semidefinite.

We prove by induction on $n$ that every positive semidefinite symmetric matrix $B=(b_{ij})$ satisfies

$$
\det B\le \prod_{i=1}^{n} b_{ii}.
\tag{1}
$$

For $n=1$, (1) is immediate.

Assume $n>1$ and that (1) holds for matrices of order $n-1$.

If $b_{11}=0$, positive semidefiniteness implies $b_{1j}=0$ for all $j$. Indeed, for any real $t$,

$$
0\le
\begin{vmatrix}
b_{11} & b_{1j}\\
b_{j1} & b_{jj}
\end{vmatrix}
=
b_{11}b_{jj}-b_{1j}^2
=
-b_{1j}^2,
$$

so $b_{1j}=0$. The first row and column are therefore zero, and $\det B=0$. Thus (1) holds.

Assume now that $b_{11}>0$. Perform the determinant-preserving elimination

$$
R_i\leftarrow R_i-\frac{b_{i1}}{b_{11}}R_1,
\qquad
2\le i\le n.
$$

The resulting matrix has first column entries $0$ below the diagonal, hence

$$
\det B
=
b_{11}\det C,
$$

where $C=(c_{ij})_{2\le i,j\le n}$ and

$$
c_{ij}
=
b_{ij}-\frac{b_{i1}b_{1j}}{b_{11}}.
$$

The matrix $C$ is again positive semidefinite. Therefore, by the induction hypothesis,

$$
\det C
\le
\prod_{i=2}^{n} c_{ii}.
\tag{2}
$$

For each $i\ge2$,

$$
c_{ii}
=
b_{ii}-\frac{b_{i1}^2}{b_{11}}.
$$

Since every $2\times2$ principal minor of a positive semidefinite matrix is nonnegative,

$$
b_{11}b_{ii}-b_{i1}^2\ge0,
$$

hence

$$
c_{ii}\le b_{ii}.
\tag{3}
$$

Combining (2) and (3),

$$
\det B
=
b_{11}\det C
\le
b_{11}\prod_{i=2}^{n}c_{ii}
\le
b_{11}\prod_{i=2}^{n}b_{ii}
=
\prod_{i=1}^{n}b_{ii}.
$$

Thus (1) is proved.

Applying (1) to $B=AA^T$ gives

$$
(\det A)^2
=
\det(AA^T)
\le
\prod_{i=1}^{n}(AA^T)_{ii}.
$$

Since

$$
(AA^T)_{ii}
=
\sum_{j=1}^{n}a_{ij}^2
=
\|r_i\|^2,
$$

we obtain

$$
(\det A)^2
\le
\prod_{i=1}^{n}\|r_i\|^2.
$$

Taking square roots yields

$$
|\det A|
\le
\prod_{i=1}^{n}\|r_i\|.
$$

This is Hadamard's inequality (25).

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification

The proof uses only the following facts:

1. $AA^T$ is positive semidefinite because

$$
xAA^Tx^T=(xA)(xA)^T\ge0.
$$
2. $\det(AA^T)=(\det A)^2$.
3. For a positive semidefinite matrix,

$$
b_{11}b_{ii}-b_{i1}^2\ge0,
$$

which implies

$$
b_{ii}-\frac{b_{i1}^2}{b_{11}}\le b_{ii}.
$$
4. Induction on the order of the matrix yields

$$
\det B\le\prod b_{ii}.
$$

Substituting $b_{ii}=|r_i|^2$ gives the desired inequality.

## Notes

Equality holds precisely when the rows of $A$ are pairwise orthogonal. In that case $AA^T$ is diagonal, so

$$
\det(AA^T)=\prod_{i=1}^{n}\|r_i\|^2,
$$

and therefore

$$
|\det A|
=
\prod_{i=1}^{n}\|r_i\|.
$$

This characterizes the extremal case of Hadamard's inequality.
