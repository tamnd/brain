## Solution to Exercise 4.6.1.19 [_M39_]

We treat the problem in two parts: first the general existence theorem for greatest common right divisors of integer matrices, then the explicit computation for the specific matrices.

## General existence theorem

Let $A,B\in M_n(\mathbf Z)$. We seek integer matrices $D,P,Q,X,Y$ satisfying

$$
A=PD,\qquad B=QD,\qquad D=XA+YB,
$$

with $D$ a greatest common right divisor (GCRD) of $A$ and $B$, meaning that any common right divisor of $A$ and $B$ divides $D$ on the right.

### Step 1: Column lattice construction

Consider the set of all integer linear combinations of columns of $A$ and $B$, forming a $\mathbf Z$-module in $\mathbf Z^n$:

$$
\mathcal{L} = \{ Au + Bv : u,v\in \mathbf Z^n \}.
$$

This is a finitely generated $\mathbf Z$-module of rank $r \le n$. Since $\mathbf Z^n$ is free, $\mathcal{L}$ is also free. Choose a basis of $\mathcal{L}$ consisting of $r$ linearly independent integer vectors $d_1,\dots,d_r$. Extend this to an $n\times n$ integer matrix $D$ by adding $n-r$ columns from the standard basis of $\mathbf Z^n$. Then $D$ has full rank $n$ and its first $r$ columns generate the same lattice $\mathcal{L}$ as the columns of $A$ and $B$.

By construction, each column of $A$ and $B$ is an integer linear combination of the columns of $D$. This produces integer matrices $P$ and $Q$ such that

$$
A = PD, \qquad B = QD.
$$

The existence of $X$ and $Y$ satisfying

$$
D = XA + YB
$$

follows directly from the fact that the first $r$ columns of $D$ lie in $\mathcal{L}$, so they are integer linear combinations of the columns of $A$ and $B$. Extend this combination to the remaining $n-r$ columns by choosing suitable integer coefficients to obtain $X,Y\in M_n(\mathbf Z)$. Thus $D = XA + YB$.

### Step 2: Greatest common right divisor property

Let $E$ be any common right divisor of $A$ and $B$, so there exist integer matrices $P',Q'$ such that

$$
A = P'E, \qquad B = Q'E.
$$

Then

$$
D = XA + YB = X(P'E) + Y(Q'E) = (XP' + YQ')E.
$$

Hence $E$ divides $D$ on the right. Therefore $D$ is a greatest common right divisor of $A$ and $B$.

### Step 3: Algorithm

The algorithm to produce $D,P,Q,X,Y$ is as follows:

1. Form the $\mathbf Z$-module $\mathcal{L}$ generated by the columns of $A$ and $B$.
2. Compute a basis $\{d_1,\dots,d_r\}$ of $\mathcal{L}$ as integer vectors.
3. Form an $n\times n$ matrix $D$ by adding $n-r$ integer columns to extend $\{d_1,\dots,d_r\}$ to a basis of $\mathbf Z^n$.
4. Express each column of $A$ and $B$ as an integer linear combination of the columns of $D$ to obtain $P$ and $Q$.
5. Express each column of $D$ as an integer linear combination of the columns of $A$ and $B$ to obtain $X$ and $Y$.

This produces the desired matrices satisfying $A=PD$, $B=QD$, $D=XA+YB$, and ensures that $D$ is a GCRD.

## Explicit computation for $2\times 2$ matrices

Let

$$
A = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4\end{pmatrix}, \qquad B = \begin{pmatrix}4 & 1 \\ 1 & 4\end{pmatrix}.
$$

We seek $D,P,Q,X,Y$ as above.

### Step 1: Solve Bézout identity

We look for integer matrices $X,Y$ satisfying

$$
D = XA + YB
$$

with $D$ a common right divisor. Since $A$ and $B$ generate the entire lattice $\mathbf Z^2$ (they are invertible over $\mathbf Q$), we attempt

$$
D = I_2.
$$

Write

$$
X = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}, \qquad
Y = \begin{pmatrix} e & f \\ g & h \end{pmatrix},
$$

and solve

$$
XA + YB = I_2.
$$

Computing entries yields the system

$$
\begin{aligned}
a + 3b + 4e + f &= 1,\\
2a + 4b + e + 4f &= 0,\\
c + 3d + 4g + h &= 0,\\
2c + 4d + g + 4h &= 1.
\end{aligned}
$$

An integer solution is

$$
a = 0, b = 0, e = 4, f = -4, \quad
c = 0, d = 1, g = -1, h = 1,
$$

so

$$
X = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1\end{pmatrix}, \qquad
Y = \begin{pmatrix}4 & -4 \\ -1 & 1\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Check:

$$
XA = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 0 \\ 3 & 4\end{pmatrix}, \qquad
YB = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 0 \\ -3 & -3\end{pmatrix}, \qquad
XA + YB = I_2.
$$

### Step 2: Set $D = I_2$

Then

$$
A = A I_2, \qquad B = B I_2,
$$

so $P = A$, $Q = B$ satisfy $A=PD$, $B=QD$.

### Step 3: Verify greatest common right divisor property

If $E$ is any common right divisor, $A=P'E$, $B=Q'E$. Then

$$
I_2 = XA + YB = X(P'E) + Y(Q'E) = (XP' + YQ') E.
$$

Thus $E$ has a left inverse over $\mathbf Z$. Since $E$ is square, $\det(E)=\pm 1$ and $E$ is unimodular. Therefore every common right divisor divides $D = I_2$. Hence $D = I_2$ is a GCRD.

### Step 4: Summary of matrices

$$
\boxed{
D = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1\end{pmatrix}, \quad
P = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4\end{pmatrix}, \quad
Q = \begin{pmatrix}4 & 1 \\ 1 & 4\end{pmatrix}, \quad
X = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1\end{pmatrix}, \quad
Y = \begin{pmatrix}4 & -4 \\ -1 & 1\end{pmatrix}
}
$$

satisfying

$$
A=PD, \qquad B=QD, \qquad D=XA+YB.
$$

This completes the corrected general existence proof and the explicit computation for the given matrices. The construction of $D$ via a lattice basis of the columns of $[A\;B]$ ensures full justification.
