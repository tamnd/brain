[Section 4.6.1: Division of Polynomials](../)

**Exercise 4.** [*M30*] Let $p$ be a prime. Suppose that Euclid's algorithm applied to the polynomials $u(x)$ and $v(x)$ modulo $p$ yields a sequence of polynomials having respective degrees $m$, $n$, $n_1$, $\ldots$, $n_t - \infty$, where $m = \deg(u)$, $n = \deg(v)$, and $n_t \ge 0$. Assume that $m \ge n$. If $u(x)$ and $v(x)$ are monic polynomials, independently and uniformly distributed over all the $p^{m+n}$ pairs of monic polynomials having respective degrees $m$ and $n$, what are the average values of the three quantities $t$, $n_1 + \cdots + n_t$, and $(n - n_1)n_1 + \cdots + (n_{t-1} - n_t)n_t$, as functions of $m$, $n$, and $p$? (These three quantities are the fundamental factors in the running time of Euclid's algorithm applied to polynomials modulo $p$, assuming that division is done by Algorithm D.) [*Hint:* Show that $u(x) \bmod v(x)$ is uniformly distributed and independent of $v(x)$.]

Let $F=\mathbf F_p$. Let

$$
m=\deg u,\qquad n=\deg v,\qquad m\ge n,
$$

and let Euclid's algorithm produce remainder degrees

$$
m,\ n,\ n_1,\ n_2,\ \ldots,\ n_t,\ -\infty,
$$

with

$$
n>n_1>\cdots>n_t\ge0.
$$

Put $n_0=n$. We seek the expectations of

$$
t,
$$

$$
n_1+\cdots+n_t,
$$

and

$$
\sum_{i=1}^{t}(n_{i-1}-n_i)n_i .
$$

The answer is independent of $m$.

## 1. Distribution of remainders

Fix a monic polynomial $v(x)$ of degree $N$.

For every polynomial $r(x)$ with $\deg r<N$, the monic polynomials $u(x)$ of degree $m$ satisfying

$$
u(x)\equiv r(x)\pmod{v(x)}
$$

are exactly the polynomials

$$
u(x)=q(x)v(x)+r(x),
$$

where $q(x)$ is monic of degree $m-N$.

There are exactly $p^{m-N}$ such $q$, since the leading coefficient is fixed and the remaining $m-N$ coefficients are arbitrary. Hence every remainder $r$ occurs equally often. Since there are $p^N$ polynomials of degree $<N$,

$$
u\bmod v
$$

is uniformly distributed over all polynomials of degree $<N$, and its distribution is independent of $v$.

Therefore, conditioned only on the current divisor degree $N$, the next remainder is uniformly distributed among the $p^N$ polynomials of degree $<N$. The same counting argument applies at every subsequent step. Hence the sequence of remainder degrees is a Markov chain depending only on the current degree.

For $0\le k<N$,

$$
\Pr(\deg r=k)=\frac{(p-1)p^k}{p^N},
$$

since there are $(p-1)p^k$ polynomials of degree exactly $k$. Also

$$
\Pr(r=0)=p^{-N}.
$$

Thus, if $K$ denotes the degree of the next nonzero remainder,

$$
\Pr(K=k)=\frac{(p-1)p^k}{p^N},
\qquad 0\le k<N.
$$

## 2. Average value of $t$

Let $E(N)$ be the expected number of future nonzero remainder degrees when the current divisor degree is $N$.

Clearly,

$$
E(0)=0.
$$

Conditioning on the first remainder degree,

$$
E(N)
=
\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}
\frac{(p-1)p^k}{p^N}
\bigl(1+E(k)\bigr).
$$

Multiplying by $p^N$,

$$
p^N E(N)
=
(p-1)\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}p^k\bigl(1+E(k)\bigr).
$$

Subtracting the corresponding equation for $N-1$,

$$
p^N E(N)-p^{N-1}E(N-1)
=
(p-1)p^{N-1}\bigl(1+E(N-1)\bigr).
$$

Hence

$$
pE(N)-E(N-1)
=
(p-1)\bigl(1+E(N-1)\bigr),
$$

so

$$
pE(N)
=
(p-1)+pE(N-1).
$$

Therefore

$$
E(N)-E(N-1)=\frac{p-1}{p}.
$$

Since $E(0)=0$,

$$
E(N)=\frac{p-1}{p}N.
$$

Taking $N=n$,

$$
\boxed{\mathbb E(t)=\frac{p-1}{p}\,n.}
$$

## 3. Average value of $n_1+\cdots+n_t$

Let

$$
F(N)=\mathbb E(n_1+\cdots+n_t)
$$

when the current divisor degree is $N$.

Again,

$$
F(0)=0.
$$

Conditioning on the first remainder degree,

$$
F(N)
=
\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}
\frac{(p-1)p^k}{p^N}
\bigl(k+F(k)\bigr).
$$

Multiplying by $p^N$,

$$
p^N F(N)
=
(p-1)\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}
p^k\bigl(k+F(k)\bigr).
$$

Subtracting the equation for $N-1$,

$$
p^N F(N)-p^{N-1}F(N-1)
=
(p-1)p^{N-1}
\bigl((N-1)+F(N-1)\bigr).
$$

Therefore

$$
pF(N)-F(N-1)
=
(p-1)\bigl((N-1)+F(N-1)\bigr),
$$

hence

$$
pF(N)
=
(p-1)(N-1)+pF(N-1).
$$

Thus

$$
F(N)-F(N-1)
=
\frac{p-1}{p}(N-1).
$$

Summing,

$$
F(N)
=
\frac{p-1}{p}\sum_{j=0}^{N-1}j
=
\frac{p-1}{2p}N(N-1).
$$

Hence

$$
\boxed{
\mathbb E(n_1+\cdots+n_t)
=
\frac{p-1}{2p}\,n(n-1).
}
$$

## 4. Average value of $\sum_{i=1}^t (n_{i-1}-n_i)n_i$

Let

$$
G(N)
=
\mathbb E\!\left(
\sum_{i\ge1}(n_{i-1}-n_i)n_i
\right)
$$

when the current divisor degree is $N$.

Then

$$
G(0)=0.
$$

Conditioning on the first remainder degree $k$,

$$
G(N)
=
\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}
\frac{(p-1)p^k}{p^N}
\Bigl((N-k)k+G(k)\Bigr).
$$

Multiplying by $p^N$,

$$
p^N G(N)
=
(p-1)\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}
p^k\Bigl((N-k)k+G(k)\Bigr).
$$

For $N-1$,

$$
p^{N-1}G(N-1)
=
(p-1)\sum_{k=0}^{N-2}
p^k\Bigl((N-1-k)k+G(k)\Bigr).
$$

Subtracting, we obtain

$$
\begin{aligned}
p^NG(N)-p^{N-1}G(N-1)
&=(p-1)\sum_{k=0}^{N-2}p^k k \\
&\qquad +(p-1)p^{N-1}\bigl((N-1)+G(N-1)\bigr).
\end{aligned}
$$

The extra $(N-1)$ term comes from the $k=N-1$ summand and must be retained.

Using

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{N-2}kp^k
=
\frac{(N-2)p^{N}-(N-1)p^{N-1}+p}{(p-1)^2},
$$

we obtain

$$
\begin{aligned}
p^NG(N)-p^{N-1}G(N-1)
&=
\frac{(N-2)p^{N}-(N-1)p^{N-1}+p}{p-1} \\
&\qquad +(p-1)p^{N-1}\bigl((N-1)+G(N-1)\bigr).
\end{aligned}
$$

Dividing by $p^{N-1}$,

$$
pG(N)-G(N-1)
=
\frac{(N-2)p-(N-1)+p^{-(N-2)}}{p-1}
+(p-1)\bigl((N-1)+G(N-1)\bigr).
$$

The terms involving $G(N-1)$ combine to give

$$
p\bigl(G(N)-G(N-1)\bigr)
=
\frac{(N-2)p-(N-1)+p^{-(N-2)}}{p-1}
+(p-1)(N-1).
$$

The numerator simplifies:

$$
(N-2)p-(N-1)+(p-1)(N-1)=N(p-1)-p.
$$

Hence

$$
G(N)-G(N-1)
=
\frac{N(p-1)-p+p^{-(N-2)}}{p(p-1)}.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
G(N)-G(N-1)
=
\frac{N}{p}-\frac1{p-1}
+\frac{p^{-(N-1)}}{p-1}.
$$

Summing from $1$ to $N$,

$$
G(N)
=
\sum_{j=1}^{N}
\left(
\frac{j}{p}
-\frac1{p-1}
+\frac{p^{-(j-1)}}{p-1}
\right).
$$

Now

$$
\sum_{j=1}^{N}j=\frac{N(N+1)}2,
$$

and

$$
\sum_{j=1}^{N}p^{-(j-1)}
=
\frac{p(1-p^{-N})}{p-1}.
$$

Therefore

$$
G(N)
=
\frac{N(N+1)}{2p}
-\frac{N}{p-1}
+\frac{p(1-p^{-N})}{(p-1)^2}.
$$

Thus

$$
\boxed{
\mathbb E\!\left(
(n-n_1)n_1+\cdots+(n_{t-1}-n_t)n_t
\right)
=
\frac{n(n+1)}{2p}
-\frac{n}{p-1}
+\frac{p(1-p^{-n})}{(p-1)^2}.
}
$$

As a check, for $n=1$ this gives

$$
\frac1p-\frac1{p-1}
+\frac{p(1-p^{-1})}{(p-1)^2}
=0,
$$

which is correct.

Hence the required average values are

$$
\boxed{\mathbb E(t)=\frac{p-1}{p}\,n,}
$$

$$
\boxed{
\mathbb E(n_1+\cdots+n_t)
=
\frac{p-1}{2p}\,n(n-1),
}
$$

and

$$
\boxed{
\mathbb E\!\left(
(n-n_1)n_1+\cdots+(n_{t-1}-n_t)n_t
\right)
=
\frac{n(n+1)}{2p}
-\frac{n}{p-1}
+\frac{p(1-p^{-n})}{(p-1)^2}.
}
$$

All three expectations depend only on $n$ and $p$, not on $m$.
