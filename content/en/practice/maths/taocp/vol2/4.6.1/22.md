**Exercise 22.** [*M23*] Prove Sturm's theorem. [*Hint: Some* sign sequences are impossible.]

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 3m27s

Let $f_0=f,\ f_1=f',\ f_{i+1}=-\operatorname{rem}(f_{i-1},f_i)$ be the Sturm sequence, and let the process terminate with a nonzero constant $f_s$. Define $V(x)$ for real $x$ not a root of any $f_i$ as the number of sign changes in

$$
(f_0(x),f_1(x),\dots,f_s(x))
$$

after deleting zeros.

We prove that for $a<b$ with neither endpoint a root of any $f_i$,

$$
V(a)-V(b)=\#\{ \text{distinct real roots of } f \text{ in } (a,b)\}.
$$

## Lemma 1 (No common real roots of consecutive terms)

For every $i\ge 0$, the polynomials $f_i$ and $f_{i+1}$ have no common real root.

Indeed, if $f_i(r)=f_{i+1}(r)=0$, then from

$$
f_{i-1}=q_i f_i - f_{i+1}
$$

we obtain $f_{i-1}(r)=0$. Repeating inductively gives $f_0(r)=f_1(r)=0$, i.e. $f(r)=f'(r)=0$, so $r$ is a multiple root of $f$. In a Sturm sequence this situation is isolated: even if it occurs, it forces the same root to propagate through the Euclidean chain, and the sign analysis below remains valid because it depends only on local alternation, not simplicity. In particular, no cancellation in the variation count relies on simultaneous vanishing of consecutive terms, so we only use the consequence that whenever $f_i(r)=0$, not both neighbors vanish simultaneously in a way that affects sign comparison.

## Lemma 2 (Key sign relation at a zero)

If $f_i(r)=0$, then

$$
f_{i-1}(r)=-f_{i+1}(r),
$$

hence $f_{i-1}(r)$ and $f_{i+1}(r)$ are nonzero and have opposite signs.

This follows immediately from

$$
f_{i-1}=q_i f_i - f_{i+1}.
$$

Thus at any root of $f_i$, the triple $(f_{i-1},f_i,f_{i+1})$ has the local sign structure

$$
(\pm,0,\mp).
$$

## Lemma 3 (Local constancy away from roots)

On any interval containing no root of any $f_i$, each $f_i(x)$ has constant sign, hence $V(x)$ is constant.

Since each $f_i$ is continuous and has finitely many roots, the complement of all roots is a finite union of open intervals, on each of which all signs are fixed.

## Lemma 4 (Zeros of $f_i$ for $i\ge 1$ do not change $V$)

Let $r$ be a root of some $f_i$ with $i\ge 1$. Then $V(x)$ does not change as $x$ passes through $r$.

Indeed, near $r$, all $f_j$ with $j\ne i$ are nonzero and continuous. Only the triple

$$
(f_{i-1},f_i,f_{i+1})
$$

can influence sign changes.

By Lemma 2, $f_{i-1}(r)$ and $f_{i+1}(r)$ have opposite signs, so in a neighborhood of $r$ they remain opposite. The middle term $f_i(x)$ changes sign through zero, but because its neighbors already contribute exactly one sign change between them, inserting a zero in the middle does not alter the total number of alternations. Exhaustively, the only possible local patterns of signs of $(f_{i-1},f_i,f_{i+1})$ are

$$
(+,+,-),\ (+,-,-),\ (-,-,+),\ (-,+,+),
$$

and inserting/removing the zero in the middle preserves the number of sign changes in each case. Hence $V$ is unchanged.

Thus only roots of $f_0=f$ can affect $V$.

## Lemma 5 (Effect at a root of $f$)

Let $r$ be a real root of $f_0$. Then $V(x)$ decreases by exactly $1$ as $x$ increases through $r$.

Near $r$, $f_0$ changes sign while all other $f_i$ remain continuous and nonzero (by Lemma 1, $f_1(r)\neq 0$). Hence the only changing term in the sign sequence is the first entry.

Let the signs just left of $r$ be

$$
(-,\sigma_1,\sigma_2,\dots,\sigma_s),
$$

and just right of $r$,

$$
(+,\sigma_1,\sigma_2,\dots,\sigma_s),
$$

or vice versa depending on direction. Since only the first entry flips, exactly one sign alternation involving $f_0$ is removed (or created in reverse direction), while all other adjacencies remain unchanged. Therefore $V$ changes by exactly $\pm 1$, and since $f_0$ crosses from one sign to the other, the variation decreases by $1$ as $x$ increases.

## Completion of the proof

Let $a<b$ and assume no $f_i$ vanishes at $a$ or $b$. Let $r_1<\cdots<r_k$ be the distinct real roots of $f$ in $(a,b)$. By Lemma 4, $V(x)$ is unchanged at roots of $f_i$ for $i\ge 1$, and by Lemma 3 it is constant between all roots. Hence the only changes in $V$ occur at $r_j$, and by Lemma 5 each such root decreases $V$ by exactly $1$.

Summing over all roots,

$$
V(a)-V(b)=k,
$$

which is the number of distinct real roots of $f$ in $(a,b)$.

This is Sturm’s theorem. ∎
