**Exercise 19.** [*HM25*] (R. L. Duncan.) Prove that the leading digits of Fibonacci numbers obey the logarithmic law of fraction parts: $\Pr(10f_{F_n} < r) = \log_{10} r$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m53s  

---

## Solution

Let $F_n$ be the Fibonacci numbers. The statement

$$
\Pr(10f_{F_n} < r) = \log_{10} r
$$

means

$$
\lim_{k\to\infty}\frac{1}{k}\#\{1 \le n \le k : (\log_{10} F_n)\bmod 1 < \log_{10} r\} = \log_{10} r.
$$

Define

$$
x_n = (\log_{10} F_n)\bmod 1.
$$

The goal is to determine the limiting distribution of $x_n$.

### 1. Logarithmic form of Fibonacci numbers

Let

$$
\alpha = \frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2}, \qquad \beta = \frac{1-\sqrt{5}}{2}.
$$

Binet’s formula gives

$$
F_n = \frac{\alpha^n - \beta^n}{\sqrt{5}}.
$$

Since $|\beta|<1$, write

$$
F_n = \frac{\alpha^n}{\sqrt{5}}\left(1 - \left(\frac{\beta}{\alpha}\right)^n\right).
$$

Taking $\log_{10}$,

$$
\log_{10} F_n
= n\log_{10}\alpha - \log_{10}\sqrt{5}
+ \log_{10}\!\left(1 - \left(\frac{\beta}{\alpha}\right)^n\right).
$$

Define

$$
\theta = \log_{10}\alpha, \qquad \gamma = -\log_{10}\sqrt{5}.
$$

Let

$$
\varepsilon_n = \log_{10}\!\left(1 - \left(\frac{\beta}{\alpha}\right)^n\right).
$$

Since $|\beta/\alpha|<1$, we have $\varepsilon_n \to 0$ and in fact $|\varepsilon_n| = O((|\beta|/\alpha)^n)$.

Thus

$$
\log_{10} F_n = n\theta + \gamma + \varepsilon_n.
$$

Taking fractional parts,

$$
x_n = \{n\theta + \gamma + \varepsilon_n\}.
$$

### 2. Irrationality of $\theta$

Assume $\theta = \log_{10}\alpha$ is rational, so $\theta = p/q$ with integers $p,q>0$. Then

$$
\alpha^q = 10^p.
$$

The number $\alpha$ is an algebraic integer satisfying $x^2-x-1=0$, hence $\alpha^q$ is also an algebraic integer. Therefore $10^p$ would be an algebraic integer.

The only rational algebraic integers are the integers, so $10^p$ is an integer, which is true, but then $\alpha^q$ must be an integer.

However, $\alpha$ is irrational and lies in $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{5})$. Every power $\alpha^q$ has the form

$$
\alpha^q = a_q \alpha + b_q
$$

with integers $a_q,b_q$ (proved by induction from $\alpha^2=\alpha+1$). If $\alpha^q$ is an integer, then $a_q=0$ and $\alpha^q=b_q$, which forces $\alpha$ to satisfy a linear relation over $\mathbb{Q}$, contradicting irrationality of $\alpha$.

Thus $\theta$ is irrational.

### 3. Equidistribution of ${n\theta}$

For irrational $\theta$, the sequence ${n\theta}$ is equidistributed modulo $1$:

for every interval $[0,r)$,

$$
\lim_{k\to\infty}\frac{1}{k}\#\{1\le n\le k : \{n\theta\}<r\} = r.
$$

This follows from Weyl’s criterion for equidistribution of linear sequences with irrational slope.

### 4. Removal of the constant shift

The sequence ${n\theta+\gamma}$ is a translate of ${n\theta}$ modulo $1$, hence also equidistributed. Therefore

$$
\{n\theta+\gamma\}
$$

has the same limiting distribution.

### 5. Stability under the perturbation $\varepsilon_n$

Let

$$
y_n = \{n\theta+\gamma\}.
$$

We compare $x_n$ and $y_n$. Since $\varepsilon_n \to 0$, for every $\delta>0$ there exists $N$ such that $|\varepsilon_n|<\delta$ for all $n\ge N$.

Fix $r\in[0,1]$ and define the indicator functions

$$
I_n = \mathbf{1}(x_n < r), \qquad J_n = \mathbf{1}(y_n < r).
$$

If $x_n \ne y_n$ in the sense of crossing the threshold at $r$, then $y_n$ must lie within distance $\delta$ of $r$. Hence

$$
|I_n - J_n| \le \mathbf{1}(\text{dist}(y_n,r)<\delta)
$$

for $n\ge N$.

By equidistribution of $y_n$, the proportion of indices with $y_n$ in an interval of length $2\delta$ tends to $2\delta$. Therefore

$$
\limsup_{k\to\infty}\frac{1}{k}\sum_{n=1}^k |I_n-J_n| \le 2\delta.
$$

Since $\delta$ is arbitrary, the limiting densities of $I_n$ and $J_n$ coincide. Thus $x_n$ and $y_n$ have the same distribution.

### 6. Final density computation

From equidistribution of $y_n$,

$$
\lim_{k\to\infty}\frac{1}{k}\#\{1\le n\le k : y_n < r\} = r.
$$

By the stability result,

$$
\lim_{k\to\infty}\frac{1}{k}\#\{1\le n\le k : x_n < r\} = r.
$$

Thus

$$
\Pr\big((\log_{10} F_n)\bmod 1 < r\big) = r.
$$

Since $10f_{F_n}<r$ is equivalent to $(\log_{10} F_n)\bmod 1 < \log_{10} r$, substituting $r \mapsto \log_{10} r$ gives

$$
\Pr(10f_{F_n} < r) = \log_{10} r.
$$

### Final result

$$
\boxed{\Pr(10f_{F_n} < r) = \log_{10} r}
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
