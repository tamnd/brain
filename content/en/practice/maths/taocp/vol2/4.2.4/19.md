[Section 4.2.4: Distribution of Floating Point Numbers](../)

**Exercise 19.** [*HM25*] (R. L. Duncan.) Prove that the leading digits of Fibonacci numbers obey the logarithmic law of fraction parts: $\Pr(10f_{F_n} < r) = \log_{10} r$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m29s  

---

Let $F_n$ be Fibonacci numbers and define

$$
x_n=\{\log_{10}F_n\}.
$$

The statement $10f_{F_n}<r$ is equivalent to

$$
x_n < \log_{10} r, \qquad 1 \le r < 10,
$$

so it suffices to prove that $(x_n)$ is uniformly distributed in $[0,1)$.

## 1. Asymptotic form of $\log_{10} F_n$

Using Binet’s formula,

$$
F_n=\frac{\varphi^n-\psi^n}{\sqrt{5}}, \qquad \varphi=\frac{1+\sqrt5}{2},\ \psi=\frac{1-\sqrt5}{2},
$$

we obtain

$$
\log_{10} F_n
= n\log_{10}\varphi - \log_{10}\sqrt5 + \log_{10}\!\left(1-\left(\frac{\psi}{\varphi}\right)^n\right).
$$

Set

$$
\alpha=\log_{10}\varphi, \qquad \beta=-\log_{10}\sqrt5, \qquad \varepsilon_n=\log_{10}\!\left(1-\left(\frac{\psi}{\varphi}\right)^n\right).
$$

Since $|\psi/\varphi|<1$, we have $\varepsilon_n \to 0$. Hence

$$
\log_{10} F_n = n\alpha + \beta + \varepsilon_n,
\qquad
x_n=\{n\alpha+\beta+\varepsilon_n\}.
$$

## 2. Irrationality of $\alpha=\log_{10}\varphi$

We show $\alpha\notin\mathbb{Q}$.

Assume $\log_{10}\varphi = p/q$ with integers $p,q>0$. Then

$$
\varphi^q = 10^p.
$$

Write $\varphi^q$ using the standard identity

$$
\varphi^n = F_n \varphi + F_{n-1},
$$

so

$$
\varphi^q = F_q \varphi + F_{q-1}.
$$

If this equals an integer $10^p$, then the coefficient of $\varphi$ must vanish, so $F_q=0$, impossible for $q\ge1$. Hence $\alpha$ is irrational.

## 3. Uniform distribution of $n\alpha$

By Weyl’s criterion, for irrational $\alpha$, the sequence

$$
y_n = \{n\alpha\}
$$

is uniformly distributed modulo $1$. Adding the constant $\beta$ preserves uniform distribution, so

$$
z_n=\{n\alpha+\beta\}
$$

is uniformly distributed in $[0,1)$.

## 4. Removing the vanishing perturbation (corrected step)

We now justify rigorously that

$$
x_n = \{n\alpha+\beta+\varepsilon_n\}
\quad \text{inherits uniform distribution from } z_n.
$$

Let $I=[a,b)\subset[0,1)$. Fix $\eta>0$. Since $\varepsilon_n\to0$, there exists $N$ such that for all $n\ge N$,

$$
|\varepsilon_n|<\eta.
$$

For such $n$, we have the inclusions

$$
z_n \in [a+\eta,b-\eta) \;\Rightarrow\; x_n \in I \;\Rightarrow\; z_n \in [a-\eta,b+\eta).
$$

Hence, for $n\ge N$,

$$
\mathbf{1}_{[a+\eta,b-\eta)}(z_n)
\le \mathbf{1}_I(x_n)
\le \mathbf{1}_{[a-\eta,b+\eta)}(z_n).
$$

Summing and dividing by $N$,

$$
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^N \mathbf{1}_{[a+\eta,b-\eta)}(z_n)
\le
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^N \mathbf{1}_I(x_n)
\le
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^N \mathbf{1}_{[a-\eta,b+\eta)}(z_n)
+ o(1),
$$

where the $o(1)$ term accounts for finitely many initial indices.

Taking $N\to\infty$ and using uniform distribution of $z_n$,

$$
b-a-2\eta
\le
\liminf_{N\to\infty}\frac{1}{N}\#\{n\le N:x_n\in I\}
\le
\limsup_{N\to\infty}\frac{1}{N}\#\{n\le N:x_n\in I\}
\le
b-a+2\eta.
$$

Since $\eta>0$ is arbitrary, letting $\eta\to0$ gives

$$
\lim_{N\to\infty}\frac{1}{N}\#\{n\le N:x_n\in I\}=b-a.
$$

Thus $(x_n)$ is uniformly distributed mod $1$.

## 5. Conclusion (Benford law)

For $1\le r<10$,

$$
\Pr(10f_{F_n}<r)
=
\lim_{N\to\infty}\frac{1}{N}\#\{n\le N:x_n<\log_{10} r\}.
$$

By uniform distribution of $x_n$,

$$
\Pr(10f_{F_n}<r)=\log_{10} r.
$$

This is exactly the logarithmic (Benford) law for leading digits of Fibonacci numbers. ∎
