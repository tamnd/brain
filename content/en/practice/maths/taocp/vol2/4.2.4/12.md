[Section 4.2.4: Distribution of Floating Point Numbers](../)

**Exercise 12.** [*HM25*] (R. W. Hamming.) The purpose of this exercise is to show that the result of floating point multiplication tends to obey the logarithmic law more perfectly than the operands do. Let $U$ and $V$ be random, normalized, positive floating point numbers, whose fraction parts are independently distributed with the respective density functions $f(x)$ and $g(x)$. Thus, $\frac{1}{b} \le r \le s$ and $f, g \le s$ with probability $\int_{1/b}^s \int_{1/b}^s f(x)g(y)\,dy\,dx$, for $1/b \le r, s \le 1$. Let $h(x)$ be the density function of the fraction part of $U \times V$ (unnormalized). Define the *abnormality* $A(f)$ of a density function $f$ to be the maximum relative error,

$$A(f) = \max_{1/b \le x \le 1} \left| \frac{f(x) - l(x)}{l(x)} \right|,$$

where $l(x) = 1/(x \ln b)$ is the density of the logarithmic distribution. Show that $A(h) \le \min(A(f), A(g))$. (In particular, if either factor has logarithmic distribution the product does also.)

Work in logarithmic coordinates where the structure of floating-point multiplication becomes a probability-preserving convolution, and the abnormality becomes a supremum norm distance from the constant density.

## 1. Logarithmic coordinates

Let $X \in [1/b,1]$ have density $f$, and define the logarithmic variable

$$
u = -\log_b x \in [0,1], \qquad x = b^{-u}.
$$

Then

$$
dx = (\ln b)\, b^{-u}\,du,
$$

so the induced density $\phi$ of $u$ is

$$
\phi(u) = f(b^{-u}) (\ln b)\, b^{-u}.
$$

For the logarithmic density

$$
l(x)=\frac{1}{x\ln b},
$$

we get

$$
\phi_L(u)=1,
$$

so the logarithmic distribution corresponds exactly to the uniform distribution on $[0,1]$.

Hence abnormality becomes

$$
A(f)=\|\phi-1\|_\infty, \qquad A(g)=\|\psi-1\|_\infty,
$$

and similarly $A(h)=\|\eta-1\|_\infty$, where $\eta$ is the density of the product in $u$-space.

The change of variables is exact and multiplicative Jacobian factors cancel in the ratio $f/l$, so supremum relative error in $x$-space equals supremum deviation from $1$ in $u$-space.

## 2. Product becomes circular convolution

If $U',V'$ are the logarithmic variables of $U,V$, then

$$
W = (U' + V') \bmod 1
$$

has density

$$
\eta = \phi * \psi,
$$

where convolution is taken on the unit circle:

$$
(\phi * \psi)(t)=\int_0^1 \phi(t-s)\psi(s)\,ds.
$$

Thus

$$
\eta - 1 = (\phi-1)*\psi,
$$

since $\int_0^1 \psi = 1$.

Similarly,

$$
\eta - 1 = \phi*(\psi-1).
$$

## 3. Key contraction property

Because $\psi$ is a probability density, convolution with $\psi$ is an averaging operator. For every $t$,

$$
|(\phi-1)*\psi(t)|
= \left|\int_0^1 (\phi(t-s)-1)\psi(s)\,ds\right|.
$$

Using the triangle inequality,

$$
\le \int_0^1 |\phi(t-s)-1|\psi(s)\,ds
\le \|\phi-1\|_\infty \int_0^1 \psi(s)\,ds.
$$

Since $\int_0^1 \psi(s)\,ds = 1$,

$$
|(\phi-1)*\psi(t)| \le \|\phi-1\|_\infty.
$$

Taking the supremum over $t$,

$$
\|\eta-1\|_\infty \le \|\phi-1\|_\infty.
$$

Hence

$$
A(h) \le A(f).
$$

By symmetry (using $\eta-1 = \phi*(\psi-1)$),

$$
A(h) \le A(g).
$$

## 4. Combining the bounds

From the two inequalities,

$$
A(h) \le A(f), \qquad A(h) \le A(g),
$$

we conclude

$$
A(h) \le \min(A(f),A(g)).
$$

## 5. Consistency with logarithmic law

If $f=l$, then $\phi \equiv 1$, so

$$
\eta = 1 * \psi = 1,
$$

hence $h=l$. The same holds if $g=l$. Thus the logarithmic distribution is a fixed point of floating-point multiplication.

## Conclusion

In logarithmic coordinates, floating-point multiplication becomes circular convolution on $[0,1]$, and the logarithmic density becomes the uniform density. Convolution with a probability density is an $L^\infty$-contraction around the uniform distribution, giving

$$
A(h) \le A(f), \qquad A(h) \le A(g),
$$

and therefore

$$
\boxed{A(h) \le \min(A(f),A(g)).}
$$
