[Section 4.2.4: Distribution of Floating Point Numbers](../)

**Exercise 12.** [*HM25*] (R. W. Hamming.) The purpose of this exercise is to show that the result of floating point multiplication tends to obey the logarithmic law more perfectly than the operands do. Let $U$ and $V$ be random, normalized, positive floating point numbers, whose fraction parts are independently distributed with the respective density functions $f(x)$ and $g(x)$. Thus, $\frac{1}{b} \le r \le s$ and $f, g \le s$ with probability $\int_{1/b}^s \int_{1/b}^s f(x)g(y)\,dy\,dx$, for $1/b \le r, s \le 1$. Let $h(x)$ be the density function of the fraction part of $U \times V$ (unnormalized). Define the *abnormality* $A(f)$ of a density function $f$ to be the maximum relative error,

$$A(f) = \max_{1/b \le x \le 1} \left| \frac{f(x) - l(x)}{l(x)} \right|,$$

where $l(x) = 1/(x \ln b)$ is the density of the logarithmic distribution. Show that $A(h) \le \min(A(f), A(g))$. (In particular, if either factor has logarithmic distribution the product does also.)

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m06s

## Solution

Let $x \in [1/b,1]$. Define

$l(x)=\frac{1}{x\ln b}.$

Assume $f$ and $g$ are densities on $[1/b,1]$ and that $U$ and $V$ are independent. Let $h$ be the density of the fraction part of $UV$ (before normalization). Work on logarithmic coordinates.

Define the change of variable

$x=b^{-u}, \quad u \in [0,1],$

so that

$u=-\log_b x,\quad dx = -(\ln b)\, b^{-u}\,du.$

For a density $f(x)$ on $[1/b,1]$, define the associated density on $[0,1]$ by

$\phi(u)=f(b^{-u}) (\ln b)\, b^{-u}.$

Similarly define

$\psi(u)=g(b^{-u}) (\ln b)\, b^{-u}.$

For the logarithmic density,

$l(b^{-u})(\ln b)\, b^{-u}=1,$

so uniform density on $[0,1]$ corresponds exactly to the logarithmic law.

The fraction part of $UV$ corresponds to addition modulo $1$ of logarithms:

$-\log_b(UV) \equiv -\log_b U - \log_b V \pmod 1.$

Hence the density $\eta$ of the transformed product variable is the circular convolution on $[0,1]$:

$\eta(u)=\int_0^1 \phi(t)\,\psi(u-t)\,dt,$

where subtraction is taken modulo $1$.

Define the abnormality in logarithmic coordinates:

$A(f)=\sup_{u \in [0,1]} |\phi(u)-1|,$

and similarly for $g$ and $h$:

$A(h)=\sup_{u \in [0,1]} |\eta(u)-1|.$

Since both $\phi$ and $\psi$ integrate to $1$ on $[0,1]$, we may write

$\eta(u)-1=\int_0^1 (\phi(t)-1)\psi(u-t)\,dt.$

Hence

\le \int_0^1 |\phi(t)-1|\,\psi(u-t)\,dt.$$
Because $\psi \ge 0$ and $\int_0^1 \psi(v)\,dv=1$, it follows that
$$|\eta(u)-1| \le \left(\sup_{t \in [0,1]} |\phi(t)-1|\right)\int_0^1 \psi(v)\,dv = A(f).$$
Thus
$$A(h)\le A(f).$$
Interchanging the roles of $f$ and $g$ yields similarly
$$A(h)\le A(g).$$
Combining both inequalities gives
$$A(h)\le \min(A(f),A(g)).$$ This completes the proof. ∎
