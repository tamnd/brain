[Section 4.2.4: Distribution of Floating Point Numbers](../)

**Exercise 14.** &#9654; [*HM30*] Let $U$ and $V$ be random, normalized, positive floating point numbers whose fraction parts are independently distributed according to the logarithmic law, and let $p_k$ be the probability that the difference in their exponents is $k$. Assuming that the distribution of the exponents is independent of the fraction parts, give an equation for the probability that "fraction overflow" occurs during the floating point addition of $U \oplus V$, in terms of the quantities $p_0, p_1, p_2, \ldots$ . Compare this result with exercise 1. (Ignore rounding.)

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m19s  

---

Let

$$
U=b^{e_u}f_u,\qquad V=b^{e_v}f_v,
$$

where $U,V>0$ are normalized floating-point numbers in base $b$, so

$$
\frac{1}{b}\le f_u,f_v<1.
$$

Assume the fraction parts $f_u,f_v$ are independent and logarithmically distributed, and independent of the exponent difference.

Let

$$
k = |e_u-e_v|,\qquad p_k=\Pr(|e_u-e_v|=k).
$$

## 1. Conditioning on the exponent difference

Without loss of generality assume $e_u\ge e_v$. Then after alignment,

$$
U+V = b^{e_u}\bigl(f_u + b^{-k}f_v\bigr).
$$

A fraction overflow occurs exactly when normalization requires a right shift, i.e. when

$$
f_u + b^{-k}f_v \ge 1.
$$

Define

$$
q_k=\Pr\!\bigl(f_u + b^{-k}f_v \ge 1\bigr).
$$

By conditioning on $k$,

$$
\Pr(\text{overflow})=\sum_{k\ge 0} p_k\,q_k.
$$

## 2. Expression for $q_k$

Since $f_u,f_v$ are independent with logarithmic density

$$
\ell(x)=\frac{1}{x\ln b},\qquad \frac{1}{b}\le x<1,
$$

we obtain

$$
q_k
=\int_{1/b}^{1}\int_{1/b}^{1}
\mathbf{1}\!\left(x+b^{-k}y\ge 1\right)
\frac{1}{x\ln b}\frac{1}{y\ln b}\,dy\,dx.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
q_k
=\int_{1/b}^{1}\frac{1}{x\ln b}
\left(\int_{\max(1/b,\;b^{k}(1-x))}^{1}\frac{1}{y\ln b}\,dy\right)dx.
$$

This completely determines $q_k$ for each $k$, hence determines the overflow probability.

## 3. Final form

$$
\boxed{
\Pr(\text{fraction overflow})
=\sum_{k\ge 0} p_k\,q_k
}
$$

with $q_k$ given above.

## 4. Comparison with Exercise 1

In Exercise 1, the analysis corresponds to a fixed exponent configuration (or equivalently a fixed alignment case), so the overflow probability is computed directly for a single exponent difference $k$, namely $q_k$, without averaging over different values of $k$.

In the present problem, the exponent difference is itself random with distribution $p_k$. The result therefore generalizes Exercise 1 by taking an expectation over all possible alignment cases:

$$
\Pr(\text{overflow})=\mathbb{E}[q_k]=\sum_{k\ge 0} p_k q_k.
$$

Thus, Exercise 1 provides the conditional probability of overflow given a fixed shift amount, while this exercise incorporates the stochastic variability of the exponent gap by mixing those conditional probabilities using the weights $p_k$.
