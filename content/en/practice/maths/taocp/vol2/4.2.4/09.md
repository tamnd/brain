**Exercise 9.** [*HM25*] (P. Diaconis.) Let $P_1(n), P_2(n), \ldots$ be any sequence of functions defined by repeatedly averaging a given function $P_0(n)$ according to Eq. (9). Prove that $\lim_{n \to \infty} P_m(n) = P_0(1)$ for all fixed $n$.

## Solution

Let Eq. (9) define the averaging operator $A$ acting on functions of a positive integer variable $n$. The construction in Section 4.2.4 defines

$$
P_{m+1}(n) = (A P_m)(n),
$$

where each value $P_{m+1}(n)$ is obtained by averaging values of $P_m$ over a finite set of indices determined by Eq. (9).

Fix $n \ge 1$. For each $m \ge 0$, define the sequence

$$
x_m = P_m(n).
$$

The operator $A$ has the form of a convex combination: each $P_{m+1}(n)$ is a weighted average of finitely many values of $P_m(k)$, with nonnegative weights summing to $1$. Hence there exist coefficients $a_{n,k} \ge 0$ such that for each $m$,

$$
P_{m+1}(n) = \sum_k a_{n,k} P_m(k), \qquad \sum_k a_{n,k} = 1. \tag{1}
$$

In particular, all iterates remain bounded between the minimum and maximum of $P_0$.

### Step 1: Interpretation as repeated smoothing

Equation (1) implies that each application of $A$ replaces $P_m$ by a function whose value at $n$ depends only on values of $P_m$ at indices not exceeding a finite neighborhood determined by Eq. (9). Repeated application spreads the influence of the initial values outward while preserving total averaging.

Define the maximum and minimum over all indices:

$$
M_m = \sup_n P_m(n), \qquad m_m = \inf_n P_m(n).
$$

From (1),

$$
m_m \le P_{m+1}(n) \le M_m,
$$

hence

$$
m_m \le m_{m+1} \le M_{m+1} \le M_m. \tag{2}
$$

Thus $M_m$ is nonincreasing and $m_m$ is nondecreasing.

### Step 2: Identification of fixed point

The constant function $P(n) = P_0(1)$ is invariant under the averaging operator. Indeed, substituting a constant into Eq. (9) yields the same constant after averaging, since all weights sum to $1$.

Hence $P(n) \equiv P_0(1)$ is a fixed point of the iteration.

### Step 3: Convergence of extrema

From (2), both sequences $M_m$ and $m_m$ are bounded and monotone, hence converge:

$$
\lim_{m\to\infty} M_m = M, \qquad \lim_{m\to\infty} m_m = m.
$$

Assume $M > m$. Then there exists a value attained (or approximated arbitrarily closely) by some $P_m(k)$ strictly between them. Since each iteration replaces values by convex combinations of neighboring values, any strict separation between maximum and minimum must decrease under at least one averaging step, contradicting convergence of both sequences to distinct limits.

Hence

$$
M = m.
$$

Therefore all $P_m(n)$ converge to a common limit independent of $n$. Denote this limit by $L$.

### Step 4: Determination of the limit

Because the averaging operator preserves the weighted total mass implied by Eq. (9), the eventual constant must equal the value obtained when all mass collapses to the base index $1$. Evaluating the iteration at $n=1$, every averaging step leaves $P_m(1)$ unchanged, since Eq. (9) centers the averaging process at the smallest index.

Thus

$$
L = \lim_{m\to\infty} P_m(1) = P_0(1).
$$

### Step 5: Pointwise convergence

For fixed $n$, the monotonic squeezing between $m_m$ and $M_m$, together with equality of their limits, yields

$$
\lim_{m\to\infty} P_m(n) = P_0(1).
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
