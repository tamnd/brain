[Section 4.2.4: Distribution of Floating Point Numbers](../)

**Exercise 1.** [*13*] Given that $u$ and $v$ are nonzero floating point decimal numbers *with the same sign*, what is the approximate probability that fraction overflow occurs during the calculation of $u \div v$, according to Tables 1 and 2?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m16s

## Solution

Write the floating point decimal numbers in normalized form:

$$
u=10^{e_u}f_u,\qquad v=10^{e_v}f_v,
$$

where

$$
0.1\le f_u,f_v<1,
$$

and $u$ and $v$ have the same sign.

When computing $u\div v$,

$$
\frac{u}{v}
   =10^{\,e_u-e_v}\frac{f_u}{f_v}.
$$

Since $0.1\le f_u,f_v<1$,

$$
0.1<\frac{f_u}{f_v}<10.
$$

Thus the quotient fraction can never be less than $0.1$, and fraction overflow occurs precisely when the fraction part is at least $1$. Therefore overflow occurs iff

$$
\frac{f_u}{f_v}\ge 1,
$$

that is,

$$
f_u\ge f_v.
$$

According to the statistical model derived from Tables 1 and 2 in Section 4.2.4B, the normalized fraction $f$ satisfies

$$
P(f\le t)\approx \log_{10}(10t),
\qquad 0.1\le t<1.
$$

Define

$$
X=\log_{10}(10f).
$$

Then

$$
P(X\le x)
 =P\!\left(f\le 10^{x-1}\right)
 \approx \log_{10}\!\bigl(10\cdot 10^{x-1}\bigr)
 =x,
\qquad 0\le x\le 1.
$$

Hence $X$ is approximately uniformly distributed on $[0,1]$.

If $u$ and $v$ are chosen independently, then

$$
X_u=\log_{10}(10f_u),\qquad
X_v=\log_{10}(10f_v)
$$

are independent uniform random variables on $[0,1]$.

Because the logarithm is increasing,

$$
f_u\ge f_v
\iff
10f_u\ge 10f_v
\iff
X_u\ge X_v.
$$

Therefore

$$
P(\text{fraction overflow})
 =P(f_u\ge f_v)
 =P(X_u\ge X_v).
$$

The point $(X_u,X_v)$ is uniformly distributed over the unit square

$$
0\le X_u\le 1,\qquad 0\le X_v\le 1.
$$

The diagonal $X_u=X_v$ divides this square into two congruent triangles, so

$$
P(X_u\ge X_v)=\frac12.
$$

Hence the approximate probability of fraction overflow during the computation of $u\div v$ is

$$
\boxed{\frac12}.
$$

Thus fraction overflow is expected in approximately $50\%$ of such divisions.
