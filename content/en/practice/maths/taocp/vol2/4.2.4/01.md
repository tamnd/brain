[Section 4.2.4: Distribution of Floating Point Numbers](../)

**Exercise 1.** [*13*] Given that $u$ and $v$ are nonzero floating point decimal numbers *with the same sign*, what is the approximate probability that fraction overflow occurs during the calculation of $u \div v$, according to Tables 1 and 2?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m14s

## Solution

Let $u = f_u \cdot b^{e_u}$ and $v = f_v \cdot b^{e_v}$ be normalized floating point numbers with the same sign, where the fraction parts satisfy

$b^{-1} \le f_u < 1,\qquad b^{-1} \le f_v < 1.$

In floating point division,

$\frac{u}{v} = \left(\frac{f_u}{f_v}\right)b^{e_u-e_v}.$

A _fraction overflow_ occurs exactly when the intermediate quotient of fraction parts satisfies

$\frac{f_u}{f_v} \ge 1,$

since the resulting fraction must then be normalized by a shift to the right and a corresponding increase of the exponent.

Thus the problem reduces to computing

$\Pr(f_u \ge f_v).$

The distribution of normalized fraction parts induced by floating point representation is invariant under scaling by powers of $b$, and the standard model in Section 4.2.4 treats the fraction parts as identically distributed on $[b^{-1},1)$ with logarithmic density proportional to $1/x$. Under this distribution, the pair $(f_u,f_v)$ is exchangeable, so

$\Pr(f_u \ge f_v) = \Pr(f_v \ge f_u).$

Since ties have probability $0$ in a continuous model,

$\Pr(f_u \ge f_v) + \Pr(f_v > f_u) = 1,$

and symmetry gives equality of the two terms, hence

$\Pr(f_u \ge f_v) = \frac{1}{2}.$

Therefore, the probability that fraction overflow occurs during $u \div v$ is

$\boxed{\tfrac{1}{2}}.$

This completes the proof. ∎
