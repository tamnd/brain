**Exercise 13.** &#9654; [*M20*] The floating point multiplication routine, Algorithm 4.2.1M, requires zero or one left shifts during normalization, depending on whether $f_u f_v \ge 1/b$ or not. Assuming that the input operands are independently distributed according to the logarithmic law, what is the probability that no left shift is needed for normalization of the result?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m11s  

---

## Solution

Let $X$ and $Y$ denote the fraction parts of the normalized floating point numbers $u$ and $v$. Under the logarithmic law in base $b$, their density is

$l(x) = \frac{1}{x \ln b}, \qquad \frac{1}{b} \le x < 1,$

and $X$ and $Y$ are independent.

In Algorithm 4.2.1M, no left shift is required during normalization of the product if and only if

$XY \ge \frac{1}{b}.$

Hence the desired probability is

$\Pr(XY \ge 1/b) = 1 - \Pr(XY < 1/b).$

For fixed $x$, the inequality $xy < 1/b$ is equivalent to $y < 1/(bx)$. Since $x \ge 1/b$, one has $1/(bx) \le 1$, and since $x \le 1$, one has $1/(bx) \ge 1/b$. The event $XY < 1/b$ therefore corresponds to

$\frac{1}{b} \le y < \frac{1}{bx}, \qquad \frac{1}{b} \le x \le 1.$

The probability of this region is

$\int_{1/b}^{1} \int_{1/b}^{1/(bx)} \frac{1}{x \ln b} \cdot \frac{1}{y \ln b} \, dy \, dx.$

The inner integral is

$\int_{1/b}^{1/(bx)} \frac{1}{y} \, dy = \ln\left(\frac{1}{bx}\right) - \ln\left(\frac{1}{b}\right).$

Using $\ln(1/(bx)) = -\ln b - \ln x$ and $\ln(1/b) = -\ln b$, this equals $-\ln x$. The double integral becomes

$\frac{1}{(\ln b)^2} \int_{1/b}^{1} \frac{-\ln x}{x} \, dx.$

Let $t = \ln x$, so $dt = dx/x$. When $x = 1/b$, $t = -\ln b$, and when $x = 1$, $t = 0$. The integral transforms to

$\frac{1}{(\ln b)^2} \int_{-\ln b}^{0} -t \, dt.$

Evaluation yields

$\int_{-\ln b}^{0} -t \, dt = \left[-\frac{t^2}{2}\right]_{-\ln b}^{0} = \frac{(\ln b)^2}{2}.$

Hence

$\Pr(XY < 1/b) = \frac{1}{(\ln b)^2} \cdot \frac{(\ln b)^2}{2} = \frac{1}{2}.$

Therefore,

$\Pr(XY \ge 1/b) = 1 - \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{2}.$

$\boxed{\frac{1}{2}}$
