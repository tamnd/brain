**Exercise 15.** [*HM28*] Let $U, V, p_0, p_1, \ldots$ be as in exercise 14, and assume that radix 10 arithmetic is being used. Show that regardless of the values of $p_0, p_1, p_2, \ldots$, the sum $U \oplus V$ will not obey the logarithmic law exactly; and in fact the probability that $U \oplus V$ has leading digit 1 is always strictly *less* than $\log_{10} 2$.

Let $U=10^aX$, $V=10^bY$, where $X,Y\in[1,10)$ are independent and satisfy Benford’s law on $[1,10)$, i.e.

$$
f(x)=\frac{1}{x\ln 10},\qquad 1\le x<10.
$$

Let $p_k=\Pr(a-b=k)$, independent of $(X,Y)$. Fix radix $10$.

For $k\in\mathbb Z$, set $t=10^{-k}\in(0,10]$. Conditioning on $a-b=k$, we may write (after factoring out $10^a$):

$$
U\oplus V = 10^a\,W,\qquad W=\mathrm{normalize}(S),\quad S=X+tY.
$$

The leading digit of $U\oplus V$ is $1$ iff $W\in[1,2)$.

## 1. Exact reduction of the event

Since $S\in[1,20)$ (because $X,Y<10$ and $t\le 1$), normalization gives:

- if $1\le S<10$, then $W=S$;
- if $10\le S<20$, then $W=S/10$.

Hence

$$
W\in[1,2)
\iff S\in[1,2)\ \cup\ [10,20).
$$

So for fixed $k$,

$$
\Pr(W\in[1,2)\mid k)=\Pr(S\in[1,2)\cup[10,20)).
$$

We now prove this is strictly $<\log_{10}2$ for every $t>0$.

## 2. Conditional probability for fixed $Y=y$

Fix $y\in[1,10)$. Condition further on $Y=y$. Then $S=X+ty$, so we integrate over $X$.

We compute the set of $x\in[1,10)$ such that $S\in[1,2)\cup[10,20)$.

### (i) Contribution to $S\in[1,2)$

$$
x+ty\in[1,2)\ \Longleftrightarrow\ x\in[1-ty,\,2-ty).
$$

Intersecting with $[1,10)$, this contributes

$$
x\in[1,\,2-ty).
$$

### (ii) Contribution to $S\in[10,20)$

$$
x+ty\in[10,20)\ \Longleftrightarrow\ x\in[10-ty,\,20-ty).
$$

Intersecting with $[1,10)$, this becomes

$$
x\in[10-ty,\,10).
$$

Therefore, for fixed $y$,

$$
\Pr(W\in[1,2)\mid Y=y, k)
=
\int_{1}^{2-ty}\frac{dx}{x\ln 10}
+
\int_{10-ty}^{10}\frac{dx}{x\ln 10}.
$$

## 3. Comparison with the Benford benchmark

For a single Benford variable $X$,

$$
\Pr(X\in[1,2))=\int_1^2 \frac{dx}{x\ln 10}=\log_{10}2.
$$

We compare:

$$
\Pr(W\in[1,2)\mid Y=y,k) - \log_{10}2
=
-\int_{2-ty}^{2}\frac{dx}{x\ln 10}
+
\int_{10-ty}^{10}\frac{dx}{x\ln 10}.
$$

Let $I_{\mathrm{loss}}(y)$ and $I_{\mathrm{gain}}(y)$ denote the two terms.

## 4. Key inequality: loss dominates gain

Since $f(x)=1/(x\ln 10)$ is strictly decreasing on $[1,10]$:

- For $x\in[2-ty,2]$, we have $f(x)\ge f(2)$.
- For $x\in[10-ty,10]$, we have $f(x)\le f(10-ty)$.

Both intervals have length $ty$. Hence:

$$
I_{\mathrm{loss}}(y)\ \ge\ ty\cdot \frac{1}{2\ln 10},
$$

$$
I_{\mathrm{gain}}(y)\ \le\ ty\cdot \frac{1}{(10-ty)\ln 10}.
$$

Since $10-ty>2$, we have

$$
\frac{1}{10-ty} < \frac{1}{2},
$$

so

$$
I_{\mathrm{gain}}(y) < I_{\mathrm{loss}}(y).
$$

Therefore,

$$
\Pr(W\in[1,2)\mid Y=y,k) < \log_{10}2
\quad\text{for every } y\in[1,10),\ t>0.
$$

## 5. Remove conditioning on $Y$

Integrate over $Y$:

$$
\Pr(W\in[1,2)\mid k)
=
\int_{1}^{10}\Pr(W\in[1,2)\mid Y=y,k)\,f(y)\,dy.
$$

Since the integrand is strictly $<\log_{10}2$ for all $y$, we obtain

$$
\Pr(W\in[1,2)\mid k) < \log_{10}2
\quad\text{for every } k.
$$

## 6. Average over exponent differences

Finally,

$$
\Pr(\text{leading digit }1)
=
\sum_k p_k\,\Pr(W\in[1,2)\mid k).
$$

Each term is strictly less than $\log_{10}2$, hence

$$
\Pr(\text{leading digit }1) < \log_{10}2.
$$

## 7. Conclusion

The floating-point sum introduces a deterministic shift $X\mapsto X+10^{-k}Y$ followed by possible scaling by $10$. This produces a strict imbalance: the mass leaving $[1,2)$ occurs in a higher-density region of the Benford distribution than the compensating mass entering via normalization near $10$. The inequality is strict for every $k$, hence remains strict after averaging.

$$
\boxed{\Pr(\text{leading digit of }U\oplus V\text{ is }1)\;<\;\log_{10}2.}
$$
