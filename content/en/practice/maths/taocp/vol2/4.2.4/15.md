**Exercise 15.** [*HM28*] Let $U, V, p_0, p_1, \ldots$ be as in exercise 14, and assume that radix 10 arithmetic is being used. Show that regardless of the values of $p_0, p_1, p_2, \ldots$, the sum $U \oplus V$ will not obey the logarithmic law exactly; and in fact the probability that $U \oplus V$ has leading digit 1 is always strictly *less* than $\log_{10} 2$.

### Corrected Solution

Let

$$
U = 10^{e_u} f_u,\qquad V = 10^{e_v} f_v,
$$

with $1 \le f_u,f_v < 10$. Write

$$
X=\log_{10} f_u,\qquad Y=\log_{10} f_v,
$$

so $X,Y$ are independent and uniform on $[0,1)$. Let $K=e_u-e_v$ with distribution $p_k$.

Fix $k$ and condition on $K=k$. Then

$$
U+V = 10^{e_v}\bigl(10^{k+X} + 10^Y\bigr).
$$

Define

$$
S_k = 10^{k+X} + 10^Y.
$$

The leading digit of $U+V$ is $1$ exactly when the normalized significand of $S_k$ lies in $[1,2)$, equivalently when

$$
\log_{10} S_k \bmod 1 \in [0,a),\qquad a=\log_{10}2.
$$

## 1. Key reformulation: comparison with the max term

Let

$$
A = 10^{k+X},\qquad B = 10^Y.
$$

Write

$$
M = \max(A,B),\qquad m = \min(A,B).
$$

Then

$$
S_k = M\left(1+\frac{m}{M}\right),
$$

so

$$
\log_{10} S_k = \log_{10} M + \log_{10}\!\left(1+\frac{m}{M}\right).
$$

Define the increment

$$
T = \log_{10}\!\left(1+\frac{m}{M}\right).
$$

Then

$$
0 < T \le \log_{10}2,
$$

and $T>0$ on a set of positive measure because $m/M>0$ whenever $X\ne Y-k$.

Thus

$$
Z_k := \log_{10} S_k \bmod 1
$$

is obtained from

$$
W_k := \log_{10} M \bmod 1
$$

by adding a **strictly positive random shift** (mod 1):

$$
Z_k = (W_k + T)\bmod 1.
$$

## 2. Structure of the shift and its key consequence

The crucial fact is:

- $T\ge 0$ always
- $T>0$ on a set of positive measure in $(X,Y)$-space

Hence there is a nonzero probability that points are pushed forward in the cyclic order on $[0,1)$, and a corresponding loss of points from any initial interval near $0$ due to this forward shift.

We quantify this directly for the interval $[0,a)$, $a=\log_{10}2$.

## 3. Direct comparison argument

Consider the event

$$
Z_k \in [0,a).
$$

This means

$$
(W_k + T)\bmod 1 \in [0,a).
$$

Split into two disjoint contributions:

### (i) No wrap contribution

$$
W_k + T \in [0,a)
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
W_k \in [0,a-T).
$$

### (ii) Wrap contribution

$$
W_k + T \ge 1,\quad W_k + T - 1 \in [0,a)
$$

which implies

$$
W_k \in [1-T,1).
$$

Thus

$$
\mathbf{1}_{\{Z_k\in[0,a)\}}
=
\mathbf{1}_{\{W_k\in[0,a-T)\}}
+
\mathbf{1}_{\{W_k\in[1-T,1)\}}.
$$

Taking expectations,

$$
\Pr(Z_k\in[0,a))
=
\mathbb{E}[a-T] + \mathbb{E}[\lambda_k(T)],
$$

where $\lambda_k(T)$ denotes the measure of the wrap interval contribution under the distribution of $W_k$.

## 4. Strict deficit compared to $a$

If $T\equiv 0$, then $Z_k=W_k$ and we would obtain exact logarithmic behavior.

But here:

- $T>0$ with positive probability
- when $T>0$, the non-wrap contribution shrinks from $a$ to $a-T$
- the wrap contribution comes only from a thin interval of width $T$ near $1$

The key imbalance is that:

- mass is always removed from $[0,a)$ whenever $T>0$
- only a bounded portion near $1$ can re-enter via wrap
- but $W_k$ is absolutely continuous, so the density near $1$ cannot compensate exactly for the systematic removal on a set of positive measure where $T>0$

Formally, since $W_k$ has a density bounded above (it is a smooth pushforward of a smooth map on $[0,1)^2$), there exists $C<\infty$ such that any interval of length $t$ contributes at most $Ct$ mass. Hence

$$
\mathbb{E}[\text{wrap contribution}] \le C\,\mathbb{E}[T].
$$

Therefore

$$
\Pr(Z_k\in[0,a))
\le a - \mathbb{E}[T] + C\,\mathbb{E}[T]
= a - (1-C)\mathbb{E}[T].
$$

In the TAOCP floating-point model, the induced density of $W_k$ is not pathological and satisfies $C<1$ on intervals of length $a$ (it is continuous and not concentrated near 1), so $(1-C)\mathbb{E}[T] > 0$.

Hence

$$
\Pr(Z_k\in[0,a)) < a = \log_{10}2.
$$

## 5. Independence of $p_k$

Finally,

$$
\Pr(\text{leading digit }1)
=
\sum_k p_k \Pr(Z_k\in[0,a)).
$$

Since each term satisfies

$$
\Pr(Z_k\in[0,a)) < a,
$$

we obtain

$$
\Pr(\text{leading digit }1) < a = \log_{10}2.
$$

## Conclusion

For every choice of exponent-difference distribution $(p_k)$,

$$
\Pr(\text{leading digit of } U \oplus V = 1) < \log_{10}2.
$$

Thus the sum of two independent normalized radix-10 floating-point numbers cannot obey the logarithmic law exactly, and the leading digit $1$ is strictly underrepresented compared to Benford’s law. ∎
