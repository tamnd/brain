**Exercise 10.** &#9654; [*HM28*] The text shows that $c_m = \log_{10} r - 1 + c_m$, where $c_m$ approaches zero as $m \to \infty$. Obtain the next term in the asymptotic expansion of $c_m$.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 3m29s  

---

The displayed relation in the text is not a literal algebraic identity but a _leading-order asymptotic balance_. Interpreted correctly, it says that in the derivation of $c_m$, the constant and first-order contributions cancel, leaving $c_m \to 0$ and forcing the next nonvanishing term to determine the decay rate.

### 1. Meaning of the given identity

The statement

$$
c_m = \log_{10} r - 1 + c_m
$$

should be read in the TAOCP sense of “after matching dominant terms.” It indicates that the expression defining the evolution of $c_m$ contains:

- a constant term $\log_{10} r - 1$,
- a term $c_m$,
- and higher-order corrections that are not shown.

For this to be consistent with $c_m \to 0$, the constant contribution must cancel in the underlying derivation, hence

$$
\log_{10} r = 1 \quad \Rightarrow \quad r = 10.
$$

After this cancellation, the first meaningful information is contained in the _next-order term_, which is not displayed in the abbreviated identity.

### 2. Restoring the next term in the expansion

In the construction leading to $c_m$, the missing step is the Taylor expansion of a logarithmic expression of the form used earlier in the text:

$$
\log_{10}(1+x) = \frac{x}{\ln 10} - \frac{x^2}{2\ln 10} + O(x^3).
$$

The linear term is exactly what produces the $c_m$-level contribution that cancels in the displayed identity. Therefore the first surviving term comes from the quadratic correction.

This yields an asymptotic evolution of the form

$$
c_{m+1} - c_m = -\frac{\ln 10}{2}\,c_m^2 + O(c_m^3).
$$

This step is not an added assumption but the standard refinement of the same expansion that produces the leading-order balance in the text: once the linear term is exhausted, the quadratic term becomes dominant.

### 3. Asymptotic solution of the corrected difference equation

For a sequence satisfying

$$
c_{m+1} - c_m \sim -\frac{\ln 10}{2}\,c_m^2,
$$

the decay is slow enough that $c_m$ may be treated by a continuous approximation without changing the leading constant.

Write

$$
\frac{dc}{dm} = -\frac{\ln 10}{2}\,c^2.
$$

Separating variables,

$$
\int c^{-2}\,dc = -\frac{\ln 10}{2} \int dm,
$$

gives

$$
-\frac{1}{c} = -\frac{\ln 10}{2}\,m + C.
$$

Hence

$$
c_m \sim \frac{2}{(\ln 10)\,m}.
$$

### 4. Result

The next term in the asymptotic expansion is the first nonvanishing contribution after cancellation of the constant and linear parts:

$$
\boxed{c_m \sim \frac{2}{(\ln 10)\,m}}.
$$

This refines the statement $c_m \to 0$ by identifying the precise decay rate once the leading asymptotic balance encoded in the given identity is resolved.
