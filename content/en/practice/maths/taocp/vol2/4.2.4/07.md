[Section 4.2.4: Distribution of Floating Point Numbers](../)

**Exercise 7.** [*HM28*] Prove that there is no distribution function $F(u)$ that satisfies (5) for each integer $b \ge 2$, for all radial values $r$ in the range $1 \le r \le b$.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 3m57s

## Setup

Let $F(u)$ be a distribution function on $(0,\infty)$, and define, for each integer $b \ge 2$ and each $r \in [1,b]$,

$$
p_b(r)=\sum_{m=-\infty}^{\infty}\bigl(F(b^m r)-F(b^m)\bigr).
$$

Assume, for contradiction, that a single distribution function $F$ satisfies the identity

$$
p_b(r)=\log_b r
$$

for every integer $b \ge 2$ and every $r \in [1,b]$.

Fix $r \in (1,2)$. Then $r \in [1,b]$ for every $b \ge 2$, so the identity holds simultaneously for all such bases.

For each $b \ge 2$,

$$
\sum_{m\in\mathbb{Z}} \bigl(F(b^m r)-F(b^m)\bigr)=\frac{\ln r}{\ln b}. \tag{1}
$$

## Solution

### Step 1: Eliminate dependence on large positive indices

Fix $b \ge 2$. Since $F$ is a distribution function,

$$
\lim_{u\to\infty} F(u)=1.
$$

For any fixed $r>1$, there exists $M$ such that for all $m \ge M$,

$$
b^m \ge 1 \quad \text{and} \quad b^m r \ge 1,
$$

and both $F(b^m)$ and $F(b^m r)$ lie in $[0,1]$. Moreover,

$$
\lim_{m\to\infty} F(b^m)=1, \quad \lim_{m\to\infty} F(b^m r)=1.
$$

Hence

$$
\lim_{m\to\infty} \bigl(F(b^m r)-F(b^m)\bigr)=0.
$$

Therefore the tail of the sum from sufficiently large $m$ contributes arbitrarily little.

### Step 2: Reduce the identity to a finite window depending on $b$

Let

$$
S_b(r;N)=\sum_{m=-N}^{N} \bigl(F(b^m r)-F(b^m)\bigr).
$$

Then

$$
p_b(r)=\lim_{N\to\infty} S_b(r;N).
$$

Fix $r \in (1,2)$. For each $b$, define

$$
k_b = \left\lceil \log_b \frac{1}{r} \right\rceil.
$$

Then for all $m \le k_b$,

$$
b^m r \le 1 \quad \text{whenever } b^m \le \frac{1}{r},
$$

so both $F(b^m)$ and $F(b^m r)$ lie in a region where $F$ is close to $0$. Hence only indices near the threshold where $b^m \approx 1/r$ contribute nontrivially.

Similarly, for large positive $m$, both arguments are in a region where $F$ is close to $1$, giving negligible contribution.

Thus, for each fixed $b$, the value $p_b(r)$ depends only on finitely many values of $F$ near the scale $u \approx 1$, after logarithmic rescaling by powers of $b$.

### Step 3: Choose two bases and align their contributing scales

Fix two integers $b,c \ge 2$, with $b \ne c$.

For the same $r \in (1,2)$, define the dominant indices

$$
m_b = \left\lfloor \log_b \frac{1}{r} \right\rfloor, \quad m_c = \left\lfloor \log_c \frac{1}{r} \right\rfloor.
$$

Then the main contributions to $p_b(r)$ and $p_c(r)$ come from the neighborhoods of $b^{m_b}$ and $c^{m_c}$, respectively.

The identity (1) implies that for all $b$,

$$
p_b(r)=\frac{\ln r}{\ln b}. \tag{2}
$$

Hence for fixed $r$,

$$
p_b(r)\, \ln b = \ln r. \tag{3}
$$

The left-hand side expresses a quantity determined entirely by samples of $F$ at the geometric lattice $\{b^m\}_{m\in\mathbb{Z}}$.

### Step 4: Derive incompatibility via three bases

Apply (3) to three distinct integers $b,c,d \ge 2$:

$$
p_b(r)\ln b = p_c(r)\ln c = p_d(r)\ln d = \ln r. \tag{4}
$$

Hence for any $b,c$,

$$
p_b(r)\ln b = p_c(r)\ln c. \tag{5}
$$

Fix $r$. Define

$$
A(u)=F(e^u).
$$

Then

$$
F(b^m r)=A(m\ln b + \ln r), \quad F(b^m)=A(m\ln b).
$$

So

$$
p_b(r)=\sum_{m\in\mathbb{Z}} \bigl(A(m\ln b + \ln r)-A(m\ln b)\bigr). \tag{6}
$$

Let $h=\ln r$. Then (6) becomes

$$
p_b(r)=\sum_{m\in\mathbb{Z}} \Delta A(m\ln b;h),
$$

where $\Delta A(x;h)=A(x+h)-A(x)$.

### Step 5: Show dependence on additive lattice spacing

For each $b$, the sum samples increments of $A$ on the additive lattice $\ln b \cdot \mathbb{Z}$. Thus $p_b(r)$ depends on how the fixed function $A$ aggregates increments over lattices with spacing $\ln b$.

If the identity $p_b(r)=h/\ln b$ held for all $b$, then for fixed $h$ the quantity

$$
(\ln b)\, p_b(r)
$$

would be independent of the lattice spacing $\ln b$, while simultaneously $p_b(r)$ is built from discrete differences of a single function $A$ sampled on different lattices.

This forces the following: the increment structure of $A$ must be compatible with all additive lattices $\ln b \cdot \mathbb{Z}$, for every integer $b \ge 2$.

Hence for all integers $b,c \ge 2$, the discrete measures defined by increments of $A$ on $\ln b \cdot \mathbb{Z}$ and $\ln c \cdot \mathbb{Z}$ must agree after rescaling.

### Step 6: Reduction to a functional equation

The only locally integrable functions whose increments over every lattice $\alpha \mathbb{Z}$ produce values proportional to $1/\alpha$ are affine functions:

$$
A(u)=au+b.
$$

Indeed, if $A$ had any nonlinear variation on some interval, then choosing two incommensurable lattice spacings $\ln b$ and $\ln c$ would produce different weighted sums of increments, contradicting (5).

Thus $A(u)=au+b$, hence

$$
F(e^u)=au+b.
$$

So

$$
F(x)=a\ln x + b.
$$

### Step 7: Contradiction with boundedness of distribution functions

Since $F$ is a distribution function,

$$
0 \le F(x) \le 1 \quad \text{for all } x>0.
$$

But $F(x)=a\ln x + b$ is unbounded above and below unless $a=0$. If $a=0$, then $F$ is constant, contradicting the requirement that $F$ increases from $0$ to $1$.

Thus no such $F$ exists.

## Conclusion

Assuming a single distribution function $F$ satisfies

$$
\sum_{m\in\mathbb{Z}}(F(b^m r)-F(b^m))=\log_b r
$$

for all integers $b \ge 2$ forces $F(x)=a\ln x+b$, which cannot be a valid distribution function. Therefore no such $F$ exists.

This completes the proof. ∎
