### Corrected solution

Let $\mu$ be a probability measure on $(0,\infty)$ with distribution function $F$. The condition is that for every integer $b\ge 2$ and every $r\in[1,b]$,

$$
\sum_{m\in\mathbb Z}\mu\bigl((b^m,b^m r]\bigr)=\log_b r.
\tag{1}
$$

## 1. Logarithmic reformulation

Define a measure $\nu$ on $\mathbb R$ by pushing forward $\mu$ under the map $x=e^t$:

$$
\nu(A)=\mu(e^A).
$$

Let $B=\log b$ and $a=\log r\in[0,B]$. Then

$$
(b^m,b^m r] \;\longleftrightarrow\; (mB,mB+a].
$$

Hence (1) becomes

$$
\nu\!\left(\bigcup_{m\in\mathbb Z}(mB,mB+a]\right)=\frac{a}{B},\qquad 0\le a\le B.
\tag{2}
$$

Define

$$
T_{B,a}=\bigcup_{m\in\mathbb Z}(mB,mB+a].
$$

## 2. Consequence: uniformity modulo $B$

Fix $B$. The sets $T_{B,a}$ correspond exactly to subsets of $\mathbb R$ of the form

$$
\{t\in\mathbb R : t \bmod B \in (0,a]\}.
$$

Thus (2) says that the distribution of $t \bmod B$ under $\nu$ is uniform on $[0,B]$.

Equivalently, for every Borel set $A\subseteq[0,B)$,

$$
\nu\Bigl(\bigcup_{m\in\mathbb Z}(A+mB)\Bigr)=\frac{\lambda(A)}{B},
\tag{3}
$$

where $\lambda$ is Lebesgue measure.

## 3. First critical consequence: invariance under shifts by $B$

Let $\mathcal{I}_B$ be the collection of intervals in $\mathbb R$. For any interval $I$,

$$
\nu(I+B)=\nu(I),
$$

because shifting $I$ by $B$ permutes its intersections with the residue classes $mB+[0,B)$, and each class contributes proportionally by (3).

Now define

$$
\mathcal{S}=\{E\subseteq\mathbb R : \nu(E+B)=\nu(E)\}.
$$

- $\mathcal{S}$ is a $\lambda$-system (closed under complements and countable disjoint unions).
- All intervals belong to $\mathcal{S}$, hence the $\pi$-system of intervals is contained in $\mathcal{S}$.

By the $\pi$-$\lambda$ theorem, $\mathcal{S}$ contains the $\sigma$-algebra generated by intervals, i.e. all Borel sets. Therefore,

$$
\nu(E+B)=\nu(E)\quad \text{for all Borel }E.
\tag{4}
$$

So $\nu$ is translation-invariant under $B=\log b$.

## 4. Apply the same argument for a second base

Let $c\ge 2$ be another integer and set $C=\log c$. Repeating the same reasoning gives

$$
\nu(E+C)=\nu(E)\quad \text{for all Borel }E.
\tag{5}
$$

Thus $\nu$ is invariant under translations by both $B$ and $C$.

## 5. Density of the generated translation group

The subgroup

$$
G=\{mB+nC : m,n\in\mathbb Z\}
$$

is dense in $\mathbb R$ provided $B/C\notin\mathbb Q$.

Indeed, if $B/C=p/q\in\mathbb Q$, then $b^q=c^p$, contradicting multiplicative independence of distinct integers such as $2$ and $3$. Hence for $b=2,c=3$,

$$
\frac{\log 2}{\log 3}\notin\mathbb Q.
$$

So $G$ is dense.

## 6. Extension to all translations

From (4) and (5), $\nu$ is invariant under all shifts in $G$:

$$
\nu(E+g)=\nu(E)\quad (g\in G).
$$

Let $t\in\mathbb R$. Choose $g_n\in G$ with $g_n\to t$. For any interval $I$,

$$
\nu(I+t)=\lim_{n\to\infty}\nu(I+g_n)=\nu(I),
$$

using continuity of measures from above and below and approximation of translates of intervals.

Since intervals generate the Borel $\sigma$-algebra, invariance extends to all Borel sets:

$$
\nu(E+t)=\nu(E)\quad \text{for all }t\in\mathbb R.
\tag{6}
$$

Thus $\nu$ is fully translation-invariant.

## 7. Final contradiction

A translation-invariant finite measure on $\mathbb R$ must satisfy

$$
\nu([a,b])=\nu([0,b-a])
$$

for all $a<b$, and by additivity this forces $\nu$ to be a constant multiple of Lebesgue measure:

$$
\nu = c\,\lambda.
$$

But $\nu$ is a probability measure, so $\nu(\mathbb R)=1$, while Lebesgue measure of $\mathbb R$ is infinite. This is impossible.

Hence no such $\nu$, and therefore no such $\mu$, exists.

## Conclusion

There is no distribution function $F$ such that for every integer $b\ge 2$ and every $r\in[1,b]$,

$$
\sum_{m\in\mathbb Z}\bigl(F(b^m r)-F(b^m)\bigr)=\log_b r.
$$

$$
\boxed{\text{No such distribution function }F \text{ exists.}}
$$
