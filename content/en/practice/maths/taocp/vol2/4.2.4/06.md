[Section 4.2.4: Distribution of Floating Point Numbers](../)

**Exercise 6.** [*25*] If we have binary computer words containing $n + 1$ bits, we might use $p$ bits for the fraction part of floating binary numbers, $one$ bit for the sign, and $n - p$ bits for the exponent. This means that the range of values representable, namely the ratio of the largest positive normalized value to the smallest, is essentially $2^{2^{n-p}}$. The same computer word could be used to represent floating *hexadecimal* numbers, that is, floating point numbers with radix 16, with $p + 2$ bits for the fraction part ($(p + 2)/4$ hexadecimal digits) and $n - p - 2$ bits for the exponent; then the range of values would be $16^{2^{n-p-2}} = 2^{2^{n-p}}$, the same as before, and with more bits in the fraction part. This may sound as if we are getting something for nothing, but the normalization condition for base 16 is weaker in that there may be up to three leading zero bits in the fraction part; thus not all of the $p + 2$ bits are "significant."

On the basis of the logarithmic law, what are the probabilities that the fraction part of a positive normalized radix 16 floating point number has exactly 0, 1, 2, and 3 leading zero bits? Discuss the desirability of hexadecimal versus binary.

## Solution

Let the radix be $16$. Under the logarithmic law, the leading hex digit $D \in {1,2,\dots,15}$ of a normalized positive floating-point number satisfies

$\Pr(D=d)=\log_{16}\!\left(1+\frac{1}{d}\right)=\log_{16}(d+1)-\log_{16}(d).$

A normalized radix-$16$ fraction has first nonzero hex digit $D$. Write $D$ in binary using four bits. The number of leading zero bits in this 4-bit block determines the number of leading zero bits in the fraction.

For $d \in {1,\dots,15}$, the binary length of $d$ is $\lfloor \log_2 d \rfloor + 1$, hence the number of leading zero bits in the 4-bit representation is

$z(d)=4-(\lfloor \log_2 d \rfloor + 1)=3-\lfloor \log_2 d \rfloor.$

Thus:

- $z(d)=0$ when $d \in {8,9,10,11,12,13,14,15}$,
- $z(d)=1$ when $d \in {4,5,6,7}$,
- $z(d)=2$ when $d \in {2,3}$,
- $z(d)=3$ when $d={1}$.

### Case $z=0$

$$
\Pr(z=0)=\sum_{d=8}^{15}\left(\log_{16}(d+1)-\log_{16}(d)\right).
$$

This is a telescoping sum:

$$
\Pr(z=0)=\log_{16}(16)-\log_{16}(8)=1-\log_{16}(8)=\log_{16}\!\left(\frac{16}{8}\right)=\log_{16}(2).
$$

Since $16=2^4$,

$$
\log_{16}(2)=\frac{1}{4}.
$$

Hence $\Pr(z=0)=\frac{1}{4}$.

### Case $z=1$

$$
\Pr(z=1)=\sum_{d=4}^{7}\left(\log_{16}(d+1)-\log_{16}(d)\right)
= \log_{16}(8)-\log_{16}(4).
$$

Thus

$$
\Pr(z=1)=\log_{16}\!\left(\frac{8}{4}\right)=\log_{16}(2)=\frac{1}{4}.
$$

### Case $z=2$

$$
\Pr(z=2)=\sum_{d=2}^{3}\left(\log_{16}(d+1)-\log_{16}(d)\right)
= \log_{16}(4)-\log_{16}(2).
$$

Thus

$$
\Pr(z=2)=\log_{16}\!\left(\frac{4}{2}\right)=\log_{16}(2)=\frac{1}{4}.
$$

### Case $z=3$

$$
\Pr(z=3)=\log_{16}(2)-\log_{16}(1)=\log_{16}(2)=\frac{1}{4}.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\boxed{\Pr(z=0)=\Pr(z=1)=\Pr(z=2)=\Pr(z=3)=\frac{1}{4}}.
$$

## Notes

The four cases partition the interval $[1,16)$ into subintervals $[8,16)$, $[4,8)$, $[2,4)$, and $[1,2)$ in the logarithmic scale. Each interval has equal logarithmic measure $\log_{16}(2)$, so the distribution of leading-zero-bit counts is uniform.

Hexadecimal representation therefore distributes normalization inefficiency evenly across all possible leading-bit patterns. In contrast, binary normalization eliminates leading-zero variability entirely, so each normalized number has a fixed leading bit structure, which makes normalization more uniform and generally more efficient for arithmetic implementation.
