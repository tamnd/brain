[Section 4.2.4: Distribution of Floating Point Numbers](../)

**Exercise 6.** [*25*] If we have binary computer words containing $n + 1$ bits, we might use $p$ bits for the fraction part of floating binary numbers, $one$ bit for the sign, and $n - p$ bits for the exponent. This means that the range of values representable, namely the ratio of the largest positive normalized value to the smallest, is essentially $2^{2^{n-p}}$. The same computer word could be used to represent floating *hexadecimal* numbers, that is, floating point numbers with radix 16, with $p + 2$ bits for the fraction part ($(p + 2)/4$ hexadecimal digits) and $n - p - 2$ bits for the exponent; then the range of values would be $16^{2^{n-p-2}} = 2^{2^{n-p}}$, the same as before, and with more bits in the fraction part. This may sound as if we are getting something for nothing, but the normalization condition for base 16 is weaker in that there may be up to three leading zero bits in the fraction part; thus not all of the $p + 2$ bits are "significant."

On the basis of the logarithmic law, what are the probabilities that the fraction part of a positive normalized radix 16 floating point number has exactly 0, 1, 2, and 3 leading zero bits? Discuss the desirability of hexadecimal versus binary.

## Solution

Let a positive normalized radix $16$ floating point number have fraction part $f$ satisfying $1/16 \le f < 1$. Write the leading hexadecimal digit of $f$ as $d$, where $d \in {1,2,\dots,15}$.

By the logarithmic law in base $16$, the probability that the leading digit equals $d$ is

$$
\Pr(d) = \log_{16}\!\left(1 + \frac{1}{d}\right).
$$

Express $d$ in binary using exactly four bits. Define $k(d)$ as the number of leading zero bits in this 4-bit representation. Then:

- $k(d)=3$ for $d=1$,
- $k(d)=2$ for $d \in {2,3}$,
- $k(d)=1$ for $d \in {4,5,6,7}$,
- $k(d)=0$ for $d \in {8,9,10,11,12,13,14,15}$.

The required probabilities are obtained by summing $\Pr(d)$ over the corresponding sets of digits.

For $k=3$,

$$
\Pr(k=3)=\Pr(1)=\log_{16} 2.
$$

For $k=2$,

$$
\Pr(k=2)=\log_{16}\!\left(1+\frac{1}{2}\right)+\log_{16}\!\left(1+\frac{1}{3}\right)
=\log_{16}\!\left(\frac{3}{2}\cdot\frac{4}{3}\right)
=\log_{16} 2.
$$

For $k=1$,

$$
\Pr(k=1)=\sum_{d=4}^{7}\log_{16}\!\left(1+\frac{1}{d}\right)
=\log_{16}\!\left(\frac{5}{4}\cdot\frac{6}{5}\cdot\frac{7}{6}\cdot\frac{8}{7}\right)
=\log_{16} 2.
$$

For $k=0$,

$$
\Pr(k=0)=\sum_{d=8}^{15}\log_{16}\!\left(1+\frac{1}{d}\right)
=\log_{16}\!\left(\frac{9}{8}\cdot\frac{10}{9}\cdots\frac{16}{15}\right)
=\log_{16} 2.
$$

Since $16=2^4$, one has

$$
\log_{16} 2 = \frac{\log_2 2}{\log_2 16} = \frac{1}{4}.
$$

Each probability equals $1/4$.

$$
\boxed{\Pr(k=0)=\Pr(k=1)=\Pr(k=2)=\Pr(k=3)=\frac{1}{4}}
$$

The radix $16$ normalization allows leading hexadecimal digits $1$ through $7$ to correspond to fractions with at least one leading zero bit in their binary representation, while radix $2$ normalization fixes the leading binary digit to $1$. The radix $16$ representation spreads significant bits unevenly across hexadecimal digits, since smaller leading hex digits force more leading zero bits and therefore reduce effective binary significance in a nonuniform way. This weakens the control over precision distribution compared with binary normalization, where every normalized number has a fixed leading bit structure.
