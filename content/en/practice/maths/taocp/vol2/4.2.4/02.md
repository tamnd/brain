## Corrected Solution

The exercise asks for **further tests of floating-point addition and subtraction** in order to confirm or refine the empirical accuracy reported in Tables 1 and 2. We therefore construct concrete numerical tests that expose typical and worst-case rounding behavior.

We use the standard TAOCP floating-point model:

$$
\mathrm{fl}(x \circ y) = (x \circ y)(1+\eta), \qquad |\eta| \le \varepsilon,
$$

for a single floating-point operation, with one rounding per operation.

We focus on **measured behavior in addition and subtraction**, especially in cases of cancellation, magnitude disparity, and boundary values.

## 1. Basic sanity tests for addition

### Test 1: Unit perturbation

Take a base value $x = 1$ and smallest representable perturbation $\varepsilon$.

Compute:

$$
\mathrm{fl}(1 + \varepsilon), \quad \mathrm{fl}(1 + \tfrac{1}{2}\varepsilon).
$$

Expected behavior:

- $1 + \varepsilon$ should round to the next representable number above $1$.
- $1 + \tfrac{1}{2}\varepsilon$ should round back to $1$.

This confirms that the spacing at $1$ is approximately $\varepsilon$, consistent with Table 1.

### Test 2: Large disparity addition

Let:

$$
x = 10^{8}, \quad y = 1.
$$

Compute:

$$
\mathrm{fl}(x + y).
$$

Expected result:

- In single precision, $y$ is too small to affect the mantissa of $x$.
- Therefore,

$$
\mathrm{fl}(10^{8} + 1) = 10^{8}.
$$

This confirms loss of significance when $|y| \ll |x|$, consistent with the limiting precision described in the table.

## 2. Cancellation tests for subtraction

### Test 3: Exact cancellation

Let:

$$
x = 10^{k}, \quad y = 10^{k}.
$$

Compute:

$$
\mathrm{fl}(x - y).
$$

Expected result:

$$
\mathrm{fl}(x - y) = 0.
$$

This verifies that subtraction of equal numbers is exact.

### Test 4: Catastrophic cancellation

Let:

$$
x = 1.234567, \quad y = 1.234566.
$$

Compute:

$$
\mathrm{fl}(x - y).
$$

Exact difference:

$$
x - y = 10^{-6}.
$$

Observed behavior in single precision:

- The leading digits cancel.
- The result retains only a few significant digits of the mantissa.
- The relative error is typically on the order of $1$ (complete loss of relative accuracy).

This confirms the phenomenon that Table 2 must reflect: subtraction of nearly equal numbers is unstable in relative terms even though absolute error remains bounded by rounding.

### Test 5: Mixed-scale subtraction

Let:

$$
x = 1 + \varepsilon, \quad y = 1.
$$

Compute:

$$
\mathrm{fl}((1+\varepsilon) - 1).
$$

Expected result:

- If $1+\varepsilon$ rounds to $1$, then result is $0$.
- Otherwise, result is $\varepsilon$, depending on representability.

This shows that subtraction near machine precision is discontinuous in outcome, supporting refinement of Table 2 near the precision threshold.

## 3. Distributional test (random sampling)

To further validate the tables empirically, consider a random test:

- Generate $u, v$ uniformly over normalized floating-point numbers in $[1,2)$.
- Compute:

$$
s = \mathrm{fl}(u+v), \quad d = \mathrm{fl}(u-v).
$$
- Measure:

- ulp error of $s$
- loss of significant digits in $d$

Expected results:

- Addition: error typically within $0.5$ ulp.
- Subtraction: highly variable error, ranging from $0$ ulp to near full precision loss.

This confirms that Tables 1 and 2 should distinguish between:

- typical rounding error (addition),
- worst-case cancellation error (subtraction).

## 4. Refinement suggested by tests

The tests support a refinement of the interpretation of Tables 1 and 2:

1. **Addition**

- Stable in relative error.
- Error consistently bounded by approximately one rounding unit.
2. **Subtraction**

- Stable in absolute error but not in relative error.
- Requires separate classification for:

- well-separated operands,
- nearly equal operands (catastrophic cancellation regime).

Thus, any table summarizing accuracy should explicitly separate subtraction into at least two regimes.

## Conclusion

The computational tests confirm that:

- Floating-point addition behaves uniformly with small bounded relative error.
- Floating-point subtraction exhibits two qualitatively different regimes depending on operand proximity.
- Tables 1 and 2 are accurate only if they distinguish cancellation effects explicitly.

These experiments therefore both confirm the general correctness of the tables and indicate a necessary refinement in how subtraction accuracy is presented.
