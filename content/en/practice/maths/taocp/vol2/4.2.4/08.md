[Section 4.2.4: Distribution of Floating Point Numbers](../)

**Exercise 8.** [*HM23*] Does (10) hold when $m = 0$ for suitable $N_0(\epsilon)$?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 2m45s  

---

Let (10) denote the statement proved earlier in the section that, for each fixed exponent block $m$, the empirical frequencies of mantissa-dependent events in that block converge (uniformly in the mantissa classes) to the same limiting distribution, independent of $m$, once the sample size is sufficiently large.

We examine whether this remains valid for $m=0$, the lowest normalized exponent block.

### Structure of the argument for interior blocks

For $m \neq 0$, the proof of (10) rests on a purely combinatorial symmetry: multiplication by the base $b$ gives a bijection between normalized floating-point numbers in exponent block $m$ and those in block $m+1$. This bijection preserves mantissas exactly and does not interact with rounding once normalization is fixed. Hence each exponent block inherits identical counting behavior.

The only potential obstruction is that this bijection might fail at a boundary where normalization or representability breaks.

### The boundary case $m=0$

For $m=0$, we check whether any step of the interior argument breaks.

A normalized floating-point number has the form

$$
x = \pm (0.d_1 d_2 \ldots d_t)_b \, b^m,
\quad d_1 \neq 0.
$$

The key point is that the classification by mantissa $(d_1,\ldots,d_t)$ is independent of $m$ as long as the number is normalized.

Now consider multiplication by $b$:

$$
x \mapsto bx.
$$

If $x$ lies in exponent block $0$, then $bx$ lies in block $1$, and this map is a bijection between the sets of normalized numbers in these two blocks. Its inverse is division by $b$, which preserves normalization for all numbers in block $1$. Crucially, neither map ever leaves the set of normalized representable numbers when restricted to blocks $m \ge 0$.

Thus, even at $m=0$, there is no loss of symmetry inside the normalized system: the same bijection used for interior blocks applies between $m=0$ and $m=1$.

### Absence of boundary distortion inside block $m=0$

The earlier flawed argument incorrectly introduced “underflow contamination” within the block $m=0$. This is not relevant to (10), since:

- Underflow concerns numbers that would correspond to exponents $m<0$,
- but $m=0$ already denotes the lowest _normalized_ exponent class,
- and (10) is formulated only in terms of normalized floating-point numbers.

Hence no element of the $m=0$ block is altered or removed by underflow; underflow affects only values that are not part of the normalized partition used in the statement of (10).

Therefore, the combinatorial structure used in the proof of (10) is identical for $m=0$ and for interior blocks.

### Consequence for empirical frequencies

Since the bijection between exponent blocks preserves mantissas exactly, every finite sample that is stratified by exponent block satisfies:

$$
\#\{\text{occurrences with mantissa } d \text{ in block } 0\}
=
\#\{\text{occurrences with mantissa } d \text{ in block } 1\}
$$

whenever the corresponding exponent shift is applied to the same underlying operations.

Thus the empirical frequency relations used to prove (10) hold for $m=0$ with no additional error term.

### Choice of $N_0(\epsilon)$

Because the argument does not introduce any additional distortion or boundary error at $m=0$, the same threshold condition as for interior blocks applies. In fact, no extra stabilization is required.

We may take

$$
N_0(\epsilon) = N_1(\epsilon),
$$

where $N_1(\epsilon)$ is the threshold already established in the proof of (10) for interior exponent blocks. If one separates the dependence on $m$, one may equivalently take $N_0(\epsilon)=1$, since no new convergence issue arises at the boundary.

### Conclusion

The derivation of (10) is unaffected at $m=0$ because the proof relies only on a mantissa-preserving bijection between exponent blocks, and this bijection remains valid at the lower normalized boundary.

Therefore, (10) continues to hold for $m=0$ with the same (or no stronger) choice of $N_0(\epsilon)$. ∎
