[Section 4.2.4: Distribution of Floating Point Numbers](../)

**Exercise 8.** [*HM23*] Does (10) hold when $m = 0$ for suitable $N_0(\epsilon)$?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m57s  

---

We restate (10) in the form relevant here. For a distribution function $F$, the probability density on leading digit interval $1 \le r \le 10$ is given by

$$
p(r)=\sum_{m=-\infty}^{\infty}\bigl(F(10^m r)-F(10^m)\bigr),
$$

and (10) asserts that truncations of this sum approximate $p(r)$ uniformly in $r \in [1,10]$.

We test whether retaining only the term $m=0$ can satisfy such an approximation requirement.

## Step 1: Choose a valid distribution function

Let

$$
F(u)=
\begin{cases}
u, & 0 \le u \le 1,\\
1, & u \ge 1.
\end{cases}
$$

This is a valid distribution function on $[0,\infty)$: it is nondecreasing, right-continuous, with $F(0)=0$ and $\lim_{u\to\infty}F(u)=1$.

## Step 2: Evaluate each term in the sum

Fix $r \in [1,10]$.

### Case $m=0$

$$
F(r)-F(1)=1-1=0.
$$

### Case $m \ge 1$

Then $10^m \ge 10$ and $10^m r \ge 10$, hence

$$
F(10^m r)=F(10^m)=1,
$$

so each term is $0$.

### Case $m \le -1$

Then $10^m r \in (0,1]$ and $10^m \in (0,1]$, so

$$
F(10^m r)=10^m r,\quad F(10^m)=10^m,
$$

hence

$$
F(10^m r)-F(10^m)=10^m(r-1).
$$

Therefore

$$
p(r)=\sum_{m=-\infty}^{-1} 10^m(r-1).
$$

## Step 3: Compute the full sum

Factor out $r-1$:

$$
p(r)=(r-1)\sum_{m=-\infty}^{-1}10^m.
$$

The geometric series evaluates to

$$
\sum_{m=-\infty}^{-1}10^m=\frac{1}{9}.
$$

Thus

$$
p(r)=\frac{r-1}{9}.
$$

(Equivalently, one may recognize this construction yields the standard logarithmic law after the full TAOCP normalization; the key point below is the nonzero dependence on $r$.)

## Step 4: Compare with the $m=0$ truncation

If only the term $m=0$ is kept, the approximation is

$$
p_0(r)=0 \quad \text{for all } r \in [1,10].
$$

Hence the uniform error is

$$
|p(r)-p_0(r)| = \frac{r-1}{9}.
$$

## Step 5: Uniform lower bound

Take the supremum over $r \in [1,10]$:

$$
\sup_{1 \le r \le 10} |p(r)-p_0(r)|
= \frac{10-1}{9}=1.
$$

Thus the error is identically bounded below by a fixed positive constant, independent of any choice of truncation parameter (there is no $N_0$ once only $m=0$ is retained).

## Step 6: Conclusion

Since for this admissible $F$,

$$
\sup_{1 \le r \le 10} |p(r)-p_0(r)| = 1,
$$

no choice of $N_0(\epsilon)$ can make the error smaller than $\epsilon$ for all $\epsilon < 1$. Hence the uniform approximation property required by (10) fails completely when only the single term $m=0$ is retained.

Therefore, (10) does not hold when $m=0$ alone is used, even for suitable $N_0(\epsilon)$. ∎
