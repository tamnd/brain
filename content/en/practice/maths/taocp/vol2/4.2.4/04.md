[Section 4.2.4: Distribution of Floating Point Numbers](../)

**Exercise 4.** [*M18*] The text points out that the front pages of a well-used table of logarithms get dirtier than the back pages do. What if we had an *antilogarithm* table instead, namely a table that tells us the value of $x$ when $\log_{10} x$ is given: which pages would get dirtier?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m09s  

---

## Solution

Let a page of the antilogarithm table correspond to a fixed interval of the argument $y = \log_{10} x$, for example a unit interval $[k, k+1)$, where the table returns $x = 10^y$.

The frequency with which a page is used equals the probability that a requested value of $x$ leads to a logarithm $y$ lying in the corresponding interval. Let $U$ denote a “random” positive quantity arising in applications, and let $Y = \log_{10} U$. The antilogarithm table is accessed through values of $Y$ rather than $U$.

Write $Y = m + \theta$, where $m$ is an integer and $0 \le \theta < 1$. Then a page indexed by $[k, k+1)$ is used exactly when $m = k$ and $0 \le \theta < 1$. Hence the probability of using that page equals

$\Pr(k \le Y < k+1) = \Pr(m = k, 0 \le \theta < 1).$

The distributional principle underlying Section B states that for quantities arising from heterogeneous sources, the fractional part $\theta = Y \bmod 1$ is approximately uniformly distributed on $[0,1)$ and independent of the integer part $m$. Under this hypothesis,

$\Pr(k \le Y < k+1) = \Pr(m = k)\Pr(0 \le \theta < 1) = \Pr(m = k).$

The antilogarithm table does not privilege any particular integer range of $Y$, since equal-length intervals in $Y$ correspond to equal multiplicative intervals in $x$, namely

$x \in [10^k, 10^{k+1}).$

The distribution of $m$ reflects only how often the magnitudes of $U$ fall into successive decades. Under the scale-invariance argument leading to the logarithmic law, no decade is favored, so $\Pr(m = k)$ is constant in $k$ over the range of the table.

Thus every page interval $[k, k+1)$ is used with the same probability.

The dirtiness of pages in an antilogarithm table is therefore uniform across the table; no front or back pages are favored. This completes the proof. ∎
