[Section 4.2.4: Distribution of Floating Point Numbers](../)

**Exercise 4.** [*M18*] The text points out that the front pages of a well-used table of logarithms get dirtier than the back pages do. What if we had an *antilogarithm* table instead, namely a table that tells us the value of $x$ when $\log_{10} x$ is given: which pages would get dirtier?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m25s  

---

Let $X$ denote the “natural” positive data whose logarithms are being computed. The model implicit in Section 4.2.4B is that $X$ is approximately scale invariant over many decades, so that for $x$ in a wide range,

$$
\Pr(x \le X < x+dx) \approx \frac{C}{x}\,dx .
$$

Let

$$
Y = \log_{10} X.
$$

We determine the distribution of $Y$ directly by transformation. Since $X = 10^Y$, we have

$$
dx = 10^y \ln 10 \, dy.
$$

Hence

$$
\Pr(Y \in dy)
= \Pr(X \in dx)
\approx \frac{C}{10^y} \cdot 10^y \ln 10 \, dy
= C \ln 10 \, dy.
$$

Thus $Y$ has approximately constant density over its range. In other words, $Y$ is (to a very good approximation) uniformly distributed over each large interval corresponding to a decade structure.

Now consider an antilogarithm table. Such a table is indexed by $y$ and returns $x = 10^y$. A “page” corresponds to a fixed interval

$$
y \in [y_0, y_0 + \Delta]
$$

for some small $\Delta$, and is used exactly when the input logarithm $Y$ falls in that interval.

Since $Y$ has approximately constant density, we get

$$
\Pr(y_0 \le Y < y_0 + \Delta) \approx C \ln 10 \cdot \Delta,
$$

which is independent of $y_0$. Therefore every page of the antilogarithm table is accessed with approximately the same frequency.

Finally, mapping back to $x = 10^y$: although equal increments in $y$ correspond to multiplicative (not additive) intervals in $x$, this does not affect page usage, because pages are not selected by $x$ but by the input variable $y$. The Jacobian factor that biases the logarithm table in favor of small $x$ is exactly canceled in the transformation to $Y$.

Hence there is no systematic front or back concentration of wear: all pages of an antilogarithm table become equally dirty, up to negligible boundary effects at the ends of the range.
