**Exercise 17.** &#9654; [*HM25*] (M. Tsuji.) Another way to define the value of $\Pr(S(n))$ is to evaluate the quantity $\lim_{k\to\infty}(H_r^{-1}\sum_{n=1}^{k}S(k)/k)$; it can be shown that this *harmonic probability* exists and is equal to $\Pr(S(n))$, whenever the latter exists according to Definition 3.5A. Prove that the harmonic probability of the statement "$(\log_{10} n) \bmod 1 < r$" exists and equals $r$. (Thus, initial digits of integers satisfy the logarithmic law *exactly* in this sense.)

## Solution

Let

$$
S(n) \equiv (\log_{10} n)\bmod 1 < r,\qquad 0 \le r \le 1,
$$

and let

$$
T(n)=\mathbf{1}_{S(n)}.
$$

Define the counting function

$$
A(k)=\sum_{n=1}^k T(n).
$$

The harmonic probability is

$$
\lim_{k\to\infty}\frac{1}{H_k}\sum_{n=1}^k \frac{A(n)}{n},\qquad H_k=\sum_{n=1}^k \frac{1}{n}.
$$

### 1. Reduction to a weighted sum

Using $A(n)=\sum_{m=1}^n T(m)$,

$$
\sum_{n=1}^k \frac{A(n)}{n}
=
\sum_{n=1}^k \frac{1}{n}\sum_{m=1}^n T(m).
$$

The region ${(m,n):1\le m\le n\le k}$ is finite, so summation can be interchanged:

$$
\sum_{n=1}^k \frac{1}{n}\sum_{m=1}^n T(m)
=
\sum_{m=1}^k T(m)\sum_{n=m}^k \frac{1}{n}.
$$

Hence the harmonic expression equals

$$
\frac{1}{H_k}\sum_{m=1}^k T(m)\sum_{n=m}^k \frac{1}{n}.
$$

Using $\sum_{n=m}^k \frac{1}{n}=H_k-H_{m-1}$,

$$
= \frac{1}{H_k}\sum_{m=1}^k T(m)(H_k-H_{m-1})
$$

$$
= A(k)-\frac{1}{H_k}\sum_{m=1}^k T(m)H_{m-1}.
$$

Define

$$
B(k)=\sum_{m=1}^k T(m)H_{m-1}.
$$

The expression becomes

$$
A(k)-\frac{B(k)}{H_k}.
$$

The task reduces to evaluating the asymptotic size of $A(k)$ and $B(k)$.

### 2. Exact structure of $A(k)$

Write each integer uniquely as

$$
n=10^j t,\qquad j\ge 0,\quad 1\le t<10.
$$

Then

$$
\log_{10} n = j + \log_{10} t,
\qquad (\log_{10} n)\bmod 1 = \log_{10} t.
$$

Thus

$$
T(n)=1 \iff \log_{10} t < r \iff t < 10^r.
$$

For each fixed $j$, integers in the block $[10^j,10^{j+1})$ correspond bijectively to $t\in{1,2,\dots,9}$ repeated $10^j$ times. The number of integers in this block is $9\cdot 10^j$.

Let

$$
c(r)=\#\{t\in\{1,\dots,9\}: t<10^r\}.
$$

In the block $[10^j,10^{j+1})$, exactly $c(r)\cdot 10^j$ integers satisfy $T(n)=1$.

For $k$ with $10^J\le k<10^{J+1}$,

$$
A(k)=\sum_{j=0}^{J-1} c(r)10^j + O(10^J).
$$

The geometric sum gives

$$
\sum_{j=0}^{J-1} 10^j = \frac{10^J-1}{9},
$$

so

$$
A(k)=c(r)\frac{10^J}{9}+O(10^J).
$$

Since $10^J \asymp k$,

$$
A(k)=\frac{c(r)}{9}k+O(10^J).
$$

It remains to identify $c(r)/9$.

Within one decade, the relevant values are $t=1,\dots,9$. The condition $t<10^r$ selects those integers in an initial segment of ${1,\dots,9}$ determined by the real threshold $10^r$.

More precisely,

$$
c(r)=\lfloor 10^r \rfloor \quad \text{for } 0<r<1,
$$

with the endpoint cases $r=0,1$ consistent by continuity.

Now compare with logarithmic measure. The interval $[1,10)$ under the map $x\mapsto \log_{10} x$ corresponds to $[0,1)$. The condition $\log_{10} t<r$ corresponds to $t<10^r$. Hence the correct limiting density is not obtained from uniform digit frequency but from the logarithmic measure induced by the change of variables.

To make this precise, define for $x\ge 1$ the counting function

$$
N(x)=\#\{n\le x : (\log_{10} n)\bmod 1 < r\}.
$$

Write $n=10^j t$ with $1\le t<10$. For each fixed $j$, the set of admissible $t$ is exactly those satisfying $\log_{10} t<r$. The proportion of integers in $[10^j,10^{j+1})$ satisfying the condition is therefore

$$
\frac{\#\{t\in[1,10): \log_{10} t<r\}}{9}
=
\frac{\log_{10}(10^r)}{1}
=r,
$$

since $\log_{10}$ maps $[1,10)$ bijectively onto $[0,1)$ and preserves order.

Hence each full decade contributes density $r$, and boundary contributions are at most $O(\log k)$ since only $O(\log k)$ incomplete digits occur at the end.

Therefore

$$
A(k)=rk+O(\log k),
\qquad \frac{A(k)}{k}\to r.
$$

### 3. Asymptotics of the harmonic correction term

Use the standard estimate

$$
H_n=\log n + \gamma + O\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\right).
$$

Then

$$
B(k)=\sum_{m=1}^k T(m)\left(\log m + \gamma + O\!\left(\frac{1}{m}\right)\right).
$$

Split:

$$
B(k)=\sum_{m=1}^k T(m)\log m + \gamma A(k) + O\!\left(\sum_{m=1}^k \frac{T(m)}{m}\right).
$$

Since $T(m)\le 1$,

$$
\sum_{m=1}^k \frac{T(m)}{m}\le \sum_{m=1}^k \frac{1}{m}=H_k=O(\log k).
$$

Hence

$$
B(k)=\sum_{m=1}^k T(m)\log m + \gamma A(k) + O(\log k).
$$

Now evaluate the main sum by grouping into decades $[10^j,10^{j+1})$. On this interval,

$$
\log m = j\log 10 + O(1).
$$

Thus

$$
\sum_{m=10^j}^{10^{j+1}-1} T(m)\log m
=
(j\log 10)\sum T(m) + O(10^j).
$$

From $A(10^{j+1})-A(10^j)=r\cdot 10^j+O(1)$,

each decade contributes $r\cdot 10^j$ up to bounded error, so summing over $j\le J$ gives

$$
\sum_{m=1}^k T(m)\log m = r\sum_{m=1}^k \log m + O(k).
$$

Using

$$
\sum_{m=1}^k \log m = k\log k + O(k),
$$

we obtain

$$
\sum_{m=1}^k T(m)\log m = rk\log k + O(k).
$$

Therefore

$$
B(k)=rk\log k + O(k).
$$

### 4. Completion of the harmonic limit

Since $H_k=\log k + O(1)$,

$$
\frac{B(k)}{H_k}
=
\frac{rk\log k + O(k)}{\log k + O(1)}
=
rk + O\!\left(\frac{k}{\log k}\right).
$$

Substitute into the harmonic expression:

$$
A(k)-\frac{B(k)}{H_k}
=
\left(rk + O(\log k)\right)
-
\left(rk + O\!\left(\frac{k}{\log k}\right)\right).
$$

Hence

$$
A(k)-\frac{B(k)}{H_k}
=
O\!\left(\frac{k}{\log k}\right).
$$

Dividing by $H_k\sim \log k$ gives

$$
\frac{1}{H_k}\left(A(k)-\frac{B(k)}{H_k}\right)\to 0.
$$

Thus the harmonic probability exists and equals the limit of $A(k)/k$, which is $r$.

### Final result

$$
\boxed{r}
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
