**Exercise 17.** &#9654; [*HM25*] (M. Tsuji.) Another way to define the value of $\Pr(S(n))$ is to evaluate the quantity $\lim_{k\to\infty}(H_r^{-1}\sum_{n=1}^{k}S(k)/k)$; it can be shown that this *harmonic probability* exists and is equal to $\Pr(S(n))$, whenever the latter exists according to Definition 3.5A. Prove that the harmonic probability of the statement "$(\log_{10} n) \bmod 1 < r$" exists and equals $r$. (Thus, initial digits of integers satisfy the logarithmic law *exactly* in this sense.)

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m20s  

---

## Solution

Let $S(n)$ be the statement

$$
(\log_{10} n) \bmod 1 < r, \qquad 0 \le r \le 1.
$$

The harmonic probability of $S(n)$ is defined by

$$
\Pr_h(S) = \lim_{k\to\infty} \frac{1}{H_k} \sum_{\substack{1 \le n \le k \\ S(n)}} \frac{1}{n},
\qquad
H_k = \sum_{n=1}^k \frac{1}{n}.
$$

For each integer $m \ge 0$, define the interval

$$
I_m = [10^m, 10^{m+1}).
$$

Within $I_m$, the condition $S(n)$ is equivalent to

$$
10^m \le n < 10^{m+r}.
$$

Hence the contribution of $I_m$ to the numerator is

$$
A_m(k) = \sum_{\substack{10^m \le n < \min(10^{m+r},k+1)}} \frac{1}{n}.
$$

For $k$ large, the cutoff at $k$ affects only finitely many terminal intervals and does not influence the limit. It suffices to evaluate the asymptotic growth of

$$
A_m = \sum_{10^m \le n < 10^{m+r}} \frac{1}{n}.
$$

For integers $a < b$,

$$
\int_a^b \frac{dx}{x} \le \sum_{n=a}^{b-1} \frac{1}{n} \le \int_{a-1}^{b-1} \frac{dx}{x}.
$$

Applying this with $a = 10^m$, $b = 10^{m+r}$,

$$
\int_{10^m}^{10^{m+r}} \frac{dx}{x}
\le A_m
\le \int_{10^m-1}^{10^{m+r}-1} \frac{dx}{x}.
$$

The left integral equals

$$
\int_{10^m}^{10^{m+r}} \frac{dx}{x}
= \ln(10^{m+r}) - \ln(10^m)
= r \ln 10.
$$

The right integral differs from $r \ln 10$ by a quantity that tends to $0$ uniformly in $m$ as $m \to \infty$, since replacing $10^m$ by $10^m-1$ changes the logarithm by

$$
\ln\!\left(\frac{10^m}{10^m-1}\right) = O(10^{-m}),
$$

and similarly at the upper endpoint. Hence

$$
A_m = r \ln 10 + o(1)
\quad \text{as } m \to \infty.
$$

Let $M$ be such that $10^M \le k < 10^{M+1}$. The numerator satisfies

$$
\sum_{\substack{1 \le n \le k \\ S(n)}} \frac{1}{n}
= \sum_{m=0}^{M-1} A_m + O(1).
$$

Using $A_m = r \ln 10 + o(1)$,

$$
\sum_{m=0}^{M-1} A_m
= Mr \ln 10 + o(M).
$$

For the denominator,

$$
H_k = \sum_{n=1}^k \frac{1}{n}
= \sum_{m=0}^{M-1} \sum_{n=10^m}^{10^{m+1}-1} \frac{1}{n} + O(1).
$$

Each full interval satisfies

$$
\int_{10^m}^{10^{m+1}} \frac{dx}{x}
\le \sum_{n=10^m}^{10^{m+1}-1} \frac{1}{n}
\le \int_{10^m-1}^{10^{m+1}-1} \frac{dx}{x},
$$

hence

$$
\sum_{n=10^m}^{10^{m+1}-1} \frac{1}{n}
= \ln(10^{m+1}) - \ln(10^m) + o(1)
= \ln 10 + o(1).
$$

Therefore,

$$
H_k = M \ln 10 + o(M).
$$

Combining numerator and denominator,

$$
\frac{1}{H_k} \sum_{\substack{1 \le n \le k \\ S(n)}} \frac{1}{n}
= \frac{Mr \ln 10 + o(M)}{M \ln 10 + o(M)}.
$$

Dividing numerator and denominator by $M \ln 10$,

$$
\frac{Mr \ln 10 + o(M)}{M \ln 10 + o(M)} \to r.
$$

Hence the harmonic probability exists and equals $r$:

$$
\Pr_h\big((\log_{10} n) \bmod 1 < r\big) = r.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
