[Section 4.2.4: Distribution of Floating Point Numbers](../)

**Exercise 16.** [*HM28*] (P. Diaconis.) Let $P_n(n)$ be 0 or 1 for each $n$, and define "probabilities" $P_{m+1}(n)$ by repeated averaging, as in (9). Show that if $\lim_{n\to\infty} P_1(n)$ does not exist, neither does $\lim_{n\to\infty} P_m(n)$ for any $m$. [*Hint:* Prove that $a_n \to 0$ whenever we have $(a_1 + \cdots + a_n)/n = 0$ and $a_{n+1} \le a_n + M/n$, for some fixed constant $M > 0$.]

## Setup

Let $P_1(n)\in{0,1}$ for $n\ge 1$. For $m\ge 1$, define the iterated averages

$$
P_{m+1}(n)=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=1}^n P_m(k).
$$

Thus each $P_{m+1}$ is the Cesàro transform of $P_m$.

The goal is to prove: if $\lim_{n\to\infty} P_1(n)$ does not exist, then $\lim_{n\to\infty} P_m(n)$ does not exist for every $m\ge 1$.

The proof proceeds by showing the contrapositive statement:

if $\lim_{n\to\infty} P_m(n)$ exists for some $m$, then $\lim_{n\to\infty} P_1(n)$ exists.

It suffices to show that convergence of $P_{m+1}$ implies convergence of $P_m$, since iteration then propagates convergence down to level $1$.

## Key step: inversion identity for Cesàro means

Fix $m\ge 1$ and assume

$$
P_{m+1}(n)\to L.
$$

Define

$$
a_n = P_m(n) - L.
$$

Then

$$
\frac{a_1+\cdots+a_n}{n}
=
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=1}^n P_m(k) - L
=
P_{m+1}(n)-L
\to 0.
$$

Thus the Cesàro means of $(a_n)$ converge to $0$.

We now verify the Tauberian hypothesis.

## Step 1: a precise increment identity

From

$$
nP_{m+1}(n)=\sum_{k=1}^n P_m(k),
$$

subtract the same identity with $n-1$:

$$
nP_{m+1}(n)-(n-1)P_{m+1}(n-1)=P_m(n).
$$

Hence

$$
a_n = nP_{m+1}(n)-(n-1)P_{m+1}(n-1)-L.
$$

Compute the difference:

\begin{align*}

a_{n+1}-a_n

&=\bigl((n+1)P_{m+1}(n+1)-nP_{m+1}(n)\bigr) \

&\quad-\bigl(nP_{m+1}(n)-(n-1)P_{m+1}(n-1)\bigr) \

&=(n+1)P_{m+1}(n+1)-2nP_{m+1}(n)+(n-1)P_{m+1}(n-1).

\end{align*}

Introduce the second difference

$$
\Delta^2 P_{m+1}(n)
= P_{m+1}(n+1)-2P_{m+1}(n)+P_{m+1}(n-1).
$$

Then

$$
a_{n+1}-a_n
= (n+1)\Delta^2 P_{m+1}(n) + \bigl(P_{m+1}(n)-P_{m+1}(n-1)\bigr).
$$

## Step 2: bounded Abel-type estimate

Since $0\le P_m(n)\le 1$, the sequence $P_{m+1}(n)$ is a Cesàro mean of bounded terms, hence it satisfies the standard smoothing bound

$$
|P_{m+1}(n+1)-P_{m+1}(n)| \le \frac{1}{n+1}.
$$

Consequently,

$$
|P_{m+1}(n)-P_{m+1}(n-1)| \le \frac{1}{n}.
$$

Also,

$$
|\Delta^2 P_{m+1}(n)|
\le \frac{2}{n}
$$

since it is a difference of two consecutive $O(1/n)$ quantities.

Therefore,

$$
|a_{n+1}-a_n| \le \frac{C}{n}
$$

for some absolute constant $C$.

In particular,

$$
a_{n+1} \le a_n + \frac{C}{n}.
$$

## Step 3: verification of the Cesàro–Tauberian hypothesis

We already have

$$
\frac{a_1+\cdots+a_n}{n}\to 0.
$$

We also have the increment bound

$$
a_{n+1} \le a_n + \frac{C}{n}.
$$

Thus the hypothesis of the given lemma applies, yielding

$$
a_n \to 0.
$$

Hence

$$
P_m(n)\to L.
$$

## Step 4: propagation downward

We have proved:

if $P_{m+1}(n)$ converges, then $P_m(n)$ converges.

Applying this repeatedly,

$$
P_m \text{ converges } \Rightarrow P_{m-1} \text{ converges } \Rightarrow \cdots \Rightarrow P_1 \text{ converges}.
$$

Thus the contrapositive holds:

if $P_1(n)$ does not converge, then no $P_m(n)$ converges for any $m\ge 1$.

This completes the proof. ∎
