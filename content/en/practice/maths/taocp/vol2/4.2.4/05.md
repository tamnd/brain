**Exercise 5.** &#9654; [*M20*] Let $U$ be a random number that is uniformly distributed in the interval $0 \le U < 1$. What is the distribution of the leading digits of $U$?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 56s

## Solution

Let $U$ be uniformly distributed on $[0,1)$.

Write the decimal expansion of $U$ in normalized form

$U = 0.d_1 d_2 d_3 \ldots,$

where $d_1 \in {1,2,\ldots,9}$ except on a set of measure $0$ corresponding to terminating expansions ending in repeating $9$.

The leading digit of $U$ is $d$ if and only if there exists an integer $k \ge 1$ such that

$d \cdot 10^{-k} \le U < (d+1)\cdot 10^{-k},$

since the first nonzero digit occurs in the $k$-th decimal place and equals $d$ precisely when $U$ lies in the corresponding base-$10$ interval.

For fixed $k$, the interval

$[d \cdot 10^{-k}, (d+1)\cdot 10^{-k})$

has length $10^{-k}$, and these intervals are disjoint for different $k$ because the leading digit position is uniquely determined by the highest power of $10$ in the normalized representation.

Since $U$ is uniformly distributed on $[0,1)$, the probability of each such interval equals its length. Hence

$\Pr(\text{leading digit of } U = d) = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} 10^{-k}.$

The geometric series satisfies

$\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} 10^{-k} = \frac{10^{-1}}{1 - 10^{-1}} = \frac{1}{9}.$

This value is independent of $d$, so every digit $d \in {1,2,\ldots,9}$ occurs with equal probability.

Therefore,

$\boxed{\Pr(\text{leading digit of } U = d) = \frac{1}{9} \quad \text{for } d=1,2,\ldots,9.}$

This completes the proof. ∎
