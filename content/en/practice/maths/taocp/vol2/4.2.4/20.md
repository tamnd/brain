**Exercise 20.** [*HM40*] Sharpen (16) by finding the asymptotic behavior of $P_m(10^{10^n}) - S_m(s)$ as $n \to \infty$.

**Verified:** no

## Setup

Let $P_m(x)$ denote the $m$-fold leading-digit distribution function introduced in (16), and let $S_m(s)$ denote its limiting Benford-type distribution in the variable $s$, where $s \in [0,1)$ represents the fractional part of an iterated logarithm scale.

From the structure of (16), the relation has the form

$$
P_m(x) = S_m\!\big(\{\log_{10}^{(m)} x\}\big) + o(1)
\quad (x \to \infty),
$$

where $\log_{10}^{(m)}$ denotes the $m$-fold iterated logarithm and ${\cdot}$ denotes fractional part.

The problem asks for the asymptotic behavior of

$$
P_m(10^{10^n}) - S_m(s), \qquad n \to \infty.
$$

## Solution

Define

$$
x_n = 10^{10^n}.
$$

### 1. Iterated logarithms of $x_n$

The first logarithm gives

$$
\log_{10} x_n = 10^n.
$$

This is an integer for every $n$. Hence

$$
\{\log_{10} x_n\} = 0.
$$

Applying the logarithm again,

$$
\log_{10}^{(2)} x_n = \log_{10}(10^n) = n,
$$

which is also an integer, so

$$
\{\log_{10}^{(2)} x_n\} = 0.
$$

Inductively, for every fixed $m \ge 1$ and all sufficiently large $n$,

$$
\log_{10}^{(m)} x_n \in \mathbb{Z},
\qquad \{\log_{10}^{(m)} x_n\} = 0.
$$

### 2. Evaluation of $P_m(10^{10^n})$

Substituting into the structural approximation from (16),

$$
P_m(x_n) = S_m(0) + o(1), \qquad n \to \infty.
$$

Since the fractional-part argument is identically zero, no oscillatory term appears; the only remaining deviation is the error term in the approximation.

Thus,

$$
\lim_{n \to \infty} P_m(10^{10^n}) = S_m(0).
$$

### 3. Asymptotic difference

Subtracting $S_m(s)$ yields

$$
P_m(10^{10^n}) - S_m(s)
= S_m(0) - S_m(s) + o(1).
$$

Hence the difference does not decay to zero unless $s = 0$. It converges to a constant depending only on $s$.

## Verification

The key structural fact is that each iterated logarithm of $10^{10^n}$ remains an integer because base-10 exponentiation preserves integrality under repeated logarithms until the exponent $10^n$ is reached. This forces all fractional parts appearing in the limiting Benford transformation to vanish identically.

The substitution into the standard decomposition from (16) isolates the limiting distribution term $S_m(\cdot)$ and leaves only a vanishing error term, yielding a constant asymptotic offset.

## Final result

$$
\boxed{
P_m(10^{10^n}) - S_m(s)
= S_m(0) - S_m(s) + o(1)
\quad (n \to \infty)
}
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
