[Section 3.3.4: The Spectral Test](../)

**Exercise 26.** [*M22*] Explain why the derivation of ($x_3$) leads to a similar bound on

$$\left|\sum_{0 \le n < N} e^{2\pi i u X_n}\right|$$

for $0 < q < m$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m06s  

---

## Solution

Exercise 25 established a bound for the exponential sums

$$
S_N(u)=\sum_{0\le n<N} e^{2\pi i uX_n/m},
$$

where $u\not\equiv0\pmod m$. The derivation of $(x_3)$ depends only on the fact that the summand is a nontrivial additive character modulo $m$ and on the periodic structure of the sequence. The same argument applies when the denominator $m$ is absorbed into the frequency parameter.

Let

$$
T_N(u)=\sum_{0\le n<N} e^{2\pi i uX_n},
$$

and assume that $0<q<m$. Write

$$
u=\frac{q}{m}.
$$

Then

$$
T_N(u)
=
\sum_{0\le n<N}
e^{2\pi i(q/m)X_n}
=
\sum_{0\le n<N}
e^{2\pi i qX_n/m}.
$$

Since $0<q<m$, the residue class $q$ is nonzero modulo $m$. Therefore the exponential factor is exactly the same nontrivial additive character that occurs in the derivation of $(x_3)$.

Every step in the proof of $(x_3)$ depends only on the parameter modulo $m$, not on whether it is denoted by $u$ or by $q$. Replacing the integer parameter $u$ of that derivation by $q$ yields the identical estimate. Hence

$$
\left|
\sum_{0\le n<N} e^{2\pi i uX_n}
\right|
=
\left|
\sum_{0\le n<N} e^{2\pi i qX_n/m}
\right|
\le B(q),
$$

where $B(q)$ is the same bound obtained in $(x_3)$ for the corresponding nonzero residue class $q$ modulo $m$.

Thus the derivation of $(x_3)$ immediately gives a similar bound for

$$
\left|
\sum_{0\le n<N} e^{2\pi i uX_n}
\right|
$$

whenever $u=q/m$ with $0<q<m$; the proof is unchanged because the exponential is the same nontrivial character modulo $m$ that was treated in the derivation of $(x_3)$.

This completes the proof.
