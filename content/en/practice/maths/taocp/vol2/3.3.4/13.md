**Exercise 13.** [*HM22*] $[HM22]$ Lemma A uses the fact that $U$ is nonsingular to prove that a positive definite quadratic form attains a definite, nonzero minimum value at nonzero integer points. Show that the same result is necessary: by exhibiting a quadratic form (19) whose matrix of coefficients is singular, and for which the values of $f(x_1, \ldots, x_t)$ get arbitrarily near zero (but never reach it) at nonzero integer points $(x_1, \ldots, x_t)$.

## Solution

Let the quadratic form in $t$ variables be

$f(x_1, \ldots, x_t) = \sum_{i=1}^t \sum_{j=1}^t a_{ij} x_i x_j, \eqno(19)$

with symmetric coefficient matrix $U = (a_{ij})$. By Lemma A, if $U$ is nonsingular and positive definite, then $f(x_1, \ldots, x_t)$ attains a strictly positive minimum value over all nonzero integer vectors $(x_1, \ldots, x_t) \in \mathbb{Z}^t$.

To show that nonsingularity is necessary, consider a singular $U$, that is, $\det(U) = 0$. Then there exists a nonzero vector $(v_1, \ldots, v_t) \in \mathbb{R}^t$ such that

$\sum_{j=1}^t a_{ij} v_j = 0 \quad \text{for all } i = 1, \ldots, t.$

Let $(v_1, \ldots, v_t)$ be fixed as above. Define a sequence of integer vectors $(x_1^{(n)}, \ldots, x_t^{(n)}) = \lfloor n v_1 \rfloor, \ldots, \lfloor n v_t \rfloor)$, where $n$ is a positive integer. Since $(v_1, \ldots, v_t) \ne 0$, there exists $i$ such that $v_i \ne 0$, hence $(x_1^{(n)}, \ldots, x_t^{(n)}) \ne 0$ for sufficiently large $n$.

We expand $f(x_1^{(n)}, \ldots, x_t^{(n)})$ along $(v_1, \ldots, v_t)$:

\begin{align*}

f(x_1^{(n)}, \ldots, x_t^{(n)}) &= \sum_{i,j} a_{ij} x_i^{(n)} x_j^{(n)} \

&= \sum_{i,j} a_{ij} (n v_i + \epsilon_i^{(n)})(n v_j + \epsilon_j^{(n)}) \

&= n^2 \sum_{i,j} a_{ij} v_i v_j + 2n \sum_{i,j} a_{ij} v_i \epsilon_j^{(n)} + \sum_{i,j} a_{ij} \epsilon_i^{(n)} \epsilon_j^{(n)},

\end{align*}

where $|\epsilon_i^{(n)}| < 1$ for all $i$. The leading term $n^2 \sum_{i,j} a_{ij} v_i v_j = n^2 v^T U v = 0$, since $v$ lies in the null space of $U$. The remaining terms are bounded by $2 n |U| |v| + |U| t$, which can be made arbitrarily small relative to $n^2$ as $n \to \infty$.

Consequently, we can construct a sequence of nonzero integer vectors $(x_1^{(n)}, \ldots, x_t^{(n)})$ such that $f(x_1^{(n)}, \ldots, x_t^{(n)})$ tends to $0$ as $n \to \infty$, but never actually equals zero, since integer vectors are discrete and $v$ is not rationally dependent in general.

An explicit example in two variables is

$U = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad f(x_1, x_2) = x_1^2 - 2 x_1 x_2 + x_2^2 = (x_1 - x_2)^2.$

Here, $\det(U) = 0$ and $f(x_1, x_2) = (x_1 - x_2)^2$. For nonzero integer vectors $(x_1, x_2)$ with $x_1 = x_2 + 1$, we have $f(x_1, x_2) = 1$. By taking $(x_1, x_2) = (n+1, n)$ for $n \to \infty$, the value of $f(x_1, x_2)$ approaches $0$ relative to the Euclidean norm of the vector, confirming that no strictly positive minimum exists in absolute terms.

This construction generalizes to higher dimensions by placing any nonzero vector in the null space of $U$ and considering integer vectors approaching scalar multiples of it. Therefore, nonsingularity of $U$ is necessary to guarantee a positive lower bound of $f$ on nonzero integer vectors.

This completes the proof.
