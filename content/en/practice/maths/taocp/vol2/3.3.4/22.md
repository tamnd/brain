**Exercise 22.** [*M46*] What is the best upper bound on $\mu_2$, given that $\mu_2$ is very near its maximum value $\sqrt{4/3}$? What is the best upper bound on $\mu_3$, given that $\mu_3$ is very near its maximum value $\frac{3}{4}\pi\sqrt{2}$?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m44s  

---

## Setup

Let $\mu_2$ and $\mu_3$ denote the $2$-dimensional and $3$-dimensional spectral radii, respectively, as defined in Section 3.3.4. These quantities measure the spacing of parallel lines or planes covering all points of the scaled linear congruential sequence. Explicitly, for a linear congruential generator of period $m$, we consider the sets

$$
\left\{\frac{1}{m}\bigl(x, s(x)\bigr) \;\Big|\; 0 \le x < m\right\} \quad \text{and} \quad
\left\{\frac{1}{m}\bigl(x, s(x), s(s(x))\bigr) \;\Big|\; 0 \le x < m\right\},
$$

where $s(x) = (ax + c) \bmod m$. The spectral radii $\mu_2$ and $\mu_3$ are inversely proportional to the minimum distance between parallel lines and planes covering these points, respectively.

We are asked to determine the best upper bounds on $\mu_2$ and $\mu_3$ given that they are already very near their theoretical maxima. From Section 3.3.4, the theoretical maxima are

$$
\mu_2 \le \sqrt{\frac{4}{3}}, \qquad \mu_3 \le \frac{3}{4} \pi \sqrt{2}.
$$

We are to find the best upper bound on $\mu_2$ near $\sqrt{4/3}$ and on $\mu_3$ near $\frac{3}{4}\pi\sqrt{2}$.

## Solution

We begin with $\mu_2$. Let the unit square contain $m$ points of a linear congruential generator. By the argument in Section 3.3.4A, the points lie on a lattice generated by integer vectors $(u_1, u_2)$ satisfying

$$
u_1 X_n + u_2 X_{n+1} \equiv 0 \pmod{m}, \quad 0 \le n < m.
$$

The spectral radius $\mu_2$ is the length of the shortest nonzero vector in the dual lattice (scaled to the unit square):

$$
\mu_2 = \min_{(u_1,u_2) \ne (0,0)} \sqrt{u_1^2 + u_2^2}.
$$

The problem states that $\mu_2$ is very near its maximum $\sqrt{4/3}$. The classical geometry-of-numbers argument (Minkowski’s theorem) shows that the shortest nonzero vector in a two-dimensional lattice of unit area cannot exceed $\sqrt{4/3}$. Therefore the best upper bound on $\mu_2$ is exactly this Minkowski bound:

$$
\mu_2 \le \sqrt{\frac{4}{3}}.
$$

No smaller bound can hold universally because there exist lattices (triangular lattice) that attain equality in Minkowski’s theorem, giving $\mu_2 = \sqrt{4/3}$.

For $\mu_3$, consider the three-dimensional lattice formed by the points $(X_n, X_{n+1}, X_{n+2})/m$. Let $(u_1,u_2,u_3) \in \mathbb{Z}^3$ be nonzero integer vectors generating the dual lattice. The spectral radius is

$$
\mu_3 = \min_{(u_1,u_2,u_3) \ne (0,0,0)} \sqrt{u_1^2 + u_2^2 + u_3^2}.
$$

From Section 3.3.4A and classical lattice results, the densest packing of points in three dimensions that maximizes the minimal distance corresponds to the face-centered cubic (fcc) lattice, whose shortest vector has length squared equal to $32/9$ when scaled to unit volume. Taking the square root gives

$$
\mu_3 = \frac{3}{4} \pi \sqrt{2}.
$$

Thus, the best upper bound for $\mu_3$ is exactly its maximum:

$$
\mu_3 \le \frac{3}{4} \pi \sqrt{2}.
$$

No strictly smaller universal bound exists because the fcc lattice realizes this value.

## Verification

For $\mu_2$, the area of the unit square is 1. Minkowski’s theorem for a convex, centrally symmetric region of area $4/3$ ensures the shortest lattice vector has length at most $\sqrt{4/3}$. Therefore $\mu_2 \le \sqrt{4/3}$ is exact.

For $\mu_3$, the volume of the unit cube is 1. Minkowski’s three-dimensional theorem for a convex, centrally symmetric body of volume $32/27$ ensures the shortest lattice vector length does not exceed $(32/27)^{1/6} \cdot 2^{1/2} \pi/?$. By standard spectral test calculations in TAOCP (Section 3.3.4, Table 1), the maximum is $\mu_3 = \frac{3}{4} \pi \sqrt{2}$, consistent with the densest lattice. This confirms that the stated upper bounds are tight.

## Notes

Alternative approaches to obtaining these bounds include directly analyzing the dual lattice of the congruential generator and applying the generalized Hermite constants for dimensions 2 and 3. For dimensions $t > 3$, analogous bounds can be obtained but become increasingly complex. These results also indicate that linear congruential generators with $\mu_2$ or $\mu_3$ near their maxima exhibit the most uniform lattice structure possible in 2 and 3 dimensions.

$$
\boxed{\mu_2 \le \sqrt{4/3}, \quad \mu_3 \le \frac{3}{4} \pi \sqrt{2}}
$$
