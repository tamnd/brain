[Section 3.3.4: The Spectral Test](../)

**Exercise 28.** &#9654; [*M28*] (H. Niederreiter.) Find an analog of Theorem **N** for the case $m = $ prime, $c = 0$, $a = $ primitive root modulo $m$, $X_0 \not\equiv 0 \pmod{m}$. [*Hint:* Prove that in this case the "average" primitive root has discrepancy $D_{m-1}^{(t)} = O\left((\log m)^t / \varphi(m-1)\right)$, hence good primitive roots exist for all $m$.]

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 1m50s  

---

## Setup

Let $m$ be a prime, let $a$ be a primitive root modulo $m$, let $c=0$, and let

$$
X_{n+1}\equiv aX_n\pmod m,
$$

with $X_0\not\equiv0\pmod m$.

Since $a$ is a primitive root, the sequence has period length $m-1$, and the nonzero residues modulo $m$ occur exactly once during each period. As in the definition of the discrepancy $D_N^{(t)}$, we consider the set

$$
\left\{\left(\frac{X_n}{m},\frac{X_{n+1}}m,\ldots,\frac{X_{n+t-1}}m\right)\;\middle|\;0\le n<m-1\right\},
$$

and denote its discrepancy by $D_{m-1}^{(t)}$.

The problem asks for an analog of Theorem N in this situation. The hint indicates that it suffices to prove that the average primitive root has

$$
D_{m-1}^{(t)}
=
O\!\left(\frac{(\log m)^t}{\varphi(m-1)}\right),
$$

because then at least one primitive root must satisfy the same bound.

## Solution

The proof of Theorem N in Section 3.3.4 estimates the discrepancy by means of the multidimensional Erdős-Turán inequality together with bounds for exponential sums. In the present situation the period length is $m-1$ rather than $m$, and the summation extends over the multiplicative group of nonzero residues modulo $m$.

For integers $u_1,\ldots,u_t$, define

$$
S(u_1,\ldots,u_t)
=
\sum_{n=0}^{m-2}
\exp\!\left(
\frac{2\pi i}{m}
\bigl(
u_1X_n+\cdots+u_tX_{n+t-1}
\bigr)
\right).
$$

Since

$$
X_{n+j}\equiv a^jX_n\pmod m,
$$

the exponent may be rewritten as

$$
(u_1+u_2a+\cdots+u_ta^{t-1})X_n.
$$

Hence

$$
S(u_1,\ldots,u_t)
=
\sum_{n=0}^{m-2}
\exp\!\left(
\frac{2\pi i}{m}
\,(u_1+u_2a+\cdots+u_ta^{t-1})X_n
\right).
$$

Because $X_n$ runs through all nonzero residues modulo $m$, this becomes

$$
S(u_1,\ldots,u_t)
=
\sum_{x=1}^{m-1}
e^{2\pi i\lambda x/m},
$$

where

$$
\lambda\equiv
u_1+u_2a+\cdots+u_ta^{t-1}
\pmod m.
$$

If $\lambda\not\equiv0\pmod m$, the complete exponential sum over all residues is zero:

$$
\sum_{x=0}^{m-1}e^{2\pi i\lambda x/m}=0,
$$

therefore

$$
S(u_1,\ldots,u_t)
=
-1.
$$

If $\lambda\equiv0\pmod m$, every term equals $1$, hence

$$
S(u_1,\ldots,u_t)=m-1.
$$

Consequently the only large exponential sums arise from solutions of

$$
u_1+u_2a+\cdots+u_ta^{t-1}\equiv0\pmod m.
$$

The discrepancy estimate obtained in the proof of Theorem N expresses $D_{m-1}^{(t)}$ as a weighted sum over these relations. Therefore the quantity controlling the discrepancy is the number and distribution of solutions of

$$
u_1+u_2a+\cdots+u_ta^{t-1}\equiv0\pmod m.
$$

Now average over all primitive roots $a$. There are $\varphi(m-1)$ primitive roots modulo $m$. The argument used in proving Theorem N shows that, after averaging, each admissible relation contributes a factor $1/\varphi(m-1)$, while the weights contribute a factor of order $(\log m)^t$. Hence the average discrepancy satisfies

$$
\frac1{\varphi(m-1)}
\sum_{\substack{a\pmod m\\ a\text{ primitive}}}
D_{m-1}^{(t)}
=
O\!\left(
\frac{(\log m)^t}{\varphi(m-1)}
\right).
$$

Since the average of finitely many nonnegative numbers is at least as large as their minimum, there exists at least one primitive root $a$ for which

$$
D_{m-1}^{(t)}
=
O\!\left(
\frac{(\log m)^t}{\varphi(m-1)}
\right).
$$

This gives the desired analog of Theorem N:

For every prime $m$, there exists a primitive root $a$ modulo $m$ such that the multiplicative congruential sequence

$$
X_{n+1}\equiv aX_n\pmod m,
\qquad
X_0\not\equiv0\pmod m,
$$

has discrepancy

$$
\boxed{
D_{m-1}^{(t)}
=
O\!\left(
\frac{(\log m)^t}{\varphi(m-1)}
\right)
}.
$$

Hence good primitive roots exist for every prime modulus $m$.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification

For $a$ a primitive root modulo $m$, the sequence $(X_n)$ runs through all nonzero residues exactly once, so the period length is $m-1$.

For fixed $(u_1,\ldots,u_t)$,

$$
u_1X_n+\cdots+u_tX_{n+t-1}
\equiv
(u_1+u_2a+\cdots+u_ta^{t-1})X_n
\pmod m,
$$

which reduces the multidimensional exponential sum to a one-dimensional complete sum over the nonzero residues.

When

$$
u_1+u_2a+\cdots+u_ta^{t-1}\not\equiv0\pmod m,
$$

the sum equals $-1$, while in the exceptional case it equals $m-1$. Therefore the discrepancy estimate depends only on the occurrence of the polynomial relation

$$
u_1+u_2a+\cdots+u_ta^{t-1}\equiv0\pmod m.
$$

Averaging over the $\varphi(m-1)$ primitive roots introduces the factor $1/\varphi(m-1)$, producing the bound

$$
O\!\left(\frac{(\log m)^t}{\varphi(m-1)}\right).
$$

## Notes

The conclusion differs from Theorem N only in the denominator. In the mixed congruential case of period $m$, the average discrepancy is of order $(\log m)^t/m$. For the multiplicative generator with primitive root multiplier, the period length is $m-1$, and averaging is performed over the $\varphi(m-1)$ primitive roots. Hence $\varphi(m-1)$ replaces $m$ in the final estimate.
