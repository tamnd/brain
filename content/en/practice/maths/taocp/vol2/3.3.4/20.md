## Solution

Let $X_{n+1} = aX_n \bmod 2^e$ with $c = 0$, $X_0$ odd, and $a \bmod 8 \in \{3,5\}$. Since $a$ is odd, multiplication by $a$ permutes the odd residue classes modulo $2^e$, hence the period is $2^{e-2}$ on the reduced state space of odd integers.

The normalized $t$-tuples used in the spectral test are

$$
\left(\frac{X_n}{2^e}, \frac{X_{n+1}}{2^e}, \ldots, \frac{X_{n+t-1}}{2^e}\right)
=
\frac{X_n}{2^e}\,(1,a,a^2,\ldots,a^{t-1}) \bmod 1.
$$

Thus every point in the set (2) lies on the image of a single linear direction vector $(1,a,\ldots,a^{t-1})$ modulo $1$, and the geometric structure is a finite subset of a rank-1 lattice embedded in $[0,1)^t$ with strong folding due to reduction mod $1$.

The spectral test in dimension $t$ is determined by the family of parallel hyperplanes containing all points, equivalently by nonzero integer vectors $u = (u_1,\ldots,u_t)$ such that

$$
u_1 \frac{X_n}{2^e} + u_2 \frac{X_{n+1}}{2^e} + \cdots + u_t \frac{X_{n+t-1}}{2^e} \in \mathbb{Z}
$$

for all $n$. Multiplying by $2^e$ and substituting $X_{n+k} \equiv a^k X_n \pmod{2^e}$, this condition becomes

$$
X_n\,(u_1 + u_2 a + \cdots + u_t a^{t-1}) \equiv 0 \pmod{2^e}.
$$

Since $X_n$ runs over all odd residues modulo $2^e$, the factor $X_n$ is invertible modulo $2^e$, hence the condition is equivalent to the congruence

$$
u_1 + u_2 a + \cdots + u_t a^{t-1} \equiv 0 \pmod{2^e}. \tag{6}
$$

The set of admissible hyperplanes is therefore the integer lattice

$$
\Lambda_t = \{u \in \mathbb{Z}^t : u_1 + u_2 a + \cdots + u_t a^{t-1} \equiv 0 \pmod{2^e}\}.
$$

In the spectral test, the quantity $\nu_t$ is the reciprocal of the length of the shortest nonzero vector in $\Lambda_t$, since parallel hyperplane spacing is inversely proportional to the Euclidean norm of the defining integer normal vector.

The structure of $\Lambda_t$ is governed by the 2-adic properties of powers of $a$. Since $a \equiv 3$ or $5 \pmod{8}$, one has $a \equiv 1 \pmod{2}$ and

$$
v_2(a-1) = 1 \text{ or } 2,
$$

while higher powers satisfy increasing divisibility relations of the form

$$
a^{2^k} \equiv 1 \pmod{2^{k+2}},
$$

obtained by repeated binomial expansion. This produces unusually small linear combinations of $1,a,\ldots,a^{t-1}$ modulo high powers of $2$, and therefore unusually short vectors in $\Lambda_t$.

In dimension $t=2$, condition (6) reduces to

$$
u_1 + u_2 a \equiv 0 \pmod{2^e}.
$$

Every solution corresponds to a rational approximation $a \approx -u_1/u_2$ with error divisible by $2^e/|u_2|$. The best approximations arise from exploiting the 2-adic closeness of $a$ to 1, producing vectors with $|u_2|$ of order about $2^{e/2}$ and hence Euclidean norm of the same magnitude. This yields a spectral bound

$$
\nu_2 \asymp 2^{e/2}.
$$

In higher dimension $t$, the same mechanism applies to the relation (6). Short vectors arise from lifting the 2-adic cancellations among $1,a,a^2,\ldots,a^{t-1}$, which can be generated recursively by eliminating leading 2-adic terms. Each additional dimension introduces a higher-order cancellation constraint, and the shortest vectors correspond to minimal integer relations among truncated 2-adic expansions of the sequence $1,a,a^2,\ldots$.

The appropriate spectral test is therefore carried out by constructing, for each fixed $t$, the integer lattice $\Lambda_t$ defined by (6), then determining its shortest nonzero vector with respect to Euclidean length. This can be done by systematic reduction of the basis vectors corresponding to the congruence relation, or by exploiting successive 2-adic approximations of $a^k$ to build progressively shorter relations. The value of $\nu_t$ is then the reciprocal of this minimal length, and the families of covering hyperplanes are given by the corresponding shortest normal vectors.

The resulting structure shows a pronounced deterioration of accuracy as $t$ increases, since all relations ultimately depend on the single 2-adic generator $a$ and its powers modulo $2^e$. This produces a lattice with relatively short basis vectors in every low dimension, so the spectral test detects strong anisotropy in dimensions $t = 2,3,4$ and beyond, confirming that these multiplicative generators modulo $2^e$ have poor multidimensional uniformity.

This completes the description of how the spectral test is implemented for the given class of generators. ∎
