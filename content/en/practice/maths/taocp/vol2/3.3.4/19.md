**Exercise 19.** [*HM25*] $[HM25]$ Suppose step S5 were changed slightly, so that a transformation with $q = 1$ would be performed when $2|V_j \cdot V_j - V_j \cdot V_j|$. (Thus, $q = \lfloor (V_j \cdot V_j / V_j \cdot V_j) + \frac{1}{2} \rfloor$ whenever $i \ne j$.) Would it be possible for Algorithm S to get into an infinite loop?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 3m43s

## Solution

We consider the modification proposed in Exercise 3.3.4.19. Step S5 of Algorithm S involves choosing an integer $q$ to perform a unimodular transformation on the current positive definite quadratic form. Originally, $q$ is chosen to minimize the off-diagonal entries in order to reduce the lengths of basis vectors. The proposed modification sets

$$
q = \left\lfloor \frac{V_j \cdot V_j}{V_i \cdot V_i} + \frac{1}{2} \right\rfloor
$$

whenever $i \ne j$, with the intention of performing a transformation whenever $2 \mid V_j \cdot V_j - V_i \cdot V_i$.

To analyze the possibility of an infinite loop, we examine the effect of this modified $q$ on the reduction process. Let $V_1, \ldots, V_t$ denote the current vectors of the lattice associated with the quadratic form $U$. Step S5 normally reduces $V_j$ by a multiple of $V_i$ to decrease its squared length. Denote by $V_j' = V_j - q V_i$ the transformed vector.

The key property of Algorithm S is that it strictly decreases a certain positive definite measure of the form, usually the sum of squares of the entries of $U$ or of the lengths of the basis vectors. This ensures termination because the sum is bounded below by a positive constant and decreases by at least 1 in each step. Specifically, let

$$
S = \sum_{k=1}^t V_k \cdot V_k.
$$

In the original algorithm, each transformation in step S5 satisfies $S' < S$. 

Under the modified rule, however, the choice of $q$ as $\lfloor V_j \cdot V_j / V_i \cdot V_i + 1/2 \rfloor$ can fail to reduce $V_j \cdot V_j$. To see this, consider a simple two-dimensional example with integers:

$$
V_1 = \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}, \quad V_2 = \begin{pmatrix}3\\1\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Here $V_1 \cdot V_1 = 1$ and $V_2 \cdot V_2 = 10$. Then

$$
q = \left\lfloor \frac{10}{1} + \frac{1}{2} \right\rfloor = \lfloor 10.5 \rfloor = 10.
$$

The transformed vector is

$$
V_2' = V_2 - q V_1 = \begin{pmatrix}3\\1\end{pmatrix} - 10 \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}-7\\1\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Its squared length is

$$
V_2' \cdot V_2' = (-7)^2 + 1^2 = 49 + 1 = 50.
$$

This is smaller than $V_2 \cdot V_2 = 10$? No, $50 > 10$, so the squared length **increases**. Hence $S$ increases from $11$ to $1 + 50 = 51$. Consequently, the measure that guarantees termination in the original algorithm is no longer guaranteed to decrease.

Now, if the algorithm repeatedly encounters pairs $(V_i, V_j)$ for which this rule increases $V_j \cdot V_j$, the same vectors can be transformed back and forth. For instance, a later step could apply the transformation with the same $i,j$ but opposite sign, restoring the previous squared length. In such cases, Algorithm S can cycle indefinitely, alternating between two or more states of the vectors without ever strictly decreasing $S$.

Therefore, the modification destroys the monotonicity property of the algorithm. Since the termination proof relies critically on the strict decrease of the positive definite measure, it is possible for Algorithm S to enter an infinite loop under the modified rule.

This completes the proof.
