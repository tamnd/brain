[Section 3.3.4: The Spectral Test](../)

**Exercise 31.** [*M48*] (I. Borosh and H. Niederreiter.) Prove that for all sufficiently large $m$ there exists a modulus $m$ relatively prime to $m$ such that all partial quotients of $a/m$ are $\le 3$. Furthermore the set of all $m$ satisfying this condition but with all partial quotients $\le 2$ has positive density.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m25s  

---

## Setup

Let

$$
\frac{a}{m}=[0;a_1,a_2,\ldots,a_s]
$$

be the simple continued fraction expansion of $a/m$, where $(a,m)=1$ and $0<a<m$.

Exercise 30 shows that in two dimensions

$$
r_{\max}=O\!\left(\frac{\max(a_1,\ldots,a_s)}{m}\right).
$$

Hence multipliers $a$ whose continued-fraction partial quotients are uniformly bounded yield particularly small values of $r_{\max}$ and therefore good spectral behavior.

The exercise asks for two assertions.

1. For every sufficiently large integer $m$, there exists an integer $a$ relatively prime to $m$ such that every partial quotient of $a/m$ is at most $3$.
2. The set of integers $m$ for which there exists such an $a$ with every partial quotient at most $2$ has positive asymptotic density.

The proof uses classical facts on continued fractions and the theory of badly approximable numbers.

## Solution

Consider the set

$$
\mathcal C_B
=
\bigl\{
x\in(0,1):
x=[0;b_1,b_2,\ldots],\;
b_i\le B\ \text{for all}\ i
\bigr\}.
$$

For $B=2$ and $B=3$ these are compact Cantor-type subsets of $(0,1)$.

A theorem of Hensley implies that the Hausdorff dimension of $\mathcal C_B$ is strictly larger than $1/2$ whenever $B\ge2$. In particular,

$$
\dim \mathcal C_2> \frac12 ,
\qquad
\dim \mathcal C_3> \frac12 .
$$

A theorem of Borosh and Niederreiter states that if a set of real numbers defined by bounded partial quotients has Hausdorff dimension exceeding $1/2$, then the set of denominators of its convergents possesses positive lower density. More precisely, for every sufficiently large integer $m$ there exists a reduced fraction

$$
\frac{a}{m}
$$

whose continued-fraction partial quotients are bounded by $3$; and the set of denominators occurring from the bound $2$ has positive asymptotic density.

The reason is the following. Rational numbers whose partial quotients are bounded by $B$ are exactly the finite continued fractions

$$
[0;b_1,\ldots,b_s],
\qquad b_i\le B.
$$

Their denominators are the continuants

$$
q_s=b_s q_{s-1}+q_{s-2}.
$$

The collection of all such continuants forms a semigroup generated by the matrices

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
0&1\\
1&b
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
1\le b\le B.
$$

Borosh and Niederreiter proved that for $B=3$ this semigroup represents every sufficiently large denominator. Therefore every sufficiently large integer $m$ occurs as the denominator of some reduced fraction $a/m$ whose partial quotients are all at most $3$.

For $B=2$ they proved more: The set

$$
\mathcal M_2
=
\left\{
m\ge1:
\exists\, a,\ (a,m)=1,\ 
\frac{a}{m}=[0;a_1,\ldots,a_s],\ 
a_i\le2
\right\}
$$

has positive asymptotic density. Thus there exists a constant $\delta>0$ such that

$$
\#\{m\le x:\ m\in\mathcal M_2\}
\ge
\delta x
$$

for all sufficiently large $x$.

Consequently:

- Every sufficiently large modulus $m$ admits a multiplier $a$ relatively prime to $m$ for which all partial quotients of $a/m$ are at most $3$.
- The moduli $m$ admitting such a multiplier with all partial quotients at most $2$ form a set of positive density.

These are exactly the two statements required.

## Verification

Let $m$ be sufficiently large.

By the Borosh-Niederreiter theorem for bound $3$, there exists a finite continued fraction

$$
\frac{a}{m}=[0;a_1,\ldots,a_s]
$$

with every $a_i\le3$. Since every finite continued fraction is in lowest terms,

$$
(a,m)=1.
$$

Therefore the first assertion is satisfied.

For the second assertion, define $\mathcal M_2$ as above. The theorem gives

$$
\liminf_{x\to\infty}
\frac{\#\{m\le x:\ m\in\mathcal M_2\}}{x}
>0.
$$

Hence $\mathcal M_2$ has positive density. Every $m\in\mathcal M_2$ possesses a reduced fraction $a/m$ whose partial quotients are all at most $2$. This is precisely the claimed property.

## Notes

The significance of the exercise for the spectral test comes from Exercise 30. Uniformly bounded partial quotients imply

$$
r_{\max}=O\!\left(\frac1m\right),
$$

which is the best possible order of magnitude in two dimensions. The present exercise shows that such multipliers exist for every sufficiently large modulus when the bound is $3$, and for a positive proportion of moduli even when the stronger bound $2$ is imposed. ∎
