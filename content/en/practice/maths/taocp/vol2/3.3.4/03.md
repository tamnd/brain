**Exercise 3.** [*M24*] Determine $\nu_2$ and $\nu_6$ for all linear congruential generators of potency 2 and period length $m$.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 4m54s

The previous solution fails in the 6-dimensional step because it incorrectly assumes that coordinate-difference vectors such as $(1,-1,0,0,0,0)$ lie in the dual lattice. This is false in general for linear congruential generators, since adjacent coordinates are not independent in that way. The correct treatment must explicitly characterize the affine 2-dimensional subspace containing all 6-tuples.

## 1. Structure of a potency-2 linear congruential generator

Let

$$
X_{n+1} \equiv aX_n + c \pmod m.
$$

Define the first difference

$$
Y_n = X_{n+1} - X_n.
$$

Then

$$
Y_{n+1} = X_{n+2} - X_{n+1} \equiv aX_{n+1} + c - X_{n+1} \equiv a(X_{n+1} - X_n) = aY_n \pmod m.
$$

Thus the system reduces to a multiplicative recurrence in $Y_n$, and every term can be written as

$$
X_{n+k} = X_n + \sum_{j=0}^{k-1} Y_{n+j}
= X_n + Y_n(1 + a + \cdots + a^{k-1}) \pmod m.
$$

Define

$$
S_k = 1 + a + \cdots + a^{k-1}.
$$

Then each coordinate satisfies

$$
X_{n+k} = X_n + S_k Y_n \pmod m.
$$

Hence the 6-dimensional point is

$$
\left(\frac{X_n}{m}, \frac{X_{n+1}}{m}, \dots, \frac{X_{n+5}}{m}\right)
=
\frac{X_n}{m}(1,1,\dots,1)
+
\frac{Y_n}{m}(0,S_1,S_2,S_3,S_4,S_5),
$$

so all points lie in a 2-dimensional affine subspace of $\mathbb{R}^6$.

Let

$$
u = (1,1,1,1,1,1), \quad v = (0,S_1,S_2,S_3,S_4,S_5).
$$

## 2. Correct interpretation of $\nu_2$

In two dimensions we consider

$$
\left(\frac{X_n}{m}, \frac{X_{n+1}}{m}\right)
=
\frac{X_n}{m}(1,1) + \frac{Y_n}{m}(0,1).
$$

The associated direction vectors are

$$
u_2 = (1,1), \quad v_2 = (0,1).
$$

The dual lattice in this case is generated by integer vectors orthogonal to the span of $(1,a)$ in the standard spectral formulation, yielding the classical result:

$$
\|h\| = \sqrt{1+a^2}, \quad \nu_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+a^2}}.
$$

This remains valid, including degenerate cases:

- If $a=0$, then $\nu_2 = 1$.
- If $a=\pm 1$, the lattice becomes more regular but the same formula still applies.

Thus

$$
\boxed{\nu_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+a^2}}.}
$$

## 3. Correct formulation of the 6-dimensional dual lattice

For $t=6$, the spectral value is defined as

$$
\nu_6 = \frac{1}{\lambda_{\min}},
$$

where $\lambda_{\min}$ is the Euclidean length of the shortest nonzero integer vector

$$
h = (h_0,\dots,h_5) \in \mathbb{Z}^6
$$

satisfying

$$
h \cdot u = 0, \quad h \cdot v = 0.
$$

### 3.1 Explicit orthogonality conditions

From $u = (1,1,1,1,1,1)$,

$$
h_0 + h_1 + h_2 + h_3 + h_4 + h_5 = 0.
$$

From $v = (0,S_1,S_2,S_3,S_4,S_5)$,

$$
h_1 S_1 + h_2 S_2 + h_3 S_3 + h_4 S_4 + h_5 S_5 = 0.
$$

Thus the dual lattice is the set of integer solutions to this 2-equation system.

## 4. Why the original claim $\nu_6 = 1$ is false

The previous argument assumed vectors such as

$$
(1,-1,0,0,0,0)
$$

lie in the dual lattice. However:

$$
(1,-1,0,0,0,0)\cdot v = -S_1 \neq 0 \quad \text{in general}.
$$

Hence such unit vectors are not valid dual-lattice elements unless special choices of $a$ force cancellations, which is not true for arbitrary LCGs of potency 2.

Therefore:

- there is no universal unit-length dual vector,
- the spectral bound in 6 dimensions depends on $a$.

## 5. Correct conclusion for $\nu_6$

The dual lattice is a rank-4 integer lattice defined by the two constraints above. Its shortest vector length is:

$$
\lambda_{\min}
=
\min_{\substack{h \in \mathbb{Z}^6 \setminus \{0\} \\
h_0+\cdots+h_5=0 \\
h_1 S_1 + \cdots + h_5 S_5=0}}
\|h\|.
$$

Therefore

$$
\boxed{
\nu_6 =
\frac{1}{
\min\{\|h\| : h \in \mathbb{Z}^6 \setminus \{0\},\,
h \cdot u = 0,\,
h \cdot v = 0\}
}.
}
$$

This value depends on $a$ through the coefficients $S_k$. No universal constant simplification exists for all potency-2 LCGs.

## Final corrected result

$$
\boxed{\nu_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+a^2}}}
$$

$$
\boxed{
\nu_6 =
\frac{1}{
\min_{\substack{h \in \mathbb{Z}^6 \setminus \{0\} \\
h \cdot (1,1,1,1,1,1)=0 \\
h \cdot (0,S_1,S_2,S_3,S_4,S_5)=0}}
\|h\|
}
}
$$

with $S_k = 1 + a + \cdots + a^{k-1}$.

The key correction is that the 6-dimensional spectral structure is governed by a nontrivial integer intersection problem in the orthogonal complement of a 2-dimensional affine subspace, not by coordinate-difference vectors.
