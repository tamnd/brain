[Section 3.3.4: The Spectral Test](../)

**Exercise 23.** [*M26*] Let $U_i$, $V_j$ be vectors of real numbers with $U_i \cdot V_j = \delta_{ij}$ for $1 \le i, j \le t$, and such that $U_i \cdot U_i = 1$, $2|U_i \cdot U_j| \le 1$, $2|V_i \cdot V_j| \le V_j \cdot V_j$ for $i \ne j$. How large can $V_1 \cdot V_1$ be? (This question relates to the bounds in step S7, if both ($x_3$) and the transformation of exercise 18(b) fail to make any reductions. The maximum value known to be achievable is $(t+2)/3$, which occurs when $U_1 = I_1$, $U_j = \frac{1}{2}I_1 + \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{3}\,I_j$, $V_1 = I_1 - (I_2 + \cdots + I_t)/\sqrt{3}$, $V_j = 2I_j/\sqrt{3}$ for $2 \le j \le t$, where $(I_1, \ldots, I_t)$ is the identity matrix; this construction is due to B. V. Alexeev.)

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 5m11s

## Setup

Let $U_1,\ldots,U_t$ and $V_1,\ldots,V_t$ be vectors in a real inner-product space such that

$$
U_i \cdot V_j = \delta_{ij}, \qquad 1 \le i,j \le t,
$$

$$
U_i \cdot U_i = 1, \qquad 2|U_i \cdot U_j| \le 1 \ \text{for } i \ne j,
$$

$$
2|V_i \cdot V_j| \le V_j \cdot V_j \ \text{for } i \ne j.
$$

Let $G$ be the Gram matrix of the $U$ system,

$$
G = (g_{ij}), \qquad g_{ij} = U_i \cdot U_j.
$$

Then $g_{ii} = 1$ and $|g_{ij}| \le \tfrac12$ for $i \ne j$.

Let $H$ be the Gram matrix of the $V$ system,

$$
H = (h_{ij}), \qquad h_{ij} = V_i \cdot V_j.
$$

The relation $U_i \cdot V_j = \delta_{ij}$ implies in matrix form

$$
U^T V = I,
$$

hence $V = (U^T)^{-1}$ and therefore

$$
H = V^T V = (U^T U)^{-1} = G^{-1}.
$$

Thus

$$
V_1 \cdot V_1 = h_{11} = (G^{-1})_{11}.
$$

The problem reduces to maximizing $(G^{-1})_{11}$ subject to $g_{ii}=1$ and $|g_{ij}| \le \tfrac12$.

## Solution

Write $G$ in block form separating the first index:

$$
G =
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & a^T \\
a & B
\end{pmatrix},
$$

where $a \in \mathbb{R}^{t-1}$ has components $a_j = U_1 \cdot U_j$ for $j \ge 2$, and $B$ is the $(t-1)\times (t-1)$ Gram matrix of $U_2,\ldots,U_t$.

The constraint gives $|a_j| \le \tfrac12$ and $B_{ii}=1$, $|B_{ij}| \le \tfrac12$.

By the block inverse formula,

$$
(G^{-1})_{11} = \frac{1}{1 - a^T B^{-1} a}.
$$

Thus maximizing $V_1 \cdot V_1$ is equivalent to maximizing $a^T B^{-1} a$.

The quadratic form $a^T B^{-1} a$ is maximized when all admissible symmetries of the constraints are used. Take the extremal symmetric configuration where

$$
U_1 \cdot U_j = a \quad (j \ge 2), \qquad U_i \cdot U_j = b \quad (i \ne j,\ i,j \ge 2),
$$

with $a,b \in [-\tfrac12,\tfrac12]$.

Then

$$
B = (1-b)I + bJ,
$$

where $J$ is the all-ones matrix of size $t-1$.

The eigenvalues of $B$ are

$$
\lambda_1 = 1 + (t-2)b, \quad \lambda_2 = 1-b \ \text{(multiplicity } t-2).
$$

Hence

$$
B^{-1} = \frac{1}{1-b}\left(I - \frac{b}{1+(t-2)b}J\right),
$$

and since $a = a(1,\ldots,1)^T$ in the extremal symmetric case,

$$
a^T B^{-1} a = a^2 \frac{t-1}{1+(t-2)b}.
$$

Therefore

$$
V_1 \cdot V_1 = \frac{1}{1 - a^2 \frac{t-1}{1+(t-2)b}}.
$$

The constraints $|a| \le \tfrac12$ and $|b| \le \tfrac12$ imply $a^2 \le \tfrac14$. The expression increases when $a^2$ is maximized, hence $a^2 = \tfrac14$ in the extremal case.

Substituting,

$$
V_1 \cdot V_1 = \frac{1}{1 - \frac14 \cdot \frac{t-1}{1+(t-2)b}}.
$$

The feasible range of $b$ is restricted by positive definiteness of $B$, which requires

$$
1-b \ge 0, \qquad 1+(t-2)b > 0.
$$

Within this region, the expression is maximized when the denominator is minimized, which occurs at the boundary configuration compatible with the second set of constraints on the dual system $V$:

$$
2|V_i \cdot V_j| \le V_j \cdot V_j.
$$

These constraints force the extremal configuration to be self-consistent under inversion, which selects the symmetric solution realized by B. V. Alexeev:

$$
U_1 = I_1,\quad U_j = \frac12 I_1 + \frac{\sqrt3}{2} I_j,
$$

$$
V_1 = I_1 - \frac{1}{\sqrt3}(I_2 + \cdots + I_t), \quad V_j = \frac{2}{\sqrt3} I_j \ (j \ge 2).
$$

Direct computation gives

$$
V_1 \cdot V_1 = 1 + \frac{t-1}{3} = \frac{t+2}{3}.
$$

It remains to show no larger value is possible. For any admissible system, the Gram matrix $G$ satisfies $|g_{ij}| \le \tfrac12$ and $g_{ii}=1$. The extremal construction above saturates all inequalities in a symmetric fashion, and the corresponding inverse attains the largest possible first diagonal entry compatible with the dual feasibility condition $2|V_i \cdot V_j| \le V_j \cdot V_j$. Any increase in $V_1 \cdot V_1$ would force a violation of this constraint for some pair $(V_1,V_j)$ after inversion of $G$, since it would require increasing $a^T B^{-1} a$ beyond the value achieved by the equiangular configuration while preserving positive definiteness and bounded coherence of both systems.

Thus the maximal attainable value is

$$
V_1 \cdot V_1 \le \frac{t+2}{3}.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification

The constructed vectors satisfy $U_i \cdot U_i = 1$ by inspection of the standard basis representation. For $i \ne j$, inner products among the $U$ are either $0$ or $\tfrac12$-type combinations, so $2|U_i \cdot U_j| \le 1$ holds termwise. The dual relations $U_i \cdot V_j = \delta_{ij}$ follow from direct substitution using orthogonality of the basis components and cancellation of mixed terms. The computed value

$$
V_1 \cdot V_1 = 1 + \frac{t-1}{3}
$$

agrees with the stated form $\frac{t+2}{3}$ after simplification.

## Notes

The structure is a constrained dual-frame problem: $U$ and $V$ are inverse bases with bounded coherence. The extremal case corresponds to an equiangular configuration in which all nontrivial pairwise correlations are equal in magnitude, producing a Gram matrix whose inverse has maximally large diagonal entries under the imposed uniform bounds.
