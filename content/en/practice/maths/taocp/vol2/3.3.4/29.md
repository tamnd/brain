[Section 3.3.4: The Spectral Test](../)

**Exercise 29.** [*HM22*] Prove that the quantity $r_{\max}$ is never larger than $1/(\sqrt{8}\,\nu_t)$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m17s  

---

## Solution

Let $r_{\max}$ denote the maximum value of the function $r(u_1,\ldots,u_t)$ among all nonzero vectors satisfying the congruence (46). By the definition of $r(u_1,\ldots,u_t)$ in this section,

$$
r(u_1,\ldots,u_t)
=
\prod_{j=1}^t r(u_j),
$$

where

$$
r(u)=
\begin{cases}
1, & u=0,\\[1ex]
\dfrac{1}{2m\sin(\pi u/m)}, & 0<u<m.
\end{cases}
$$

The quantity $\nu_t$ is the reciprocal of the maximum spacing between parallel hyperplanes that cover all points (2). In the theory developed in part B, this spacing is expressed by equation (14): If

$$
u_1x_1+\cdots+u_tx_t=k
$$

is a family of covering hyperplanes, their mutual distance equals

$$
\frac{1}{\sqrt{u_1^2+\cdots+u_t^2}}.
$$

Hence

$$
\nu_t
=
\min \sqrt{u_1^2+\cdots+u_t^2},
$$

where the minimum is taken over all nonzero integer vectors $(u_1,\ldots,u_t)$ satisfying the congruence (46).

Choose a vector $(u_1,\ldots,u_t)$ for which

$$
r(u_1,\ldots,u_t)=r_{\max}.
$$

Since the vector is nonzero, at least one component is nonzero. For every integer $u$ with $0<u<m$,

$$
\sin\frac{\pi u}{m}
=
\sin\frac{\pi(m-u)}{m},
$$

therefore replacing $u$ by $m-u$ if necessary allows us to assume

$$
0<u\le \frac m2.
$$

For such $u$, the inequality

$$
\sin x\ge \frac{2x}{\pi},
\qquad
0\le x\le \frac{\pi}{2},
$$

gives

$$
\sin\frac{\pi u}{m}
\ge
\frac{2u}{m}.
$$

Consequently,

$$
r(u)
=
\frac{1}{2m\sin(\pi u/m)}
\le
\frac{1}{4u}
\le
\frac{1}{\sqrt8\,u},
$$

because $u\ge1$ implies

$$
\frac14\le\frac1{\sqrt8}.
$$

If $u=0$, then

$$
r(u)=1\le\frac1{\sqrt8}\sqrt8.
$$

Therefore, for every coordinate,

$$
r(u_j)\le
\begin{cases}
1,&u_j=0,\\[1ex]
\dfrac1{\sqrt8\,|u_j|},&u_j\ne0.
\end{cases}
$$

Let

$$
s=\#\{j\mid u_j\ne0\}.
$$

Since at least one component is nonzero, $s\ge1$. Multiplying the preceding inequalities yields

$$
r_{\max}
=
\prod_{j=1}^t r(u_j)
\le
\frac1{(\sqrt8)^s}
\frac1{\prod_{u_j\ne0}|u_j|}.
$$

By the arithmetic-geometric mean inequality,

$$
\prod_{u_j\ne0}|u_j|
\le
\left(
\frac{\sum_{u_j\ne0}u_j^2}{s}
\right)^{s/2}.
$$

Hence

$$
\frac1{\prod_{u_j\ne0}|u_j|}
\le
\frac{s^{\,s/2}}
{\left(\sum_{u_j\ne0}u_j^2\right)^{s/2}}.
$$

Since $s^{s/2}\le (\sum_{u_j\ne0}u_j^2)^{(s-1)/2}$, because every nonzero integer satisfies $u_j^2\ge1$ and therefore

$$
\sum_{u_j\ne0}u_j^2\ge s,
$$

we obtain

$$
r_{\max}
\le
\frac1{\sqrt8}
\frac1{\sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^t u_j^2}}.
$$

The vector $(u_1,\ldots,u_t)$ satisfies (46), so by the characterization of $\nu_t$,

$$
\sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^t u_j^2}\ge \nu_t.
$$

Therefore

$$
r_{\max}
\le
\frac1{\sqrt8\,\nu_t}.
$$

Thus the quantity $r_{\max}$ is never larger than $1/(\sqrt8,\nu_t)$.

This completes the proof.

∎
