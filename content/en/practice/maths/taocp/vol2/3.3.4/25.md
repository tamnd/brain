[Section 3.3.4: The Spectral Test](../)

**Exercise 25.** [*HM24*] Let $d$ be a divisor of $m$ and let $0 \le q < d$. Prove that $\sum r(k)$, summed over all $0 \le k < m$ such that $k \bmod d = q$, is at most $(2/d\pi)\ln(m/d) + O(1)$. (Here $r(k)$ is defined in Eq. (40) when $t = 1$.)

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 5m25s  

---

For $t=1$, Eq. (40) gives

$$
r(k)=\frac1m\sum_{h=1}^{m-1}
\frac{\sin(2\pi hk/m)}{\sin(\pi h/m)}.
$$

Let

$$
N=\frac md,
$$

and consider

$$
S(q)=\sum_{\substack{0\le k<m\\k\equiv q\pmod d}}r(k)
=\sum_{j=0}^{N-1}r(q+jd).
$$

The essential point is that one must preserve the cancellations in the Fourier expansion. Therefore we substitute the expression for $r(k)$ directly and interchange the finite sums:

$$
S(q)
=
\frac1m
\sum_{h=1}^{m-1}
\frac1{\sin(\pi h/m)}
\sum_{j=0}^{N-1}
\sin\!\left(
\frac{2\pi h(q+jd)}m
\right).
$$

Since $m=dN$,

$$
\frac{2\pi h(q+jd)}m
=
\frac{2\pi hq}{m}
+
\frac{2\pi hj}{N},
$$

hence

$$
S(q)
=
\frac1m
\sum_{h=1}^{m-1}
\frac1{\sin(\pi h/m)}
\sum_{j=0}^{N-1}
\sin\!\left(
\frac{2\pi hq}{m}
+
\frac{2\pi hj}{N}
\right).
$$

Write

$$
\theta_h=\frac{2\pi hq}{m}.
$$

Using imaginary parts,

$$
\sum_{j=0}^{N-1}
\sin\!\left(
\theta_h+\frac{2\pi hj}{N}
\right)
=
\Im\left(
e^{i\theta_h}
\sum_{j=0}^{N-1}
e^{2\pi ihj/N}
\right).
$$

The geometric sum equals $0$ unless $N\mid h$, in which case every term is $1$ and the sum equals $N$. Therefore

$$
\sum_{j=0}^{N-1}
\sin\!\left(
\theta_h+\frac{2\pi hj}{N}
\right)
=
\begin{cases}
N\sin\theta_h,&N\mid h,\\
0,&N\nmid h.
\end{cases}
$$

Thus all Fourier modes except the multiples of $N$ cancel completely. Writing $h=\ell N$, with

$$
1\le \ell\le d-1,
$$

we obtain

$$
S(q)
=
\frac Nm
\sum_{\ell=1}^{d-1}
\frac{
\sin(2\pi \ell q/d)
}{
\sin(\pi \ell/d)
}.
$$

Since $N=m/d$,

$$
S(q)
=
\frac1d
\sum_{\ell=1}^{d-1}
\frac{
\sin(2\pi \ell q/d)
}{
\sin(\pi \ell/d)
}.
$$

Therefore the sum over a residue class depends only on $d$ and $q$, not on $m$.

Now we need an upper bound. Because

$$
\left|
\sin(2x)
\right|
=
2|\sin x\,\cos x|
\le
2|\sin x|,
$$

we have

$$
\left|
\frac{\sin(2\pi \ell q/d)}
{\sin(\pi \ell/d)}
\right|
\le 2.
$$

Hence

$$
|S(q)|
\le
\frac1d
\sum_{\ell=1}^{d-1}2
=
2\left(1-\frac1d\right)
=O(1).
$$

Since $O(1)\le (2/(d\pi))\ln(m/d)+O(1)$, it follows immediately that

$$
S(q)
\le
\frac{2}{d\pi}\ln\!\left(\frac md\right)+O(1).
$$

Therefore

$$
\boxed{
\sum_{\substack{0\le k<m\\k\equiv q\pmod d}}
r(k)
\le
\frac{2}{d\pi}\ln\!\left(\frac md\right)+O(1).
}
$$

The crucial correction is that no absolute values are introduced inside the Fourier series. Averaging over one residue class modulo $d$ annihilates every Fourier mode except those with frequency divisible by $m/d$, and the resulting quantity is bounded uniformly. This is stronger than the estimate required in the exercise.
