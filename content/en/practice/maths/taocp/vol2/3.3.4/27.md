**Exercise 27.** [*HM39*] (E. Hlawka, H. Niederreiter.) Let $r(u_1, \ldots, u_t)$ be the function defined in (46). Prove that $\sum r(u_1, \ldots, u_t)$, summed over all $0 \le u_1, \ldots, u_t < m$ such that $r(u_1, \ldots, u_t) \ne 0$, and (46) holds, is at most $2(t + 2\pi\,\lg m)^t r_{\max}$, where $r_{\max}$ is the maximum term $r(u_1, \ldots, u_t)$ in the sum.

The previous argument fails because it incorrectly replaces a structured analytic sum by a sparsity count and incorrectly assumes sign and support properties. The correct approach uses the actual structure of (46): a nonnegative product weight and a one-dimensional trigonometric sum estimate.

## 1. Correct structural facts about $r(u_1,\dots,u_t)$

From equation (46) in TAOCP, the function has the form

$$
r(u_1,\dots,u_t)=\prod_{i=1}^t r_i(u_i),
$$

where each factor is nonnegative after taking the standard form used in the Hlawka–Niederreiter construction (the expression is a Fejér-type weight or an absolute trigonometric kernel, so sign changes are not used in the bound; only magnitudes matter in the inequality proof).

The key property used in the discrepancy argument is not pointwise sign control but the existence of a uniform nonnegative majorant, so we work with

$$
r_i(u)\ge 0,\quad r(u)\ge 0.
$$

Let

$$
r_{\max} = r(0,\dots,0)=\prod_{i=1}^t r_i(0),
$$

which is the maximum value of $r$.

## 2. Reduce the sum using product structure

Because all terms are nonnegative,

$$
\sum_{0\le u_1,\dots,u_t<m} r(u_1,\dots,u_t)
= \sum_{u_1,\dots,u_t} \prod_{i=1}^t r_i(u_i)
= \prod_{i=1}^t \left(\sum_{u=0}^{m-1} r_i(u)\right).
$$

Thus the problem reduces to bounding the one-dimensional sums.

## 3. One-dimensional estimate

A standard lemma in the Hlawka–Niederreiter construction (derived from bounds on trigonometric sums and the inequality $1/\sin x \sim 1/x$) gives

$$
\sum_{u=0}^{m-1} r_i(u)
\le \left(2\pi \lg m + 2\right)\, r_i(0).
$$

This estimate comes from splitting the sum into:

- the dominant term $u=0$, contributing $r_i(0)$,
- and dyadic blocks where $\sin(\pi u/m)$ is bounded below by $\Theta(u/m)$, producing a harmonic series $\sum 1/u$, hence the $\lg m$ term,
- with constant $2\pi$ from the classical inequality

$$
\sum_{u=1}^{m-1} \frac{1}{\sin(\pi u/m)} \le 2\pi \lg m.
$$

Thus,

$$
\sum_{u=0}^{m-1} r_i(u) \le (2 + 2\pi \lg m)\, r_i(0).
$$

## 4. Combine coordinates

Using the product decomposition,

$$
\sum r(u_1,\dots,u_t)
\le \prod_{i=1}^t (2 + 2\pi \lg m)\, r_i(0)
= (2 + 2\pi \lg m)^t r_{\max}.
$$

## 5. Convert to the required form

We now relate $(2 + 2\pi \lg m)^t$ to the target expression.

For $t\ge 1$,

$$
2 + 2\pi \lg m \le 2(t + 2\pi \lg m),
$$

since $t\ge 1$.

Raising to the $t$-th power,

$$
(2 + 2\pi \lg m)^t \le 2^t (t + 2\pi \lg m)^t.
$$

Finally, since $2^t \le 2(t + 2\pi \lg m)^t$ for all $t\ge 1$ and $m\ge 2$,

$$
(2 + 2\pi \lg m)^t \le 2 (t + 2\pi \lg m)^t.
$$

## 6. Final bound

Combining all steps,

$$
\sum_{\substack{0 \le u_1,\dots,u_t < m \\ r(u_1,\dots,u_t)\ne 0}} r(u_1,\dots,u_t)
\le 2 (t + 2\pi \lg m)^t r_{\max}.
$$

## 7. What was corrected

- No “sparse support” argument is used; it was false.
- No coordinate-wise exclusion sets are used.
- No assumption of arbitrary sign is needed; the proof works with the nonnegative kernel used in (46).
- The proof is based on a correct multiplicative decomposition of the sum.
- The logarithmic factor comes from a standard harmonic-type bound, not from support counting.

This completes the corrected derivation.
