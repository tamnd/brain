**Exercise 6.** &#9654; [*20*] [20] Find all multipliers $a$ that satisfy the conditions of Theorem A when $m = 10^8 - 1$. (See Table 3.2.1.1-1.)

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 4m57s

## Solution

By Theorem A, the multipliers that yield the maximum period are characterized by the conditions

$$
a-1 \equiv 0 \pmod p
$$

for every prime divisor $p$ of $m$, together with the additional condition

$$
a-1 \equiv 0 \pmod 4
$$

only when $4\mid m$.

For

$$
m=10^8-1=99\,999\,999,
$$

we factor $m$ as

$$
10^8-1=(10^4-1)(10^4+1)
      =9999\cdot10001
      =(3^2\cdot11\cdot101)(73\cdot137).
$$

Hence

$$
m=3^2\cdot11\cdot73\cdot101\cdot137.
$$

The distinct prime divisors are

$$
3,\ 11,\ 73,\ 101,\ 137.
$$

Since $m$ is odd, condition (iii) of Theorem A is irrelevant. Therefore $a$ must satisfy

$$
a\equiv1\pmod3,\qquad
a\equiv1\pmod{11},\qquad
a\equiv1\pmod{73},\qquad
a\equiv1\pmod{101},\qquad
a\equiv1\pmod{137}.
$$

Because these moduli are pairwise relatively prime, this is equivalent to

$$
a\equiv1
\pmod{3\cdot11\cdot73\cdot101\cdot137}.
$$

The product is

$$
3\cdot11\cdot73\cdot101\cdot137
=33\,333\,333.
$$

Thus

$$
a\equiv1\pmod{33\,333\,333}.
$$

Modulo $m=99\,999\,999=3\cdot33\,333\,333$, there are exactly three residue classes satisfying this congruence:

$$
a\equiv1,\qquad
a\equiv1+33\,333\,333,\qquad
a\equiv1+2\cdot33\,333\,333
\pmod{99\,999\,999}.
$$

Hence the complete set of multipliers is

$$
a\equiv1,\ 33\,333\,334,\ 66\,666\,667
\pmod{99\,999\,999}.
$$

Conversely, each of these values satisfies

$$
a-1\equiv0
\pmod{3\cdot11\cdot73\cdot101\cdot137},
$$

so $a-1$ is divisible by every prime factor of $m$. Therefore conditions (ii) and (iii) of Theorem A hold, and together with condition (i) on $c$, the resulting linear congruential sequence has period $m$.

Thus all multipliers are

$$
\boxed{a\equiv1,\ 33\,333\,334,\ 66\,666\,667
\pmod{99\,999\,999}}.
$$

This completes the proof.

∎
