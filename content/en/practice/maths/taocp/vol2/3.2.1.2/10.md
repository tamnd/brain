[Section 3.2.1.2: Choice of Multiplier](../)

**Exercise 10.** [*M21*] [M21] For what values of $m$ is $\lambda(m) = \varphi(m)$?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 28m41s  

---

## Solution

Write

$$
m=2^{e}p_1^{e_1}\cdots p_t^{e_t},
$$

where $p_1,\dots,p_t$ are distinct odd primes. Recall that

$$
\lambda(m)
=\operatorname{lcm}\!\bigl(\lambda(2^e),
\lambda(p_1^{e_1}),\dots,\lambda(p_t^{e_t})\bigr),
$$

and

$$
\varphi(m)
=\varphi(2^e)\prod_{i=1}^{t}\varphi(p_i^{e_i}).
$$

For odd prime powers,

$$
\lambda(p^k)=\varphi(p^k)=p^{k-1}(p-1).
$$

For powers of $2$,

$$
\lambda(2)=1,\qquad
\lambda(4)=2,\qquad
\lambda(2^e)=2^{e-2}\quad(e\ge3),
$$

while

$$
\varphi(2)=1,\qquad
\varphi(4)=2,\qquad
\varphi(2^e)=2^{e-1}\quad(e\ge3).
$$

We seek all $m$ such that $\lambda(m)=\varphi(m)$.

### 1. Prime powers

If $m=p^k$ with $p$ odd, then

$$
\lambda(m)=\varphi(m).
$$

Also,

$$
\lambda(2)=\varphi(2)=1,\qquad
\lambda(4)=\varphi(4)=2.
$$

But for $e\ge3$,

$$
\lambda(2^e)=2^{e-2}
<2^{e-1}
=\varphi(2^e).
$$

Hence the prime-power solutions are

$$
m=2,\qquad m=4,\qquad m=p^k\ (p\ \text{odd prime}).
$$

### 2. Numbers divisible by at least two odd primes

Suppose $t\ge2$. Let

$$
A_i=\lambda(p_i^{e_i})
=\varphi(p_i^{e_i}).
$$

Then

$$
\lambda(m)\le \operatorname{lcm}(A_1,\dots,A_t)
< A_1A_2\cdots A_t,
$$

because at least two positive integers are involved in the lcm. Therefore

$$
\lambda(m)
<
\prod_{i=1}^{t}\varphi(p_i^{e_i})
\le
\varphi(m).
$$

Hence no such $m$ satisfies $\lambda(m)=\varphi(m)$.

Thus there can be at most one odd prime factor.

### 3. Numbers of the form $2^e p^k$

Let $p$ be odd.

#### Case $e\ge3$

Since

$$
\lambda(2^e)<\varphi(2^e),
$$

we obtain

$$
\lambda(2^e p^k)
=
\operatorname{lcm}\!\bigl(\lambda(2^e),\varphi(p^k)\bigr)
\le
\lambda(2^e)\varphi(p^k)
<
\varphi(2^e)\varphi(p^k)
=
\varphi(2^e p^k).
$$

So no solutions occur.

#### Case $e=2$

Then

$$
\lambda(4p^k)
=
\operatorname{lcm}\!\bigl(2,\varphi(p^k)\bigr)
=
\varphi(p^k),
$$

because $\varphi(p^k)$ is even. Hence

$$
\lambda(4p^k)=\varphi(p^k)
<
2\varphi(p^k)
=
\varphi(4p^k).
$$

So there are no solutions.

#### Case $e=1$

Then

$$
\lambda(2p^k)
=
\operatorname{lcm}\!\bigl(1,\varphi(p^k)\bigr)
=
\varphi(p^k).
$$

Also

$$
\varphi(2p^k)
=
\varphi(2)\varphi(p^k)
=
\varphi(p^k).
$$

Therefore

$$
\lambda(2p^k)=\varphi(2p^k).
$$

Thus every number of the form $2p^k$ is a solution.

### Conclusion

The values of $m$ for which $\lambda(m)=\varphi(m)$ are exactly

$$
\boxed{
m=p^k,\qquad
m=2p^k,
}
$$

where $p$ is an odd prime and $k\ge1$, together with

$$
\boxed{m=2,\quad m=4.}
$$

Equivalently,

$$
\boxed{
m\in\{2,4\}
\ \text{or}\ 
m=(1\ \text{or}\ 2)\cdot p^k
\ \text{with}\ p\ \text{odd prime}.
}
$$

∎
