[Section 3.2.1.2: Choice of Multiplier](../)

**Exercise 14.** [*M15*] [M15] If $e > 1$ and $a$ is a primitive element modulo $p$, prove that either $a$ or $a + p$ is a primitive element modulo $p^e$. [*Hint:* See exercise 12.]

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 14m17s  

---

## Solution

Assume $e>1$ and that $a$ is a primitive element modulo $p$.

By Exercise 12, for every odd prime $p$, an integer $b$ is a primitive element modulo $p^e$ if and only if

1. $b$ is a primitive element modulo $p$, and
2. $b^{p-1}\not\equiv1\pmod{p^2}$.

Since $a$ is primitive modulo $p$, so is $a+p$, because $a+p\equiv a\pmod p$.

It therefore suffices to prove that at least one of the congruences

$$
a^{p-1}\not\equiv1\pmod{p^2},
\qquad
(a+p)^{p-1}\not\equiv1\pmod{p^2}
$$

holds.

Suppose, on the contrary, that both fail. Then

$$
a^{p-1}\equiv1\pmod{p^2}
\tag{1}
$$

and

$$
(a+p)^{p-1}\equiv1\pmod{p^2}.
\tag{2}
$$

Expand $(a+p)^{p-1}$ by the binomial theorem:

$$
(a+p)^{p-1}
=
a^{p-1}
+\binom{p-1}{1}a^{p-2}p
+\sum_{k=2}^{p-1}\binom{p-1}{k}a^{p-1-k}p^k .
$$

Every term in the summation is divisible by $p^2$, hence

$$
(a+p)^{p-1}
\equiv
a^{p-1}
+(p-1)a^{p-2}p
\pmod{p^2}.
$$

Using (1),

$$
(a+p)^{p-1}
\equiv
1+(p-1)a^{p-2}p
\pmod{p^2}.
$$

Combining this with (2) gives

$$
(p-1)a^{p-2}p\equiv0\pmod{p^2},
$$

hence

$$
(p-1)a^{p-2}\equiv0\pmod p.
$$

Since $a$ is a primitive element modulo $p$, it is not divisible by $p$. Therefore $a^{p-2}\not\equiv0\pmod p$, and also $p-1\not\equiv0\pmod p$. Their product cannot be congruent to $0$ modulo $p$, a contradiction.

Thus at least one of

$$
a^{p-1}\not\equiv1\pmod{p^2},
\qquad
(a+p)^{p-1}\not\equiv1\pmod{p^2}
$$

must hold. Since both $a$ and $a+p$ are primitive modulo $p$, Exercise 12 implies that whichever of these two numbers satisfies the noncongruence is a primitive element modulo $p^e$.

Hence either $a$ or $a+p$ is a primitive element modulo $p^e$.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Notes

The statement is intended for odd primes $p$, since Exercise 12 applies only in that case. The powers of $2$ are handled separately by Theorem C.
