## Setup

Let $x$ be an odd integer with $x>1$.

Part (a) asks us to prove that there exists a unique integer $f>1$ such that

$$
x\equiv 2^f\pm1 \pmod{2^{f+1}}.
$$

Part (b) assumes

$$
1<x<2^e-1,
$$

and asks us to prove that the order of $x$ modulo $2^e$ is

$$
2^{\,e-f},
$$

where $f$ is the integer determined in part (a).

Part (c) asks us to deduce parts (i)-(iv) of Theorem C from the result of part (b).

## Solution

Since $x$ is odd, either $x-1$ or $x+1$ is divisible by $4$, but not both. Define

$$
y=
\begin{cases}
x-1,&x\equiv1\pmod4,\\[2mm]
x+1,&x\equiv3\pmod4.
\end{cases}
$$

Then $y$ is divisible by $4$. Let

$$
2^f\Vert y,
$$

that is, $2^f$ divides $y$ but $2^{f+1}$ does not. Since $4\mid y$, we have $f>1$.

Write

$$
y=2^f q,
$$

where $q$ is odd. Then

$$
y\equiv 2^f \pmod{2^{f+1}},
$$

because $q\equiv1\pmod2$. Replacing $y$ by $x-1$ or $x+1$ according to the definition above yields

$$
x\equiv 2^f+1 \pmod{2^{f+1}}
\qquad\text{or}\qquad
x\equiv 1-2^f \pmod{2^{f+1}},
$$

that is,

$$
x\equiv 2^f\pm1 \pmod{2^{f+1}}.
$$

This proves existence.

For uniqueness, suppose

$$
x\equiv 2^f\pm1 \pmod{2^{f+1}}
\quad\text{and}\quad
x\equiv 2^g\pm1 \pmod{2^{g+1}}
$$

with $f,g>1$.

Exactly one of $x-1$ and $x+1$ is divisible by $4$. Hence the sign is forced by the residue class of $x$ modulo $4$.

Assume first that $x\equiv1\pmod4$. Then both congruences must have the plus sign, and therefore

$$
x-1\equiv 2^f \pmod{2^{f+1}},
\qquad
x-1\equiv 2^g \pmod{2^{g+1}}.
$$

These congruences state precisely that

$$
2^f\Vert(x-1),
\qquad
2^g\Vert(x-1),
$$

hence $f=g$.

The case $x\equiv3\pmod4$ is identical, using $x+1$ instead of $x-1$. Therefore $f$ is unique.

This proves part (a).

Now let $f$ be the integer from part (a). We may write

$$
x=\pm(1+2^f q),
$$

where $q$ is odd.

First consider the positive sign,

$$
x=1+2^f q.
$$

Since $q$ is odd,

$$
x\equiv1\pmod{2^f},
\qquad
x\not\equiv1\pmod{2^{f+1}}.
$$

Applying Lemma P repeatedly with $p=2$ gives

$$
x^{2^g}\equiv1\pmod{2^{f+g}},
\qquad
x^{2^g}\not\equiv1\pmod{2^{f+g+1}}
\qquad(g\ge0).
$$

Taking $g=e-f$ yields

$$
x^{2^{e-f}}\equiv1\pmod{2^e}.
$$

Taking $g=e-f-1$ yields

$$
x^{2^{e-f-1}}\not\equiv1\pmod{2^e}.
$$

Hence the order of $x$ modulo $2^e$ is exactly $2^{e-f}$.

Now consider the negative sign,

$$
x=-(1+2^f q).
$$

Then

$$
x^2=(1+2^f q)^2.
$$

Since $f>1$,

$$
(1+2^f q)^2
=1+2^{f+1}q+2^{2f}q^2
\equiv1+2^{f+1}q
\pmod{2^{f+2}},
$$

and $q$ is odd. Therefore

$$
x^2\equiv1\pmod{2^{f+1}},
\qquad
x^2\not\equiv1\pmod{2^{f+2}}.
$$

Repeated application of Lemma P gives

$$
x^{2^{g+1}}\equiv1\pmod{2^{f+g+1}},
\qquad
x^{2^{g+1}}\not\equiv1\pmod{2^{f+g+2}}.
$$

Setting $g=e-f-1$ yields

$$
x^{2^{e-f}}\equiv1\pmod{2^e},
$$

while

$$
x^{2^{e-f-1}}\not\equiv1\pmod{2^e}.
$$

Again the order is exactly $2^{e-f}$.

Thus in all cases the order of $x$ modulo $2^e$ is

$$
\boxed{2^{e-f}}.
$$

This proves part (b).

For part (c), Theorem B states that the maximum possible order modulo $2^e$ is

$$
\lambda(2^e)=2^{e-2}
\qquad(e\ge3).
$$

By part (b), an odd integer $x$ has order $2^{e-f}$. This order is maximal exactly when

$$
2^{e-f}=2^{e-2},
$$

hence

$$
f=2.
$$

The condition $f=2$ means

$$
x\equiv2^2\pm1\pmod{2^3},
$$

that is,

$$
x\equiv3 \text{ or }5\pmod8.
$$

For $e\ge4$, the primitive elements modulo $2^e$ are therefore precisely the residue classes $3$ and $5$ modulo $8$, which is part (iv) of Theorem C.

For $e=3$, every odd residue modulo $8$ except $1$ has order $2$, namely the classes $3$, $5$, and $7$, giving part (iii).

For $e=2$, the unique primitive class modulo $4$ is $3$, giving part (ii).

For $e=1$, every odd residue modulo $2$ has order $1$, giving part (i).

This completes the proof.

## Verification

For $x\equiv1\pmod4$, the integer $f$ is the exact exponent of $2$ dividing $x-1$.

For $x\equiv3\pmod4$, the integer $f$ is the exact exponent of $2$ dividing $x+1$.

Lemma P increases by one the exact power of $2$ dividing $x^{2^g}-1$ at each squaring step. Consequently,

$$
2^{f+g}\Vert(x^{2^g}-1)
$$

in the positive-sign case, and

$$
2^{f+g+1}\Vert(x^{2^{g+1}}-1)
$$

in the negative-sign case. These exact divisibility statements yield the order $2^{e-f}$ immediately.

## Notes

Part (b) determines the exact order of every odd residue modulo $2^e$ solely from the highest power of $2$ dividing either $x-1$ or $x+1$. Theorem C for powers of $2$ is therefore a direct consequence of identifying the unique case $f=2$, which yields the maximal order.
