[Section 3.2.1.2: Choice of Multiplier](../)

**Exercise 9.** [*M30*] [M30] (W. E. Thomson.) When $c = 0$ and $m = 2^e \ge 16$, Theorems B and C say that the period has length $2^{e-2}$ if and only if the multiplier $a$ satisfies $a \bmod 8 = 3$ or $a \bmod 8 = 5$. Show that every such sequence is essentially a linear congruential sequence with $m = 2^{e-2}$, having *full* period, in the following sense:

a) If $X_{n+1} = (4e + 1)X_n \bmod 2^e$, and $X_n = 4Y_n + 1$, then

$$Y_{n+1} = ((4e+1)Y_n + c) \bmod 2^{e-2}.$$

b) If $X_{n+1} = (4e - 1)X_n \bmod 2^e$, and $X_n = ((-1)^n(4Y_n + 1)) \bmod 2^e$, then

$$X_{n+1} = ((1 - 4e)Y_n - c) \bmod 2^{e-2}.$$

[*Note:* In these formulas, $e$ is an odd integer. The literature contains several statements to the effect that sequences with $c = 0$ satisfying Theorem B are somehow more random than sequences satisfying Theorem A, in spite of the fact that the period is only one-fourth as long in the case of Theorem B. This exercise refutes such statements; in essence, we must give up two bits of the word length in order to save the addition of $c$, when $m$ is a power of 2.]

**Corrected Solution for Exercise 3.2.1.2.9**

We are asked to show that linear congruential sequences modulo $2^e$ with multiplier $a \equiv 3 \pmod 8$ or $a \equiv 5 \pmod 8$ and increment $c = 0$ can be reduced to sequences modulo $2^{e-2}$ with full period. We treat parts (a) and (b) separately, using careful modular arithmetic.

**Part (a).** Suppose $X_{n+1} = (4e + 1) X_n \pmod{2^e}$ and define $X_n = 4Y_n + 1$. We want to show that

$$
Y_{n+1} = ((4e + 1)Y_n + c) \bmod 2^{e-2}.
$$

**Step 1: Substitution.**

Set $X_n = 4Y_n + 1$. Then

$$
X_{n+1} = (4e + 1) X_n \equiv (4e + 1)(4Y_n + 1) \pmod{2^e}.
$$

Expand the product:

$$
(4e + 1)(4Y_n + 1) = (4e + 1)\cdot 4 Y_n + (4e + 1)\cdot 1 = 16 e Y_n + 4 Y_n + 4e + 1.
$$

Combine like terms:

$$
X_{n+1} = 16 e Y_n + 4 Y_n + 4 e + 1 = 4 (4 e Y_n + Y_n + e) + 1.
$$

**Step 2: Factor out 4.**

Define

$$
Y_{n+1} = 4 e Y_n + Y_n + e = (4 e + 1) Y_n + e \pmod{2^{e-2}}.
$$

Here the modulo reduces from $2^e$ to $2^{e-2}$ because $4Y_{n+1}$ appears in $X_{n+1}$ and multiplying by 4 eliminates the two least significant bits. The additive constant $e$ corresponds to $c$ in the original formula. If $c = 0$, the recurrence simplifies further to

$$
Y_{n+1} \equiv (4e + 1) Y_n \pmod{2^{e-2}}.
$$

**Step 3: Period analysis.**

Since $(4e + 1) \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$, it is coprime to $2^{e-2}$. Therefore the sequence $(Y_n)$ modulo $2^{e-2}$ has full period $2^{e-2}$. Hence $X_n$ modulo $2^e$ has period $2^{e-2}$, and we have established the reduction.

**Part (b).** Suppose $X_{n+1} = (4e - 1) X_n \pmod{2^e}$ and define

$$
X_n = (-1)^n (4Y_n + 1).
$$

We want to show

$$
Y_{n+1} \equiv ((1 - 4e) Y_n - c) \pmod{2^{e-2}}.
$$

**Step 1: Substitute $X_n$.**

We have

$$
X_{n+1} = (4e - 1) X_n = (4e - 1) (-1)^n (4Y_n + 1) = (-1)^n (-(4e - 1))(4Y_n + 1) = (-1)^{n+1} (4e - 1)(4Y_n + 1).
$$

Thus

$$
(-1)^{n+1} X_{n+1} = (4e - 1)(4Y_n + 1) = 16 e Y_n - 4 Y_n + 4e - 1 = 4 ((4e - 1) Y_n + e - 1) + 1.
$$

**Step 2: Define $Y_{n+1}$.**

Set

$$
Y_{n+1} = -((4e - 1) Y_n + e - 1) = (1 - 4e) Y_n - (e - 1) \pmod{2^{e-2}}.
$$

This shows the recurrence for $Y_n$ modulo $2^{e-2}$ and again the period is full because $1 - 4e$ is odd, hence coprime to $2^{e-2}$.
