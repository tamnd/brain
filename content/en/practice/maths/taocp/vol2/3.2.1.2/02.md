**Exercise 2.** &#9654; [*16*] The following two conditions are sufficient to guarantee the maximum length period, when $m$ is a power of 2? "(i) $c$ is odd; (ii) $a \bmod 4 = 1$."

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 46m21s  

---

Let \(x_{n+1} \equiv a x_n + c \pmod{m}\) with \(m = 2^k\), and consider the conditions

\[
c \text{ is odd}, \qquad a \equiv 1 \pmod{4}.
\]

We want to determine whether these are sufficient to guarantee that the LCG has maximal period \(m = 2^k\).

## Step 1: Maximal period criterion for LCGs

Knuth’s theorem (TAOCP 3.2.1.2) states that the LCG
\[
x_{n+1} \equiv a x_n + c \pmod{m}
\]
has period \(m\) for every starting value if and only if:

1. \(\gcd(c, m) = 1\),
2. \(a - 1\) is divisible by every prime factor of \(m\),
3. If \(4 \mid m\), then \(a - 1\) is divisible by \(4\).

## Step 2: Specialization to \(m = 2^k\)

Since \(m = 2^k\), the only prime factor of \(m\) is \(2\). We rewrite each condition:

### Condition 1

\[
\gcd(c, 2^k) = 1 \iff c \text{ is odd}.
\]

This is immediate because any even \(c\) shares a factor \(2\) with \(2^k\), while any odd \(c\) is coprime to \(2^k\).

### Condition 2

The requirement that \(a - 1\) is divisible by the prime factor \(2\) gives:
\[
a \equiv 1 \pmod{2},
\]
which is already implied by \(a \equiv 1 \pmod{4}\).

### Condition 3

Since \(4 \mid 2^k\) for all \(k \ge 2\), we must have:
\[
a \equiv 1 \pmod{4}.
\]

This is exactly one of the given assumptions.

## Step 3: Sufficiency

The two given conditions

\[
c \text{ is odd}, \qquad a \equiv 1 \pmod{4}
\]

satisfy all requirements of Knuth’s theorem for every \(k \ge 1\):

- \(c\) odd ensures \(\gcd(c, 2^k) = 1\),
- \(a \equiv 1 \pmod{4}\) ensures both \(a \equiv 1 \pmod{2}\) and, when \(k \ge 2\), the required \(a \equiv 1 \pmod{4}\).

Hence the LCG has full period \(2^k\).

## Step 4: Correct verification example

Take \(m = 8\), \(a = 5\), \(c = 3\), \(x_0 = 0\). Then

\[
x_{n+1} \equiv 5x_n + 3 \pmod{8}.
\]

Compute carefully:

\[
\begin{aligned}
x_0 &= 0 \\
x_1 &= 3 \\
x_2 &= 5\cdot 3 + 3 = 18 \equiv 2 \pmod{8} \\
x_3 &= 5\cdot 2 + 3 = 13 \equiv 5 \pmod{8} \\
x_4 &= 5\cdot 5 + 3 = 28 \equiv 4 \pmod{8} \\
x_5 &= 5\cdot 4 + 3 = 23 \equiv 7 \pmod{8} \\
x_6 &= 5\cdot 7 + 3 = 38 \equiv 6 \pmod{8} \\
x_7 &= 5\cdot 6 + 3 = 33 \equiv 1 \pmod{8} \\
x_8 &= 5\cdot 1 + 3 = 8 \equiv 0 \pmod{8}.
\end{aligned}
\]

The sequence returns to \(0\) after \(8\) steps, and all residues \(0,1,\dots,7\) appear exactly once, so the period is \(8\).

## Step 5: Conclusion

The conditions

\[
c \text{ odd}, \qquad a \equiv 1 \pmod{4}
\]

satisfy all parts of Knuth’s maximal-period criterion when \(m = 2^k\). Therefore they are sufficient to guarantee maximal period \(2^k\) for the linear congruential generator.
