[Section 3.2.1.2: Choice of Multiplier](../)

**Exercise 7.** &#9654; [*M23*] [M23] The period of a congruential sequence need not start with $X_0$, but we can always find indices $\mu \ge 0$ and $\lambda > 0$ such that $X_{n+\lambda} = X_n$ whenever $n \ge \mu$, and for which $\mu$ and $\lambda$ are the smallest possible values with this property. (See exercises 3.1-6 and 3.2.1-1.) If $\mu_j$ and $\lambda_j$ are the indices corresponding to the sequences

$$\left(X_0 \bmod p_j^{e_j},\ a \bmod p_j^{e_j},\ c \bmod p_j^{e_j}\right),$$

and if $\mu$ and $\lambda$ correspond to the composite sequence $(X_0, a, c, p_1^{e_1} \ldots p_t^{e_t})$. Lemma Q states that $\lambda$ is the least common multiple of $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_t$. What is the value of $\mu$ in terms of the values of $\mu_1, \ldots, \mu_t$? What is the maximum possible value of $\mu$ obtainable by varying $X_0$, $a$, and $c$, when $m = p_1^{e_1} \cdots p_t^{e_t}$ is fixed?

## Solution

Let the modulus be

$$
m = p_1^{e_1} p_2^{e_2} \cdots p_t^{e_t},
$$

and let $(X_n)$ denote the linear congruential sequence defined by $(X_0, a, c, m)$:

$$
X_{n+1} \equiv a X_n + c \pmod{m}, \quad n \ge 0.
$$

For each prime power $p_j^{e_j}$, define the corresponding residue sequence

$$
Y_n^{(j)} = X_n \bmod p_j^{e_j},
$$

which is itself a linear congruential sequence modulo $p_j^{e_j}$ with parameters $(X_0 \bmod p_j^{e_j}, a \bmod p_j^{e_j}, c \bmod p_j^{e_j})$. Let $\mu_j \ge 0$ and $\lambda_j > 0$ be the minimal preperiod and period of $Y_n^{(j)}$, i.e.,

$$
Y_{n+\lambda_j}^{(j)} = Y_n^{(j)} \quad \text{for all } n \ge \mu_j,
$$

with $\mu_j$ minimal.

By the Chinese Remainder Theorem, the original sequence $(X_n)$ satisfies

$$
X_n \equiv Y_n^{(j)} \pmod{p_j^{e_j}} \quad \text{for all } j = 1,\dots,t \text{ and all } n \ge 0.
$$

Hence $(X_n)$ enters a periodic regime with period

$$
\lambda = \mathrm{lcm}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_t),
$$

and preperiod $\mu$ equal to the smallest integer such that

$$
X_{n+\lambda} = X_n \quad \text{for all } n \ge \mu.
$$

### Step 1: Determination of $\mu$

By definition, $\mu$ is the minimal integer such that $(X_n)$ is periodic for all $n \ge \mu$. Equivalently,

$$
n \ge \mu \implies Y_{n+\lambda}^{(j)} = Y_n^{(j)} \quad \text{for all } j.
$$

- **Sufficiency:** If $n \ge \max(\mu_1, \ldots, \mu_t)$, then $n \ge \mu_j$ for each $j$. Therefore, $Y_{n+\lambda}^{(j)} = Y_n^{(j)}$ for all $j$, hence $X_{n+\lambda} = X_n$.
- **Necessity:** Suppose $\mu < \max(\mu_1, \ldots, \mu_t)$. Let $j$ be an index with $\mu_j > \mu$. By minimality of $\mu_j$, there exists some $n \ge \mu$ such that $Y_{n+\lambda_j}^{(j)} \ne Y_n^{(j)}$. Since $\lambda$ is a multiple of $\lambda_j$, we also have $Y_{n+\lambda}^{(j)} \ne Y_n^{(j)}$, hence $X_{n+\lambda} \ne X_n$. This contradicts minimality of $\mu$.

Therefore, the minimal preperiod of the composite sequence is

$$
\mu = \max(\mu_1, \ldots, \mu_t).
$$

This argument completes the justification for the first part.

### Step 2: Maximum possible value of $\mu$ for fixed $m$

We now determine the largest possible preperiod $\mu$ obtainable by varying $X_0$, $a$, and $c$, keeping $m$ fixed.

1. For a sequence modulo a prime power $p_j^{e_j}$, the maximal preperiod $\mu_j$ occurs when the sequence takes all nonrepeating residues before entering its cycle. Standard results on linear congruential sequences modulo $p^e$ (see Exercise 3.2.1.2-1 and 3.1-6) imply that the maximum preperiod of a sequence modulo $p_j^{e_j}$ is

$$
\mu_j^{\max} = p_j^{e_j}.
$$

This is achieved by suitable choice of $X_0, a, c$, for instance by taking $a \equiv 1 \pmod{p_j^{e_j}}$ and $c$ coprime to $p_j$, so that the sequence cycles through all residues before repeating.

1. Since $\mu = \max(\mu_1, \ldots, \mu_t)$, the maximum possible $\mu$ occurs when the largest of the $\mu_j$ is itself maximal. Therefore, the maximum achievable preperiod of $(X_n)$ modulo $m$ is

$$
\mu_{\max} = \max(p_1^{e_1}, p_2^{e_2}, \ldots, p_t^{e_t}).
$$

- **Attainability:** Choose parameters so that the component sequence modulo the prime power $p_j^{e_j}$ with the largest $p_j^{e_j}$ attains its maximal preperiod, while the others can have shorter preperiods. By the Chinese Remainder Theorem, these choices can be made simultaneously modulo each prime power, so the bound is achievable.

### Step 3: Conclusion

- The preperiod of the composite sequence $(X_n)$ is

$$
\boxed{\mu = \max(\mu_1, \ldots, \mu_t)}.
$$

- The maximum possible preperiod when $m = p_1^{e_1} \cdots p_t^{e_t}$ is

$$
\boxed{\mu_{\max} = \max(p_1^{e_1}, \ldots, p_t^{e_t})}.
$$

This completes the solution. ∎
