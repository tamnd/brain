**Exercise 5.** [*14*] Find all multipliers $a$ that satisfy the conditions of Theorem A when $m = 2^{35} + 1$. (The prime factors of $m$ may be found in Table 3.2.1.1–1.)

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m32s

## Solution

We are asked to find all multipliers $a$ satisfying the conditions of Theorem A when $m = 2^{35} + 1$. Theorem A states that a linear congruential sequence $(X_0, a, c, m)$ attains period $m$ if and only if

i) $c$ is relatively prime to $m$;

ii) $b = a - 1$ is a multiple of every prime divisor $p$ of $m$;

iii) $b$ is a multiple of 4 if $m$ is divisible by 4.

We are given $m = 2^{35} + 1$. The prime factorization of $m$ can be obtained from Table 3.2.1.1–1, which gives

$2^{35} + 1 = 3 \cdot 5 \cdot 7 \cdot 13 \cdot 17 \cdot 97 \cdot 131 \cdot 521.$

All of these factors are odd primes, so condition (iii) is automatically satisfied, because $m$ is not divisible by 4. Condition (i) restricts $c$ to be odd and not divisible by any of these primes, but the exercise asks only for the multipliers $a$, so we focus on conditions (ii) and (iii).

Let

$b = a - 1.$

Condition (ii) requires $b$ to be divisible by each prime factor of $m$, that is,

$b \equiv 0 \pmod{3}, \quad b \equiv 0 \pmod{5}, \quad b \equiv 0 \pmod{7}, \quad b \equiv 0 \pmod{13},$

$b \equiv 0 \pmod{17}, \quad b \equiv 0 \pmod{97}, \quad b \equiv 0 \pmod{131}, \quad b \equiv 0 \pmod{521}.$

Therefore $b$ must be a multiple of the least common multiple of these primes, which is their product, since all are distinct:

$b = k \cdot (3 \cdot 5 \cdot 7 \cdot 13 \cdot 17 \cdot 97 \cdot 131 \cdot 521)$

for some positive integer $k$. Denote

$M = 3 \cdot 5 \cdot 7 \cdot 13 \cdot 17 \cdot 97 \cdot 131 \cdot 521.$

Hence

$a = 1 + kM.$

Since $1 < a < m$, we require $1 < 1 + kM < 2^{35} + 1$, giving

$0 < kM < 2^{35} \quad \Rightarrow \quad 0 < k < \frac{2^{35}}{M}.$

We compute $M$ step by step:

$3 \cdot 5 = 15$

$15 \cdot 7 = 105$

$105 \cdot 13 = 1365$

$1365 \cdot 17 = 23205$

$23205 \cdot 97 = 2,250,285$

$2,250,285 \cdot 131 = 294,788,535$

$294,788,535 \cdot 521 = 153,587,983,935$

so $M = 153,587,983,935$. Next, $2^{35} = 34,359,738,368$, so

$0 < k < \frac{34,359,738,368}{153,587,983,935} \approx 0.2237.$

Since $k$ must be a positive integer, there is no integer $k$ satisfying this inequality. Therefore no $a$ with $1 < a < m$ exists that meets condition (ii).

Consequently, there are **no multipliers $a$** that satisfy the conditions of Theorem A when $m = 2^{35} + 1$.

$\boxed{\text{no such multipliers exist}}$

This completes the proof.

∎
