## Corrected Solution

The goal is to design a single-word floating-point representation in which the exponent range increases as its magnitude increases, while the precision of the fraction decreases correspondingly. The key challenge is to define a **well-formed, uniquely decodable representation** that does not suffer from circular dependencies.

### 1. Tapered Floating-Point Concept

Let a machine word have $N$ base-$\beta$ digits. A nonzero real number is represented in the form

$$
x = f \cdot \beta^e,
$$

where $f$ is a normalized fraction and $e$ is an integer exponent. The normalization condition is

$$
\frac{1}{\beta} \le |f| < 1.
$$

To allow the exponent range to grow while decreasing fraction precision, we introduce the notion of a **tapered representation**: the word is partitioned into exponent and fraction fields, but the exponent field can vary in length according to its value, while remaining **uniquely decodable**.

### 2. Prefix Encoding for the Exponent

To eliminate the circular dependency in field length, we adopt a **prefix code** for the exponent length. Let the exponent field be composed of two parts:

1. **Length prefix**: a unary code indicating the number of exponent digits. Specifically, if the exponent is represented in $k$ base-$\beta$ digits, we store $k-1$ consecutive ones followed by a zero. This prefix is always uniquely decodable, because a sequence of ones terminated by a zero can only be parsed in one way. Let the prefix length be $k$ digits.
2. **Exponent magnitude and sign**: the remaining $k$ digits encode $|e|$ in positional base-$\beta$ notation, and a separate bit encodes the sign of $e$.

The total exponent field length is thus $2k$ digits (or slightly more if the sign is stored in one of the digits). The fraction field consists of the remaining $N - (2k)$ digits. This design ensures that the **start and end of the exponent field are uniquely identifiable**.

### 3. Fraction Precision

Let the exponent magnitude require $k$ base-$\beta$ digits. Then the remaining digits $N - (k + k_{\text{prefix}})$ are assigned to the fraction:

$$
p(e) = N - (k_{\text{prefix}} + k)
$$

where $k_{\text{prefix}} = k$ in the unary scheme above. Hence, as $|e|$ increases, $k$ increases, and $p(e)$ decreases.

The fraction is rounded to $p(e)$ digits. Normalization proceeds as usual, scaling $f$ to satisfy

$$
\frac{1}{\beta} \le |f| < 1.
$$

### 4. Maximum Representable Exponent

Let $N$ be the total word length. The largest exponent occurs when the fraction is reduced to at least one digit:

$$
p_{\min} = 1 \implies k_{\max} + k_{\text{prefix,max}} = N - 1.
$$

Since $k_{\text{prefix,max}} = k_{\max}$ in our unary prefix scheme, we have

$$
2 k_{\max} = N - 1 \implies k_{\max} = \left\lfloor \frac{N-1}{2} \right\rfloor.
$$

Hence the maximum representable exponent magnitude is

$$
|e_{\max}| = \beta^{k_{\max}} - 1.
$$

This demonstrates that the exponent range grows exponentially in $N$, whereas a fixed-length exponent field would only allow linear growth in the number of digits.

### 5. Encoding and Decoding

1. **Encoding a nonzero number $x = f \cdot \beta^e$**:

- Determine $k$ such that $|e| < \beta^k$.
- Encode $k-1$ ones followed by a zero as the prefix.
- Encode $|e|$ in $k$ base-$\beta$ digits.
- Encode the sign of $e$ in a single bit.
- Encode the normalized fraction $f$ rounded to $p(e) = N - (2k)$ digits.
2. **Decoding**:

- Read consecutive ones until the first zero to recover $k$.
- Read the next $k$ digits as $|e|$.
- Read the sign bit to recover the full exponent.
- Read the remaining $N - (2k)$ digits as the fraction $f$.

The prefix code ensures that **no circular dependency occurs**, and the representation is **unique and fully decodable**.

### 6. Arithmetic Operations

Addition and subtraction proceed similarly to standard floating-point arithmetic:

1. Align the exponents by shifting the fraction of the operand with smaller exponent.
2. If the shift exceeds the available fraction digits $p(e)$, the contribution of that operand is zero.
3. Perform the addition or subtraction on the available fraction digits.
4. Normalize the result and encode it using the prefix-based tapered format.

Multiplication and division simply add or subtract exponents and multiply or divide fractions. After the operation, normalization restores the invariant fraction range and updates the exponent field according to the prefix scheme.

### 7. Verification of Correctness

1. **Unique Decodability**: The unary prefix ensures that the length of the exponent field is always determinable from the word itself.
2. **Exponent Range**: The maximum exponent is determined by the available digits for the exponent and prefix. This guarantees a large representable range that grows exponentially with $N$.
3. **Fraction Precision**: The remaining digits are always allocated to the fraction, so the precision is explicitly determined by the exponent magnitude.
4. **Normalization**: The scaling operation always terminates since $f$ is adjusted in base $\beta$ and a finite number of digits remain.
5. **Arithmetic Consistency**: Shifts and rounding are well-defined because the number of fraction digits is known at all times.

### 8. Conclusion

A single-word tapered floating-point system can be implemented by using a **prefix-encoded variable-length exponent**. This scheme avoids circular dependencies, is uniquely decodable, and ensures that larger exponents are representable at the cost of reduced fraction precision. The system satisfies all the requirements of the exercise: extended exponent range, tapered fraction precision, normalization, and consistent arithmetic.
