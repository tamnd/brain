**Exercise 8.** [*20*] Give examples of normalized eight-digit floating decimal numbers $u$ and $v$ for which addition yields (a) exponent underflow, (b) exponent overflow, assuming that exponent values satisfy $0 \le e \le E$.

## Solution

We consider floating point arithmetic in the context of base-$b$ digits with normalized representation, following Section 4.2.1. Let $u = (e_u, f_u)$ and $v = (e_v, f_v)$ be normalized eight-digit decimal floating point numbers, so that $f_u, f_v \in [0.1, 1)$ and $e_u, e_v \in [0, E]$ for some maximum exponent $E$. The sum $w = u \oplus v$ is formed using Algorithm A, and normalization is performed by Algorithm N.

**(a) Exponent underflow.** An underflow occurs when the result of addition yields a raw exponent $e_w < 0$ after normalization. Since the inputs are normalized, underflow can happen only if $f_u$ and $f_v$ are extremely small and have opposite signs (so that cancellation reduces the fraction to near zero), or if both are extremely small positive numbers and $e_u = e_v = 0$, but $f_u + f_v$ still requires left shifting beyond $e = 0$.

We construct an example. Let $b = 10$, $p = 8$, $E \ge 0$, and choose

$u = 0.10000000 \times 10^0, \quad v = -0.10000000 \times 10^0.$

Step A2 compares exponents and finds $e_u = e_v = 0$, so no interchange occurs. Step A5 aligns fractions, but since $e_u - e_v = 0$, $f_v$ is unshifted. Step A6 adds fractions:

$f_w = f_u + f_v = 0.10000000 + (-0.10000000) = 0.$

Step A7 invokes normalization (Algorithm N). Step N1 detects $f_w = 0$ and sets $e_w$ to its lowest possible value. In MIX, the lowest exponent is $0$. If we now attempt to normalize a slightly smaller number, for example

$u = 0.10000000 \times 10^0, \quad v = -0.09999999 \times 10^0,$

then $f_w = 0.00000001$, which requires 8 left shifts to reach $f_w \ge 0.1$. Each left shift decrements $e_w$ by 1, giving

$e_w = 0 - 8 = -8 < 0.$

Algorithm N detects $e_w < 0$ and signals exponent underflow. Hence a concrete example is

$\boxed{u = 0.10000000 \times 10^0, \quad v = -0.09999999 \times 10^0}.$

**(b) Exponent overflow.** Overflow occurs when the raw fraction after addition satisfies $|f_w| \ge 1$ and normalization shifts it right so that $e_w > E$. To produce this, choose numbers with the maximum exponent $E$ and large fractions close to 1:

$u = 0.99999999 \times 10^E, \quad v = 0.99999999 \times 10^E.$

Algorithm A adds fractions after aligning exponents (here $e_u = e_v = E$), giving

$f_w = f_u + f_v = 0.99999999 + 0.99999999 = 1.99999998.$

Step N4 of Algorithm N detects $|f_w| \ge 1$ and shifts right by one digit, dividing $f_w$ by $10$, giving

$f_w \approx 0.199999998,$

and increments $e_w$ by 1:

$e_w = E + 1.$

Since $e_w > E$, Algorithm N signals exponent overflow. Hence a concrete example is

$\boxed{u = 0.99999999 \times 10^E, \quad v = 0.99999999 \times 10^E}.$

These constructions explicitly demonstrate that Algorithm A together with Algorithm N produces exponent underflow and overflow when the resulting fraction requires normalization beyond the allowed exponent range. This completes the solution. ∎
