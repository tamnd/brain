**Exercise 2.** [*12*] Assume that the exponent $e$ is constrained to lie in the range $0 \le e \le E$; what are the largest and smallest positive values that can be written as base $b$, excess $q$, $p$-digit floating point numbers? What are the largest and smallest positive values that can be written as *normalized* floating point numbers with these specifications?

## Solution

We are asked to determine the largest and smallest positive values representable in a base-$b$, excess-$q$, $p$-digit floating point system, both in general and under the _normalized_ constraint.

Let a floating point number be represented as $(e, f)$, where $e$ is the exponent and $f$ is the fraction. The value of the number is

$x = f \cdot b^{\,e - q}.$

Here, $f$ is an integer satisfying $0 \le f < b^p$ in general, and $0 \le e \le E$ by assumption. Normalized fractions satisfy $f \ge b^{,p-1}$ for nonzero values.

**Step 1. Largest positive number (general)**

To maximize $x = f \cdot b^{,e-q}$, we must take $f$ and $e$ as large as possible. By the definitions:

- Maximum fraction: $f_{\max} = b^p - 1$, since $f < b^p$.
- Maximum exponent: $e_{\max} = E$.

Thus the largest positive number is

$$
x_{\max} = (b^p - 1) \cdot b^{E - q}.
$$

**Step 2. Smallest positive number (general)**

To minimize $x = f \cdot b^{,e-q}$ among positive numbers, we must take $f$ and $e$ as small as possible, with $f \neq 0$. The smallest positive fraction is

$$
f_{\min} = 1
$$

since $f$ is an integer in $[0, b^p - 1]$. The smallest exponent is $e_{\min} = 0$.

Hence the smallest positive number is

$$
x_{\min} = 1 \cdot b^{0 - q} = b^{-q}.
$$

**Step 3. Largest normalized number**

Normalized numbers satisfy $f \ge b^{,p-1}$ (or $f = 0$ with minimal $e$, which corresponds to zero). To maximize $x$, we choose the largest fraction $f = b^p - 1$ and the largest exponent $e = E$. Therefore, the largest normalized positive number coincides with the largest general positive number:

$$
x_{\max}^{\text{norm}} = (b^p - 1) \cdot b^{E - q}.
$$

**Step 4. Smallest normalized number**

For the smallest normalized positive number, the fraction must satisfy $f \ge b^{,p-1}$. To minimize $x$, we take the smallest allowed fraction, $f = b^{,p-1}$, and the smallest exponent $e = 0$. Thus

$$
x_{\min}^{\text{norm}} = b^{\,p-1} \cdot b^{0 - q} = b^{\,p-1-q}.
$$

This fraction is the minimal normalized value; any smaller $f$ would violate the normalization condition.

**Step 5. Summary**

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{Largest positive number: } & x_{\max} = (b^p - 1) \, b^{E - q}, \\
\text{Smallest positive number: } & x_{\min} = b^{-q}, \\
\text{Largest normalized positive number: } & x_{\max}^{\text{norm}} = (b^p - 1) \, b^{E - q}, \\
\text{Smallest normalized positive number: } & x_{\min}^{\text{norm}} = b^{\,p-1 - q}.
\end{aligned}
$$

All bounds are tight. The largest numbers are obtained by setting $f$ and $e$ to their maxima, and the smallest numbers are obtained by setting $f$ and $e$ to their minima within the allowed ranges. The normalized lower bound follows directly from the definition of normalized fractions.

This completes the proof.

∎

}
