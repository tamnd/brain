**Exercise 11.** &#9654; [*M20*] Give an example of normalized, excess 50, eight-digit floating decimal numbers $u$ and $v$ for which rounding overflow occurs in multiplication.

## Solution

We are asked to exhibit normalized, eight-digit floating decimal numbers $u$ and $v$, with excess 50, such that multiplication of $u$ and $v$ results in _rounding overflow_. By the definitions in Section 4.2.1, rounding overflow occurs when the fraction of the raw product exceeds $1$ after rounding to $p$ digits, necessitating a normalization step that shifts the fraction right and increments the exponent by 1.

Let the base be $b = 10$ and the precision $p = 8$ digits. Denote normalized numbers in excess-50 representation: an exponent $e$ is stored as $E = e + 50$. A normalized number has fraction $f \in [0.1, 1)$; the most significant digit of $f$ is nonzero.

Consider numbers $u = (e_u, f_u)$ and $v = (e_v, f_v)$ with fraction parts

$f_u = 0.99999999, \quad f_v = 0.99999999.$

These are the largest possible normalized eight-digit fractions in base 10. Let the stored exponents be $e_u = e_v = 50$ (so that the excess-50 stored values are $E_u = E_v = 100$). Multiplication yields

$$
u \otimes v = (e_u + e_v, f_u \cdot f_v) = (100, 0.99999999 \cdot 0.99999999) = (100, 0.9999999800000001).
$$

Rounding this product to $p = 8$ digits gives

$$
f_w = 1.0000000,
$$

which exceeds the allowed range $[0.1,1)$ for a normalized fraction. By Algorithm N, step N4 is invoked: the fraction is shifted right by one digit (divide by $b$) and the exponent is incremented:

$$
f_w \leftarrow 0.10000000, \quad e_w \leftarrow e_u + e_v + 1 = 101.
$$

Thus, the product undergoes _rounding overflow_, since the rounding step increased the fraction beyond $1$. The numbers $u$ and $v$ are normalized, stored in excess 50 representation, and the multiplication demonstrates the phenomenon requested.

The final normalized result in excess-50 representation is

$$
e_w + 50 = 101 + 50 = 151, \quad f_w = 0.10000000.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎

## Notes

Choosing $f_u = f_v$ slightly smaller, such as $0.99999998$, would avoid rounding overflow. Therefore, this construction maximizes the fraction to ensure rounding overflow occurs. The argument depends only on the largest possible $p$-digit fractions; the exponents may be any values that keep $e_w + 1$ within the machine range.
