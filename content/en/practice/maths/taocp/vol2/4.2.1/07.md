## Exercise 4.2.1.7 [_27_]

**Problem.** Discuss floating point arithmetic using balanced ternary notation.

## Solution

### 1. Balanced ternary setup

Balanced ternary uses radix $b = 3$ with digit set

$$
d \in \{-1,0,+1\}.
$$

A normalized floating point fraction with $p$ digits has the form

$$
f = \pm (.d_1 d_2 \cdots d_p)_3,
$$

where $d_1 \neq 0$ unless $f=0$. Since the leading digit is $\pm 1$, every nonzero normalized fraction satisfies

$$
\frac{1}{3} \le |f| < 1.
$$

A floating point number is represented as

$$
x = f \cdot 3^e,
$$

where $e$ is an integer exponent. The standard floating point operations of Section 4.2.1 apply with modifications for balanced ternary digits.

### 2. Addition and balanced carries

Let $u = f_u \cdot 3^{e_u}$ and $v = f_v \cdot 3^{e_v}$. Without loss of generality, assume $e_u \ge e_v$. To add $u+v$, align exponents:

$$
f_w = f_u + f_v \cdot 3^{e_v - e_u}.
$$

Digit-wise addition produces sums in $\{-2,-1,0,1,2\}$. To maintain balanced digits, we apply the correct balanced ternary carry rule:

$$
2 \mapsto -1 \text{ with carry } +1, \qquad
-2 \mapsto +1 \text{ with carry } -1.
$$

This follows because $2 = (-1) + 1\cdot 3$ and $-2 = (+1) - 1\cdot 3$. Carry propagation proceeds from right to left as in ordinary radix addition.

**Example of addition.** Let

$$
f_u = (.1\overline{1}0)_3 = \frac{1}{3} - \frac{1}{9} + 0 = \frac{2}{9}, \quad
f_v = (.11)_3 = \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{9} = \frac{4}{9}.
$$

Compute $f_w = f_u + f_v$. Align digits:

$$
\begin{array}{cccc}
& 1 & \overline{1} & 0 \\
+ & 1 & 1 & 0 \\
\hline
? & ? & ? & ?
\end{array}
$$

Step by step:

1. Rightmost digit: $0 + 1 = 1$ (no carry).
2. Middle digit: $\overline{1} + 1 = 0$ (no carry).
3. Leftmost digit: $1 + 1 = 2$. Apply balanced carry: $2 \mapsto -1$ with carry $+1$ to the next higher position. If there is no higher digit, prepend $+1$.

Thus the sum is:

$$
f_w = (1.-1 0 1)_3 \text{ normalized to } (.1 0 1)_3 \cdot 3^1.
$$

Check numerically:

$$
(.101)_3 = \frac{1}{3} + 0 - \frac{1}{27} = \frac{8}{27}.
$$

Adjust exponent to match $\frac{2}{3} = \frac{18}{27}$: multiply by 3:

$$
(.101)_3 \cdot 3^1 = 1.01_3 = \frac{18}{27} = \frac{2}{3}.
$$

The result is correctly normalized.

### 3. Negation and subtraction

Negation is obtained by digitwise inversion:

$$
-(.d_1 d_2 \cdots d_p)_3 = (. -d_1 -d_2 \cdots -d_p)_3.
$$

Subtraction is implemented as addition of the negated number:

$$
u - v = u + (-v).
$$

No separate sign bit is required because the digit set is symmetric.

### 4. Normalization

A nonzero fraction $f = .d_1 d_2 \cdots d_p$ is normalized if $|d_1| = 1$. If the leading digit is 0, multiply $f$ by 3 and decrement the exponent:

$$
f \leftarrow 3 f, \quad e \leftarrow e - 1,
$$

repeating until $|d_1| = 1$.

**Example.** Let $f = (.01\overline{1})_3 = \frac{1}{9} - \frac{1}{27} = \frac{2}{27}$. Multiply by 3:

$$
3f = (.1\overline{1})_3 = \frac{1}{3} - \frac{1}{9} = \frac{2}{9}.
$$

The fraction is now normalized.

The smallest positive normalized fraction with $p$ digits is

$$
f_{\min} = (.10\cdots0)_3 = \frac{1}{3},
$$

and the largest is

$$
f_{\max} = (.111\cdots1)_3 = 1 - 3^{-p}.
$$

### 5. Rounding

Let $r = .d_{p+1} d_{p+2} \cdots$ denote the discarded tail after computation. Round to the nearest representable fraction. If $r$ is exactly halfway between two consecutive fractions, a symmetric tie-breaking rule is necessary. One option is **round to nearest with the last retained digit chosen to minimize the magnitude**. That is, if the last retained digit is $\pm 1$ and $r$ is exactly half, reduce its magnitude toward zero. This rule preserves symmetry about zero.

Unlike radix-even rounding, which depends on an even radix to select the "even" digit in ties, in odd radix 3 we use magnitude-minimizing symmetry.

### 6. Range and digit utilization

The total number of normalized $p$-digit balanced ternary fractions is

$$
2 \cdot 3^{p-1},
$$

because the leading digit can be $\pm 1$ and the remaining $p-1$ digits each have 3 choices. This ensures a uniform distribution of positive and negative numbers. The exponent range still determines the overall floating point range; the digit set affects the distribution of representable fractions but not the absolute minimum or maximum magnitude achievable.

### 7. Summary

Balanced ternary floating point arithmetic:

1. Represents fractions using digits $\{-1,0,+1\}$, so negation is simple.
2. Normalized fractions satisfy $|f| \in [1/3,1)$.
3. Addition uses digit-wise sums with correct balanced carry:

$$
2 \mapsto -1, \text{ carry }+1;\quad -2 \mapsto +1, \text{ carry }-1.
$$

1. Subtraction is addition of the negated fraction.
2. Normalization shifts fractions until the leading digit is nonzero.
3. Rounding uses nearest representable fraction; exact half cases require a symmetric tie-breaking rule.
4. The total number of normalized $p$-digit fractions is $2\cdot 3^{p-1}$, with uniform coverage of positive and negative values.

All standard floating point algorithms apply with these modifications to balanced ternary digits and rounding rules.
