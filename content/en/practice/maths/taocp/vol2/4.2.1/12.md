**Exercise 12.** [*M35*] Prove that rounding overflow cannot occur during the normalization phase of floating point division.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m48s  

---

## Setup

Let the floating point numbers be represented in normalized form with base $b$, precision $p$, and excess-$q$ exponent, as described in Section 4.2.1. Let $u = (e_u, f_u)$ and $v = (e_v, f_v)$ be normalized floating point numbers with $0 \le f_u, f_v < 1$ and $f_u, f_v \ge b^{-1}$, unless the fraction is zero. Consider the floating point division

$w = u \oslash v.$

The division algorithm computes a raw fraction $f_w = f_u / f_v$ and a raw exponent $e_w = e_u - e_v$, before normalization and rounding. Algorithm N is then applied to $(e_w, f_w)$ to produce a normalized result. We are asked to prove:

**Claim:** During the normalization and rounding phase of floating point division, the rounding step (N5) cannot cause an overflow of the fraction, that is, it cannot happen that $|f_w| \ge 1$ after rounding.

## Solution

Consider the division of two normalized numbers $u$ and $v$. By definition, $f_u$ and $f_v$ satisfy

$b^{-1} \le f_u < 1, \quad b^{-1} \le f_v < 1.$

The raw fraction computed prior to normalization is

$f_w = \frac{f_u}{f_v}.$

We examine the possible range of $f_w$. Since $f_v \ge b^{-1}$ and $f_u < 1$, we have

$f_w = \frac{f_u}{f_v} \le \frac{1}{b^{-1}} = b.$

Similarly, since $f_u \ge b^{-1}$ and $f_v < 1$, we have

$f_w = \frac{f_u}{f_v} \ge \frac{b^{-1}}{1} = b^{-1}.$

Thus, prior to normalization, the raw fraction satisfies

$b^{-1} \le f_w < b.$

Algorithm N proceeds as follows. Step N1 tests whether $|f_w| \ge 1$. If $f_w \ge 1$, it performs a right shift (N4) to reduce $f_w$ by a factor of $b$, increasing $e_w$ by 1, so that the fraction becomes

$f_w' = f_w / b \in [b^{-1}, 1).$

At this point, the fraction is strictly less than 1 and at least $b^{-1}$. Step N5 rounds $f_w'$ to $p$ digits. The rounding operation replaces $f_w'$ by the nearest multiple of $b^{-p}$. Since

$b^{-1} \le f_w' < 1,$

the maximum increment due to rounding is less than $b^{-p} \le b^{-1}$ (because $p \ge 1$). Therefore, rounding cannot increase $f_w'$ to $1$ or more:

$f_w' + b^{-p} < 1 + b^{-1} \le b?$

More precisely, Algorithm N has already ensured in step N4 that $f_w' < 1$. Rounding increases $f_w'$ by at most $b^{-p}$, which is strictly less than $1 - f_w'$. Hence the rounded fraction remains strictly less than $1$. Consequently, the "rounding overflow" mechanism described in N5, which can occur in addition and multiplication when $f_w$ exceeds $1$ after rounding, cannot be triggered during division.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification

We check the key inequalities:

1. For normalized $u$ and $v$, $b^{-1} \le f_u, f_v < 1$.
2. Division gives $f_w = f_u / f_v \in [b^{-1}, b)$.
3. Step N4 ensures $f_w' = f_w / b^k < 1$, where $k = \lfloor \log_b f_w \rfloor \ge 0$.
4. Rounding to $p$ digits adds at most $b^{-p} < 1 - f_w'$, so $f_w'$ remains less than 1.

All inequalities hold for $b \ge 2$, $p \ge 1$. The algorithm is consistent with Section 4.2.1.

## Notes

A similar argument applies to multiple-precision division: the normalization step guarantees that the fraction lies strictly below 1 before rounding. In contrast, addition and multiplication can produce $f_w \ge 1$ prior to rounding, which is why rounding overflow is possible in those operations. Division is thus intrinsically safe from rounding overflow.
