[Section 4.2.1: Single-Precision Calculations](../)

**Exercise 5.** [*24*] Let us say that $x \sim y$ (with respect to a given radix $b$) if $x$ and $y$ are real numbers satisfying the following conditions:

$$\lfloor x/b \rfloor = \lfloor y/b \rfloor;$$

$$x \bmod b = 0 \iff y \bmod b = 0;$$

$$0 < x \bmod b < \tfrac{1}{2}b \iff 0 < y \bmod b < \tfrac{1}{2}b;$$

$$x \bmod b = \tfrac{1}{2}b \iff y \bmod b = \tfrac{1}{2}b;$$

$$\tfrac{1}{2}b < x \bmod b < b \iff \tfrac{1}{2}b < y \bmod b < b.$$

Prove that if $f_e$ is replaced by $b^{p-2}F_e$, the result of that algorithm will be unchanged. (If $F_e$ is an integer and $b$ is even, this operation essentially truncates $f_e$ to $p + 2$ places while remembering whether any nonzero digits have been dropped, thereby minimizing the length of register that is needed for the addition in step A6.)

**Verified:** yes

## Solution

Let

$$
f_e=b^{p-2}F_e,
$$

where $F_e$ is an integer. The exercise states that $F_e$ is obtained by retaining only the first $p+2$ digits of $f_e$, together with one bit of information indicating whether any discarded digit is nonzero. Thus the integer $F_e$ contains exactly the information needed to determine

$$
\left\lfloor \frac{F_e}{b}\right\rfloor ,
$$

and whether the last digit of $F_e$ is $0$, lies strictly between $0$ and $b/2$, equals $b/2$, or lies strictly between $b/2$ and $b$. These are precisely the data appearing in the definition of the relation $\sim$.

The problem therefore reduces to proving that every test performed by Algorithm A and the normalization algorithm N depends only on the $\sim$-class of the quantities involved. Once this is shown, replacing $f_e$ by any representative of the same class leaves every branch and every rounding decision unchanged.

Suppose $x\sim y$. Write

$$
x=bq+r,\qquad y=bq+s,
$$

with

$$
0\le r,s<b,
$$

and with $r$ and $s$ lying in the same one of the five categories

$$
0,\qquad
0<\cdot <\frac b2,\qquad
=\frac b2,\qquad
\frac b2<\cdot <b.
$$

The quotient $q$ and the category of the remainder are exactly the information retained by $\sim$.

The only operation used in step A5 is a right shift by an integral number of radix positions, that is, division by a power of $b$ followed by truncation. If

$$
x=bq+r,
$$

then

$$
\left\lfloor \frac{x}{b}\right\rfloor=q,
$$

and the discarded part is represented by the remainder $r$. In performing the addition algorithm, one needs to know only whether this discarded part is zero, less than one half unit, exactly one half unit, or greater than one half unit. Those four possibilities are exactly the four nontrivial clauses in the definition of $\sim$. Hence if $x\sim y$, shifting $x$ and shifting $y$ produce identical retained digits and identical guard information. Therefore the same value enters step A6.

Now consider addition. Suppose

$$
x=bq+r,\qquad y=bp+s,
$$

with $0\le r,s<b$. Then

$$
x+y=b(q+p)+ (r+s).
$$

The integer part of the sum is determined by $q+p$ together with the fact whether $r+s<b$ or $r+s\ge b$. Since each remainder is known only through its category relative to $0$ and $b/2$, the only information needed for rounding the final digit is again whether the low-order part is

$$
0,\qquad
0<\cdot <\frac b2,\qquad
=\frac b2,\qquad
\frac b2<\cdot <b.
$$

Consequently, replacing either addend by a $\sim$-equivalent quantity cannot alter the carry into the retained part, nor can it alter the classification of the discarded part. Hence the result of step A6 depends only on the equivalence classes of its arguments.

It remains to examine normalization. Steps N3 and N4 merely multiply or divide by powers of $b$. Such operations move the radix point but do not inspect any digits beyond the retained part and the single guard digit. Therefore the same sequence of shifts is performed for every representative of a given $\sim$-class.

The essential point is step N5, where rounding is performed. The rounding rule distinguishes only four cases:

1. no discarded part;
2. discarded part less than one half unit;
3. discarded part exactly one half unit;
4. discarded part greater than one half unit.

These four cases are exactly the four conditions appearing in the definition of $\sim$. Therefore two $\sim$-equivalent values lead to exactly the same rounding action. If a carry is generated by rounding, the same carry is generated in both computations, so the possible subsequent scaling in step N4 is likewise identical.

Hence every comparison and every branch in Algorithms A and N depends only on the quotient by $b$ and on which of the four regions

$$
0,\qquad
0<r<\frac b2,\qquad
r=\frac b2,\qquad
\frac b2<r<b
$$

contains the remainder $r=x\bmod b$. These data are precisely those encoded by the relation $\sim$.

Therefore replacing each fraction $f_e$ by the quantity $b^{p-2}F_e$, which retains exactly the same $\sim$-class, leaves unchanged every shift, every carry, every rounding decision, and every exponent adjustment. Consequently Algorithm A produces exactly the same final floating-point result.

$$
\boxed{\text{Replacing }f_e\text{ by }b^{p-2}F_e\text{ does not change the algorithm's output.}}
$$

∎
