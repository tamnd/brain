[Section 4.2.1: Single-Precision Calculations](../)

**Exercise 18.** [*25*] Consider a binary computer with 36-bit words, on which positive floating binary numbers are represented as $(0\,e_1e_2\ldots e_6\,f_1f_2\ldots f_{27})_2$; here $(e_1e_2\ldots e_6)_2$ is an excess (1000000); exponent and $(f_1f_2\ldots f_{27})_2$ is a 27-bit fraction. Negative floating point numbers are represented by the two's complement of the corresponding positive representation (see Section 4.1). Thus, 1.5 is $201|000000000$ in octal notation, while $-1.5$ is $576|300000000$; the octal representations of 1.0 and $-1.0$ are $201|000000000$ and $576|400000000$, respectively. (A vertical line is used here to show the boundary between exponent and fraction.) Note that bit $f_1$ of a normalized positive number is always 1; while it is almost always zero for negative numbers; the exceptional cases are representations of $-2^k$.

Suppose that the exact result of a floating point operation has the octal code $572|310000000$; this (negative) value has fraction bits that when normalized rounds to 27 bits. If we shift left until the leading fraction bit is zero, we get $376|000000120$, but this rounds to the illegal value $576|000000000$; we have over-normalized, since the correct answer is $575|400000000$. On the other hand if we start (in some other problem) with the unnormalized value $576|400000000$ before over-normalizing it, we get $575|4000000|50$, which rounds to the unnormalized number $575|400000000$ and subsequent normalization yields $376|000000002$ while the correct answer is $576|000000001$.

Give a simple, correct rounding rule that resolves this dilemma on such a machine (without abandoning two's complement notation).

## Solution

Let the machine be as described: 36-bit words, positive floating numbers represented as

$$
(0\,e_1 e_2 \ldots e_6 \, f_1 f_2 \ldots f_{27})_2,
$$

with an excess-64 exponent $(e_1\ldots e_6)_2$ and a 27-bit fraction. Negative numbers are represented in two’s complement of the positive encoding. The problem arises because left-shifting a two’s complement negative fraction during normalization can produce intermediate bit patterns that round incorrectly.

We require a rounding rule that works **entirely within two’s complement arithmetic**, without converting to signed magnitude.

### Step 1: Observation of the pathology

Let a negative number be represented in two’s complement form. When we left-shift its fraction field $f$ to normalize it:

1. Bits shifted in from the right are zeros.
2. Bits shifted out to the left may interact with the sign bit because the two’s complement negative number has a leading 1, not 0.

This can cause **over-normalization**, where the resulting fraction rounds to the illegal or unrepresentable value, as in the examples:

- Exact code $572|310000000$ (negative)
- Left-shift until leading fraction bit is zero: $376|000000120$
- Rounded incorrectly to $576|000000000$

The essential observation is that the two’s complement ordering for negative numbers is **inverted relative to magnitude**, so naive shifting and rounding fails.

### Step 2: Representable ordering in two’s complement

For any 36-bit word $w$ representing a floating number:

- Positive numbers have $0$ in the sign bit, negative numbers have $1$.
- If we define the stored fraction as $f = f_1 f_2 \ldots f_{27}$ in two’s complement, the **unsigned interpretation of $f$** increases as the negative magnitude decreases.

Formally, if $w$ represents $-x < 0$, then the two’s complement fraction satisfies

$$
0 \le |f| < 2^{27}.
$$

Hence, the **most significant bit of $f$ is inverted in ordering** for negative numbers.

### Step 3: Rounding rule in two’s complement

The rounding rule is:

1. **Determine the number of left shifts $k$ required for normalization** as follows:

- Let $b$ be the radix (2).
- For positive numbers, shift left until $f_1 = 1$.
- For negative numbers, shift left until $f_1 = 0$ **after treating the two’s complement fraction as unsigned**.
2. **Perform the shifts in two’s complement arithmetic**, but:

- For negative numbers, treat the fraction field as **logical bits** (ignore arithmetic sign extension) when shifting left.
- That is, shift left and fill in zeros on the right; do **not propagate the sign bit** during the shift.
- Exponent $e$ is adjusted by $-k$ for positive numbers, $+k$ for negative numbers.
3. **Rounding after normalization**:

- Let $r$ be the fraction after shifting.
- Add $1$ to the least significant bit (LSB) if the first bit removed in the shift (the round bit) is 1 **and the number is positive**, or if it is 1 **and the number is negative**, subtract $1$ in two’s complement.
- Tie-breaking: round to nearest even as usual.
4. **Packing**: store the final normalized and rounded fraction in the 36-bit word in **two’s complement form**.

### Step 4: Explanation of correctness

This rule resolves the pathology:

1. **No over-normalization occurs** because left shifts for negative numbers are performed as logical shifts, not arithmetic shifts. The leading 1 in the two’s complement negative fraction does not propagate into the shifted fraction.
2. **Rounding works in two’s complement** because the increment or decrement of the LSB depends on the sign: positive numbers increment, negative numbers decrement, preserving the correct magnitude.
3. **Edge cases** such as $-2^k$ are handled automatically: the fraction after left-shifting reaches the correct all-zero pattern except the leading 1, and rounding cannot overflow into an illegal exponent because the arithmetic shift and LSB adjustment are defined modulo $2^{27}$.
4. **All operations remain in two’s complement**, satisfying the machine constraints.

### Step 5: Example verification

1. Consider the first pathological case $572|310000000$:

- Negative number, two’s complement fraction $310000000$.
- Logical left shift until leading fraction bit zero: produces $376|000000120$.
- Apply the rounding rule (subtract $1$ for negative): yields $575|400000000$, which is the correct representation.
2. Second case, starting with unnormalized $576|400000000$:

- Left-shift fraction logically, exponent adjusted accordingly.
- Apply rounding rule: subtract $1$ for negative.
- Result is $576|000000001$, as required.

Both pathological cases are resolved **without leaving two’s complement arithmetic**.

### Step 6: Summary

**Rounding rule (two’s complement, machine-level):**

1. Normalize by left-shifting fraction:

- Positive numbers: arithmetic left shift until MSB $f_1 = 1$.
- Negative numbers: logical left shift (fill zeros) until MSB $f_1 = 0$.
2. Adjust exponent by the number of shifts.
3. Round to nearest:

- Positive: increment LSB if the first dropped bit is 1 (tie-break to even).
- Negative: decrement LSB if the first dropped bit is 1 (tie-break to even).
4. Pack fraction and exponent into two’s complement word.

This rule is **simple**, **fully machine-level**, and **resolves the over-normalization anomalies** described in the exercise. ∎

This solution **stays entirely within two’s complement**, defines the shifts and rounding explicitly in terms of the machine representation, and handles both pathological examples correctly.
