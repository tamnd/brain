**Solution to Exercise 4.6.3.12** $[M10]$

The answer is **yes**.

Let $l(n)$ denote the length of a shortest addition chain for $n$. We ask whether there exists an infinite rooted tree such that, for every positive integer $n$, the unique path from the root to the node labeled $n$ is a shortest addition chain for $n$.

The question is one of compatibility among shortest chains. A priori, two integers $m$ and $n$ might require shortest chains whose initial portions are incompatible, in which case no single tree could realize both simultaneously. We show that such incompatibilities can always be avoided.

Consider the directed graph $G$ whose vertices are the positive integers. For each $n>1$, join $n$ to every integer $m<n$ for which there exists a shortest addition chain for $n$ whose penultimate element is $m$. Equivalently,

$$
1=a_0<a_1<\cdots<a_{k-1}=m<a_k=n,
$$

with $k=l(n)$, and the chain above is shortest for $n$. Then every edge $m\to n$ satisfies

$$
l(n)=l(m)+1.
$$

The graph $G$ is infinite and acyclic, because all edges go from smaller to larger integers.

We now construct a spanning tree $T$ of $G$. Begin with the root $1$. For each $n>1$, choose one predecessor $p(n)$ among the vertices $m$ such that $m\to n$ is an edge of $G$. Since every $n>1$ possesses at least one shortest addition chain, at least one such predecessor exists. Include in $T$ the edge

$$
p(n)\to n .
$$

Because every vertex $n>1$ receives exactly one incoming edge and all edges point from smaller to larger integers, the resulting graph $T$ is a rooted tree with root $1$.

It remains to verify that the path from $1$ to $n$ in $T$ is a shortest addition chain for $n$.

Proceed by induction on $n$. For $n=1$, the statement is trivial. Assume it holds for all integers smaller than $n$. Let $p(n)=m$. By construction of $G$,

$$
l(n)=l(m)+1,
$$

and there exists a shortest chain for $n$ whose penultimate term is $m$. By the induction hypothesis, the path from $1$ to $m$ in $T$ has length $l(m)$ and is a shortest chain for $m$. Appending the edge $m\to n$ gives a chain for $n$ of length

$$
l(m)+1=l(n).
$$

Hence the path from $1$ to $n$ in $T$ is a shortest addition chain for $n$.

Therefore every positive integer occurs in a single infinite rooted tree in such a way that its root-to-node path is a minimum-multiplication method for computing $x^n$.

Consequently, the tree of Fig. 15 can indeed be extended to an infinite tree having this property.

$\square$
