## Solution

Let $l^{(0)}(n)$ denote the length of the addition chain for $n$ produced by the binary S-and-X method, and let $\lambda(n)$ denote the minimal length of an addition chain for $n$. Recall that the binary S-and-X method satisfies

$$
l^{(0)}(n)=\lfloor \log_2 n \rfloor + \nu(n),
$$

where $\nu(n)$ is the number of ones in the binary expansion of $n$ (see §4.6.3).

The exercise asks us to show that

$$
\frac{l^{(0)}(n)}{\lambda(n)}
$$

does not tend to a limit as $n\to\infty$. To do this, it suffices to produce two sequences along which the ratio tends to different limits.

### Sequence 1: powers of two

Consider the sequence

$$
n_k = 2^k.
$$

Then $\nu(n_k)=1$ and $\lfloor \log_2 n_k\rfloor = k$, so the binary S-and-X method gives

$$
l^{(0)}(2^k) = k + 1.
$$

On the other hand, a minimal addition chain for $2^k$ is

$$
1,2,4,\dots,2^k,
$$

which has length exactly $k$. Hence

$$
\lambda(2^k) = k,
$$

and therefore

$$
\frac{l^{(0)}(2^k)}{\lambda(2^k)} = \frac{k+1}{k} \longrightarrow 1 \quad \text{as } k \to \infty.
$$

This establishes the first subsequential limit.

### Sequence 2: Mersenne-type numbers

Now consider the sequence of numbers

$$
m_k = 2^{k+1}-1.
$$

These numbers have $k+1$ ones in binary, so

$$
\nu(m_k) = k+1, \qquad \lfloor \log_2 m_k \rfloor = k.
$$

Hence the S-and-X method gives

$$
l^{(0)}(m_k) = k + (k+1) = 2k+1.
$$

We need a lower bound on $\lambda(m_k)$ to show that the ratio $l^{(0)}(m_k)/\lambda(m_k)$ can differ from the previous subsequence. Observe that $m_k$ is of the form $2^{k+1}-1$, which is known to admit a minimal addition chain of length exactly $k$ (see §4.6.3, Exercise 4.6.3.3). To see this, consider the doubling-and-adding strategy: start with 1, repeatedly double to obtain $1,2,4,\dots,2^k$, then successively add previous terms to form $2^1-1, 2^2-1, \dots, 2^{k+1}-1$. Each new term requires one addition beyond the previous chain, giving

$$
\lambda(2^{k+1}-1) = k.
$$

Thus for the sequence $m_k$ we have

$$
\frac{l^{(0)}(m_k)}{\lambda(m_k)} = \frac{2k+1}{k} \longrightarrow 2 \quad \text{as } k \to \infty.
$$

### Conclusion

We have constructed two sequences $(n_k)$ and $(m_k)$ such that

$$
\lim_{k\to\infty} \frac{l^{(0)}(n_k)}{\lambda(n_k)} = 1, \qquad
\lim_{k\to\infty} \frac{l^{(0)}(m_k)}{\lambda(m_k)} = 2.
$$
