[Section 4.6.3: Evaluation of Powers](../)

**Exercise 28.** [*HM33*] *[HM33]* (A. Schönhage.) The object of this exercise is to give a fairly short proof that $l(n) \ge \Lambda(n) + \lg \nu(n) - O(\log \log(\nu(n) + 1))$.

a) When $x = (x_1 \ldots x_0 \cdot x_{-1} \ldots)_2$ and $y = (y_k \ldots y_0 \cdot y_{-1} \ldots)_2$ are real numbers written in binary notation, let us write $x \le y$ if $x_j \le y_j$ for all $j$. Give a simple rule for constructing the smallest number $z$ with the property that $x' \le z$ and $y' \le y$ implies $x' + y' \le z$. Denoting this number by $x \nabla y$, prove that $v(x \nabla y) \le v(x) + v(y)$.

b) Given any addition chain $(1)$ with $r = \lambda(n)$, let the sequence $d_0, d_1, \ldots, d_r$ be defined as in (35), and define the sequence $A_0, A_1, \ldots, A_r$ by the following rules: $A_0 = 1$; if $a_i = 2a_{i-1}$, then $A_i = 2A_{i-1}$; otherwise if $a_i = a_j + a_k$ for some $0 \le k \le j < i$, then $A_i = A_j \cdot \nabla(A_j/2^{d_j - d_k})$. Prove that this sequence "covers" the given chain, in the sense that $a_i \le A_i$ for $0 \le i \le r$.

c) Let $\delta$ be a positive integer (to be selected later). Call the nondoubling step $a_i = a_j + a_k$ a "baby step" if $d_j - d_k \ge \delta$, otherwise call it a "close step." Let $B_0 = 1$; $B_i = 2B_{i-1}$ if $a_i = 2a_{i-1}$; $B_i = B_j \cdot \nabla(B_j/2^{\rho(j) - \delta})$ if $a_i = a_j + a_k$ is a baby step; and $B_i = \rho(2B_{i-1})$ otherwise, where $\rho(j)$ is the least number $y$ such that $j/2^y \le y$ for $0 \le j \le \delta \cdot 2^{\delta}$. Show that $\nu(B_i) \le (1 + 4c_i)2^{\delta}$ for $0 \le i \le r$, where $b_i$ and $c_i$ respectively denote the number of baby steps and close steps $\le i$. [*Hint:* Show that the $1$s in $B_i$ appear in consecutive blocks of size $\ge 1 + 4c_i$.]

d) We now have $l(n) = r = b_r + c_r + d_r$ and $\nu(n) \le \nu(B_r) \le (1 + 4c_r)2^{\delta}$. Explain how to choose $\delta$ in order to obtain the inequality stated at the beginning of this exercise. [*Hint:* See (16), and note that $n \le 2^{d_r} a^{b_r}$ for some $a < 1$ depending on $\delta$.]

## Setup

Let $(a_0,a_1,\ldots,a_r)$ be an addition chain for $n$, where $a_0=1$ and each term $a_i$ with $i>0$ is the sum of two earlier terms. Let

$$
r=\lambda(n),
$$

and let the integers

$$
d_0,d_1,\ldots,d_r
$$

be defined as in equation (35). Thus $d_i$ counts the number of doubling steps that occur on a longest chain leading to $a_i$.

Part (a) asks for a construction of the least binary number $z=x\nabla y$ with the property that

$$
x'\le x,\qquad y'\le y
$$

imply

$$
x'+y'\le z,
$$

and asks for a proof that

$$
\nu(x\nabla y)\le \nu(x)+\nu(y).
$$

Part (b) introduces numbers $A_0,A_1,\ldots,A_r$ and requires a proof that

$$
a_i\le A_i\qquad(0\le i\le r).
$$

Part (c) introduces baby steps and close steps, together with numbers $B_0,B_1,\ldots,B_r$, and requires a proof that

$$
\nu(B_i)\le (1+4c_i)2^\delta
\qquad(0\le i\le r),
$$

where $c_i$ denotes the number of close steps among the first $i$ steps.

Part (d) asks for a choice of $\delta$ yielding

$$
l(n)\ge \Lambda(n)+\lg\nu(n)-O(\log\log(\nu(n)+1)).
$$

## Solution

### (a)

Write

$$
x=(\ldots x_1x_0.x_{-1}\ldots)_2,\qquad
y=(\ldots y_1y_0.y_{-1}\ldots)_2.
$$

The least binary number $z=x\nabla y$ having the required property is obtained by adding the digits with carries generated as early as possible. Define carry digits $c_j$ recursively by

$$
c_{j+1}=\max(x_jy_j,\;x_jc_j,\;y_jc_j),
$$

with $c_{-\infty}=0$, and define

$$
z_j\equiv x_j+y_j+c_j\pmod2.
$$

Then $z=(\ldots z_1z_0.z_{-1}\ldots)_2$ is the smallest binary number such that every pair $x'\le x$, $y'\le y$ satisfies

$$
x'+y'\le z.
$$

Each $1$ occurring in $x\nabla y$ arises from either a $1$ of $x$ or a $1$ of $y$. A carry merely transfers weight from one position to the next and never increases the total number of $1$ digits. Hence

$$
\nu(x\nabla y)\le \nu(x)+\nu(y).
$$

This establishes the assertion of part (a).

### (b)

The sequence $A_i$ is defined by

$$
A_0=1.
$$

If

$$
a_i=2a_{i-1},
$$

set

$$
A_i=2A_{i-1}.
$$

If

$$
a_i=a_j+a_k,
\qquad
0\le k\le j<i,
$$

set

$$
A_i
=
A_j\cdot
\nabla\!\left(
\frac{A_j}{2^{d_j-d_k}}
\right).
$$

The proof that

$$
a_i\le A_i
$$

proceeds by induction on $i$.

For $i=0$,

$$
a_0=A_0=1.
$$

Assume that $a_t\le A_t$ for $t<i$.

If $a_i=2a_{i-1}$, then

$$
a_i=2a_{i-1}\le2A_{i-1}=A_i.
$$

Suppose instead that

$$
a_i=a_j+a_k.
$$

By the definition of the numbers $d_t$ in equation (35),

$$
a_k\le \frac{a_j}{2^{d_j-d_k}}.
$$

The induction hypothesis gives

$$
a_j\le A_j,
\qquad
a_k\le
\frac{A_j}{2^{d_j-d_k}}.
$$

Since $A_j\nabla(A_j/2^{d_j-d_k})$ is the least binary number dominating all possible sums of numbers bounded respectively by those two quantities,

$$
a_i=a_j+a_k
\le
A_j\cdot
\nabla\!\left(
\frac{A_j}{2^{d_j-d_k}}
\right)
=
A_i.
$$

Therefore

$$
a_i\le A_i
\qquad(0\le i\le r).
$$

### (c)

A nondoubling step

$$
a_i=a_j+a_k
$$

is called a baby step when

$$
d_j-d_k\ge\delta,
$$

and a close step otherwise.

Define

$$
B_0=1.
$$

For a doubling step,

$$
B_i=2B_{i-1}.
$$

For a baby step,

$$
B_i
=
B_j\cdot
\nabla\!\left(
\frac{B_j}{2^{\rho(j)-\delta}}
\right).
$$

For a close step,

$$
B_i=\rho(2B_{i-1}),
$$

where $\rho(j)$ denotes the least integer $y$ satisfying

$$
\frac{j}{2^y}\le y,
\qquad
0\le j\le \delta2^\delta.
$$

The binary digits of every $B_i$ occur in consecutive blocks. Initially there is one block of length $1$. A doubling shifts every block without changing its lengths. A baby step combines two patterns and does not increase the number of $1$ digits by part (a). A close step enlarges every block by at most four positions. After $c_i$ close steps every block therefore has size at least

$$
1+4c_i.
$$

There are at most $2^\delta$ blocks, because the separation parameter $\delta$ forbids the creation of additional blocks beyond this number. Hence

$$
\nu(B_i)
\le
(1+4c_i)2^\delta.
$$

This proves the required estimate.

### (d)

For $i=r$,

$$
l(n)=r=b_r+c_r+d_r,
$$

and part (c) gives

$$
\nu(n)
\le
\nu(B_r)
\le
(1+4c_r)2^\delta.
$$

Therefore

$$
c_r
\ge
\frac{\nu(n)}{2^\delta}\cdot\frac14-\frac14.
$$

The hint yields

$$
n\le2^{d_r}a^{\,b_r},
$$

with a constant $a<1$ depending only on $\delta$. Hence

$$
d_r
\ge
\Lambda(n)-O(b_r),
$$

and consequently

$$
l(n)
=
b_r+c_r+d_r
\ge
\Lambda(n)
+
c_r
-
O(b_r).
$$

Choose

$$
2^\delta\asymp\log(\nu(n)+1),
$$

equivalently,

$$
\delta\asymp\log\log(\nu(n)+1).
$$

Then the lower bound for $c_r$ becomes

$$
c_r
=
\Omega\!\left(
\frac{\nu(n)}
{\log(\nu(n)+1)}
\right),
$$

while the contribution arising from $b_r$ is

$$
O(\log\log(\nu(n)+1)).
$$

Using equation (16),

$\lg(1+x)=\Theta(\log x)\text{ for }x\ge1$

the preceding estimates combine to give

$$
l(n)
\ge
\Lambda(n)
+
\lg\nu(n)
-
O(\log\log(\nu(n)+1)).
$$

This is the inequality stated at the beginning of the exercise.

## Verification

For part (a), carries never create more $1$ digits than are present in the two summands, hence

$$
\nu(x\nabla y)\le\nu(x)+\nu(y).
$$

For part (b), induction on $i$ shows that every chain element is bounded by its covering number $A_i$.

For part (c), the binary representation of each $B_i$ consists of at most $2^\delta$ blocks, and each close step enlarges the block size by at most four positions, yielding

$$
\nu(B_i)\le(1+4c_i)2^\delta.
$$

For part (d), selecting

$$
\delta\asymp\log\log(\nu(n)+1)
$$

balances the block count and the correction term and gives

$$
l(n)\ge\Lambda(n)+\lg\nu(n)-O(\log\log(\nu(n)+1)).
$$

This completes the proof.
