[Section 4.6.3: Evaluation of Powers](../)

**Exercise 34.** [*M23*] *[M23]* The binary addition chain of length $n = 2^{e_0} + \cdots + 2^{e_t}$, when $e_0 > \cdots > e_t \ge 0$, is $1, 2, \ldots, 2^{e_0+1}, 2^{e_0-1}+1, \ldots, 2^{e_0-e_1}+2^{e_0-e_1}+1, \ldots, n$. This corresponds to the S-and-X method described at the beginning of this section, while Algorithm A corresponds to the addition chain obtained by sorting the two sequences $(1, 2, 4, \ldots, 2^{e_0})$ and $(2^{e_0}, 2^{e_0}+2^{e_1}, \ldots, 2^{e_0}+2^{e_1}+\cdots+2^{e_t} = n)$ into increasing order. Prove or disprove: Each of these addition chains is a dual of the other.

## Solution

We are asked to consider two addition chains for an integer

$$
n = 2^{e_0} + 2^{e_1} + \cdots + 2^{e_t}, \quad e_0 > e_1 > \cdots > e_t \ge 0,
$$

and to determine whether the **S-and-X chain** and the **Algorithm A chain** are duals of each other.

### Step 1: Recall definitions

**S-and-X chain** (as given in the exercise) has the form:

$$
1, 2, 4, \dots, 2^{e_0}, \; 2^{e_0} + 2^{e_1}, \; 2^{e_0} + 2^{e_1} + 2^{e_2}, \dots, n.
$$

More explicitly, Knuth describes it recursively: first build powers of 2 up to the largest term, then add smaller powers sequentially to form partial sums.

**Algorithm A chain** is obtained by merging the sequences

$$
(1, 2, 4, \dots, 2^{e_0})
$$

and

$$
(2^{e_0}, 2^{e_0}+2^{e_1}, \dots, n)
$$

into **increasing order**, giving an addition chain whose elements are sorted overall.

**Duality of addition chains** (Knuth, Section 4.6.3) means: two chains $a_0, a_1, \dots, a_r$ and $b_0, b_1, \dots, b_r$ of the same length $r$ are dual if

$$
a_i + b_{r-i} = n \quad \text{for all } 0 \le i \le r.
$$

### Step 2: Examine the chains in a small example

Take $n = 13 = 8 + 4 + 1$, so $e_0 = 3, e_1 = 2, e_2 = 0$.

1. **S-and-X chain:**

$$
1,2,4,8,9,12,13
$$

Here the steps are:

- Build powers of 2: $1,2,4,8$
- Add $2^0 = 1$ to 8: $9$
- Add $2^1 = 4$ to 9: $13$

1. **Algorithm A chain** (merging):

$$
1,2,4,8,9,12,13
$$

- Merge $1,2,4,8$ and $8,12,13$ into increasing order.

In this example, the chains **coincide**.

### Step 3: Consider a general case

Let us try a slightly larger example: $n = 11 = 8 + 2 + 1$, $e_0=3, e_1=1, e_2=0$.

1. **S-and-X chain:**

- Powers of 2: $1,2,4,8$
- Add smaller powers sequentially:

- $8+2 = 10$
- $10+1 = 11$

Chain: $1,2,4,8,10,11$

1. **Algorithm A chain**:

- Merge $1,2,4,8$ and $8,10,11$ in increasing order:

$$
1,2,4,8,10,11
$$

Again, chains coincide.

This suggests that **S-and-X chain and Algorithm A chain coincide whenever the sequence of partial sums of the smaller powers is increasing relative to the largest power**. In such a case, the chains trivially are self-dual.

### Step 4: Check the duality condition

Let $a_0, \dots, a_r$ denote the S-and-X chain, $b_0, \dots, b_r$ denote the Algorithm A chain.

- Duality requires $a_i + b_{r-i} = n$ for all $i$.
- In the examples above, since $a_i = b_i$, we have

$$
a_i + a_{r-i} = n?
$$

Check $n=11$, chain $1,2,4,8,10,11$ ($r=5$):

- $i=0$: $1 + 11 = 12 \neq 11$
- $i=1$: $2 + 10 = 12 \neq 11$

So the duality condition **fails**.

Hence, **even when the chains coincide, they are not dual in the Knuth sense**.

### Step 5: General argument

- The first half of the S-and-X chain consists of powers of 2 up to $2^{e_0}$.
- The last half consists of sums of smaller powers added sequentially.
- Algorithm A produces the same chain as S-and-X when merged, but duality requires that $a_i + b_{r-i} = n$.
- Since $a_0 = 1$ and $a_r = n$, the sum is $1 + n = n+1 \neq n$.
- Therefore, the duality condition **cannot hold for the first element**, and in general fails for all elements in the first half.

Thus, the S-and-X chain and Algorithm A chain **are not duals of each other**.

### Step 6: Conclusion

$$
\boxed{\text{The S-and-X addition chain and the Algorithm A addition chain are generally not duals of each other.}}
$$

A concrete counterexample is $n=11 = 8+2+1$, chains:

$$
1,2,4,8,10,11
$$

Here $1 + 11 = 12 \neq 11$, violating the duality condition.

**Answer:** Disprove. The chains are generally not duals.
