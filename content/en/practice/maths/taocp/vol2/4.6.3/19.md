[Section 4.6.3: Evaluation of Powers](../)

**Exercise 19.** [*M25*] A "multiset" is like a set, but it may contain identical elements repeated a finite number of times. If $A$ and $B$ are multisets, we define new multisets $A \uplus B$, $A \cup B$, and $A \cap B$ in the following way: An element occurring exactly $a$ times in $A$ and $b$ times in $B$ occurs exactly $a + b$ times in $A \uplus B$, exactly $\max(a, b)$ times in $A \cup B$, and exactly $\min(a, b)$ times in $A \cap B$. (A "set" is a multiset that contains no elements more than once; if $A$ and $B$ are sets, so are $A \uplus B$, $A \cup B$, and $A \cap B$, and the definitions given in this exercise agree with the customary definitions of set union and intersection.)

a) The prime factorization of a positive integer $n$ is a multiset $N$ whose elements are primes, where $\prod_{p \in N} p = n$. The fact that every positive integer can be uniquely factored into primes gives us a one-to-one correspondence between the positive integers and the finite multisets of prime numbers; for example, if $n = 2^2 \cdot 3^3 \cdot 17$, the corresponding multiset is $N = \{2, 2, 3, 3, 3, 17\}$. If $M$ and $N$ are the multisets corresponding respectively to $m$ and $n$, what multisets correspond to $\gcd(m, n)$, $\text{lcm}(m, n)$, and $mn$?

b) Every monic polynomial $f(z)$ over the complex numbers corresponds in a natural way to the multiset $F$ of its "roots"; we have $f(z) = \prod_{\zeta \in F}(z - \zeta)$. If $f(z)$ and $g(z)$ are the polynomials corresponding to the finite multisets $F$ and $G$ of complex numbers, what polynomials correspond to $F \uplus G$, $F \cup G$, and $F \cap G$?

c) Find as many interesting identities as you can that hold between multisets, with respect to the three operations $\uplus$, $\cup$, $\cap$.

## Solution

Let the multiplicity of an element $x$ in a multiset $A$ be denoted by $m_A(x)$. By definition,

$$
m_{A\uplus B}(x)=m_A(x)+m_B(x),
$$

$$
m_{A\cup B}(x)=\max\bigl(m_A(x),m_B(x)\bigr),
$$

$$
m_{A\cap B}(x)=\min\bigl(m_A(x),m_B(x)\bigr).
$$

### (a)

Let

$$
m=\prod_p p^{a_p},\qquad n=\prod_p p^{b_p},
$$

and let $M,N$ be the corresponding multisets of primes. Thus the multiplicity of a prime $p$ in $M$ is $a_p$, and its multiplicity in $N$ is $b_p$.

Since

$$
\gcd(m,n)=\prod_p p^{\min(a_p,b_p)},
$$

the multiset corresponding to $\gcd(m,n)$ is

$$
\boxed{M\cap N}.
$$

Since

$$
\operatorname{lcm}(m,n)=\prod_p p^{\max(a_p,b_p)},
$$

the multiset corresponding to $\operatorname{lcm}(m,n)$ is

$$
\boxed{M\cup N}.
$$

Since

$$
mn=\prod_p p^{a_p+b_p},
$$

the multiset corresponding to $mn$ is

$$
\boxed{M\uplus N}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\boxed{\gcd(m,n)\leftrightarrow M\cap N,\qquad
\operatorname{lcm}(m,n)\leftrightarrow M\cup N,\qquad
mn\leftrightarrow M\uplus N.}
$$

### (b)

Suppose

$$
f(z)=\prod_{\zeta\in F}(z-\zeta),
\qquad
g(z)=\prod_{\eta\in G}(z-\eta),
$$

where multiplicities are included.

For $F\uplus G$, each root appears with multiplicity equal to the sum of its multiplicities in $F$ and $G$. Hence

$$
\prod_{\xi\in F\uplus G}(z-\xi)
=
\left(\prod_{\zeta\in F}(z-\zeta)\right)
\left(\prod_{\eta\in G}(z-\eta)\right),
$$

so the corresponding polynomial is

$$
\boxed{f(z)g(z).}
$$

For $F\cap G$, each root $\xi$ has multiplicity

$$
\min\bigl(m_F(\xi),m_G(\xi)\bigr).
$$

This is precisely the multiplicity with which $\xi$ occurs in the greatest common divisor of $f$ and $g$. Since both polynomials are monic, the corresponding polynomial is

$$
\boxed{\gcd(f(z),g(z)).}
$$

For $F\cup G$, each root $\xi$ has multiplicity

$$
\max\bigl(m_F(\xi),m_G(\xi)\bigr).
$$

If

$$
d(z)=\gcd(f(z),g(z)),
$$

then the multiplicity of $\xi$ in

$$
\frac{f(z)g(z)}{d(z)}
$$

is

$$
m_F(\xi)+m_G(\xi)-\min\bigl(m_F(\xi),m_G(\xi)\bigr)
=
\max\bigl(m_F(\xi),m_G(\xi)\bigr).
$$

Therefore the polynomial corresponding to $F\cup G$ is

$$
\boxed{\frac{f(z)g(z)}{\gcd(f(z),g(z))}}
=
\boxed{\operatorname{lcm}(f(z),g(z))}
$$

when the least common multiple is taken to be monic.

Hence

$$
\boxed{
F\uplus G\leftrightarrow fg,\qquad
F\cap G\leftrightarrow \gcd(f,g),\qquad
F\cup G\leftrightarrow \operatorname{lcm}(f,g).
}
$$

### (c)

All identities may be proved by comparing multiplicities. If

$$
a=m_A(x),\qquad b=m_B(x),\qquad c=m_C(x),
$$

then $\uplus$ corresponds to addition, $\cup$ to $\max$, and $\cap$ to $\min$. Consequently the following identities hold.

Commutative laws:

$$
A\uplus B=B\uplus A,
$$

$$
A\cup B=B\cup A,
$$

$$
A\cap B=B\cap A.
$$

Associative laws:

$$
(A\uplus B)\uplus C=A\uplus(B\uplus C),
$$

$$
(A\cup B)\cup C=A\cup(B\cup C),
$$

$$
(A\cap B)\cap C=A\cap(B\cap C).
$$

Idempotent laws:

$$
A\cup A=A,
$$

$$
A\cap A=A.
$$

Absorption laws:

$$
A\cup(A\cap B)=A,
$$

$$
A\cap(A\cup B)=A.
$$

Distributive laws:

$$
A\cup(B\cap C)
=
(A\cup B)\cap(A\cup C),
$$

$$
A\cap(B\cup C)
=
(A\cap B)\cup(A\cap C).
$$

Relations involving $\uplus$:

$$
(A\uplus B)\cap(A\uplus C)
=
A\uplus(B\cap C),
$$

because

$$
\min(a+b,a+c)=a+\min(b,c),
$$

and

$$
(A\uplus B)\cup(A\uplus C)
=
A\uplus(B\cup C),
$$

because

$$
\max(a+b,a+c)=a+\max(b,c).
$$

A further identity is

$$
(A\cup B)\uplus(A\cap B)=A\uplus B,
$$

since

$$
\max(a,b)+\min(a,b)=a+b.
$$

Another useful identity is

$$
(A\cup B)\cap C
=
(A\cap C)\cup(B\cap C),
$$

because

$$
\min(\max(a,b),c)
=
\max(\min(a,c),\min(b,c)).
$$

Similarly,

$$
(A\cap B)\cup C
=
(A\cup C)\cap(B\cup C),
$$

because

$$
\max(\min(a,b),c)
=
\min(\max(a,c),\max(b,c)).
$$

These identities show that finite multisets form a distributive lattice under $\cup$ and $\cap$, with $\uplus$ interacting with them through the relations above.

The answers are

$$
\boxed{\gcd(m,n)\leftrightarrow M\cap N,\qquad
\operatorname{lcm}(m,n)\leftrightarrow M\cup N,\qquad
mn\leftrightarrow M\uplus N,}
$$

and

$$
\boxed{
F\uplus G\leftrightarrow fg,\qquad
F\cap G\leftrightarrow \gcd(f,g),\qquad
F\cup G\leftrightarrow \operatorname{lcm}(f,g).
}
$$

∎
