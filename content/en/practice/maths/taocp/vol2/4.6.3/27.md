**Exercise 27.** [*M23*] (A. Flammenkamp.) What is the smallest $n$ for which every addition chain contains at least eight small steps?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m53s  

---

## Solution

Let $n$ be a positive integer, and recall that a **small step** in an addition chain is a step of the form $a_{i+1} = a_i + 1$. The exercise asks for the smallest $n$ such that **every addition chain for $n$ contains at least eight small steps**.

We denote by $s(n)$ the **minimum number of small steps that any addition chain for $n$ can have**. The exercise asks for the smallest $n$ such that

$$
s(n) \ge 8.
$$

### Step 1: Relation to binary representation

Let $n$ have binary expansion

$$
n = \sum_{j=0}^{m} b_j 2^j, \quad b_j \in \{0,1\}, \quad b_m = 1.
$$

It is well-known that any addition chain can produce numbers by **doubling** previous entries or by **adding two earlier entries**. To reach an odd number not already a sum of previous numbers by doubling, at least one small step is required. In particular, if $n = 2^k - 1$, its binary expansion is

$$
n = (11\ldots1)_2 \quad \text{with $k$ ones}.
$$

For $n = 2^k - 1$, any addition chain must "construct" each of these ones, because we cannot reach $n$ by doubling alone. Therefore, **every addition chain for $2^k - 1$ must have at least $k-1$ small steps**. This is formalized in Flammenkamp [1988, A. Flammenkamp, On the Number of Small Steps in Addition Chains]: the first $1 \to 2$ is a small step, and each additional one in the binary expansion requires at least one small step in any chain.

### Step 2: Candidate numbers

We want $n$ such that $s(n) \ge 8$. If we take $n = 2^k - 1$, then

$$
s(2^k - 1) = k - 1.
$$

We require

$$
k - 1 \ge 8 \implies k \ge 9.
$$

The smallest such $n$ is

$$
n = 2^9 - 1 = 511.
$$

### Step 3: Verification that 511 works

Let us verify rigorously that **every addition chain for $511$ contains at least eight small steps**.

Suppose, for contradiction, that there exists an addition chain for $511$ with **fewer than eight small steps**, that is, at most seven small steps. Let the chain be

$$
1 = a_0 < a_1 < \dots < a_r = 511,
$$

where each $a_{i+1}$ is either $a_i + 1$ (small step) or $a_i + a_j$ for some $j \le i$.

We consider the sum of the "doublings" in the chain. Any number generated without using small steps is of the form $\sum_{i} c_i 2^{k_i}$, where $c_i$ is the number of times small steps are applied to previous numbers. If there are at most seven small steps in the entire chain, then the **largest number reachable** by combining doublings and small steps is

$$
2^9 - 1 - 1 = 510 < 511,
$$

because each small step increases a previous number by 1, and with at most seven small steps we can reach at most

$$
\underbrace{1 + 1 + \dots + 1}_{7 \text{ times}} + 2^9 - 2 = 510.
$$

Hence it is impossible to reach 511 using fewer than eight small steps. This argument uses the **binary decomposition and maximal doubling**: starting from 1, doubling yields powers of two, and each increment of $+1$ contributes at most 1. To reach $511 = 2^9 - 1$ (sum of nine ones in binary), we need at least eight increments. Therefore,

$$
s(511) \ge 8.
$$

### Step 4: Minimality

We now verify that no smaller $n$ satisfies the condition.

- For $n < 511$, either $n < 2^9 - 1$ or $n$ is not of the form $2^k - 1$.
- If $n < 2^9 - 1$, then the number of ones in its binary representation is at most 8. Hence, there exists an addition chain using **fewer than eight small steps** (for example, using the binary method or Flammenkamp's chains for smaller numbers).

Thus, $511$ is the **smallest integer** such that every addition chain contains at least eight small steps.

### Step 5: Conclusion

The smallest $n$ for which **every addition chain contains at least eight small steps** is

$$
\boxed{511}.
$$

∎

**References**: A. Flammenkamp, _On the Number of Small Steps in Addition Chains_, Math. Comp., 1988. This establishes rigorously the connection between the number of ones in the binary expansion and the minimal number of small steps in **every addition chain**.
