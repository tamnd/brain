## Solution

Let $i_1 < i_2 < \cdots < i_t$ denote the ordered indices under consideration. The construction of the intervals proceeds as follows:

1. **Initialize** the first interval at $i_1$. Let $b_0 = 1$ denote the starting index of the first interval.
2. **Scan the sequence** from left to right. For each consecutive pair $i_r, i_{r+1}$, check whether the property defining a block continues to hold between $i_r$ and $i_{r+1}$.
3. **Detect transitions**: If the property fails between $i_r$ and $i_{r+1}$, terminate the current interval at $i_r$, and start a new interval at $i_{r+1}$. Record each transition point as $b_1, b_2, \ldots, b_{k-1}$. Let $b_k = t + 1$ denote the endpoint after the last index.
4. **Define the intervals**: For each $\nu = 1, \ldots, k$, set

$$
J_\nu = [\, i_{b_{\nu-1}},\, i_{b_\nu - 1}\,].
$$

By this construction:

- The intervals $J_1, \ldots, J_k$ are **pairwise disjoint** and cover all indices $i_1, \ldots, i_t$.
- Each interval is **maximal** in the sense that it cannot be extended to include the next index without violating the property required by Lemma J.
- The endpoints are determined **uniquely** by the first index at which the property fails, ensuring no ambiguity.

Thus, the intervals are obtained by a simple **left-to-right scan** of the ordered indices, cutting at every point where the defining property fails. The resulting sequence of maximal contiguous blocks $J_1, \ldots, J_k$ is exactly what Lemma J requires.

This construction ensures that all arguments in the lemma that rely on splitting the sequence into such blocks are valid and that no block can be enlarged without violating the defining condition.
