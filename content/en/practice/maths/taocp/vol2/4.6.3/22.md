**Exercise 22.** [*M26*] Prove that the addition chain constructed in the proof of Theorem F is an $l^F$-chain.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 13m15s

### Solution

Let $C(n)$ denote the addition chain produced by the construction in the proof of Theorem F. We must show that $C(n)$ is an $l^{F}$-chain, that is, that $C(n)$ is an addition chain for $n$ whose length is exactly $l^{F}(n)$.

Recall the recursive definition used in Theorem F. For $n>1$,

$$
l^{F}(n)=\min_{\,d\mid n,\;1<d<n}
\bigl(l^{F}(d)+l^{F}(n/d)\bigr),
$$

with the convention

$$
l^{F}(p)=l(p)
$$

when $p$ is prime. The proof of Theorem F constructs a chain recursively according to the same minimization.

We prove by induction on $n$ that the constructed chain $C(n)$ is an $l^{F}$-chain.

For $n=1$, the chain consists only of $1$, hence has length $0$. Since

$$
l^{F}(1)=0,
$$

the result is true.

Now assume $n>1$, and assume inductively that for every $m<n$, the chain $C(m)$ is an $l^{F}$-chain.

There are two cases.

#### 1. $n$ is prime

In this case the construction of Theorem F uses an optimal addition chain for $n$. Therefore the resulting chain has length

$$
l(n)=l^{F}(n),
$$

so $C(n)$ is an $l^{F}$-chain.

#### 2. $n$ is composite

Let

$$
n=ab
$$

be the factorization chosen by the construction, so that

$$
l^{F}(n)=l^{F}(a)+l^{F}(b).
$$

By the induction hypothesis, $C(a)$ is an $l^{F}$-chain for $a$, having length $l^{F}(a)$.

The construction then forms a chain for $n$ by adjoining to $C(a)$ a suitably scaled copy of the chain $C(b)$. If

$$
1=b_0,b_1,\ldots,b_t=b
$$

is the chain $C(b)$, then the appended terms are

$$
ab_1,\;ab_2,\;\ldots,\;ab_t.
$$

To verify that these are legitimate addition-chain steps, suppose

$$
b_i=b_j+b_k
\qquad (j,k<i).
$$

Then

$$
ab_i
   =a(b_j+b_k)
   =ab_j+ab_k.
$$

The numbers $ab_j$ and $ab_k$ have already appeared earlier in the scaled copy, so each new term $ab_i$ is the sum of two previously constructed terms. Hence every appended step satisfies the addition-chain condition.

The chain therefore ends at

$$
ab=n,
$$

so it is an addition chain for $n$.

It remains to count the length. The initial chain $C(a)$ contributes

$$
l^{F}(a)
$$

steps. The scaled copy contributes exactly

$$
l^{F}(b)
$$

additional steps, because $C(b)$ itself has that length by the induction hypothesis.

Consequently the total length of $C(n)$ is

$$
l^{F}(a)+l^{F}(b).
$$

By the choice of the factorization,

$$
l^{F}(a)+l^{F}(b)=l^{F}(n).
$$

Hence the constructed chain for $n$ has exactly $l^{F}(n)$ steps.

By induction, for every $n$, the chain produced in the proof of Theorem F is an addition chain whose length is $l^{F}(n)$. Therefore it is an $l^{F}$-chain.

$$
\boxed{\text{The addition chain constructed in the proof of Theorem F is an } l^{F}\text{-chain.}}
$$
