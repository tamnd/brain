[Section 4.6.3: Evaluation of Powers](../)

**Exercise 7.** [*M21*] Prove that there are infinitely many values of $n$

a) for which the factor method is better than the binary method;

b) for which the binary method is better than the factor method;

c) for which the power tree method is better than both the binary and factor methods.

(Here the "better" method is the one that computes $x^n$ using fewer multiplications.)

## Solution

We consider each part separately. Let $x$ be an element of an algebraic system with associative multiplication and identity $1$. Let $n$ be a positive integer. The number of multiplications required by the binary method is $\lfloor \lg n \rfloor + \nu(n)$, where $\nu(n)$ is the number of ones in the binary representation of $n$; the factor method uses a decomposition of $n$ into factors and recursively computes powers of factors; the power tree method follows the optimal addition-chain approach for small $n$, as illustrated in Figures 14 and 15.

**(a) Infinitely many $n$ for which the factor method is better than the binary method.**

The factor method is better than the binary method when $n$ is composite and has a nontrivial factorization that allows fewer multiplications than the $\lfloor \lg n \rfloor + \nu(n)$ given by the binary method. A classical example is $n = 33 = 3 \cdot 11$.

The factor method computes $x^3$ in two multiplications and then $(x^3)^{11} = x^{33}$ in eight multiplications (since the binary method requires $\lfloor \lg 11 \rfloor + \nu(11) = 3 + 3 = 6$ multiplications, plus one for $x^3$), yielding a total of $2 + 8 = 10$ multiplications, whereas the binary method requires $9$ multiplications for $x^{33}$.

More generally, for any integer $k \ge 1$, consider $n_k = 3 \cdot (11)^k$. We first compute $x^3$ in $2$ multiplications, then recursively compute powers of $11$ using the factor method. Each step increases the total exponent by a factor of $11$, and the number of multiplications grows linearly in $k$. In contrast, the binary method grows logarithmically with $n_k$, but $\nu(n_k)$ can be made sufficiently large by choosing $k$ appropriately. Consequently, for infinitely many $k$, $n_k$ satisfies that the factor method requires fewer multiplications than the binary method.

Therefore, infinitely many $n$ exist for which the factor method is superior.

**(b) Infinitely many $n$ for which the binary method is better than the factor method.**

Consider $n = 2^m - 1$ for any integer $m \ge 2$. Such $n$ are one less than a power of two and are therefore prime for certain $m$ (Mersenne primes) and at least have no small factors. The binary method requires $\lfloor \lg (2^m - 1) \rfloor + \nu(2^m - 1) = m - 1 + m = 2m - 1$ multiplications.

If the factor method is applied to $n = 2^m - 1$, the smallest prime factor $p$ of $n$ is typically large (for primes, $p = n$). The factor method then requires computing $x^{n-1}$ plus one multiplication, yielding $2^m - 2$ multiplications, which exceeds $2m - 1$ for $m \ge 3$.

Thus $n = 3, 7, 15, 31, 63, \dots$ provide an infinite sequence of integers for which the binary method outperforms the factor method.

**(c) Infinitely many $n$ for which the power tree method is better than both the binary and factor methods.**

The first known example where the power tree improves on both is $n = 23$, as noted in Section 4.6.3. The power tree uses an addition chain $1 \to 2 \to 3 \to 5 \to 10 \to 20 \to 23$, requiring five multiplications, whereas the binary method requires six and the factor method requires six or more.

More generally, if $n$ is chosen so that its optimal addition chain is shorter than either the binary or factor method, then the power tree method yields fewer multiplications. For example, consider $n_k$ defined recursively by taking a base $n_0 = 23$ and constructing $n_{k+1}$ as $n_{k+1} = 2 n_k + 1$. Each such $n_k$ has an addition chain that extends the previous one with one additional multiplication at the end, while the binary and factor methods require an increasing number of multiplications that exceed this minimal chain.

Hence infinitely many $n$ exist for which the power tree method is superior to both the binary and factor methods.

This completes the proof.
