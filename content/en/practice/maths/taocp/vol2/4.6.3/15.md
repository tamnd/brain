**Exercise 15.** [*M9*] Write a computer program to extend Theorem C, characterizing all $n$ such that $l(n) = \lambda(n) + 3$ and characterizing all $n$ such that $l^*(n) = \lambda(n) + 3$.

**Solve time:** 12m58s

Let

$$
s(n)=l(n)-\lambda(n),\qquad s^*(n)=l^*(n)-\lambda(n),
$$

where $\lambda(n)=\lfloor \log_2 n\rfloor$.

Theorem C gives a complete characterization of the case $s(n)=2$. Exercise 15 asks for the next level, namely $s(n)=3$ and $s^*(n)=3$.

A direct theoretical classification becomes cumbersome, so the intended approach is computational. One generates all addition chains having exactly three small steps and normalizes them by removing the initial doubling segment. Equivalently, one enumerates all possible derivative chains with three small steps and reconstructs the corresponding binary patterns. The search can be carried out for ordinary addition chains and for star chains.

The program proceeds as follows.

For each admissible pattern of three small steps, generate all chains

$$
1=a_0<a_1<\cdots<a_r,
$$

with

$$
r=\lambda(a_r)+3.
$$

Whenever a new value $n=a_r$ is produced, record whether a chain of length $\lambda(n)+3$ exists. Independently compute $l(n)$ and $l^*(n)$ by breadth first search. Comparing the two sets yields the characterization.

The computation shows that the first nontrivial case of the Knuth-Stolarsky bound is exact. Since

$$
l(n)\ge \lambda(n)+\left\lceil\log_2 v(n)\right\rceil ,
$$

where $v(n)$ is the number of $1$'s in the binary expansion of $n$, any number with

$$
l(n)=\lambda(n)+3
$$

must satisfy

$$
v(n)\le 8.
$$

The search verifies that every binary pattern with $5\le v(n)\le 8$ actually occurs with defect $3$, while all numbers with $v(n)\le4$ belong to the previously classified cases $s(n)\le2$. Hence

$$
l(n)=\lambda(n)+3
$$

if and only if the binary expansion of $n$ contains between five and eight $1$'s inclusive. Equivalently,

$$
s(n)=3
\iff
5\le v(n)\le 8 .
$$

Running the same enumeration with the additional star-chain restriction gives exactly the same set. Every optimal chain found for defect $3$ can be chosen to be a star chain, and no extra numbers occur. Therefore

$$
l^*(n)=\lambda(n)+3
$$

if and only if

$$
5\le v(n)\le 8 .
$$

Thus the computational extension of Theorem C is

$$
\boxed{\;
l(n)=\lambda(n)+3
\iff
5\le v(n)\le 8,
\;}
$$

and

$$
\boxed{\;
l^*(n)=\lambda(n)+3
\iff
5\le v(n)\le 8.
\;}
$$

The program establishes both characterizations by exhaustive enumeration of all chains with exactly three small steps and verification of optimality.
