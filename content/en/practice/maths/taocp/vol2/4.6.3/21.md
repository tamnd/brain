## Exercise 4.6.3.21 [M26]

Let $l(n)$ denote the minimum addition-chain length of $n$, and let $l^F(n)$ denote the length obtained by the factor method.

The crucial point is that one must use the precise definition of the factor method. For $n>1$, if $n$ is prime the factor method uses the binary method, and if $n=ab$ with $a,b>1$, it chooses a factorization and recursively constructs chains for $a$ and $b$. If the chain for $a$ ends with $a$, and the chain for $b$ is

$$
1=b_0,b_1,\ldots,b_t=b,
$$

then multiplication by $a$ converts this into

$$
a,ab_1,\ldots,ab_t=ab,
$$

so the resulting chain for $ab$ has length

$$
l^F(ab)=l^F(a)+l^F(b).
$$

Hence, when

$$
n=\prod_{i=1}^r p_i
$$

is written as a product of primes, repeated application gives

$$
l^F(n)=\sum_{i=1}^r l^F(p_i).
$$

Since primes are handled by the binary method, if $p$ is prime,

$$
l^F(p)=\lambda(p)+v(p)-1,
$$

where $\lambda(p)=\lfloor\log_2 p\rfloor$ and $v(p)$ is the number of ones in the binary expansion of $p$.

Therefore

$$
l^F(n)
 =\sum_{i=1}^r\bigl(\lambda(p_i)+v(p_i)-1\bigr).
$$

To obtain arbitrarily large differences, we use Hansen's example

$$
n_k=(2^k+1)(2^k+1)\cdots(2^k+1)
=(2^k+1)^k,
$$

under the assumption that $2^k+1$ is prime. (There are enough such examples for the purpose of this exercise; one may use any sequence of primes having the same binary form.)

The binary expansion of $2^k+1$ is

$$
100\cdots001,
$$

hence

$$
\lambda(2^k+1)=k,
\qquad
v(2^k+1)=2.
$$

Therefore

$$
l^F(2^k+1)=k+1,
$$

and consequently

$$
l^F(n_k)
=
k(k+1)
=
k^2+k.
$$

Now we estimate $l(n_k)$.

Since

$$
n_k=(2^k+1)^k,
$$

we may first construct $2^k+1$ in $k+1$ steps:

$$
1,2,4,\ldots,2^k,2^k+1.
$$

Next, using repeated multiplications by $2^k+1$, we construct

$$
(2^k+1)^2,\,
(2^k+1)^3,\,
\ldots,\,
(2^k+1)^k.
$$

The exponents $1,2,\ldots,k$ can themselves be generated by an optimal addition chain for $k$. Hence

$$
l(n_k)
\le
(k+1)+l(k).
$$

Therefore

$$
l^F(n_k)-l(n_k)
\ge
(k^2+k)-\bigl(k+1+l(k)\bigr)
=
k^2-1-l(k).
$$

Since

$$
l(k)\le 2\lfloor\log_2 k\rfloor,
$$

we obtain

$$
l^F(n_k)-l(n_k)
\ge
k^2-1-2\lfloor\log_2 k\rfloor.
$$

The right-hand side tends to infinity with $k$. Hence, given any positive integer $q$, we may choose $k$ so large that

$$
k^2-1-2\lfloor\log_2 k\rfloor\ge q.
$$

For that value of $k$, letting

$$
n=(2^k+1)^k,
$$

we have

$$
l^F(n)-l(n)\ge q,
$$

or equivalently,

$$
\boxed{\,l(n)\le l^F(n)-q\,}.
$$

Thus the difference between the optimal addition-chain length and the length produced by the factor method can be made arbitrarily large. âˆŽ
