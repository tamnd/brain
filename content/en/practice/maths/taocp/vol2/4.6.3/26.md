## Corrected Solution

We are asked to compute the $n$th Fibonacci number $F_n$ modulo $m$, for given large integers $n$ and $m$. A naive iteration requires $O(n)$ additions, which is inefficient for large $n$. We exploit the algebraic structure of Fibonacci numbers to construct an $O(\log n)$ algorithm based on repeated squaring of matrices.

### Step 1: Matrix representation of Fibonacci numbers

The Fibonacci sequence $(F_k)$ satisfies

$$
F_0 = 0, \quad F_1 = 1, \quad F_{k+2} = F_{k+1} + F_k \quad \text{for } k \ge 0.
$$

Consider the $2\times 2$ matrix

$$
M = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\[2mm] 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}.
$$

We claim that, for all $k \ge 0$,

$$
M^k = \begin{pmatrix} F_{k+1} & F_k \\[1mm] F_k & F_{k-1} \end{pmatrix},
$$

where we interpret $F_{-1} = 1$ for consistency. Equivalently, one may verify by induction that

$$
\begin{pmatrix} F_{k+1} \\[1mm] F_k \end{pmatrix} = M^k \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\[1mm] 0 \end{pmatrix}.
$$

**Proof by induction.** For $k=0$, we have $M^0 = I = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\0&1\end{pmatrix}$ and

$$
\begin{pmatrix} F_1 \\ F_0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} = M^0 \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}.
$$

Suppose the identity holds for $k$. Then

$$
\begin{pmatrix} F_{k+2} \\ F_{k+1} \end{pmatrix} = 
\begin{pmatrix} F_{k+1} + F_k \\ F_{k+1} \end{pmatrix} = 
\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} F_{k+1} \\ F_k \end{pmatrix} = M \cdot M^k \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} = M^{k+1} \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}.
$$

Thus the formula holds for all $k \ge 0$. ∎

Consequently, $F_n$ is precisely the top entry of $M^{n-1} \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 0 \end{pmatrix}$ for $n \ge 1$. Equivalently, $F_n$ is the $(1,1)$ entry of $M^{,n-1}$. This distinction is crucial: one must compute $M^{n-1}$, not $M^n$, to obtain $F_n$ in the $(1,1)$ position.

### Step 2: Binary exponentiation modulo $m$

Let $n \ge 1$ be given. To compute $M^{,n-1} \bmod m$ efficiently, we apply the standard right-to-left binary exponentiation algorithm. Let $N = n-1$. Write

$$
N = \sum_{i=0}^{k-1} d_i 2^i, \quad d_i \in \{0,1\},
$$

with $d_0$ the least significant bit. Then

$$
M^N = \prod_{i=0}^{k-1} (M^{2^i})^{d_i}.
$$

We accumulate the product modulo $m$ using repeated squaring, reducing modulo $m$ after each matrix multiplication.

### Step 3: Algorithm

**Algorithm F (Compute $F_n \bmod m$).**

**F1. [Initialize.]** If $n = 0$, return $0$. Otherwise, set $N \leftarrow n-1$, $Y \leftarrow I_2$ (the $2 \times 2$ identity), $Z \leftarrow M \bmod m$.

**F2. [Binary loop.]** While $N > 0$, perform the following steps:

- Set $t \leftarrow N \bmod 2$, $N \leftarrow \lfloor N/2 \rfloor$.
- If $t = 1$, set $Y \leftarrow Y \cdot Z \bmod m$.
- Set $Z \leftarrow Z \cdot Z \bmod m$.

**F3. [Return result.]** Return $F_n \equiv Y_{1,1} \bmod m$.

### Step 4: Correctness

We claim that, at any point in the loop, the invariant

$$
Y \cdot Z^N = M^{\,n-1} \bmod m
$$

holds. Initially, $Y = I$ and $Z = M$, so $Y \cdot Z^N = M^N = M^{,n-1}$. Each iteration halves $N$ and squares $Z$, multiplying $Y$ by $Z$ if the removed least significant bit is $1$, exactly as in standard binary exponentiation. Upon termination ($N = 0$), we have $Y = M^{,n-1} \bmod m$, so returning $Y_{1,1}$ yields $F_n \bmod m$ as required.

Matrix multiplications and reductions modulo $m$ ensure that all intermediate numbers remain bounded by $m$, so the algorithm is efficient for large $n$ and $m$. The number of matrix multiplications is $O(\log n)$, and each multiplication takes $O(1)$ operations for $2 \times 2$ matrices, giving an overall time complexity of $O(\log n)$.

### Step 5: Example

To compute $F_5 \bmod 10$, we have $M = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 1\1 & 0\end{pmatrix}$ and $n-1 = 4 = 100_2$. Repeated squaring produces

$$
M^1 = M, \quad M^2 = M^1 \cdot M^1, \quad M^4 = M^2 \cdot M^2 \bmod 10.
$$

Finally, $F_5 = (M^4)_{1,1} \bmod 10 = 5$, which agrees with the Fibonacci sequence.

### Step 6: Conclusion

The corrected algorithm computes $F_n \bmod m$ efficiently for large $n$ and $m$, using $O(\log n)$ time and $O(1)$ space. The essential points are computing $M^{,n-1}$, not $M^n$, and returning the $(1,1)$ entry, with binary exponentiation modulo $m$ ensuring efficiency.
