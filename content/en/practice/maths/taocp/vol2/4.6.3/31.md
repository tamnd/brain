### Solution to Exercise 4.6.3.31 [_M46_]

We are asked to **explore the problem of minimizing**

$$
f = cq + (r - q)
$$

for an addition chain

$$
1 = a_0 < a_1 < \dots < a_r = n,
$$

where:

- $q$ is the number of _simple steps_, i.e., steps of the form $a_i = a_{i-1} + 1$,
- $r$ is the total length of the chain, and
- $c > 0$ is a small "weight" associated with simple steps.

The exercise comes from Lehmer's idea that increment steps can be cheaper in actual computation than general multiplications, e.g., computing $x^n$.

We aim to analyze and describe the minimization of the **weighted cost** $f$.

### Step 1: Rewriting the cost function

The total cost can be written as

$$
f = cq + (r - q) = r + (c-1)q.
$$

Observations:

1. If $c < 1$, simple steps are cheaper than general addition steps.
2. If $c > 1$, simple steps are more expensive.
3. The exercise assumes a "small" positive $c$, so typically $0 < c < 1$.

Hence, minimizing $f$ balances the **chain length $r$** against the **number of simple steps $q$**.

### Step 2: Extremes of the cost

1. **Linear chain** (all simple steps):

$$
1, 2, 3, \dots, n
$$

- Number of steps: $r = n-1$,
- Number of simple steps: $q = n-1$,
- Weighted cost:

$$
f = c(n-1) + ((n-1) - (n-1)) = c(n-1).
$$

For very small $c$, this can be extremely cheap because each simple step contributes little.

1. **Minimal-length addition chain** (fewest total steps $r$):

- Let $r_{\min}(n)$ denote the minimal-length chain.
- Let $q_0$ denote the number of simple steps in such a chain.
- Weighted cost:

$$
f = r_{\min}(n) + (c-1)q_0.
$$

If $c < 1$, the term $(c-1)q_0 < 0$, so including more simple steps can reduce $f$ compared to a purely minimal-length chain.

### Step 3: Strategy for minimization

Given $f = r + (c-1)q$ with $0 < c < 1$:

1. Each simple step reduces the weighted cost relative to a general step.
2. Each general addition step reduces the total number of steps $r$.

Thus, an optimal chain balances:

- **Doubling or combining steps** to increase $a_i$ quickly (reduces $r$), and
- **Simple increment steps** to finish exactly at $n$ cheaply (reduces the effective weighted cost via $(c-1)q$).

### Step 4: Illustrative examples

#### Example 1: $n = 15$

1. Minimal-length chain (binary-style):

$$
1, 2, 4, 8, 12, 14, 15
$$

- Total steps: $r = 6$
- Simple steps: $q = 1$
- Weighted cost: $f = 6 + (c-1)\cdot 1 = 5 + c$

1. Increment-heavy chain:

$$
1, 2, 3, 4, \dots, 15
$$

- Total steps: $r = 14$
- Simple steps: $q = 14$
- Weighted cost: $f = 14 + (c-1)\cdot 14 = 14c$

If $c \ll 1$, $14c < 5 + c$, so the long linear chain is cheaper under the weighted cost.

This illustrates the tradeoff: **very small $c$ favors long chains of cheap simple steps**, while $c \approx 1$ favors short chains with fewer simple steps.

### Step 5: General observations

Let $r_0(n)$ denote the minimal chain length of $n$. Then:

$$
f_{\min}(n) = \min_{q} \left\{ r(q) + (c-1) q \right\},
$$

where $r(q)$ is the length of a chain with $q$ simple steps.

1. $c \to 0$: many simple steps are optimal. Linear chains can dominate.
2. $c \approx 1$: minimal-length chains dominate; fewer simple steps are included.
3. Intermediate $0 < c < 1$: chains combine general doubling/addition steps with simple increments at the end to minimize $f$.

The strategy is therefore:

- **Maximize growth using general additions** until close to $n$,
- **Finish with cheap increments** to reach $n$ exactly, balancing $r$ and $q$.

### Step 6: Bounds

1. **Lower bound:** all steps are simple:

$$
f \ge c(n-1)
$$

1. **Upper bound:** no simple steps (or minimal-length chain with few simple steps):

$$
f \le r_{\min}(n)
$$

The optimal $f$ lies between these extremes, depending on $c$.

### Step 7: Conclusion

- The **weighted addition-chain problem** interpolates between two extremes: long linear chains for very small $c$ and classical minimal-length chains for $c \approx 1$.
- Optimal chains are **hybrids**: general steps to reach a large number quickly, then finishing with simple increments if cheap.
- The solution requires balancing the contribution of $q$ and $r$ according to the small weight $c$.
