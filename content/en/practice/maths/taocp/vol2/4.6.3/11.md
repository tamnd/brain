**Exercise 11.** &#9654; [*M26*] The tree of Fig. 15 depicts addition chains $a_0, a_1, \ldots, a_r$ having $l(a_i) = i$ for all $i$ in the chain. Find all addition chains for $n$ that have this property, when $n = 43$ and when $n = 77$. Show that any tree such as Fig. 15 must include either the path 1, 2, 4, 8, 9, 17, 34, 43, 77 or the path 1, 2, 4, 8, 9, 17, 34, 68, 77.

## Exercise 4.6.3.11 [_M26_], Solution

Let

$$
1=a_0<a_1<\cdots<a_r=n
$$

be an addition chain for $n$, and let $l(n)$ denote the minimal length of an addition chain for $n$. The exercise asks for all addition chains for $n=43$ and $n=77$ such that

$$
l(a_i)=i \quad \text{for all } i.
$$

Equivalently, every initial segment

$$
1=a_0<a_1<\cdots<a_i
$$

is itself an optimal addition chain for $a_i$. These are precisely the root-to-node paths in the addition-chain tree described in Fig. 15.

From known optimal chain lengths (Fig. 15):

$$
l(43)=7, \quad l(77)=8.
$$

Hence any chain for $43$ under consideration has length $7$, and any chain for $77$ has length $8$.

## Step 1: Determine all chains for $43$ with $l(a_i)=i$

We systematically analyze possibilities. Consider the first four steps. Since $l(2)=1$, $l(4)=2$, $l(8)=3$, the initial segment of any chain of length $\ge4$ must be

$$
1,2,4,8.
$$

At step $4$, we have two possibilities for $a_4$ with $l(a_4)=4$:

$$
a_4=16 \quad \text{or} \quad a_4=9,
$$

since $l(16)=4$ and $l(9)=4$.

### Case 1: $a_4=16$

The chain so far is $1,2,4,8,16$. Then $a_5$ must satisfy $l(a_5)=5$ and $a_5>16$. Candidates are sums of previous terms:

$$
a_5 = 16+8=24, \quad 16+4=20, \quad 16+2=18, \quad 16+1=17.
$$

Only $17,18,20,24$ satisfy $a_5>16$ and are achievable in one addition. Among these, only $17$ satisfies $l(17)=5$ (since $l(18)=6, l(20)=6, l(24)=6$), so $a_5=17$.

Then $a_6$ must satisfy $l(a_6)=6$ and be greater than $17$. Possible sums with previous terms are:

$$
17+17=34, \quad 17+16=33, \quad 17+9=26, \quad 17+8=25.
$$

Among these, only $34$ satisfies $l(34)=6$. Finally, $a_7=43$ can be formed as $43=34+9$.

Thus one valid chain is

$$
\mathbf{1,2,4,8,9,17,34,43}.
$$

### Case 2: $a_4=9$

The chain so far is $1,2,4,8,9$. We now find $a_5>9$ with $l(a_5)=5$. Possible sums:

$$
9+8=17, \quad 9+4=13, \quad 9+2=11, \quad 9+1=10.
$$

Among these, only $17$ satisfies $l(17)=5$, so $a_5=17$.

Next, $a_6>17$ with $l(a_6)=6$. Sums with previous elements:

$$
17+17=34, \quad 17+9=26, \quad 17+8=25, \quad 17+4=21, \quad 17+2=19, \quad 17+1=18.
$$

Only $34$ satisfies $l(34)=6$. Finally, $a_7=43=34+9$.

This reproduces the same chain as above:

$$
1,2,4,8,9,17,34,43.
$$

### Case 3: $a_4=16$ but $a_5 \neq 17$

As noted above, other sums at level 5 yield numbers with $l(a_5)\ge6$, so no valid chains occur.

### Case 4: $a_4=16$ and $a_5=17$ (already covered)

No new chains arise.

Hence the **unique addition chain for $43$ satisfying $l(a_i)=i$ is**

$$
\boxed{1,2,4,8,9,17,34,43}.
$$

## Step 2: Determine all chains for $77$ with $l(a_i)=i$

Any chain for $77$ has length $8$. Its first seven terms form an optimal chain for some $a_7<n=77$. From Step 1, the prefix up to $a_6$ must be

$$
1,2,4,8,9,17,34.
$$

This is because $a_6=34$ is the unique choice satisfying $l(a_6)=6$ and allowing a valid chain to 43 or 77.

Now $a_7>34$ must satisfy $l(a_7)=7$. Possible sums with previous elements:

$$
34+34=68, \quad 34+17=51, \quad 34+9=43, \quad 34+8=42, \quad 34+4=38, \quad 34+2=36, \quad 34+1=35.
$$

Among these, only numbers with $l(a_7)=7$ are $43$ and $68$. Other sums either have smaller chain lengths ($35$) or larger ($36,38,42,51$ all have $l\ge8$).

Thus there are exactly **two possibilities**:

1. $a_7=43$
2. $a_7=68$

Finally, $a_8=77$ must be obtained as a sum of $a_7$ and some previous element:

- If $a_7=43$, then $77-43=34$ is present in the chain. Valid.
- If $a_7=68$, then $77-68=9$ is present in the chain. Valid.

Hence the **two addition chains for $77$ satisfying $l(a_i)=i$ are**

$$
\boxed{1,2,4,8,9,17,34,43,77}, \quad \boxed{1,2,4,8,9,17,34,68,77}.
$$

## Step 3: Any tree as in Fig. 15 must include one of these paths

A tree such as Fig. 15 contains all minimal-length chains to each node. Since we have classified all addition chains for $77$ satisfying $l(a_i)=i$, any root-to-$77$ path in such a tree must follow **exactly one of the two chains above**.

Thus any tree of the type shown in Fig. 15 contains either the path

$$
1,2,4,8,9,17,34,43,77
$$

or the path

$$
1,2,4,8,9,17,34,68,77.
$$

This completes the classification.

## Verification

**Chain for 43:**

$$
4=2+2, \quad 8=4+4, \quad 9=8+1, \quad 17=9+8, \quad 34=17+17, \quad 43=34+9.
$$

Length $7$, so $l(a_i)=i$ holds.

**Chains for 77:**

- $43+34=77$ (from chain $a_7=43$)
- $68+9=77$ (from chain $a_7=68$)

Length $8$, $l(a_i)=i$ holds.

All intermediate sums satisfy the minimal-length property. No other choices exist.

## Conclusion

1. The **unique addition chain for $43$** with $l(a_i)=i$ is

$$
\mathbf{1,2,4,8,9,17,34,43}.
$$

1. The **two addition chains for $77$** with $l(a_i)=i$ are

$$
\mathbf{1,2,4,8,9,17,34,43,77}, \quad \mathbf{1,2,4,8,9,17,34,68,77}.
$$

1. Any tree as in Fig. 15 must include either of these two paths to $77$.

∎
