**Exercise 14.** &#9654; [*M50*] *(Mersenne multiplication.)* The cyclic convolution of $(x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_{n-1})$ and $(y_0, y_1, \ldots, y_{n-1})$ is defined to be $(z_0, z_1, \ldots, z_{n-1})$, where

$$z_k = \sum_{s+j \equiv k \pmod{n}} x_s y_j, \qquad \text{for } 0 \le k < n.$$

We will study efficient algorithms for cyclic convolution in Sections 4.3.3 and 4.6.4.

Consider $q$-bit integers $u$ and $v$ that are represented in the form

$$u = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} u_k 2^{\lfloor kq/n \rfloor}, \qquad v = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} v_k 2^{\lfloor kq/n \rfloor},$$

where $0 \le u_k, v_k < 2^{\lceil (k+1)q/n \rceil - \lfloor kq/n \rfloor}$. (This representation is a mixture of radices $2^{\lfloor q/n \rfloor}$ and $2^{\lceil q/n \rceil}$.) Suggest a good way to find the representation of

$$w = (uv) \bmod (2^q - 1),$$

using an appropriate cyclic convolution. [*Hint:* Do not be afraid of floating point arithmetic.]

## Solution to Exercise 4.3.2.14 [_M50_]

We are asked to compute

$$
w = (uv) \bmod (2^q - 1)
$$

for two $q$-bit integers $u$ and $v$ given in the mixed-radix representation

$$
u = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} u_k 2^{\lfloor kq/n \rfloor}, \qquad 
v = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} v_k 2^{\lfloor kq/n \rfloor},
$$

where the digits $u_k, v_k$ satisfy

$$
0 \le u_k, v_k < 2^{\lceil (k+1)q/n \rceil - \lfloor kq/n \rfloor}.
$$

The exercise suggests using cyclic convolution to perform the computation efficiently modulo the Mersenne number $2^q - 1$.

### Step 1: Observe the cyclic property modulo $2^q-1$

For any integer $x$, we have

$$
2^q \equiv 1 \pmod{2^q-1}.
$$

Hence, for $u$ and $v$ represented as above, any power of two can be reduced modulo $2^q-1$ by taking its exponent modulo $q$. More precisely, for exponents $m \ge q$,

$$
2^m \equiv 2^{m \bmod q} \pmod{2^q-1}.
$$

Thus, the product

$$
uv = \left(\sum_{s=0}^{n-1} u_s 2^{\lfloor sq/n \rfloor}\right) 
     \left(\sum_{t=0}^{n-1} v_t 2^{\lfloor tq/n \rfloor}\right)
$$

can be expanded as

$$
uv = \sum_{s=0}^{n-1} \sum_{t=0}^{n-1} u_s v_t 2^{\lfloor sq/n \rfloor + \lfloor tq/n \rfloor}.
$$

Modulo $2^q-1$, each term $2^{\lfloor sq/n \rfloor + \lfloor tq/n \rfloor}$ is equivalent to

$$
2^{(\lfloor sq/n \rfloor + \lfloor tq/n \rfloor) \bmod q}.
$$

If we define the sequence of exponents

$$
k = (\lfloor sq/n \rfloor + \lfloor tq/n \rfloor) \bmod q,
$$

then the coefficient of $2^k$ in $w$ is obtained by summing all $u_s v_t$ whose exponents satisfy this congruence. This is exactly the definition of a **cyclic convolution** of length $n$ if we group digits according to the mapping $k = \lfloor kq/n \rfloor$.

### Step 2: Define an appropriate cyclic convolution

Let us define sequences

$$
x_k = u_k, \quad y_k = v_k, \quad 0 \le k < n.
$$

Their **cyclic convolution** of length $n$ is

$$
z_k = \sum_{s+j \equiv k \pmod n} x_s y_j.
$$

The sum

$$
w = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} z_k 2^{\lfloor kq/n \rfloor} \pmod{2^q-1}
$$

produces the desired modular product. The correspondence is as follows: multiplying the $n$-digit mixed-radix numbers corresponds to convolving the sequences $u_k$ and $v_k$ modulo $n$, and reducing the result modulo $2^q-1$ identifies exponents differing by multiples of $q$.

### Step 3: Algorithm using floating-point arithmetic

1. Represent $u$ and $v$ as sequences of $n$ digits $(u_0, \dots, u_{n-1})$ and $(v_0, \dots, v_{n-1})$.
2. Compute their **cyclic convolution** $(z_0, \dots, z_{n-1})$. This can be done efficiently using the **Fast Fourier Transform** (FFT) over the complex numbers if $n$ is large.
3. Construct

$$
w = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} z_k 2^{\lfloor kq/n \rfloor} \pmod{2^q-1}.
$$

1. If some $z_k$ exceed their digit bounds, carry propagation can be performed in the same way as in standard radix multiplication, taking care to reduce modulo $2^q-1$ at each step.

The hint in the exercise indicates that **floating-point FFT** is acceptable, because all coefficients are bounded and rounding errors can be corrected by standard techniques (e.g., using double precision and rounding to nearest integers).

### Step 4: Summary

The procedure can be summarized as follows:

1. Split $u$ and $v$ into $n$ digits according to the mixed-radix representation.
2. Compute the length-$n$ cyclic convolution $z = u \ast v$ modulo $n$.
3. Form $w = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} z_k 2^{\lfloor kq/n \rfloor} \bmod (2^q-1)$.
4. Perform digit carries if necessary to ensure each $w_k$ satisfies

$$
0 \le w_k < 2^{\lceil (k+1)q/n \rceil - \lfloor kq/n \rfloor}.
$$

This produces the representation of $w$ in the same mixed-radix form as $u$ and $v$.

### Step 5: Justification

The correctness relies on two facts:

1. **Modulo $2^q-1$, exponent addition is cyclic.** That is, $2^{a+b} \equiv 2^{(a+b) \bmod q} \pmod{2^q-1}$. Therefore, cyclic convolution precisely models the wraparound of exponents modulo $q$.
2. **Mixed-radix decomposition is compatible with convolution.** Each $2^{\lfloor kq/n \rfloor}$ acts as a base, and carries between digits can be corrected after convolution. The final sum modulo $2^q-1$ recovers the correct product modulo the Mersenne number.

Hence, the cyclic convolution provides an efficient and mathematically correct method to compute $w = (uv) \bmod (2^q-1)$ in mixed-radix form.
