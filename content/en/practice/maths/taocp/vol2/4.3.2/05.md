## Correctness

The solution does not correctly determine the outcome of the greedy procedure in part (a). The claimed greedy set

$$
99,\ 97,\ 95,\ 91,\ 89,\ 83
$$

is not the complete set produced by the method of (13). After these choices, there are still many odd integers less than $100$ that are relatively prime to all previously selected moduli. For example,

$$
79
$$

is relatively prime to

$$
99,\ 97,\ 95,\ 91,\ 89,\ 83,
$$

so the greedy procedure would continue and select $79$. Therefore the subsequent computation of the greedy product $G$ is not the product actually produced by the greedy method.

Since the greedy set is identified incorrectly, the proof of optimality in part (a) does not address the actual greedy construction.

Part (b) is based on a reasonable optimization idea, but the exercise asks for the largest possible value of $m_1m_2\cdots m_r$, and the proposed solution does not establish part (a) correctly.

## Gaps and Errors

**Critical error.** The statement

> Every odd integer greater than $1$ that remains below $100$ contains at least one prime factor among $3,5,7,11,13,19,83,89,97$.

is false.

The odd prime

$$
17
$$

is less than $100$, is not in that list, and does not contain any of those primes as a factor. The same is true for many other odd primes such as $23,29,31,\ldots,79$.

**Critical error.** The conclusion

> Consequently no further $m_j$ can be chosen.

does not follow and is false. For example,

$$
79
$$

is relatively prime to all six selected moduli and would be chosen next by the greedy algorithm.

**Critical error.** The quantity

$$
G=99\cdot97\cdot95\cdot91\cdot89\cdot83
$$

is not the product produced by the completed greedy method, because the greedy process has not terminated at that point.

**Critical error.** The optimality proof in part (a) is built on the incorrect identification of the greedy set and therefore does not prove the stated claim.

## Summary

The solution fails because part (a) incorrectly claims that the greedy process stops after six moduli; in fact additional admissible choices such as $79$ remain, so the computed greedy product and the subsequent optimality argument are invalid.
