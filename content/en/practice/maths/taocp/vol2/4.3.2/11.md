**Exercise 11.** [*M23*] Assume that all the $m_j$ are odd, and that $u = (u_1, \ldots, u_r)$ is known to be even, where $0 \le u < m$. Find a reasonably fast method to compute $u/2$ using modular arithmetic.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m12s  

---

## Solution

Let $m_1, m_2, \ldots, m_r$ be odd integers, and let $u = (u_1, \ldots, u_r)$ satisfy $0 \le u < m$, where $m = m_1 m_2 \cdots m_r$, and suppose that $u$ is even. We wish to compute $u/2$ efficiently in modular representation, that is, to find $v = (v_1, \ldots, v_r)$ such that

$v_j \equiv u/2 \pmod{m_j} \quad \text{for } 1 \le j \le r.$

Since $u$ is even, each residue $u_j \equiv u \pmod{m_j}$ must also be even or odd according to $u \bmod m_j$, but we must account for the modular arithmetic modulo the odd modulus $m_j$.

Observe that each $m_j$ is odd, so $\gcd(2, m_j) = 1$. Therefore the integer $2$ has a multiplicative inverse modulo $m_j$, which we denote by $2^{-1} \bmod m_j$. Explicitly, $2^{-1}$ is the unique integer $w_j$ such that

$2 w_j \equiv 1 \pmod{m_j}, \qquad 0 \le w_j < m_j.$

Multiplying both sides of $v_j \equiv u/2 \pmod{m_j}$ by $2$ gives

$2 v_j \equiv u \equiv u_j \pmod{m_j}.$

Hence we can solve for $v_j$ as

$v_j \equiv w_j u_j \pmod{m_j}, \qquad 1 \le j \le r.$

This computation requires one multiplication and one reduction modulo $m_j$ for each $j$. The numbers $w_j = 2^{-1} \bmod m_j$ can be precomputed, since $m_j$ are fixed. This yields a fast method to compute $u/2$ in modular arithmetic:

1. Precompute $w_j = 2^{-1} \bmod m_j$ for $1 \le j \le r$.
2. For each $j$, compute $v_j = (w_j u_j) \bmod m_j$.

The resulting $v = (v_1, \ldots, v_r)$ satisfies

$v \equiv u/2 \pmod{m}, \qquad 0 \le v < m,$

by the Chinese Remainder Theorem (Theorem C). No additional carries or conditional adjustments are required, because multiplication by $w_j$ modulo $m_j$ automatically produces a residue in the range $0 \le v_j < m_j$.

This completes the proof.
