[Section 4.3.2: Modular Arithmetic](../)

**Exercise 3.** &#9654; [*M26*] (*Generalized Chinese Remainder Theorem.*) Let $m_1, m_2, \ldots, m_r$ be positive integers. Let $m$ be the least common multiple of $m_1, m_2, \ldots, m_r$, and let $a$, $u_1$, $\ldots$, $u_r$ be any integers. Prove that there is exactly one integer $u$ that satisfies the conditions

$$a \le u < a + m, \qquad u \equiv u_j \pmod{m_j}, \quad 1 \le j \le r,$$

provided that

$$u_i \equiv u_j \pmod{\gcd(m_i, m_j)}, \qquad 1 \le i < j \le r;$$

and there is no such integer $u$ when the latter condition fails to hold.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 3m34s

## Setup

Let

$$
m=\operatorname{lcm}(m_1,m_2,\ldots,m_r).
$$

We must prove two assertions.

First, if

$$
u_i\equiv u_j \pmod{\gcd(m_i,m_j)}
\qquad (1\le i<j\le r),
$$

then there exists exactly one integer $u$ such that

$$
a\le u<a+m,
\qquad
u\equiv u_j\pmod{m_j}
\quad (1\le j\le r).
$$

Second, if the congruence condition fails for some pair $(i,j)$, then no such integer $u$ exists.

The proof will separate uniqueness, necessity, and existence.

## Solution

Assume first that integers $u$ and $v$ satisfy

$$
u\equiv u_j\pmod{m_j},
\qquad
v\equiv u_j\pmod{m_j}
\qquad (1\le j\le r).
$$

Then

$$
u-v\equiv0\pmod{m_j}
\qquad (1\le j\le r),
$$

hence every $m_j$ divides $u-v$. Since $m$ is the least common multiple of the $m_j$,

$$
m\mid(u-v).
$$

Therefore any two solutions are congruent modulo $m$. Among the $m$ consecutive integers

$$
a\le x<a+m,
$$

there is at most one integer in a given residue class modulo $m$. Hence there is at most one solution in the required interval.

Next suppose that a solution $u$ exists. For any pair $i,j$,

$$
u\equiv u_i\pmod{m_i},
\qquad
u\equiv u_j\pmod{m_j}.
$$

Let

$$
d=\gcd(m_i,m_j).
$$

Since $d\mid m_i$ and $d\mid m_j$,

$$
u\equiv u_i\pmod d,
\qquad
u\equiv u_j\pmod d.
$$

Subtracting,

$$
u_i-u_j\equiv0\pmod d,
$$

hence

$$
u_i\equiv u_j\pmod{\gcd(m_i,m_j)}.
$$

Therefore the stated compatibility condition is necessary.

It remains to prove existence under that condition.

Define

$$
M_j=\frac{m}{m_j}
\qquad (1\le j\le r).
$$

For each prime power $p^\alpha$ dividing $m$, choose an index $j(p^\alpha)$ such that $p^\alpha$ divides $m_{j(p^\alpha)}$. Such an index exists because $m$ is the least common multiple.

Consider the system of congruences

$$
x\equiv u_{j(p^\alpha)}
\pmod{p^\alpha}
$$

for all prime powers $p^\alpha\mid m$.

This system is consistent. Indeed, suppose that two congruences involve powers of the same prime:

$$
x\equiv u_i\pmod{p^\alpha},
\qquad
x\equiv u_j\pmod{p^\beta},
\qquad
\alpha\le\beta.
$$

Since $p^\alpha$ divides both $m_i$ and $m_j$,

$$
p^\alpha\mid\gcd(m_i,m_j).
$$

The hypothesis gives

$$
u_i\equiv u_j
\pmod{\gcd(m_i,m_j)},
$$

hence in particular

$$
u_i\equiv u_j\pmod{p^\alpha}.
$$

Thus the two congruences are compatible.

For distinct primes, the moduli are relatively prime. Therefore, by Theorem C applied to all prime-power factors of $m$, there exists a residue class modulo $m$ satisfying every congruence above.

Let $x$ be any representative of that class. Fix $j$. Every prime power dividing $m_j$ appears among the congruences defining $x$, and for each such prime power $p^\alpha$ we have

$$
x\equiv u_j\pmod{p^\alpha}.
$$

Since the distinct prime powers occurring in the factorization of $m_j$ are relatively prime, Theorem C implies

$$
x\equiv u_j\pmod{m_j}.
$$

Thus $x$ satisfies all required congruences modulo the $m_j$.

Finally, choose the unique integer $u$ with

$$
a\le u<a+m
$$

and

$$
u\equiv x\pmod m.
$$

Because each $m_j$ divides $m$,

$$
u\equiv x\equiv u_j\pmod{m_j}
\qquad (1\le j\le r).
$$

Hence $u$ satisfies all required conditions. Existence is proved.

Combining existence and uniqueness, there is exactly one such integer $u$ whenever

$$
u_i\equiv u_j\pmod{\gcd(m_i,m_j)}
\qquad (1\le i<j\le r),
$$

and there is none otherwise.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification

Assume a solution exists. Then for every pair $i,j$,

$$
u_i-u_j=(u_i-u)+(u-u_j).
$$

Since $m_i\mid(u_i-u)$ and $m_j\mid(u-u_j)$, the integer $\gcd(m_i,m_j)$ divides both summands. Therefore

$$
\gcd(m_i,m_j)\mid(u_i-u_j),
$$

which is exactly the required compatibility condition.

Assume the compatibility condition holds. The construction above produces a residue class modulo $m$ satisfying all congruences modulo the prime-power divisors of $m$. Reassembling the prime powers belonging to each modulus $m_j$ yields

$$
u\equiv u_j\pmod{m_j}
\qquad (1\le j\le r).
$$

Since every solution is determined modulo $m=\operatorname{lcm}(m_1,\ldots,m_r)$, exactly one representative lies in the interval $a\le u<a+m$.

## Notes

Theorem C is the special case in which the moduli are pairwise relatively prime. Then

$$
\gcd(m_i,m_j)=1
\qquad (i\ne j),
$$

so the compatibility condition is automatic, and the modulus governing uniqueness becomes

$$
m=m_1m_2\cdots m_r.
$$

The present theorem shows that pairwise relative primality is not needed. The only obstruction to solvability is incompatibility modulo the common divisors of the moduli.
