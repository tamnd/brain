[Section 4.3.2: Modular Arithmetic](../)

**Exercise 1.** [*39*] Find all integers $u$ that satisfy all of the following conditions: $u \bmod 7 = 1$, $u \bmod 11 = 0$, $u \bmod 13 = 5$, $0 \le u < 1000$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 17m17s  

---

## Setup

Find all integers $u$ satisfying

$$
u \equiv 1 \pmod 7,
\qquad
u \equiv 0 \pmod{11},
\qquad
u \equiv 5 \pmod{13},
$$

with

$$
0 \le u < 1000.
$$

The moduli $7$, $11$, and $13$ are pairwise relatively prime. By Theorem C, with

$$
m = 7 \cdot 11 \cdot 13 = 1001,
$$

there exists exactly one solution in the interval

$$
0 \le u < 1001.
$$

Since the required interval $0 \le u < 1000$ is contained in $0 \le u < 1001$, it is enough to determine the unique solution modulo $1001$ and check that it is not equal to $1000$.

## Solution

Use the constructive form of Theorem C.

Set

$$
\frac{m}{7}=143,\qquad
\frac{m}{11}=91,\qquad
\frac{m}{13}=77.
$$

For the modulus $7$,

$$
143 \equiv 3 \pmod 7,
$$

and

$$
3 \cdot 5 = 15 \equiv 1 \pmod 7.
$$

Hence

$$
M_1 = 143 \cdot 5 = 715.
$$

For the modulus $11$,

$$
91 \equiv 3 \pmod{11},
$$

and

$$
3 \cdot 4 = 12 \equiv 1 \pmod{11}.
$$

Hence

$$
M_2 = 91 \cdot 4 = 364.
$$

For the modulus $13$,

$$
77 \equiv -1 \pmod{13},
$$

and

$$
(-1)(-1) \equiv 1 \pmod{13}.
$$

Hence

$$
M_3 = 77 \cdot 12 = 924.
$$

These numbers satisfy equation (7):

$$
M_1 \equiv 1 \pmod 7,\qquad
M_1 \equiv 0 \pmod{11},\qquad
M_1 \equiv 0 \pmod{13},
$$

$$
M_2 \equiv 0 \pmod 7,\qquad
M_2 \equiv 1 \pmod{11},\qquad
M_2 \equiv 0 \pmod{13},
$$

$$
M_3 \equiv 0 \pmod 7,\qquad
M_3 \equiv 0 \pmod{11},\qquad
M_3 \equiv 1 \pmod{13}.
$$

By equation (9) with $a=0$,

$$
u \equiv 1\cdot M_1 + 0\cdot M_2 + 5\cdot M_3
\pmod{1001}.
$$

Therefore

$$
u \equiv 715 + 5\cdot924
= 715 + 4620
= 5335
\pmod{1001}.
$$

Now

$$
5335 = 5\cdot1001 + 330,
$$

so

$$
u \equiv 330 \pmod{1001}.
$$

The unique solution in the interval $0 \le u < 1001$ is

$$
u = 330.
$$

Since

$$
0 \le 330 < 1000,
$$

it lies in the required range.

## Verification

Direct computation gives

$$
330 = 7\cdot47 + 1,
$$

hence

$$
330 \equiv 1 \pmod 7.
$$

Also,

$$
330 = 11\cdot30,
$$

hence

$$
330 \equiv 0 \pmod{11}.
$$

Finally,

$$
330 = 13\cdot25 + 5,
$$

hence

$$
330 \equiv 5 \pmod{13}.
$$

Therefore $u=330$ satisfies all three congruences.

By Theorem C there is exactly one solution in the interval $0 \le u < 1001$. Since $330$ is that solution, no other integer in the interval $0 \le u < 1000$ satisfies the given conditions.

The complete set of solutions in the required range is

$$
\boxed{330}.
$$

This completes the proof.

∎

## Notes

Theorem C implies that every solution of the congruence system is congruent to $330$ modulo $1001$. Thus the complete set of integer solutions is

$$
u = 330 + 1001k,
\qquad k \in \mathbb Z.
$$

Among these, only $u=330$ lies in the interval $0 \le u < 1000$.
