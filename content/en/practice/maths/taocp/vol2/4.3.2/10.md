**Exercise 10.** [*M25*] An integer $u$ that lies in the symmetrical range (10) might be represented by finding the numbers $u_1, \ldots, u_r$ such that $u \equiv u_j \pmod{m_j}$ and $-m_j/2 < u_j < m_j/2$, instead of insisting that $0 \le u_j < m_j$ as in the text. Discuss the modular arithmetic procedures that would be appropriate in connection with such a symmetrical representation (including the conversion process, (24)).

## Solution

Suppose an integer $u$ lies in the symmetrical range

$-\frac{m}{2} < u < \frac{m}{2}, \qquad m = m_1 m_2 \cdots m_r, \eqno(10)$

and we seek numbers $u_1, \ldots, u_r$ such that

$u \equiv u_j \pmod{m_j}, \qquad -\frac{m_j}{2} < u_j < \frac{m_j}{2}. \eqno(10')$

The first task is to modify the usual residue arithmetic procedures to accommodate the possibility of negative residues.

For addition, suppose $u \equiv u_j \pmod{m_j}$ and $v \equiv v_j \pmod{m_j}$, with $-m_j/2 < u_j, v_j < m_j/2$. The naive sum $u_j + v_j$ may lie in $(-m_j, m_j)$. To reduce it to the symmetrical interval $(-m_j/2, m_j/2)$, we define

$(u_j \oplus v_j)_{\text{sym}} = u_j + v_j - m_j [u_j + v_j \ge m_j/2] + m_j [u_j + v_j < -m_j/2]. \eqno(1)$

Here $[P]$ denotes 1 if $P$ is true and 0 otherwise, consistent with the notation of Section 3.2.1.1. This formula ensures that the sum is always in the desired symmetrical interval.

Similarly, for subtraction, the difference $u_j - v_j$ lies in $(-m_j, m_j)$. Reduction to the symmetrical interval gives

$(u_j \ominus v_j)_{\text{sym}} = u_j - v_j - m_j [u_j - v_j \ge m_j/2] + m_j [u_j - v_j < -m_j/2]. \eqno(2)$

For multiplication, the product $u_j v_j$ modulo $m_j$ can first be reduced modulo $m_j$ in the conventional manner, producing a value in $[0, m_j)$. If this residue exceeds $m_j/2$, we subtract $m_j$ to obtain a residue in $(-m_j/2, m_j/2)$:

$(u_j \otimes v_j)_{\text{sym}} = (u_j v_j \bmod m_j) - m_j [(u_j v_j \bmod m_j) \ge m_j/2]. \eqno(3)$

This guarantees that all three basic operations remain closed in the symmetrical residue set.

Conversion between the symmetrical residues $u_j$ and the mixed-radix representation $v_j$ of (24) must also be adjusted. In the original algorithm, the first step computes

$v_1 \leftarrow u_1 \bmod m_1, \qquad 0 \le v_1 < m_1.$

With symmetrical residues, we instead set

$v_1 \leftarrow u_1, \qquad -\frac{m_1}{2} < v_1 < \frac{m_1}{2}.$

At each subsequent step, the update

$v_j \leftarrow \bigl(u_j - (v_1 + m_1(v_2 + m_2(\cdots + m_{j-1} v_{j-1})\cdots))\bigr) C_j \bmod m_j$

is replaced by a reduction into the symmetrical interval: after computing the right-hand side modulo $m_j$ in $[0, m_j)$, subtract $m_j$ if the result exceeds $m_j/2$ to obtain $-m_j/2 < v_j < m_j/2$. The multiplicative constants $C_j$ remain the same as in Section 4.3.2.

The inverse conversion from mixed-radix back to residues uses the same principle: compute $u_j$ modulo $m_j$ and, if necessary, adjust by adding or subtracting $m_j$ to place the result in $(-m_j/2, m_j/2)$.

This symmetrical representation has advantages for computations in which negative numbers occur naturally, since it avoids large positive residues that are close to $m_j$ and allows intermediate sums and differences to remain smaller in absolute value. The algorithms of (11), (12), (16), (17), and (18) are all modified in the same manner: whenever a result may lie outside the interval $(-m_j/2, m_j/2)$, one adds or subtracts $m_j$ as needed.

This completes the discussion of modular arithmetic procedures in connection with a symmetrical residue representation.
