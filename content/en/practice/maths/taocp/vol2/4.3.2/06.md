[Section 4.3.2: Modular Arithmetic](../)

**Exercise 6.** [*M22*] Let $e$, $f$, and $g$ be nonnegative integers.
a) Show that $2^e \equiv 2^f \pmod{2^g - 1}$ if and only if $e \equiv f \pmod{g}$.
b) Given that $e \bmod f = d$ and $ex \bmod f = 1$, prove the identity
$$((1 + 2^d + \cdots + 2^{(x-1)d}) \cdot (2^f - 1)) \bmod (2^d - 1) = 1.$$
(Thus, we have a comparatively simple formula for the inverse of $2^d - 1$, modulo $2^f - 1$, as required in (23).)

## Solution

Edit

### (a)

Let

$$
m=2^g-1.
$$

If

$$
e\equiv f \pmod g,
$$

then $e=f+qg$ for some integer $q$. Since

$$
2^g\equiv1\pmod m,
$$

we have

2^f(2^g)^q
\equiv
2^f
\pmod m.
$$
Hence
$$
2^e\equiv2^f\pmod{2^g-1}.
$$
Conversely, assume that
$$
2^e\equiv2^f\pmod{2^g-1}.
$$
Without loss of generality let $e\ge f$ and write
$$
d=e-f.
$$
Then
$$
2^f(2^d-1)\equiv0\pmod{2^g-1}.
$$
Since $2^g-1$ is odd,
$$
\gcd(2^f,2^g-1)=1,
$$
therefore
$$
2^d-1\equiv0\pmod{2^g-1}.
$$
Write
$$
d=qg+r,
\qquad
0\le r<g.
$$
Because $2^g\equiv1\pmod{2^g-1}$,
2^{qg+r}
\equiv
2^r
\pmod{2^g-1}.
$$

Hence

$$
2^r-1\equiv0\pmod{2^g-1}.
$$

If $r>0$, then

$$
0<2^r-1<2^g-1,
$$

which is impossible. Therefore $r=0$, so $g\mid d$ and

$$
e\equiv f\pmod g.
$$

Thus

$$
2^e\equiv2^f\pmod{2^g-1}
\qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad
e\equiv f\pmod g.
$$

This completes the proof.

∎

### (b)

The statement about inverses required in equation (23) is equivalent to proving that

$$
(2^d-1)
\bigl(1+2^d+\cdots+2^{(x-1)d}\bigr)
\equiv1
\pmod{2^f-1}.
$$

Let

$$
S=1+2^d+\cdots+2^{(x-1)d}.
$$

Since

$$
e\bmod f=d
\quad\text{and}\quad
ex\bmod f=1,
$$

we have

$$
dx\equiv ex\equiv1\pmod f.
$$

Therefore there exists an integer $q$ such that

$$
dx=1+qf.
$$

Using the geometric-series identity,

2^{dx}-1.
$$
Substituting $dx=1+qf$ gives
2^{1+qf}-1.
$$

Modulo $2^f-1$,

$$
2^f\equiv1,
$$

hence

2(2^f)^q
\equiv2.
$$
Consequently,
$$
(2^d-1)S
\equiv
2-1
\equiv
1
\pmod{2^f-1}.
$$
Thus
1+2^d+\cdots+2^{(x-1)d}
$$

is the multiplicative inverse of $2^d-1$ modulo $2^f-1$:

$$
\boxed{
(2^d-1)^{-1}
\equiv
1+2^d+\cdots+2^{(x-1)d}
\pmod{2^f-1}
}.
$$

This completes the proof.

∎

A small caution: the displayed congruence in the exercise statement appears to contain a typographical error. The identity proved above is the one that yields the inverse of $2^d-1$ modulo $2^f-1$, exactly as required for equation (23).
