We are asked whether Theorem C from _The Art of Computer Programming_ would still hold if the variables $a$, $u_1, u_2, \dots, u_r$, and $u$ were allowed to be arbitrary real numbers instead of integers. To answer this, we need to carefully examine the statement and proof of Theorem C, identify the assumptions that depend on integrality, and determine whether the conclusion still holds over the reals.

**Corrected Solution:**

Theorem C, as stated in TAOCP, concerns the representation of a number $u$ in a mixed-radix system: given integers $a$, $u_1, \dots, u_r$, and $u$, there exist unique integers $x_1, \dots, x_r$ such that

$$
0 \le x_i < u_i \quad \text{for } i = 1, \dots, r
$$

and

$$
u = x_1 + u_1(x_2 + u_2(x_3 + \dots + u_{r-1} (x_r) \dots)).
$$

The proof of Theorem C relies crucially on the fact that the variables are integers. Specifically, the construction proceeds recursively: at each stage we compute an integer quotient and remainder using the division algorithm for integers. The uniqueness of the representation also relies on the property that the remainder of integer division satisfies $0 \le \text{remainder} < \text{divisor}$.

If we now allow $a$, $u_1, \dots, u_r$, and $u$ to be arbitrary real numbers, the division algorithm no longer guarantees integer quotients and remainders. For real numbers, the notion of "remainder less than the divisor" does not produce integers uniquely because between any two real numbers there are infinitely many other real numbers. For example, consider a simple case with $r=1$ and $u_1 = 2$, $u = 3.5$. Over the integers, there is a unique $x_1$ satisfying $0 \le x_1 < 2$ such that $3.5 = x_1 + 2k$ for some integer $k$, but if $x_1$ is allowed to be real, there are infinitely many solutions:

$$
3.5 = x_1 + 2 k \quad \text{with } x_1 \in [0,2), \; k \in \mathbb{R}.
$$

Indeed, taking $k = 1.0$, we would have $x_1 = 1.5$; taking $k = 0.75$, we would have $x_1 = 1.0$. There is no longer a unique solution.

Therefore, the essential property of Theorem C, the **existence of a unique representation in the given mixed-radix form**, fails when the variables are allowed to be arbitrary real numbers. The theorem relies on the discrete structure of the integers, particularly the integer division algorithm and the bounded remainder condition. Once we move to the continuum of real numbers, these discrete guarantees disappear, and the theorem no longer holds in its original form.

**Conclusion:**

Theorem C does **not** generalize to arbitrary real numbers. Its validity depends critically on the variables being integers. Over the real numbers, representations of the type given in Theorem C are generally **non-unique** and the constructive proof using integer division fails.
