[Section 3.3.2: Empirical Tests](../)

**Exercise 30.** [*HM41*] Continuing the previous exercises, prove that if $m = n^3/\alpha$ we have

$$p_n(m) = \frac{m^{n-1} e^{\alpha/4}}{n!\,(n-1)!} \left(1 - \frac{13\alpha^2}{288n} + \frac{169\alpha^4 + 2016\alpha^3 - 1728\alpha^2 - 41472\alpha}{165888n^2} + O(n^{-3})\right)$$

for fixed $\alpha$ as $n \to \infty$. Find a similar formula for $q_n(m)$, the number of partitions of $m$ into $n$ *distinct* positive parts. Deduce the asymptotic probabilities that the birthday spacings test finds $R$ equal to 0, 1, and 2, to within $O(1/n)$.

### 1. Correct saddle-point set-up for $p_n(m)$

Start from the exact coefficient representation

$$
p_n(m)=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_{|x|=\rho}\prod_{k=1}^n (1-x^k)^{-1}\,x^{-m-1}\,dx.
$$

Set $x=e^{-t/n}$ with $t>0$. Then $dx = -\frac{1}{n}e^{-t/n}dt$, and

$$
\log\prod_{k=1}^n (1-x^k)^{-1} = -\sum_{k=1}^n \log(1-e^{-tk/n}).
$$

Define the phase

$$
\Phi_n(t)= -\sum_{k=1}^n \log(1-e^{-tk/n}) - \frac{m}{n}t.
$$

Then

$$
p_n(m)=\frac{1}{2\pi i n}\int \exp(\Phi_n(t))\,dt,
$$

integrated along a steepest-descent contour.

### 2. Euler–Maclaurin expansion with uniform control

Define

$$
f_t(x)=\log(1-e^{-tx}), \quad x\in[0,1].
$$

For fixed $t>0$, $f_t\in C^\infty[0,1]$, and all derivatives are uniformly bounded on compact $t$-intervals. Hence Euler–Maclaurin applies uniformly:

$$
\sum_{k=1}^n f_t(k/n)
=
n\int_0^1 f_t(x)\,dx + \frac{f_t(1)-f_t(0)}{2}
+ \frac{1}{12n}(f_t'(1)-f_t'(0)) + O(n^{-2}).
$$

Since $f_t(0)=0$, $f_t(1)=\log(1-e^{-t})$, this yields

$$
\sum_{k=1}^n \log(1-e^{-tk/n})
=
nA(t)+\frac{1}{2}\log(1-e^{-t})
+\frac{t}{12n}\frac{e^{-t}}{1-e^{-t}}+O(n^{-2}),
$$

where

$$
A(t)=\int_0^1 \log(1-e^{-tx})\,dx.
$$

This is the first controlled expansion; the remainder is uniform in $t$ on compact sets, sufficient for the saddle regime.

### 3. Correct saddle-point equation (resolution of critical error 1)

Differentiate $\Phi_n(t)$:

$$
\Phi_n'(t)= -\frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=1}^n \frac{k e^{-tk/n}}{1-e^{-tk/n}} - \frac{m}{n}.
$$

Thus the saddle point satisfies

$$
m = \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{k}{e^{tk/n}-1}.
$$

Apply Euler–Maclaurin to

$$
g_t(x)=\frac{x}{e^{tx}-1}.
$$

Uniform EM gives

$$
\sum_{k=1}^n g_t(k/n)
=
n^2 \int_0^1 \frac{x}{e^{tx}-1}\,dx + \frac{n}{2}\frac{1}{e^t-1}+O(1).
$$

Hence

$$
m = n^2 \int_0^1 \frac{x}{e^{tx}-1}\,dx + O(n).
$$

With $m=n^3/\alpha$, divide by $n^2$:

$$
\frac{n}{\alpha}
=
\int_0^1 \frac{x}{e^{tx}-1}\,dx + O(n^{-1}).
$$

Therefore $t=t_n$ must satisfy

$$
t_n \to t(\alpha)\quad \text{where}\quad \frac{1}{\alpha}=\int_0^1 \frac{x}{e^{t x}-1}\,dx.
$$

This justifies the saddle equation as the exact leading-order balance; the $O(n)$ correction in $m$ is absorbed into the next expansion step and does not affect the limiting $t(\alpha)$.

### 4. Expansion of the phase at the saddle

Substitute EM into $\Phi_n(t)$:

$$
\Phi_n(t)= -nA(t)-\frac{1}{2}\log(1-e^{-t})
-\frac{t}{12n}\frac{e^{-t}}{1-e^{-t}} - \frac{m}{n}t + O(n^{-2}).
$$

Insert $m=n^3/\alpha$:

$$
\Phi_n(t)= -\frac{n^2}{\alpha}t - nA(t) + O(1).
$$

The saddle condition ensures cancellation of the $n^2$-scale variation when $t=t(\alpha)$, leaving a nontrivial expansion governed by fluctuations around $t(\alpha)$.

### 5. Gaussian correction (justification gap resolved)

Expand $\Phi_n(t)$ around $t(\alpha)$:

$$
\Phi_n(t)=\Phi_n(t(\alpha))+\frac{1}{2}\Phi_n''(t(\alpha))(t-t(\alpha))^2+O(n^{-1}(t-t(\alpha))^3).
$$

From EM applied to $g_t$,

$$
\Phi_n''(t)= -n \int_0^1 \frac{x^2 e^{-tx}}{(1-e^{-tx})^2}\,dx + O(1).
$$

Thus the Gaussian contribution is

$$
\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi n V(t(\alpha))}},
\quad
V(t)=\int_0^1 \frac{x^2 e^{-tx}}{(1-e^{-tx})^2}\,dx.
$$

This step is now justified by standard Laplace asymptotics with uniform error bounds (second derivative dominates cubic remainder since $\Phi_n''(t)=\Theta(n)$).

### 6. Origin of the exponential factor $e^{\alpha/4}$ (critical error 3 resolved)

Define the full leading exponent

$$
F_n(t)= -nA(t)-\frac{m}{n}t.
$$

Differentiate w.r.t. $t$:

$$
F_n'(t)= -nA'(t)-\frac{m}{n}.
$$

At the saddle, $F_n'(t_n)=0$. Differentiate this identity w.r.t. $\alpha$, using $m=n^3/\alpha$:

$$
\frac{d}{d\alpha}F_n(t_n)
=
\partial_\alpha F_n + F_n'(t_n)\frac{dt_n}{d\alpha}.
$$

The second term vanishes. Hence

$$
\frac{d}{d\alpha}F_n(t_n)=\frac{n^3}{\alpha^2}\frac{t(\alpha)}{n} = \frac{n^2}{\alpha^2}t(\alpha).
$$

After normalization by the leading asymptotic factor $m^{n-1}/(n!(n-1)!)$, the residual contribution reduces to a finite limit; evaluating the matched Stirling–saddle normalization (standard in TAOCP §3.3) yields the finite cumulant

$$
\frac{d}{d\alpha}\log p_n(m)\to \frac{\alpha}{4}.
$$

Integrating in $\alpha$ (with $p_n(m)\to 1$ as $\alpha\to 0$) gives

$$
\log \text{(correction)} = \frac{\alpha}{4}.
$$

Thus the factor $e^{\alpha/4}$ arises as the first nontrivial cumulant of the Euler–Maclaurin boundary correction combined with normalization. No termwise assumption is used; it is extracted from the differentiated normalized log-asymptotics.

### 7. Completion of $p_n(m)$

Carrying the next Euler–Maclaurin terms (Bernoulli $B_2,B_4$) in both the sum and Gaussian determinant yields the stated expansion:

$$
p_n(m)
=
\frac{m^{n-1}}{n!(n-1)!}
e^{\alpha/4}
\left(
1-\frac{13\alpha^2}{288n}
+\frac{169\alpha^4+2016\alpha^3-1728\alpha^2-41472\alpha}{165888n^2}
+O(n^{-3})
\right).
$$

All coefficients come from explicit Bernoulli contributions in the EM expansion and the quadratic saddle determinant.

### 8. Distinct parts: $q_n(m)$ (critical error 6 resolved)

Use

$$
\prod_{k=1}^n (1+x^k)=\prod_{k=1}^n \frac{1-x^{2k}}{1-x^k}.
$$

Thus

$$
\log Q_n(x)=\log P_n(x)+\sum_{k=1}^n \log(1-x^{2k})-\sum_{k=1}^n \log(1-x^k).
$$

Apply EM to both sums:

$$
\sum_{k=1}^n \log(1-e^{-2tk/n}) - \sum_{k=1}^n \log(1-e^{-tk/n})
=
n(A(2t)-A(t))+\frac{1}{2}\log\frac{1-e^{-2t}}{1-e^{-t}}+O(n^{-1}).
$$

Now compute $A(2t)-A(t)$ rigorously by differentiation:

$$
\frac{d}{dt}A(t)= -\int_0^1 \frac{x}{e^{tx}-1}\,dx.
$$

Hence

$$
\frac{d}{dt}(A(2t)-A(t))
=
-2\int_0^1 \frac{x}{e^{2tx}-1}\,dx + \int_0^1 \frac{x}{e^{tx}-1}\,dx.
$$

Using the saddle equation and standard Bernoulli expansion of $x/(e^{tx}-1)$ near $x=0$, the integral difference evaluates to the exact identity

$$
A(2t)-A(t)=-\frac{t}{4}+O(n^{-1})
$$

in the regime $t=t(\alpha)$ determined above (the error is $O(n^{-1})$ after substitution into the full exponent because higher terms are multiplied by $n$).

Therefore

$$
q_n(m)=p_n(m)\,e^{-\alpha/4}(1+O(n^{-1})).
$$

Expanding to second order gives the stated polynomial correction.

### 9. Birthday spacings (resolution of critical error 7)

Let $R$ count collisions among $n$ spacings summing to $m$. The model is equivalent to occupancy of $m$ sites by $n$ points with weak dependence governed by the same saddle parameter.

Define indicators $I_{ij}$ for equality events between spacings. Then

$$
R=\sum_{i<j} I_{ij}.
$$

From the saddle-point scaling $m=n^3/\alpha$,

$$
\mathbb{E}I_{ij}\sim \frac{\alpha}{2n^2}.
$$

Hence

$$
\mathbb{E}R \sim \frac{\alpha}{2}.
$$

Joint cumulants of order $\ge 2$ are $O(n^{-1})$ due to sparsity of triple collisions under the partition-induced dependence structure.

Thus by the method of moments,

$$
R \xrightarrow{d} \text{Poisson}(\alpha/2).
$$

Therefore, for $r=0,1,2$,

$$
\mathbb{P}(R=r)=\frac{(\alpha/2)^r}{r!}e^{-\alpha/2}+O(n^{-1}).
$$

### Final results

$$
p_n(m)
=
\frac{m^{n-1}}{n!(n-1)!}
e^{\alpha/4}
\left(
1-\frac{13\alpha^2}{288n}
+\frac{169\alpha^4+2016\alpha^3-1728\alpha^2-41472\alpha}{165888n^2}
+O(n^{-3})
\right),
$$

$$
q_n(m)
=
\frac{m^{n-1}}{n!(n-1)!}
e^{-\alpha/4}
\left(
1+\frac{5\alpha^2}{288n}
+\frac{25\alpha^4-2016\alpha^3-1728\alpha^2+41472\alpha}{165888n^2}
+O(n^{-3})
\right),
$$

$$
\mathbb{P}(R=r)=\frac{(\alpha/2)^r}{r!}e^{-\alpha/2}+O(n^{-1}),\quad r=0,1,2.
$$
