**Exercise 32.** [*M20*] True or false: If $X$ and $Y$ are independent, identically distributed random variables with mean 0, and if they are more likely to be positive than negative, then $X + Y$ is more likely to be positive than negative.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m40s  

---

**Solution (corrected)**

The statement is **false**.

We construct a counterexample showing that even if $X$ is mean-zero and more likely positive than negative, the sum $X+Y$ (with $Y$ an independent copy of $X$) can be more likely negative than positive.

### Step 1: Construct a distribution for $X$

Let $X$ take two values:

$$
X =
\begin{cases}
1 & \text{with probability } 0.51,\\
-a & \text{with probability } 0.49,
\end{cases}
$$

where $a>0$ is chosen so that $\mathbb{E}[X]=0$.

The mean condition gives

$$
0.51 \cdot 1 + 0.49 \cdot (-a) = 0,
$$

so

$$
a = \frac{0.51}{0.49} > 1.
$$

Hence:

- $X>0$ with probability $0.51$,
- $X<0$ with probability $0.49$,
- $\mathbb{E}[X]=0$.

So the hypotheses are satisfied.

### Step 2: Analyze $X+X'$

Let $X'$ be an independent copy of $X$. We compute the probability that $X+X'>0$.

The possible sums are:

- $1+1 = 2 > 0$,
- $1 + (-a) = 1-a < 0$ since $a>1$,
- $(-a) + 1 = 1-a < 0$,
- $(-a)+(-a) = -2a < 0$.

Thus the **only way** to get a positive sum is when both variables equal $1$.

Therefore,

$$
\Pr(X+X' > 0) = \Pr(X=1, X'=1) = (0.51)^2 = 0.2601.
$$

### Step 3: Compare with $1/2$

$$
\Pr(X+X' > 0) = 0.2601 < \tfrac12.
$$

So $X+Y$ is actually **more likely to be negative than positive**, despite $X$ being more likely positive and having mean zero.

### Conclusion

The claim is **false**. A mean-zero distribution can still be sufficiently skewed so that convolution with itself shifts mass toward negative values.

$$
\boxed{\text{The statement is false.}}
$$
