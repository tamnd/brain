[Section 3.3.2: Empirical Tests](../)

**Exercise 17.** [*M25*] Given any numbers $U_0, \ldots, U_{n-1}, V_0, \ldots, V_{n-1}$, let their mean values be

$$\bar{u} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{0 \le k < n} U_k, \qquad \bar{v} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{0 \le k < n} V_k.$$

a) Let $U_k' = U_k - \bar{u}$, $V_k' = V_k - \bar{v}$. Show that the correlation coefficient $C$ given in Eq. (24) is equal to

$$\sum_{0 \le k < n} U_k' V_k' \Bigg/ \sqrt{\sum_{0 \le k < n} U_k'^2 \cdot \sum_{0 \le k < n} V_k'^2}.$$

b) Let $C = N/D$, where $N$ and $D$ denote the numerator and denominator of the expression in part (a). Show that $N^2 \le D^2$, hence $-1 \le C \le 1$; and obtain a formula for the difference $D^2 - N^2$. [*Hint:* See exercise 1.2.3–30.]

c) If $C = \pm 1$, show that $\alpha U_k + \beta V_k = r$, $0 \le k < n$, for some constants $\alpha$, $\beta$, and $r$, not all zero.

## Solution

Let

$$
U_k' = U_k-\bar u,\qquad V_k' = V_k-\bar v,
$$

where

$$
\bar u=\frac1n\sum_{0\le k<n}U_k,\qquad
\bar v=\frac1n\sum_{0\le k<n}V_k.
$$

Since $\bar u$ and $\bar v$ are the mean values,

$$
\sum_{0\le k<n}U_k'=0,
\qquad
\sum_{0\le k<n}V_k'=0.
$$

### a)

Equation (24) defines the correlation coefficient by

$$
C=
\frac{
\sum_{0\le k<n}(U_k-\bar u)(V_k-\bar v)
}{
\sqrt{
\left(\sum_{0\le k<n}(U_k-\bar u)^2\right)
\left(\sum_{0\le k<n}(V_k-\bar v)^2\right)
}
}.
$$

Substituting

$$
U_k-\bar u=U_k',\qquad
V_k-\bar v=V_k',
$$

gives

$$
\sum_{0\le k<n}(U_k-\bar u)(V_k-\bar v)
=
\sum_{0\le k<n}U_k'V_k',
$$

and

$$
\sum_{0\le k<n}(U_k-\bar u)^2
=
\sum_{0\le k<n}U_k'^2,
\qquad
\sum_{0\le k<n}(V_k-\bar v)^2
=
\sum_{0\le k<n}V_k'^2.
$$

Hence

$$
C=
\frac{\sum_{0\le k<n}U_k'V_k'}
{\sqrt{\sum_{0\le k<n}U_k'^2\;
       \sum_{0\le k<n}V_k'^2}},
$$

which is the required formula.

### b)

Let

$$
N=\sum_{0\le k<n}U_k'V_k',
$$

and

$$
D=
\sqrt{
\sum_{0\le k<n}U_k'^2\;
\sum_{0\le k<n}V_k'^2}.
$$

Then

$$
D^2
=
\left(\sum_i U_i'^2\right)
\left(\sum_j V_j'^2\right)
=
\sum_{i,j}U_i'^2V_j'^2,
$$

while

$$
N^2
=
\left(\sum_iU_i'V_i'\right)^2
=
\sum_{i,j}U_i'U_j'V_i'V_j'.
$$

Therefore

$$
D^2-N^2
=
\sum_{i,j}
\bigl(U_i'^2V_j'^2-U_i'U_j'V_i'V_j'\bigr).
$$

For $i=j$, the summand is $0$. For $i\ne j$, combine the terms corresponding to $(i,j)$ and $(j,i)$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
&U_i'^2V_j'^2+U_j'^2V_i'^2
-2U_i'U_j'V_i'V_j' \\
&=(U_i'V_j'-U_j'V_i')^2.
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence

$$
D^2-N^2
=
\sum_{0\le i<j<n}
(U_i'V_j'-U_j'V_i')^2.
$$

This is the identity requested in the hint.

Since every term on the right-hand side is nonnegative,

$$
D^2-N^2\ge0,
$$

hence

$$
N^2\le D^2.
$$

If $C=N/D$ is defined, then $D>0$, and therefore

$$
|C|
=
\frac{|N|}{D}
\le
1.
$$

Thus

$$
-1\le C\le 1.
$$

The required formula is

$$
\boxed{
D^2-N^2
=
\sum_{0\le i<j<n}
(U_i'V_j'-U_j'V_i')^2
}.
$$

### c)

Assume that

$$
C=\pm1.
$$

Since $C=N/D$,

$$
N^2=D^2.
$$

By the identity proved in part (b),

$$
\sum_{0\le i<j<n}
(U_i'V_j'-U_j'V_i')^2=0.
$$

A sum of squares can vanish only when every square vanishes. Therefore

$$
U_i'V_j'-U_j'V_i'=0
\qquad(0\le i,j<n).
$$

We distinguish two cases.

#### Case 1. $U_k'=0$ for all $k$

Then $U_k=\bar u$ for all $k$. Hence

$$
1\cdot U_k+0\cdot V_k=\bar u
\qquad(0\le k<n).
$$

Thus the required relation holds with

$$
\alpha=1,\qquad \beta=0,\qquad r=\bar u.
$$

#### Case 2. $U_m'\neq0$ for some index $m$

Setting $j=m$ in

$$
U_i'V_j'-U_j'V_i'=0
$$

gives

$$
U_k'V_m'-U_m'V_k'=0
\qquad(0\le k<n).
$$

Since $U_m'\neq0$,

$$
V_k'
=
\frac{V_m'}{U_m'}\,U_k'
\qquad(0\le k<n).
$$

Let

$$
\lambda=\frac{V_m'}{U_m'}.
$$

Then

$$
V_k'=\lambda U_k'
\qquad(0\le k<n).
$$

Substituting the definitions of $U_k'$ and $V_k'$,

$$
V_k-\bar v
=
\lambda(U_k-\bar u).
$$

Therefore

$$
\lambda U_k - V_k
=
\lambda\bar u-\bar v
\qquad(0\le k<n).
$$

Hence the required relation

$$
\alpha U_k+\beta V_k=r
$$

holds for all $k$, with

$$
\alpha=\lambda,\qquad
\beta=-1,\qquad
r=\lambda\bar u-\bar v.
$$

Since $\beta=-1$, the constants are not all zero.

Thus, whenever $C=\pm1$, there exist constants $\alpha,\beta,r$, not all zero, such that

$$
\boxed{\alpha U_k+\beta V_k=r\qquad(0\le k<n).}
$$

This completes the proof.
