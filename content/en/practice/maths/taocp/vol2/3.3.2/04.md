## Solution

Let $\langle U_n \rangle = U_0, U_1, U_2, \ldots$ be a sequence of independent uniform random variables on $[0,1)$, and let $0 \le \alpha < \beta \le 1$. Define $p = \beta - \alpha$, the probability that a single $U_j$ falls in $[\alpha, \beta)$. We examine the gap test as described in Algorithm G, where a gap of length $r$ is a maximal sequence of $r$ consecutive $U$'s lying outside $[\alpha, \beta)$, immediately followed by a $U$ in $[\alpha, \beta)$.

Let $X$ denote the length of a single gap. Then, by definition, $X = r$ if the first $r$ trials fall outside $[\alpha, \beta)$ and the $(r+1)$-th trial falls inside. The probability of this event is

$$
\Pr(X = r) = (1 - p)^r \, p, \quad r = 0, 1, 2, \ldots
$$

since each $U_j$ is independent, the first $r$ trials must each be outside $[\alpha, \beta)$ with probability $(1-p)^r$, and the next trial must be inside with probability $p$. This establishes the formula for $p_r$ in equation (4) for $0 \le r \le t-1$.

The last category, $r = t$, corresponds to a gap of length at least $t$, i.e., $X \ge t$. By summing the probabilities of the geometric distribution for $r \ge t$, we obtain

$$
p_t = \Pr(X \ge t) = \sum_{r = t}^{\infty} (1-p)^r \, p = (1-p)^t \sum_{r=0}^{\infty} (1-p)^r \, p = (1-p)^t,
$$

because the sum of a geometric series with ratio $1-p < 1$ equals 1.

Thus, the probability mass function for the gap length as observed by Algorithm G is

$$
p_r = p (1-p)^r \quad \text{for } 0 \le r \le t-1, \qquad p_t = (1-p)^t,
$$

which matches exactly the formula given in (4). The derivation uses only independence and the uniform probability of each $U_j$ falling inside $[\alpha, \beta)$. This completes the proof.
