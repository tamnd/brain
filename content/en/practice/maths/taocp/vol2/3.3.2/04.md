**Exercise 4.** [*M12*] Prove that the probabilities in (4) are correct for the gap test.

## Solution

Let $U_0, U_1, \ldots$ be a sequence of independent random variables uniformly distributed on $[0,1)$, and let $p = \beta - \alpha$ be the probability that a single $U_j$ falls into the interval $[\alpha, \beta)$. We wish to justify the probabilities given in equation (4) for the lengths of gaps between successive occurrences of values in this interval. Let a _gap of length $r$_ be defined as a consecutive subsequence $U_j, U_{j+1}, \ldots, U_{j+r}$ in which $U_j, \ldots, U_{j+r-1} \notin [\alpha, \beta)$ and $U_{j+r} \in [\alpha, \beta)$. Gaps of length $r \ge t$ are recorded in the final category $\text{COUNT}[t]$.

Consider first the probability that a single gap has exactly length $r$, for $0 \le r \le t-1$. By definition, this requires that $r$ consecutive $U$'s lie outside the interval, followed immediately by a $U$ inside the interval. Since the $U$'s are independent, the probability that $U_j \notin [\alpha, \beta)$ is $1-p$ and the probability that $U_j \in [\alpha, \beta)$ is $p$. Therefore the probability of the sequence described above is

$$
\Pr(\text{gap length } r) = (1-p)^r \cdot p.
$$

This gives the formula

$$
p_r = p(1-p)^r, \qquad 0 \le r \le t-1,
$$

which coincides with the first part of equation (4).

Next, consider the category $r = t$, which counts all gaps of length at least $t$. A gap of length $\ge t$ consists of at least $t$ consecutive $U$'s not in $[\alpha, \beta)$, followed immediately by a $U$ inside the interval, or possibly the sequence ends before the next $U$ falls in the interval. For a random sequence long enough, the probability that a gap reaches length $t$ or more without seeing a $U$ in $[\alpha, \beta)$ is exactly the probability that the first $t$ $U$'s after the last occurrence all lie outside $[\alpha, \beta)$. Since the first $t$ $U$'s are independent, each with probability $1-p$ of being outside, this probability is

$$
p_t = (1-p)^t.
$$

This matches the second part of equation (4).

Equation (4) thus enumerates all mutually exclusive possibilities for the next occurrence of a $U_j \in [\alpha, \beta)$, and the sum

$$
\sum_{r=0}^{t-1} p_r + p_t = \sum_{r=0}^{t-1} p (1-p)^r + (1-p)^t = 1
$$

verifies that the probabilities form a valid distribution. The factor $(1-p)^r$ accounts for $r$ failures to hit the interval, and the multiplicative $p$ or $(1-p)^t$ accounts for the success or truncation event, respectively.

This derivation relies only on the independence of the $U_j$ and the constant probability $p$ of falling in the interval. The formula is therefore exact for a truly random sequence, independent of $n$, $\alpha$, or $\beta$.

This completes the proof.
