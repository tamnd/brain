**Exercise 15.** [*M10*] In the maximum-of-$t$ test, why are $V_0', V_1', \ldots, V_{n-1}'$ supposed to be uniformly distributed between zero and one?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m06s  

---

Let $M_j$ be the maximum of the $t$ successive values in the $j$th sample. For $0 \le x \le 1$,

$$
\Pr\{M_j \le x\}
 = \Pr\{U_{jt}, U_{jt+1}, \ldots, U_{jt+t-1} \le x\}
 = x^t,
$$

because the $U$'s are independent and uniformly distributed on $[0,1]$. Hence the distribution function of $M_j$ is $F(x)=x^t$.

The maximum-of-$t$ test defines

$$
V_j' = M_j^{\,t}.
$$

Therefore, for $0 \le y \le 1$,

$$
\Pr\{V_j' \le y\}
 = \Pr\{M_j^{\,t} \le y\}
 = \Pr\{M_j \le y^{1/t}\}
 = \left(y^{1/t}\right)^t
 = y.
$$

Thus the distribution function of $V_j'$ is $F(y)=y$, so the values $V_0', V_1', \ldots, V_{n-1}'$ are uniformly distributed between zero and one. $\boxed{V_j' \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1).}$
