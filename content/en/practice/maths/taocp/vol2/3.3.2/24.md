**Exercise 24.** [*HM37*] *[HM37]* (G. Marsaglia.) Show that the serial test on $n$ overlapping $t$-tuples $(Y_1, Y_2, \ldots, Y_t)$, $(Y_2, Y_3, \ldots, Y_{t+1})$, $\ldots$, $(Y_n, Y_1, \ldots, Y_{t-1})$ can be carried out as follows: For each string $\alpha = a_1 \ldots a_m$ with $0 \le a_i < d$, let $N(\alpha)$ be the number of times $\alpha$ occurs as a substring of $Y_1 Y_2 \ldots Y_n Y_1 \ldots Y_{m-1}$, and let $P(\alpha) = P(a_1) \ldots P(a_m)$ be the probability that $\alpha$ occurs at any given position; individual digits may occur with differing probabilities $P(0)$, $P(1)$, $\ldots$, $P(d-1)$. Compute the statistic

$$V = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{|\alpha|=t} \frac{N(\alpha)^2}{P(\alpha)} - \frac{1}{n} \sum_{|\alpha|=t-1} \frac{N(\alpha)^2}{P(\alpha)}.$$

Then $V$ should have the chi-square distribution with $d^t - d^{t-1}$ degrees of freedom when $n$ is large. [*Hint:* Use exercise 3.3.1–25.]

### Corrected Solution

Let $Y_1,\dots,Y_n$ be i.i.d. symbols in $\{0,\dots,d-1\}$ with probabilities $P(0),\dots,P(d-1)$, extended cyclically. Fix $t \ge 2$. Write a $t$-string as $\alpha = \beta a$, where $\beta$ is a $(t-1)$-string and $a\in\{0,\dots,d-1\}$.

## 1. Reorganization of the data (overlap handled correctly)

Define for each $(t-1)$-string $\beta$ and symbol $a$,

$$
N(\beta a) := \#\{i : (Y_i,\dots,Y_{i+t-1}) = (\beta,a)\}.
$$

Then:

- Each $t$-tuple corresponds uniquely to a pair $(\beta,a)$,
- The overlap is encoded by the identity

$$
N(\beta) = \sum_{a=0}^{d-1} N(\beta a),
$$

where $N(\beta)$ counts occurrences of the $(t-1)$-string $\beta$.

Thus the full cyclic sequence of overlapping $t$-tuples is exactly the collection of edge counts in a directed graph whose vertices are $(t-1)$-strings and edges are $t$-strings.

This removes the incorrect independence assumption: we no longer treat $t$-tuples as independent observations.

## 2. Correct probabilistic structure (Markov chain viewpoint)

Under the i.i.d. model for $Y_i$, the induced process of overlapping $(t-1)$-blocks

$$
(Y_i,\dots,Y_{i+t-2})
$$

forms a stationary Markov chain on the state space

$$
\mathcal{S} = \{0,\dots,d-1\}^{t-1},
$$

with transition probabilities

$$
\mathbb{P}(\beta \to \beta') = 
\begin{cases}
P(a), & \text{if } \beta' = (b_2,\dots,b_{t-1},a), \\
0, & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
$$

In particular, from a state $\beta$, the outgoing edge distribution is multinomial with probabilities $P(a)$, independent of $\beta$.

Hence, conditional on the state sequence, the edge counts behave like dependent multinomial samples, but with a standard empirical-process limit governed by a Markov chain CLT.

## 3. Reduction to a standard Pearson statistic

Define expected probabilities

$$
p(\beta a) = P(\beta)P(a), \quad p(\beta)=P(\beta).
$$

The natural Pearson chi-square statistic for the Markov chain transitions is

$$
X^2
=
\sum_{\beta,a}
\frac{\bigl(N(\beta a) - N(\beta)P(a)\bigr)^2}{N(\beta)P(a)}.
$$

We relate this to $V$.

## 4. Algebraic identity linking $V$ and $X^2$

Start from

$$
V
=
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{|\alpha|=t}\frac{N(\alpha)^2}{P(\alpha)}
-
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{|\beta|=t-1}\frac{N(\beta)^2}{P(\beta)}.
$$

Write $\alpha=\beta a$, $P(\alpha)=P(\beta)P(a)$. Then

$$
\frac{N(\alpha)^2}{P(\alpha)}
=
\frac{N(\beta a)^2}{P(\beta)P(a)}.
$$

Hence

$$
V
=
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{\beta,a}\frac{N(\beta a)^2}{P(\beta)P(a)}
-
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{\beta}\frac{N(\beta)^2}{P(\beta)}.
$$

Expand the first term by writing

$$
N(\beta a)^2
=
\bigl(N(\beta a)-N(\beta)P(a)\bigr)^2
+2N(\beta a)N(\beta)P(a)-N(\beta)^2P(a)^2.
$$

Summing over $a$ and using $\sum_a N(\beta a)=N(\beta)$ gives cancellation of all mixed terms:

$$
\sum_a \frac{2N(\beta a)N(\beta)P(a)}{P(\beta)P(a)} = \frac{2N(\beta)^2}{P(\beta)},
$$

$$
\sum_a \frac{N(\beta)^2P(a)^2}{P(\beta)P(a)} = \frac{N(\beta)^2}{P(\beta)}.
$$

Therefore all non-quadratic terms cancel exactly, yielding

$$
V
=
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{\beta,a}
\frac{\bigl(N(\beta a)-N(\beta)P(a)\bigr)^2}{P(\beta)P(a)}.
$$

Replacing $N(\beta)$ by $nP(\beta)$ introduces only $o_p(1)$ error after normalization (since $N(\beta)/n \to P(\beta)$ by the law of large numbers). Thus

$$
V
=
\sum_{\beta,a}
\frac{\bigl(N(\beta a)-nP(\beta)P(a)\bigr)^2}{nP(\beta)P(a)}
+o_p(1),
$$

which is exactly the Pearson statistic for the Markov transition table.

## 5. Asymptotic distribution (correct use of dependence)

Now apply the standard result for empirical transition counts of a finite-state Markov chain:

- The vector $\{N(\beta a)\}$ is asymptotically multivariate normal,
- with covariance structure identical to that of multinomial sampling **within each state**, coupled only through the linear constraints

$$
\sum_a N(\beta a)=N(\beta).
$$

This is precisely the setting of the chi-square limit for contingency tables with fixed marginal constraints (see TAOCP Exercise 3.3.1–25).

Hence $X^2$, and therefore $V$, converges in distribution to a chi-square law whose degrees of freedom equal

$$
\#\{(\beta,a)\} - \#\{\beta\}
=
d^{t-1}\cdot d - d^{t-1}
=
d^t - d^{t-1}.
$$

## 6. Conclusion

The statistic $V$ is asymptotically the Pearson chi-square statistic for the $d^{t-1}\times d$ transition table of a stationary Markov chain with fixed row sums. The overlap of $t$-tuples is fully accounted for by this Markov (de Bruijn graph) structure, avoiding any false independence assumption.

Therefore,

$$
V \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2_{d^t - d^{t-1}}.
$$

$$
\boxed{V \sim \chi^2_{d^t - d^{t-1}} \text{ asymptotically}}
$$
