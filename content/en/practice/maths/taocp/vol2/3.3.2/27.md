**Exercise 27.** &#9654; [*HM36*] *[HM36]* (*Iterated spacings.*) In the notation of the previous exercise, show that the numbers $S'_1 = nS_{(1)}$, $S'_2 = (n-1)(S_{(2)} - S_{(1)})$, $\ldots$, $S'_n = 1 \cdot (S_{(n)} - S_{(n-1)})$ have the same joint probability distribution as the original spacings $S_1, \ldots, S_n$ of $n$ uniform deviates. Therefore we can sort them into order, $S'_{(1)} \le \cdots \le S'_{(n)}$, and repeat this transformation to get yet another set of random spacings $S''_1, \ldots, S''_n$, etc. Each successive set of spacings $S^{(k)}_1, \ldots, S^{(k)}_n$ can be subjected to the Kolmogorov–Smirnov

test, using

$$K_{n-1}^{+} = \sqrt{n-1} \max_{1 \le j \le n} \left(\frac{j}{n-1} - S_1^{(k)} - \cdots - S_j^{(k)}\right),$$

$$K_{n-1}^{-} = \sqrt{n-1} \max_{1 \le j \le n} \left(S_1^{(k)} + \cdots + S_j^{(k)} - \frac{j-1}{n-1}\right).$$

Examine the transformation from $(S_1, \ldots, S_n)$ to $(S_1', \ldots, S_n')$ in detail in the cases $n = 2$ and $n = 3$; explain why continued repetition of this process will break down eventually when it is applied to computer-generated numbers with finite precision. (One way to compare random number generators is to see how long they can continue to survive such a torture test.)

We restart from the correct probabilistic model used in TAOCP for spacings of uniform deviates and avoid introducing any artificial Dirichlet or ordered-spacing representation.

## 1. Correct model of spacings

Let $U_1,\dots,U_n$ be i.i.d. uniform on $(0,1)$. Let

$$
U_{(1)} < \cdots < U_{(n)}
$$

be the order statistics, and define the spacings

$$
S_1 = U_{(1)}, \quad S_i = U_{(i)} - U_{(i-1)} \ (2 \le i \le n), \quad S_{n+1} = 1 - U_{(n)}.
$$

Then the vector

$$
(S_1,\dots,S_{n+1})
$$

has joint density constant on the simplex

$$
S_i \ge 0,\quad \sum_{i=1}^{n+1} S_i = 1.
$$

This is the standard Dirichlet$(1,\dots,1)$ fact used throughout TAOCP.

The exercise, however, works with only the first $n$ spacings, i.e. it suppresses the final gap $S_{n+1}$. We therefore write

$$
S_{n+1} = 1 - \sum_{i=1}^n S_i.
$$

Thus $(S_1,\dots,S_n)$ lives on the simplex

$$
\sum_{i=1}^n S_i \le 1,\quad S_i \ge 0.
$$

## 2. The ordered spacings transformation

Let

$$
S_{(1)} \le \cdots \le S_{(n)}
$$

be the order statistics of $S_1,\dots,S_n$.

Define

$$
S'_{1} = n S_{(1)}, \quad
S'_{2} = (n-1)(S_{(2)} - S_{(1)}), \ \ldots,\ 
S'_{n} = 1\cdot (S_{(n)} - S_{(n-1)}).
$$

Introduce increments

$$
D_j = S_{(j)} - S_{(j-1)}, \quad S_{(0)} = 0.
$$

Then

$$
S'_j = (n-j+1)D_j.
$$

Also note

$$
S_{(j)} = \sum_{i=1}^j D_i.
$$

The map $S \mapsto S'$ is piecewise linear on each permutation region $S_{\sigma(1)} < \cdots < S_{\sigma(n)}$.

## 3. Reduction to the correct simplex structure

Fix a permutation $\sigma$. On the region

$$
S_{\sigma(1)} < \cdots < S_{\sigma(n)},
$$

we have

$$
S_{(j)} = S_{\sigma(j)}.
$$

Thus on this region the transformation becomes a fixed linear map:

$$
S'_{j} = (n-j+1)(S_{\sigma(j)} - S_{\sigma(j-1)}),
\quad S_{\sigma(0)} = 0.
$$

Equivalently, writing in ordered coordinates $x_j = S_{(j)}$,

$$
S'_j = (n-j+1)(x_j - x_{j-1}), \quad x_0=0.
$$

This is an invertible linear transformation from the ordered simplex

$$
0 \le x_1 \le \cdots \le x_n \le 1
$$

onto the standard simplex

$$
S'_j \ge 0,\quad \sum_{j=1}^n S'_j = 1.
$$

The inverse is obtained by summation:

$$
x_j = \sum_{i=1}^j \frac{S'_i}{n-i+1}.
$$

## 4. Verification of simplex preservation

Compute the sum:

$$
\sum_{j=1}^n S'_j
= \sum_{j=1}^n (n-j+1)(x_j - x_{j-1}).
$$

Expand:

$$
= \sum_{j=1}^n (n-j+1)x_j - \sum_{j=1}^n (n-j+1)x_{j-1}.
$$

Shift index in the second sum:

$$
= \sum_{j=1}^n (n-j+1)x_j - \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} (n-j)x_j.
$$

Since $x_0=0$, this becomes

$$
= \sum_{j=1}^{n-1} \big[(n-j+1)-(n-j)\big]x_j + x_n
= \sum_{j=1}^{n-1} x_j + x_n
= x_n.
$$

But in the ordered spacings representation of $n$ uniform points with the final gap suppressed, we have $x_n = 1$. Hence

$$
\sum_{j=1}^n S'_j = 1.
$$

So the image lies in the same simplex.

## 5. Jacobian computation (correct domain)

On each permutation region the transformation is linear. The matrix from $(x_1,\dots,x_n)$ to $(S'_1,\dots,S'_n)$ is lower triangular:

- diagonal entries: $n, n-1, \dots, 1$

Hence determinant:

$$
\det = n!.
$$

Therefore the Jacobian of the inverse map is:

$$
\frac{1}{n!}.
$$

## 6. Distributional invariance

The original unordered spacings $(S_1,\dots,S_n)$ have constant joint density on each permutation region, and there are $n!$ such regions.

Thus the density of the ordered vector $(x_1,\dots,x_n)$ is

$$
n! \quad \text{on } 0<x_1<\cdots<x_n<1.
$$

Under the linear map to $(S'_1,\dots,S'_n)$, densities transform by the Jacobian:

$$
n! \cdot \frac{1}{n!} = 1.
$$

Hence $(S'_1,\dots,S'_n)$ has constant density on the simplex and therefore has the same joint distribution as $(S_1,\dots,S_n)$.

This proves:

$$
(S'_1,\dots,S'_n) \stackrel{d}{=} (S_1,\dots,S_n).
$$

## 7. Cases $n=2$ and $n=3$

### Case $n=2$

We have $x_1 \le x_2$, $x_2=1$.

$$
S'_1 = 2x_1,\quad S'_2 = x_2 - x_1 = 1 - x_1.
$$

Thus

$$
(S'_1,S'_2) = (2x, 1-x),\quad 0<x<1.
$$

This maps the triangular ordered region bijectively onto the full segment $s_1+s_2=1$ with uniform density preserved.

### Case $n=3$

$$
S'_1 = 3x_1,\quad
S'_2 = 2(x_2 - x_1),\quad
S'_3 = x_3 - x_2 = 1 - x_2.
$$

Inverse:

$$
x_1 = \frac{S'_1}{3},\quad
x_2 = \frac{S'_1}{3} + \frac{S'_2}{2},\quad
x_3 = 1.
$$

This is a bijection between the ordered simplex $0<x_1<x_2<1$ and the full simplex $S'_i \ge 0, \sum S'_i=1$, again with constant Jacobian $1/6$.

## 8. Why iteration breaks in finite precision

In exact arithmetic the transformation preserves the uniform simplex distribution.

In floating-point arithmetic:

1. Ordering becomes unstable when two spacings are closer than machine precision, producing ties.
2. Differences $x_j - x_{j-1}$ eventually underflow to zero for small gaps.
3. Once a spacing becomes zero, it remains zero under iteration because subsequent differences eliminate it permanently.
4. The effective dimension of the simplex decreases step by step.
5. The system converges to a degenerate state with a small number of nonzero components, after which the transformation stabilizes.

Thus the process is not ergodic under finite precision and collapses to boundary faces of the simplex.

## Conclusion

The correct argument is:

- The transformation is piecewise linear on permutation regions of the simplex.
- On each region it is an invertible linear map with determinant $n!$.
- The ordered spacings have density $n!$ on the ordered simplex.
- Jacobian cancellation yields a uniform density on the simplex again.

Therefore the transformed spacings have the same joint distribution as the original spacings, while finite precision destroys the continuous symmetry that makes this invariance valid.
