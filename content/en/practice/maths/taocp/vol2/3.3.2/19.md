[Section 3.3.2: Empirical Tests](../)

**Exercise 19.** [*M30*] (J. P. Butler.) Let $U_0, \ldots, U_{n-1}$ be independent random variables having the same distribution. Prove that the expected value of the serial correlation coefficient (23), averaged over all cases with nonzero denominator, is $-1/(n-1)$.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 1m35s  

---

## Setup

Let the serial correlation coefficient of equation (23) be

$$
C=\frac{\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}(U_k-\bar u)(U_{k+1}-\bar u)}
{\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}(U_k-\bar u)^2},
$$

where the subscripts are taken modulo $n$ and

$$
\bar u=\frac1n\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}U_k.
$$

Let

$$
U_k' = U_k-\bar u ,
$$

so that

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}U_k'=0.
$$

Define

$$
N=\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}U_k'U_{k+1}', \qquad
D=\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}(U_k')^2,
$$

hence

$$
C=\frac ND.
$$

The variables $U_0,\ldots,U_{n-1}$ are assumed independent and identically distributed. The denominator is zero precisely when all $U_k$ are equal, by Exercise 18(c). The problem asks for the expected value of $C$, averaged over all cases for which $D\neq0$.

## Solution

Consider the cyclic shifts

$$
T_j=(U_j,U_{j+1},\ldots,U_{j+n-1}),
\qquad 0\le j<n,
$$

with indices interpreted modulo $n$.

Since the random variables are independent and have the same distribution, every cyclic shift has exactly the same probability distribution as the original sequence. Therefore the conditional expectation of the serial correlation coefficient is unchanged by cyclic shift. If $C_j$ denotes the serial correlation coefficient computed from the shifted sequence $T_j$, then

$$
E(C_j\mid D\neq0) =
E(C\mid D\neq0)
\qquad (0\le j<n).
$$

Hence

$$
E(C\mid D\neq0) =
E\!\left(
\frac1n\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}C_j
\,\middle|\,
D\neq0
\right).
\tag{1}
$$

For the shifted sequence $T_j$ the denominator remains $D$, because cyclic permutation does not change the set of values $U_k'$. The numerator becomes

$$
N_j=\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}U_{k+j}'\,U_{k+j+1}'.
$$

Therefore

$$
C_j=\frac{N_j}{D}.
$$

Summing over all shifts gives

$$
\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}C_j =
\frac1D\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}N_j.
\tag{2}
$$

Now

$$
\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}N_j =
\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}
U_{k+j}'\,U_{k+j+1}'.
$$

Replacing $m=k+j$ modulo $n$,

$$
\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}N_j =
\sum_{m=0}^{n-1}\sum_{r=0}^{n-1}
U_m' U_r' -
\sum_{m=0}^{n-1}(U_m')^2.
$$

The first term is

$$
\left(\sum_{m=0}^{n-1}U_m'\right)^2=0,
$$

because the centered variables sum to zero. Thus

$$
\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}N_j =
-D.
\tag{3}
$$

Substituting (3) into (2),

$$
\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}C_j =
-1
\qquad (D\neq0).
\tag{4}
$$

Equation (4) is an identity holding for every realization with nonzero denominator. Consequently,

$$
\frac1n\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}C_j =
-\frac1n.
\tag{5}
$$

At this point one must distinguish the correlation coefficient of equation (23) from the usual sample correlation coefficient. Equation (23) uses the cyclic pairs

$$
(U_0,U_1),\,
(U_1,U_2),\,
\ldots,\,
(U_{n-2},U_{n-1}),\,
(U_{n-1},U_0).
$$

The identity (4) is the standard relation among the $n-1$ nontrivial cyclic lag correlations. Writing

$$
C^{(m)} =
\frac{\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}U_k'U_{k+m}'}
     {\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}(U_k')^2},
\qquad 0\le m<n,
$$

we have

$$
\sum_{m=0}^{n-1}C^{(m)}=0,
$$

since

$$
\sum_{m=0}^{n-1}\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}
U_k'U_{k+m}' =
\left(\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}U_k'\right)^2
=0.
$$

Because

$$
C^{(0)}=1,
$$

it follows that

$$
\sum_{m=1}^{n-1}C^{(m)}=-1.
\tag{6}
$$

The joint distribution is invariant under permutation of the indices, hence for every lag $m\ne0$,

$$
E\!\left(C^{(m)}\mid D\neq0\right) =
E\!\left(C^{(1)}\mid D\neq0\right).
\tag{7}
$$

Taking conditional expectations of (6) and using (7),

$$
(n-1)\,
E\!\left(C^{(1)}\mid D\neq0\right) =
-1.
$$

Therefore

$$
E\!\left(C^{(1)}\mid D\neq0\right) =
-\frac1{n-1}.
$$

Since $C^{(1)}$ is exactly the serial correlation coefficient (23),

$$
\boxed{
E(C\mid D\neq0) =
-\frac1{\,n-1\,}.
}
$$

## Verification

Exercise 18 gives

$$
C=-1 \quad (n=2),
\qquad
C=-\frac12 \quad (n=3),
$$

whenever the denominator is nonzero.

The formula proved above yields

$$
-\frac1{2-1}=-1,
\qquad
-\frac1{3-1}=-\frac12,
$$

which agrees exactly with those special cases.

## Notes

The key identity is

$$
\sum_{m=0}^{n-1}C^{(m)}=0,
$$

which comes directly from

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}U_k'=0.
$$

Exchangeability of the independent identically distributed variables implies that all nonzero lags have the same conditional expectation. Dividing the total contribution $-1$ equally among the $n-1$ nonzero lags yields the result.

This completes the proof.

∎
