[Section 3.3.2: Empirical Tests](../)

**Exercise 1.** [*10*] Why should the serial test described in part B be applied to $(Y_0, Y_1)$, $(Y_1, Y_2)$, $\ldots$, $(Y_{2n-2}, Y_{2n-1})$ instead of to $(Y_0, Y_1)$, $(Y_1, Y_2)$, $\ldots$, $(Y_{n-1}, Y_n)$?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m39s  

---

The pairs used in the serial test should be disjoint. If the observations are

$(Y_0,Y_1),\ (Y_2,Y_3),\ \ldots,\ (Y_{2n-2},Y_{2n-1}),$

then each pair is formed from different random variables, and under the hypothesis that the $Y_j$ are independently distributed, the $n$ observations are themselves independent. The chi-square test is based on this independence.

If instead the observations are

$(Y_0,Y_1),\ (Y_1,Y_2),\ \ldots,\ (Y_{n-1},Y_n),$

successive pairs are not independent, since adjacent observations share a common element. For example, both $(Y_i,Y_{i+1})$ and $(Y_{i+1},Y_{i+2})$ depend on $Y_{i+1}$. The counts in the $d^2$ categories therefore have correlations that are not accounted for by the ordinary chi-square theory. Consequently the usual serial test should be applied to disjoint pairs, not to overlapping pairs. ∎
