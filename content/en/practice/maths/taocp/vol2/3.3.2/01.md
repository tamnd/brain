[Section 3.3.2: Empirical Tests](../)

**Exercise 1.** [*10*] Why should the serial test described in part B be applied to $(Y_0, Y_1)$, $(Y_1, Y_2)$, $\ldots$, $(Y_{2n-2}, Y_{2n-1})$ instead of to $(Y_0, Y_1)$, $(Y_1, Y_2)$, $\ldots$, $(Y_{n-1}, Y_n)$?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 6m52s  

---

The serial test is defined in terms of $n$ observations of pairs that are intended to behave like independent draws from the $d^2$ equally likely categories. If the sequence is split into $2n$ values and grouped as $(Y_{2j}, Y_{2j+1})$ for $0 \le j < n$, each $Y_i$ is used exactly once, so the $n$ pairs are disjoint in their underlying data. Under the hypothesis that the $Y_i$ are independent and uniformly distributed on ${0,\dots,d-1}$, this construction produces $n$ approximately independent observations from the $d^2$-cell multinomial model required for the chi-square approximation.

If instead one uses overlapping pairs $(Y_0,Y_1),(Y_1,Y_2),\ldots,(Y_{n-1},Y_n)$, then adjacent observations share a common variable. This creates dependence between successive category indicators, so the counts in the $d^2$ categories are no longer generated by independent trials. The chi-square test for $k=d^2$ categories assumes independent sampling with fixed probabilities $1/d^2$, and this dependence invalidates that model. The resulting statistic no longer has the chi-square distribution with $d^2-1$ degrees of freedom, since the effective sample consists of correlated observations rather than $n$ independent ones. ∎
