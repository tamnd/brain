[Section 3.3.2: Empirical Tests](../)

**Exercise 1.** [*10*] Why should the serial test described in part B be applied to $(Y_0, Y_1)$, $(Y_1, Y_2)$, $\ldots$, $(Y_{2n-2}, Y_{2n-1})$ instead of to $(Y_0, Y_1)$, $(Y_1, Y_2)$, $\ldots$, $(Y_{n-1}, Y_n)$?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m16s  

---

The pairs $(Y_0,Y_1),(Y_1,Y_2),\ldots,(Y_{n-1},Y_n)$ are not independent observations. Consecutive pairs share a common component, since $(Y_j,Y_{j+1})$ and $(Y_{j+1},Y_{j+2})$ both contain $Y_{j+1}$. The usual chi-square analysis of the serial test assumes that the counts in the various categories arise from independent trials. When overlapping pairs are used, this assumption fails, and the theoretical chi-square distribution no longer applies directly.

For example, if $(Y_j,Y_{j+1})=(q,r)$ has been observed, the next pair is constrained to have first component $r$. Hence the occurrence of one category affects the probabilities of categories in the next observation. By contrast, the pairs $(Y_0,Y_1),(Y_2,Y_3),\ldots,(Y_{2n-2},Y_{2n-1})$ are formed from disjoint sets of random variables. Since the $Y_j$ are assumed to be independently distributed, these pairs are themselves independent and uniformly distributed over the $d^2$ possible categories. This is precisely the situation required for the standard chi-square test used in the serial test.

This completes the proof.
