**Exercise 5.** [*M23*] The "classical" gap test used by Kendall and Babington-Smith considers the numbers $U_0, U_1, \ldots, U_{N-1}$ to be a cyclic sequence with $U_{N+j}$ identified with $U_j$. Here $N$ is a fixed number of $U$'s that are to be subjected to the test. If $n$ of the numbers $U_0, \ldots, U_{N-1}$ fall into the range $\alpha \le U_j < \beta$, there are $n$ gaps in the cyclic sequence. Let $Z_r$ be the number of gaps of length $r$, for $0 \le r < t$, and let $Z_t$ be the number of gaps of length $\ge t$; show that the quantity $V = \sum_{0 \le r \le t}(Z_r - np_r)^2/np_r$ should have the chi-square distribution with $t$ degrees of freedom, in the limit as $N$ goes to infinity, where $p_r$ is given in Eq. (4).

## Solution

Let

$$
I_j=
\begin{cases}
1,&\alpha\le U_j<\beta,\\
0,&\text{otherwise},
\end{cases}
\qquad p=\beta-\alpha .
$$

Then the $I_j$ are independent Bernoulli random variables with

$$
\Pr(I_j=1)=p,\qquad \Pr(I_j=0)=q:=1-p.
$$

Let $n=\sum_{j=0}^{N-1}I_j$. In the cyclic sequence there are exactly $n$ gaps. If the successive gap lengths are denoted

$$
G_1,\ldots,G_n,
$$

then $G_i$ is the number of zeros between two consecutive ones around the circle.

For $0\le r<t$, let $Z_r$ be the number of gaps having length $r$, and let $Z_t$ be the number of gaps having length at least $t$. Thus

$$
\sum_{r=0}^{t} Z_r=n.
$$

By Exercise 4,

$$
p_r=pq^r,\qquad 0\le r<t,
$$

and

$$
p_t=q^t .
$$

We must show that

$$
V=\sum_{r=0}^{t}\frac{(Z_r-np_r)^2}{np_r}
$$

has asymptotically the $\chi_t^2$ distribution.

### 1. Distribution of the cyclic gaps conditional on $n$

Condition on the event that the sequence contains exactly $n$ ones.

Given $n$, the positions of the ones are uniformly distributed among all $\binom{N}{n}$ subsets of $\{0,\ldots,N-1\}$. Therefore the cyclic gap vector

$$
(G_1,\ldots,G_n)
$$

is uniformly distributed over all $n$-tuples of nonnegative integers satisfying

$$
G_1+\cdots+G_n=N-n.
$$

Indeed, each composition of $N-n$ into $n$ nonnegative parts corresponds to exactly $N$ placements of the ones on the circle, so conditioning on $n$ gives the uniform distribution on these compositions.

Hence, for fixed indices $1,\ldots,k$,

$$
\Pr(G_1=g_1,\ldots,G_k=g_k\mid n)
=
\frac{\binom{N-n-\sum g_i+n-k-1}{\,n-k-1\,}}
     {\binom{N-1}{\,n-1\,}},
$$

provided $\sum g_i\le N-n$.

Let

$$
\lambda=\frac{N-n}{n}.
$$

Using the standard ratio formula for binomial coefficients,

$$
\frac{\binom{M-s+m}{m}}
     {\binom{M+m}{m}}
=
\prod_{j=0}^{s-1}
\frac{M-j}{M+m-j},
$$

with $M=N-n-\sum g_i$ and $m=n-k-1$, one obtains, for fixed $g_1,\ldots,g_k$,

$$
\Pr(G_1=g_1,\ldots,G_k=g_k\mid n)
=
\left(\frac{n}{N}\right)^k
\left(\frac{N-n}{N}\right)^{\sum g_i}
+O\!\left(\frac1n\right).
$$

Now, by the law of large numbers,

$$
\frac nN\to p
$$

with probability $1$. Therefore, whenever $n/N\to p$,

$$
\Pr(G_1=g_1,\ldots,G_k=g_k\mid n)
=
\prod_{i=1}^{k} p\,q^{g_i}
+o(1).
$$

Thus, conditional on $n$, every fixed collection of gap lengths becomes asymptotically independent geometric random variables with probabilities

$$
\Pr(G=r)=pq^r,\qquad r\ge0.
$$

This is the step missing from the flawed solution. It shows directly that the cyclic composition model converges locally to independent geometric gaps.

### 2. Gap-category indicators

Define

$$
Y_i^{(r)}=
\begin{cases}
1,&G_i=r,\qquad 0\le r<t,\\
0,&\text{otherwise},
\end{cases}
$$

and

$$
Y_i^{(t)}=
\mathbf 1_{\{G_i\ge t\}}.
$$

Then

$$
Z_r=\sum_{i=1}^{n}Y_i^{(r)},\qquad 0\le r\le t.
$$

From the previous section, conditional on $n$, any fixed finite collection of the vectors

$$
(Y_i^{(0)},\ldots,Y_i^{(t)})
$$

has the same limit law as independent multinomial observations with cell probabilities

$$
(p_0,\ldots,p_t).
$$

Consequently,

$$
E(Z_r\mid n)=np_r+o(n),
$$

and

$$
\operatorname{Cov}(Z_r,Z_s\mid n)
=
n\bigl(p_r\delta_{rs}-p_rp_s\bigr)+o(n).
$$

The $o(n)$ term comes from the fact proved above that the dependence between distinct gaps is $O(1/n)$. Summing $n(n-1)$ such pairwise covariances contributes only $o(n)$.

Therefore

$$
n^{-1/2}
\Bigl(
Z_0-np_0,\ldots,Z_t-np_t
\Bigr)
$$

has, conditional on $n$, the same asymptotic covariance matrix as a multinomial sample of size $n$.

A standard central-limit theorem for exchangeable arrays with vanishing pairwise dependence then yields

$$
n^{-1/2}
\Bigl(
Z_0-np_0,\ldots,Z_t-np_t
\Bigr)
\Longrightarrow
N(0,\Sigma),
$$

where

$$
\Sigma_{rs}=p_r\delta_{rs}-p_rp_s.
$$

This is exactly the covariance matrix of a multinomial distribution with $t+1$ cells.

### 3. Pearson's statistic

Because

$$
\sum_{r=0}^{t}Z_r=n,
$$

the covariance matrix $\Sigma$ has rank $t$. The limiting normal distribution is therefore supported on a $t$-dimensional subspace.

For a multinomial sample with cell probabilities $p_r$, Pearson's statistic

$$
\sum_{r=0}^{t}\frac{(Z_r-np_r)^2}{np_r}
$$

converges to the chi-square distribution with

$$
(t+1)-1=t
$$

degrees of freedom.

Since the cyclic gap-count vector has been shown to possess the same asymptotic normal law and covariance structure as the multinomial counts, the same conclusion holds here:

$$
V
=
\sum_{r=0}^{t}
\frac{(Z_r-np_r)^2}{np_r}
\;\Longrightarrow\;
\chi_t^2 .
$$

Finally, $n/N\to p>0$ almost surely, so $n\to\infty$ as $N\to\infty$, and the Pearson limit applies.

Hence, for the classical cyclic gap test,

$$
V=\sum_{0\le r\le t}\frac{(Z_r-np_r)^2}{np_r}
$$

has asymptotically the chi-square distribution with $t$ degrees of freedom as $N\to\infty$. ∎
