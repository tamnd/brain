**Exercise 5.** [*M23*] The "classical" gap test used by Kendall and Babington-Smith considers the numbers $U_0, U_1, \ldots, U_{N-1}$ to be a cyclic sequence with $U_{N+j}$ identified with $U_j$. Here $N$ is a fixed number of $U$'s that are to be subjected to the test. If $n$ of the numbers $U_0, \ldots, U_{N-1}$ fall into the range $\alpha \le U_j < \beta$, there are $n$ gaps in the cyclic sequence. Let $Z_r$ be the number of gaps of length $r$, for $0 \le r < t$, and let $Z_t$ be the number of gaps of length $\ge t$; show that the quantity $V = \sum_{0 \le r \le t}(Z_r - np_r)^2/np_r$ should have the chi-square distribution with $t$ degrees of freedom, in the limit as $N$ goes to infinity, where $p_r$ is given in Eq. (4).

## Solution

Let $U_0, U_1, \ldots, U_{N-1}$ be a sequence of $N$ real numbers in $[0,1)$, and let $n$ of these numbers satisfy $\alpha \le U_j < \beta$. Consider the sequence cyclic, so that $U_{N+j} = U_j$. The cyclic arrangement implies that there are exactly $n$ gaps between successive occurrences of numbers in $[\alpha, \beta)$.

Denote by $Z_r$ the number of gaps of length $r$ for $0 \le r < t$, and by $Z_t$ the number of gaps of length $\ge t$. By definition of a gap, a gap of length $r$ consists of $r$ consecutive $U$'s outside $[\alpha, \beta)$ followed by a $U$ in $[\alpha, \beta)$.

If the sequence were truly random, each $U_j$ is independently in $[\alpha, \beta)$ with probability $p = \beta - \alpha$, and outside this interval with probability $1 - p$. Therefore, the probability that a randomly chosen gap has length exactly $r$ is

$$
p_r = p (1 - p)^r, \quad 0 \le r \le t - 1,
$$

and the probability that it has length at least $t$ is

$$
p_t = (1 - p)^t,
$$

matching Eq. (4).

Since we are examining the cyclic sequence, the $n$ gaps are disjoint and independent in the limit $N \to \infty$. Denote by $\mathbf{Z} = (Z_0, Z_1, \ldots, Z_t)$ the vector of gap counts. The vector $\mathbf{Z}$ follows a multinomial distribution with $n$ trials and probabilities $(p_0, p_1, \ldots, p_t)$. For a multinomial distribution with $n$ trials and probabilities $p_r$, the standard chi-square statistic

$$
V = \sum_{r=0}^{t} \frac{(Z_r - n p_r)^2}{n p_r}
$$

is asymptotically distributed as chi-square with $t$ degrees of freedom. The degrees of freedom are $t$ rather than $t+1$ because the sum of $Z_r$ is constrained:

$$
\sum_{r=0}^{t} Z_r = n.
$$

Hence, only $t$ counts can vary independently.

Formally, let $\mathbf{Z}$ follow $\text{Multinomial}(n; p_0, \ldots, p_t)$. The covariance matrix $\Sigma$ of $(Z_0, \ldots, Z_{t-1})$ has entries

$$
\text{Cov}(Z_r, Z_s) = \begin{cases}
n p_r (1 - p_r), & r = s,\\
- n p_r p_s, & r \ne s.
\end{cases}
$$

Then, the standardized quadratic form

$$
V = (\mathbf{Z} - n \mathbf{p})^T \Sigma^{-1} (\mathbf{Z} - n \mathbf{p})
$$

reduces to the familiar chi-square statistic above. By the classical result for multinomial asymptotics (see Section 3.3.1), $V$ converges in distribution to $\chi^2_t$ as $n \to \infty$. Since $n/N \to p$ by the law of large numbers, taking $N \to \infty$ ensures $n \to \infty$ and independence of the gap lengths. Therefore, the asymptotic distribution of $V$ is chi-square with $t$ degrees of freedom.

This completes the proof.
