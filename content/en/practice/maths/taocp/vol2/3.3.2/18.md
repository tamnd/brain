**Exercise 18.** [*M20*] (a) Show that if $n = 2$, the serial correlation coefficient (23) is always equal to $-1$ (unless the denominator is zero). (b) Similarly, show that when $n = 3$, the serial correlation coefficient always equals $-\frac{1}{2}$. (c) Show that the denominator in (23) is zero if and only if $U_0 = U_1 = \cdots = U_{n-1}$.

**Verified:** yes

## Solution

In Exercise 3.3.2.18, equation (23) is the serial correlation coefficient with the sequence treated cyclically:

$$
C=\frac{\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}(U_k-\bar U)(U_{k+1}-\bar U)}
       {\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}(U_k-\bar U)^2},
\qquad
\bar U=\frac1n\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}U_k,
$$

where the subscripts are taken modulo $n$, so that $U_n=U_0$.

Let

$$
x_k=U_k-\bar U .
$$

Then

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}x_k=0,
$$

and

$$
C=\frac{\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}x_kx_{k+1}}
        {\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}x_k^2}.
$$

### (a) Case $n=2$

Let

$$
x_0=U_0-\bar U,\qquad x_1=U_1-\bar U.
$$

Since $x_0+x_1=0$,

$$
x_1=-x_0.
$$

The numerator is

$$
x_0x_1+x_1x_0
=2x_0x_1
=-2x_0^2.
$$

The denominator is

$$
x_0^2+x_1^2
=x_0^2+x_0^2
=2x_0^2.
$$

Hence, provided the denominator is nonzero,

$$
C=\frac{-2x_0^2}{2x_0^2}=-1.
$$

Thus the serial correlation coefficient is always $-1$ when $n=2$.

### (b) Case $n=3$

Let

$$
x_0=U_0-\bar U,\qquad
x_1=U_1-\bar U,\qquad
x_2=U_2-\bar U.
$$

Then

$$
x_0+x_1+x_2=0.
$$

Squaring this identity gives

$$
0=(x_0+x_1+x_2)^2
=x_0^2+x_1^2+x_2^2
 +2(x_0x_1+x_1x_2+x_2x_0).
$$

Therefore

$$
x_0x_1+x_1x_2+x_2x_0
=-\frac12\,(x_0^2+x_1^2+x_2^2).
$$

But the numerator of $C$ is precisely

$$
x_0x_1+x_1x_2+x_2x_0,
$$

and the denominator is

$$
x_0^2+x_1^2+x_2^2.
$$

Hence, whenever the denominator is nonzero,

$$
C =
\frac{-\frac12(x_0^2+x_1^2+x_2^2)}
     {x_0^2+x_1^2+x_2^2}
=-\frac12.
$$

Thus the serial correlation coefficient is always

$$
\boxed{-\frac12}
$$

when $n=3$.

### (c) When is the denominator zero?

The denominator of (23) is

$$
D=\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}(U_k-\bar U)^2.
$$

Each term in this sum is a square and is therefore nonnegative.

If $D=0$, a sum of nonnegative terms can vanish only if every term is zero. Hence

$$
(U_k-\bar U)^2=0
$$

for every $k$, so

$$
U_k=\bar U
$$

for every $k$. Therefore

$$
U_0=U_1=\cdots=U_{n-1}.
$$

Conversely, if

$$
U_0=U_1=\cdots=U_{n-1}=a,
$$

then $\bar U=a$, and every term $U_k-\bar U$ is zero. Hence

$$
D=\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}0^2=0.
$$

Therefore

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}(U_k-\bar U)^2=0
$$

if and only if

$$
U_0=U_1=\cdots=U_{n-1}.
$$

This proves part (c). ∎
