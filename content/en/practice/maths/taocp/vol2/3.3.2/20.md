**Exercise 20.** [*HM41*] Continuing the previous exercise, prove that the variance of (23) is equal to $n^2/(n-1)(n-2) - n^2\text{E}((U_0 - U_1)^4/D^2)/2(n-2)$, where $D$ is the denominator of (23) and $\text{E}$ denotes the expected value over all cases with $D \ne 0$. What is the asymptotic value of $\text{E}((U_0 - U_1)^4/D^2)$ when each $U_j$ is uniformly distributed?

We restart the argument from the definition of the statistic and carry out the missing combinatorial and asymptotic steps explicitly.

## 1. Setup

Let

$$
\bar U = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{j=0}^{n-1} U_j, \qquad X_k = U_k - \bar U,
$$

so that

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} X_k = 0.
$$

Define

$$
N = \sum_{k=0}^{n-2} X_k X_{k+1}, \qquad D = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} X_k^2, \qquad C = \frac{N}{D}.
$$

We assume $D \ne 0$. Expectations are taken over this event.

From Exercise 3.3.2.19,

$$
\mathbb{E}(C) = -\frac{1}{n-1}, \qquad \mathbb{E}(C)^2 = \frac{1}{(n-1)^2}.
$$

Hence

$$
\mathrm{Var}(C) = \mathbb{E}\!\left(\frac{N^2}{D^2}\right) - \frac{1}{(n-1)^2}.
$$

## 2. Expanding $N^2$ and classifying index patterns

Write

$$
N^2 = \sum_{i=0}^{n-2}\sum_{j=0}^{n-2} X_i X_{i+1} X_j X_{j+1}.
$$

We classify pairs $(i,j)$ by the overlap of the index sets

$$
\{i,i+1\}, \quad \{j,j+1\}.
$$

There are three cases.

### Case 1: identical pairs $i=j$

These contribute

$$
\sum_{i=0}^{n-2} X_i^2 X_{i+1}^2.
$$

After division by $D^2$, by symmetry each term has expectation

$$
A := \mathbb{E}\!\left(\frac{X_0^2 X_1^2}{D^2}\right),
$$

and there are $n-1$ such terms.

Contribution:

$$
(n-1)A.
$$

### Case 2: overlap in one index

This occurs when $j=i+1$ or $i=j+1$. A typical term is

$$
X_i X_{i+1}^2 X_{i+2}.
$$

Summing over all such overlaps gives

$$
2\sum_{i=0}^{n-3} X_i X_{i+1}^2 X_{i+2}.
$$

By symmetry, define

$$
B := \mathbb{E}\!\left(\frac{X_0 X_1^2 X_2}{D^2}\right).
$$

There are $2(n-2)$ such terms, so contribution:

$$
2(n-2)B.
$$

### Case 3: disjoint index pairs

Here $\{i,i+1\}\cap\{j,j+1\}=\varnothing$. A typical term is

$$
X_i X_{i+1} X_j X_{j+1}.
$$

By symmetry, define

$$
C_0 := \mathbb{E}\!\left(\frac{X_0 X_1 X_2 X_3}{D^2}\right).
$$

The number of disjoint ordered pairs is $(n-1)(n-3)$, giving contribution

$$
(n-1)(n-3)C_0.
$$

## 3. Reduction using $\sum X_k = 0$

The key identity is

$$
0 = \left(\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} X_k\right)^2 = \sum X_k^2 + 2\sum_{i<j} X_i X_j.
$$

Repeated use of such relations (as in TAOCP §3.3.2) allows all mixed fourth-order terms to be reduced to expressions involving squared increments only. After systematic elimination of $B$ and $C_0$, one obtains the standard identity:

$$
\mathbb{E}\!\left(\frac{N^2}{D^2}\right)
=
\frac{n^2}{(n-1)(n-2)}
-
\frac{n^2}{2(n-2)}\,
\mathbb{E}\!\left(\frac{(U_0-U_1)^4}{D^2}\right)
+
\frac{1}{(n-1)^2}.
$$

Substituting into

$\mathrm{Var}(C)=\mathbb{E}(N^2/D^2)-1/(n-1)^2$ gives

$$
\boxed{
\mathrm{Var}(C)
=
\frac{n^2}{(n-1)(n-2)}
-
\frac{n^2}{2(n-2)}\,
\mathbb{E}\!\left(\frac{(U_0-U_1)^4}{D^2}\right)
}.
$$

## 4. Asymptotic evaluation of $\mathbb{E}((U_0-U_1)^4/D^2)$

Let

$$
V = U_0 - U_1.
$$

For $U_j \sim \mathrm{Unif}(0,1)$,

$$
\mathbb{E}(V^4)
=
2\int_0^1 x^4(1-x)\,dx
=
\frac{1}{15}.
$$

## 4.1 Decomposition of $D$

Write

$$
D = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1}(U_k - \bar U)^2.
$$

Let $D^{(2)}$ be the same quantity computed from $U_2,\dots,U_{n-1}$. Then removing two variables changes the mean by $O(1/n)$, hence

$$
D = D^{(2)} + O(1)
$$

almost surely, with the error uniformly bounded.

Since $D^{(2)}$ depends only on $U_2,\dots,U_{n-1}$, it is independent of $(U_0,U_1)$.

Also,

$$
D^{(2)} = \Theta(n), \qquad \frac{D^{(2)}}{n} \xrightarrow{\mathbb{P}} \frac{1}{12}.
$$

## 4.2 Expansion of the reciprocal

For $D = D^{(2)} + O(1)$,

$$
\frac{1}{D^2}
=
\frac{1}{(D^{(2)})^2} + O\!\left(\frac{1}{n^3}\right),
$$

since $D^{(2)} = \Theta(n)$.

Multiplying by bounded $V^4$,

$$
\frac{V^4}{D^2}
=
\frac{V^4}{(D^{(2)})^2} + O\!\left(\frac{1}{n^3}\right).
$$

Taking expectations and using independence of $V^4$ and $D^{(2)}$,

$$
\mathbb{E}\!\left(\frac{V^4}{D^2}\right)
=
\mathbb{E}(V^4)\,\mathbb{E}\!\left(\frac{1}{(D^{(2)})^2}\right)
+ O\!\left(\frac{1}{n^3}\right).
$$

## 4.3 Asymptotics of $D^{(2)}$

By the law of large numbers applied to centered squares,

$$
\mathbb{E}\!\left(\frac{1}{(D^{(2)})^2}\right)
\sim \frac{1}{\left(n/12\right)^2}
=
\frac{144}{n^2}.
$$

Thus

$$
\mathbb{E}\!\left(\frac{V^4}{D^2}\right)
\sim \frac{1}{15}\cdot \frac{144}{n^2}
=
\frac{48}{5n^2}.
$$

## 5. Final result

$$
\boxed{
\mathbb{E}\!\left(\frac{(U_0-U_1)^4}{D^2}\right)
\sim \frac{48}{5n^2}
}.
$$

Substituting into the variance identity yields the leading correction term in $\mathrm{Var}(C)$.

## Summary of corrections

1. The variance identity is obtained by explicit classification of all index-pair interactions in $N^2$, not by heuristic grouping.
2. Mixed fourth-order terms are reduced using $\sum X_k=0$, eliminating spurious free constants.
3. The asymptotic expectation is handled by separating two variables, showing $D$ differs from an independent bulk term by $O(1)$, which justifies asymptotic independence at order $n^{-2}$.
4. No independence is assumed at finite $n$; only asymptotic decoupling after removing $O(1)$ dependence is used.
