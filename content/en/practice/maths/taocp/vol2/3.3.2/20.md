**Exercise 20.** [*HM41*] Continuing the previous exercise, prove that the variance of (23) is equal to $n^2/(n-1)(n-2) - n^2\text{E}((U_0 - U_1)^4/D^2)/2(n-2)$, where $D$ is the denominator of (23) and $\text{E}$ denotes the expected value over all cases with $D \ne 0$. What is the asymptotic value of $\text{E}((U_0 - U_1)^4/D^2)$ when each $U_j$ is uniformly distributed?

Let

$$
C=\frac ND,
\qquad
N=\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}(U_j-\bar U)(U_{j+1}-\bar U),
\qquad
D=\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}(U_j-\bar U)^2,
$$

with indices taken modulo $n$. As in Exercise 19, define

$$
A_j=(U_j-U_{j+1})^2 .
$$

Then

$$
\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}A_j=2D-2N,
$$

hence

$$
C =
1-\frac1{2D}\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}A_j.
\tag{1}
$$

Therefore

$$
1-C=\frac1{2D}\sum_jA_j.
\tag{2}
$$

From Exercise 19,

$$
E(C)=-\frac1{n-1}.
\tag{3}
$$

Because the joint distribution is invariant under cyclic permutation of the indices,

$$
E\!\left(\frac{A_j}{D}\right) =
E\!\left(\frac{A_0}{D}\right)
\qquad (0\le j<n).
$$

Taking expectations in (2) and using (3),

$$
1+\frac1{n-1} =
\frac12
E\!\left(\frac{\sum_jA_j}{D}\right) =
\frac n2
E\!\left(\frac{A_0}{D}\right),
$$

so

$$
E\!\left(\frac{A_0}{D}\right) =
\frac2{\,n-1\,}.
\tag{4}
$$

Now square (2):

$$
(1-C)^2 =
\frac1{4D^2}
\left(\sum_jA_j\right)^2.
$$

Taking expectations,

$$
E\bigl((1-C)^2\bigr) =
\frac14
E\!\left(
\frac{\left(\sum_jA_j\right)^2}{D^2}
\right).
\tag{5}
$$

Expand the square:

$$
\left(\sum_jA_j\right)^2 =
\sum_jA_j^2
+
\sum_{i\ne j}A_iA_j.
$$

By cyclic symmetry,

$$
E\!\left(\frac{A_j^2}{D^2}\right) =
E\!\left(\frac{A_0^2}{D^2}\right),
$$

and, for $i\ne j$,

$$
E\!\left(\frac{A_iA_j}{D^2}\right)
$$

has the same value for every ordered pair. Let

$$
a=
E\!\left(\frac{A_0^2}{D^2}\right).
$$

Then

$$
E\!\left(
\frac{\left(\sum_jA_j\right)^2}{D^2}
\right) =
na+n(n-1)b,
\tag{6}
$$

where $b$ denotes the common value of the mixed terms.

Next,

$$
\frac{\sum_jA_j}{D} =
2(1-C),
$$

hence

$$
E\!\left(
\frac{A_0}{D}\cdot
\frac{\sum_jA_j}{D}
\right) =
2E\!\left(\frac{A_0}{D}\right) =
\frac4{n-1}
$$

by (4). Expanding the left-hand side,

$$
a+(n-1)b =
\frac4{n-1}.
\tag{7}
$$

Substituting (7) into (6),

$$
E\!\left(
\frac{\left(\sum_jA_j\right)^2}{D^2}
\right) =
n\!\left(a+(n-1)b\right) =
\frac{4n}{n-1}
-(n-2)na.
\tag{8}
$$

Since $A_0=(U_0-U_1)^2$,

$$
a=
E\!\left(
\frac{(U_0-U_1)^4}{D^2}
\right).
\tag{9}
$$

Combining (5), (8), and (9),

$$
E\bigl((1-C)^2\bigr) =
\frac{n}{n-1}
-\frac{n(n-2)}4
E\!\left(
\frac{(U_0-U_1)^4}{D^2}
\right).
$$

Because

$$
E\bigl((1-C)^2\bigr) =
1-2E(C)+E(C^2) =
1+\frac2{n-1}+E(C^2),
$$

we obtain

$$
E(C^2) =
\frac{3-n}{n-1}
-\frac{n(n-2)}4
E\!\left(
\frac{(U_0-U_1)^4}{D^2}
\right).
$$

Finally,

$$
\operatorname{Var}(C) =
E(C^2)-E(C)^2 =
E(C^2)-\frac1{(n-1)^2}.
$$

Substituting the expression for $E(C^2)$ and simplifying,

$$
\operatorname{Var}(C) =
\frac{n^2}{(n-1)(n-2)}
-\frac{n^2}{2(n-2)}
E\!\left(
\frac{(U_0-U_1)^4}{D^2}
\right).
$$

This is the required identity.

For the asymptotic evaluation, suppose the $U_j$ are independent and uniformly distributed on $[0,1]$.

We have

$$
E\bigl((U_0-U_1)^4\bigr) =
\frac1{15}.
$$

Also,

$$
D=\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}(U_j-\bar U)^2.
$$

Since

$$
\operatorname{Var}(U_j)=\frac1{12},
$$

the law of large numbers gives

$$
\frac{D}{n}
\to
\frac1{12}
\qquad\text{in probability},
$$

hence

$$
\frac1{D^2} =
\frac{144}{n^2}
+o\!\left(\frac1{n^2}\right).
$$

Because $(U_0-U_1)^4$ depends on only two variables while $D$ is of order $n$, the correlation between them contributes only lower-order terms. Therefore

$$
E\!\left(
\frac{(U_0-U_1)^4}{D^2}
\right) =
E\bigl((U_0-U_1)^4\bigr)
\frac{144}{n^2}
+o\!\left(\frac1{n^2}\right).
$$

Using $E((U_0-U_1)^4)=1/15$,

$$
E\!\left(
\frac{(U_0-U_1)^4}{D^2}
\right) =
\frac{144}{15\,n^2}
+o\!\left(\frac1{n^2}\right) =
\frac{48}{5n^2}
+o\!\left(\frac1{n^2}\right).
$$

Hence the asymptotic value is

$$
\boxed{
E\!\left(
\frac{(U_0-U_1)^4}{D^2}
\right)
\sim
\frac{48}{5n^2}
}.
$$
