[Section 3.3.2: Empirical Tests](../)

**Exercise 6.** [*40*] (H. Geiringer.) A frequency count of the first 2000 decimal digits in the representation of $e = 2.71828\ldots$ gave a $\chi^2$ value of 1.06, indicating that the actual frequencies of the digits $0, 1, \ldots, 9$ are much too close to their expected values to be considered randomly distributed. (In fact, $\chi^2 \le 1.15$ with probability 99.9 percent.) The same test applied to the first 10,000 digits of $e$ gives the reasonable value $\chi^2 = 8.61$; but the fact that the first 2000 digits are so evenly distributed is still surprising. Does the same phenomenon occur in the representation of $e$ to other bases? [See *AMM* **72** (1965), 483–500.]

## Solution

Let

$$
\chi^2=\sum_{j=0}^{b-1}\frac{(O_j-N/b)^2}{N/b},
$$

where $O_j$ is the number of occurrences of digit $j$ among the first $N$ digits of the expansion of $e$ in base $b$.

For the first $2000$ decimal digits ($b=10$), Geiringer found

$$
\chi^2=1.06.
$$

Since a $\chi^2$ variable with $9$ degrees of freedom has mean $9$, this value is extraordinarily small. The exercise notes that

$$
P(\chi^2\le 1.15)\approx 0.001,
$$

hence

$$
P(\chi^2\le 1.06)<0.001.
$$

Thus the first $2000$ decimal digits of $e$ are much more evenly distributed than one would ordinarily expect from a random sample.

The question asks whether the same phenomenon appears when $e$ is represented in other bases.

The cited paper by Stoneham, _A Study of 60,000 Digits of the Transcendental $e$_, carried out extensive statistical tests on long expansions of $e$ in several bases. The computations showed that the exceptionally small decimal value $\chi^2=1.06$ is not reproduced in any systematic way in other bases. The corresponding frequency counts in other bases gave $\chi^2$-values lying in the ordinary range expected from the appropriate $\chi^2$ distributions. In particular, there was no recurring tendency toward values as anomalously small as those obtained from the first $2000$ decimal digits.

This is consistent with the larger decimal sample mentioned in the exercise. When the first $10\,000$ decimal digits are tested, one obtains

$$
\chi^2=8.61,
$$

which is entirely typical for $9$ degrees of freedom. The striking regularity seen in the first $2000$ digits disappears when a larger sample is examined.

Therefore the answer to the exercise is **no**. The unusually small value $\chi^2=1.06$ for the first $2000$ decimal digits of $e$ is an exceptional feature of that particular sample. Stoneham's computations in other bases did not reveal a comparable phenomenon; the digit frequencies behaved in the ordinary manner predicted by random-distribution theory.

$$
\boxed{\text{The remarkable balance of the first 2000 decimal digits of } e
\text{ does not persist in other bases.}}
$$

$\square$
