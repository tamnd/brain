**Exercise 6.** [*40*] (H. Geiringer.) A frequency count of the first 2000 decimal digits in the representation of $e = 2.71828\ldots$ gave a $\chi^2$ value of 1.06, indicating that the actual frequencies of the digits $0, 1, \ldots, 9$ are much too close to their expected values to be considered randomly distributed. (In fact, $\chi^2 \le 1.15$ with probability 99.9 percent.) The same test applied to the first 10,000 digits of $e$ gives the reasonable value $\chi^2 = 8.61$; but the fact that the first 2000 digits are so evenly distributed is still surprising. Does the same phenomenon occur in the representation of $e$ to other bases? [See *AMM* **72** (1965), 483–500.]

## Setup

Let

$$
e = 2.718281828\ldots
$$

and let the first $N$ digits of its representation in base $b$ be subjected to the frequency test of Section 3.3.2A. If the digits behave like independent uniformly distributed symbols from the set

$$
\{0,1,\ldots,b-1\},
$$

the expected frequency of each digit among the first $N$ digits is $N/b$, and the corresponding chi-square statistic is

$$
\chi^2 =
\sum_{r=0}^{b-1}
\frac{(f_r-N/b)^2}{N/b},
$$

where $f_r$ denotes the observed frequency of digit $r$.

The exercise asks whether the anomalously small value

$$
\chi^2=1.06
$$

obtained from the first $2000$ decimal digits of $e$ is a phenomenon that also appears when $e$ is written in other bases.

## Solution

The exercise refers to the investigation of R. G. Stoneham, who studied $60,000$ digits of $e$ and applied frequency and related statistical tests in a variety of bases. Stoneham's computations showed that the exceptionally small chi-square value obtained from the first $2000$ decimal digits is not a persistent feature of the expansion of $e$; it is an isolated fluctuation occurring at that particular truncation point.

To understand why this is surprising, consider the frequency test with $b$ categories. Under the randomness hypothesis, the statistic $\chi^2$ should be distributed approximately as a chi-square random variable with $b-1$ degrees of freedom. In base $10$ this gives $9$ degrees of freedom, whose mean is

$$
E(\chi^2)=9.
$$

A value as small as $1.06$ lies in the extreme lower tail of that distribution. The statement quoted in the exercise, that $\chi^2\le 1.15$ occurs with probability about $0.001$, expresses precisely how unusual this event is.

Stoneham's computations in other bases did not reveal a corresponding systematic tendency toward abnormally small chi-square values. The frequency statistics fluctuated in the manner expected from ordinary sampling variation. The decimal value $1.06$ for the first $2000$ digits is therefore not evidence of a special base-independent regularity in the digits of $e$; it is a rare local phenomenon.

The conclusion is that the same phenomenon does **not** occur consistently in other bases. Statistical tests applied to sufficiently long expansions of $e$ in various bases produce values compatible with the behavior expected of a random sequence of digits, and the remarkable value obtained from the first $2000$ decimal digits appears to be an exceptional accident rather than a persistent property of the number.

This completes the proof.

∎
