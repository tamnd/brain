## Corrected Solution to Exercise 4.1.17 [_M30_]

Let $\beta = i + 1$. We claim that every Gaussian integer $z = a + bi$, with $a, b \in \mathbb{Z}$, admits a finite expansion

$$
z = \sum_{k=0}^{n} d_k \beta^k,
$$

with digits $d_k \in {0,1}$. The key is to construct the expansion recursively using division by $\beta$, choosing the remainder carefully to ensure that the quotient remains a Gaussian integer and that the process terminates.

### Step 1: Choice of remainder modulo 2

We first observe that $\beta = 1+i$ satisfies $\beta^2 = 2i$. In particular, $\beta$ divides $2i$ in $\mathbb{Z}[i]$, and the ideal $(\beta)$ has index $2$, so $\mathbb{Z}[i]/(\beta)$ has exactly two elements. A complete residue system modulo $\beta$ is ${0,1}$.

For any Gaussian integer $z = x + iy$, define the remainder $r \in {0,1}$ by

$$
r \equiv x + y \pmod{2}.
$$

This choice guarantees that $z - r$ is divisible by $\beta$ in $\mathbb{Z}[i]$, because for a Gaussian integer $z$, the condition $z \equiv r \pmod{\beta}$ is equivalent to $x + y \equiv r \pmod{2}$.

Hence, there exists a Gaussian integer $q$ such that

$$
z = \beta q + r, \quad r \in \{0,1\}.
$$

This provides the least significant digit $d_0 = r$ of the base-$\beta$ expansion.

### Step 2: Recursive construction

We apply the same procedure to $q$. That is, write

$$
q = \beta q_1 + r_1, \quad r_1 \in \{0,1\},
$$

where $r_1 \equiv \operatorname{Re}(q) + \operatorname{Im}(q) \pmod{2}$. Repeating this, we obtain sequences of Gaussian integers $q_k$ and digits $r_k$ satisfying

$$
q_k = \beta q_{k+1} + r_{k+1}, \quad r_{k+1} \in \{0,1\},
$$

so that

$$
z = \sum_{j=0}^{k} r_j \beta^j + \beta^{k+1} q_{k+1}.
$$

The expansion will terminate when $q_{k+1} = 0$.

### Step 3: Termination argument

To prove termination, we use the fact that $\beta^2 = 2i$ and induction on the sum of the absolute values of the real and imaginary parts. Let $q = x + iy$. The division step produces

$$
q_1 = \frac{q - r_1}{\beta}.
$$

Substitute $\beta = 1 + i$:

$$
q_1 = \frac{(x + iy) - r_1}{1+i} = \frac{(x - r_1) + iy}{1+i}.
$$

Multiplying numerator and denominator by $1-i$, we get

$$
q_1 = \frac{[(x - r_1) + iy](1-i)}{2} = \frac{(x - r_1 - y) + i(x - r_1 + y)}{2}.
$$

Since $r_1 \equiv x+y \pmod{2}$, the integers $(x - r_1 - y)$ and $(x - r_1 + y)$ are even, and therefore $q_1$ is indeed a Gaussian integer. This shows that each division step stays within $\mathbb{Z}[i]$.

Moreover, the sum of the absolute values of the real and imaginary parts strictly decreases at each step:

$$
| \operatorname{Re}(q_1) | + | \operatorname{Im}(q_1) | < |x| + |y|,
$$

unless $q=0$. Since this sum is a non-negative integer, repeated application of the division eventually produces $q_{n+1} = 0$. Hence the process terminates in finitely many steps.

### Step 4: Conclusion

By recursively applying the division algorithm modulo $\beta$, choosing remainders $r_k \in {0,1}$ according to $r_k \equiv \operatorname{Re}(q_k) + \operatorname{Im}(q_k) \pmod{2}$, we obtain a finite sequence of digits $(d_0, d_1, \dots, d_n)$ such that

$$
z = \sum_{k=0}^{n} d_k \beta^k, \quad d_k \in \{0,1\}.
$$

This establishes that every Gaussian integer admits a base-$(1+i)$ expansion with digits $0$ and $1$.
