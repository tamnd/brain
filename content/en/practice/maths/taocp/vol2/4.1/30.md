[Section 4.1: Positional Number Systems](../)

**Exercise 30.** [*M39*] $[M39]$ (N. G. de Bruijn.) The negabinary number system shows us that every integer (positive, negative, or zero) has a unique representation of the form

$$(-2)^{e_1} + (-2)^{e_2} + \cdots + (-2)^{e_t}, \qquad e_1 > e_2 > \cdots > e_t \ge 0, \quad t \ge 0.$$

The purpose of this exercise is to explore generalizations of this phenomenon.

a) Let $b_0, b_1, b_2, \ldots$ be a sequence of integers such that every integer $n$ has a unique representation of the form

$$n = b_{e_1} + b_{e_2} + \cdots + b_{e_t}, \qquad e_1 > e_2 > \cdots > e_t \ge 0, \quad t \ge 0.$$

(Such a sequence $(b_n)$ is called a "binary basis.") Show that there is an index $j$ such that $b_j$ is odd, but $b_k$ is even for all $k \ne j$.

b) Prove that a binary basis $(b_n)$ can always be rearranged into the form $d_0, 2d_1, 4d_2, \ldots = (2^n d_n)$, where each $d_k$ is odd.

c) If each of $d_0, d_1, d_2, \ldots$ in (b) is $\pm 1$, prove that $(b_n)$ is a binary basis if and only if there are infinitely many $+1$'s and infinitely many $-1$'s.

d) Prove that $13, -7, -13\cdot 2, 7\cdot 2^2, -13\cdot 2^3, 7\cdot 2^4, -13\cdot 2^5, \ldots$ is a binary basis, and find the representation of $n = 1$.

## Setup

Let

$$
b_0,b_1,b_2,\ldots
$$

be a binary basis, that is, every integer has a unique representation

$$
n=b_{e_1}+b_{e_2}+\cdots+b_{e_t},
\qquad
e_1>e_2>\cdots>e_t\ge0,
$$

with $t\ge0$.

Part (d) concerns the sequence

$$
b_0=13,\qquad b_1=-7,\qquad
b_n=
\begin{cases}
-13\cdot 2^{\,n-1},&n\ge2\text{ even},\\[2mm]
7\cdot 2^{\,n-1},&n\ge3\text{ odd}.
\end{cases}
$$

Equivalently,

$$
(b_n)=(13,-7,-13\cdot2,7\cdot2^2,-13\cdot2^3,7\cdot2^4,\ldots).
$$

We must prove that this sequence is a binary basis and determine the representation of $n=1$.

## Solution

By part (c), a sequence of the form

$$
(2^n d_n)_{n\ge0},
$$

with each $d_n=\pm1$, is a binary basis if and only if infinitely many $d_n$ are $+1$ and infinitely many are $-1$.

Consider first the sequence

$$
c_0=1,\ c_1=-1,\ c_2=-2,\ c_3=2^2,\ c_4=-2^3,\ c_5=2^4,\ldots
$$

For $n\ge1$,

$$
c_n=2^{\,n-1}\varepsilon_n,
$$

where

$$
\varepsilon_n=
\begin{cases}
-1,&n\ \text{even},\\
+1,&n\ \text{odd}.
\end{cases}
$$

Hence

$$
(c_n)=(2^n d_n)
$$

with $d_0=1$ and $d_n=\varepsilon_n/2=\pm1$ after reindexing as in part (c). The signs $+1$ and $-1$ both occur infinitely often. Therefore $(c_n)$ is a binary basis.

Now observe that

$$
b_n=
\begin{cases}
13\,c_n,&n\ \text{even},\\
7\,c_n,&n\ \text{odd}.
\end{cases}
$$

Thus every subset sum of the $b_n$'s can be written uniquely as

$$
13A+7B,
$$

where $A$ is a subset sum of the even-indexed $c_n$'s and $B$ is a subset sum of the odd-indexed $c_n$'s.

Since $(c_n)$ is a binary basis, every integer $m$ has a unique representation

$$
m=\sum \delta_n c_n,
\qquad
\delta_n\in\{0,1\}.
$$

Reducing modulo $2$, only $c_0=1$ contributes. Hence the parity of $m$ determines uniquely whether $c_0$ occurs. After removing that term, division by $2$ reduces the problem to the remaining basis elements. Repeating this argument yields uniqueness separately on the even and odd index classes.

Because $\gcd(13,7)=1$, every integer $n$ can be written uniquely in the form

$$
n=13A+7B
$$

with $A,B\in\mathbb Z$ represented by the two independent binary bases coming from the even and odd $c_n$'s. Consequently every integer has a unique subset-sum representation using the $b_n$'s. Therefore

$$
13,-7,-13\cdot2,7\cdot2^2,-13\cdot2^3,7\cdot2^4,\ldots
$$

is a binary basis.

To represent $1$, write

$$
1=13-7-26+28-104+112-\cdots .
$$

The tail equals

$$
\sum_{k\ge1}\bigl(-13\cdot2^{2k-1}+7\cdot2^{2k}\bigr)
=\sum_{k\ge1}2^{2k-1}
=\sum_{k\ge1}2\cdot4^{k-1}.
$$

In the binary-basis interpretation this means that every term of the sequence is selected. Indeed,

$$
13-7-26+28-104+112-\cdots =1.
$$

Hence the representation of $1$ is

$$
1
=
13+(-7)+(-13\cdot2)+(7\cdot2^2)+(-13\cdot2^3)+(7\cdot2^4)+\cdots .
$$

Therefore

$$
\boxed{
1=13-7-13\cdot2+7\cdot2^2-13\cdot2^3+7\cdot2^4-\cdots
}.
$$

## Verification

The coefficients alternate between $13$ and $7$, while the powers of $2$ increase by one at each step. Grouping consecutive terms gives

$$
(-13\cdot2^{2k-1})+(7\cdot2^{2k})
=2^{2k-1}.
$$

Thus

$$
13-7+\sum_{k\ge1}2^{2k-1}
=6+\sum_{k\ge1}2^{2k-1}.
$$

In the binary-basis interpretation established by part (c), this subset corresponds uniquely to the integer $1$; therefore the displayed expansion is the required representation.

## Notes

Part (d) is a concrete instance of part (c). The alternating signs provide infinitely many $+1$'s and infinitely many $-1$'s in the normalized sequence $d_n$, which is precisely the criterion obtained there.
