[Section 4.1: Positional Number Systems](../)

**Exercise 25.** [*M25*] (S. A. Cook.) Let $b$, $u$, and $v$ be positive integers, where $b \ge 2$ and $0 < v < b^u$. Show that the radix-$b$ representation of $u/v$ does not contain a run of $m$ consecutive digits equal to $b - 1$, anywhere to the right of the radix point. (By convention, the runs of infinitely many $(b-1)$'s are permitted in the standard radix-$b$ representation.)

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m50s  

---

## Solution

Let

$$
x=\frac{u}{v},
$$

where $b\ge2$, $u>0$, $v>0$, and

$$
0<v<b^u.
$$

Assume that the standard radix-$b$ representation of $x$ contains a run of $m$ consecutive digits equal to $b-1$ somewhere to the right of the radix point. Thus there exist integers $r\ge1$ and $m\ge1$ such that

$$
x=\frac{A}{b^r}
   +\frac{b-1}{b^{r+1}}
   +\frac{b-1}{b^{r+2}}
   +\cdots
   +\frac{b-1}{b^{r+m}}
   +\theta,
$$

where $A$ is an integer and

$$
0\le\theta<\frac1{b^{r+m}}.
$$

The inequality for $\theta$ expresses the fact that the run of $(b-1)$'s ends after exactly $m$ positions.

Since

$$
\frac{b-1}{b^{r+1}}+\cdots+\frac{b-1}{b^{r+m}}
=\frac1{b^r}-\frac1{b^{r+m}},
$$

we have

$$
x=\frac{A+1}{b^r}-\frac1{b^{r+m}}+\theta.
$$

Hence

$$
0<
\frac{A+1}{b^r}-x
<
\frac1{b^{r+m}}.
$$

Multiplying by $b^r$ gives

$$
0<
(A+1)-b^r x
<
\frac1{b^m}.
$$

Substituting $x=u/v$,

$$
0<
(A+1)-\frac{u\,b^r}{v}
<
\frac1{b^m}.
$$

Multiplying by $v$ yields

$$
0<
v(A+1)-u\,b^r
<
\frac{v}{b^m}.
$$

The quantity

$$
N=v(A+1)-u\,b^r
$$

is an integer. Therefore

$$
0<N<\frac{v}{b^m}.
$$

An integer strictly between $0$ and a positive number can exist only if that positive number exceeds $1$; consequently

$$
\frac{v}{b^m}>1,
$$

so

$$
b^m<v.
$$

Since $v<b^u$, it follows that

$$
b^m<b^u,
$$

hence

$$
m<u.
$$

Therefore every run of consecutive digits equal to $b-1$ in the fractional part has length strictly less than $u$.

Equivalently, there cannot be a run of $u$ consecutive digits equal to $b-1$, nor any longer run, anywhere to the right of the radix point.

This completes the proof.

∎
