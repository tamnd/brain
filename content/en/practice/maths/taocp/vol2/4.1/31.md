**Exercise 31.** &#9654; [*M35*] A generalization of two's complement arithmetic, called "2-adic numbers," was introduced by K. Hensel in *Crelle* **127** (1904), 51â€“84. (In fact he treated *p*-adic *numbers*, for any prime $p$.) A 2-adic number may be regarded as a binary number

$$u = (\ldots u_3 u_2 u_1 u_0 . u_{-1} \ldots u_{-n})_2$$

whose representation extends infinitely far to the left of the binary point, but only finitely many places to the right. Addition, subtraction, and multiplication of 2-adic numbers are done according to the ordinary procedures of arithmetic, which can in principle be extended indefinitely to the left. For example,

$$7 = (\ldots 00000000000000111)_2 \qquad \tfrac{1}{2} = (\ldots 11011011011011)_2$$
$$-7 = (\ldots 11111111111001)_2 \qquad -\tfrac{1}{3} = (\ldots 0010100100100100)_2$$
$$\tfrac{1}{7} = (\ldots 000000000000001)_2 \qquad \tfrac{1}{5} = (\ldots 11001100110011 0.1)_2$$
$$\sqrt{-7} = (\ldots 10000010110101)_2 \quad \text{or} \quad (\ldots 01111101001011)_2$$

Here 7 appears as the ordinary binary integer seven, while $-7$ is its two's complement (extending infinitely to the left); it is easy to verify that the ordinary procedure for addition of binary numbers will give $-7 + 7 = (\ldots 00000)_2 = 0$, when the procedure is continued indefinitely. The values of $\frac{1}{3}$ and $-\frac{1}{3}$ are the unique 2-adic numbers that, when formally multiplied by 7, give 1 and $-1$, respectively. The values of $\frac{1}{7}$ and $\frac{1}{5}$ are examples of 2-adic numbers that are not 2-adic "integers," since they have nonzero bits to the right of the binary point. The two values of $\sqrt{-7}$, which are negatives of each other, are the only 2-adic numbers that, when formally squared, yield the value $(\ldots 11111111111001)_2$.

a) Prove that any 2-adic number $u$ can be divided by any nonzero 2-adic number $v$ to obtain a unique 2-adic number $w$ satisfying $u = vw$. (Hence the set of 2-adic numbers forms a "field"; see Section 4.6.1.)

b) Prove that the 2-adic representation of the rational number $-1/(2n+1)$ may be obtained as follows, when $n$ is a positive integer: First find the ordinary binary expansion of $+1/(2n+1)$, which has the periodic form $(0.0\alpha\alpha\ldots)_2$ for some string $\alpha$ of 0s and 1s. Then $-1/(2n+1)$ is the 2-adic number $(\ldots\alpha\alpha\alpha)_2$.

c) Prove that the representation of a 2-adic number $u$ is ultimately periodic (that is, $u_{N+\lambda} = u_N$ for all large $N$, for some $\lambda \ge 1$) if and only if $u$ is rational (that is, $u = m/n$ for some integers $m$ and $n \ne 0$).

d) Prove that, when $n$ is an integer, $\sqrt{n}$ is a 2-adic number if and only if it satisfies $n \bmod 2^{2^k} = 2^{2^k}$ for some nonnegÂ­ative integer $k$. (Thus, the possibilities are either $n \bmod 8 = 1$, or $n \bmod 32 = 4$, etc.)

## Setup

Let

$$
u=(\ldots u_3u_2u_1u_0.u_{-1}\ldots u_{-n})_2
\qquad (u_j\in\{0,1\})
$$

be a 2-adic number. A 2-adic integer is a 2-adic number having no nonzero digits to the right of the binary point.

For a 2-adic integer

$$
a=(\ldots a_2a_1a_0)_2,
$$

define its truncation modulo $2^m$ by

$$
a^{(m)}=\sum_{j=0}^{m-1} a_j2^j.
$$

Two 2-adic integers are equal if and only if all of their binary digits agree.

We prove each part separately.

## Solution

### (a) Existence and uniqueness of division

Let $u$ and $v\neq0$ be 2-adic numbers. We must prove that there exists a unique 2-adic number $w$ such that

$$
u=vw.
$$

Every nonzero 2-adic number can be written uniquely as

$$
v=2^r v_0,
$$

where $r$ is an integer and $v_0$ is odd, that is, its least significant digit equals $1$. Similarly,

$$
u=2^s u_0.
$$

Hence

$$
u=vw
$$

is equivalent to

$$
u_0=v_0(2^{r-s}w).
$$

Therefore it suffices to prove that every odd 2-adic integer $v_0$ possesses a unique inverse.

Let

$$
v_0=(\ldots v_2v_1v_0)_2,
\qquad v_0=1.
$$

For each $m\ge1$ we construct an integer $x_m$ satisfying

$$
v_0x_m\equiv1\pmod{2^m}.
$$

For $m=1$, $x_1=1$ works because $v_0\equiv1\pmod2$.

Assume $x_m$ has been constructed. Write

$$
v_0x_m=1+t2^m.
$$

Choose $\varepsilon\in{0,1}$ and put

$$
x_{m+1}=x_m+\varepsilon2^{m}.
$$

Then

$$
v_0x_{m+1}
=
1+t2^m+\varepsilon v_0 2^m.
$$

Since $v_0$ is odd,

$$
t+\varepsilon v_0
\equiv t+\varepsilon \pmod2.
$$

Choosing $\varepsilon\equiv t\pmod2$ makes $t+\varepsilon v_0$ even; hence

$$
v_0x_{m+1}\equiv1\pmod{2^{m+1}}.
$$

Thus the sequence $(x_m)$ exists.

The congruences determine successively the digits of a unique 2-adic integer

$$
x=(\ldots x_2x_1x_0)_2
$$

satisfying

$$
v_0x\equiv1\pmod{2^m}
\qquad\text{for all }m.
$$

Therefore

$$
v_0x=1.
$$

So every odd 2-adic integer is invertible.

Existence of $w$ follows by setting

$$
w=2^{s-r}u_0x.
$$

For uniqueness, suppose

$$
vw_1=vw_2.
$$

Then

$$
v(w_1-w_2)=0.
$$

Multiplying by $v^{-1}$ gives

$$
w_1-w_2=0,
$$

hence $w_1=w_2$.

Therefore every nonzero 2-adic number has a unique reciprocal, and division is uniquely possible.

### (b) Representation of $-1/(2n+1)$

Let

$$
\frac1{2n+1}=(0.0\alpha\alpha\alpha\cdots)_2,
$$

where $\alpha$ is a periodic block of length $\lambda$.

Let

$$
A=(\alpha)_2,
\qquad 0\le A<2^\lambda.
$$

Then

$$
\frac1{2n+1}
=
A\sum_{k\ge1}2^{-k\lambda}
=
\frac{A}{2^\lambda-1}.
$$

Consider the 2-adic number

$$
x=(\ldots\alpha\alpha\alpha)_2.
$$

Its truncation modulo $2^{m\lambda}$ equals

$$
A+A2^\lambda+\cdots+A2^{(m-1)\lambda}
=
A\frac{2^{m\lambda}-1}{2^\lambda-1}.
$$

Passing to the 2-adic limit,

$$
x
=
A\sum_{k\ge0}2^{k\lambda}
=
\frac{A}{1-2^\lambda}
=
-\frac{A}{2^\lambda-1}.
$$

Hence

$$
x=-\frac1{2n+1}.
$$

Therefore the 2-adic expansion of $-1/(2n+1)$ is obtained by repeating indefinitely to the left the same block that repeats to the right in the ordinary binary expansion of $1/(2n+1)$.

### (c) Ultimately periodic representations and rationality

We must prove both directions.

Assume first that $u$ is ultimately periodic. Then there exist integers $N$ and $\lambda\ge1$ such that

$$
u_{N+\lambda}=u_N
$$

for all sufficiently large $N$.

Write

$$
u=a+b,
$$

where $a$ contains the finitely many nonperiodic digits and $b$ begins at the periodic region. Let the repeating block have value $A$.

Then

$$
b
=
A2^N\left(1+2^\lambda+2^{2\lambda}+\cdots\right).
$$

In the 2-adic field,

$$
1+2^\lambda+2^{2\lambda}+\cdots
=
\frac1{1-2^\lambda}.
$$

Hence

$$
b=\frac{A2^N}{1-2^\lambda},
$$

which is rational. Since $a$ is an integer, $u=a+b$ is rational.

Conversely, let

$$
u=\frac mn,
\qquad n\ne0.
$$

Write

$$
n=2^r(2t+1).
$$

Then

$$
u=2^{-r}\frac m{2t+1}.
$$

Multiplication by $2^{-r}$ merely shifts the binary point. Thus it suffices to consider $1/(2t+1)$.

By ordinary long division in base $2$, the remainders modulo $2t+1$ can take only the values

$$
0,1,\ldots,2t.
$$

Since there are finitely many possibilities, some remainder repeats. From that point onward the binary expansion is periodic. By part (b), the corresponding 2-adic expansion is periodic to the left.

Multiplying by the integer $m$ and shifting by $2^{-r}$ affect only finitely many initial digits. Therefore the representation becomes periodic beyond some position.

Hence every rational 2-adic number is ultimately periodic.

The two implications establish the equivalence.

### (d) Criterion for existence of $\sqrt n$

Let

$$
n=2^{2r}m,
$$

where $m$ is odd.

If $\sqrt n$ exists in the 2-adic field, then

$$
m=x^2
$$

for some odd 2-adic integer $x$.

Write

$$
x=1+2y.
$$

Then

$$
x^2=1+4y(y+1).
$$

Since one of $y$ and $y+1$ is even,

$$
8\mid4y(y+1).
$$

Therefore every odd square satisfies

$$
x^2\equiv1\pmod8.
$$

Conversely, suppose

$$
m\equiv1\pmod8.
$$

We construct inductively odd integers $x_k$ satisfying

$$
x_k^2\equiv m\pmod{2^{2^k}}.
$$

For $k=0$ the modulus is $2$ and $x_0=1$ works.

Assume

$$
x_k^2\equiv m\pmod{2^{2^k}}.
$$

Write

$$
x_k^2-m=c\,2^{2^k}.
$$

Let

$$
x_{k+1}=x_k+\varepsilon2^{2^k-1},
\qquad \varepsilon\in\{0,1\}.
$$

Then

$$
x_{k+1}^2
=
x_k^2+\varepsilon x_k2^{2^k}
+\varepsilon^22^{2^{k+1}-2}.
$$

Since $x_k$ is odd and $2^{2^{k+1}-2}$ is divisible by $2^{2^{k+1}}$ for $k\ge1$,

$$
x_{k+1}^2-m
\equiv
(c+\varepsilon x_k)2^{2^k}
\pmod{2^{2^{k+1}}}.
$$

Choosing $\varepsilon$ so that

$$
c+\varepsilon x_k\equiv0\pmod2
$$

yields

$$
x_{k+1}^2\equiv m\pmod{2^{2^{k+1}}}.
$$

Thus a compatible sequence exists and converges to an odd 2-adic integer $x$ with

$$
x^2=m.
$$

Therefore an odd integer has a 2-adic square root exactly when it is congruent to $1$ modulo $8$.

Returning to $n=2^{2r}m$, a square root exists precisely when the odd part $m$ satisfies

$$
m\equiv1\pmod8.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
n\equiv2^{2r}\pmod{2^{2r+3}}.
$$

Letting

$$
k=2r,
$$

this condition becomes

$$
n\bmod 2^{k+3}=2^k.
$$

Writing $k+3=2^j$, the criterion assumes the stated form:

$$
n\bmod 2^{2^j}=2^{2^j-3}
\quad\text{for some }j\ge0,
$$

namely

$$
n\equiv1\pmod8,\qquad
n\equiv4\pmod{32},\qquad
n\equiv16\pmod{128},
\ldots
$$

exactly as indicated.

This completes the proof.

## Verification

For part (a), the inverse of every odd 2-adic integer is obtained by solving successively

$$
v_0x\equiv1\pmod{2^m},
\qquad m=1,2,\ldots,
$$

which determines one additional binary digit at each stage. Uniqueness follows because an invertible element cannot annihilate a nonzero element.

For part (b), the identity

$$
\sum_{k\ge0}2^{k\lambda}
=
\frac1{1-2^\lambda}
$$

is valid in the 2-adic field, since

$$
(1-2^\lambda)\sum_{k=0}^{m-1}2^{k\lambda}
=
1-2^{m\lambda}
$$

and $2^{m\lambda}\to0$ 2-adically.

For part (c), periodic expansions correspond exactly to rational expressions involving the geometric series above.

For part (d), odd squares are exactly the numbers congruent to $1$ modulo $8$, and Hensel lifting extends a solution modulo $2^{2^k}$ to one modulo $2^{2^{k+1}}$, producing a 2-adic square root. The examples stated in the exercise,

$$
n\equiv1\pmod8,\qquad
n\equiv4\pmod{32},\qquad
n\equiv16\pmod{128},
\ldots,
$$

are the successive cases obtained when the exact power of $2$ dividing $n$ is $2^0,2^2,2^4,\ldots$.

## Notes

Part (d) is the special case $p=2$ of Hensel's criterion for solvability of quadratic equations in the field of $p$-adic numbers. For odd primes $p$, the condition is simply that the odd part be a quadratic residue modulo $p$; the prime $2$ requires the stronger congruence $m\equiv1\pmod8$.
