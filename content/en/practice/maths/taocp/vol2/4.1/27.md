**Exercise 27.** [*M21*] $[M21]$ Show that every nonzero integer has a unique "reversing binary representation"

$$2^{e_0} - 2^{e_1} + \cdots + (-1)^r 2^{e_r},$$

where $e_0 < e_1 < \cdots < e_r$.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 3m03s  

---

## Solution

Let $n$ be a nonzero integer. We claim that $n$ can be expressed uniquely in the form

$$
n = 2^{e_0} - 2^{e_1} + 2^{e_2} - \cdots + (-1)^r 2^{e_r}, \qquad e_0 < e_1 < \cdots < e_r,
$$

which we call the _reversing binary representation_. We treat the cases $n > 0$ and $n < 0$ separately.

**Existence.** Suppose first that $n > 0$. Let $e_0$ be the largest integer such that $2^{e_0} \le n$. Then $0 \le n - 2^{e_0} < 2^{e_0}$. If $n - 2^{e_0} = 0$, we are done with $r = 0$. Otherwise, $n - 2^{e_0} > 0$, and we seek to represent it as $2^{e_1} - 2^{e_2} + \cdots + (-1)^r 2^{e_r}$ with $e_1 > e_0$.

Proceed recursively: given a positive integer $m$, let $e$ be the largest integer such that $2^e \le m$; set the next term in the alternating sum to $(-1)^{k} 2^e$ where $k$ is the position in the sequence. The remainder $m - (-1)^k 2^e$ is strictly smaller in absolute value than $2^e$, and by construction the next exponent must exceed $e$. Since the sequence of remainders strictly decreases in magnitude and consists of nonnegative integers, the process terminates after finitely many steps with a representation of the desired form.

If $n < 0$, apply the same procedure to $-n$ and then multiply the entire expression by $-1$. This yields a representation for any nonzero integer.

**Uniqueness.** Suppose there exist two distinct representations

$$
n = 2^{e_0} - 2^{e_1} + \cdots + (-1)^r 2^{e_r} = 2^{f_0} - 2^{f_1} + \cdots + (-1)^s 2^{f_s}, \qquad e_0 < \cdots < e_r, \; f_0 < \cdots < f_s.
$$

Let $k$ be the smallest index for which $e_k \ne f_k$. Without loss of generality, assume $e_k < f_k$. Then the partial sum up to $e_k$ in the first representation is strictly smaller than the corresponding partial sum in the second representation, because adding terms $(-1)^{k+1}2^{e_{k+1}} - \cdots$ can change the sum by at most

$2^{e_{k+1}} - 2^{e_{k+2}} + \cdots + (-1)^r 2^{e_r} < 2^{e_{k+1}} \le 2^{f_k},$

while the second representation contributes at least $2^{f_k} > 2^{e_k}$. Therefore the two sums cannot be equal, which contradicts the assumption that they represent the same integer. Hence the representation is unique.

This completes the proof.

∎
