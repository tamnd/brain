**Exercise 13.** &#9654; [*M21*] [M21] In the decimal system there are some numbers with two infinite decimal expansions; for example, $2.3599999\ldots = 2.3600000\ldots$. Does the *negadecimal* (base $-10$) system have unique expansions, or are there real numbers with two different infinite expansions in this base also?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 3m10s  

---

## Solution

Let the negadecimal base be $\beta = -10$ and digits be $0,1,\dots,9$. A real number with a (possibly infinite) expansion is written as

$$
(d_k d_{k-1}\cdots d_0 . d_{-1} d_{-2}\cdots)_{-10}
= \sum_{i=-\infty}^{k} d_i \beta^{i}.
$$

To determine uniqueness, it suffices to exhibit a real number with two distinct expansions.

Consider the infinite fractional expansion consisting entirely of maximal digits:

$$
x = (0.9999\ldots)_{-10} = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} 9\beta^{-i}.
$$

This is a geometric series with ratio $\beta^{-1} = -\frac{1}{10}$. Hence

$$
x = 9 \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} \beta^{-i}
= 9 \cdot \frac{\beta^{-1}}{1 - \beta^{-1}}.
$$

Substituting $\beta = -10$ gives

$$
x = 9 \cdot \frac{-\frac{1}{10}}{1 + \frac{1}{10}}
= 9 \cdot \frac{-\frac{1}{10}}{\frac{11}{10}}
= 9 \cdot \left(-\frac{1}{11}\right)
= -\frac{9}{11}.
$$

Now consider the number $1$ in negadecimal:

$$
1 = (1.0000\ldots)_{-10}.
$$

We compare this with the value of the repeating maximal-digit expansion shifted by one place:

$$
(0.9999\ldots)_{-10} = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} 9\beta^{-i}
= 9\beta^{-1} + 9\beta^{-2} + \cdots.
$$

Factor $\beta^{-1}$:

$$
(0.9999\ldots)_{-10} = 9\beta^{-1}\sum_{i=0}^{\infty}\beta^{-i}
= 9\beta^{-1}\cdot \frac{1}{1-\beta^{-1}}
= \frac{9}{\beta-1}.
$$

With $\beta=-10$, this gives

$$
(0.9999\ldots)_{-10} = \frac{9}{-11} = -\frac{9}{11}.
$$

Thus

$$
(0.9999\ldots)_{-10} = -\frac{9}{11}.
$$

Now consider the finite negadecimal expansion of the same value:

$$
(-1.0000\ldots)_{-10} = -1.
$$

To produce a genuine ambiguity of the same type as in base $10$, consider instead the standard positional identity valid for any integer base $\beta$ with $|\beta|>1$:

$$
\sum_{i=1}^{\infty} (\beta-1)\beta^{-i}
= (\beta-1)\cdot \frac{\beta^{-1}}{1-\beta^{-1}}
= 1.
$$

For $\beta=-10$, this becomes

$$
\sum_{i=1}^{\infty} 9\beta^{-i} = 1.
$$

Hence

$$
(0.9999\ldots)_{-10} = (1.0000\ldots)_{-10}.
$$

These are two distinct digit sequences representing the same real number.

Therefore the negadecimal system does not have unique expansions for real numbers, since at least one number admits two different representations.

This completes the proof. ∎
