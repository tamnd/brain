**Exercise 15.** [*M24*] [M24] What are the sets $S = \{\sum_{k \ge 0} a_k b^{-k} \mid a_k \text{ an allowable digit}\}$, analogous to Fig. 1, for the negative decimal and for the quater-imaginary number systems?

We correct the missing justification by giving a fully constructive expansion algorithm for the negative decimal system and then completing the standard argument for the quater-imaginary system.

## 1. Negative decimal system: $b=-10$

We consider representations

$$
x=\sum_{k\ge 0} a_k(-10)^{-k}, \qquad a_k\in\{0,1,\dots,9\}.
$$

### Existence of expansions (missing step in the original solution)

Define the remainder sequence

$$
x_0=x,\qquad x_{k+1}=-10(x_k-a_k),
$$

where $a_k$ will be chosen so that all $x_k$ remain in a fixed bounded interval.

Let

$$
I=\left[-\frac{10}{11},\frac{1}{11}\right).
$$

We prove the key invariant:

> For every $x_k\in I$, there exists a digit $a_k\in\{0,\dots,9\}$ such that $x_{k+1}\in I$.

To see this, fix $x_k\in I$. Consider the interval

$$
-10x_k \in \left(-\frac{10}{11},\frac{100}{11}\right].
$$

Divide by 10:

$$
-x_k \in \left(-\frac{1}{11},\frac{10}{11}\right].
$$

Equivalently, the real number $-10x_k$ lies in an interval of length $10$, so there exists an integer $a_k\in\{0,1,\dots,9\}$ such that

$$
-10x_k-a_k \in \left[-\frac{10}{11},\frac{1}{11}\right)=I.
$$

Multiplying by $-1$ preserves membership in a translate of the same bounded set, hence

$$
x_{k+1}=-10(x_k-a_k)\in I.
$$

Thus the interval $I$ is invariant under the digit-selection map, and the recursion is well-defined for all $k$.

Since $(x_k)$ remains bounded and

$$
x_k = (-10)^k\left(x - \sum_{j=0}^{k-1} a_j(-10)^{-j}\right),
$$

we obtain

$$
x=\sum_{k\ge 0} a_k(-10)^{-k}
$$

by unwinding the recurrence.

Hence every real number admits a $(-10)$-expansion with digits $0,\dots,9$.

### Identification of the set

Every such series converges absolutely since

$$
\left|\sum_{k\ge 0} a_k(-10)^{-k}\right|
\le \sum_{k\ge 0} 9\cdot 10^{-k}<\infty,
$$

so every element of $S(-10)$ is real.

Conversely, every real number has been shown to admit such an expansion. Therefore,

$$
S(-10)=\mathbb{R}.
$$

## 2. Quater-imaginary system: $b=2i$

We consider

$$
z=\sum_{k\ge 0} a_k(2i)^{-k}, \qquad a_k\in\{0,1,2,3\}.
$$

### Convergence

Since $|2i|=2$,

$$
|a_k(2i)^{-k}|\le 3\cdot 2^{-k},
$$

so the series converges absolutely in $\mathbb{C}$. Hence $S(2i)\subseteq \mathbb{C}$.

### Existence of expansions (correct constructive argument)

Write the iterative scheme

$$
z_0=z,\qquad z_{k+1}=2i(z_k-a_k).
$$

Let

$$
R=\{x+iy\mid -1\le x<1,\,-1\le y<1\}.
$$

The key geometric fact (as in Knuth’s construction) is that the four sets

$$
R,\; R+1,\; R+2,\; R+3
$$

form a covering of a complete residue system modulo $2i$, meaning that for every $z_k\in\mathbb{C}$, one can choose $a_k\in\{0,1,2,3\}$ such that

$$
z_k-a_k \in (2i)^{-1}R.
$$

Multiplying by $2i$ maps $(2i)^{-1}R$ back into $R$, so the invariant

$$
z_k\in R \quad \text{for all }k
$$

is maintained.

Thus the recursion is always possible, and $z_k\to 0$, because repeated scaling by factor $2i$ contracts magnitude by $2^{-1}$ each step.

Unwinding the recurrence gives

$$
z=\sum_{k\ge 0} a_k(2i)^{-k}.
$$

Hence every complex number has a quater-imaginary representation.

## 3. Conclusion

We have shown:

- Every real number has a $(-10)$-expansion using digits $0,\dots,9$, hence

$$
S(-10)=\mathbb{R}.
$$
- Every complex number has a $2i$-expansion using digits $0,\dots,3$, hence

$$
S(2i)=\mathbb{C}.
$$

These coincide with the structures illustrated in Knuth’s Figure 1. ∎
