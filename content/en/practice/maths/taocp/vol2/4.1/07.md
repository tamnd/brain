**Exercise 7.** [*M20*] [M20] The text defines ten's complement notation only for integers represented in a single computer word. Is there a way to define a ten's complement notation *for all real numbers*, having "infinite precision," analogous to the text's definition? Is there a similar way to define a nines' complement notation for all real numbers?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m43s  

---

## Solution

For integers represented in $n$ digits in base $10$, ten’s complement is defined modulo $10^n$. If an integer $a$ satisfies $0 \le a < 10^n$, its ten’s complement representation corresponds to $10^n - a$, and arithmetic is carried out modulo $10^n$. Nines’ complement corresponds to digitwise replacement $d \mapsto 9-d$, followed by the same modular interpretation.

To extend this idea to real numbers, a representation must first fix a positional system for reals. A real number $x$ is written as

$$
x = \sum_{i=-\infty}^{m} d_i 10^i,
$$

with digits $d_i \in {0,1,\dots,9}$ and a fixed radix point. “Infinite precision” means that the digit sequence extends without truncation to the left or right.

A direct analogue of ten’s complement would require a modulus $M$ such that every real number is interpreted modulo $M$, and addition corresponds to arithmetic modulo $M$ with carries. No such finite modulus exists for the set of all real numbers, since for any candidate modulus $M$ and any real $x$, the value $x+M$ is distinct from $x$ but indistinguishable under the modular rule, contradicting injective representation of reals.

A partial extension is possible only after restricting the domain. Fix an integer $m$ and consider reals in the interval $[0,10^m)$. Each such number has a finite integer part bounded by $10^m$, while the fractional part may have infinitely many digits. Define ten’s complement of $x$ in this interval by

$$
\mathrm{comp}(x) = 10^m - x.
$$

This is well-defined and preserves the integer complement behavior on all finite truncations. Arithmetic can be carried out with carry propagation exactly as in the finite-word case, since every digit position interacts with only finitely many carries.

A similar construction applies to nines’ complement. For $x \in [0,10^m)$ with digit expansion $x = d_m d_{m-1}\dots d_0.d_{-1}d_{-2}\dots$, define digitwise complement

$$
d_i \mapsto 9 - d_i,
$$

producing a new digit sequence corresponding to $10^m - x - 10^{-k}$ effects caused by the standard infinite decimal ambiguity at repeating 9’s. As in the integer case, adding one unit in the least significant position converts nines’ complement to ten’s complement.

Beyond bounded intervals, no global definition exists that simultaneously satisfies the complement identity, preserves digitwise arithmetic with finite carry propagation, and represents all real numbers uniquely. The obstruction is that ten’s complement is inherently modular, while the real line is not compact under any integer modulus.

This completes the solution. ∎
