## Solution

Let a number be represented in _mixed-radix_ notation, that is, by a sequence of digits $(d_0, d_1, \ldots, d_{n-1})$ associated with radices $(b_0, b_1, \ldots, b_{n-1})$, where each $d_i$ satisfies $0 \le d_i < b_i$. By equation (5) of Section 4.1, for a single radix $b$ we have the representation of a nonnegative integer $N$ as

$N = d_0 + d_1 b + d_2 b^2 + \cdots + d_{n-1} b^{\,n-1}, \qquad 0 \le d_i < b. \eqno(5)$

To generalize this to mixed radices, we define the _place values_ recursively. Let $B_0 = 1$, and for $i \ge 1$ define

$B_i = b_0 b_1 \cdots b_{i-1}.$

Then the integer $N$ represented by the digits $d_0, d_1, \ldots, d_{n-1}$ in radices $b_0, b_1, \ldots, b_{n-1}$ is given by

$N = \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} d_i B_i = d_0 + d_1 b_0 + d_2 b_0 b_1 + \cdots + d_{n-1} b_0 b_1 \cdots b_{n-2}. \eqno(12)$

This formula reduces to (5) when $b_i = b$ for all $i$, because then $B_i = b^i$. The digits still satisfy $0 \le d_i < b_i$ for each $i$.

For numbers with a fractional part, let the representation be

$(d_{-1}, d_{-2}, \ldots)$

with radices $(b_{-1}, b_{-2}, \ldots)$ corresponding to the digits after the radix point. We define the _place values_ for negative indices by

$B_{-1} = \frac{1}{b_{-1}}, \qquad B_{-2} = \frac{1}{b_{-1} b_{-2}}, \quad \text{and in general} \quad B_{-j} = \frac{1}{b_{-1} b_{-2} \cdots b_{-j}}.$

Then the fractional value is

$F = \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} d_{-j} B_{-j} = \frac{d_{-1}}{b_{-1}} + \frac{d_{-2}}{b_{-1} b_{-2}} + \cdots. \eqno(13)$

Combining integer and fractional parts, a number in mixed-radix notation can be expressed as

$N + F = \sum_{i=-\infty}^{n-1} d_i B_i, \eqno(14)$

where the product for $B_i$ is defined by

$B_i = \begin{cases} 1, & i = 0; \\ \prod_{j=0}^{i-1} b_j, & i > 0; \\ \prod_{j=i}^{-1} \frac{1}{b_j}, & i < 0. \end{cases} \eqno(15)$

This fully generalizes equation (5) to mixed-radix representation. The recursive definition of $B_i$ guarantees that each digit $d_i$ is multiplied by the cumulative product of all lower-order radices, exactly as in the single-radix case, preserving the property that each number has a unique representation when $0 \le d_i < b_i$.

This completes the proof.
