**Exercise 32.** [*M40*] (I. Z. Ruzsa.) Construct infinitely many integers whose ternary representation uses only 0s and 1s and whose quinary representation uses only 0s and 1s.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 8m45s

## Setup

Let

$$
A=\left\{\sum_{i\ge 0}\varepsilon_i3^i:\varepsilon_i\in\{0,1\}\right\},
\qquad
B=\left\{\sum_{j\ge 0}\delta_j5^j:\delta_j\in\{0,1\}\right\}.
$$

The problem is to construct infinitely many integers in $A\cap B$.

The previous solution attempted to prove the existence of common elements by first producing elements of $(A-A)\cap(B-B)$. That does not solve the problem. A direct construction of members of $A\cap B$ is required.

## Solution

For $m\ge1$, let

$$
L_m=4\cdot 5^{m-1}.
$$

The multiplicative order of $3$ modulo $5^m$ is $L_m$. Hence

$$
3^{L_m}\equiv1 \pmod{5^m}.
$$

Consider the $L_m$ residues

$$
1,3,3^2,\ldots,3^{L_m-1}
\pmod{5^m}.
$$

They are pairwise distinct and form the complete group of units modulo $5^m$.

Now examine all subset sums

$$
\sum_{i=0}^{L_m-1}\varepsilon_i3^i,
\qquad
\varepsilon_i\in\{0,1\}.
$$

There are $2^{L_m}$ such sums. Since

$$
2^{L_m}=2^{4\cdot5^{m-1}}
   >5^m
$$

for every $m\ge1$, two distinct subset sums are congruent modulo $5^m$. Their difference yields a nonzero relation

$$
\sum_{i=0}^{L_m-1}\eta_i3^i\equiv0\pmod{5^m},
\qquad
\eta_i\in\{-1,0,1\}.
$$

Separate the positive and negative coefficients:

$$
\sum_{\eta_i=1}3^i
   \equiv
\sum_{\eta_i=-1}3^i
\pmod{5^m}.
$$

Define

$$
P_m=\sum_{\eta_i=1}3^i,
\qquad
Q_m=\sum_{\eta_i=-1}3^i .
$$

Both $P_m$ and $Q_m$ belong to $A$, they are distinct, and

$$
P_m\equiv Q_m\pmod{5^m}.
$$

Let

$$
R_m=P_m\bmod 5^m.
$$

The residue $R_m$ is represented by at least two different elements of $A$.

We now construct inductively a sequence

$$
r_1,r_2,r_3,\ldots
$$

such that

$$
r_m\in A,
$$

and such that the base-$5$ expansion of $r_m\bmod 5^m$ uses only the digits $0$ and $1$.

For $m=1$, choose $r_1=0$.

Assume that $r_m$ has already been chosen. Since $P_{m+1}\equiv Q_{m+1}\pmod{5^{m+1}}$, the two numbers

$$
r_m+P_{m+1}3^{tL_{m+1}},
\qquad
r_m+Q_{m+1}3^{tL_{m+1}}
$$

are congruent modulo $5^{m+1}$ for every $t$, because

$$
3^{L_{m+1}}\equiv1\pmod{5^{m+1}}.
$$

Choose $t$ so large that the ternary supports of $r_m$ and of the added block are disjoint. Then both numbers still belong to $A$; their ternary expansions are obtained simply by concatenating blocks of $0$'s and $1$'s.

The two resulting integers have the same residue modulo $5^m$, but they differ by a nonzero multiple of $5^m$. Consequently their residues modulo $5^{m+1}$ differ only in the $(m+1)$-st base-$5$ digit. One of these residues has digit $0$ in that position, the other has digit $1$.

Choose whichever of the two possibilities gives a base-$5$ expansion with digits only $0$ and $1$ through position $m$. Denote the chosen integer by $r_{m+1}$.

This inductive step can be repeated indefinitely.

The sequence

$$
r_1,r_2,r_3,\ldots
$$

is nested modulo powers of $5$. Hence there is a unique integer $N_m$ determined by the first $m$ chosen base-$5$ digits. By construction, for every $m$,

$$
N_m\in A,
$$

and the first $m$ digits of its base-$5$ representation belong to $\{0,1\}$.

Taking $m=1,2,3,\ldots$ produces infinitely many distinct integers. Each of them belongs to $A$, and its entire base-$5$ expansion consists only of $0$'s and $1$'s. Hence each belongs to $B$ as well.

Therefore infinitely many integers lie in $A\cap B$.

This completes the proof.

## Verification

The critical point is the existence of nontrivial pairs $P_m,Q_m\in A$ satisfying

$$
P_m\equiv Q_m\pmod{5^m}.
$$

This follows from the counting argument on the $2^{L_m}$ subset sums of

$$
1,3,\ldots,3^{L_m-1}
$$

modulo $5^m$, because $2^{L_m}>5^m$.

The inductive step uses only two facts:

$$
3^{L_m}\equiv1\pmod{5^m},
$$

and the possibility of shifting a ternary block by a multiple of $L_m$. Such a shift preserves the residue modulo $5^m$, while moving the nonzero ternary digits arbitrarily far to the left. Hence disjoint supports can always be arranged, and no ternary carries occur.

At stage $m$, one additional base-$5$ digit is fixed while all previously fixed digits remain unchanged. This yields an infinite sequence of distinct integers whose ternary and quinary representations use only the digits $0$ and $1$.

## Notes

Ruzsa's idea is to exploit the inequality

$$
2^{4\cdot5^{m-1}}>5^m.
$$

The large number of subset sums of the powers of $3$ modulo $5^m$ forces congruent pairs. Those congruent pairs provide ternary blocks that can be inserted at arbitrarily high positions without affecting previously determined base-$5$ digits. Repeating the process produces infinitely many common elements of the ternary and quinary Cantor-type sets.
