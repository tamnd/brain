Let

$$
\alpha = - \sum_{m>1} 10^{-m!}.
$$

We are asked to prove that for any real number $x$ and any $\epsilon > 0$, there exist finitely many digits $a_0,\dots,a_N \in \{0,1,\alpha\}$ such that

$$
0 < \Bigl| x - \sum_{k=0}^{N} a_k 10^k \Bigr| < \epsilon.
$$

## Corrected Solution

The key idea is to exploit the extremely sparse factorial exponents in $\alpha$ to construct finite sums of the form $\sum_{k=0}^N a_k 10^k$ that approximate any given real number. The sparsity ensures that the “tails” of the series contribute independently at well-separated scales, which allows an explicit inductive construction.

### Step 1: Structure of $\alpha$

Write

$$
\alpha = - \sum_{m=2}^{\infty} 10^{-m!} = - 10^{-2} - 10^{-6} - 10^{-24} - \cdots.
$$

For each integer $k \ge 0$, define

$$
\alpha 10^k = - \sum_{m=2}^{\infty} 10^{k - m!}.
$$

Observe that the exponents $k - m!$ are **strictly decreasing** for $m > k$ and the corresponding terms satisfy

$$
0 < 10^{k - m!} < 1 \quad \text{for all sufficiently large } m.
$$

This separation of scales will allow independent contributions at different powers of 10.

$$
F = \{ s_n : n \ge 2 \}.
$$
