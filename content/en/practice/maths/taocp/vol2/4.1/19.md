**Exercise 19.** &#9654; [*23*] [23] (David W. Matula.) Let $D$ be a set of $b$ integers, containing exactly one solution to the congruence $x \equiv j \pmod{b}$ for $0 \le j < b$. Prove that all integers $m$ (positive, negative, or zero) can be represented in the form $m = (a_n \ldots a_1 a_0)_b$, where all the $a_j$ are in $D$, if and only if all integers in the range $l \le m \le u$ can be so represented,

where $l = -\max\{a \mid a \in D\}/(b-1)$ and $u = \min\{a \mid a \in D\}/(b-1)$. For example, $D = \{-1, 0, \ldots, b-2\}$ satisfies the conditions for all $b \ge 2$. [*Hint:* Design an algorithm that constructs a suitable representation.]

## Corrected Solution

Let $D$ be a set of $b$ integers such that for each residue $j$ modulo $b$, there exists exactly one element $a \in D$ with

$$
a \equiv j \pmod{b}, \quad 0 \le j < b.
$$

We wish to show that every integer $m \in \mathbb{Z}$ can be represented in the form

$$
m = (a_n a_{n-1} \dots a_0)_b = \sum_{j=0}^{n} a_j b^j, \quad a_j \in D,
$$

if and only if every integer in the interval

$$
[l,u], \qquad l = -\frac{\max(D)}{b-1}, \quad u = -\frac{\min(D)}{b-1}
$$

can be represented in this form.

We treat necessity and sufficiency separately.

### Necessity

Suppose that every integer $m$ has a representation

$$
m = \sum_{j=0}^n a_j b^j, \quad a_j \in D.
$$

Then, in particular, all integers $m$ with $l \le m \le u$ also have representations. This proves necessity.

### Sufficiency

Assume that every integer in the interval $[l,u]$ has a representation with digits in $D$. We now prove that every integer $m \in \mathbb{Z}$ has a representation. The argument is constructive and uses induction on $|m|$.

#### Step 1: Choice of least significant digit

Let $m \in \mathbb{Z}$ be arbitrary. Since $D$ contains exactly one representative modulo $b$ for each residue, there exists a unique $a_0 \in D$ such that

$$
a_0 \equiv m \pmod{b}.
$$

Define

$$
m_1 = \frac{m - a_0}{b}.
$$

Then $m_1 \in \mathbb{Z}$ and

$$
m = a_0 + b m_1.
$$

If $m_1 \in [l,u]$, then by assumption $m_1$ has a representation

$$
m_1 = \sum_{j=1}^{n} a_j b^{j-1}, \quad a_j \in D.
$$

Substituting back, we obtain

$$
m = a_0 + b m_1 = \sum_{j=0}^{n} a_j b^j, \quad a_j \in D.
$$

Thus, in this case, a representation exists.

#### Step 2: Induction on $|m|$

Suppose $m_1 \notin [l,u]$. We will show that repeated application of the same procedure produces an integer that eventually lies in $[l,u]$.

Consider the function

$$
f(x) = \frac{x - a}{b}, \quad a \in D, \ a \equiv x \pmod{b}.
$$

Let $x_0 = m$ and define the sequence

$$
x_{k+1} = \frac{x_k - a_k}{b}, \quad a_k \in D, \ a_k \equiv x_k \pmod{b}.
$$

We claim that this sequence eventually reaches an integer in $[l,u]$.

**Proof of termination.** Let $M = \max(|l|,|u|)$. Observe that for any $x \notin [l,u]$, the choice of $a \in D$ ensures

$$
x - a \in b \mathbb{Z}.
$$

Hence,

$$
|x_{k+1}| = \frac{|x_k - a_k|}{b}.
$$

If $x_k > u$, then $x_k - a_k \le x_k - \min(D)$, so

$$
x_{k+1} = \frac{x_k - a_k}{b} \le \frac{x_k - \min(D)}{b}.
$$

But by definition of $u$,

$$
u = -\frac{\min(D)}{b-1} \implies \min(D) = -(b-1) u.
$$

Substituting gives

$$
x_{k+1} \le \frac{x_k + (b-1)u}{b} = \frac{x_k - b u + u}{b} = \frac{x_k - b u}{b} + \frac{u}{b} = \frac{x_k - b u}{b} + \frac{u}{b}.
$$

Since $x_k > u$, it follows that $x_k - b u > 0$, so $x_{k+1} < x_k$.

Similarly, if $x_k < l$, one shows $x_{k+1} > x_k$.

Therefore, the sequence $|x_k|$ is **monotonically decreasing whenever $|x_k| > \max(|l|,|u|)$**. Since all $x_k$ are integers and the decrease is by at least 1 after finitely many steps (because $b \ge 2$), the sequence must eventually reach some $x_N \in [l,u]$.

#### Step 3: Representation

Once $x_N \in [l,u]$, by assumption it has a representation with digits in $D$:

$$
x_N = \sum_{j=N}^{n} a_j b^{j-N}, \quad a_j \in D.
$$

Reconstructing $m$ backwards via

$$
m = a_0 + b(a_1 + b(a_2 + \dots)) = \sum_{j=0}^{n} a_j b^j, \quad a_j \in D,
$$

yields a representation of $m$ using digits in $D$.

This completes the sufficiency proof.

### Example

Let $D = \{-1,0,1,\dots,b-2\}$ for $b \ge 2$. Then

$$
\max(D) = b-2, \quad \min(D) = -1.
$$

Hence

$$
l = -\frac{\max(D)}{b-1} = -\frac{b-2}{b-1}, \quad u = -\frac{\min(D)}{b-1} = \frac{1}{b-1}.
$$

The only integer contained in $[l,u]$ is $0$, and indeed $0 \in D$. Therefore, the condition is satisfied. By the construction above, every integer has a representation with digits in $D$.

For instance, for $b = 2$, $D = \{-1,0\}$, the interval $[l,u] = [-1,1]$ contains $0$. Every integer can then be represented recursively using the algorithm described.

### Conclusion

We have shown that the condition that every integer in $[l,u]$ has a representation is both necessary and sufficient for all integers to have a representation in base $b$ with digits in $D$. The recursive algorithm is guaranteed to terminate in finitely many steps because the absolute value of the current integer decreases at each step outside the interval $[l,u]$.
