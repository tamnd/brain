**Exercise 28.** &#9654; [*M24*] $[M24]$ Show that every nonzero complex number of the form $a + bi$ where $a$ and $b$ are integers has a unique "revolving binary representation"

$$(1+i)^{e_0} + i(1+i)^{e_1} - (1+i)^{e_2} - i(1+i)^{e_3} + \cdots + i^r(1+i)^{e_r},$$

where $e_0 < e_1 < \cdots < e_r$. (Compare with exercise 27.)

## Solution

Let $z = a + bi$ be a nonzero Gaussian integer, where $a, b \in \mathbb{Z}$. We claim that $z$ admits a unique _revolving binary representation_

$$
z = (1+i)^{e_0} + i(1+i)^{e_1} - (1+i)^{e_2} - i(1+i)^{e_3} + \cdots + i^r (1+i)^{e_r},
$$

with strictly increasing exponents $e_0 < e_1 < \cdots < e_r$, where the coefficients rotate through $1, i, -1, -i$ according to $k \bmod 4$. The proof proceeds in three steps: existence, uniqueness, and a constructive procedure.

### Step 1: Existence

We proceed by induction on the _norm_ $N(z) = a^2 + b^2$ of $z$, which is a nonnegative integer.

For the base case, $N(z) = 1$ corresponds to $z = \pm 1$ or $z = \pm i$. Each of these is already of the desired form with a single term: for example, $1 = (1+i)^0$, $i = i(1+i)^0$, $-1 = -(1+i)^0 = i^2(1+i)^0$, and $-i = -i = i^3(1+i)^0$.

Assume that all Gaussian integers with norm strictly less than $N(z)$ admit a revolving binary representation. We now construct the representation for $z$.

Observe that $(1+i)^n$ is a Gaussian integer for all $n \ge 0$, with norm $N((1+i)^n) = 2^n$. Define the _least significant digit_ of $z$ in base $1+i$ as follows. Since $1+i$ divides $2$, we can write

$$
z = d_0 + (1+i) w
$$

for some $d_0 \in \{0, 1, i, 1+i\}$ and $w \in \mathbb{Z}[i]$. This is possible because $\mathbb{Z}[i]/(1+i) \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ and each residue class modulo $1+i$ can be uniquely represented as $0,1$. If necessary, we absorb the rotation by $i$ into $d_0$ to ensure the first term has the correct coefficient among $1,i,-1,-i$.

Once $d_0$ is selected, define

$$
z_1 = z - d_0.
$$

Since $(1+i)$ divides $z_1$, there exists $w = z_1/(1+i)$. Then $N(w) < N(z)$, because $N(z_1) \le N(z) - 1$ and division by $(1+i)$ reduces the norm by a factor of 2. By the induction hypothesis, $w$ admits a revolving binary representation. Multiplying each term of that representation by $1+i$ and appending $d_0$ at the front gives a revolving binary representation for $z$.

This recursive procedure guarantees that each subtraction strictly reduces the norm and eventually reaches the base case. Therefore, every nonzero Gaussian integer has a revolving binary representation.

### Step 2: Uniqueness

Suppose there exist two distinct representations

$$
z = \sum_{j=0}^r i^j (1+i)^{e_j} = \sum_{j=0}^s i^j (1+i)^{f_j},
$$

with $e_0 < e_1 < \cdots < e_r$ and $f_0 < f_1 < \cdots < f_s$. Let $m$ be the smallest index such that $e_m \neq f_m$. Without loss of generality, assume $e_m < f_m$.

Factor out $(1+i)^{e_m}$ from both sums:

$$
i^m + \sum_{j>m} i^j (1+i)^{e_j - e_m} = \sum_{j\ge m} i^j (1+i)^{f_j - e_m}.
$$

The left-hand side is a Gaussian integer not divisible by $1+i$ because the term $i^m$ has exponent 0 and is therefore a unit modulo $1+i$. The right-hand side is divisible by $1+i$ because $f_m - e_m > 0$. This is a contradiction: a Gaussian integer cannot be simultaneously divisible and not divisible by $1+i$. Therefore, no such $m$ exists, and the sequence of exponents must coincide. The corresponding coefficients are also uniquely determined modulo 4 by their position in the sequence. Hence, the representation is unique.

### Step 3: Constructive procedure

The representation can be computed recursively as follows:

1. Let $z_0 = z$.
2. At step $k$, determine the _least significant term_ $i^k (1+i)^{e_k}$ of $z_k$ by taking $d_k = z_k \bmod (1+i)$, selecting the unique element among $\{1,i,-1,-i\}$ congruent to $z_k \bmod (1+i)$. Then choose $e_k$ so that $(1+i)^{e_k}$ divides $z_k - i^k (1+i)^{e_k}$ maximally.
3. Set $z_{k+1} = (z_k - i^k (1+i)^{e_k}) / (1+i)$.
4. Repeat until $z_{k+1} = 0$.

At each step, $N(z_{k+1}) < N(z_k)$, so the process terminates in finitely many steps. The resulting sequence $(e_0, e_1, \dots, e_r)$ with coefficients $i^k$ gives the unique revolving binary representation of $z$.

### Step 4: Example

Let $z = 3 + 2i$.

1. Compute $d_0 = z \bmod (1+i) = 1$ (coefficient for $(1+i)^0$).
2. Subtract and divide: $w_0 = (3+2i - 1)/(1+i) = 2+i$.
3. Next term: $d_1 = w_0 \bmod (1+i) = i$ (coefficient for $(1+i)^1$).
4. Subtract and divide: $w_1 = (2+i - i(1+i))/(1+i) = 1$.
5. Final term: $d_2 = w_1 = 1$ (coefficient for $(1+i)^2$).

Thus, the revolving binary representation of $3 + 2i$ is

$$
3 + 2i = (1+i)^0 + i(1+i)^1 + (1+i)^2.
$$

This completes the proof. Every nonzero Gaussian integer has a unique revolving binary representation.
