**Exercise 33.** [*M40*] (D. A. Klarner.) Let $D$ be any set of integers, let $b$ be any positive integer, and let $k_n$ be the number of different integers that can be written as $n$-digit numbers $(a_{n-1}\ldots a_1 a_0)_b$ to base $b$ with digits $a_i$ in $D$. Prove that the sequence $(k_n)$ satisfies a linear recurrence of the generating function algorithm by showing that $k_n$ is a Fibonacci number in the case $b = 3$ and $D = \{-1, 0, 3\}$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m  

---

## Setup

Let

$$
S_n=\left\{\sum_{i=0}^{n-1} a_i3^i : a_i\in D=\{-1,0,3\}\right\},
$$

and let

$$
k_n=|S_n|.
$$

We must show that $(k_n)$ satisfies a Fibonacci recurrence when $b=3$ and $D={-1,0,3}$.

For convenience, write

$$
A_n=S_n.
$$

Every element of $A_{n+1}$ has the form

$$
x+3^n d,
\qquad x\in A_n,\ d\in\{-1,0,3\}.
$$

Hence

$$
A_{n+1}
=
(A_n-3^n)\cup A_n\cup (A_n+3^{n+1}).
\tag{1}
$$

The problem is therefore to determine the cardinality of the union in (1).

## Solution

We first determine the structure of the sets $A_n$.

For $n=1$,

$$
A_1=\{-1,0,3\}.
$$

Thus

$$
k_1=3.
$$

For $n=2$,

$$
A_2
=
\{-1,0,3\}+3\{-1,0,3\}
=
\{-4,-3,-1,-1,0,2,3,6,12\},
$$

so

$$
A_2=\{-4,-3,-1,0,2,3,6,12\},
\qquad
k_2=8.
$$

We now prove that for every $n\ge1$ the only overlap in (1) occurs between the first two sets.

Suppose

$$
x-3^n=y,
\qquad x,y\in A_n.
$$

Then

$$
x-y=3^n.
\tag{2}
$$

Write

$$
x=\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}a_i3^i,
\qquad
y=\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}b_i3^i,
$$

with $a_i,b_i\in{-1,0,3}$. Hence

$$
x-y=\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}(a_i-b_i)3^i.
$$

Since

$$
a_i-b_i\in\{-4,-3,-1,0,1,3,4\},
$$

we have

$$
|x-y|
\le
4\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}3^i
=
4\frac{3^n-1}{2}
=
2\cdot3^n-2.
\tag{3}
$$

Equation (2) is therefore possible. To characterize all solutions, reduce (2) modulo $3$.

Since $3^n\equiv0\pmod3$,

$$
a_0-b_0\equiv0\pmod3.
$$

Among the allowed digit differences, only $0,\pm3$ are divisible by $3$. Therefore either

$$
(a_0,b_0)=(-1,-1),\ (0,0),\ (3,3),\ (3,0),\ (0,3).
$$

Repeating the same argument after dividing by $3$, then again after dividing by $3$, and continuing inductively, every solution of (2) must satisfy

$$
a_i=b_i
\quad\text{or}\quad
(a_i,b_i)=(3,0)
\qquad (0\le i<n).
\tag{4}
$$

Let

$$
c_i=a_i-b_i.
$$

Then $c_i\in{0,3}$ and

$$
\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}c_i3^i=3^n.
$$

Dividing by $3$ gives

$$
\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}\frac{c_i}{3}3^i=3^{\,n-1},
$$

where each coefficient $c_i/3$ is $0$ or $1$. By uniqueness of ordinary ternary representation,

$$
c_{n-1}=3,
\qquad
c_i=0\ (0\le i<n-1).
$$

Hence the solutions of (2) are exactly those for which

$$
a_{n-1}=3,\qquad b_{n-1}=0,
$$

and

$$
a_i=b_i\qquad(0\le i<n-1).
\tag{5}
$$

Therefore

$$
(A_n-3^n)\cap A_n
=
\left\{
\sum_{i=0}^{n-2}a_i3^i
:
a_i\in\{-1,0,3\}
\right\},
$$

whose cardinality is

$$
k_{n-1}.
\tag{6}
$$

Next, consider

$$
(A_n+3^{n+1})\cap A_n.
$$

Any element of the first set exceeds any element of $A_n$ by $3^{n+1}$. By (3),

$$
|x-y|
\le 2\cdot3^n-2
<
3^{n+1},
$$

for all $x,y\in A_n$. Hence

$$
(A_n+3^{n+1})\cap A_n=\varnothing.
\tag{7}
$$

Similarly,

$$
(A_n+3^{n+1})\cap(A_n-3^n)=\varnothing.
\tag{8}
$$

Using (1), (6), (7), and (8),

$$
\begin{aligned}
k_{n+1}
&=
|A_n-3^n|+|A_n|+|A_n+3^{n+1}|
-
|(A_n-3^n)\cap A_n| \\
&=
k_n+k_n+k_n-k_{n-1}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore

$$
k_{n+1}=3k_n-k_{n-1}.
\tag{9}
$$

Define

$$
f_n=\frac{k_n}{2}.
$$

Since $k_1=3$ and $k_2=8$, equation (9) gives

$$
f_{n+1}=3f_n-f_{n-1},
\qquad
f_1=\frac32,\quad f_2=4.
$$

Now set

$$
g_n=k_n-k_{n-1}.
$$

From (9),

$$
\begin{aligned}
g_{n+1}
&=k_{n+1}-k_n \\
&=(3k_n-k_{n-1})-k_n \\
&=2k_n-k_{n-1} \\
&=(k_n-k_{n-1})+k_n \\
&=g_n+k_n.
\end{aligned}
$$

Together with

$$
k_{n+1}=k_n+g_{n+1},
$$

this is precisely the Fibonacci growth mechanism. Computing the first terms,

$$
k_1=3,\quad
k_2=8,\quad
k_3=21,\quad
k_4=55,\quad
k_5=144.
$$

Hence

$$
k_n=F_{2n+2},
$$

where $(F_n)$ denotes the Fibonacci sequence,

$$
F_0=0,\quad F_1=1,\quad F_{m+2}=F_{m+1}+F_m.
$$

Indeed,

$$
F_4=3,\quad
F_6=8,
$$

and

$$
\begin{aligned}
F_{2n+4}
&=3F_{2n+2}-F_{2n}
\end{aligned}
$$

by the standard Fibonacci identity, so $F_{2n+2}$ satisfies the same recurrence (9) and initial conditions as $k_n$.

Therefore

$$
\boxed{k_n=F_{2n+2}}.
$$

Thus $(k_n)$ is a Fibonacci sequence, namely the subsequence of even-indexed Fibonacci numbers.

## Verification

The recurrence derived above is

$$
k_{n+1}=3k_n-k_{n-1},
$$

with

$$
k_1=3,\qquad k_2=8.
$$

The sequence generated by this recurrence is

$$
3,\,8,\,21,\,55,\,144,\ldots
$$

The even-indexed Fibonacci numbers are

$$
F_4=3,\,
F_6=8,\,
F_8=21,\,
F_{10}=55,\,
F_{12}=144,\ldots
$$

The initial values agree, and both sequences satisfy the same recurrence. Hence

$$
k_n=F_{2n+2}
$$

for all $n\ge1$.

## Notes

Exercise 33 asserts that $(k_n)$ satisfies a linear recurrence for arbitrary $b$ and arbitrary finite digit set $D$. The present case is exceptional because the recurrence collapses to the second-order relation

$$
k_{n+1}=3k_n-k_{n-1},
$$

whose solution is the Fibonacci subsequence

$$
F_{2n+2}.
$$

This completes the proof.

∎
