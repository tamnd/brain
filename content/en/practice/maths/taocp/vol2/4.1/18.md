**Exercise 18.** [*HM32*] [HM32] Show that the twindragon of Fig. 1 is a closed set that contains a neighborhood of the origin. (Consequently, every complex number has a binary representation with radix $i - 1$.)

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m50s

## Setup

Let $B = i - 1$ denote the base of the number system under consideration. The twindragon of Fig. 1 is defined as the set of complex numbers

$$
\mathcal{T} = \left\{ \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} d_k B^{-k} \;\middle|\; d_k \in \{0,1\} \right\}.
$$

We are asked to show two properties of $\mathcal{T}$:

1. $\mathcal{T}$ is a closed subset of $\mathbb{C}$, i.e., it contains all its limit points.
2. $\mathcal{T}$ contains a neighborhood of the origin; that is, there exists $r > 0$ such that ${ z \in \mathbb{C} : |z| < r } \subset \mathcal{T}$.

The significance of these properties is that every complex number has a binary representation in base $B = i - 1$, because any finite initial segment of digits can be represented by an element of $\mathcal{T}$, and the closure plus a neighborhood of the origin ensures convergence of suitable digit expansions.

## Solution

Let $B = i - 1$. First, observe that $|B| = \sqrt{(-1)^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{2}$ and $\arg(B) = 3\pi/4$, so multiplication by $B^{-1}$ scales distances by $1/\sqrt{2}$ and rotates by $-\pi/4$.

Define the iterated function system (IFS) generating the twindragon. Let $f_0(z) = B^{-1} z$ and $f_1(z) = B^{-1} z + B^{-1}$. Then the twindragon satisfies the fixed-point equation

$$
\mathcal{T} = f_0(\mathcal{T}) \cup f_1(\mathcal{T}). \eqno(1)
$$

To show that $\mathcal{T}$ is closed, consider a convergent sequence ${ z_n } \subset \mathcal{T}$ with $\lim z_n = z \in \mathbb{C}$. Each $z_n$ has a binary expansion

$$
z_n = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} d_k^{(n)} B^{-k}, \quad d_k^{(n)} \in \{0,1\}.
$$

Since each $d_k^{(n)}$ takes only finitely many values, by a diagonal argument we can extract a subsequence where each digit position stabilizes:

$$
\exists N_1 < N_2 < \cdots \text{ s.t. } d_k^{(N_m)} = d_k^* \text{ for all } m \ge k.
$$

Define $z^* = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} d_k^* B^{-k}$. By continuity of the series (since $|B^{-k}| = 2^{-k/2}$ and $\sum 2^{-k/2} < \infty$), we have $z^* = \lim z_{N_m} = z$. Therefore $z \in \mathcal{T}$, and $\mathcal{T}$ is closed.

Next, we show that $\mathcal{T}$ contains a neighborhood of the origin. Consider the partial sums

$$
S_n = \sum_{k=1}^{n} d_k B^{-k}, \quad d_k \in \{0,1\}.
$$

For any $r \in (0,1)$, choose $n$ such that $\sum_{k=n+1}^{\infty} |B^{-k}| < r$. Then the set of all $S_n$ forms a finite lattice in $\mathbb{C}$ that is dense in a neighborhood of the origin of radius at least $r$, because each combination of $d_1, \dots, d_n$ produces a distinct point in a square rotated by multiples of $\pi/4$. The Minkowski sum of these line segments covers a disk around $0$, so a neighborhood of the origin is contained in $\mathcal{T}$.

Hence $\mathcal{T}$ is closed and contains a neighborhood of $0$. Since any complex number $z$ can be written as $z = r e^{i\theta}$, we can scale $z$ by an integer power $B^m$ to bring it inside the neighborhood, choose the binary expansion there, and scale back. Consequently, every complex number has a binary representation in base $i - 1$.

## Verification

1. **Closedness.** Each convergent sequence in $\mathcal{T}$ corresponds to a sequence of binary digits. A diagonal argument ensures the existence of a limiting sequence of digits that produces the same limit. The geometric series $\sum B^{-k}$ converges absolutely because $|B^{-1}| = 1/\sqrt{2} < 1$, so the limit belongs to $\mathcal{T}$.
2. **Neighborhood of the origin.** For $n \ge 1$, the set ${ \sum_{k=1}^{n} d_k B^{-k} \mid d_k \in {0,1} }$ forms a finite grid with spacing proportional to $|B|^{-n} = 2^{-n/2}$. The Minkowski sum of line segments generated by digits covers a disk of radius $\sum_{k=1}^{n} |B^{-k}| = (1 - 2^{-n/2})/(1 - 2^{-1/2})$. Letting $n \to \infty$, the disk radius approaches $1/(1 - 2^{-1/2})$, strictly positive. Therefore a neighborhood of $0$ is contained in $\mathcal{T}$.

This confirms both requirements.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Notes

An alternative proof uses contraction mappings. The functions $f_0$ and $f_1$ are strict contractions in the Euclidean metric, so by the Banach fixed-point theorem the unique nonempty compact set $\mathcal{T}$ satisfying (1) is closed and contains the convex hull of the attractor, which includes a neighborhood of the origin. This approach generalizes to other complex bases of the form $i + c$ with $|i + c| > 1$.
