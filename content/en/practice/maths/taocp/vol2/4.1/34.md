**Exercise 34.** &#9654; [*22*] (G. W. Reitwiesner, 1960.) Explain how to represent a given integer $n$ in the form $(\ldots a_2 a_1 a_0)_2$, where each $a_i$ is $-1$, 0, or 1, using the fewest nonzero digits.

**Solve time:** 1m53s

## Solution

Let

$$
n=\sum_{i\ge0} a_i2^i,
\qquad a_i\in\{-1,0,1\}.
$$

Such a representation is called a signed binary expansion. The problem is to find a representation with the minimum possible number of nonzero digits.

The desired expansion is obtained by the following rule. Starting with the given integer $n$, repeatedly determine the least significant digit and divide by $2$.

If $n=0$, stop.

If $n$ is even, set

$$
a_0=0,\qquad n\leftarrow n/2.
$$

If $n$ is odd and $n=\pm1$, set

$$
a_0=n,\qquad n\leftarrow0.
$$

If $n$ is odd and $|n|>1$, exactly one of $n-1$ and $n+1$ is divisible by $4$. Choose

$$
a_0=
\begin{cases}
 1,&n\equiv1\pmod4,\\
-1,&n\equiv3\pmod4,
\end{cases}
$$

and replace $n$ by

$$
n\leftarrow \frac{n-a_0}{2}.
$$

Equivalently, for odd $n$ with $|n|>1$,

$$
a_0=2-(n\bmod4),
$$

where $n\bmod4\in{1,3}$.

The process terminates because at each step $|n|$ decreases.

Let the resulting digits be $a_0,a_1,\ldots$. Then

$$
n=a_0+2n_1
   =a_0+2a_1+2^2n_2
   =\cdots
   =\sum_{i\ge0}a_i2^i,
$$

so the algorithm indeed produces a valid representation.

It remains to prove that no representation can contain fewer nonzero digits.

Let

$$
n=\sum_{i\ge0} b_i2^i,
\qquad b_i\in\{-1,0,1\},
$$

be any representation having the minimum possible number of nonzero digits.

If $n$ is even, then $b_0=0$, since the parity of $n$ equals the parity of $b_0$. Hence every optimal representation of an even integer begins with digit $0$, and after dividing by $2$ the problem reduces to representing $n/2$ optimally.

Now suppose that $n$ is odd.

Then necessarily $b_0=\pm1$. Write

$$
n=b_0+2m.
$$

The number of nonzero digits in the representation of $n$ equals

$$
1+\text{(number of nonzero digits in the representation of }m).
$$

Therefore an optimal representation of $n$ must choose $b_0$ so that $m=(n-b_0)/2$ admits a representation with as few nonzero digits as possible.

Assume $|n|>1$. Then exactly one of the choices $b_0=1$ and $b_0=-1$ makes $m$ even. Indeed,

$$
m=\frac{n-1}{2}
\quad\text{or}\quad
m=\frac{n+1}{2},
$$

and one of $n-1$, $n+1$ is divisible by $4$, while the other is congruent to $2$ modulo $4$.

Suppose $m$ is odd. Every representation of $m$ must begin with a nonzero digit, because odd numbers have odd least significant digit. Hence every representation of $m$ contains at least one nonzero digit in position $0$.

Suppose instead that $m$ is even. Every optimal representation of $m$ begins with digit $0$, as shown above. Thus the least significant digit of the representation of $m$ contributes no nonzero digit.

Consequently, for fixed odd $n$, choosing the sign $b_0$ that makes $(n-b_0)/2$ even cannot increase the number of nonzero digits and decreases it whenever the alternative choice produces an odd quotient. Therefore every minimum-weight representation must choose

$$
b_0=
\begin{cases}
 1,&n\equiv1\pmod4,\\
-1,&n\equiv3\pmod4,
\end{cases}
$$

except for the terminal cases $n=\pm1$.

After this forced choice of $b_0$, optimality of the remaining digits is exactly the same problem for the smaller integer $(n-b_0)/2$. By induction on $|n|$, the algorithm produces an optimal representation at every stage.

Hence the expansion generated above has the minimum possible number of nonzero digits.

This representation is the nonadjacent form: no two consecutive digits are nonzero. Indeed, whenever $a_i=\pm1$, the next quotient is even, so $a_{i+1}=0$. Any representation with adjacent nonzero digits could be replaced locally by one with fewer nonzero digits, therefore the nonadjacent form is uniquely optimal.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Notes

For example,

$$
15=(1111)_2
$$

contains four nonzero digits in ordinary binary notation, whereas the above procedure gives

$$
15=16-1=(1000\overline1)_2,
$$

where $\overline1=-1$. The signed-digit representation therefore uses only two nonzero digits, and no representation using digits $-1,0,1$ can do better.
