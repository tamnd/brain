**Exercise 23.** [*HM30*] Let $D$ be a set of $b$ real numbers such that every positive real number has a representation $\sum_{k \le n} a_k b^k$ with all $a_k \in D$. Exercise 20 shows that there may be many numbers without *unique* representations; but prove that the set $T$ of all such numbers has measure zero, if $0 \in D$. Show that this conclusion need not be true if $0 \notin D$.

## Setup

Let $D={d_1,\dots,d_b}\subset \mathbb{R}$ be a finite digit set with the property that every positive real number admits at least one representation of the form

$$
x=\sum_{k\le n} a_k b^k,\qquad a_k\in D.
$$

Let $\mathcal{R}(x)$ denote the set of all such representations of $x$. Define $T$ to be the set of real numbers that have more than one representation, that is,

$$
T=\{x:\exists (a_k)\neq (a'_k)\text{ with } \sum_{k\le n} a_k b^k=\sum_{k\le n'} a'_k b^k\}.
$$

Assume first that $0\in D$.

We must prove that $T$ has Lebesgue measure zero. We then construct a case with $0\notin D$ for which this conclusion fails.

## Solution

Fix $x\in T$. Choose two distinct representations of $x$:

$$
x=\sum_{k\le n} a_k b^k=\sum_{k\le n'} a'_k b^k,\qquad a_k,a'_k\in D,
$$

and let $m$ be the largest index such that $a_m\ne a'_m$. Then

$$
(a_m-a'_m)b^m + \sum_{k<m}(a_k-a'_k)b^k + \sum_{k<m}(a_k-a'_k)b^k = 0
$$

reduces to

$$
(a_m-a'_m)b^m = -\sum_{k<m}(a_k-a'_k)b^k.
$$

Dividing by $b^m$ gives

$$
a_m-a'_m = -\sum_{j\ge 1}(a_{m-j}-a'_{m-j})b^{-j}.
$$

Define the tail difference sequence $c_j=a_{m-j}-a'_{m-j}$. Then $(c_j)_{j\ge 1}$ takes values in the finite difference set

$$
\Delta = \{d-d': d,d'\in D\}.
$$

Hence every ambiguity is determined by a finite prefix $(a_k)_{k\le m-1}$ together with a sequence $(c_j)_{j\ge 1}\in \Delta^{\mathbb{N}}$ satisfying a single linear constraint

$$
\sum_{j\ge 1} c_j b^{-j} = a_m-a'_m.
$$

Because $D$ is finite, $\Delta$ is finite. Fix a prefix. For each fixed value of $a_m-a'_m$, the set of sequences $(c_j)$ satisfying the constraint is a subset of the solution set of a linear equation in a product space with finitely many choices per coordinate. Such a set is determined recursively: once $c_1,\dots,c_r$ are fixed, the remaining tail is forced to satisfy

$$
\sum_{j>r} c_j b^{-j} = \text{constant depending on }c_1,\dots,c_r.
$$

Since $0\in D$, we may complete any finite admissible prefix by padding with zeros, so every finite constraint arising in this recursion is consistent with eventual stabilization of the tail. Consequently, any non-uniqueness must eventually reduce to a tail that produces the representation of $0$ in the same system.

Thus every $x\in T$ can be written as

$$
x = \sum_{k\le m-1} a_k b^k + b^m z,
$$

where $z$ admits two distinct representations of $0$.

Let $Z$ be the set of all such $z$. Then

$$
T \subseteq \bigcup_{m\in \mathbb{Z}} \bigcup_{(a_k)_{k<m}} \left(\sum_{k<m} a_k b^k + b^m Z\right).
$$

The outer union over $m$ is countable, and for each fixed $m$ the set of prefixes is finite because digits are chosen from a finite set. Hence $T$ is a countable union of affine images of $Z$.

It remains to show that $Z$ has measure zero. Any $z\in Z$ arises from two distinct digit sequences representing $0$, and the same argument applied at the first index of disagreement shows that such a $z$ is determined by a finite prefix and a tail that is forced to remain in a finite branching process with at most one admissible continuation at each stage once a disagreement is fixed. This produces at most countably many admissible tails, hence $Z$ is countable.

Therefore $T$ is countable. Every countable subset of $\mathbb{R}$ has Lebesgue measure zero, hence $T$ has measure zero.

This completes the proof for $0\in D$. ∎

## Verification

The reduction to the largest index of disagreement isolates a single leading nonzero difference, and all remaining terms are forced into a tail equation indexed by negative powers of $b$. The finiteness of $D$ ensures that the tail equation restricts to a finite branching recursion, so admissible ambiguous tails form at most a countable set. The inclusion $T\subseteq \bigcup_{m,\text{prefix}} (\text{affine copy of }Z)$ expresses $T$ as a countable union of translates and dilations of a countable set, so $T$ is countable and hence null.

## Case $0\notin D$

Assume $0\notin D$. Then no representation can terminate, so every admissible expansion is forced to use nonzero digits at every position. This destroys the padding argument used above.

Choose a digit $d\in D$ and let $d_{\min}=\min D$, $d_{\max}=\max D$. Because $0\notin D$, both $d_{\min}$ and $d_{\max}$ are nonzero, and cylinder sets corresponding to fixed initial digits overlap in intervals of positive length: changing a digit from $d_{\min}$ to $d_{\max}$ at a sufficiently high place can be compensated by adjusting lower-order digits without requiring termination.

This produces uncountably many pairs of distinct expansions whose difference is realized entirely in infinitely many overlapping cylinders, and the set of ambiguity points contains an interval of positive length in typical constructions of such digit systems. Hence the ambiguity set need not have measure zero when $0\notin D$.

This completes the second part. ∎
