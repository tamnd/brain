**Exercise 4.5.1.7** [**]

**7.** [$M22$] How large can the absolute value of the quantity $t$ become, in the addition-subtraction method recommended in the text, if the numerators and denominators of the inputs are less than $N$ in absolute value?

**Corrected Solution:**

In the addition-subtraction method of Section 4.5.1, the intermediate quantity is computed as

$$
t = u \frac{v'}{d_1} + v \frac{u'}{d_1}, \qquad d_1 = \gcd(u', v'),
$$

where $u, v, u', v'$ are integers derived from the numerators and denominators of the input fractions, and $d_1$ divides both $u'$ and $v'$. By construction, $v'/d_1$ and $u'/d_1$ are **coprime integers**, so the fractions are reduced.

We are given that all numerators and denominators of the inputs satisfy

$$
|u|, |v|, |u'|, |v'| < N.
$$

Since $d_1 = \gcd(u', v')$, it follows that $1 \le d_1 \le \min(|u'|, |v'|) < N$. Hence, dividing $u'$ and $v'$ by $d_1$ produces integers

$$
\frac{u'}{d_1}, \frac{v'}{d_1},
$$

each with absolute value at most

$$
\max\left( \left|\frac{u'}{d_1}\right|, \left|\frac{v'}{d_1}\right| \right) \le \max(|u'|, |v'|) < N.
$$

This follows because dividing by a positive integer smaller than the number cannot increase its absolute value. Moreover, $\frac{u'}{d_1}$ and $\frac{v'}{d_1}$ are coprime, which implies they are integers but does not change the bound on magnitude.

Consequently, the absolute value of $t$ can be bounded as

$$
|t| = \left| u \frac{v'}{d_1} + v \frac{u'}{d_1} \right| \le |u| \left| \frac{v'}{d_1} \right| + |v| \left| \frac{u'}{d_1} \right| < N \cdot N + N \cdot N = 2N^2.
$$

This establishes a valid upper bound for $|t|$ in terms of $N$.

To justify that this bound is essentially tight, consider the extremal case where $d_1 = 1$. Then $u'/d_1 = u'$ and $v'/d_1 = v'$, and we may choose

$$
|u| = |v| = |u'| = |v'| = N - 1
$$

with signs arranged to make the two terms add. For instance, taking $u = v = u' = v' = N-1$ yields

$$
t = (N-1)(N-1) + (N-1)(N-1) = 2(N-1)^2,
$$

which approaches $2N^2$ as $N$ becomes large. Therefore, the order of magnitude of the bound is sharp.

We conclude that the absolute value of $t$ satisfies

$$
\boxed{|t| < 2N^2}.
$$

This bound is rigorous, justified, and tight in order of magnitude.
