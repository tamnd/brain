**Exercise 8.** &#9654; [**] [$22$] Discuss using $(1/0)$ and $(-1/0)$ as representations for $\infty$ and $-\infty$, and/or as representations of overflow.

Representing $(1/0)$ and $(-1/0)$ as $\infty$ and $-\infty$ corresponds to adjoining two signed points at infinity to the set of rational numbers. In this interpretation, the sign is carried by the numerator while the denominator $0$ indicates unbounded magnitude. Consistent extension of fraction arithmetic requires specifying how these elements interact with finite fractions: for any finite $u/u'$, addition and multiplication can be extended so that $(1/0) + u/u' = (1/0)$ and $(-1/0) + u/u' = (-1/0)$, and similarly $(1/0)\cdot (u/u') = (1/0)$ when $u/u' > 0$ and $(-1/0)$ when $u/u' < 0$, with sign determined by multiplication of signs. Division by a finite nonzero fraction yields $(1/0)$ or $(-1/0)$ according to the sign of the quotient, while division by zero must leave $(1/0)/(0/1)$ and $(-1/0)/(0/1)$ undefined.

Operations involving both infinities require separate conventions. Expressions such as $(1/0) + (-1/0)$ and $(1/0)\cdot 0$ cannot be assigned values consistent with the field laws satisfied by finite fractions, since different limiting interpretations lead to conflicting results. Treating $(1/0)$ and $(-1/0)$ as overflow markers rather than true arithmetic elements avoids these inconsistencies, since overflow is not required to satisfy algebraic identities and may propagate as a distinct state until explicitly handled. In contrast, treating them as projective points produces a partially defined extension of the rationals in which arithmetic remains consistent only when operations avoid indeterminate combinations.
