**Exercise 1.** [*15*] [15] Suggest a reasonable computational method for comparing two fractions, to test whether or not $(u/u') < (v/v')$.

The previous solution does not address the exercise at all. The exercise asks for a method to determine whether

$$
\frac{u}{u'} < \frac{v}{v'},
$$

while the proposed solution discusses an unrelated binary-string problem. The argument must be restarted from first principles.

For positive denominators, the most direct method is cross-multiplication. We have

$$
\frac{u}{u'} < \frac{v}{v'}
$$

if and only if

$$
uv' < vu'.
$$

This follows because multiplication by the positive quantity $u'v'$ preserves the inequality:

$$
\frac{u}{u'} < \frac{v}{v'}
\iff
u v' < v u'.
$$

Thus a comparison of two fractions can be reduced to a comparison of two integer products.

This method avoids the inaccuracies that can arise from converting the fractions to floating-point numbers. It also avoids performing an actual division.

If the integers may be so large that the products $uv'$ and $vu'$ could overflow the available machine arithmetic, a more robust method is based on the Euclidean algorithm. Assume all quantities are nonnegative and denominators are positive.

Write

$$
u = q u' + r,
\qquad
v = q' v' + r',
$$

with

$$
0 \le r < u',
\qquad
0 \le r' < v'.
$$

If $q \ne q'$, then the comparison is decided immediately:

$$
\frac{u}{u'} < \frac{v}{v'}
\iff
q < q'.
$$

If $q=q'$, then

$$
\frac{u}{u'}
=
q+\frac{r}{u'},
\qquad
\frac{v}{v'}
=
q+\frac{r'}{v'},
$$

so the common integer part may be discarded and we need only compare

$$
\frac{r}{u'}
\quad\text{and}\quad
\frac{r'}{v'}.
$$

Provided $r>0$ and $r'>0$,

$$
\frac{r}{u'} < \frac{r'}{v'}
\iff
\frac{u'}{r} > \frac{v'}{r'}.
$$

The comparison has been transformed into another comparison of fractions with smaller numerators. Repeating this process yields essentially the Euclidean algorithm, terminating when one remainder becomes zero.

Hence a reasonable computational method is:

1. Use the test $uv' < vu'$ when integer multiplication can be performed safely.
2. For very large integers where overflow is a concern, compare the fractions by repeatedly comparing quotient parts and taking reciprocals of the fractional remainders, exactly as in the Euclidean algorithm or continued-fraction expansion.

The first method is the simplest and is ordinarily preferred; the second is overflow-safe and closely related to the continued-fraction representation of rational numbers.
