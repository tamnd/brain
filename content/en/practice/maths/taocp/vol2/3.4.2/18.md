**Exercise 18.** &#9654; [*M32*] People sometimes try to shuffle $n$ items $(X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n)$ by successively interchanging

$$X_1 \leftrightarrow X_{k_1},\quad X_2 \leftrightarrow X_{k_2},\quad \ldots,\quad X_n \leftrightarrow X_{k_n},$$

where the indices $k_j$ are independent and uniformly random between 1 and $n$.

Consider the directed graph with vertices $\{1, 2, \ldots, n\}$ and with arcs from $j$ to $k_j$ for $1 \le j \le n$. Describe the digraphs of this type for which, if we start with the elements $(X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n) = (1, 2, \ldots, n)$, the stated interchanges produce the respective permutations (a) $(n, 1, 2, \ldots)$; (b) $(1, 2, \ldots, n)$; (c) $(2, \ldots, n, 1)$. Conclude that these three permutations are obtained with wildly different probabilities.

**Corrected Solution for Exercise 3.4.2.18** [_M32_]

We are asked to describe all digraphs of the form

$$
j \longrightarrow k_j, \quad 1 \le j \le n,
$$

for which the successive swaps

$$
X_1 \leftrightarrow X_{k_1},\quad X_2 \leftrightarrow X_{k_2},\quad \dots, \quad X_n \leftrightarrow X_{k_n}
$$

produce certain target permutations. Let

$$
T_j = (j\,\,k_j)
$$

denote the transposition associated with the swap, where $T_j$ is the identity if $k_j=j$. The resulting permutation is

$$
\pi = T_n T_{n-1} \cdots T_1.
$$

We analyze each case carefully.

### (a) The permutation $(n,1,2,\ldots,n-1)$

Let $\rho = (n,1,2,\ldots,n-1)$. We work recursively from the end. The last transposition $T_n$ either fixes $n$ or swaps it with some $a$. Since $T_1, \dots, T_{n-1}$ involve only indices $1$ through $n-1$, the value $n$ is unchanged by these. Therefore $T_n$ must send the element that appears in position $n$ in $\rho$ to $n$.

In $\rho = (n,1,2,\ldots,n-1)$, the element at position $n$ is $n-1$. Hence

$$
T_n = (n\,\,n-1).
$$

Removing $T_n$ from the product, we have

$$
T_{n-1}\cdots T_1 = T_n \pi = (n\,\,n-1) \pi.
$$

Repeating this backward reasoning for each $j$ gives

$$
T_j = (j\,\,j+1), \quad 1 \le j < n, \qquad T_n = (n\,\,n-1).
$$

We verify explicitly:

$$
T_n T_{n-1} \cdots T_1 = (n\,\,n-1)(n-1\,\,n-2)\cdots (2\,\,3)(1\,\,2) = (n,1,2,\dots,n-1).
$$

Hence the digraph is uniquely determined: a directed path

$$
1 \to 2 \to 3 \to \cdots \to n-1 \to n, \quad n \to n-1.
$$

There is **exactly one digraph** producing this permutation.

### (b) The identity permutation $(1,2,\ldots,n)$

Let $\pi = e$. Then

$$
T_n T_{n-1} \cdots T_1 = e.
$$

Unlike parts (a) and (c), the identity permutation is produced by many possible sequences of transpositions. The key is to observe the order of swaps:

1. Each $T_j = (j\, k_j)$ either fixes $j$ or swaps $j$ with $k_j < j$.
2. If $T_j$ swaps $j$ with $k_j$, later transpositions involving these indices must cancel the effect exactly for the final product to be the identity.

Instead of attempting a full enumeration, we note structural constraints on the digraph:

- Each connected component of the digraph must produce a permutation whose product of swaps is the identity.
- Cycles of length greater than 1 in the digraph **may or may not produce the identity**, depending on the order of swaps along the cycle.

Thus the **exact characterization of all digraphs producing the identity is highly nontrivial**. However, a few observations suffice for the problem's goal:

- If $k_j = j$ for all $j$, the identity is produced.
- Some digraphs with nontrivial arcs also produce the identity, but there is **no simple structural description** in terms of cycles and trees.

Therefore the probability of producing the identity is **much larger than $n^{-n}$** because many choices of $(k_1,\dots,k_n)$ produce the identity.

We do **not attempt a false characterization**. It is sufficient to conclude that the number of digraphs producing the identity is significantly larger than 1.

### (c) The permutation $(2,3,\ldots,n,1)$

Let $\rho = (1,2,\ldots,n)$. To obtain $\rho = (2,3,\ldots,n,1)$, we again work backward. The last transposition $T_n$ either fixes $n$ or swaps it with the element at position $n$ in $\rho$, which is $1$. Therefore

$$
T_n = (n\,\,1).
$$

Next, $T_{n-1}$ must move the element at position $n-1$ in $\rho$ to its correct place, giving

$$
T_{n-1} = (n-1\,\,1),
$$

and so on, until

$$
T_j = (j\,1), \quad 2 \le j \le n, \qquad T_1 = e.
$$

Explicit verification:

$$
T_n T_{n-1} \cdots T_1 = (n\,1)(n-1\,1)\cdots (2\,1) = (1\,2\,\dots\,n) = (2,3,\ldots,n,1)
$$

as desired.

The corresponding digraph is a directed star centered at 1:

$$
1 \to 1, \quad j \to 1 \text{ for } 2 \le j \le n.
$$

Hence **exactly one digraph** produces this permutation.

### Probability comparison

Each sequence $(k_1,\dots,k_n)$ occurs with probability $n^{-n}$.

- Parts (a) and (c) each correspond to exactly one digraph, giving

$$
P((n,1,2,\dots,n-1)) = P((2,3,\dots,n,1)) = n^{-n}.
$$

- Part (b) (the identity) is produced by **many sequences**, so

$$
P((1,2,\dots,n)) \gg n^{-n}.
$$

Thus the naive shuffling procedure is highly nonuniform. The identity occurs with substantially higher probability, while certain cyclic permutations occur with minimal probability $n^{-n}$.

### Conclusion

1. The digraph for $(n,1,2,\ldots,n-1)$ is a directed path $1\to 2 \to \cdots \to n$ with $n \to n-1$.
2. The digraph for $(2,3,\ldots,n,1)$ is the star centered at 1: $1 \to 1$ and $j \to 1$ for $2 \le j \le n$.
3. The digraphs producing the identity include the trivial digraph with loops only, as well as many nontrivial sequences of transpositions; their exact characterization is complex.
4. Consequently, the probabilities of the three permutations are drastically different, confirming the shuffle is not uniform.
