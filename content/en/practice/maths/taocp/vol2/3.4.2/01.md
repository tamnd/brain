## Corrected Solution to Exercise 3.4.2.1 [_M12_]

We are asked to **explain Eq. (1)** in the context of a puzzle represented as an $n \times m$ grid with distinct numbers $1, 2, \dots, nm$, and to reason about the minimum number of swaps needed to make the puzzle solvable along a path visiting consecutive numbers in order.

### Problem Restatement

Let the grid contain numbers $1, 2, \dots, nm$ in arbitrary order. A **solution path** is a sequence of adjacent cells such that each number is visited in increasing order: the first visit to $x+1$ occurs after the first visit to $x$.

A swap exchanges the contents of any two cells. Let the goal be:

1. Determine the minimum number of swaps required to make the puzzle solvable.
2. If exactly one swap suffices, count all distinct valid swap pairs.

We denote by **adjacency** the Manhattan distance between two cells $(r_1, c_1)$ and $(r_2, c_2)$:

$$
d((r_1, c_1), (r_2, c_2)) = |r_1 - r_2| + |c_1 - c_2|.
$$

A necessary condition for solvability is that every consecutive pair of numbers $(i, i+1)$ appears in **adjacent cells**:

$$
d(\text{pos}(i), \text{pos}(i+1)) \le 1.
$$

This follows because a valid path must move from $i$ to $i+1$ without visiting any number out of order.

### Correct Justification of Eq. (1)

Let us denote by $P = {i \in [1, nm-1] : d(\text{pos}(i), \text{pos}(i+1)) > 1}$ the set of **bad pairs**, i.e., consecutive numbers not adjacent. Eq. (1) expresses the **minimum number of swaps** as:

$$
\text{min\_swaps} = 
\begin{cases}
0 & \text{if } P = \varnothing,\\[2mm]
1 & \text{if } |P| = 1 \text{ and a single swap can fix the pair},\\[1mm]
2 & \text{if } |P| > 1 \text{ or one swap cannot fix the single pair}.
\end{cases}
$$

**Justification:**

1. If $P = \varnothing$, every consecutive number pair is adjacent. A valid path exists, so no swaps are needed.
2. If $|P| = 1$, let the bad pair be $(a, a+1)$. To make the puzzle solvable with one swap, we must swap numbers in such a way that:

$$
d(\text{pos}'(a), \text{pos}'(a+1)) \le 1,
$$

where $\text{pos}'$ denotes positions after the swap.

A **formal argument** shows that any single swap fixing a puzzle with exactly one bad pair must involve **at least one element of the bad pair**. Indeed, if neither $a$ nor $a+1$ were moved, their distance would remain $>1$, leaving the puzzle unsolvable. Therefore, considering swaps that include $a$ or $a+1$ is **sufficient** to enumerate all single-swap solutions.
