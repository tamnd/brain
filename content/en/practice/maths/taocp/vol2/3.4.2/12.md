**Exercise 3.4.2.12 [M36]**

We are given that any permutation $\pi$ on ${1,2,\dots,t}$ can be written uniquely as

$$
\pi = (a_1\,2)(a_2\,3)\cdots(a_t\,t), \qquad 1 \le a_j \le j.
$$

We are asked to prove that there exists a unique representation

$$
\pi = (b_t\,2)(b_{t-1}\,3)\cdots(b_1\,t), \qquad 1 \le b_j \le j,
$$

and to describe an algorithm that computes the $b_j$'s from the $a_j$'s in $O(t)$ steps.

### Step 1: Existence

We construct the $b$-representation explicitly. Let $\pi$ be given in its $a$-form. Define a sequence of intermediate permutations $\pi^{(j)}$ by

$$
\pi^{(j)} = (a_1\,2)(a_2\,3)\cdots(a_j\,j), \quad 1 \le j \le t.
$$

Let $x_j = \pi^{(j)}(j)$ denote the final image of $j$ after applying the first $j$ transpositions in the $a$-representation. Then $1 \le x_j \le j$ because each transposition $(a_j,j)$ only swaps $j$ with an element $\le j$.

We now define the $b$'s recursively **from right to left**:

1. Set $b_1 = \pi(1)$.
2. For $j = 2,3,\dots,t$, let $b_j$ be the element that is sent to position $j$ by $\pi$ **after all previous $(b_1,t)\cdots(b_{j-1},(t-j+2))$ transpositions have been applied**. More concretely, define

$$
b_j = (\text{current permutation})^{-1}(j),
$$

where "current permutation" is the product of already determined $(b_1,t),\dots,(b_{j-1},(t-j+2))$ applied to the identity. Then $1 \le b_j \le j$ because only positions $\le j$ are affected when placing element $j$.

This gives a **constructive procedure** to compute $b_1,\dots,b_t$. Therefore, the $b$-representation exists.

### Step 2: Uniqueness

Suppose that there are two sequences $b_1,\dots,b_t$ and $b'_1,\dots,b'_t$ giving the same permutation:

$$
(b_t\,2)(b_{t-1}\,3)\cdots(b_1\,t) = (b'_t\,2)(b'_{t-1}\,3)\cdots(b'_1\,t).
$$

Consider the **smallest index $j$** such that $b_j \ne b'_j$. Then the transposition $(b_j, (t-j+2))$ moves $(t-j+2)$ to a position $b_j \le t-j+2$, whereas $(b'_j, (t-j+2))$ moves it to a different position $b'_j \ne b_j$. This contradicts the equality of the permutations. Therefore $b_j = b'_j$ for all $j$, and the representation is unique.
