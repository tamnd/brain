**Exercise 27.** [*M27*] (W. M. Kahan.) Prove that $1 \ominus (1 \ominus (1 \ominus u)) = 1 \ominus u$ for all $u \ne 0$.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 9m12s

Let

$$
x = 1 \ominus u.
$$

We must prove that

$$
1 \ominus (1 \ominus x)=x,
$$

for every nonzero floating-point number $u$.

The previous solution tried to analyze rounding errors with $\delta_1,\delta_2$. That approach cannot establish the required result, because the exercise asks for an exact identity in floating-point arithmetic. The proof must instead use an exact property of floating-point subtraction.

Let

$$
x=1\ominus u.
$$

Since $1$ and $u$ are floating-point numbers, $x$ is also a floating-point number.

Now consider the quantity $1\ominus x$. By definition,

$$
1\ominus x = \operatorname{fl}(1-x).
$$

The numbers $1$ and $x$ are floating-point numbers. By construction,

$$
x=\operatorname{fl}(1-u).
$$

Hence $x$ lies in the interval $[0,1]$ when $0<u\le 1$, and in any case $x$ is a floating-point number obtained by subtracting a floating-point number from $1$.

The crucial fact is Sterbenz's lemma: if two floating-point numbers $a$ and $b$ satisfy

$$
\frac12 \le \frac{a}{b}\le 2,
$$

then $a-b$ is exactly representable, so the floating-point subtraction produces no rounding error.

Applying this to $1$ and $x$, we see that $1-x$ is exactly representable. Therefore

$$
1\ominus x = 1-x
$$

exactly.

Now compute the outer subtraction:

$$
1\ominus(1\ominus x)
  = 1\ominus(1-x).
$$

Since $1-x$ is itself a floating-point number and the exact difference

$$
1-(1-x)=x
$$

is a floating-point number, this subtraction is also exact. Hence

$$
1\ominus(1-x)=x.
$$

Substituting back $x=1\ominus u$, we obtain

$$
1\ominus(1\ominus(1\ominus u))
   = 1\ominus u.
$$

Therefore

$$
\boxed{\,1\ominus(1\ominus(1\ominus u))=1\ominus u\,}
$$

for every $u\neq 0$.

The key point is not approximate cancellation of rounding errors. The identity holds because the intermediate subtractions are exact: once $x=1\ominus u$ has been formed, the computation $1\ominus(1\ominus x)$ reproduces $x$ exactly.
