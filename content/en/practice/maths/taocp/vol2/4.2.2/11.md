## Solution

We are asked to **prove Lemma T** (TAOCP, Section 4.2.2). We begin by stating the lemma.

**Lemma T (4.2.2.11).** _For floating-point numbers in a normalized base-$\beta$ system with precision $p$, let $x$ and $y$ be positive normalized floating-point numbers. Then_

$$
\operatorname{fl}(x \otimes y) = x \cdot y \, (1 + \delta), \quad |\delta| \le \epsilon,
$$

_where $\epsilon = \tfrac12 \beta^{1-p}$ is the machine epsilon, and $\operatorname{fl}$ denotes the floating-point representation rounded to the nearest._

### Proof

Let the normalized floating-point system be defined by base $\beta$, precision $p$, and exponent range $[E_{\min}, E_{\max}]$. Any positive normalized floating-point number $x$ can be written as

$$
x = m_x \, \beta^{e_x}, \quad 1 \le m_x < \beta,
$$

with a $p$-digit base-$\beta$ mantissa $m_x$ and integer exponent $e_x$. Similarly, write

$$
y = m_y \, \beta^{e_y}.
$$

Consider the exact product

$$
x \cdot y = m_x m_y \, \beta^{e_x + e_y}.
$$

Let $m_x m_y = m_z + \theta$, where $m_z$ is the nearest $p$-digit mantissa representable in the system and $\theta$ is the rounding error in forming the $p$-digit approximation. By the properties of rounding to nearest,

$$
|\theta| \le \tfrac12 \beta^{1-p}.
$$

Hence the floating-point product satisfies

$$
x \otimes y = \operatorname{fl}(x \cdot y) = m_z \, \beta^{e_x + e_y}.
$$

Define

$$
\delta = \frac{x \otimes y - x \cdot y}{x \cdot y}.
$$

Then

$$
|\delta| = \frac{|m_z - m_x m_y|}{m_x m_y} \le \frac{\tfrac12 \beta^{1-p}}{m_x m_y}.
$$

Since $1 \le m_x, m_y < \beta$, it follows that $m_x m_y \ge 1$, so

$$
|\delta| \le \tfrac12 \beta^{1-p} = \epsilon.
$$

Therefore

$$
\operatorname{fl}(x \otimes y) = x \cdot y \, (1 + \delta), \quad |\delta| \le \epsilon.
$$

This establishes Lemma T.

∎

### Remarks

1. The key step is expressing the exact product $m_x m_y$ in terms of the nearest representable $p$-digit mantissa $m_z$ and bounding the relative error.
2. The proof relies only on normalization and rounding-to-nearest properties; no assumptions about exponent range violations are required as long as no overflow or underflow occurs.

This completes the proof of Lemma T.
