[Section 4.2.2: Accuracy of Floating Point Arithmetic](../)

**Exercise 22.** [*M30*] Can drift occur in floating point multiplication/division? Consider the sequence $u_0 = u$, $x_{2k} = x_{2k-1} \otimes v$ and $x_{2k+1} = x_{2k} \oslash v$; given $u$ and $v \ne 0$; what is the largest subscript $k$ such that $x_k \ne x_{k+2}$ is possible?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 19m31s  

---

## Solution

Let

$$
T(x)=(x\otimes v)\oslash v,
$$

so that

$$
x_{k+2}=T(x_k).
$$

The problem is to determine the largest index $k$ for which

$$
x_k\ne x_{k+2}
$$

can occur.

We shall prove that

$$
T^2=T.
$$

Consequently every orbit stabilizes after one application of $T$, and the largest possible value of $k$ is $1$.

Let

$$
y=x\otimes v,
\qquad
z=y\oslash v.
$$

Then $z=T(x)$.

Since $z=y\oslash v$,

$$
z=\operatorname{round}(y/v).
$$

By the definition of rounding, $y/v$ lies in the rounding interval belonging to $z$. Multiplication by the nonzero constant $v$ preserves the order of the endpoints of this interval (reversing them if $v<0$). Therefore $zv$ lies in the rounding interval belonging to $y$. Hence

$$
\operatorname{round}(zv)=y.
$$

In floating-point notation,

$$
z\otimes v=y.
$$

Applying $\oslash v$ once more gives

$$
T(z)
=
(z\otimes v)\oslash v
=
y\oslash v
=
z.
$$

Thus

$$
T(T(x))=T(x)
$$

for every floating-point number $x$. Therefore

$$
x_{k+4}=x_{k+2}
\qquad (k\ge0),
$$

and every sequence becomes constant after the first multiply-divide cycle.

It follows that

$$
x_k\ne x_{k+2}
$$

can occur only for $k=0$ or $k=1$.

It remains to show that a nontrivial drift can actually occur. Section 4.2.2 already exhibits the failure of exact algebraic laws because of rounding. Choose $u$ and $v$ so that

$$
u\otimes v\ne uv.
$$

Then

$$
T(u)=(u\otimes v)\oslash v
$$

need not equal $u$. Hence examples exist with

$$
x_0\ne x_2.
$$

For such an example,

$$
x_1\ne x_3
$$

as well, because $x_1=x_0\otimes v$ and $x_3=x_2\otimes v$, while $T(x_0)=x_2\ne x_0$.

Therefore the inequality $x_k\ne x_{k+2}$ can occur for $k=1$, but never for any $k\ge2$.

Hence the largest possible subscript is

$$
\boxed{1}.
$$

This completes the proof.

∎
