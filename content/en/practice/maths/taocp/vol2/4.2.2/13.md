[Section 4.2.2: Accuracy of Floating Point Arithmetic](../)

**Exercise 13.** &#9654; [*M25*] Some programming languages (and even some compilers) make use of floating point arithmetic only, with no provision for exact calculations with integers. If operations on integers are desired, we can, of course, represent an integer as a floating point number. When the floating point operations satisfy our basic definitions in (9), we know that all floating point operations will be exact, provided that the operands and the answer can each be represented exactly with $p$ significant digits. Therefore, so long as we know that the numbers aren't too large, we can add, subtract, or multiply integers with no inaccuracy due to rounding errors.

But suppose that a programmer wants to determine if $m$ is an exact multiple of $n$, when $m$ and $n \ne 0$ are integers. Suppose further that a subroutine is available to calculate the quantity $\text{round}(u \bmod 1) = u \ominus \lfloor u \rfloor$ for any given floating point number $u$, as in exercise 4.2.1–15. One good way to determine whether or not $m$ is a multiple of $n$ might be to test whether or not $(m \oslash n) \ominus \lfloor m \oslash n \rfloor = 0$, using the assumed subroutine; but perhaps rounding errors in the floating point calculations will invalidate this test in certain cases.

Find suitable conditions on the range of integer values $n \ne 0$ and $m$, such that $m$ is a multiple of $n$ if and only if $(m \oslash n) \ominus \lfloor m \oslash n \rfloor = 0$. In other words, show that if $m$ and $n$ are not too large, this test is valid.

## Solution

Let $m$ and $n\ne 0$ be integers represented exactly as normalized floating point numbers with $p$ significant digits in base $b$. Denote their floating point representations by the same symbols $m$ and $n$. Consider the floating point division

$$
q = m \oslash n = \operatorname{round}(m/n),
$$

where $\operatorname{round}$ is defined as in (9). Because $m$ and $n$ are integers, the exact quotient $m/n$ is either an integer or a non-integer rational number. We wish to determine conditions under which the test

$$
q \ominus \lfloor q \rfloor = 0
$$

correctly identifies whether $m$ is divisible by $n$.

By equation (9), floating point division of two exactly representable numbers with result within the representable range satisfies

$$
m \oslash n = \operatorname{round}(m/n).
$$

If $m/n$ is itself an integer and its absolute value is less than $b^p$, then $m/n$ can be represented exactly as a $p$-digit floating point number. In that case, rounding does not change its value:

$$
\operatorname{round}(m/n) = m/n.
$$

Hence $q = m \oslash n = m/n$ exactly, and consequently

$$
q \ominus \lfloor q \rfloor = q - \lfloor q \rfloor = 0.
$$

Conversely, if $m/n$ is not an integer, then $m/n = k + r$ with integer $k$ and $0 < r < 1$. If $|m/n| < b^p$, then $m/n$ can be represented exactly as a $p$-digit floating point number, so rounding does not change its value:

$$
q = \operatorname{round}(m/n) = k + r.
$$

Then

$$
q \ominus \lfloor q \rfloor = (k + r) \ominus k = r \ne 0.
$$

Therefore the test $q \ominus \lfloor q \rfloor = 0$ correctly distinguishes exact multiples from non-multiples of $n$.

It remains to determine suitable bounds on $m$ and $n$ so that all intermediate results are exactly representable. Let $m/n$ be the quotient and assume $m$ and $n$ are normalized floating point numbers with $p$ significant digits. The exact quotient $m/n$ must satisfy

$$
|m/n| < b^p,
$$

so that it can be represented exactly as a floating point number with $p$ digits. Equivalently, we require

$$
|m| < b^p |n|.
$$

Since $n\ne 0$ is represented exactly, $|n|$ must also satisfy

$$
|n| \le b^p - 1
$$

to avoid loss of significance in representation. Together, the sufficient conditions are

$$
0 < |n| \le b^p - 1, \qquad |m| < b^p |n|.
$$

Under these bounds, $m$, $n$, and $m/n$ are all exactly representable as floating point numbers with $p$ digits, so that

$$
m \text{ is a multiple of } n \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad (m \oslash n) \ominus \lfloor m \oslash n \rfloor = 0.
$$

This completes the argument.
