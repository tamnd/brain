**Exercise 31.** [*M25*] (U. Kulisch.) When the polynomial $2y^2 + 9x^2 - y^4$ is evaluated for $x = .408855776$ and $y = .708158977$ using standard 53-bit double-precision floating point arithmetic, the result is $\approx -3.7 \times 10^{10}$. Evaluating it in the alternative form $2y^2 + (3x^2 - y^2)(3x^2 + y^2)$ gives $\approx +1.0 \times 10^{10}$. The true answer, however, is 1.0 (exactly). Explain how to construct similar examples of numerical instability.

## Solution

The phenomenon arises from catastrophic cancellation. Construct a polynomial that is identically equal to a small quantity, but whose evaluation in floating point arithmetic requires subtracting nearly equal large numbers.

Consider first the exact identity

$$
(a+b)^2-a^2-2ab=b^2.
$$

If $|a|$ is very large and $|b|$ is moderate, the three terms on the left are all of order $a^2$, while the exact result is only $b^2$. The computation therefore depends on cancellations among quantities that are much larger than the final answer.

Let

$$
P(a,b)=(a+b)^2-a^2-2ab.
$$

In exact arithmetic, $P(a,b)=b^2$. Suppose that floating point arithmetic has relative error bounded by about one ulp, say

$$
\operatorname{round}(x)=x(1+\delta), \qquad |\delta|\le \varepsilon.
$$

When $P(a,b)$ is evaluated directly, the intermediate quantities have magnitude approximately $a^2$. Hence the absolute rounding errors introduced into those quantities are approximately

$$
\varepsilon a^2.
$$

After subtraction, these errors remain, while the true result is only $b^2$. Consequently the relative error in the final answer can be as large as

$$
\frac{\varepsilon a^2}{b^2}.
$$

By choosing

$$
a \gg b\,\varepsilon^{-1/2},
$$

this relative error becomes arbitrarily large. The computed value may then have either sign and may differ from the true value by many orders of magnitude.

The example in the exercise has exactly this structure. The polynomial

$$
2y^2+9x^2-y^4
$$

is evaluated at values of $x$ and $y$ chosen so that

$$
2y^2+9x^2 \approx y^4,
$$

while each individual term is large. Thus the exact value equals

$$
1,
$$

but the subtraction of nearly equal quantities destroys almost all significant digits.

A systematic method for constructing similar examples is therefore as follows.

Choose a polynomial identity

$$
F(\mathbf{x})-G(\mathbf{x})=H(\mathbf{x}),
$$

where $F$ and $G$ are large and nearly equal, while $H$ is small. Select arguments $\mathbf{x}$ so that

$$
|F(\mathbf{x})|\approx |G(\mathbf{x})|\gg |H(\mathbf{x})|.
$$

Then evaluate

$$
F(\mathbf{x})-G(\mathbf{x})
$$

in floating point arithmetic. The subtraction magnifies the relative effect of rounding errors by approximately

$$
\frac{|F(\mathbf{x})|}{|H(\mathbf{x})|}.
$$

Since this ratio can be made arbitrarily large, the computed answer can be arbitrarily inaccurate even though each individual arithmetic operation is performed with high relative precision.

A particularly transparent family is obtained from

$$
P_n(x)=(x+1)^n-\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}\binom{n}{k}x^k.
$$

By the binomial theorem,

$$
P_n(x)=1.
$$

For large $x$, each term in the subtraction is of magnitude about $x^n$, whereas the exact result is $1$. Evaluating the expression term by term therefore requires cancellation of nearly all leading digits, and the computed value can be grossly inaccurate. Increasing $x$ increases the instability without changing the exact answer.

Thus numerical instability can be generated whenever an expression is arranged so that large rounded quantities must cancel to produce a small final result. This completes the explanation requested in the exercise.
