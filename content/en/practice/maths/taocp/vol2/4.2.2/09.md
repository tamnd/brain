## Solution

Equation (33) in Section 4.2.2 asserts the following statement for normalized floating point numbers $u$, $v$, and $w$, with $\epsilon_1$, $\epsilon_2$ denoting the relative errors in the floating point operations:

$(u \oplus v) \oplus w = u \oplus (v \oplus w) \quad \text{approximately}, \quad \text{with relative error at most } \epsilon_1 + \epsilon_2. \eqno(33)$

We are asked to prove (33) and to explain why the conclusion cannot be strengthened to $u \approx w$ with relative error $\epsilon_1 + \epsilon_2$.

Let $u$, $v$, and $w$ be normalized floating point numbers. By definition of floating point addition (equation (9)), we have

$(u \oplus v) = (u + v)(1 + \delta_1), \quad |\delta_1| \le \epsilon_1,$

$(v \oplus w) = (v + w)(1 + \delta_2), \quad |\delta_2| \le \epsilon_2,$

where $\epsilon_1, \epsilon_2$ denote bounds on the relative errors for each rounding step.

First, consider the left-hand side $(u \oplus v) \oplus w$:

$$
(u \oplus v) \oplus w = \text{round}((u \oplus v) + w) = \text{round}((u + v)(1 + \delta_1) + w).
$$

Let $\delta_3$ denote the relative error introduced in this second rounding. Then

$$
(u \oplus v) \oplus w = \bigl((u + v)(1 + \delta_1) + w\bigr)(1 + \delta_3), \quad |\delta_3| \le \epsilon_2.
$$

Expanding and neglecting second-order terms $\delta_1 \delta_3$, which are of order $\epsilon^2$, we obtain

$$
(u \oplus v) \oplus w \approx (u + v + w)(1 + \delta_1 + \delta_3).
$$

Similarly, for the right-hand side $u \oplus (v \oplus w)$, let $\delta_4$ denote the rounding error in $v \oplus w$ and $\delta_5$ denote the rounding error in adding $u$:

$$
v \oplus w = (v + w)(1 + \delta_4), \quad |\delta_4| \le \epsilon_1,
$$

$$
u \oplus (v \oplus w) = \text{round}\bigl(u + (v + w)(1 + \delta_4)\bigr) = \bigl(u + (v + w)(1 + \delta_4)\bigr)(1 + \delta_5), \quad |\delta_5| \le \epsilon_2.
$$

Expanding and neglecting second-order terms $\delta_4 \delta_5$, we obtain

$$
u \oplus (v \oplus w) \approx (u + v + w)(1 + \delta_4 + \delta_5).
$$

In both cases, the accumulated relative error is bounded in magnitude by $|\delta_1 + \delta_3| \le \epsilon_1 + \epsilon_2$ and $|\delta_4 + \delta_5| \le \epsilon_1 + \epsilon_2$. Hence

$$
(u \oplus v) \oplus w \sim u \oplus (v \oplus w), \quad \text{with relative error at most } \epsilon_1 + \epsilon_2,
$$

which establishes equation (33).

To see why the conclusion cannot be strengthened to $u \approx w$ with relative error $\epsilon_1 + \epsilon_2$, observe that in general $u$ and $w$ may differ by an amount that is much larger than the sum of the rounding errors. The approximate equality $\sim$ in (33) refers to the relative error in the computed sums $(u \oplus v) \oplus w$ and $u \oplus (v \oplus w)$, not to the values of $u$ and $w$ themselves. For example, if $u = 1$, $v = 10^4$, and $w = 1$, then $u + w = 2$ is negligible compared with $v$, and rounding errors in adding $v$ dominate the small terms. Therefore, it is possible that $u \oplus (v \oplus w)$ differs from $w$ by an arbitrarily large factor, while the accumulated relative error of the sums remains bounded by $\epsilon_1 + \epsilon_2$. Strengthening the conclusion to $u \approx w$ would ignore this fundamental dependence on the magnitudes of $u$, $v$, and $w$.

This completes the proof.
