[Section 4.2.2: Accuracy of Floating Point Arithmetic](../)

**Exercise 23.** &#9654; [*M26*] Prove or disprove: $u \oslash (u \ominus u) = [u]$, for all floating point $u$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 9m32s  

---

The statement is **false**.

Recall the meaning of the floating-point operations. For floating-point numbers $x$ and $y$,

$$
x \ominus y = [x-y],
\qquad
x \oslash y = [x/y],
$$

where $[\cdot]$ denotes the floating-point result obtained by rounding the exact value.

For any floating-point number $u$,

$$
u \ominus u = [u-u] = [0] = 0.
$$

Hence the left-hand side becomes

$$
u \oslash (u \ominus u)
=
u \oslash 0.
$$

Now consider $u=1$, which is certainly a floating-point number. Then

$$
1 \ominus 1 = 0,
$$

so

$$
1 \oslash (1 \ominus 1)
=
1 \oslash 0.
$$

But $1 \oslash 0$ is not equal to $[1]=1$. Depending on the floating-point system, division by zero either produces $+\infty$ (IEEE arithmetic) or is undefined/traps. In either case it is not equal to $1$.

Thus

$$
1 \oslash (1 \ominus 1)\neq [1],
$$

providing a counterexample.

For completeness, if $u=0$, then

$$
0 \oslash (0 \ominus 0)
=
0 \oslash 0,
$$

which is likewise undefined or NaN, not $[0]=0$.

Therefore the proposition

$$
u \oslash (u \ominus u) = [u]
$$

does **not** hold for all floating-point $u$.

$$
\boxed{\text{The statement is false.}}
$$
