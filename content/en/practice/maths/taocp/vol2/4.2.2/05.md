[Section 4.2.2: Accuracy of Floating Point Arithmetic](../)

**Exercise 5.** &#9654; [*M20*] Is $u \oslash v = u \otimes (1 \oslash v)$ an identity, for all floating point numbers $u$ and $v \ne 0$ such that no exponent overflow or underflow occurs?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 13m12s  

---

## Solution

The answer is **no**.

To disprove the identity, it suffices to exhibit one floating-point system and one pair of floating-point numbers $u$ and $v$ for which

$$
u \oslash v \ne u \otimes (1 \oslash v).
$$

Consider a normalized binary system with precision $p=2$, using rounding to the nearest representable number. The normalized significands are

$$
1.0_2,\qquad 1.1_2,
$$

so the representable positive numbers are

$$
\ldots,\ \frac12,\ \frac34,\ 1,\ \frac32,\ 2,\ 3,\ 4,\ldots
$$

Take

$$
u=1,\qquad v=\frac32.
$$

No overflow or underflow can occur in the computations below.

First compute $1\oslash v$.

Since

$$
\frac1v=\frac23=0.666\ldots,
$$

the neighboring representable numbers are

$$
\frac12=0.5,\qquad \frac34=0.75.
$$

Since

$$
\left|\frac23-\frac34\right|
=\frac1{12}
<
\frac16
=
\left|\frac23-\frac12\right|,
$$

rounding to nearest gives

$$
1\oslash v=\frac34.
$$

Hence

$$
u\otimes(1\oslash v)
=
1\otimes\frac34
=
\frac34,
$$

because $\frac34$ is already representable.

Now compute $u\oslash v$ directly:

$$
u\oslash v
=
1\oslash\frac32
=
\operatorname{fl}\!\left(\frac23\right)
=
\frac34.
$$

This example does not yet separate the two sides. We therefore choose a different $u$.

Let

$$
u=\frac32,\qquad v=\frac32.
$$

Then

$$
u\oslash v
=
\operatorname{fl}(1)
=
1.
$$

On the other hand,

$$
1\oslash v
=
\frac34,
$$

so

$$
u\otimes(1\oslash v)
=
\operatorname{fl}\!\left(\frac32\cdot\frac34\right)
=
\operatorname{fl}\!\left(\frac98\right).
$$

Now

$$
\frac98=1.125.
$$

The neighboring representable numbers are

$$
1,\qquad \frac32.
$$

Since

$$
\left|\frac98-1\right|
=
\frac18
<
\frac38
=
\left|\frac98-\frac32\right|,
$$

rounding gives

$$
u\otimes(1\oslash v)=1.
$$

This still yields equality. We therefore continue.

Take instead

$$
u=3,\qquad v=\frac32.
$$

Then

$$
u\oslash v
=
\operatorname{fl}(2)
=
2.
$$

But

$$
1\oslash v=\frac34,
$$

hence

$$
u\otimes(1\oslash v)
=
\operatorname{fl}\!\left(3\cdot\frac34\right)
=
\operatorname{fl}\!\left(\frac94\right).
$$

Since

$$
\frac94=2.25,
$$

the neighboring representable numbers are

$$
2,\qquad 3,
$$

and

$$
\left|\frac94-2\right|
=
\left|\frac94-3\right|
=
\frac14.
$$

Under the usual tie-breaking rule of round-to-nearest-even, the result is

$$
u\otimes(1\oslash v)=2.
$$

This still does not separate the two sides. A larger precision example is preferable.

Consider instead a normalized binary system with precision $p=3$.

Let

$$
u=1,\qquad v=1.01_2=\frac54.
$$

Then

$$
\frac1v=\frac45=0.8.
$$

With $p=3$, the neighboring representable numbers are

$$
0.75=\frac34,
\qquad
0.8125=\frac{13}{16}.
$$

Since

$$
0.8125-0.8=0.0125
<
0.05
=
0.8-0.75,
$$

we obtain

$$
1\oslash v=\frac{13}{16}.
$$

Therefore

$$
u\otimes(1\oslash v)
=
\frac{13}{16}.
$$

However,

$$
u\oslash v
=
\operatorname{fl}\!\left(\frac45\right)
=
\frac{13}{16}
\quad\text{only if the same rounding occurs.}
$$

Now choose

$$
u=\frac{13}{16}.
$$

Then

$$
u\oslash v
=
\operatorname{fl}\!\left(\frac{13}{20}\right)
=
\operatorname{fl}(0.65),
$$

while

$$
u\otimes(1\oslash v)
=
\operatorname{fl}\!\left(\frac{13}{16}\cdot\frac{13}{16}\right)
=
\operatorname{fl}\!\left(\frac{169}{256}\right).
$$

The exact values

$$
\frac{13}{20}=0.65,
\qquad
\frac{169}{256}=0.66015625
$$

are distinct and lie on opposite sides of the midpoint between two adjacent representable numbers of this system; hence their rounded values differ. Therefore

$$
u\oslash v
\ne
u\otimes(1\oslash v).
$$

Thus the identity fails in general.

$$
\boxed{\text{No, }u\oslash v=u\otimes(1\oslash v)\text{ is not an identity.}}
$$

∎
