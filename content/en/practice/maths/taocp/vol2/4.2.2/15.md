**Exercise 15.** &#9654; [*M24*] (H. Björk.) Does the computed midpoint of an interval always lie between the endpoints? (In other words, does $u \le v$ imply that $u \lesssim (u \oplus v) \oslash 2 \lesssim v$?)

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 6m28s  

---

## Solution

We are asked whether, in floating-point arithmetic, the computed midpoint of an interval always lies between the endpoints. In other words, does

$$
u \le v \implies u \lesssim (u \oplus v) \oslash 2 \lesssim v
$$

hold for all floating-point numbers $u$ and $v$? Here, $\oplus$ and $\oslash$ denote floating-point addition and division with rounding to nearest, and $\lesssim$ is the approximate-order relation defined in Section 4.2.2.

We will show that the implication is **not universally valid** by constructing an explicit counterexample.

### Step 1: Choose a floating-point system

Let us consider a simple floating-point system in **radix 2** with **precision $p=2$**, i.e., each normalized number has two significant binary digits. The normalized positive numbers are of the form

$$
x = 1.b_1 \times 2^e
$$

where $b_1 \in \{0,1\}$ and $e$ is any integer, and rounding is to the nearest floating-point number.

With this system, the normalized numbers for small exponents are:

$$
1.0, 1.1, 10_2=2, 11_2=3, 100_2=4, \dots
$$

and the rounding of a real number is done to the nearest representable floating-point number.

### Step 2: Identify a candidate interval

We need two floating-point numbers $u < v$ such that computing the midpoint in floating-point arithmetic produces a value **outside the interval**, violating either

$$
u \lesssim (u \oplus v) \oslash 2 \quad \text{or} \quad (u \oplus v) \oslash 2 \lesssim v.
$$

Let us take

$$
u = 1.1_2 = 1.5, \quad v = 10_2 = 2.
$$

Both $u$ and $v$ are exactly representable in this system.

### Step 3: Compute the floating-point sum

The exact sum is

$$
u + v = 1.5 + 2 = 3.5.
$$

The two nearest floating-point numbers with $p=2$ digits are $11_2 = 3$ and $100_2 = 4$. The number $3.5$ lies **exactly midway**, so rounding to nearest with tie-to-even selects $4$. Hence

$$
u \oplus v = 4.
$$

### Step 4: Compute the floating-point midpoint

Now divide by 2 in floating-point arithmetic:

$$
(u \oplus v) \oslash 2 = 4 \oslash 2.
$$

Exact division gives $2$, which is representable. Therefore,

$$
(u \oplus v) \oslash 2 = 2.
$$

### Step 5: Compare with endpoints

We have

$$
u = 1.5, \quad v = 2, \quad (u \oplus v) \oslash 2 = 2.
$$

The approximate-order relation $\lesssim$ is defined so that $x \lesssim y$ allows for rounding errors. In this system, $2 \not\lesssim 2$ is false only if the computed value **exceeds $v$ in exact arithmetic**, but here the computed midpoint equals $v$. Therefore the inequality **is not violated**.

We need a case where the rounding **pushes the sum above the representable range so that the midpoint exceeds $v$**.

### Step 6: Construct a true counterexample

Consider the same system with **precision $p=2$** and numbers at a higher exponent:

$$
u = 11_2 \times 2^1 = 6, \quad v = 100_2 \times 2^1 = 8.
$$

Then the exact sum is

$$
u + v = 6 + 8 = 14.
$$

The nearest floating-point numbers with $p=2$ are $12$ and $16$. The number $14$ is closer to $16$ than $12$, so

$$
u \oplus v = 16.
$$

Now divide by 2 in floating-point arithmetic:

$$
(u \oplus v) \oslash 2 = 16 \oslash 2 = 8.
$$

The upper endpoint is $v = 8$. The computed midpoint equals the **upper endpoint**, which is allowed by the approximate-order relation.

To **exceed** $v$, choose

$$
u = 11_2 \times 2^1 = 6, \quad v = 11.1_2 \times 2^1 = 7.
$$

Then $u+v = 13$. The nearest floating-point numbers are $12$ and $16$; rounding gives

$$
u \oplus v = 16.
$$

Divide by 2:

$$
(u \oplus v) \oslash 2 = 16 \oslash 2 = 8.
$$

Now the computed midpoint **exceeds the upper endpoint** $v = 7$. Therefore

$$
(u \oplus v) \oslash 2 \not\lesssim v,
$$

and the implication fails.

### Step 7: Conclusion

We have exhibited a floating-point system and specific floating-point numbers

$$
u = 6, \quad v = 7
$$

for which the computed midpoint

$$
(u \oplus v) \oslash 2 = 8
$$

does **not** satisfy

$$
(u \oplus v) \oslash 2 \lesssim v.
$$

Hence the property

$$
u \le v \implies u \lesssim (u \oplus v) \oslash 2 \lesssim v
$$

is **not universally valid**.

$$
\boxed{\text{No, the computed midpoint need not always lie between the endpoints.}}
$$
