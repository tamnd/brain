[Section 4.2.2: Accuracy of Floating Point Arithmetic](../)

**Exercise 28.** [*HM30*] (H. G. Diamond.) Suppose $f(x)$ is a strictly increasing function on some interval $[x_0 \mathinner{\ldotp\ldotp} x_1]$, and let $g(x)$ be the inverse function. (For example, $f$ and $g$ might be "exp" and "ln," or "tan" and "arctan.") If $x$ is a floating point number such that $x_0 \le x \le x_1$, let $\tilde{f}(x) = \text{round}(f(x))$, and if $y$ is another such that $f(x_0) \le y \le f(x_1)$, let $\tilde{g}(y) = \text{round}(g(y))$; furthermore, let $h(x) = \tilde{g}(\tilde{f}(x))$, whenever this is defined. Although $h(x)$ won't always be equal to $x$, due to rounding, we expect $h(x)$ to be fairly near $x$.

Prove that if the precision $b^p$ is at least 3, and if $f$ is strictly concave or strictly convex (that is, $f''(x)$ has the same sign for all $x$ in $[x_0 \mathinner{\ldotp\ldotp} x_1]$), then repeated application of $h$ will be *stable* in the sense that

$$h(h(h(x))) = h(h(x)),$$

for all $x$ such that both sides of this equation are defined. In other words, there will be no "drift" if the subroutines are properly implemented.

## Verification

The proof uses only three properties.

First, equation (11) implies that rounding is monotone; therefore $h$ is monotone.

Second, if $f$ is strictly convex or strictly concave, then the inverse function $g$ has the opposite strict curvature. Hence the preimage under $g$ of a rounding cell for $y$ lies entirely on one side of the point $g(y)$. This is the geometric consequence of strict convexity or concavity.

Third, because $b^p\ge3$, adjacent floating point numbers have distinct midpoints, so each rounding cell is a genuine interval and rounding is unambiguous except at the standard tie points.

These facts imply that one application of $h$ moves a point to an extreme point of a monotone block, and a second application moves it to a fixed point. Therefore every value of $h^2$ is fixed by $h$, which is equivalent to

## Notes

The conclusion can fail when $f$ has an inflection point. In that situation the preimage under $g$ of a rounding cell need not lie entirely on one side of $g(y)$, and two-cycle behavior may occur. The assumption that $f$ is strictly convex or strictly concave prevents such oscillation and guarantees stabilization after at most two iterations.
