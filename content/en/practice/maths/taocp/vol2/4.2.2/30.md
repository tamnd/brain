[Section 4.2.2: Accuracy of Floating Point Arithmetic](../)

**Exercise 30.** [*M30*] (W. M. Kahan.) Let $f(x) = 1 + x + \cdots + x^{106} = (1 - x^{107})/(1 - x)$ for $x < 1$, and let $g(y) = f((\frac{1}{3} - y^2)(3 + 3.45y^2))$ for $0 < y < 1$. Evaluate $g(y)$ on one or more pocket calculators, for $y = 10^{-7}$, $10^{-8}$, $10^{-9}$, $10^{-10}$, and explain all inaccuracies in the results you obtain. Since most present-day calculators do not round correctly, the results are often surprising. Note that $g(\epsilon) = 107 - 10491.35\epsilon^2 + 65974 9.9625\epsilon^4 - 30141386.2 6625\epsilon^6 + O(\epsilon^8)$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 12m38s

## Setup

Let

$$
x(y)=\left(\frac13-y^2\right)(3+3.45y^2).
$$

Then

$$
x(y)=1-1.85y^2-3.45y^4.
$$

Hence

$$
g(y)=f(x(y))
=\frac{1-x(y)^{107}}{1-x(y)}.
$$

The mathematical value of $g(y)$ is very close to $107$, because $x(y)$ is extremely close to $1$. The purpose of the exercise is not to determine the exact value of $g(y)$, but to explain why direct calculator evaluation of

$$
\frac{1-x^{107}}{1-x}
$$

can produce wildly inaccurate answers.

The first step is to verify the expansion supplied in the exercise.

## Solution

Write

$$
x=1-\delta,
\qquad
\delta=1.85y^2+3.45y^4.
$$

Since

$$
g(y)=\frac{1-(1-\delta)^{107}}{\delta},
$$

the binomial theorem gives

$$
g(y)
=
107
-\binom{107}{2}\delta
+\binom{107}{3}\delta^2
-\binom{107}{4}\delta^3
+O(\delta^4).
$$

Now

$$
\binom{107}{2}=5671,
\qquad
\binom{107}{3}=198485,
\qquad
\binom{107}{4}=5150610.
$$

Also

$$
\delta=1.85y^2+3.45y^4,
$$

$$
\delta^2=3.4225y^4+12.765y^6+O(y^8),
$$

$$
\delta^3=6.331625y^6+O(y^8).
$$

Therefore

$$
g(y)
=
107
-5671(1.85)y^2
+\bigl[-5671(3.45)+198485(3.4225)\bigr]y^4
$$

$$
\qquad
+\bigl[198485(12.765)-5150610(6.331625)\bigr]y^6
+O(y^8).
$$

The coefficients are

$$
-5671(1.85)=-10491.35,
$$

$$
-5671(3.45)+198485(3.4225)
=
659749.9625,
$$

$$
198485(12.765)-5150610(6.331625)
=
-30141386.26625.
$$

Hence

$$
g(y)
=
107
-10491.35y^2
+659749.9625y^4
-30141386.26625y^6
+O(y^8),
$$

which agrees with the expansion stated in the exercise.

Consequently,

$$
g(10^{-7})
=
106.9999999998950865\ldots,
$$

$$
g(10^{-8})
=
106.9999999999989509\ldots,
$$

$$
g(10^{-9})
=
106.9999999999999895\ldots,
$$

$$
g(10^{-10})
=
106.9999999999999999\ldots.
$$

These are the correct mathematical values.

The numerical behavior of a pocket calculator is entirely different.

Assume first a calculator carrying about ten significant digits internally. For

$$
y=10^{-7},
$$

$$
1-x
=
1.85\times10^{-14}+3.45\times10^{-28}
\approx1.85\times10^{-14}.
$$

The quantity $x$ differs from $1$ only in the fourteenth decimal place. A ten-digit machine stores

$$
x=1.000000000.
$$

Then

$$
x^{107}=1.000000000,
$$

and the displayed evaluation of

$$
\frac{1-x^{107}}{1-x}
$$

becomes

$$
\frac00,
$$

which may appear as an error message, overflow indication, or some machine-dependent nonsense value.

Exactly the same phenomenon occurs for

$$
y=10^{-8},\quad10^{-9},\quad10^{-10},
$$

because

$$
1.85\times10^{-16},
\qquad
1.85\times10^{-18},
\qquad
1.85\times10^{-20}
$$

are still farther below the resolution of such a calculator.

A calculator with thirteen or fourteen significant digits can distinguish $x$ from $1$ when $y=10^{-7}$, but not when $y=10^{-8}$ or smaller. Such a machine may give a reasonable answer for the first case and fail completely for the remaining three.

A calculator with guard digits but without correct rounding exhibits a different pathology. Suppose the machine forms

$$
x=1-\delta
$$

accurately enough that $x\neq1$. Then

$$
x^{107}=1-107\delta+O(\delta^2).
$$
