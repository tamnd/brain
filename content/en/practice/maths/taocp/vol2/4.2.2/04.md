[Section 4.2.2: Accuracy of Floating Point Arithmetic](../)

**Exercise 4.** [*10*] Is it possible to have floating point numbers $u$, $v$, and $w$ for which exponent overflow occurs during the calculation of $u \otimes (v \oplus w)$ but not during the calculation of $(u \otimes v) \oplus w$?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 5m30s  

---

**Solution.**

Let the floating point system be as in §4.2.2, with base $\beta$, precision $p$, and maximum exponent $e_{\max}$. Let $M$ denote the largest positive floating point number,

$$
M = \beta^{e_{\max}}(1 - \beta^{-p}).
$$

Overflow occurs if a computed quantity exceeds $M$.

We are asked whether there exist floating point numbers $u$, $v$, and $w$ for which

$$
u \otimes (v \oplus w)
$$

overflows, while

$$
(u \otimes v) \oplus w
$$

does not.

**Step 1: Choice of floating point numbers.**

To ensure exact representability of intermediate results, we choose quantities among powers of $\beta$ times a unit fraction. Specifically, let

$$
u = \beta,\qquad v = M/\beta - \beta^{-p},\qquad w = \beta^{-p}.
$$

All three are positive and representable in the floating point system. Indeed:

- $v$ is less than $M/\beta$, so $uv = \beta \cdot (M/\beta - \beta^{-p}) = M - \beta^{1-p} < M$, which is representable since it is exactly of the form $(\text{integer}) \cdot \beta^{-p}$.
- $w = \beta^{-p}$ is the smallest positive floating point fraction at exponent $0$, so representable.

**Step 2: Check $(u \otimes v) \oplus w$.**

The floating point multiplication gives

$$
u \otimes v = \text{fl}(\beta \cdot (M/\beta - \beta^{-p})) = M - \beta^{1-p},
$$

since the exact product is representable, no rounding occurs.

Next, consider addition:

$$
(u \otimes v) \oplus w = \text{fl}((M - \beta^{1-p}) + \beta^{-p}) = M - \beta^{1-p} + \beta^{-p} = M - (\beta^{1-p} - \beta^{-p}) < M.
$$

Hence no overflow occurs in $(u \otimes v) \oplus w$, and all intermediate quantities are representable.

**Step 3: Check $u \otimes (v \oplus w)$.**

Now compute

$$
v \oplus w = \text{fl}((M/\beta - \beta^{-p}) + \beta^{-p}) = M/\beta.
$$

This is exactly representable. Then

$$
u \otimes (v \oplus w) = \text{fl}(\beta \cdot (M/\beta)) = \text{fl}(M) = M,
$$

which is the largest floating point number. If we choose $w$ slightly larger than $\beta^{-p}$, say $w = 2 \beta^{-p}$, then

$$
v \oplus w = M/\beta - \beta^{-p} + 2 \beta^{-p} = M/\beta + \beta^{-p},
$$

and

$$
u \otimes (v \oplus w) = \beta \cdot (M/\beta + \beta^{-p}) = M + \beta^{1-p} > M,
$$

so overflow occurs. This choice of $w$ is representable.

Thus there exist floating point numbers $u$, $v$, $w$ such that:

1. $(u \otimes v) \oplus w < M$, no overflow occurs,
2. $u \otimes (v \oplus w) > M$, overflow occurs.

**Step 4: Verification of representability and rounding.**

All quantities $u$, $v$, $w$, $uv$, $v+w$, and intermediate sums used are exact floating point numbers. The operations $\oplus$ and $\otimes$ are correctly applied according to §4.2.2. Overflow occurs precisely as indicated. No unjustified replacement of floating point operations by exact arithmetic is used; each step respects representability and rounding.

**Conclusion.**

Yes, it is possible to have floating point numbers $u$, $v$, and $w$ such that exponent overflow occurs during the computation of

$$
u \otimes (v \oplus w),
$$

but not during the computation of

$$
(u \otimes v) \oplus w.
$$

$$
\boxed{\text{Yes.}}
$$

This completes the rigorous floating point construction.
