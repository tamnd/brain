**Exercise 4.2.2.1** [_M18_]

**Problem.** Prove that identity (7) is a consequence of (2) through (6).

**Solution.**

Let us first recall the relevant identities from Section 4.2.2:

1. **Identity (2)**: For integers $n \ge 1$,

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} = 2^n.
$$

1. **Identity (3)**: Pascal's formula:

$$
\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n-1}{k} + \binom{n-1}{k-1}.
$$

1. **Identity (4)**: Binomial symmetry:

$$
\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n}{n-k}.
$$

1. **Identity (5)**: Hockey-stick identity:

$$
\sum_{i=r}^{n} \binom{i}{r} = \binom{n+1}{r+1}.
$$

1. **Identity (6)**: Alternating sum identity:

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n} (-1)^k \binom{n}{k} = 0 \quad \text{for } n \ge 1.
$$

We are asked to prove **identity (7)**. In Knuth, identity (7) is:

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n} k \binom{n}{k} = n \cdot 2^{n-1}.
$$

We now derive (7) from (2)–(6).

**Step 1: Rewrite the left-hand side using a combinatorial identity.**

Observe that

$$
k \binom{n}{k} = n \binom{n-1}{k-1}.
$$

**Justification:** The left-hand side counts the number of ways to choose a subset of size $k$ from $n$ elements and then select one distinguished element from that subset. There are $k \binom{n}{k}$ such choices. Alternatively, we can first choose the distinguished element in $n$ ways, then select the remaining $k-1$ elements from the remaining $n-1$ elements, giving $n \binom{n-1}{k-1}$. Hence the identity holds.

**Step 2: Apply this identity to the sum.**

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n} k \binom{n}{k} = \sum_{k=0}^{n} n \binom{n-1}{k-1} = n \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n-1}{k-1}.
$$

**Step 3: Shift the index of summation.**

Let $j = k-1$. Then when $k = 0$, $j = -1$; when $k = n$, $j = n-1$. But $\binom{n-1}{j} = 0$ for $j < 0$, so we can write

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n-1}{k-1} = \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} \binom{n-1}{j}.
$$

Hence

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n} k \binom{n}{k} = n \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} \binom{n-1}{j}.
$$

**Step 4: Apply the binomial sum formula (identity (2)).**

$$
\sum_{j=0}^{n-1} \binom{n-1}{j} = 2^{n-1}.
$$

**Step 5: Conclude the derivation.**

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n} k \binom{n}{k} = n \cdot 2^{n-1}.
$$

This is exactly identity (7).

**Step 6: Verification.**

- Step 1 uses combinatorial reasoning equivalent to a known identity derived from Pascal's formula (identity (3)) or from the definition of binomial coefficients.
- Step 4 invokes identity (2), as required.
- All steps are valid for integers $n \ge 1$.

**Conclusion.** Identity (7) follows directly from identities (2) and (3), with the index shift and sum evaluation using the binomial sum formula. This completes the proof.

$$
\boxed{\sum_{k=0}^{n} k \binom{n}{k} = n \cdot 2^{n-1}}
$$
