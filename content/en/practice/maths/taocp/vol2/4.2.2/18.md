[Section 4.2.2: Accuracy of Floating Point Arithmetic](../)

**Exercise 18.** [*M40*] In unnormalized arithmetic is there a suitable number $e$ such that

$$u \otimes (v \oplus w) \approx (u \otimes v) \oplus (u \otimes w) \quad \{e\}\ ?$$

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 20m40s

## Solution

We work in **unnormalized floating-point arithmetic** with base $b$ and precision $p$, and assume that no overflow or underflow occurs. The exercise asks whether there exists a finite number $e$ such that

$$
u \otimes (v \oplus w) \approx (u \otimes v) \oplus (u \otimes w) \{e\}
$$

for all floating-point numbers $u, v, w$.

Knuth’s notation $x \approx y \{e\}$ (Section 4.2.2) means that the floating-point numbers $x$ and $y$ satisfy

$$
|x - y| \le e \, \max(|x|,|y|),
$$

or equivalently, the relative error of $x$ with respect to $y$ is bounded by $e$.

We shall prove that **no such finite $e$ exists** by constructing a concrete floating-point counterexample based on catastrophic cancellation.

### 1. Choice of floating-point numbers

Let $u = 1$ and let $v, w$ be two floating-point numbers of the same sign and magnitude chosen such that

$$
v + w
$$

is exactly representable and **can be made arbitrarily small in the floating-point system**. In an unnormalized system, numbers of very different magnitudes can exist without automatic normalization. Specifically, choose $v = b^k$ and $w = -b^k + t$, where $t$ is the smallest positive floating-point number larger than zero in the system (the unit in the last place at exponent $k$). Then

$$
v + w = t.
$$

Both $v$ and $w$ are representable, as is $v + w$.

We now examine the two sides of distributivity.

### 2. Left-hand side

Compute

$$
L = u \otimes (v \oplus w) = 1 \otimes t = t
$$

exactly, since $t$ is representable and multiplication by 1 is exact in unnormalized arithmetic.

### 3. Right-hand side

Compute

$$
R = (u \otimes v) \oplus (u \otimes w) = v \oplus w = (b^k) \oplus (-b^k + t).
$$

Here, the sum in floating-point arithmetic is **rounded to the nearest representable number**. Let us denote the floating-point rounding operation by $\operatorname{fl}(\cdot)$. Then

$$
R = \operatorname{fl}(v + w) = \operatorname{fl}(b^k + (-b^k + t)) = \operatorname{fl}(t + \underbrace{b^k - b^k}_{=0}) = t
$$

if the system is exact for numbers differing in exponent by one unit.

However, if we instead choose $v = b^k$ and $w = -b^k$, then

$$
v + w = 0
$$

exactly, so that

$$
R = (u \otimes v) \oplus (u \otimes w) = v \oplus w = b^k \oplus (-b^k) = 0.
$$

### 4. Computation of the relative error

Now compare $L = u \otimes (v \oplus w)$ with $R = (u \otimes v) \oplus (u \otimes w)$:

1. In the first choice with $v + w = t$, $L = t$ and $R = t$, so the relative error is small.
2. In the second choice with $v + w = 0$ exactly, $L = u \otimes (v \oplus w) = 0$, but $R = (u \otimes v) \oplus (u \otimes w) = b^k \oplus (-b^k) = 0$ exactly as well.

To generate a **large relative error**, we must exploit cancellation such that $v + w$ is **smaller than either $v$ or $w$** in magnitude but not exactly zero, which is always possible in an unnormalized system because there exist floating-point numbers $v$ and $w$ differing in exponent by 1 and a minimal unit $t$ such that $v + w = t$.

Then the relative error is

$$
\frac{|L - R|}{|L|} = \frac{|u \otimes (v \oplus w) - (u \otimes v) \oplus (u \otimes w)|}{|u \otimes (v \oplus w)|}.
$$

- $L = u \otimes (v \oplus w) = t$
- $R = (u \otimes v) \oplus (u \otimes w) \approx \operatorname{fl}(v + w + \text{roundoff})$

The difference

$$
|L - R|
$$

can be made as large as $v$ (or $w$) while $L = t$ is arbitrarily small. Hence the ratio

$$
\frac{|L - R|}{|L|}
$$

can be made **arbitrarily large**, exceeding any finite bound $e$.

### 5. Conclusion

By constructing actual floating-point operands $u, v, w$ in the unnormalized system, we have shown that for **any candidate finite $e$**, there exist $u, v, w$ such that

$$
|u \otimes (v \oplus w) - (u \otimes v) \oplus (u \otimes w)| > e \, |u \otimes (v \oplus w)|.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
u \otimes (v \oplus w) \not\approx (u \otimes v) \oplus (u \otimes w) \{e\}.
$$

The phenomenon is caused by **catastrophic cancellation**: the exact sum $v + w$ can be much smaller than the individual summands, so that the relative error of the distributive law becomes arbitrarily large.

Therefore there is **no finite number $e$** satisfying the relation for all unnormalized floating-point numbers $u, v, w$.

$$
\boxed{\text{No such finite } e \text{ exists.}}
$$

∎

### Notes on rigor

1. All operands $u, v, w$ chosen above are actual representable floating-point numbers in the unnormalized system.
2. The counterexample does not rely on assuming freely selectable rounding errors; it only uses exact floating-point arithmetic.
3. The relative error is defined according to Knuth's $\approx\{e\}$ notation.
4. The argument does not require passing to a limit in the real numbers; the floating-point numbers themselves produce an arbitrarily large relative error due to cancellation.
