**Exercise 25.** &#9654; [*15*] [15] When people speak about inaccuracy in floating point arithmetic they often ascribe errors to "cancellation" that occurs during the subtraction of nearly equal quantities. But when $u$ and $v$ are approximately equal, the difference $u \ominus v$ is obtained exactly, with no error. What do these people really mean?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 9m33s  

---

The point is that _cancellation_ is often misunderstood. The subtraction itself is not necessarily inaccurate. Rather, the subtraction can expose and magnify errors that were already present in the quantities being subtracted.

Let $u$ and $v$ be floating-point numbers. By the exact subtraction property (Sterbenz's theorem), if $u$ and $v$ are sufficiently close, then $u \ominus v = u-v$ exactly. Thus no new rounding error is introduced by the subtraction.

However, in practical computations, $u$ and $v$ are usually not exact values. They are approximations to some true quantities $x$ and $y$:

$$
u=x+\delta_x,\qquad v=y+\delta_y.
$$

Suppose that $x$ and $y$ are nearly equal. Then

$$
x-y
$$

is small, while the individual errors $\delta_x$ and $\delta_y$ may be of ordinary size relative to $x$ and $y$.

The computed difference is

$$
u-v=(x-y)+(\delta_x-\delta_y).
$$

Even if this subtraction is performed exactly, the error in the result is

$$
(\delta_x-\delta_y).
$$

The crucial point is that this error is being compared with the true quantity $x-y$, which is small. Hence the _relative_ error becomes

$$
\frac{\delta_x-\delta_y}{x-y},
$$

and this can be very large when $x-y$ is tiny.

For example, suppose

$$
x=1.000000,\qquad y=0.999999,
$$

so that

$$
x-y=10^{-6}.
$$

If $u$ and $v$ each contain errors of size about $10^{-8}$, then the difference $u-v$ may have an error of order $10^{-8}$ while the true result is only $10^{-6}$. The relative error is then about $1\%$, far larger than the relative errors in the original quantities.

Thus the usual complaint about "cancellation" is not that the subtraction operation introduces error. In fact, when the operands are close, the subtraction may be exact. What people really mean is that subtracting nearly equal quantities causes leading digits to cancel, leaving a small result whose relative accuracy is poor because the pre-existing errors in the operands have been amplified. Cancellation is therefore a loss of significant digits and a magnification of prior errors, not an error produced by the subtraction itself.
