[Section 4.6: Polynomial Arithmetic](../)

**Exercise 2.** [*17*] True or false: (a) The product of monic polynomials is monic. (b) The product of polynomials of degrees $m$ and $n$ has degree $m+n$. (c) The sum of polynomials of degrees $m$ and $n$ has degree $\max(m, n)$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m11s  

---

## Solution

Let $S$ be a commutative ring with identity, and let polynomial degree be defined by (2) and (3).

### (a)

Let $u(x)$ and $v(x)$ be monic polynomials. Then $\ell(u)=1$ and $\ell(v)=1$. Let $\deg(u)=r$ and $\deg(v)=s$. In the product

$u(x)v(x)=w_{r+s}x^{r+s}+\cdots,$

the coefficient of $x^{r+s}$ equals $u_r v_s$ by (4). Hence

$\ell(uv)=\ell(u)\ell(v)=1\cdot 1=1.$

The leading coefficient of $uv$ is $1$, so $uv$ is monic.

This statement is true.

### (b)

Let $u(x)=u_r x^r+\cdots$ and $v(x)=v_s x^s+\cdots$ with $u_r\neq 0$ and $v_s\neq 0$. The coefficient of $x^{r+s}$ in $uv$ is $u_r v_s$ by (4). In a general commutative ring with identity, $u_r v_s$ may equal $0$ even when both factors are nonzero, since zero divisors are not excluded.

Take $S=\mathbb{Z}_6$. Let

$u(x)=2x+1,\qquad v(x)=3x+1.$

Then $\deg(u)=\deg(v)=1$. The product is

$u(x)v(x)=6x^2+5x+1,$

and $6\equiv 0 \pmod 6$, so

$u(x)v(x)=5x+1,$

which has degree $1$, not $2$. Hence $\deg(uv)\neq 1+1$.

The statement is false.

### (c)

Let $u(x)$ and $v(x)$ have degrees $m$ and $n$. Without loss of generality, assume $m\ge n$. Then

$u(x)=u_m x^m+\cdots,\qquad v(x)=v_n x^n+\cdots.$

The sum is

$u(x)+v(x)=u_m x^m+\cdots+v_n x^n+\cdots.$

The coefficient of $x^m$ in the sum equals $u_m$ when $m>n$, since no term from $v(x)$ contributes to $x^m$. In this case the leading coefficient remains nonzero, so the degree is $m$.

When $m=n$, the coefficient of $x^m$ in the sum equals $u_m+v_m$. This sum may equal $0$ in $S$, which forces the degree of $u(x)+v(x)$ to be less than $m$. Hence the degree need not equal $\max(m,n)$.

A counterexample over $\mathbb{Z}$ is

$u(x)=x+1,\qquad v(x)=-x+2.$

Then $\deg(u)=\deg(v)=1$, while

$u(x)+v(x)=3,$

which has degree $0$.

The statement is false. This completes the proof. âˆŽ
