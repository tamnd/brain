[Section 4.6: Polynomial Arithmetic](../)

**Exercise 2.** [*17*] True or false: (a) The product of monic polynomials is monic. (b) The product of polynomials of degrees $m$ and $n$ has degree $m+n$. (c) The sum of polynomials of degrees $m$ and $n$ has degree $\max(m, n)$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m29s  

---

Let $S$ be a commutative ring with identity and let $u(x), v(x)\in S[x]$. Degrees are taken in the usual sense, with $\deg 0 = -\infty$.

### (a) The product of monic polynomials is monic

Let $\deg u = r$ and $\deg v = s$. Since both are monic, their leading coefficients are $1$. Write

$$
u(x)=x^r + a_{r-1}x^{r-1}+\cdots,\quad v(x)=x^s + b_{s-1}x^{s-1}+\cdots.
$$

In the product $u(x)v(x)$, the only way to obtain the term $x^{r+s}$ is by multiplying the leading terms $x^r\cdot x^s$. Hence the coefficient of $x^{r+s}$ in $uv$ is

$$
1\cdot 1 = 1.
$$

All other products produce powers strictly less than $r+s$, so they do not affect the leading term. Therefore $uv$ has leading coefficient $1$, and the product is monic.

Thus (a) is true.

### (b) The product of polynomials of degrees $m$ and $n$ has degree $m+n$

This statement is false in a general commutative ring with identity because zero divisors may exist.

Let $S=\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$. Take

$$
u(x)=2x^m,\quad v(x)=3x^n.
$$

Then $\deg u = m$ and $\deg v = n$, since $2\neq 0$ and $3\neq 0$ in $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$. However,

$$
u(x)v(x)=6x^{m+n}=0.
$$

Thus $uv$ is the zero polynomial, so $\deg(uv)=-\infty$, which is not $m+n$.

Hence the conclusion $\deg(uv)=m+n$ fails in general. The correct statement would require the coefficient ring to be an integral domain.

Thus (b) is false.

### (c) The sum of polynomials of degrees $m$ and $n$ has degree $\max(m,n)$

This statement is also false in general because leading terms may cancel.

Assume $m=n$. Let

$$
u(x)=x^m,\quad v(x)=-x^m
$$

over any commutative ring with identity containing $-1$. Then

$$
u(x)+v(x)=0,
$$

so the sum is the zero polynomial and has degree $-\infty$, not $m$.

If $m>n$, then no cancellation affects the $x^m$ term, so in that case the degree is $m$. However, since cancellation can occur when $m=n$, the degree of the sum is not determined solely by $m$ and $n$.

Thus (c) is false.

### Final answers

$$
\text{(a) True, \quad (b) False, \quad (c) False.}
$$
