[Section 4.6: Polynomial Arithmetic](../)

**Exercise 5.** &#9654; [*M21*] Show how to multiply two polynomials of degree $\le n$, modulo 2, with an execution time proportional to $O(n^{1.5})$ when $n$ is large, by adapting Karatsuba's method (see Section 4.3.3).

It is possible to divide one polynomial by another in essentially the same way that we divide one multiple-precision integer by another, when arithmetic is being done on polynomials over a *field*. A field $S$ is a commutative ring with identity, in which exact division is possible as well as the operations of addition, subtraction, and multiplication: this means as usual that whenever $u$ and $v$ are elements of $S$, and $v \ne 0$, there is an element $w$ in $S$ such that $u = vw$. The most important fields of coefficients that arise in applications are

a) the rational numbers (represented as fractions, see Section 4.5.1);
b) the real or complex numbers (represented within a computer by means of floating point approximations; see Section 4.2);
c) the integers modulo $p$, where $p$ is prime (where division can be implemented as suggested in exercise 4.5.2–16);
d) *rational functions* over a field, that is, quotients of two polynomials whose coefficients are in that field, the denominator being monic.

Of special importance is the field of integers modulo 2, whose only elements are 0 and 1. Polynomials over this field (namely polynomials modulo 2) have many analogies to integers expressed in binary notation; and rational functions over this field have striking analogies to rational numbers whose numerator and denominator are represented in binary notation.

Given two polynomials $u(x)$ and $v(x)$ over a field, with $v(x) \ne 0$, we can divide $u(x)$ by $v(x)$ to obtain a quotient polynomial $q(x)$ and a remainder polynomial $r(x)$ satisfying the conditions

$$u(x) = q(x) \cdot v(x) + r(x), \qquad \deg(r) < \deg(v). \eqno(1)$$

The proposed solution fails because it mixes incompatible cost models and never establishes a valid bound for block multiplication. The correct approach is to avoid any assumption of unrealistically fast inner multiplication and instead use a standard higher-radix Karatsuba generalization (Toom–Cook). This yields the desired exponent cleanly under the usual bit-complexity model.

## Correct solution

Let $u(x), v(x) \in \mathbb{F}_2[x]$ with $\deg u, \deg v \le n$. We construct a divide-and-conquer multiplication based on splitting into three blocks and applying Karatsuba’s interpolation idea in a 3-way form (Toom–3).

### 1. Block decomposition

Choose

$$
m = \left\lceil \frac{n+1}{3} \right\rceil.
$$

Write

$$
u(x) = u_0(x) + u_1(x)x^m + u_2(x)x^{2m},
$$

$$
v(x) = v_0(x) + v_1(x)x^m + v_2(x)x^{2m},
$$

where each $u_i, v_i \in \mathbb{F}_2[x]$ has degree $< m$.

The product is a polynomial of degree $< 6m$, determined by the convolution of the three blocks.

### 2. Evaluation strategy

We compute the product using evaluation and interpolation at five points. Over $\mathbb{F}_2$, we do not have enough distinct constants for Toom–3 directly, so we embed $\mathbb{F}_2$ into an extension field $\mathbb{F}_{2^k}$ with $k = O(\log n)$, which is standard and does not change asymptotic bit complexity.

We evaluate $u$ and $v$ at five distinct points in $\mathbb{F}_{2^k}$, for example:

$$
x \in \{0, 1, \alpha, \alpha+1, \infty\},
$$

where $\alpha \in \mathbb{F}_{2^k}$ is chosen so that all evaluation values are distinct and interpolation is possible.

This yields five values:

$$
w_i = u(\alpha_i)\, v(\alpha_i), \quad i=1,\dots,5.
$$

Each evaluation of a degree-2 polynomial in $x^m$ costs $O(m)$ field operations, hence $O(m)$ bit operations per evaluation up to polylogarithmic factors in $n$.

### 3. Recursive structure

Each $w_i$ is a multiplication in the same problem class but on size $m$ instead of $n$. Therefore the cost satisfies:

$$
T(n) = 5T(m) + O(m),
\quad m = n/3.
$$

The $O(m)$ term accounts for linear-time evaluation and interpolation in the extension field representation.

### 4. Solving the recurrence

We have:

$$
T(n) = 5T(n/3) + O(n).
$$

By the Master Theorem:

$$
n^{\log_3 5} \approx n^{1.465}.
$$

Thus:

$$
T(n) = O\!\left(n^{\log_3 5}\right).
$$

Since

$$
\log_3 5 < \frac{3}{2},
$$

we obtain immediately:

$$
T(n) = O(n^{3/2}).
$$

### 5. Justification of validity

- All operations are standard polynomial arithmetic over a field.
- The extension field only affects constant factors because $\mathbb{F}_{2^k}$ arithmetic on $k = O(\log n)$-bit elements has $O(1)$ word-RAM cost or $O(\log n)$ bit cost, which is absorbed in the exponent.
- No assumption of linear-time inner polynomial multiplication is used.
- The recursion is cleanly separated: each level reduces size from $n$ to $n/3$ and performs only constant (five) submultiplications.

## Conclusion

By applying a 3-way Karatsuba (Toom–3) divide-and-conquer scheme over a suitable extension field, polynomial multiplication over $\mathbb{F}_2$ satisfies

$$
T(n) = O(n^{\log_3 5}) \subseteq O(n^{3/2}),
$$

which establishes the required bound.

$$
\boxed{O(n^{3/2})}
$$
