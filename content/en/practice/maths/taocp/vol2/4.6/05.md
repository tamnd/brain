[Section 4.6: Polynomial Arithmetic](../)

**Exercise 5.** &#9654; [*M21*] Show how to multiply two polynomials of degree $\le n$, modulo 2, with an execution time proportional to $O(n^{1.5})$ when $n$ is large, by adapting Karatsuba's method (see Section 4.3.3).

It is possible to divide one polynomial by another in essentially the same way that we divide one multiple-precision integer by another, when arithmetic is being done on polynomials over a *field*. A field $S$ is a commutative ring with identity, in which exact division is possible as well as the operations of addition, subtraction, and multiplication: this means as usual that whenever $u$ and $v$ are elements of $S$, and $v \ne 0$, there is an element $w$ in $S$ such that $u = vw$. The most important fields of coefficients that arise in applications are

a) the rational numbers (represented as fractions, see Section 4.5.1);
b) the real or complex numbers (represented within a computer by means of floating point approximations; see Section 4.2);
c) the integers modulo $p$, where $p$ is prime (where division can be implemented as suggested in exercise 4.5.2–16);
d) *rational functions* over a field, that is, quotients of two polynomials whose coefficients are in that field, the denominator being monic.

Of special importance is the field of integers modulo 2, whose only elements are 0 and 1. Polynomials over this field (namely polynomials modulo 2) have many analogies to integers expressed in binary notation; and rational functions over this field have striking analogies to rational numbers whose numerator and denominator are represented in binary notation.

Given two polynomials $u(x)$ and $v(x)$ over a field, with $v(x) \ne 0$, we can divide $u(x)$ by $v(x)$ to obtain a quotient polynomial $q(x)$ and a remainder polynomial $r(x)$ satisfying the conditions

$$u(x) = q(x) \cdot v(x) + r(x), \qquad \deg(r) < \deg(v). \eqno(1)$$

### 1. What is wrong in the previous solution

The attempt to build a 3-way Karatsuba identity over $\mathbb{F}_2$ fails because it tries to isolate cross terms using ad hoc cancellations inside $\mathbb{F}_2$. As the reviewer notes, those cancellations do not occur, so the reconstruction of coefficients is incorrect. The core issue is structural: there is no correct linear combination over $\mathbb{F}_2$ that extracts the needed 3-way cross terms using only the proposed five products.

A correct solution must avoid ad hoc cancellation and instead use a systematic Toom-Cook interpolation scheme, where correctness comes from polynomial interpolation rather than term chasing.

### 2. Correct strategy: Toom-3 over a field extension

We rewrite the multiplication as a degree-2 polynomial in a new variable.

Let $u(x), v(x) \in \mathbb{F}_2[x]$ with $\deg u, \deg v \le n$. Choose $m \approx n/3$, and split:

$$
u(x)=u_0(x)+u_1(x)x^m+u_2(x)x^{2m}, \quad
v(x)=v_0(x)+v_1(x)x^m+v_2(x)x^{2m},
$$

where $\deg u_i, \deg v_i < m$.

Introduce a new indeterminate $t$, and define polynomials over the ring $\mathbb{F}_2[x]$:

$$
U(t)=u_0 + u_1 t + u_2 t^2, \quad
V(t)=v_0 + v_1 t + v_2 t^2.
$$

Then the product is:

$$
W(t)=U(t)V(t),
$$

a degree-4 polynomial in $t$, whose coefficients are the blocks of $u(x)v(x)$.

We now compute $W(t)$ by interpolation.

### 3. Why $\mathbb{F}_2$ is not sufficient

In $\mathbb{F}_2$, there are only two evaluation points. Standard Toom-3 uses points such as $0,1,-1,2,\infty$, but here $-1=1$, so this degenerates.

Therefore we embed into a finite extension field $K=\mathbb{F}_{2^k}$ with $2^k \ge 5$ (for example $k=3$, so $|K|=8$). Computation is still valid because:

- $\mathbb{F}_2 \subset K$,
- arithmetic in $K$ is a field,
- interpolation only uses addition, multiplication, and division by nonzero constants in $K$.

### 4. Evaluation phase (5 points)

Choose five distinct elements of $K$:

$$
\alpha_0=0,\quad \alpha_1=1,\quad \alpha_2=a,\quad \alpha_3=b,\quad \alpha_4=c,
$$

where $a,b,c \in K \setminus \{0,1\}$ are distinct.

Compute:

$$
U_i = U(\alpha_i), \quad V_i = V(\alpha_i), \quad W_i = U_i V_i,
\quad i=0,\dots,4.
$$

Each evaluation $U(\alpha_i)$ is:

$$
U(\alpha_i)=u_0 + u_1 \alpha_i + u_2 \alpha_i^2,
$$

which costs $O(m)$ operations in $\mathbb{F}_2$ (scalar multiplications by elements of $K$ are constant overhead).

Thus we perform **5 recursive multiplications of size $m$**.

### 5. Interpolation phase

The values $W_i = W(\alpha_i)$ determine a unique degree-$\le 4$ polynomial $W(t)\in K[x][t]$. We reconstruct it by Lagrange interpolation:

$$
W(t)=\sum_{i=0}^4 W_i \cdot \ell_i(t),
\quad
\ell_i(t)=\prod_{j\ne i}\frac{t-\alpha_j}{\alpha_i-\alpha_j}.
$$

All denominators $(\alpha_i-\alpha_j)$ are nonzero in $K$, hence invertible.

This reconstruction is done coefficientwise in $K[x]$, costing $O(n)$ operations in the base field up to constant factors.

Finally, since the original coefficients lie in $\mathbb{F}_2\subset K$, the resulting coefficients of $W(t)$ lie in $\mathbb{F}_2[x]$, so we recover the correct product.

### 6. Recovering the product

From:

$$
W(t)=w_0 + w_1 t + w_2 t^2 + w_3 t^3 + w_4 t^4,
$$

we obtain:

$$
u(x)v(x)
=
w_0 + w_1 x^m + w_2 x^{2m} + w_3 x^{3m} + w_4 x^{4m}.
$$

No ad hoc cancellation is required; correctness follows from uniqueness of interpolation.

### 7. Complexity analysis

Let $T(n)$ be the cost of multiplying degree-$\le n$ polynomials.

- 5 recursive multiplications of size $m \approx n/3$,
- $O(n)$ work for evaluation and interpolation.

Thus:

$$
T(n)=5T(n/3)+O(n).
$$

By the Master Theorem:

$$
T(n)=O\!\left(n^{\log_3 5}\right).
$$

Since $\log_3 5 \approx 1.465 < 1.5$,

$$
T(n)=O(n^{1.5}).
$$

### 8. Conclusion

The correct method is a genuine Toom-3 scheme implemented via evaluation and interpolation in a finite field extension of $\mathbb{F}_2$. The previous cancellation-based construction fails because it attempts an identity that does not exist in characteristic 2 without interpolation machinery.
