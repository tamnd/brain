**Exercise 31.** [*28*] [28] Assume that $b = 3$ and that $u = (u_{m+n-1} \ldots u_1 u_0)_3$, $v = (v_{n-1} \ldots v_1 v_0)_3$ are integers in *balanced ternary* notation (see Section 4.1), $v_{n-1} \ne 0$. Design a long-division algorithm that divides $u$ by $v$, obtaining a remainder whose absolute value does not exceed $\frac{1}{2}|v|$. Try to find an algorithm that would be efficient if incorporated into the arithmetic circuitry of a balanced ternary computer.

Assume first that $v \ne 0$. If $v < 0$, replace $(u,v)$ by $(-u,-v)$, so we may assume $v > 0$ without loss of generality.

We work in balanced ternary, so each digit $u_i, v_j \in {-1,0,1}$, and arithmetic is performed in radix $3$ with signed digits. The goal is to construct a quotient $q = (q_{m} \ldots q_0)_3$ with $q_i \in {-1,0,1}$ and a remainder $r$ such that

$$
u = qv + r, \quad |r| \le \frac{1}{2}v.
$$

The algorithm is a digit recurrence method, analogous to long division, but using a redundant digit set to avoid carry propagation delays.

We maintain a running partial remainder $R$ stored in binary scaled form. Initially,

$$
R = 0.
$$

We process the digits of $u$ from most significant to least significant. At step $i$, we update

$$
R \leftarrow 3R + u_i.
$$

This corresponds to shifting the current remainder in base $3$ and incorporating the next digit.

We then choose the next quotient digit $q_i \in {-1,0,1}$ according to the sign and magnitude of $R$ relative to $v$:

$$
q_i =
\begin{cases}
1 & \text{if } R \ge \frac{v}{2},\\
-1 & \text{if } R \le -\frac{v}{2},\\
0 & \text{if } |R| < \frac{v}{2}.
\end{cases}
$$

After selecting $q_i$, we correct the remainder:

$$
R \leftarrow R - q_i v.
$$

This completes one iteration.

We now verify correctness and the remainder bound. The key invariant is that after the correction step, the remainder always satisfies

$$
|R| \le \frac{v}{2}.
$$

Indeed, consider the three cases.

If $|R| < \frac{v}{2}$, then $q_i = 0$, so $R$ is unchanged and already satisfies the bound.

If $R \ge \frac{v}{2}$, then $q_i = 1$, and the updated remainder is

$$
R' = R - v.
$$

Since the largest possible value before correction occurs after the shift step, we have $R \le \frac{3v}{2}$ (this follows inductively from the previous corrected bound $|R| \le \frac{v}{2}$ and the fact that $R \leftarrow 3R + u_i$ with $u_i \in {-1,0,1}$ increases magnitude by at most a factor of $3$ plus $1$ in the last digit). Hence,

$$
-\frac{v}{2} \le R - v \le \frac{v}{2}.
$$

Thus $|R'| \le \frac{v}{2}$.

If $R \le -\frac{v}{2}$, then $q_i = -1$, and

$$
R' = R + v,
$$

and the same symmetric estimate yields $|R'| \le \frac{v}{2}$.

Therefore after every correction step the invariant $|R| \le \frac{v}{2}$ holds. At termination, after processing all digits of $u$, we have

$$
u = qv + R,
$$

with the required bound $|R| \le \frac{v}{2}$.

It remains to interpret $q$ as a balanced ternary integer. Since each $q_i \in {-1,0,1}$ is produced once per digit position, the quotient is naturally formed in balanced ternary without further carry propagation. This is a crucial advantage for hardware implementation: the quotient digits are produced directly in redundant form, and no post-normalization is required.

From an implementation standpoint, each iteration requires only three simple operations in base $3$ arithmetic. The transformation $R \leftarrow 3R$ is a one-place digit shift in ternary hardware. Adding $u_i$ is a single digit addition with bounded carry in balanced representation. The comparison with $\pm v/2$ is a fixed threshold test, and the correction step $R \leftarrow R \pm v$ is a digit-wise subtraction/addition that can be performed in parallel across ternary digits. Because all intermediate values remain bounded by a constant multiple of $v$, the word length of the remainder register can be fixed, making the method suitable for efficient hardware realization.

Thus the algorithm performs balanced ternary long division with a guaranteed remainder satisfying $|r| \le \frac{1}{2}|v|$ and is structured entirely from local digit operations, making it appropriate for arithmetic circuitry.
