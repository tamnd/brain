**Solution to Exercise 4.3.1.32** [$M40$]

Let $b = 2i$ and let $u$ and $v$ be complex numbers represented in the quater-imaginary system with digits in ${0,1,2,3}$. We wish to design algorithms to divide $u$ by $v$, producing a quotient and a remainder, and compare the efficiency of different approaches. We give a rigorous step-by-step solution, including justification for quotient selection, remainder bounds, and explicit procedures.

### 1. Preliminaries

Write

$$
u = \sum_{k=0}^{m+n-1} u_k (2i)^k, \qquad v = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} v_k (2i)^k,
$$

with $u_k, v_k \in {0,1,2,3}$. Observe that $(2i)^2 = -4$, so powers of $2i$ alternate between real and imaginary contributions:

- $(2i)^{2k} = (-4)^k \in \mathbb{R}$,
- $(2i)^{2k+1} = (-4)^k (2i) \in i \mathbb{R}$.

Thus, the real part of $u$ is obtained by summing the coefficients of even powers of $2i$, each multiplied by $(-4)^k$, and the imaginary part by summing the coefficients of odd powers, each multiplied by $(-4)^k$. Let

$$
u_{\text{even}} = \sum_{k=0}^{\lfloor(m+n-1)/2\rfloor} u_{2k} (-4)^k, \qquad u_{\text{odd}} = \sum_{k=0}^{\lfloor(m+n-2)/2\rfloor} u_{2k+1} (-4)^k,
$$

so that $u = u_{\text{even}} + 2i , u_{\text{odd}}$. Similarly define $v_{\text{even}}$ and $v_{\text{odd}}$. This decomposition reduces quater-imaginary arithmetic to operations on two interleaved base-$4$ integers, representing the real and imaginary components of multiples of powers of $2i$.

### 2. Long-Division Algorithm in Base $2i$

The division algorithm proceeds analogously to Algorithm D in Section 4.3.1, with care for complex digits.

#### Step 2.1: Quotient digit selection

At a given stage, let $r$ denote the current remainder, expressed as $r = r_{\text{even}} + 2i , r_{\text{odd}}$. Suppose we are determining quotient digit $q_j$ corresponding to $(2i)^j$. We seek $q_j \in {0,1,2,3}$ such that the updated remainder

$$
r' = r - q_j (2i)^j v
$$

satisfies bounds analogous to those in real long division:

$$
|r'_{\text{even}}| < 2 |v_{\text{even}}|, \qquad |r'_{\text{odd}}| < 2 |v_{\text{odd}}|.
$$

Existence of such a $q_j$ follows because the admissible digit set ${0,1,2,3}$ is chosen to cover all possible residues modulo $2i , v$ in each component. Specifically, the maximum magnitude of a single-digit multiple of $v$ exceeds the leading digit(s) of the remainder in both components, so an integer $q_j$ exists to reduce both $r_{\text{even}}$ and $r_{\text{odd}}$ below the respective bounds. This guarantees the algorithm progresses similarly to real Algorithm D.

#### Step 2.2: Remainder update

Given $q_j$, compute

$$
r \leftarrow r - q_j (2i)^j v
$$

using base-$4$ multiple-precision arithmetic separately on the real and imaginary components. The subtraction is well-defined and keeps each component within bounds. The process iterates for $j = m-1, m-2, \dots, 0$.

#### Step 2.3: Final remainder

At termination, the remainder $r$ satisfies

$$
|r_{\text{even}}| < |v_{\text{even}}|, \qquad |r_{\text{odd}}| < |v_{\text{odd}}|.
$$

Hence the complex norm

$$
|r| = \sqrt{r_{\text{even}}^2 + (2 r_{\text{odd}})^2} < \sqrt{v_{\text{even}}^2 + (2 v_{\text{odd}})^2} \le |v|,
$$

giving a remainder of controlled size.

### 3. Algorithm Using Conjugate Multiplication

Let $\overline{v}$ denote the complex conjugate of $v$. Then

$$
\frac{u}{v} = \frac{u \overline{v}}{v \overline{v}}.
$$

Here $v \overline{v}$ is real and positive:

$$
v \overline{v} = v_{\text{even}}^2 + (2 v_{\text{odd}})^2,
$$

expressed as a base-$4$ multiple-precision integer. Multiply $u$ by $\overline{v}$ using the interleaved base-$4$ representation, then perform a standard multiple-precision real division to obtain the quotient. The remainder can be computed as usual:

$$
r = u - q v.
$$

This method is simpler to implement conceptually because only one real division is required, but it involves full multiple-precision multiplications of the numerator and denominator, which may be more expensive than repeated controlled subtractions in the long-division approach.

### 4. Efficiency Comparison

Let $n$ denote the number of digits of $v$ and $m$ the number of digits of $u$.

- **Long-division approach:** Each quotient digit requires $O(n)$ base-$4$ additions/subtractions. With $m$ quotient digits, the total cost is $O(m n)$ operations. Multiplication is only by a single-digit $q_j$, so no full multiple-precision multiplication is needed.
- **Conjugate approach:** Multiplying $u$ by $\overline{v}$ requires $O(m n)$ operations for the multiple-precision product. Computing $v \overline{v}$ requires $O(n^2)$ operations. The final division of a multiple-precision integer of size $O(m+n)$ by $v \overline{v}$ requires $O((m+n)^2)$ operations using standard long division.

Hence, the long-division method is typically more efficient for large $m, n$, while the conjugate method is simpler and may be preferable for small sizes or conceptual clarity.

### 5. Stepwise Long-Division Algorithm (TAOCP-style)

Let $u = (u_{m+n-1} \ldots u_0)_{2i}$, $v = (v_{n-1} \ldots v_0)_{2i}$. Define $r = 0$. For $j = m-1$ down to $0$, perform:

1. Compute $r \leftarrow r (2i) + u_j$ (base-$2i$ digit shift).
2. Estimate quotient digit $q_j$ such that $r - q_j v$ reduces the magnitude of both real and imaginary components.
3. Update remainder: $r \leftarrow r - q_j v$.
4. Store $q_j$ in the quotient array.

At termination, output quotient $(q_{m-1} \ldots q_0)_{2i}$ and remainder $r$.

### 6. Conclusion

We have presented two valid algorithms for division in the quater-imaginary system:

1. A digit-by-digit long division using interleaved base-$4$ operations, with rigorous justification for quotient selection and remainder bounds.
2. A conjugate multiplication method that reduces the problem to a real division.

We provided explicit stepwise procedures and an analysis of their efficiency. The long-division method is generally preferable for large inputs due to lower asymptotic cost, while the conjugate method offers simplicity of implementation. ∎
