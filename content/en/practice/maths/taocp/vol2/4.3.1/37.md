**Exercise 37.** [*20*] [20] (E. Salamin.) Explain how to avoid the normalization and unnormalization steps of Algorithm D, when $d$ is a power of 2 on a binary computer, without changing the sequence of trial quotient digits computed by that algorithm. (How can $\hat{q}$ be computed in step D3 if the normalization of step D1 hasn't been done?)

## Solution

In Algorithm D, step D1 multiplies both the dividend and the divisor by the same power of $b$ so that the leading digit of the divisor satisfies $v_{n-1} \ge \lfloor b/2 \rfloor$. In step D3, the trial quotient digit $\hat{q}$ is computed from the leading part of the normalized dividend and the leading digit of the normalized divisor, in the form

$$
\hat{q} = \left\lfloor \frac{u_j b + u_{j+1}}{v_{n-1}} \right\rfloor
$$

(up to the indexing conventions of Section 4.3.1).

Assume now that the base is binary, $b = 2$, and that $d$ (the divisor) is a power of $2$, so $d = 2^t$ for some $t \ge 0$. The normalization step D1 would multiply both dividend and divisor by a power of $2$, hence replace them by $2^s u$ and $2^s d$ for some integer $s \ge 0$. The quotient computed by the algorithm is unchanged because

$$
\frac{2^s u}{2^s d} = \frac{u}{d}.
$$

The leading digit of the normalized divisor becomes $1$, since any power of $2$ in base $2$ has a single nonzero digit after shifting. Therefore step D3 in the normalized algorithm reduces to computing

$$
\hat{q} = \left\lfloor u_j 2 + u_{j+1} \right\rfloor
$$

after appropriate scaling of indices, followed by division by $1$, which is trivial. The entire dependence on normalization disappears once division by the leading digit is recognized to be division by $1$.

Without performing D1, the divisor remains $d = 2^t$. The same trial quotient digit is obtained by computing directly

$$
\hat{q} = \left\lfloor \frac{u_j 2 + u_{j+1}}{2^t} \right\rfloor.
$$

Since division by $2^t$ in binary arithmetic is a right shift by $t$ bit positions, this computation is performed by shifting the binary representation of $(u_j u_{j+1})$ right by $t$ places and truncating.

The sequence of trial quotient digits is preserved because normalization multiplies numerator and denominator by the same power of $2$, while the expression defining $\hat{q}$ depends only on their ratio. Replacing normalized quantities by their unnormalized counterparts multiplied by a common power of $2$ leaves each value of $\hat{q}$ unchanged at every stage of step D3.

Thus step D3 can be implemented without D1 by computing $\hat{q}$ as the binary right shift of the two-digit partial dividend by $t$, where $d = 2^t$, and no scaling of operands is required.

This completes the solution. ∎
