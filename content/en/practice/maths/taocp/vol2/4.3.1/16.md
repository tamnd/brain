**Solution to Exercise 4.3.1.16**

We are asked to design an algorithm that divides a nonnegative $n$-place base-$b$ fraction $(.u_1 u_2 \ldots u_n)_b$ by a single-precision number $v$, where $0 < v < b$, producing a quotient $(.w_1 w_2 \ldots w_n)_b$ and a remainder $r$. We proceed carefully, step by step, in the standard manner of short division.

Let the given number be

$$
U = (.u_1 u_2 \ldots u_n)_b = \sum_{i=1}^{n} u_i b^{-i}, \quad 0 \le u_i < b,
$$

and let the divisor be a single-digit number $v$ such that $1 \le v < b$. We want to compute the quotient

$$
W = (.w_1 w_2 \ldots w_n)_b = \sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i b^{-i}, \quad 0 \le w_i < b,
$$

and the remainder $r$ satisfying

$$
U = v \cdot W + \frac{r}{b^n}, \quad 0 \le r < v.
$$

Here, $r$ is an integer representing the remainder in the units of $b^{-n}$.

### Step 1: Initialize

Set the initial remainder $r_0 = 0$. This remainder will carry the "partial remainder" as we process each digit of $U$ from left to right.

### Step 2: Process digits sequentially

For each digit $i = 1, 2, \ldots, n$, define the current partial remainder $R_i$ by

$$
R_i = r_{i-1} \cdot b + u_i.
$$

This expresses the fact that when dividing digit by digit, the previous remainder is "brought down" to combine with the next digit.

Then, compute the quotient digit $w_i$ as the integer part of $R_i$ divided by $v$:

$$
w_i = \left\lfloor \frac{R_i}{v} \right\rfloor.
$$

The new remainder is the leftover after this division:

$$
r_i = R_i - v \cdot w_i.
$$

By construction, $0 \le r_i < v$, ensuring that the remainder is always valid.

### Step 3: Repeat

Repeat Step 2 for all $i = 1$ to $n$. After the last digit, the remainder $r_n$ is the final remainder $r$ of the division, and the collected digits $w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_n$ form the quotient $W = (.w_1 w_2 \ldots w_n)_b$.

### Step 4: Algorithm in pseudocode

Let $r = 0$. For $i = 1$ to $n$:

1. Compute $R = r \cdot b + u_i$.
2. Set $w_i = \lfloor R / v \rfloor$.
3. Set $r = R - v \cdot w_i$.

At the end, $w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_n$ form the quotient, and $r$ is the remainder.

### Step 5: Verification of correctness

We verify by induction on $i$ that at each stage,

$$
(.u_1 u_2 \ldots u_i)_b = v \cdot (.w_1 w_2 \ldots w_i)_b + r_i b^{-i}.
$$

**Base case**: For $i = 1$, $R_1 = r_0 b + u_1 = u_1$, and $w_1 = \lfloor u_1 / v \rfloor$, $r_1 = u_1 - v w_1$. Then

$$
(.u_1)_b = \frac{u_1}{b} = \frac{v w_1 + r_1}{b} = v \cdot (.w_1)_b + \frac{r_1}{b}.
$$

**Inductive step**: Assume the property holds for $i-1$. Then

$$
(.u_1 \ldots u_i)_b = (.u_1 \ldots u_{i-1})_b + u_i b^{-i}.
$$

Substitute the induction hypothesis for the first $i-1$ digits:

$$
(.u_1 \ldots u_i)_b = v \cdot (.w_1 \ldots w_{i-1})_b + \frac{r_{i-1}}{b^{i-1}} + u_i b^{-i} = v \cdot (.w_1 \ldots w_{i-1})_b + \frac{r_{i-1} b + u_i}{b^i} = v \cdot (.w_1 \ldots w_{i-1})_b + \frac{R_i}{b^i}.
$$

Now split $R_i$ as $R_i = v w_i + r_i$, so

$$
(.u_1 \ldots u_i)_b = v \cdot \left( (.w_1 \ldots w_{i-1})_b + w_i b^{-i} \right) + r_i b^{-i} = v \cdot (.w_1 \ldots w_i)_b + r_i b^{-i}.
$$

Thus the property holds for all $i$, and in particular for $i=n$, producing the final quotient and remainder.

### Step 6: Example

Suppose $b=10$, $n=4$, $U = (.7 3 4 6)_{10}$, and $v=3$.

1. $r_0 = 0$
2. $R_1 = 0_10 + 7 = 7$, $w_1 = 7/3 = 2$, $r_1 = 7 - 2_3 = 1$
3. $R_2 = 1*10 + 3 = 13$, $w_2 = 13/3 = 4$, $r_2 = 13 - 12 = 1$
4. $R_3 = 1*10 + 4 = 14$, $w_3 = 14/3 = 4$, $r_3 = 14 - 12 = 2$
5. $R_4 = 2*10 + 6 = 26$, $w_4 = 26/3 = 8$, $r_4 = 26 - 24 = 2$

Hence the quotient is $(.2 4 4 8)_{10}$, and the remainder is $r=2$.

### Conclusion

The short division algorithm for a nonnegative $n$-digit base-$b$ fraction divided by a single-precision number $v$ works by sequentially combining the previous remainder with the next digit, dividing by $v$ to determine the quotient digit, and updating the remainder. This produces the quotient digits $w_i$ and the final remainder $r$, with each step ensuring $0 \le w_i < b$ and $0 \le r < v$.
