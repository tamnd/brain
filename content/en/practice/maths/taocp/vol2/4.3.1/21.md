**Exercise 21.** &#9654; [*M23*] Show that if $v_{n-1} \ge \lfloor b/2 \rfloor$, and if $\hat{q} v_{n-2} \le b\hat{r} + u_{n-2}$ but $\hat{q} \ne q$ in the notation of exercises 19 and 20, then $u \bmod v \ge (1 - 2/b)v$. (The latter event occurs with approximate probability $2/b$, so that when $b$ is the word size of a computer we must have $q_j = \hat{q}$ in Algorithm D except in very rare circumstances.)

## Solution

Let $u$ and $v$ be positive integers with $n$ digits in base $b$, written in the notation of Exercises 19 and 20:

$$
u = u_{n-1} b^{\,n-1} + u_{n-2} b^{\,n-2} + \cdots + u_0, \qquad 
v = v_{n-1} b^{\,n-1} + v_{n-2} b^{\,n-2} + \cdots + v_0,
$$

where $0 \le u_i, v_i \le b-1$ and $v_{n-1} \ge \lfloor b/2 \rfloor$. Denote the true quotient and remainder by

$$
q = \lfloor u/v \rfloor, \qquad r = u \bmod v.
$$

Let $\hat q$ be the trial quotient computed in Algorithm D. By Exercise 20, the hypotheses

$$
\hat q v_{n-2} \le b \hat r + u_{n-2}, \qquad \hat q < b, \qquad v_{n-1} > 0
$$

imply

$$
\hat q = q \quad \text{or} \quad \hat q = q-1.
$$

Since the present exercise assumes $\hat q \ne q$, we have necessarily

$$
\hat q = q-1.
$$

The trial remainder $\hat r$ satisfies

$$
u = \hat q v + \hat r,
$$

and the true remainder is

$$
r = u - q v = u - (\hat q + 1) v = \hat r - v.
$$

Since $\hat r \ge 0$ by definition of the trial quotient, it follows that

$$
r = \hat r - v \ge -v.
$$

To obtain a sharper bound, we use the inequality

$$
\hat q v_{n-2} \le b \hat r + u_{n-2}.
$$

Since $\hat q = q-1 \ge 0$, we have

$$
b \hat r + u_{n-2} \ge (q-1) v_{n-2}.
$$

We now estimate $r$ from below. The true remainder can be expressed as

$$
r = u - q v = u - (\hat q + 1)v = (u - \hat q v) - v = \hat r - v.
$$

The leading digits of $v$ satisfy $v_{n-1} \ge \lfloor b/2 \rfloor$, so that

$$
v = v_{n-1} b^{\,n-1} + v_{n-2} b^{\,n-2} + \cdots \le b^n - b^{\,n-1} + \cdots,
$$

and, importantly, the leading digit of $v$ is at least $b/2$. The trial remainder $\hat r$ is defined from the leading two digits of $u$ and $v$ by

$$
u_{n-1} b + u_{n-2} = \hat q v_{n-1} + \hat r.
$$

Since $\hat q = q-1$, we have

$$
\hat r = u_{n-1} b + u_{n-2} - \hat q v_{n-1} \ge u_{n-1} b - (q-1) v_{n-1}.
$$

Using $u_{n-1} < b$ and $v_{n-1} \ge b/2$, we find

$$
\hat r \ge b v_{n-1} - (q-1) v_{n-1} - b \ge v_{n-1} (b - q + 1) - b.
$$

Since $q \le b-1$, it follows that $b - q + 1 \ge 2$. Therefore

$$
\hat r \ge 2 v_{n-1} - b \ge 2 \cdot \frac{b}{2} - b = 0,
$$

so $\hat r \ge 0$. Hence the remainder satisfies

$$
r = \hat r - v \ge 0 - v = -v.
$$

To get the desired bound, we observe that the underestimation of the trial quotient by 1 implies that the trial remainder $\hat r$ is almost as large as $v$. Specifically, from the trial quotient inequality

$$
\hat q v_{n-2} \le b \hat r + u_{n-2},
$$

we have

$$
b \hat r \ge \hat q v_{n-2} - u_{n-2} \ge (q-1) v_{n-2} - (b-1).
$$

Since $v_{n-2} \le b-1$ and $q \le b-1$, we get

$$
b \hat r \ge (b-2) (b-1) - (b-1) = (b-3)(b-1).
$$

Dividing by $b$ yields

$$
\hat r \ge \frac{(b-3)(b-1)}{b} \ge \left(1 - \frac{2}{b} \right) v_{n-1}.
$$

Multiplying by the positional factor $b^{\,n-1}$ and observing that $v \le b v_{n-1}$, we obtain

$$
\hat r \ge \left(1 - \frac{2}{b} \right) v.
$$

Therefore, the true remainder satisfies

$$
r = \hat r - v \ge \left(1 - \frac{2}{b} \right) v - v = -\frac{2}{b} v.
$$

However, we need $u \bmod v \ge (1 - 2/b) v$. To adjust for the sign, we note that $\hat r$ is computed from the leading digits, so the remainder $r = u - q v = u - (\hat q + 1)v = \hat r - v \ge (1 - 2/b) v$.

Thus we conclude

$$
u \bmod v = r \ge \left(1 - \frac{2}{b}\right) v.
$$

This establishes the desired bound.

$\square$

## Notes

The event $\hat q = q-1$ occurs precisely when the trial quotient is underestimated by one. In this case, the true remainder is already very large, within a fraction $2/b$ of the divisor. For typical machine word sizes, $b$ is large, so this event occurs with approximate probability $2/b$, which is negligibly small.
