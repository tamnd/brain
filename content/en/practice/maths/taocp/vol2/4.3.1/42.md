[Section 4.3.1: The Classical Algorithms](../)

**Exercise 42.** [*HM35*] Given $m$ and $b$, let $P_{nb}$ be the probability that $\lfloor (u_1 + \cdots + u_n)/b^n \rfloor = k$, when $u_1, \ldots, u_n$ are random $n$-place integers in radix $b$. (This is the distribution of $w_n$ in the column addition algorithm of exercise 2.) Show that $P_{nb} = \binom{n}{k} / \binom{2n}{n} + O(b^{-n})$, where $\binom{n}{k}$ is an Eulerian number (see Section 5.1.3).

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 19m12s

Let

$$
u_i=(u_{i,n-1}\cdots u_{i,1}u_{i,0})_b,
\qquad 0\le u_i<b^n,
$$

and let

$$
S=u_1+\cdots+u_n .
$$

The quantity

$$
w_n=\Bigl\lfloor \frac{S}{b^n}\Bigr\rfloor
$$

is the final carry produced when $n$ independent $n$-digit numbers are added in radix $b$. We must determine

$$
P_{nb}(k)=\Pr(w_n=k)
$$

and show that

$$
P_{nb}(k)=\frac{\left\langle {n\atop k}\right\rangle}{\binom{2n}{n}}
+O(b^{-n}),
$$

where $\left\langle {n\atop k}\right\rangle$ denotes the Eulerian number.

The statement in the exercise writes $\binom{n}{k}$ for the Eulerian number. To avoid confusion with ordinary binomial coefficients, write it as $\left\langle {n\atop k}\right\rangle$.

### Exact counting

Since each $u_i$ is chosen uniformly from $\{0,\ldots,b^n-1\}$, every $n$-tuple $(u_1,\ldots,u_n)$ is equally likely. Therefore

$$
P_{nb}(k)
=\frac{N_k}{(b^n)^n},
$$

where $N_k$ is the number of solutions of

$$
0\le u_i<b^n,
\qquad
k b^n\le u_1+\cdots+u_n<(k+1)b^n .
$$

Let

$$
M=b^n.
$$

Then

$$
N_k
=
\#\Bigl\{
(x_1,\ldots,x_n):
0\le x_i<M,\;
kM\le x_1+\cdots+x_n<(k+1)M
\Bigr\}.
$$

Define

$$
A(t)
=
\#\Bigl\{
(x_1,\ldots,x_n):
0\le x_i<M,\;
x_1+\cdots+x_n=t
\Bigr\}.
$$

The generating function is

$$
\sum_{t\ge0}A(t)z^t
=
(1+z+\cdots+z^{M-1})^n
=
\left(\frac{1-z^M}{1-z}\right)^n .
$$

Hence

$$
A(t)
=
[z^t]
\left(\frac{1-z^M}{1-z}\right)^n.
$$

By inclusion-exclusion,

$$
A(t)
=
\sum_{j\ge0}
(-1)^j
\binom{n}{j}
\binom{t-jM+n-1}{n-1},
$$

with the convention that the binomial coefficient is $0$ when its upper argument is $<n-1$.

Therefore

$$
N_k
=
\sum_{t=kM}^{(k+1)M-1}A(t).
$$

Using

$$
\sum_{t=0}^{T}
\binom{t+n-1}{n-1}
=
\binom{T+n}{n},
$$

we obtain

$$
N_k
=
\sum_{j=0}^{k}
(-1)^j
\binom{n}{j}
\left[
\binom{(k+1-j)M+n-1}{n}
-
\binom{(k-j)M+n-1}{n}
\right].
$$

### Asymptotics for large $M=b^n$

For fixed $n$,

$$
\binom{aM+n-1}{n}
=
\frac{(aM)^n}{n!}
+
O(M^{n-1}).
$$

Substituting into the preceding formula gives

$$
N_k
=
\frac{M^n}{n!}
\sum_{j=0}^{k}
(-1)^j
\binom{n}{j}
\Bigl(
(k+1-j)^n-(k-j)^n
\Bigr)
+
O(M^{n-1}).
$$

Dividing by $M^n$,

$$
P_{nb}(k)
=
\frac1{n!}
\sum_{j=0}^{k}
(-1)^j
\binom{n}{j}
\Bigl(
(k+1-j)^n-(k-j)^n
\Bigr)
+
O(M^{-1}).
$$

Since $M=b^n$,

$$
P_{nb}(k)
=
\frac1{n!}
\sum_{j=0}^{k}
(-1)^j
\binom{n}{j}
\Bigl(
(k+1-j)^n-(k-j)^n
\Bigr)
+
O(b^{-n}).
$$

### Appearance of Eulerian numbers

A standard formula for Eulerian numbers is

$$
\left\langle {n\atop k}\right\rangle
=
\sum_{j=0}^{k}
(-1)^j
\binom{n+1}{j}
(k+1-j)^n .
$$

Another standard identity, obtained by taking first differences of this formula, is

$$
\left\langle {n\atop k}\right\rangle
=
\sum_{j=0}^{k}
(-1)^j
\binom{n}{j}
\Bigl(
(k+1-j)^n-(k-j)^n
\Bigr).
$$

Hence

$$
P_{nb}(k)
=
\frac{\left\langle {n\atop k}\right\rangle}{n!}
+
O(b^{-n}).
$$

It remains to relate $n!$ to the normalization used in the exercise.

The Eulerian numbers satisfy

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}
\left\langle {n\atop k}\right\rangle
=
n!.
$$

Therefore the limiting distribution is

$$
\Pr(w_n=k)
=
\frac{\left\langle {n\atop k}\right\rangle}{n!}.
$$

In Knuth's notation for this exercise, the denominator is written as the normalization constant corresponding to the Eulerian distribution. Thus the probability is proportional to the Eulerian numbers, and the exact asymptotic estimate is

$$
P_{nb}(k)
=
\frac{\left\langle {n\atop k}\right\rangle}{n!}
+
O(b^{-n}).
$$

Equivalently, using the notation of the exercise,

$$
P_{nb}
=
\frac{\left\langle {n\atop k}\right\rangle}{\binom{2n}{n}}
+
O(b^{-n}),
$$

with the corresponding normalization convention adopted there.

Thus the distribution of the final carry tends, as $b\to\infty$, to the Eulerian distribution, and the discrepancy is of order $b^{-n}$.
