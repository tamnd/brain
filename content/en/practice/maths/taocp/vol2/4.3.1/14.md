## Solution

We prove the validity of Algorithm M by induction on the outer loop variable $j$, using the method of inductive assertions from Section 1.2.1. Let the nonnegative integers $(u_{m-1} \ldots u_0)_b$ and $(v_{n-1} \ldots v_0)_b$ be given, and let $(w_{m+n-1} \ldots w_0)_b$ denote the array of digits that Algorithm M constructs.

Define the partial product after completing step M6 for a given $j$ as

$W^{(j)} = \sum_{i=0}^{m-1} \sum_{\ell=0}^{j} u_i v_\ell b^{i+\ell}.$

We claim that immediately after step M6 for each $j$, the digits $(w_0, \ldots, w_{m+j})_b$ satisfy

$(w_0 + w_1 b + \cdots + w_{m+j} b^{m+j}) = W^{(j)},$

and that the carry $k$ is correctly propagated in the range $0 \le k < b$.

**Base case.** For $j = 0$, step M1 initializes $w_i \leftarrow 0$ for $0 \le i \le m-1$, so the partial sum $W^{(-1)} = 0$ is correctly represented. Step M2 checks $v_0$; if $v_0 = 0$, then $w_m \leftarrow 0$ and the loop terminates for $j = 0$, giving $(w_0, \ldots, w_m)_b = 0 = W^{(0)}$. Otherwise, steps M3–M5 compute

$t \leftarrow u_i v_0 + w_i + k,$

$w_i \leftarrow t \bmod b, \quad k \leftarrow \lfloor t/b \rfloor,$

for $0 \le i \le m-1$, which is precisely the classical multiplication of the $m$-place number $(u_{m-1} \ldots u_0)_b$ by the single digit $v_0$, plus the initial zero content of $w_i$. The final $w_{m} \leftarrow k$ stores any remaining carry, completing the base case. By the range of $u_i$, $v_0$, and $k$, we have $0 \le k < b$.

**Inductive step.** Assume that after step M6 for some $j-1 < n$, the invariant

$(w_0 + \cdots + w_{m+j-1} b^{m+j-1}) = \sum_{i=0}^{m-1} \sum_{\ell=0}^{j-1} u_i v_\ell b^{i+\ell}$

holds, and that the carry $k$ satisfies $0 \le k < b$. At step M2, if $v_j = 0$, then setting $w_{j+m} \leftarrow 0$ preserves the invariant, since adding $u_i v_j b^{i+j} = 0$ for all $i$ does not change the sum. Otherwise, steps M3–M5 iterate over $i = 0$ to $m-1$, computing

$t \leftarrow u_i v_j + w_{i+j} + k, \quad w_{i+j} \leftarrow t \bmod b, \quad k \leftarrow \lfloor t/b \rfloor.$

By the inductive hypothesis, $w_{i+j}$ initially contains $\sum_{\ell=0}^{j-1} u_{i} v_\ell b^{i+\ell}$ in radix-$b$ form. Then $t$ accumulates the contribution from $v_j$, plus the previous partial sum, plus any carry from the lower-order digit. The assignment $w_{i+j} \leftarrow t \bmod b$ correctly stores the current digit in radix $b$, while $k \leftarrow \lfloor t/b \rfloor$ correctly propagates the carry to the next higher-order digit. Since $u_i$, $v_j$, $w_{i+j}$, and $k$ are all nonnegative and less than $b$, it follows by the argument used in Algorithm A that $0 \le k < b$ throughout the loop.

After completing the loop over $i$, setting $w_{j+m} \leftarrow k$ correctly stores the final carry for this partial product. Thus, the invariant extends to $j$, and the sum of the digits $(w_0, \ldots, w_{m+j})_b$ equals

$\sum_{i=0}^{m-1} \sum_{\ell=0}^{j} u_i v_\ell b^{i+\ell} = W^{(j)}.$

**Termination.** After $j = n-1$, we have completed the outer loop. Then $W^{(n-1)}$ equals

$\sum_{i=0}^{m-1} \sum_{\ell=0}^{n-1} u_i v_\ell b^{i+\ell} = (u_{m-1} \ldots u_0)_b \cdot (v_{n-1} \ldots v_0)_b,$

so the algorithm produces the exact radix-$b$ representation of the product in the digits $w_0, \ldots, w_{m+n-1}$.

This completes the proof.
