### Corrected solution

The claim is:

> At the beginning of step D3 of Algorithm D, we always have $u_{j+n} = 0$.

We show that this statement is **false** by giving a direct counterexample and explaining why the attempted invariant does not hold.

## 1. Counterexample (disproving the claim)

Work in base $b = 10$. Let the divisor have $n = 2$ digits and take a simple valid input:

$$
v = (v_1 v_0) = (2\,1),
\quad n = 2.
$$

Let the dividend be

$$
x = 1234.
$$

In Knuth’s Algorithm D representation, we write the working array $u$ with the standard $m+n$ digit normalization. Here we may take $m=2$, so the array has $m+n = 4$ significant digits plus possible leading zeros:

$$
u = (.u_4 u_3 u_2 u_1 u_0) = (.0\,1\,2\,3\,4).
$$

Now consider the beginning of the first execution of step D3, i.e. $j=0$. Then

$$
u_{j+n} = u_2 = 2.
$$

Thus,

$$
u_{j+n} \neq 0.
$$

This already disproves the statement, since the claim asserts that $u_{j+n} = 0$ at the beginning of every D3, including the first one.

## 2. Why the proposed inductive argument fails

The original solution assumes that step D8 forces the highest digit of the active window

$$
(u_{j+n} \ldots u_j)_b
$$

to become zero, and then treats this as an invariant propagated to the next iteration.

This is incorrect for two independent reasons.

### (a) Step D8 does not enforce $u_{j+n} = 0$

Step D8 in Algorithm D is a correction step ensuring that the _numerical value_ of the partial remainder is correct after an over-subtraction or under-subtraction. It adjusts the block

$$
(u_{j+n} \ldots u_j)_b
$$

so that it represents the correct remainder modulo the divisor.

However, nothing in the algorithm requires or enforces that the leading digit of this block is zero. After correction, $u_{j+n}$ may be positive and is simply part of the correctly represented remainder.

### (b) The index shift does not eliminate nonzero digits

When $j$ increases, previously computed digits remain in the array unless explicitly overwritten. Step D8 only ensures correctness of the current window; it does not propagate a “zero-leading-digit” condition to future positions.

Thus there is no mechanism in Algorithm D that maintains

$$
u_{j+n} = 0
$$

as an invariant.

## 3. Conclusion

The statement is false. A valid execution of Algorithm D can begin with $u_{j+n} \neq 0$, as shown by a concrete base-10 example. The attempted inductive invariant fails because step D8 preserves correctness of the remainder value, not a structural property forcing a specific digit to be zero.

$$
\boxed{\text{The claim is false.}}
$$
