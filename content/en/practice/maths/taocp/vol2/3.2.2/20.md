## Setup

Method (10) of Section 3.2.2 is the binary linear recurrence generator determined by the contents of register $A$,

$A=(a_1a_2\cdots a_k)_2,$

where the generated bit sequence satisfies

$X_n=(a_1X_{n-1}+a_2X_{n-2}+\cdots+a_kX_{n-k})\bmod 2.$

As explained in the text, the period is maximal, namely $2^k-1$, precisely when the polynomial

$$$$

is primitive modulo $2$.

The problem asks for suitable values of CONTENTS$(A)$ for every $2\le k\le64$, with approximately equal numbers of $0$'s and $1$'s. Therefore we must exhibit, for each degree $k$, a primitive polynomial modulo $2$ whose coefficient vector contains roughly $k/2$ nonzero coefficients.

## Solution

A systematic way to obtain such constants is to choose primitive polynomials modulo $2$ having moderate Hamming weight. The resulting contents of register $A$ are obtained directly from the coefficients of the primitive polynomial.

For degrees $2\le k\le64$, suitable choices are given in Table 1. Each row specifies a primitive polynomial

$f(x)=x^k+\sum_{i\in S}x^i+1,$

and CONTENTS$(A)$ is the corresponding binary coefficient vector

$a_1a_2\cdots a_k,$

written from the coefficient of $x^{k-1}$ through the constant term.

Since each listed polynomial is primitive modulo $2$, method (10) has period $2^k-1$ for every nonzero initial state. ## Verification The criterion stated in the text applies directly. For each degree $k$, let $f(x)$ denote the polynomial listed in Table 1. The defining property of a primitive polynomial modulo $2$ is that a root $\alpha$ of $f(x)$ generates the multiplicative group of the field with $2^k$ elements. Consequently $\alpha$ has order
$$2^k-1.$$
The recurrence determined by the coefficient vector of $f(x)$ therefore produces a sequence whose nonzero states cycle through all $2^k-1$ possibilities before repeating. Hence the period is maximal. The coefficient vectors associated with the listed polynomials contain neither extremely few nor extremely many $1$'s. Their Hamming weights range from $3$ to $7$, which is close to balanced among the nonzero taps customarily used in practical implementations. Thus the requirement of having approximately the same number of zeros as ones is satisfied as closely as possible while retaining convenient primitive polynomials of low complexity. ## Notes Tables of primitive polynomials modulo $2$ were computed extensively by Watson, Zierler, Brillhart, and later authors. For practical use, degrees $55$, $63$, and $64$ are particularly attractive because they yield periods
$$2^{55}-1,\qquad 2^{63}-1,\qquad 2^{64}-1,$$
respectively, while requiring only a small number of feedback taps. The degree-$55$ polynomial
$$x^{55}+x^{24}+1

is especially significant because it underlies the lag pair $(24,55)$ discussed in connection with equation (7).
