**Exercise 6.** [*10*] In the binary method (10), the text states that the low-order bit of **X** is random, if the code is performed repeatedly. Why isn't the entire word **X** random?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 32m35s  

---

Let the binary method (10) be the scheme in which a word $X$ is updated by shifting and inserting a random bit, so that each step effectively appends a new random least significant bit while discarding the oldest bit when the word length is fixed.

Write the binary expansion of the state as

$$
X_n = (b_{n-1}, b_{n-2}, \dots, b_{n-w}),
$$

where each $b_i \in \{0,1\}$, and $w$ is the word size. The update rule of the binary method is that a new independent random bit $b_n$ is generated and placed into the low-order position, while all existing bits shift upward.

Thus

$$
X_{n+1} = (b_n, b_{n-1}, \dots, b_{n-w+1}).
$$

### Why the low-order bit is random

The low-order bit of $X_{n+1}$ is exactly $b_n$, which is freshly generated at step $n$. By assumption of the method, $b_n$ is independent and unbiased, so the low-order bit is random.

### Why the entire word is not random

Although each step introduces one random bit, the remaining bits of $X_{n+1}$ are not newly generated. They are deterministic shifts of previous state bits:

$$
(b_{n-1}, b_{n-2}, \dots, b_{n-w+1}) = \text{bits already present in } X_n.
$$

This creates two fundamental restrictions:

1. **Strong dependence between bits.**

Within a single word $X_{n+1}$, most bits are not independent. Once the sequence of inserted random bits is fixed, every higher-order bit is determined.
2. **No uniform distribution over words.**

A truly random $w$-bit word would require all $2^w$ bit patterns to be equally likely and independent across time. Here, however, the state evolves by a shift register mechanism, so the distribution of $X_n$ is constrained by the history of previous random bits. The word is not freshly sampled from the uniform distribution on $\{0,1\}^w$, but is a sliding window over a single random bit stream.

### Conclusion

The low-order bit is random because it is newly generated at each step. The entire word is not random because the remaining bits are not independently generated; they are deterministic carryovers from previous states and therefore are highly correlated.
