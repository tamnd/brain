**Exercise 34.** [*M25*] Prove that the inversive congruential sequence (12) has period $p + 1$ if and only if the polynomial $f(x) = x^2 - cx - a$ has the following two properties: (i) $x^{p+1} \bmod f(x)$ is a nonzero constant, when computed with polynomial arithmetic modulo $p$; (ii) $x^{(p+1)/q} \bmod f(x)$ has degree 1 for every prime $q$ that divides $p+1$. [*Hint:* Consider powers of the matrix $\binom{0\ 1}{a\ c}$.]

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 4m36s

Let $p$ be prime and let

$$
f(x)=x^{2}-cx-a\in\mathbb{F}_{p}[x],\qquad R=\mathbb{F}_{p}[x]/(f).
$$

Write $x\in R$ for the residue class of the indeterminate. Then $x^{2}=cx+a$ in $R$, so multiplication by $x$ is an $\mathbb{F}_{p}$-linear operator $T:R\to R$ with matrix (in the basis $1,x$)

$$
T(1)=x,\qquad T(x)=x^{2}=a+cx,
$$

hence

$$
M=\begin{pmatrix}0 & a\\ 1 & c\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Thus for every $n\ge 0$,

$$
T^{n}(1)=x^{n}\bmod f(x),
\qquad
T^{n}= \text{multiplication by } x^{n}.
$$

### 1. Correct projective model of the recurrence

Consider the set of nonzero vectors $(u,v)\in\mathbb{F}_{p}^{2}$, modulo scalar multiples. A vector corresponds to the element $u+vx\in R$, and hence to a point of the projective line.

Multiplication by $x$ sends

$$
u+vx \longmapsto x(u+vx)=ux+v x^{2}=ux+v(cx+a).
$$

In coordinates,

$$
u+vx \longmapsto (av) + (u+cv)x,
$$

so the induced linear action on column vectors is exactly

$$
M=\begin{pmatrix}0 & a\\ 1 & c\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Passing to projective classes, $M$ induces a permutation of $\mathbb{P}^{1}(\mathbb{F}_{p})$. The inversive congruential sequence (12) is precisely the orbit of a projective point under this action (including the point at infinity corresponding to $v=0$). Hence:

The period of the sequence is the order of the class of $M$ in $\mathrm{PGL}(2,p)$.

### 2. Reformulation of the period condition

The sequence has period $p+1$ if and only if

$$
M^{p+1}=\lambda I \quad \text{for some } \lambda\in\mathbb{F}_{p}^{\times},
$$

and $p+1$ is minimal with this property.

In terms of $T$, this is equivalent to

$$
T^{p+1}=\lambda \,\mathrm{Id}_{R}.
$$

Since $T^{p+1}$ is multiplication by $x^{p+1}$, this is equivalent to

$$
x^{p+1}=\lambda \in R.
$$

Thus we obtain:

$$
x^{p+1}\bmod f(x)\ \text{is a nonzero constant}
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
M^{p+1}=\lambda I.
$$

This is exactly condition (i).

### 3. Necessity of condition (ii)

Let $q\mid (p+1)$ be prime. If

$$
x^{(p+1)/q}\bmod f(x)
$$

were constant, say $x^{(p+1)/q}=\mu\in\mathbb{F}_{p}^{\times}$, then

$$
M^{(p+1)/q}=\mu I \quad \text{in } \mathrm{GL}(2,p),
$$

so the projective order of $M$ would divide $(p+1)/q$, contradicting minimal period $p+1$.

Hence $x^{(p+1)/q}\bmod f(x)$ is not constant. Since multiplication by $x^{(p+1)/q}$ sends $1$ to a degree $\le 1$ polynomial, non-constancy forces degree exactly $1$. This is condition (ii).

### 4. Sufficiency of (i) and (ii)

Assume:

(i) $x^{p+1}=\lambda\in\mathbb{F}_{p}^{\times}$, so $M^{p+1}=\lambda I$.

Hence the projective order of $M$ divides $p+1$.

Let $d$ be the projective order. If $d<p+1$, choose a prime divisor $q\mid (p+1)/d$. Then $M^{(p+1)/q}$ is scalar in $\mathrm{PGL}(2,p)$, hence

$$
x^{(p+1)/q}\bmod f(x)
$$

would be constant, contradicting condition (ii).

Therefore no proper divisor of $p+1$ annihilates the projective action, so $d=p+1$.

### 5. Conclusion

The inversive congruential sequence has period $p+1$ if and only if the projective order of multiplication-by-$x$ is $p+1$, which is equivalent to:

(i) $x^{p+1}\bmod f(x)$ is a nonzero constant, and

(ii) for every prime $q\mid (p+1)$, $x^{(p+1)/q}\bmod f(x)$ has degree $1$.

This completes the proof. ∎
