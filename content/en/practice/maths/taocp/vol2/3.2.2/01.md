**Exercise 1.** &#9654; [*12*] In practice, we form random numbers using $X_{n+1} = (aX_n + c) \bmod m$, where the $X$'s are *integers*, afterwards treating them as the *fractions* $U_n = X_n/m$. The recurrence relation for $U_n$ is actually

$$U_{n+1} = (aU_n + c/m) \bmod 1.$$

Discuss the generation of random sequences using this relation *directly*, by making use of floating point arithmetic on the computer.

## Solution

We consider the linear congruential generator (LCG) in its standard integer form:

$$
X_{n+1} = (a X_n + c) \bmod m, \quad X_0 \in \{0,1,\dots,m-1\}.
$$

The corresponding uniform fractions are defined by

$$
U_n = \frac{X_n}{m}, \quad 0 \le U_n < 1.
$$

Substituting $X_n = m U_n$ into the recurrence gives

$$
X_{n+1} = a (m U_n) + c \bmod m = m(a U_n) + c \bmod m.
$$

Dividing both sides by $m$ yields

$$
U_{n+1} = \frac{X_{n+1}}{m} = (a U_n + c/m) \bmod 1.
$$

This is the exact recurrence for $U_n$ in terms of real arithmetic.

### Direct generation using floating-point arithmetic

Suppose we attempt to generate the sequence $(U_n)$ directly in floating-point arithmetic by updating

$$
U \leftarrow (a U + c/m) \bmod 1.
$$

Let us analyze the consequences of doing this.

#### 1. Floating-point representation

Let the floating-point system have $p$ bits of precision in the significand. Then the set of numbers that can be represented exactly is finite. In particular:

- Each $U_n$ can only take values from a finite set of representable floating-point numbers in $[0,1)$.
- Hence the recurrence defines a map on a **finite set**, not on the continuum $[0,1)$.

This is in contrast to the integer LCG modulo $m$, which defines a map on the finite set $\{0,1,\dots,m-1\}$, but with the guarantee that the period can reach $m$ if the parameters $a$ and $c$ are chosen appropriately.

#### 2. Effect on period

Let $\mathcal{F} \subset [0,1)$ denote the set of floating-point numbers exactly representable. Then $U_{n+1} \in \mathcal{F}$ whenever $U_n \in \mathcal{F}$. Since $|\mathcal{F}|$ is finite, the sequence generated by

$$
U_{n+1} = (a U_n + c/m) \bmod 1
$$

**must eventually repeat**, with period at most $|\mathcal{F}|$. In practice, $|\mathcal{F}| \ll m$ for typical LCG parameters, so the period is much shorter than the maximal period of the integer LCG.

Moreover, rounding errors at each step may perturb the recurrence so that even more values repeat prematurely or the sequence enters a cycle with a period **much smaller than expected**. Therefore, the theoretical properties of the LCG, maximal period and uniform coverage of all residues modulo $m$, are generally lost when floating-point arithmetic is used directly.

#### 3. Exact representation of integers

One might ask: could direct floating-point generation preserve the integer LCG properties if $m$ is a power of 2 and the floating-point significand has enough bits? Suppose $m = 2^e$ and the floating-point format has at least $e$ bits. Then each integer $0 \le X_n < m$ can be represented exactly as a floating-point number.

However, exact representation of integers alone **does not guarantee equivalence** between integer and floating-point LCGs:

1. Multiplying $U_n$ by $a$ and adding $c/m$ in floating-point arithmetic may introduce rounding, because $a U_n$ may not be exactly representable even if $U_n$ is.
2. The operation modulo 1 is implemented by subtraction of the integer part, which may itself be inexact.

Therefore, **exact integer representation is insufficient** to preserve the maximal period; one must carefully analyze the floating-point operations to determine whether rounding introduces errors. In general, it does.

#### 4. Comparison with integer LCG

- **Integer LCG**: operates on exact integers modulo $m$, can achieve full period $m$ with proper choice of $a$ and $c$, and produces exactly uniform fractions $U_n = X_n/m$.
- **Floating-point LCG**: operates on a finite set of machine numbers. Period is bounded by the number of representable numbers and may be significantly shorter. Rounding errors can alter both period and distribution. Uniformity may be degraded, and subtle correlations may appear in the sequence.

Consequently, **direct floating-point generation is generally not recommended** if the goal is to produce a long-period, high-quality pseudorandom sequence. The standard approach, generate integers modulo $m$ exactly, then divide by $m$, ensures the theoretical properties are preserved, up to the precision limits of the integers used.

#### 5. Practical remarks

- Direct floating-point generation may be convenient for small $m$ or applications where only moderate statistical quality is required.
- Care must be taken to avoid catastrophic loss of precision: if $a$ or $c/m$ is too large relative to the floating-point range, overflow or underflow may occur.
- For high-quality pseudorandom sequences with maximal period, **integer LCGs followed by scaling** remain the preferred method.

### Conclusion

Direct generation of the recurrence

$$
U_{n+1} = (a U_n + c/m) \bmod 1
$$

in floating-point arithmetic is mathematically well-defined, but:

1. The sequence is restricted to a finite set of representable floating-point numbers.
2. The period is bounded by the number of such numbers and is generally much shorter than the period of the integer LCG.
3. Rounding errors can alter the distribution and introduce correlations.
4. Even for $m = 2^e$, exact integer representation does not guarantee equivalence with the integer LCG.

Therefore, although floating-point implementation is possible, it **loses the theoretical guarantees** of the integer LCG and is generally inferior for applications requiring long-period uniform pseudorandom sequences. The standard method of integer generation followed by scaling is recommended.
