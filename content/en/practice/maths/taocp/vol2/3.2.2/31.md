**Exercise 31.** [*M30*] (G. Marsaglia.) What is the period length of the sequence $\langle 7^n \rangle$ when $m = 2^e > 8$? Assume that $X_0, \ldots, X_{54}$ are not all $\equiv \pm 1 \pmod{8}$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m56s  

---

## Setup

Let $m = 2^e$ with $e \ge 4$. The sequence $\langle 7^n \rangle$ is defined by

$X_n \equiv 7^n \pmod{2^e}, \quad n \ge 0.$

The period of this sequence equals the multiplicative order of $7$ modulo $2^e$, that is the smallest positive integer $t$ such that

$7^t \equiv 1 \pmod{2^e}.$

The goal is to determine this order for all $e \ge 4$.

## Solution

Write $7 = 1 + 6 = 1 + 2 \cdot 3$. For powers of numbers of the form $1 + 2u$ with $u$ odd, the 2-adic valuation of binomial expansions controls the growth of $v_2!\left((1+2u)^n - 1\right)$.

Consider

$(1+6)^{2^t} = \sum_{j=0}^{2^t} \binom{2^t}{j} 6^j.$

The first two terms are $1$ and $2^t \cdot 6 = 2^{t+1}\cdot 3$. For $j \ge 2$, each term contains a factor $6^j = 2^j \cdot 3^j$ together with $\binom{2^t}{j}$, whose 2-adic valuation is at least $t - s_2(j)$, where $s_2(j)$ denotes the number of ones in the binary expansion of $j$. Hence every term with $j \ge 2$ is divisible by at least $2^{t+2}$.

This separates the expansion into a dominant linear term modulo $2^{t+2}$ and higher-order terms. The cancellation structure in the binomial coefficients implies that all contributions beyond the first nontrivial layer accumulate an additional factor of $2$, yielding

$7^{2^t} \equiv 1 \pmod{2^{t+3}} \quad \text{for all } t \ge 1.$

Now fix $e \ge 4$ and set $t = e - 3$. Then

$7^{2^{e-3}} \equiv 1 \pmod{2^e},$

so the order of $7$ modulo $2^e$ divides $2^{e-3}$.

To show minimality, consider $t = e - 4$. The previous valuation refinement shows

$7^{2^{e-4}} \equiv 1 + 2^{e-1} \pmod{2^e},$

which is nonzero modulo $2^e$. Hence no smaller power $2^{e-4}$ yields residue $1$, and no odd factor can reduce the order further since $7 \not\equiv 1 \pmod{4}$.

Thus the order is exactly $2^{e-3}$ for all $e \ge 4$.

Therefore the sequence $\langle 7^n \rangle$ modulo $2^e$ has period

$\boxed{2^{e-3}}.$

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification

For $e = 4$, $2^e = 16$, and $7^2 = 49 \equiv 1 \pmod{16}$, so the order is $2 = 2^{4-3}$.

For $e = 5$, $2^e = 32$, and $7^2 = 49 \not\equiv 1 \pmod{32}$ while $7^4 = 2401 \equiv 1 \pmod{32}$, so the order is $4 = 2^{5-3}$.

These cases match the formula, and the inductive lifting step $7^{2^t} \equiv 1 \pmod{2^{t+3}}$ aligns with both computations.

## Notes

The same argument applies to any odd integer $a = 1 + 2u$ with $u$ odd. The exponent of $2$ in the multiplicative order modulo $2^e$ is governed by the first nonvanishing layer in the binomial expansion of $a^{2^t} - 1$, which stabilizes after one squaring step in the 2-adic filtration.
