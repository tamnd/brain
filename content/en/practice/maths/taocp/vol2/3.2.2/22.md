**Exercise 22.** &#9654; [*M24*] $[M24]$ The text restricts discussion of the extended linear sequences (8) to the case that $m$ is prime. Prove that reasonably long periods can also be obtained when $m$ is "squarefree," that is, the product of distinct primes. (Examination of Table 3.2.1.1 shows that $m = w \pm 1$ often satisfies this hypothesis; many of the results of the text can therefore be carried over to that case, which is somewhat more convenient for calculation.)

## Solution

Let $m = p_1 p_2 \cdots p_s$, where $p_1,\ldots,p_s$ are distinct primes. Consider the recurrence

$$
X_n \equiv a_1 X_{n-1} + \cdots + a_k X_{n-k} \pmod m,
\qquad n \ge k.
$$

For each $i$, let $\pi_i$ denote reduction modulo $p_i$. Applying $\pi_i$ to the recurrence yields

$$
\pi_i(X_n) \equiv \bar a_{1,i}\,\pi_i(X_{n-1}) + \cdots + \bar a_{k,i}\,\pi_i(X_{n-k}) \pmod{p_i},
$$

where $\bar a_{j,i} \equiv a_j \pmod{p_i}$. Hence the sequence modulo $m$ induces, for each $p_i$, a linear recurrence over $\mathbb{F}_{p_i}$ with the same order $k$ and coefficients reduced modulo $p_i$.

For each $i$, choose coefficients $a_{1,i},\ldots,a_{k,i}$ modulo $p_i$ such that the associated polynomial

$$
f_i(x) = x^k - a_{1,i}x^{k-1} - \cdots - a_{k,i}
$$

is primitive modulo $p_i$. By the result in Section 3.2.2 for prime modulus, the corresponding sequence modulo $p_i$ then has period

$$
T_i = p_i^k - 1.
$$

Since the moduli $p_i$ are pairwise coprime, the Chinese remainder theorem implies that there exist integers $a_1,\ldots,a_k$ such that

$$
a_j \equiv a_{j,i} \pmod{p_i}
\quad \text{for all } i \text{ and all } j.
$$

Fix such a choice of $a_1,\ldots,a_k$ modulo $m$. For any initial vector $(X_0,\ldots,X_{k-1})$, its projection modulo each $p_i$ evolves independently under a recurrence of period $T_i$, provided the initial state is not the zero vector modulo $p_i$.

Let $T$ be the period of the sequence modulo $m$. If the sequence returns to its initial state modulo $m$ after $T$ steps, then it returns to its initial state modulo each $p_i$ after $T$ steps, hence $T_i \mid T$ for all $i$. Therefore,

$$
\operatorname{lcm}(T_1,\ldots,T_s) \mid T.
$$

Conversely, if $T_i \mid T$ for all $i$, then after $T$ steps each residue class modulo $p_i$ returns to its initial state, and by the Chinese remainder theorem the state modulo $m$ also returns to its initial state. Hence the period modulo $m$ is exactly

$$
T = \operatorname{lcm}(p_1^k - 1,\ldots,p_s^k - 1).
$$

Since each $p_i^k - 1$ is large for typical word-size primes $p_i$, their least common multiple is also large, so the resulting period is comparable in magnitude to the prime-modulus case.

Thus linear recurrences of the form (8) admit comparably long periods when $m$ is squarefree.

This completes the proof. ∎
