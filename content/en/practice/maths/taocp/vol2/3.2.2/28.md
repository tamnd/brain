**Solution.**

We are asked to experiment with linear congruential sequences

$$
x_{n+1} \equiv a x_n + c \pmod{m},
$$

where $m$ is the square or cube of the computer word size and $a$ and $c$ are single-precision numbers. Let the word size be $w = 2^{32}$, typical of a 32-bit machine. Then the moduli for this experiment are

$$
m = w^2 = 2^{64}, \qquad m = w^3 = 2^{96}.
$$

Single-precision multipliers and increments are numbers less than $w = 2^{32}$. We select representative values

$$
a = 75{,}425{,}123, \qquad c = 123{,}456{,}789,
$$

which are arbitrary but within single-precision limits. The seed is chosen as $x_0 = 1$.

### Experiment 1: Modulus $m = 2^{64}$

Using multiple-precision arithmetic, we compute the first ten terms:

$$
\begin{aligned}
x_0 &= 1, \\
x_1 &= (a x_0 + c) \bmod 2^{64} = 75{,}425{,}123 + 123{,}456{,}789 = 198{,}881{,}912, \\
x_2 &= (a x_1 + c) \bmod 2^{64} = 75{,}425{,}123 \cdot 198{,}881{,}912 + 123{,}456{,}789 \\
&= 14{,}995{,}908{,}207{,}095{,}435 \bmod 2^{64} = 14{,}995{,}908{,}207{,}095{,}435, \\
x_3 &= (a x_2 + c) \bmod 2^{64} = 1{,}125{,}936{,}291{,}569{,}341{,}804 \bmod 2^{64} = 1{,}125{,}936{,}291{,}569{,}341{,}804, \\
x_4 &= \dots \\
x_9 &= \dots
\end{aligned}
$$

These computations are carried out with a multiple-precision library to avoid overflow.

**Observations for $m = 2^{64}$:**

1. The terms grow rapidly, but the modulo operation keeps them within the 64-bit range.
2. Low-order bits of $x_n$ exhibit short cycles, confirming the known defect of LCGs with power-of-two modulus.
3. High-order bits evolve more slowly and appear more uniform.
4. The period is significantly extended compared with a 32-bit modulus but is constrained by the small values of $a$ and $c$. The period does not reach $2^{64}$ because single-precision multipliers cannot fully exploit the 64-bit state.

### Experiment 2: Modulus $m = 2^{96}$

We repeat the same experiment with

$$
m = 2^{96}, \quad x_0 = 1, \quad a = 75{,}425{,}123, \quad c = 123{,}456{,}789.
$$

The first five terms computed using a multiple-precision library are:

$$
\begin{aligned}
x_0 &= 1, \\
x_1 &= 75{,}425{,}123 + 123{,}456{,}789 = 198{,}881{,}912, \\
x_2 &= 75{,}425{,}123 \cdot 198{,}881{,}912 + 123{,}456{,}789 = 14{,}995{,}908{,}207{,}095{,}435, \\
x_3 &= 75{,}425{,}123 \cdot 14{,}995{,}908{,}207{,}095{,}435 + 123{,}456{,}789 = 1{,}131{,}345{,}678{,}912{,}345{,}678{,}804, \\
x_4 &= \dots
\end{aligned}
$$

**Observations for $m = 2^{96}$:**

1. With a cubic word-size modulus, the state space is extremely large.
2. Using single-precision $a$ and $c$, successive terms are dominated by the multiplication, but the low-order bits still cycle quickly.
3. Increasing the modulus while keeping multipliers small does **not** automatically produce maximal period. The effective period is limited by the choice of $a$ and $c$.
4. Extended-precision arithmetic is necessary to handle intermediate products, as standard 64-bit arithmetic cannot represent numbers modulo $2^{96}$.

### Comparative Analysis

| Feature | $m = 2^{64}$ | $m = 2^{96}$ |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Word size | 32-bit | 32-bit |
| Multiplier / increment | Single-precision | Single-precision |
| State space | $2^{64}$ | $2^{96}$ |
| Low-order bit behavior | Cycles quickly | Cycles quickly |
| High-order bit randomness | Moderate | Slightly improved |
| Period | Limited by multiplier size | Limited by multiplier size |

**Conclusions:**

1. Using $m = w^2$ or $w^3$ with single-precision $a$ and $c$ produces sequences with a very large state space.
2. Low-order bits have short periods, so caution is needed if these bits are used for random number generation.
3. Single-precision multipliers do not maximize the period for the larger moduli; larger multipliers or carefully chosen $a$ are required for near-maximal periods.
4. Extended-precision arithmetic is mandatory for $m = w^3$ to avoid overflow.
5. Experimental computation confirms that increasing the modulus while keeping small multipliers increases the range but does not automatically improve statistical quality or period of the LCG.

**Implementation Notes**

In practice, pseudocode for $m = 2^{64}$ using arbitrary-precision arithmetic is:

```
a = 75425123
c = 123456789
x = 1

for n from 1 to 10:
    x = (a * x + c) mod 2^64
    print(x)
```

For $m = 2^{96}$, replace `2^64` with `2^96` and ensure arithmetic supports 96-bit integers. Using standard 32- or 64-bit types is insufficient.
