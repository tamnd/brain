**Exercise 35.** [*HM35*] How many pairs $(a, c)$ satisfy the conditions of exercise 34?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 8m05s  

---

## Setup

Let

$$
f(x)=x^2-cx-a
$$

over the field $\mathbf F_p$, where $p$ is prime. Exercise 34 shows that the inversive congruential sequence has period $p+1$ if and only if $f(x)$ satisfies:

$$
\text{(i)}\quad x^{p+1}\bmod f(x)
\text{ is a nonzero constant},
$$

and

$$
\text{(ii)}\quad x^{(p+1)/q}\bmod f(x)
\text{ has degree }1
\text{ for every prime }q\mid(p+1).
$$

The problem asks for the number of pairs $(a,c)\in\mathbf F_p^2$ satisfying these conditions.

## Solution

Let $\alpha$ denote the residue class of $x$ in the quotient ring

$$
K=\mathbf F_p[x]/(f(x)).
$$

Condition (i) implies that $\alpha^{p+1}$ is a nonzero constant. Since $\alpha$ is nonzero, it is invertible; therefore $f(x)$ cannot have a root in $\mathbf F_p$. Hence $f(x)$ is irreducible of degree $2$, and $K$ is the field $\mathbf F_{p^2}$.

In $\mathbf F_{p^2}$, condition (i) states that

$$
\alpha^{p+1}\in \mathbf F_p^\times .
$$

Since $\alpha^{p+1}$ is already fixed by the Frobenius automorphism, this is automatic for every nonzero $\alpha\in\mathbf F_{p^2}$. The essential content of (i) is that $\alpha\neq0$.

Condition (ii) says that for every prime divisor $q$ of $p+1$,

$$
\alpha^{(p+1)/q}
$$

is not a constant. Since constants are exactly the elements of $\mathbf F_p$, and since

$$
\bigl(\alpha^{(p+1)/q}\bigr)^{p-1}
=\alpha^{(p^2-1)/q},
$$

the element $\alpha^{(p+1)/q}$ lies in $\mathbf F_p^\times$ if and only if

$$
\alpha^{(p^2-1)/q}=1.
$$

Therefore condition (ii) is equivalent to

$$
\alpha^{(p^2-1)/q}\ne1
\qquad
(q\mid p+1).
$$

Let $N=\operatorname{ord}(\alpha)$ in the cyclic group

$\mathbf F_{p^2}^\times$, whose order is $p^2-1=(p-1)(p+1)$.

The preceding condition means that $N$ does not divide

$(p^2-1)/q$ for any prime divisor $q$ of $p+1$.

Condition (i) implies

$$
\alpha^{p+1}\in\mathbf F_p^\times,
$$

hence

$$
N\mid(p^2-1).
$$

Combining these facts, every prime divisor of $p+1$ must occur in $N$

with the same exponent as in $p+1$. Equivalently,

$$
p+1\mid N.
$$

Conversely, if $p+1\mid N$, then $N$ cannot divide

$(p^2-1)/q$ for any prime $q\mid(p+1)$, so condition (ii) holds.

Thus the admissible elements $\alpha$ are precisely those whose order is divisible by $p+1$.

Because $\mathbf F_{p^2}^\times$ is cyclic of order

$$
(p-1)(p+1),
$$

the number of elements whose order is divisible by $p+1$ is obtained as follows. Write

$$
\mathbf F_{p^2}^\times \cong C_{p-1}\times C_{p+1},
$$

since $\gcd(p-1,p+1)\le2$ and the group is cyclic. An element has order divisible by $p+1$ exactly when its component in the factor $C_{p+1}$ has order $p+1$.

There are $\varphi(p+1)$ choices for such a component and $p-1$ arbitrary choices for the component in $C_{p-1}$. Hence the number of admissible elements $\alpha$ equals

$$
(p-1)\varphi(p+1).
$$

Each irreducible quadratic polynomial corresponds to its two conjugate roots $\alpha$ and $\alpha^p$, and these roots determine the same coefficients

$$
c=\alpha+\alpha^p,\qquad
a=-\alpha^{p+1}.
$$

Since $\alpha\notin\mathbf F_p$, the roots are distinct. Therefore each admissible polynomial, hence each admissible pair $(a,c)$, is counted exactly twice among the admissible elements $\alpha$.

The number of pairs $(a,c)$ satisfying the conditions of Exercise 34 is therefore

$$
\frac{(p-1)\varphi(p+1)}{2}.
$$

Thus

$$
\boxed{\frac{(p-1)\varphi(p+1)}{2}}.
$$

## Verification

The multiplicative group $\mathbf F_{p^2}^\times$ has order $p^2-1$.

An admissible element $\alpha$ must satisfy $p+1\mid\operatorname{ord}(\alpha)$. The number of such elements is

$$
(p-1)\varphi(p+1).
$$

Every admissible pair $(a,c)$ arises from the minimal polynomial

$$
(x-\alpha)(x-\alpha^p)
=
x^2-(\alpha+\alpha^p)x+\alpha^{p+1},
$$

and the two roots $\alpha,\alpha^p$ produce the same pair. No other element produces that polynomial. Division by $2$ therefore gives

$$
\frac{(p-1)\varphi(p+1)}{2}.
$$

This agrees with the count obtained above.

## Notes

The result is the exact analogue of the count $\varphi(p^k-1)/k$ for primitive polynomials discussed after equation (9). Here the relevant extension field is $\mathbf F_{p^2}$, and Exercise 34 characterizes those quadratic polynomials whose associated matrix has order divisible by $p+1$. The conjugacy pairing $\alpha\leftrightarrow\alpha^p$ contributes the factor $1/2$.

This completes the proof.
