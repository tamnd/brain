**Exercise 13.** [*M20*] Let $(X_n)$ and $(Y_n)$ be sequences of integers mod $m$ with periods of lengths $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$, and combine them by letting $Z_n = (X_n + Y_n) \bmod m$. Show that if $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$ are relatively prime, the sequence $(Z_n)$ has a period of length $\lambda_1 \lambda_2$.

## Solution

Let $(X_n)$ and $(Y_n)$ be sequences of integers modulo $m$ with periods $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$, respectively. Define the sequence $(Z_n)$ by

$Z_n = (X_n + Y_n) \bmod m, \qquad n \ge 0.$

We are to show that if $\gcd(\lambda_1, \lambda_2) = 1$, then $(Z_n)$ has period $\lambda_1 \lambda_2$.

Let $P$ denote the period of $(Z_n)$. By definition, $P$ is the smallest positive integer such that

$Z_{n+P} \equiv Z_n \pmod{m} \quad \text{for all } n \ge 0.$

Expanding $Z_n$, this congruence becomes

$(X_{n+P} + Y_{n+P}) \equiv (X_n + Y_n) \pmod{m},$

or equivalently

$(X_{n+P} - X_n) + (Y_{n+P} - Y_n) \equiv 0 \pmod{m} \quad \text{for all } n \ge 0. \eqno(1)$

Since $(X_n)$ has period $\lambda_1$, we have $X_{n+\lambda_1} \equiv X_n \pmod{m}$ for all $n$, and since $(Y_n)$ has period $\lambda_2$, we have $Y_{n+\lambda_2} \equiv Y_n \pmod{m}$ for all $n$. Therefore, any common multiple of $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$ will satisfy equation (1). Let us denote by $L$ the least common multiple of $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$. Then $Z_{n+L} \equiv Z_n \pmod{m}$ for all $n$, so $P$ divides $L$.

Since $\gcd(\lambda_1, \lambda_2) = 1$, we have $L = \lambda_1 \lambda_2$. Thus $P$ divides $\lambda_1 \lambda_2$.

We now show that no smaller period exists. Suppose, for the sake of contradiction, that there exists a positive integer $Q < \lambda_1 \lambda_2$ such that $Z_{n+Q} \equiv Z_n \pmod{m}$ for all $n$. Then equation (1) implies that

$X_{n+Q} - X_n \equiv -(Y_{n+Q} - Y_n) \pmod{m} \quad \text{for all } n.$

Consider $n = 0$. Let $r_1$ be the remainder of $Q$ modulo $\lambda_1$, so that $Q = k_1 \lambda_1 + r_1$ with $0 \le r_1 < \lambda_1$. Then

$X_Q \equiv X_{k_1 \lambda_1 + r_1} \equiv X_{r_1} \pmod{m}.$

Similarly, let $r_2$ be the remainder of $Q$ modulo $\lambda_2$, so that $Q = k_2 \lambda_2 + r_2$ with $0 \le r_2 < \lambda_2$, and

$Y_Q \equiv Y_{r_2} \pmod{m}.$

Equation (1) with $n = 0$ gives

$X_Q + Y_Q \equiv X_0 + Y_0 \pmod{m} \implies X_{r_1} + Y_{r_2} \equiv X_0 + Y_0 \pmod{m}. \eqno(2)$

Since $0 \le r_1 < \lambda_1$ and $0 \le r_2 < \lambda_2$, equation (2) can hold for all initial values $X_0, Y_0$ only if $r_1 = 0$ and $r_2 = 0$. Otherwise, $X_{r_1} \not\equiv X_0$ modulo $m$ or $Y_{r_2} \not\equiv Y_0$ modulo $m$, because $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$ are the minimal periods.

Hence, $Q$ must be divisible by both $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$. Since $\gcd(\lambda_1, \lambda_2) = 1$, the minimal positive $Q$ satisfying this condition is $Q = \lambda_1 \lambda_2$.

Thus $P = \lambda_1 \lambda_2$, and no smaller period exists. This completes the proof.
