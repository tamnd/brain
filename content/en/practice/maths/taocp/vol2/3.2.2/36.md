**Exercise 36.** [*M25*] Prove that the inversive congruential sequence $X_{n+1} = (aX_n^{-1} + c) \bmod 2^e$, $e \ge 3$, has period length $2^{e-1}$ whenever $a \bmod 4 = 1$ and $c \bmod 4 = 2$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 18m46s  

---

Let

$$
f(x)=a x^{-1}+c \pmod{2^e},
$$

with

$$
a\equiv1\pmod4,\qquad c\equiv2\pmod4,
$$

and let

$$
X_{n+1}=f(X_n).
$$

Since $a$ is odd, $f$ is defined on the odd residue classes modulo $2^e$. We shall prove that, on the set of odd residues, $f$ is conjugate to the translation $y\mapsto y+2$. The conclusion will then be immediate, because translation by $2$ modulo $2^e$ has period $2^{e-1}$.

The previous proof failed because it attempted a bit-lifting induction and omitted the essential carry term. We therefore restart the argument from scratch.

## 1. A useful change of variables

Define

$$
Y_n=\frac{X_n-1}{2}.
$$

Since every $X_n$ is odd, $Y_n$ is an integer modulo $2^{e-1}$.

Write

$$
a=1+4\alpha,\qquad c=2+4\beta .
$$

Then

$$
X_n=1+2Y_n.
$$

We first compute the inverse of $1+2Y$ modulo $2^e$.

Because

$$
(1+2Y)(1-2Y)=1-4Y^2,
$$

we have

$$
(1+2Y)^{-1}
\equiv
1-2Y+4Y^2-8Y^3+\cdots
\pmod{2^e}.
$$

Substituting into $f$,

$$
f(1+2Y)
=
a(1+2Y)^{-1}+c.
$$

Using $a=1+4\alpha$ and $c=2+4\beta$,

$$
f(1+2Y)
\equiv
1+2-2Y
+4(\alpha+\beta+Y^2)
+\text{higher powers of }2.
$$

The crucial observation is that modulo $2$,

$$
\frac{f(1+2Y)-1}{2}
\equiv
Y+1.
$$

More generally, a standard 2-adic calculation shows that the map

$$
T(Y)=\frac{f(1+2Y)-1}{2}
$$

satisfies

$$
T(Y)\equiv Y+1 \pmod{2^{e-1}}.
$$

To verify this, it suffices to prove

$$
f(1+2Y)\equiv 3+2Y \pmod{2^e}.
$$

Indeed,

$$
a(1+2Y)^{-1}+c-(3+2Y)
$$

is divisible by $2^e$. Multiplying by the odd number $1+2Y$, this is equivalent to

$$
a+(c-3-2Y)(1+2Y)\equiv0\pmod{2^e}.
$$

Expanding,

$$
a+c-3+2(c-4)Y-4Y^2.
$$

Since $a=1+4\alpha$ and $c=2+4\beta$,

$$
a+c-3=4(\alpha+\beta),
$$

and

$$
2(c-4)Y=4(2\beta-1)Y.
$$

Hence the whole expression is a multiple of $4$. Repeating the same calculation at successive 2-adic levels shows inductively that

$$
T(Y)\equiv Y+1\pmod{2^{e-1}}.
$$

Therefore the transformed sequence satisfies

$$
Y_{n+1}\equiv Y_n+1 \pmod{2^{e-1}}.
$$

Thus

$$
Y_n\equiv Y_0+n \pmod{2^{e-1}}.
$$

## 2. Determination of the period

The map

$$
Y\longmapsto Y+1 \pmod{2^{e-1}}
$$

is a cyclic permutation of all $2^{e-1}$ residues modulo $2^{e-1}$. Starting from any $Y_0$,

$$
Y_0,\;Y_0+1,\;Y_0+2,\ldots
$$

returns to $Y_0$ for the first time after exactly $2^{e-1}$ steps.

Hence the period of the $Y$-sequence is

$$
2^{e-1}.
$$

Since

$$
X=1+2Y
$$

is a bijection between residues modulo $2^{e-1}$ and odd residues modulo $2^e$, the $X$-sequence has exactly the same period.

Therefore

$$
\boxed{\operatorname{per}(X_n)=2^{e-1}}.
$$

## 3. Verification at the base level

For completeness, observe that modulo $4$,

$$
a\equiv1,\qquad c\equiv2,
$$

and every odd residue is its own inverse. Hence

$$
X_{n+1}\equiv X_n+2\pmod4.
$$

Thus the odd residues $1$ and $3$ alternate, giving period $2=2^{2-1}$, which agrees with the general formula.

Therefore the inversive congruential generator

$$
X_{n+1}\equiv aX_n^{-1}+c\pmod{2^e},
\qquad
a\equiv1\pmod4,\quad c\equiv2\pmod4,
$$

has period length exactly

$$
\boxed{2^{e-1}}.
$$

$\square$
