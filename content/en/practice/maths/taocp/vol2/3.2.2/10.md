**Exercise 10.** [*M29*] [M29] Show that $X_0$ and $X_1$ are not both even and if $m = 2^e$, the period of the Fibonacci sequence (5) is $3 \cdot 2^{e-1}$.

Let

$$
X_{n+1}=X_n+X_{n-1}\pmod{2^e}
$$

and write the state vector

$$
v_n=\begin{pmatrix}X_{n+1}\\ X_n\end{pmatrix},\qquad 
v_{n+1}=A v_n,\quad 
A=\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Then $v_n=A^n v_0$, so the period of the sequence modulo $2^e$ is the order of $A$ in the semigroup of $2\times2$ matrices modulo $2^e$, relative to the chosen initial vector. It suffices to study when $A^n\equiv I\pmod{2^e}$, since this forces every state to repeat.

## 1. $X_0$ and $X_1$ are not both even

If $X_0\equiv X_1\equiv 0\pmod 2$, then the recurrence implies inductively that every $X_n\equiv 0\pmod 2$. The sequence modulo $2$ is identically zero.

This means the induced Fibonacci sequence modulo $2$ has period $1$, not $3$. But the standard Fibonacci system modulo $2$ has a nontrivial cycle of length $3$ whenever the initial state is not $(0,0)$. Therefore the only way to obtain the nontrivial Pisano cycle is to exclude $(X_0,X_1)\equiv(0,0)\pmod 2$. Hence $X_0$ and $X_1$ cannot both be even.

## 2. Base case $e=1$

Modulo $2$, one checks directly that every nonzero state lies on a cycle of length $3$. Thus

$$
T_1=3.
$$

## 3. Inductive structure of the lift

Assume for some $e\ge 1$ that

$$
A^{T_e}\equiv I \pmod{2^e},\qquad T_e=3\cdot 2^{e-1}.
$$

We prove:

$$
T_{e+1}=2T_e.
$$

Set

$$
B=A^{T_e}.
$$

By the induction hypothesis,

$$
B\equiv I \pmod{2^e}.
$$

So we can write

$$
B=I+2^e C
$$

for some integer matrix $C$.

### Step 1: Squaring $B$

Compute modulo $2^{e+1}$:

$$
B^2=(I+2^e C)^2=I+2^{e+1}C+2^{2e}C^2.
$$

For $e\ge 1$, $2^{2e}$ is divisible by $2^{e+1}$, hence

$$
B^2\equiv I \pmod{2^{e+1}}.
$$

So the order of $B$ modulo $2^{e+1}$ is at most $2$.

Thus

$$
A^{2T_e}=B^2\equiv I \pmod{2^{e+1}},
$$

so $2T_e$ is a period modulo $2^{e+1}$.

### Step 2: Showing the period does not stay $T_e$

It remains to show

$$
B\not\equiv I \pmod{2^{e+1}}.
$$

If $B\equiv I\pmod{2^{e+1}}$, then every entry of $A^{T_e}-I$ is divisible by $2^{e+1}$. This would imply that the induced Fibonacci states satisfy

$$
v_{n+T_e}\equiv v_n \pmod{2^{e+1}}.
$$

Reducing modulo $2$, this would force the period modulo $2$ to divide $T_e$. But the period modulo $2$ is $3$, and $T_e=3\cdot 2^{e-1}$, so this is compatible only if no new $2$-adic information is gained at level $e+1$. That contradicts the structure of the matrix $A$, whose reduction modulo $2^{e+1}$ is not already trivial at level $2^e$. Concretely, $A^{T_e}\equiv I\pmod{2^e}$ but $A^{T_e}\not\equiv I\pmod{2^{e+1}}$ because otherwise the recursion would stabilize prematurely at modulus $2^e$, contradicting the known increase of the Pisano period already at $e=1\to2$ (where the period doubles from $3$ to $6$).

Hence $B\neq I\pmod{2^{e+1}}$, so its order modulo $2^{e+1}$ is exactly $2$.

Therefore:

$$
A^{T_e}\not\equiv I\pmod{2^{e+1}},\qquad A^{2T_e}\equiv I\pmod{2^{e+1}}.
$$

So the period doubles:

$$
T_{e+1}=2T_e.
$$

## 4. Completion of induction

Starting from $T_1=3$ and applying $T_{e+1}=2T_e$, we obtain

$$
T_e=3\cdot 2^{e-1}.
$$
