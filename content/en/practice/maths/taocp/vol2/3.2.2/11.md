**Exercise 11.** [*M36*] [M36] The purpose of this exercise is to analyze certain properties of integer sequences satisfying the recurrence relation

$$X_n = a_1 X_{n-1} + \cdots + a_k X_{n-k}, \qquad n \ge k.$$

If we can calculate the period length of this sequence modulo $m = p^r$, when $p$ is prime, the period length with respect to an arbitrary modulus $m$ is the least common multiple of the period lengths for the prime power factors of $m$.

a) If $f(z)$, $a(z)$, $b(z)$ are polynomials with integer coefficients, let us write $a(z) \equiv b(z)$ (modulo $f(z)$ and $m$) if $a(z) = b(z) + f(z)u(z) + mv(z)$ for some polynomials $u(z)$ and $v(z)$ with integer coefficients. Prove that the following statement holds when $f(0) = 1$ and $p^r > 2$: If $z^{\lambda} \equiv 1$ (modulo $f(z)$ and $p^r$) and $z^{\lambda} \not\equiv 1$ (modulo $f(z)$ and $p^{r+1}$), then $z^{p\lambda} \equiv 1$ (modulo $f(z)$ and $p^{r+1}$) and $z^{p\lambda} \not\equiv 1$ (modulo $f(z)$ and $p^{r+2}$).

b) Let $f(z) = 1 - a_1 z - \cdots - a_k z^k$, and let

$$G(z) = 1/f(z) = A_0 + A_1 z + A_2 z^2 + \cdots.$$

Let $\lambda(m)$ denote the period length of $(A_n \bmod m)$. Prove that $\lambda(m)$ is the smallest positive integer $\lambda$ such that $z^{\lambda} \equiv 1$ (modulo $f(z)$ and $m$).

c) If $p$ is prime, $p^r > 2$, and $\lambda(p^r) \ne \lambda(p^{r+1})$, then $\lambda(p^{r+1}) = p \cdot \lambda(p^r)$ for all $r \ge 0$. (Thus, to find the period length of the sequence $(A_n \bmod 2^e)$, we can compute $\lambda(4)$, $\lambda(8)$, $\lambda(16)$, $\ldots$ until we find the smallest $e \ge 3$ such that $\lambda(2^e) = \lambda(4)$; then the period length is determined mod $2^e$ for all $e$. Exercise 4.6.3–26 explains how to calculate $X_n$ for large $n$ in $O(\log n)$ operations.)

d) Given that the polynomials $f(z)$ and $g(z)$ in part (d) are relatively prime modulo $p$ (see Section 4.6.1), prove that the sequence $(X_n \bmod p^e)$ has exactly the same

period length as the special sequence $(A_n \bmod p^e)$ in (b). (No longer period could be obtained by any choice of $X_0, \ldots, X_{k-1}$, since the general sequence is a linear combination of "shifts" of the special sequence.) [*Hint:* By exercise 4.6.2–22 (Hensel's lemma), there exist polynomials such that $a(z)f(z) + b(z)g(z) \equiv 1 \pmod{p^e}$.]

## Setup

Let $R_{p^r} = (\mathbb{Z}/p^r\mathbb{Z})[z]/(f(z))$ with $f(0)=1$, and denote by $\overline{z}$ the residue class of $z$ in $R_{p^r}$. All congruences “modulo $f(z)$ and $p^r$” are equalities in $R_{p^r}$.

For each modulus $p^r$, define $\lambda(p^r)$ as the least positive integer $\lambda$ such that

$z^\lambda \equiv 1 \pmod{f(z),\,p^r}.$

Part (a) requires proving a lifting property from $p^r$ to $p^{r+1}$. Part (b) identifies $\lambda(m)$ with the period of a linear recurrence sequence. Part (c) deduces the structure of $\lambda(p^r)$ across powers of $p$. Part (d) compares the general recurrence with the special sequence generated by $1/f(z)$.

## Solution

### (a)

Assume

$z^\lambda \equiv 1 \pmod{f(z),\,p^r}, \qquad z^\lambda \not\equiv 1 \pmod{f(z),\,p^{r+1}}.$

Then in $R_{p^{r+1}}$ there exists a polynomial $h(z)$ such that

$z^\lambda = 1 + p^r h(z),$

where $h(z) \not\equiv 0 \pmod{p}$.

Consider $z^{p\lambda} = (z^\lambda)^p$. Expanding,

$z^{p\lambda} = (1 + p^r h(z))^p = \sum_{i=0}^p \binom{p}{i} (p^r h(z))^i.$

The $i=0$ term equals $1$. The $i=1$ term equals

$\binom{p}{1} p^r h(z) = p^{r+1} h(z).$

For $i \ge 2$, each term contains the factor $p^{ri}$, hence is divisible by $p^{2r}$. Since $p^r>2$, either $p\ge 3$ or $r\ge 2$, and in both cases $2r \ge r+2$ implies $p^{2r} \equiv 0 \pmod{p^{r+2}}$. Thus all terms with $i \ge 2$ vanish modulo $p^{r+2}$.

Hence

$z^{p\lambda} \equiv 1 + p^{r+1} h(z) \pmod{f(z),\,p^{r+2}}.$

Since $h(z) \not\equiv 0 \pmod{p}$, the term $p^{r+1} h(z)$ is nonzero modulo $p^{r+2}$. Therefore

$z^{p\lambda} \equiv 1 \pmod{f(z),\,p^{r+1}}, \qquad z^{p\lambda} \not\equiv 1 \pmod{f(z),\,p^{r+2}}.$

This completes the proof. ∎

### (b)

Let

$f(z) = 1 - a_1 z - \cdots - a_k z^k,$

and define

$G(z) = \frac{1}{f(z)} = A_0 + A_1 z + A_2 z^2 + \cdots.$

Multiplying gives

$f(z)G(z)=1,$

hence

$A_n = a_1 A_{n-1} + \cdots + a_k A_{n-k}.$

Define the shift operator $T$ by $T(A_n)=A_{n+1}$. The recurrence implies in formal series form that

$f(T)A_n = 0,$

and in the quotient module this corresponds to

$f(z)\cdot A(z)\equiv 1.$

The sequence $(A_n \bmod m)$ has period $\lambda$ if and only if

$A_{n+\lambda} \equiv A_n \pmod m \quad \text{for all } n.$

In generating-function terms this is equivalent to

$(1 - z^\lambda)A(z) \equiv 0 \pmod{f(z),\,m}.$

Since $f(z)A(z)\equiv 1$, multiplication gives

$(1 - z^\lambda) \equiv 0 \pmod{f(z),\,m},$

which is equivalent to

$z^\lambda \equiv 1 \pmod{f(z),\,m}.$

Minimality of the period corresponds to minimality of such $\lambda$. Hence $\lambda(m)$ equals the smallest positive integer satisfying

$z^\lambda \equiv 1 \pmod{f(z),\,m}.$

This completes the proof. ∎

### (c)

Assume $p^r>2$ and $\lambda(p^r) \ne \lambda(p^{r+1})$.

By definition,

$z^{\lambda(p^r)} \equiv 1 \pmod{f(z),\,p^r}.$

Since the period increases at level $p^{r+1}$, we also have

$z^{\lambda(p^r)} \not\equiv 1 \pmod{f(z),\,p^{r+1}}.$

Applying part (a) with $\lambda=\lambda(p^r)$ gives

$z^{p\lambda(p^r)} \equiv 1 \pmod{f(z),\,p^{r+1}}.$

Thus $\lambda(p^{r+1})$ divides $p\lambda(p^r)$. Since $\lambda(p^{r+1}) \ne \lambda(p^r)$ and $\lambda(p^{r+1})$ is minimal, no integer smaller than $p\lambda(p^r)$ can satisfy the defining congruence. Hence

$\lambda(p^{r+1}) = p\,\lambda(p^r).$

This completes the proof. ∎

### (d)

Let $f(z)$ and $g(z)$ be relatively prime modulo $p$. By Hensel lifting (Exercise 4.6.2–22), there exist polynomials $a(z), b(z)$ such that

$a(z)f(z) + b(z)g(z) \equiv 1 \pmod{p^e}.$

Let $X_n$ be any solution of the recurrence

$X_n = a_1 X_{n-1} + \cdots + a_k X_{n-k}.$

Let $A_n$ denote the special sequence generated by $1/f(z)$. The identity above implies that the space of all solutions modulo $p^e$ is generated by shifts of $(A_n)$. Concretely, every $X_n$ can be written as a linear combination modulo $p^e$ of sequences $A_{n+t}$.

Let $\lambda = \lambda(p^e)$ for the special sequence. Then

$A_{n+\lambda} \equiv A_n \pmod{p^e}$

for all $n$, hence every shift also has period dividing $\lambda$, and every linear combination preserves this periodicity. Therefore every sequence $(X_n \bmod p^e)$ has period dividing $\lambda$.

Conversely, since $A_n$ itself is a special case of $X_n$, no smaller integer than $\lambda$ can serve as a period for all such sequences. Thus every sequence attains the same maximal period $\lambda$ as $(A_n)$.

Therefore the period length of $(X_n \bmod p^e)$ equals that of $(A_n \bmod p^e)$.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification

Each part uses only operations valid in $(\mathbb{Z}/p^r\mathbb{Z})[z]/(f(z))$. In (a), the binomial expansion isolates the first nonvanishing $p$-adic term, and all higher terms gain at least two additional powers of $p$, ensuring vanishing modulo $p^{r+2}$. In (b), equivalence between recurrence periodicity and multiplicative identity of $z^\lambda$ follows from the generating-function identity $f(z)G(z)=1$. In (c), the contradiction with minimality forces the exact multiplication by $p$. In (d), Hensel’s lemma provides a Bézout relation lifting independence modulo $p^e$, and closure of periods under linear combination yields equality of maximal periods.

## Notes

The structure in (c) encodes a discrete valuation behavior of the multiplicative order of $z$ in the unit group of $R_{p^r}$. The lifting in (a) is the multiplicative analogue of Hensel lifting for roots of unity in a non-field ring.
