**Solution to Exercise 3.2.2.37**

Let $p$ be prime, and let ${X_n}_{n\ge 0}$ be an inversive congruential sequence defined modulo $p$ by

$$
X_{n+1} \equiv a X_n^{-1} + c \pmod{p}, \quad X_n \in \mathbb{F}_p \cup \{\infty\},
$$

with period $p+1$. Let integers $0 \le b_1 < \dots < b_d \le p$ be given, and define

$$
V = \{ (X_{n+b_1}, X_{n+b_2}, \dots, X_{n+b_d}) \mid 0 \le n \le p, \ X_{n+b_j} \ne \infty \text{ for all } j\}.
$$

We are to show that no $d+1$ vectors in $V$ lie in the same $(d-1)$-dimensional hyperplane modulo $p$.

**Step 1: Express each $X_{n+b_j}$ as a Möbius transformation of $X_n$**

Consider the projective representation of $X_n$ via

$$
X_n = \frac{x_n}{y_n}, \quad \text{with } Y_n = \begin{pmatrix} x_n \\ y_n \end{pmatrix} \in \mathbb{F}_p^2, \ y_n \ne 0 \text{ for } X_n \ne \infty,
$$

so that $X_n = \infty$ corresponds to $y_n = 0$. The recurrence can be written linearly in homogeneous coordinates:

$$
Y_{n+1} = M Y_n, \quad M = \begin{pmatrix} c & a \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}.
$$

Iterating $b_j$ steps gives

$$
Y_{n+b_j} = M^{b_j} Y_n.
$$

Writing $M^{b_j} = \begin{pmatrix} \alpha_j & \beta_j \ \gamma_j & \delta_j \end{pmatrix}$, we have

$$
X_{n+b_j} = \frac{\alpha_j x_n + \beta_j y_n}{\gamma_j x_n + \delta_j y_n} = \frac{\alpha_j X_n + \beta_j}{\gamma_j X_n + \delta_j},
$$

where $X_n = x_n/y_n$ and $\gamma_j X_n + \delta_j \ne 0$ since $X_{n+b_j} \ne \infty$. This expresses each $X_{n+b_j}$ as a fractional linear (Möbius) transformation of $X_n$.

**Step 2: Hyperplane condition as a polynomial equation in $X_n$**

Suppose that $d+1$ vectors of $V$ lie in a hyperplane

$$
H = \{ (r_1, \dots, r_d) \mid v_1 r_1 + \dots + v_d r_d \equiv v_0 \pmod{p} \}, \quad (v_1, \dots, v_d) \ne (0, \dots, 0).
$$

Then there exist $d+1$ distinct integers $n_0, \dots, n_d$ such that

$$
v_1 X_{n_i+b_1} + \dots + v_d X_{n_i+b_d} \equiv v_0 \pmod{p}, \quad i = 0, \dots, d.
$$

Substitute the Möbius representations:

$$
v_1 \frac{\alpha_1 X_{n_i} + \beta_1}{\gamma_1 X_{n_i} + \delta_1} + \dots + v_d \frac{\alpha_d X_{n_i} + \beta_d}{\gamma_d X_{n_i} + \delta_d} \equiv v_0.
$$

Multiply both sides by the common denominator

$$
D(X_{n_i}) = \prod_{j=1}^d (\gamma_j X_{n_i} + \delta_j)
$$

to obtain a polynomial equation

$$
P(X_{n_i}) := \sum_{j=1}^d v_j (\alpha_j X_{n_i} + \beta_j) \prod_{\substack{k=1 \\ k \ne j}}^d (\gamma_k X_{n_i} + \delta_k) - v_0 \prod_{j=1}^d (\gamma_j X_{n_i} + \delta_j) \equiv 0 \pmod{p}.
$$

This is a polynomial of degree at most $d$ in $X_{n_i}$ because each term $\prod_{k\ne j} (\gamma_k X_{n_i} + \delta_k)$ has degree $d-1$, and the $-v_0 \prod_{j=1}^d (\gamma_j X_{n_i} + \delta_j)$ term has degree $d$.

**Step 3: The polynomial is nonzero**

The polynomial $P(X)$ is not identically zero. Suppose it were zero. Then the fractional linear combination

$$
v_1 X_{n+b_1} + \dots + v_d X_{n+b_d} \equiv v_0
$$

would hold for all $X_n \in \mathbb{F}_p \cup {\infty}$ with $X_{n+b_j} \ne \infty$. Since a fractional linear function is injective on $\mathbb{F}_p \cup {\infty}$, this is impossible unless $v_1 = \dots = v_d = 0$, which contradicts the definition of a hyperplane. Therefore $P(X)$ is a nonzero polynomial of degree at most $d$.

**Step 4: Counting solutions**

A nonzero polynomial of degree at most $d$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$ has at most $d$ roots in $\mathbb{F}_p$. Therefore, there can be at most $d$ distinct values of $X_n$ (and hence at most $d$ vectors in $V$) satisfying the hyperplane equation. In particular, $d+1$ vectors cannot lie in the same hyperplane.

**Step 5: Conclusion**

No $d+1$ vectors of $V$ lie in the same $(d-1)$-dimensional hyperplane modulo $p$. This proves the claim.

The argument uses the explicit Möbius representation of $X_{n+b_j}$ in terms of $X_n$, converts the hyperplane condition to a nonzero polynomial equation of degree at most $d$, and applies the fundamental fact that a degree-$d$ polynomial over $\mathbb{F}_p$ has at most $d$ roots.
