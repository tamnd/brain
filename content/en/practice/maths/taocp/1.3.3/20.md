**Exercise 20.** [*M20*] Given that all singleton cycles are written out explicitly, how many different ways are there to write the cycle notation of a permutation that has $\alpha_1$ one-cycles, $\alpha_2$ two-cycles, ... ? (See exercise 5.)

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m23s  

---

## Solution

Let the permutation have $\alpha_1$ cycles of length $1$, $\alpha_2$ cycles of length $2$, and in general $\alpha_m$ cycles of length $m$.

The cycle notation is not unique for two independent reasons.

First, the cycles may be written in any order. If

$$
r=\alpha_1+\alpha_2+\alpha_3+\cdots
$$

is the total number of cycles, there are $r!$ ways to arrange them. However, cycles having the same length are indistinguishable as types, so permuting the $\alpha_m$ cycles of length $m$ among themselves does not produce a new notation. Hence the number of distinct orders of the cycles is

$$
\frac{r!}{\alpha_1!\alpha_2!\alpha_3!\cdots}.
$$

Second, each individual cycle may be started at any of its elements. A cycle of length $m$ therefore has exactly $m$ different written forms:

$$
(a_1a_2\cdots a_m),
\quad
(a_2a_3\cdots a_ma_1),
\quad \ldots \quad,
(a_ma_1\cdots a_{m-1}).
$$

Since singleton cycles are written explicitly, a $1$-cycle contributes a factor $1$. Thus the total number of choices of starting points is

$$
\prod_{m\ge1} m^{\alpha_m}.
$$

The two choices are independent. Therefore the total number of different ways to write the cycle notation of the permutation is

$$
\frac{(\alpha_1+\alpha_2+\alpha_3+\cdots)!}
     {\alpha_1!\alpha_2!\alpha_3!\cdots}
\prod_{m\ge1} m^{\alpha_m}.
$$

Hence the answer is

$$
\boxed{
\frac{\left(\sum_{m\ge1}\alpha_m\right)!}
     {\prod_{m\ge1}\alpha_m!}
\prod_{m\ge1} m^{\alpha_m}
}.
$$

This completes the proof.
