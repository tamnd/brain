**Exercise 15.** [*M12*] Is there a permutation that represents exactly the same transformation both in the canonical cycle form without parentheses and in the linear form?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 4m07s  

---

## Solution

Yes. The identity permutation is the unique example.

Let $\pi$ be a permutation of $\{1,2,\ldots,n\}$. Suppose that the string obtained from the canonical cycle form of $\pi$ by deleting the parentheses is exactly the linear form of $\pi$.

Write the canonical cycle form as

$$
(c_{11}\ c_{12}\ \cdots\ c_{1\ell_1})
(c_{21}\ c_{22}\ \cdots\ c_{2\ell_2})
\cdots
(c_{r1}\ c_{r2}\ \cdots\ c_{r\ell_r}),
$$

where each cycle begins with its largest element and

$$
c_{11}<c_{21}<\cdots<c_{r1}.
$$

Let

$$
a_1a_2\cdots a_n
$$

be the string obtained by deleting the parentheses. By hypothesis this is also the linear form of $\pi$, so

$$
a_i=\pi(i)\qquad(1\le i\le n).
$$

We shall show that every cycle has length $1$.

Let

$$
m=c_{r1}.
$$

Since the cycle leaders are increasing and each leader is the largest element of its cycle, $m$ is the largest element of the permutation. Hence

$$
m=n.
$$

Consider the last cycle. Suppose it is nontrivial:

$$
(n\,b_2\,\ldots\,b_t),\qquad t\ge2.
$$

In the parenthesis-free cycle string, the entries of this cycle occupy the final $t$ positions. Let

$$
s=n-t+1.
$$

Then

$$
a_s=n.
$$

Since $a_i=\pi(i)$, we obtain

$$
\pi(s)=n.
$$

In the cycle $(n\,b_2\,\ldots\,b_t)$, the unique element mapped to $n$ is $b_t$. Therefore

$$
s=b_t.
$$

But $b_t$ belongs to the last cycle and is not its largest element, so

$$
b_t<n.
$$

Hence

$$
s<n.
$$

Now look at the position $s$ inside the parenthesis-free cycle string. Since $s=b_t$ lies in the last cycle, and $b_t$ is the final element of that cycle, position $s$ cannot be the first position of that cycle. Therefore the symbol occupying position $s$ is the element immediately following some element of the same cycle.

In a cycle, every element except the first is strictly less than the first element, which here is $n$. Thus

$$
a_s<n.
$$

On the other hand,

$$
a_s=\pi(s)=n.
$$

This is a contradiction.

Therefore the last cycle cannot have length $>1$. Hence the last cycle is the singleton cycle

$$
(n).
$$

Remove this cycle. The remaining cycles form the canonical cycle form of a permutation of $\{1,\ldots,n-1\}$. The parenthesis-free string for the remaining cycles is still identical to the corresponding initial segment of the linear form, so the same argument applies to the largest remaining element $n-1$. Thus $(n-1)$ is also a singleton cycle.

Repeating this argument inductively shows that every cycle is a singleton:

$$
(1)(2)\cdots(n).
$$

Hence $\pi$ is the identity permutation.

Conversely, the identity permutation has canonical cycle form

$$
(1)(2)\cdots(n),
$$

and deleting the parentheses yields

$$
12\cdots n,
$$

which is exactly its linear form.

Therefore the only permutation whose canonical cycle form without parentheses coincides with its linear form is the identity permutation:

$$
\boxed{\text{The unique such permutation is the identity permutation.}}
$$

$\square$
