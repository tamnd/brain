**Exercise 1.** [*20*] If we had only `LTAG`, `INFO`, and `RTAG` fields (not `LLINK`) in a level order sequential representation like `(8)`, would it be possible to reconstruct the `LLINK`s? (In other words, are the `LLINK`s redundant in `(8)`, as the `RLINK`s are in `(3)`?)

## Solution

The answer is **yes**. In representation (8), the `LLINK` fields are redundant and can be reconstructed from `LTAG`, `INFO`, and `RTAG`.

The key observation is that the nodes are stored in **level order**. Hence, after all nodes at level $k$ have been listed, the nodes at level $k+1$ appear consecutively, in exactly the left-to-right order in which they occur in the forest.

Let the nodes be

$$N_1,N_2,\ldots,N_n$$

in level-order sequence.

For each node $N_i$:

- $LTAG(N_i)=*$ iff $N_i$ has children.
- $LTAG(N_i)=]$ iff $N_i$ is terminal.
- $RTAG(N_i)=]$ iff $N_i$ is the last child of its parent (or the last root of the forest).
- $RTAG(N_i)=*$ otherwise.

We shall show that the leftmost child of every nonterminal node is uniquely determined.

### 1. The children of a level form a partition of the next level

Consider all nodes at some level $k$, listed from left to right:

$$P_1,P_2,\ldots,P_m .$$

Let

$$c_j=
\begin{cases}
1,& LTAG(P_j)=*,\\
0,& LTAG(P_j)=].
\end{cases}$$

The nodes at level $k+1$ are precisely the children of those $P_j$ for which $c_j=1$.

Because the representation is level order, the level-$(k+1)$ nodes occur as one consecutive block

$$Q_1,Q_2,\ldots,Q_t .$$

These nodes must be divided into consecutive groups, one group for each nonterminal parent $P_j$, in left-to-right order.

The question is whether this division is uniquely determined by the $RTAG$ values of the $Q$'s.

### 2. Recovering the boundaries between sibling groups

Scan the sequence

$$Q_1,Q_2,\ldots,Q_t .$$

Whenever $RTAG(Q_r)=]$, the node $Q_r$ is the last child of some parent.

Therefore the positions

$$r_1<r_2<\cdots<r_s$$

for which (RTAG(Q_{r_i})=]

]

mark the ends of the sibling groups.

Since every nonterminal parent has exactly one last child, the number $s$ of such markers equals the number of nonterminal nodes among

$$P_1,\ldots,P_m.$$

Hence the children are partitioned uniquely as

$$(Q_1,\ldots,Q_{r_1}),$$

$$(Q_{r_1+1},\ldots,Q_{r_2}),$$

$$\ldots$$

$$(Q_{r_{s-1}+1},\ldots,Q_{r_s}).$$

These groups correspond, in order, to the nonterminal parents

$$P_{j_1},P_{j_2},\ldots,P_{j_s}.$$

No alternative partition is possible, because moving a boundary would change the position of a node whose $RTAG$ indicates "last child".

### 3. Recovering the $LLINK$ values

For each nonterminal parent $P_{j_\nu}$, its children are exactly the nodes in the $\nu$-th group above.

Therefore its leftmost child is simply the first node of that group:

$$LLINK(P_{j_1}) = Q_1,$$

$$LLINK(P_{j_2}) = Q_{r_1+1},$$

and so on.

Terminal nodes have

$$LLINK = \Lambda .$$

Thus every $LLINK$ is uniquely determined.

### 4. Conclusion

The level-order sequence together with the $LTAG$ and $RTAG$ information uniquely determines how the nodes of each level are grouped as children of the preceding level. Once those groups are known, the leftmost child of every nonterminal node is known.

Therefore the $LLINK$ fields can be reconstructed uniquely from $LTAG$, $INFO$, and $RTAG$.

$$\boxed{\text{The } LLINK\text{s are redundant in representation (8).}}$$

They play the same role that the $RLINK$ fields play in representation (3): they are convenient for navigation, but they are not needed to determine the structure of the forest. ∎
