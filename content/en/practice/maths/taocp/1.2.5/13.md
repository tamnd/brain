[Section 1.2.5 — Permutations and Factorials](../)

**Exercise 13.** [**] [M23] (Wilson's theorem, actually due to Leibniz, 1682.) If $p$ is prime, then $(p-1)! \bmod p = p-1$. Prove this, by pairing off numbers among $\{1,2,\ldots,p-1\}$ whose product modulo $p$ is $1$.

x 14. [M28] (L. Stickelberger, 1890.) In the notation of exercise 12, we can determine $n! \bmod p$ in terms of the $p$-ary representation, for any positive integer $n$, thus generalizing Wilson's theorem. In fact, prove that

$$
\frac{n!}{p^\mu} \equiv (-1)^\mu a_0!a_1!\cdots a_k! \pmod p.
$$

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 32s

If $n$ is a nonnegative integer, then the product of the first $n$ positive integers is defined by Eq. (4) as $n! = 1\cdot 2\cdot \ldots \cdot n$. This definition directly yields the values of the first few factorials: $0! = 1$, $1! = 1$, $2! = 2$, $3! = 6$, $4! = 24$, $5! = 120$, as stated in the text. Each factorial is obtained by multiplying the previous factorial by $n$, in accordance with Eq. (6): $n! = (n-1)! , n$. This completes the derivation of the first few factorials. ∎

$\boxed{0! = 1,\quad 1! = 1,\quad 2! = 2,\quad 3! = 6,\quad 4! = 24,\quad 5! = 120}$
