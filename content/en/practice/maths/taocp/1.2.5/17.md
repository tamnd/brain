[Section 1.2.5 — Permutations and Factorials](../)

**Exercise 17.** [**] [HM20] Prove that the infinite product

$$
\prod_{n \ge 1}\frac{(n+\alpha_1)\cdots(n+\alpha_k)}{(n+\beta_1)\cdots(n+\beta_k)}
$$

equals

$$
\frac{\Gamma(1+\beta_1)\cdots\Gamma(1+\beta_k)}{\Gamma(1+\alpha_1)\cdots\Gamma(1+\alpha_k)},
$$

if $\alpha_1+\cdots+\alpha_k=\beta_1+\cdots+\beta_k$ and if none of the $\beta$'s is a negative integer.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 40s  

---

Let

$$
P_N=\prod_{n=1}^{N}\frac{(n+\alpha_1)\cdots(n+\alpha_k)}
{(n+\beta_1)\cdots(n+\beta_k)}.
$$

Using Euler's formula (13),

$$
\Gamma(1+\alpha)
=\lim_{m\to\infty}
\frac{m!\,m^{\alpha}}
{(1+\alpha)(2+\alpha)\cdots(m+\alpha)},
$$

hence

$$
\prod_{n=1}^{N}(n+\alpha)
=\frac{N!\,N^{\alpha}}{\Gamma(1+\alpha)}(1+o(1))
\qquad (N\to\infty),
$$

and similarly

$$
\prod_{n=1}^{N}(n+\beta)
=\frac{N!\,N^{\beta}}{\Gamma(1+\beta)}(1+o(1)).
$$

Therefore

$$
P_N =
\prod_{i=1}^{k}
\frac{\Gamma(1+\beta_i)}
{\Gamma(1+\alpha_i)}
\,N^{\alpha_i-\beta_i}
(1+o(1)).
$$

Since

$$
\alpha_1+\cdots+\alpha_k =
\beta_1+\cdots+\beta_k,
$$

the power of $N$ cancels:

$$
N^{\sum_i(\alpha_i-\beta_i)}=1.
$$

Taking the limit as $N\to\infty$, the infinite product converges and

$$
\prod_{n\ge1}
\frac{(n+\alpha_1)\cdots(n+\alpha_k)}
{(n+\beta_1)\cdots(n+\beta_k)} =
\frac{\Gamma(1+\beta_1)\cdots\Gamma(1+\beta_k)}
{\Gamma(1+\alpha_1)\cdots\Gamma(1+\alpha_k)}.
$$

The condition that no $\beta_i$ is a negative integer guarantees that the gamma factors in the denominator of Euler's formula are finite. This completes the proof.

∎
