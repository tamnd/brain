[Section 1.2.5 — Permutations and Factorials](../)

**Exercise 20.** [**] [HM21] Using the fact that

$$
0 \le e^{-t} - \left(1-\frac{t}{m}\right)^m \le t^2e^{-t/m},
\qquad \text{if } 0 \le t \le m,
$$

and the previous exercise, show that

$$
\Gamma(x) = \int_0^\infty e^{-t}t^{x-1}\,dt,
\qquad \text{if } x>0.
$$

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 29s  

---

By Exercise 19,

$$
\Gamma_m(x)
=\int_0^m\left(1-\frac{t}{m}\right)^m t^{x-1}\,dt,
\qquad x>0.
$$

Equation (13) defines $\Gamma(x)$ as $\lim_{m\to\infty}\Gamma_m(x)$, so it suffices to show that

$$
\lim_{m\to\infty}\int_0^m\left(1-\frac{t}{m}\right)^m t^{x-1}\,dt =
\int_0^\infty e^{-t}t^{x-1}\,dt.
$$

For $0\le t\le m$, the hypothesis gives

$$
0\le
e^{-t}-\left(1-\frac{t}{m}\right)^m
\le t^2e^{-t/m},
$$

hence

$$
0\le
e^{-t}t^{x-1} -
\left(1-\frac{t}{m}\right)^m t^{x-1}
\le
t^{x+1}e^{-t/m}.
$$

Since $x>0$, the integral $\int_0^\infty e^{-t}t^{x-1},dt$ converges. Also,

$$
\int_0^m t^{x+1}e^{-t/m}\,dt =
m^{x+2}\int_0^1 u^{x+1}e^{-u}\,du
\to 0
$$

after division by $m^{x+2}$ and because $e^{-u}$ is bounded on $[0,1]$. Therefore the difference between

$$
\int_0^m e^{-t}t^{x-1}\,dt
\quad\text{and}\quad
\Gamma_m(x)
$$

tends to $0$ as $m\to\infty$. Since

$$
\lim_{m\to\infty}
\int_0^m e^{-t}t^{x-1}\,dt =
\int_0^\infty e^{-t}t^{x-1}\,dt,
$$

we obtain

$$
\Gamma(x) =
\int_0^\infty e^{-t}t^{x-1}\,dt,
\qquad x>0.
$$

This completes the proof.

∎
