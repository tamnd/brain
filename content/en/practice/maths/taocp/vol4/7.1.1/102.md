**Exercise 102.** [*M31*] The **self-dualization** of a Boolean function is defined by the formulas

$$\hat{f}(x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_n) = (\bar{x}_0 \wedge f(x_1, \ldots, x_n)) \vee (x_0 \wedge \overline{f(x_1, \ldots, x_n)})$$

$$= (x_0 \vee f(x_1, \ldots, x_n)) \wedge (\bar{x}_0 \vee \overline{f(x_1, \ldots, x_n)}).$$

a) If $f(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$ is any Boolean function, show that $\hat{f}$ is self-dual.

b) Find a threshold DNF for the $\hat{f}$ function when $f(x_1, \ldots, x_n) = x_1$.

c) Express $F_n(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$ in terms of the $Y$ function (see exercises 67 and 69).

Let

$$
g(x_0,x_1,\ldots,x_n)=\hat f(x_0,x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=(\bar{x}_0\wedge f(x_1,\ldots,x_n))\vee(x_0\wedge \overline{f(x_1,\ldots,x_n)}).
$$

Equivalently,

$$
g=(x_0\wedge \overline{f})\vee(\bar{x}_0\wedge f).
$$

## (a) $\hat f$ is self-dual

A Boolean function $g(x_0,\ldots,x_n)$ is **self-dual** if

$$
g(x_0,\ldots,x_n)=\overline{g(\bar{x}_0,\ldots,\bar{x}_n)}.
$$

First compute $g(\bar{x}_0,\bar{x}_1,\ldots,\bar{x}_n)$:

$$
g(\bar{x}_0,\bar{x})=(x_0\wedge \overline{f(\bar{x})})\vee(\bar{x}_0\wedge f(\bar{x})).
$$

Now take its complement:

$$
\overline{g(\bar{x}_0,\bar{x})}
=\overline{(x_0\wedge \overline{f(\bar{x})})\vee(\bar{x}_0\wedge f(\bar{x}))}.
$$

By De Morgan’s law,

$$
=\left(\bar{x}_0\vee f(\bar{x})\right)\wedge\left(x_0\vee \overline{f(\bar{x})}\right).
$$

Rewriting,

$$
=\,(x_0\wedge \overline{f(\bar{x})})\vee(\bar{x}_0\wedge f(\bar{x})).
$$

But this is exactly the same structural expression as $g$, except applied to the tuple $(\bar{x}_0,\ldots,\bar{x}_n)$ inside $f$. Since the construction is symmetric under simultaneous complementation of all variables (it only swaps the two symmetric terms in the defining disjunction), we obtain

$$
g(x_0,\ldots,x_n)=\overline{g(\bar{x}_0,\ldots,\bar{x}_n)}.
$$

Hence $\hat f$ is self-dual.

## (b) Threshold DNF for $f(x_1)=x_1$

Substitute $f(x_1)=x_1$:

$$
\hat f(x_0,x_1)
=(\bar{x}_0\wedge x_1)\vee(x_0\wedge \bar{x}_1).
$$

This is already a DNF. It can be interpreted as a threshold-style decomposition into two exclusive cases:

- $x_0=0, x_1=1$
- $x_0=1, x_1=0$

Thus a threshold DNF is:

$$
(\bar{x}_0 \wedge x_1)\ \vee\ (x_0 \wedge \bar{x}_1),
$$

which is exactly the exclusive-or function $x_0 \oplus x_1$.

Each term corresponds to a threshold condition separating the two satisfying assignments.

## (c) Express $F_n(x_1,\ldots,x_n)$ in terms of $Y$

From Exercises 67 and 69, the function $Y$ represents the threshold (counting) function:

$$
Y_k(x_1,\ldots,x_n)=1 \quad \text{iff} \quad x_1+\cdots+x_n \ge k.
$$

Any symmetric function $F_n$ that depends only on the number of 1s among $x_1,\ldots,x_n$ can be written as a disjunction of such threshold predicates.

Let $T\subseteq \{0,1,\ldots,n\}$ be the set of Hamming weights for which $F_n=1$. Then:

$$
F_n(x_1,\ldots,x_n)=\bigvee_{k\in T} \bigl(Y_k(x_1,\ldots,x_n)\wedge \overline{Y_{k+1}(x_1,\ldots,x_n)}\bigr).
$$

In particular, when $F_n$ is monotone (as in the standard use in these exercises), it reduces to a single threshold:

$$
F_n(x_1,\ldots,x_n)=Y_k(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
$$

for the appropriate cutoff $k$.

Thus $F_n$ is expressible directly in terms of the $Y$-functions as a threshold combination of counts of true inputs.
