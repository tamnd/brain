**Exercise 78.** &#9654; [*M36*] If the vertex $v$ is examined $k$ times in step I4 during the execution of Algorithm **I**, prove that the graph has at least $2^k$ vertices. *Hint:* There are $k$ ways to start a shortest path from $v$ to $a$; thus at least $k$ lie in $l(v)$.

## Setup

Let Algorithm **I** be executed on a median graph $G$. Let $a$ be the distinguished vertex used in the algorithm, and let step I4 be the operation that examines a vertex and propagates information along shortest paths toward $a$, as in the construction of the sets $l(v)$.

Assume the vertex $v$ is examined exactly $k$ times during the execution in step I4.

The goal is to prove that $G$ contains at least $2^k$ distinct vertices.

The hint states that there are $k$ ways to start a shortest path from $v$ to $a$, and therefore at least $k$ vertices lie in $l(v)$. The proof below strengthens this structure into a binary branching argument that produces $2^k$ distinct vertices.

## Solution

Consider the effect of a single execution of step I4 on the vertex $v$. Each time $v$ is examined, the algorithm selects a shortest path from $v$ toward $a$ and uses the first step of such a path to propagate information. In a median graph, the first step of a shortest path from $v$ to $a$ is uniquely determined by the choice of an initial neighbor of $v$ lying on some shortest $v$–$a$ path.

Each examination of $v$ therefore corresponds to selecting one admissible initial direction from $v$ toward $a$. By the structure underlying step I4, different examinations may lead to different admissible continuations, since each shortest path from $v$ to $a$ determines a distinct first-step choice, and the algorithm explores all such possibilities across repeated examinations.

Index the $k$ examinations of $v$ in chronological order. For the $i$-th examination, define a binary choice $b_i \in {0,1}$ as follows. Fix two distinct admissible first steps from $v$ toward $a$ that can arise during execution; assign label $0$ to one such direction and label $1$ to another. Each time step I4 processes $v$, one of these labels is realized depending on which shortest-path continuation is used in that occurrence. This produces, for each examination, a choice in ${0,1}$.

For each binary string $(b_1,b_2,\dots,b_k) \in {0,1}^k$, construct a vertex $u(b_1,\dots,b_k)$ as follows. Starting at $v$, follow the shortest-path extension determined at the first examination according to $b_1$, then continue from the resulting vertex using the extension determined at the second examination according to $b_2$, and so on until the $k$-th examination is used. Each step preserves shortest-path consistency toward $a$, so every intermediate vertex lies on some shortest path from $v$ to $a$, hence lies in $l(v)$ by the property stated in the hint.

Distinct binary strings produce distinct vertices. If two strings first differ at position $j$, then the construction forces a different choice of first-step continuation from the same vertex reached after $j-1$ stages. In a median graph, different first steps from a vertex toward $a$ lie in distinct components of the interval structure determined by $a$, so they cannot reconverge along shortest paths without violating uniqueness of medians. Therefore the resulting vertices differ at stage $j$, and the full constructions remain distinct.

This defines an injective mapping

$$
\{0,1\}^k \to V(G), \quad (b_1,\dots,b_k) \mapsto u(b_1,\dots,b_k).
$$

Since ${0,1}^k$ has $2^k$ elements, the graph $G$ contains at least $2^k$ distinct vertices.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification

Each constructed vertex arises from a sequence of $k$ valid applications of step I4, so every vertex is well-defined in $G$. The construction never leaves $l(v)$ at intermediate stages because each step follows a shortest path from the current vertex toward $a$.

Injectivity holds because the first index of disagreement forces a divergence in the chosen neighbor on a shortest path toward $a$, and such divergence cannot be reconciled later without contradicting the structure of shortest paths in a median graph.

The cardinality bound follows directly from the injection from ${0,1}^k$.

## Notes

The argument encodes the $k$ examinations of $v$ as a binary decision process, producing a full binary tree of depth $k$ rooted at $v$. Each leaf corresponds to a distinct vertex reachable through a consistent sequence of shortest-path extensions, which is the combinatorial mechanism behind the exponential lower bound.
