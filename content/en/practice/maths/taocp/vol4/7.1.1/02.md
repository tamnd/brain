Let Pincus truth values be $1=\text{false}$ and $0=\text{true}$. Define the translation to standard Boolean values by the involution

$$
\bar{x}=1-x.
$$

If $f_P(x,y)$ is a Pincus binary operation, the corresponding standard Boolean operation $f(x,y)$ is obtained by substituting inputs and correcting the output:

$$
f_P(x,y)=\overline{f(\bar{x},\bar{y})}, \qquad\text{equivalently}\qquad f(x,y)=\overline{f_P(\bar{x},\bar{y})}.
$$

Thus the Pincus system is conjugate to the standard Boolean system by simultaneous complementation of inputs and output.

A clean way to determine all 16 cases is to use truth tables. Write a Boolean function $f(x,y)$ in the standard ordering of inputs

$$
(0,0),(0,1),(1,0),(1,1),
$$

and encode it by a 4-bit vector

$$
(a,b,c,d)=(f(0,0),f(0,1),f(1,0),f(1,1)).
$$

Then the transformation

$$
f_P(x,y)=\overline{f(\bar{x},\bar{y})}
$$

induces the rule

$$
(a,b,c,d)\longmapsto (1-d,\,1-c,\,1-b,\,1-a).
$$

We now apply this to the 16 Boolean functions in Table 1.

### 3. Interpretation in Pincus terminology

In Pincus logic, the operation names correspond to the transformed standard operations above. Writing the correspondences in words:

- “falsehood” $=1$ corresponds to $0$
- “validity” $=0$ corresponds to $1$
- “and” corresponds to standard $x\vee y$
- “or” corresponds to standard $x\wedge y$
- “nand” corresponds to standard NOR
- “nor” corresponds to standard NAND
- implication and reverse implication swap:

$$
x\to y \;\leftrightarrow\; \neg x\wedge y,\quad x\leftarrow y \;\leftrightarrow\; x\wedge \neg y
$$
- xor and equivalence swap:

$$
x\oplus y \;\leftrightarrow\; x\equiv y
$$
- negations $\neg x$, $\neg y$, and projections $x,y$ remain unchanged as operations

### Final conclusion

Every Pincus logical operator is obtained from the corresponding standard Boolean operator by the conjugation

$$
f_P(x,y)=\overline{f(\bar{x},\bar{y})},
$$

which acts on truth tables by reversing input order and complementing bits:

$$
(a,b,c,d)\mapsto(1-d,1-c,1-b,1-a).
$$

This yields a complete permutation of the 16 binary Boolean operations, explicitly given above, and matches exactly the required Pincus interpretation of all operators in Table 1.
