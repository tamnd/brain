**Exercise 7.** [*20*] [20] Which operations in Table 1 have the property that $x \circ y = z$ if and only if $y \circ z = x$?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m13s  

---

## Solution

Let $f(x,y)=x\circ y$. The requirement is that for all $x,y,z\in{0,1}$,

$$
f(x,y)=z \;\Longleftrightarrow\; f(y,z)=x.
$$

Fix $y$. For each $x$, there is a unique $z=f(x,y)$. The displayed equivalence implies that if $f(x_1,y)=f(x_2,y)$, then applying the condition with $z=f(x_1,y)$ gives

$$
f(y,z)=x_1 \quad\text{and}\quad f(y,z)=x_2,
$$

hence $x_1=x_2$. Therefore, for each fixed $y$, the map $x\mapsto f(x,y)$ is injective on a two-element set, hence bijective. The same argument with roles permuted shows that for each fixed $y$, the map $z\mapsto x$ determined by $f(y,z)=x$ is also bijective.

Thus each fixed $y$ defines a permutation of ${0,1}$ via $x\mapsto f(x,y)$, and the operation table of $f$ is a Latin square on two symbols.

There are only two Latin squares on ${0,1}$. One is

$$
f(x,y)=x\oplus y,
$$

and the other is its complement

$$
f(x,y)=\overline{x\oplus y}=x\equiv y.
$$

It remains to verify that these satisfy the required equivalence.

For $f(x,y)=x\oplus y$, assume $x\oplus y=z$. Then

$$
y\oplus z = y\oplus (x\oplus y) = x,
$$

using associativity and cancellation in $\oplus$. Conversely, if $y\oplus z=x$, then

$$
x\oplus y = (y\oplus z)\oplus y = z.
$$

Hence $x\oplus y=z$ if and only if $y\oplus z=x$.

For $f(x,y)=x\equiv y$, use $x\equiv y=\overline{x\oplus y}$. If $x\equiv y=z$, then $z=\overline{x\oplus y}$, so

$$
y\equiv z = \overline{y\oplus \overline{x\oplus y}} = x,
$$

and conversely the same symmetry holds since complementing all three values preserves the equivalence structure.

No other operation in Table 1 yields a Latin square on ${0,1}$; all remaining operations fail injectivity in at least one argument, so they cannot satisfy the stated bidirectional determination of $x$ from $(y,z)$.

Therefore the operations are exactly equivalence and exclusive-or.

$$
\boxed{\;\oplus,\ \equiv\;}
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
