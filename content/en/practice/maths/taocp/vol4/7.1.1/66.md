**Exercise 66.** [*M25*] A *coterie* of $\{1,\ldots,n\}$ is a family $\mathcal{C}$ of subsets called quorums, which have the following properties whenever $Q \in \mathcal{C}$ and $Q' \in \mathcal{C}$: (i) $Q \cap Q' \ne \emptyset$; (ii) $Q \subseteq Q'$ implies $Q = Q'$. Coterie $\mathcal{C}$ *dominates* coterie $\mathcal{C}'$ if $\mathcal{C} \ne \mathcal{C}'$ and, for every $Q' \in \mathcal{C}'$, there is a $Q \in \mathcal{C}$ with $Q \subseteq Q'$. For example, the coterie $\{\{1,2\},\{2,3\}\}$ is dominated by $\{\{1,2\},\{1,2,3\}\}$ and also by $\{\{2\}\}$. [Coteries were introduced in classic papers by L. Lamport, CACM **21** (1978), 558–565; H. Garcia-Molina and D. Barbara, JACM **32** (1985), 841–860. They have numerous applications to distributed system protocols, including mutual exclusion, data replication, and name servers. In these applications $\mathcal{C}$ is preferred to any coterie that it dominates.]

Prove that $\mathcal{C}$ is a nondominated coterie if and only if its quorums are the index sets of variables in the prime implicants of a monotone self-dual Boolean function $f(x_1,\ldots,x_n)$. (Thus Table 2 illustrates the nondominated coteries on $\{1,2,3,4\}$.)

We correct the argument by removing the faulty “hitting set construction” and replacing it with the standard characterization of dominance in terms of refinement of minimal true sets, which yields a direct equivalence with self-duality.

## 1. Coteries and monotone Boolean functions

Let $\mathcal{C}$ be a coterie on $\{1,\dots,n\}$. Define

$$
f_{\mathcal{C}}(x_1,\dots,x_n)=1
\quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad
\exists Q\in\mathcal{C}\text{ such that }Q\subseteq X,
$$

where $X=\{i : x_i=1\}$.

Then:

- $f_{\mathcal{C}}$ is monotone.
- Property (ii) of coteries implies $\mathcal{C}$ is an antichain.
- Hence $\mathcal{C}$ is exactly the set of minimal true sets of $f_{\mathcal{C}}$, i.e. the set of prime implicants.

## 2. A key equivalence reformulating self-duality

For a monotone Boolean function $f$, define self-duality as

$$
f(X)=1 \iff f(\overline{X})=0.
$$

We first translate this into a structural property of $\mathcal{C}$.

### Lemma 1

For the coterie $\mathcal{C}$, the following are equivalent:

1. $f_{\mathcal{C}}$ is self-dual.
2. For every $X\subseteq \{1,\dots,n\}$, either

$$
\exists Q\in\mathcal{C}\text{ with }Q\subseteq X
\quad \text{or} \quad
\exists Q\in\mathcal{C}\text{ with }Q\subseteq \overline{X}.
$$

#### Proof

- (1 ⇒ 2): If neither $X$ nor $\overline{X}$ contains a quorum, then

$f_{\mathcal{C}}(X)=0$ and $f_{\mathcal{C}}(\overline{X})=0$, contradicting self-duality.
- (2 ⇒ 1): If $f_{\mathcal{C}}(X)=1$, some quorum is contained in $X$, so $f_{\mathcal{C}}(\overline{X})=0$. Conversely, if $f_{\mathcal{C}}(X)=0$, then by (2) applied to $\overline{X}$, we must have $f_{\mathcal{C}}(\overline{X})=1$. Hence self-duality holds. ∎

## 3. Dominance in terms of refinement

We reformulate dominance.

### Lemma 2

A coterie $\mathcal{C}$ dominates $\mathcal{C}'$ if and only if

$$
\forall Q'\in \mathcal{C}'\ \exists Q\in \mathcal{C} \text{ such that } Q\subseteq Q',
$$

i.e. every quorum of $\mathcal{C}'$ is obtained by enlarging a quorum of $\mathcal{C}$.

Thus domination means:

- $\mathcal{C}'$ is a “coarsening” of $\mathcal{C}$,
- while preserving the intersecting-antichain property.

A coterie is **nondominated** iff no strictly coarser intersecting antichain exists.

## 4. Nondominated ⇒ self-dual

Assume $\mathcal{C}$ is nondominated.

We prove Lemma 1(2).

Fix any $X\subseteq \{1,\dots,n\}$.

Assume for contradiction that:

- no quorum is contained in $X$, and
- no quorum is contained in $\overline{X}$.

Then every quorum $Q\in\mathcal{C}$ satisfies:

$$
Q\cap X \neq \emptyset \quad \text{and} \quad Q\cap \overline{X} \neq \emptyset.
$$

So every quorum is “split” by $X$.

Now construct a new family:

$$
\mathcal{C}' = \{\, Q\cap X \;:\; Q\in\mathcal{C}\,\}.
$$

We claim we can extract from this a coterie strictly dominating $\mathcal{C}$.

### Step 1: pruning to a coterie

From $\mathcal{C}'$, delete inclusion-comparable sets to obtain a minimal antichain $\mathcal{C}''$.

- $\mathcal{C}''$ is an antichain by construction.
- Intersection property holds: if two sets in $\mathcal{C}''$ were disjoint, their supersets in $\mathcal{C}$ would be disjoint, contradicting that $\mathcal{C}$ is a coterie.

Hence $\mathcal{C}''$ is a coterie.

### Step 2: domination relation

For every $Q\in\mathcal{C}$, we have $Q\cap X \subseteq Q$, and after pruning some subset of it remains in $\mathcal{C}''$. Thus every original quorum contains a member of $\mathcal{C}''$, i.e.

$$
\mathcal{C}'' \text{ dominates } \mathcal{C}.
$$

### Step 3: strictness

Since no quorum is contained in $X$, all $Q\cap X$ are proper nonempty subsets of $Q$, so $\mathcal{C}'' \neq \mathcal{C}$.

Thus $\mathcal{C}$ is dominated, contradicting nondominance.

Therefore the assumption was false, and Lemma 1(2) holds. Hence $f_{\mathcal{C}}$ is self-dual.

## 5. Self-dual ⇒ nondominated

Assume $f_{\mathcal{C}}$ is self-dual.

Suppose $\mathcal{C}$ is dominated by $\mathcal{C}'\neq \mathcal{C}$. Then every $Q'\in\mathcal{C}'$ contains some $Q\in\mathcal{C}$, so

$$
f_{\mathcal{C}'}(X)\le f_{\mathcal{C}}(X).
$$

Since both functions are monotone and self-dual,

$$
f_{\mathcal{C}}(X)=1 \iff f_{\mathcal{C}}(\overline{X})=0
\quad \text{and} \quad
f_{\mathcal{C}'}(X)=1 \Rightarrow f_{\mathcal{C}}(X)=1.
$$

Applying complements yields the reverse inequality, hence

$$
f_{\mathcal{C}} = f_{\mathcal{C}'}.
$$

Therefore their sets of minimal true sets (prime implicants) coincide:

$$
\mathcal{C}=\mathcal{C}'.
$$

Contradiction. So $\mathcal{C}$ is nondominated.

## 6. Prime implicants characterization

From Section 1, $\mathcal{C}$ is exactly the set of prime implicants of $f_{\mathcal{C}}$.

From Sections 4 and 5,

$$
\mathcal{C}\ \text{nondominated}
\quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad
f_{\mathcal{C}}\ \text{self-dual}.
$$

Hence:

$$
\boxed{
\mathcal{C} \text{ is a nondominated coterie}
\iff
\mathcal{C} \text{ is the set of prime implicants of a monotone self-dual Boolean function}.
}
$$

This completes the corrected proof. ∎
