**Exercise 80.** [*27*] A *partial cube* is an "isometric" subgraph of a hypercube, a subgraph in which the distance between two vertices are the same as they are in the full graph. The vertices of a partial cube can therefore be labeled in such a way that the distance from $u$ to $v$ is the "Hamming distance" between $l(u)$ and $l(v)$, namely $\nu(l(u) \oplus l(v))$. Algorithm **I** shows that every median graph is a partial cube.

a) Find an induced subgraph of the 4-cube that isn't a partial cube.
b) Give an example of a partial cube that isn't a median graph.

### (a) An induced subgraph of $Q_4$ that is not a partial cube

Let

$$
V=\{0000,\,0011,\,1100,\,1111\}\subseteq \{0,1\}^4,
$$

and let $G$ be the induced subgraph of the 4-cube $Q_4$ on $V$.

Two vertices in $V$ are adjacent in $Q_4$ only if they differ in exactly one coordinate. Every pair in $V$ differs in either 2 or 4 coordinates, so $G$ has no edges. Hence $G$ is disconnected.

In $Q_4$, the Hamming distance between any two distinct vertices in $V$ is finite (either 2 or 4). In $G$, there is no path between distinct vertices, so their graph distance is not finite. Therefore there is no labeling $l$ for which

$$
d_G(u,v)=\nu(l(u)\oplus l(v))
$$

can hold for all pairs $u,v$, since the right-hand side is always finite while the left-hand side is undefined for disconnected pairs.

Thus $G$ is not isometric in any hypercube and is not a partial cube.

### (b) A partial cube that is not a median graph

Consider the induced subgraph $G$ of $Q_3$ on

$$
V=\{001,011,010,110,100,101\}.
$$

In cyclic order this is

$$
001-011-010-110-100-101-001,
$$

so $G\cong C_6$.

We first prove that $G$ is a partial cube by showing it is isometric in $Q_3$.

#### Step 1: Hamming distances in $Q_3$

Let

$$
a=001,\; b=011,\; c=010,\; d=110,\; e=100,\; f=101.
$$

Compute all Hamming distances:

From $a$:

$$
d(a,b)=1,\; d(a,c)=2,\; d(a,d)=3,\; d(a,e)=2,\; d(a,f)=1.
$$

From $b$:

$$
d(b,c)=1,\; d(b,d)=2,\; d(b,e)=3,\; d(b,f)=2.
$$

From $c$:

$$
d(c,d)=1,\; d(c,e)=2,\; d(c,f)=3.
$$

From $d$:

$$
d(d,e)=1,\; d(d,f)=2.
$$

From $e$:

$$
d(e,f)=1.
$$

#### Step 2: Graph distances in $C_6$

In the cycle $a-b-c-d-e-f-a$,

$$
d_G(x,y)=\min(k,6-k)
$$

where $k$ is the cyclic distance.

Thus:

- adjacent vertices have distance $1$,
- vertices two steps apart have distance $2$,
- opposite vertices have distance $3$.

So the cycle distances are:

- distance $1$: $(a,b),(b,c),(c,d),(d,e),(e,f),(f,a)$,
- distance $2$: $(a,c),(b,d),(c,e),(d,f),(e,a),(f,b)$,
- distance $3$: $(a,d),(b,e),(c,f)$.

#### Step 3: Comparison

Comparing with the Hamming distances computed above:

- All cycle-adjacent pairs have Hamming distance $1$.
- All distance-2 pairs in the cycle have Hamming distance $2$:

$$
d(a,c)=d(b,d)=d(c,e)=d(d,f)=d(e,a)=d(f,b)=2.
$$
- All opposite pairs have Hamming distance $3$:

$$
d(a,d)=d(b,e)=d(c,f)=3.
$$

Therefore for all $u,v\in V$,

$$
d_G(u,v)=d_{Q_3}(u,v)=\nu(u\oplus v).
$$

Hence the identity labeling inherited from $Q_3$ is an isometric embedding, so $G$ is a partial cube.

#### Step 4: $G$ is not a median graph

Take

$$
x=001,\quad y=010,\quad z=100.
$$

Then

$$
d(x,y)=d(y,z)=d(z,x)=2.
$$

A median $m$ would satisfy simultaneously:

$$
d(x,y)=d(x,m)+d(m,y),\quad
d(y,z)=d(y,m)+d(m,z),\quad
d(z,x)=d(z,m)+d(m,x).
$$

We check all vertices $m\in V$:

- $m=x$: $d(y,m)+d(m,z)=2+2=4\neq 2$.
- $m=y$: $d(x,m)+d(m,z)=2+2=4\neq 2$.
- $m=z$: $d(x,m)+d(m,y)=2+2=4\neq 2$.
- $m=a,b,c,d,e,f$ similarly each violates at least one equality since each vertex has distance pattern incompatible with lying simultaneously on all three geodesics.

Thus no vertex is a median of $(x,y,z)$, so $G$ is not a median graph.

### Conclusion

- (a) The induced subgraph on $\{0000,0011,1100,1111\}$ is disconnected, hence cannot be isometric in a hypercube and is not a partial cube.
- (b) The induced 6-cycle $C_6$ in $Q_3$ is isometric in $Q_3$, hence a partial cube, but it is not a median graph since the triple $(001,010,100)$ has no median.
