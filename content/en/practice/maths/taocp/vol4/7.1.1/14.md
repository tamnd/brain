**Exercise 14.** [*20*] [20] The probability function $F(p_1, \ldots, p_n)$ in exercise 13 is often called the *availability* of the system. Find the self-dual function (19) of maximum availability when the probabilities $(p_1, p_2, p_3)$ are (a) $(.9, .8, .7)$; (b) $(.8, .6, .4)$; (c) $(.8, .6, .1)$.

## Solution

Let $f(x_1,x_2,x_3)$ be a self-dual Boolean function, so for every $x \in {0,1}^3$,

$$
f(x) = 1 - f(\bar{x}),
$$

where $\bar{x}$ is the bitwise complement of $x$. The eight inputs split into four complementary pairs:

$$
(000,111),\ (001,110),\ (010,101),\ (011,100).
$$

In each pair, exactly one input must be assigned value $1$.

With independent component probabilities $p_1,p_2,p_3$, the probability that the system outputs $1$ is

$$
F = \sum_{x \in \{0,1\}^3} f(x)\, P(x),
$$

where

$$
P(x_1,x_2,x_3)=p_1^{x_1}(1-p_1)^{1-x_1}p_2^{x_2}(1-p_2)^{1-x_2}p_3^{x_3}(1-p_3)^{1-x_3}.
$$

Within each complementary pair ${x,\bar{x}}$, the constraint forces exactly one of $f(x),f(\bar{x})$ to equal $1$. The contribution from the pair is therefore either $P(x)$ or $P(\bar{x})$. Maximizing $F$ reduces to selecting, in each pair, the element with larger probability weight.

### (a) $(p_1,p_2,p_3)=(0.9,0.8,0.7)$

Compute probabilities:

$$
P(000)=0.1\cdot 0.2\cdot 0.3=0.006,\quad P(111)=0.9\cdot 0.8\cdot 0.7=0.504,
$$

so $f(111)=1$.

$$
P(001)=0.1\cdot 0.2\cdot 0.7=0.014,\quad P(110)=0.9\cdot 0.8\cdot 0.3=0.216,
$$

so $f(110)=1$.

$$
P(010)=0.1\cdot 0.8\cdot 0.3=0.024,\quad P(101)=0.9\cdot 0.2\cdot 0.7=0.126,
$$

so $f(101)=1$.

$$
P(011)=0.1\cdot 0.8\cdot 0.7=0.056,\quad P(100)=0.9\cdot 0.2\cdot 0.3=0.054,
$$

so $f(011)=1$.

The maximizing self-dual function satisfies

$$
f(x)=1 \text{ on } \{111,110,101,011\}.
$$

### (b) $(p_1,p_2,p_3)=(0.8,0.6,0.4)$

$$
P(000)=0.2\cdot 0.4\cdot 0.6=0.048,\quad P(111)=0.8\cdot 0.6\cdot 0.4=0.192,
$$

so $f(111)=1$.

$$
P(001)=0.2\cdot 0.4\cdot 0.4=0.032,\quad P(110)=0.8\cdot 0.6\cdot 0.6=0.288,
$$

so $f(110)=1$.

$$
P(010)=0.2\cdot 0.6\cdot 0.6=0.072,\quad P(101)=0.8\cdot 0.4\cdot 0.4=0.128,
$$

so $f(101)=1$.

$$
P(011)=0.2\cdot 0.6\cdot 0.4=0.048,\quad P(100)=0.8\cdot 0.4\cdot 0.6=0.192,
$$

so $f(100)=1$.

Thus

$$
f(x)=1 \text{ on } \{111,110,101,100\}.
$$

### (c) $(p_1,p_2,p_3)=(0.8,0.6,0.1)$

$$
P(000)=0.2\cdot 0.4\cdot 0.9=0.072,\quad P(111)=0.8\cdot 0.6\cdot 0.1=0.048,
$$

so $f(000)=1$.

$$
P(001)=0.2\cdot 0.4\cdot 0.1=0.008,\quad P(110)=0.8\cdot 0.6\cdot 0.9=0.432,
$$

so $f(110)=1$.

$$
P(010)=0.2\cdot 0.6\cdot 0.9=0.108,\quad P(101)=0.8\cdot 0.4\cdot 0.1=0.032,
$$

so $f(010)=1$.

$$
P(011)=0.2\cdot 0.6\cdot 0.1=0.012,\quad P(100)=0.8\cdot 0.4\cdot 0.9=0.288,
$$

so $f(100)=1$.

Hence

$$
f(x)=1 \text{ on } \{000,110,010,100\}.
$$

In each case, every complementary pair contributes its larger probability mass, and any deviation replaces a selected term by a strictly smaller one within that pair, decreasing $F$. This completes the proof. ∎
