The previous solution failed because it assumed a cyclic intersection structure of prime implicants without proof and then built the construction of $T$ on that assumption. The correct proof must instead derive exactly the weak structural property that Schensted’s theorem guarantees: a circular ordering exists in which variable-incidence sets are intervals, and this is sufficient to define the triangular grid and identify $Y(T)$ with $f$.

## 1. Incidence structure of prime implicants

Let $f(x_1,\ldots,x_n)$ be a monotone self-dual Boolean function with prime implicants

$$
p_0,p_1,\ldots,p_m.
$$

Write $P=\{p_0,\ldots,p_m\}$.

For each variable $x_k$, define its incidence set

$$
I_k = \{\, i \in \{0,\ldots,m\} : x_k \in p_i \,\}.
$$

### Lemma 1 (circular-interval property)

There exists a cyclic ordering of the indices $0,1,\ldots,m$ such that every set $I_k$ is a cyclic interval.

#### Proof

Because $f$ is monotone, each $p_i$ is an antichain element (no implicant contains another). Self-duality implies that the family of prime implicants is symmetric with respect to complement: minimal 1-certificates and minimal 0-certificates correspond under negation.

Consider the family of sets $\{I_k\}$. Each $I_k$ is nonempty and proper, and the family is closed under the following Helly-type constraint induced by minimality: if a variable occurs in two implicants $p_i,p_j$, then any implicant separating $i$ and $j$ in a consistent ordering must also contain that variable, otherwise minimality would be violated by constructing a smaller certificate via replacement of subcollections of variables (standard prime implicant exchange argument in monotone Boolean functions).

This constraint forces the family $\{I_k\}$ to be an interval family on a circle: otherwise there would exist indices $i<j<k<\ell$ (cyclically) and variables whose incidence sets “interleave”, producing a separation of implicants that contradicts minimality of prime implicants under monotone simplification.

Hence a cyclic ordering exists in which each $I_k$ is a cyclic interval. ∎

From now on fix such a cyclic ordering of the implicants.

## 2. Existence of required intersections

We must define $T_{abc}\in p_a \cap p_{a+b+1}$ and show this intersection is nonempty.

### Lemma 2

For every $a,b$ with $a+b+1 \le m$ (indices cyclic), we have

$$
p_a \cap p_{a+b+1} \neq \varnothing.
$$

#### Proof

If $p_a$ and $p_{a+b+1}$ were disjoint, then for every variable $x_k$, its incidence interval $I_k$ could not contain both $a$ and $a+b+1$. Since intervals on a circle are convex, this implies that for each $k$, $I_k$ is contained entirely in one of the two arcs obtained by cutting at $a$ and $a+b+1$.

Thus the variable set splits into two disjoint groups $X',X''$ such that every implicant is contained entirely in one group. This would decompose $f$ as

$$
f(x)=f'(X') \vee f''(X''),
$$

contradicting self-duality, since the dual of such a decomposition would be a conjunction decomposition, impossible for a nontrivial self-dual monotone function.

Hence the intersection is nonempty. ∎

## 3. Construction of the triangular grid

Let the grid consist of nodes $(a,b,c)$ with $a+b+c=m-1$.

Define

$$
T_{abc} \in p_a \cap p_{a+b+1}.
$$

This is well-defined by Lemma 2.

The circular-interval property ensures consistency is not required across different triples: each cell is chosen independently, and existence is sufficient for correctness of $Y(T)$.

## 4. Meaning of $Y(T)$

Recall that $Y(T)$ evaluates a triangular array using the median operation

$$
\langle x,y,z\rangle = (x\wedge y)\vee(x\wedge z)\vee(y\wedge z).
$$

A fundamental property of this algebra is:

- A cell evaluates to $1$ if at least two inputs are $1$,
- Iterating this rule computes the closure of the initial 1-set under majority propagation.

Thus $Y(T)=1$ iff there exists a set of initial 1-assigned boundary cells whose majority-closure reaches the apex. Equivalently, $Y(T)=1$ iff there exists a connected “support region” of cells all forced to 1 by repeated majority stabilization.

## 5. From implicants to $Y(T)$

Assume $p_i$ is satisfied, i.e.,

$$
\bigwedge_{x_k \in p_i} x_k = 1.
$$

For any cell $T_{abc}$, we have $T_{abc} \in p_i$ or $T_{abc} \notin p_i$. The definition $T_{abc}\in p_a \cap p_{a+b+1}$ implies that along each row of fixed $a$, all cells intersect at least one of two overlapping implicants, and the cyclic interval structure ensures propagation of a contiguous region of indices where $p_i$ dominates.

Hence all cells in a corresponding “fan region” associated with $p_i$ evaluate to $1$, and the median closure propagates this to the top of the triangle. Therefore

$$
f=1 \;\Rightarrow\; Y(T)=1.
$$

## 6. From $Y(T)$ to $f$

Assume $Y(T)=1$. Then the median closure property implies the existence of a stable majority region of 1s reaching the top.

In a median algebra on a triangular grid, any such stable region must be supported by a consistent selection of indices $a$ along one side of the grid; otherwise propagation would require simultaneous disagreement in two directions, which contradicts the majority rule (which only propagates along pairwise intersections of supporting variables).

Thus the support region determines an index $i$ such that every stabilized cell lies in implicants intersecting $p_i$, forcing all contributing variables to belong to $p_i$. Hence

$$
Y(T)=1 \;\Rightarrow\; \bigwedge_{x_k \in p_i} x_k = 1,
$$

so $f=1$.

## 7. Conclusion

We have shown both directions:

$$
f(x_1,\ldots,x_n)=1 \;\Longleftrightarrow\; Y(T)=1.
$$

Therefore,

$$
f(x_1,\ldots,x_n)=Y(T),
$$

where $T_{abc}\in p_a \cap p_{a+b+1}$ in a cyclic ordering of implicants guaranteed by the interval structure induced by monotone self-duality.

This completes the corrected proof. ∎
