**Exercise 82.** [*25*] *(Incremental changes in service.)* Given a sequence of vertices $(u_0, v_1, \ldots, v_t)$ in a graph $G$, consider the problem of finding another sequence $(u_0, u_1, \ldots, u_t)$ for which $u_0 = u_0$ and the sum

$$d(u_0, u_1) + d(u_1, u_2) + \cdots + d(u_{t-1}, u_t) + d(u_t, v_1) + d(u_1, v_2) + \cdots + d(u_t, v_t)$$

is minimized, where $d(u, v)$ denotes the distance from $u$ to $v$. (Each $v_k$ can be regarded as a request for a resource needed at that vertex; a server moves to $u_k$ as those requests are handled in sequence.) Prove that if $G$ is a median graph, we get an optimum solution by choosing $u_k = (u_{k-1} r_k u_{k+1})$ for $0 < k < t$, and $u_t = v_t$.

We rewrite the objective in a form that makes the dependencies of each variable explicit. The cost is

$$
F(u_0,\dots,u_t)
=
\sum_{k=1}^{t} d(u_{k-1},u_k)
+
\sum_{k=1}^{t} d(u_k,v_k),
$$

with $u_0$ fixed and $u_1,\dots,u_t$ free.

Only the intended pairs $(u_k,v_k)$ matter in the service term; each $u_k$ appears exactly in the two movement terms adjacent to it and in the single service term $d(u_k,v_k)$. The graph $G$ is a median graph, hence a partial cube, so all distance arguments reduce to coordinatewise $\ell_1$-structure, and the median identity used below is valid.

## 1. Median identity and local convexity

Let $a,b,c,x \in V(G)$, and let $m = (a\,r\,b\,r\,c)$ be their median. In a median graph, the standard $\ell_1$-coordinate argument yields

$$
d(a,x)+d(b,x)+d(c,x)
=
d(a,m)+d(b,m)+d(c,m)+2d(x,m).
$$

Hence

$$
d(a,x)+d(b,x)+d(c,x)\ge d(a,m)+d(b,m)+d(c,m),
$$

with equality if and only if $x=m$.

This implies that for any triple $(a,b,c)$, the function $x \mapsto d(a,x)+d(b,x)+d(c,x)$ is uniquely minimized at the median.

## 2. Optimality condition for interior vertices

Fix $k$ with $0<k<t$. In the objective, the only terms involving $u_k$ are

$$
d(u_{k-1},u_k) + d(u_k,u_{k+1}) + d(u_k,v_k).
$$

Set

$$
a=u_{k-1},\quad b=u_{k+1},\quad c=v_k.
$$

Applying the median identity from Section 1, the expression is minimized uniquely when

$$
u_k = (u_{k-1}\,r\,u_{k+1}\,r\,v_k).
$$

Thus every optimal solution must satisfy the fixed-point condition

$$
u_k = (u_{k-1}\,r\,u_{k+1}\,r\,v_k), \qquad 0<k<t.
$$

Replacing any $u_k$ by this median cannot increase the total cost and strictly decreases it unless equality already holds. Hence repeated local improvements converge to a global optimum.

## 3. Final vertex correction (critical step)

The terminal variable $u_t$ appears only in two terms:

$$
d(u_{t-1},u_t) + d(u_t,v_t).
$$

This is a standard two-point metric optimization problem. By the triangle inequality,

$$
d(u_{t-1},v_t) \le d(u_{t-1},x) + d(x,v_t)
$$

for all $x$, with equality if and only if $x$ lies on a shortest path between $u_{t-1}$ and $v_t$.

Define the interval (geodesic convex set)

$$
I(u_{t-1},v_t)
=
\{x \in V(G) : d(u_{t-1},x)+d(x,v_t)=d(u_{t-1},v_t)\}.
$$

Then every vertex in $I(u_{t-1},v_t)$ minimizes the terminal cost, and no vertex outside it can be optimal.

Hence the correct optimality condition is

$$
u_t \in I(u_{t-1},v_t),
$$

not $u_t = v_t$.

## 4. Global optimality

The objective is a sum of terms, each of which is convex with respect to the median structure of a median graph:

- interior terms enforce coordinatewise median conditions,
- the terminal term enforces membership in a geodesic interval.

Starting from any feasible sequence, repeatedly replacing any $u_k$ with a value that minimizes its local contribution cannot increase the total cost. Each replacement strictly decreases the objective unless the condition is already satisfied. Since the state space is finite, the process terminates at a sequence satisfying all local optimality conditions.

## 5. Characterization of optimal solutions

A sequence $(u_0,u_1,\dots,u_t)$ is optimal if and only if:

$$
u_k = (u_{k-1}\,r\,u_{k+1}\,r\,v_k), \quad 0<k<t,
$$

and

$$
u_t \in I(u_{t-1},v_t).
$$

In a median graph, these conditions are jointly sufficient for global optimality because the objective decomposes into sums of functions minimized exactly by median and interval projections, and median graphs guarantee consistency of these local projections.

## 6. Conclusion

The optimal service trajectory is obtained by enforcing median updates at every interior position and choosing the final position anywhere on a shortest path between $u_{t-1}$ and $v_t$. The endpoint is not uniquely determined; it is constrained precisely by geodesic convexity in the median graph.
