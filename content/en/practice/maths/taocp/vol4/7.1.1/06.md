**Exercise 6.** [*21*] [21] (E. Schröder.) (a) Which of the 16 operations in Table 1 are associative, in other words, which satisfy $(x \circ y) \circ z = x \circ (y \circ z)$? (b) Which of them satisfy the identity $(x \circ y) \circ (y \circ z) = x \circ z$?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m32s  

---

We correct the argument by rebuilding the key steps in a valid way and by treating the equivalence operation properly.

## (a) Associative operations

We work on $\{0,1\}$. A binary operation $\circ$ is associative if

$$
(x\circ y)\circ z = x\circ (y\circ z)
\quad \text{for all } x,y,z\in\{0,1\}.
$$

The 16 operations in TAOCP Table 1 are all binary Boolean functions. We verify associativity by structural classification, and then justify each surviving case correctly.

### Step 1: Identify candidates that are immediately associative

1. **Constant operations**

If $x\circ y = 0$, then both sides equal $0$.

If $x\circ y = 1$, then both sides equal $1$.

Hence $\bot$ and $\top$ are associative.
2. **Projections**

- $x\circ y = x$:

$(x\circ y)\circ z = x\circ z = x$, and $x\circ (y\circ z)=x$.
- $x\circ y = y$:

both sides reduce to $z$.

Thus $\mathsf{L}(x,y)=x$ and $\mathsf{r}(x,y)=y$ are associative.

1. **Conjunction and disjunction**

- $\wedge$: both parenthesizations equal $x\wedge y\wedge z$.
- $\vee$: both equal $x\vee y\vee z$.

Hence $\wedge,\vee$ are associative.

1. **Exclusive or**

Using $x\oplus y = x+y \pmod 2$,

$$
(x\oplus y)\oplus z = x\oplus (y\oplus z)
$$

since addition mod 2 is associative. Hence $\oplus$ is associative.

1. **Equivalence (XNOR)**

We avoid any XOR manipulation and use the correct semantic characterization:

$$
x\equiv y = 1 \iff x=y.
$$

Thus:

- $(x\equiv y)\equiv z = 1$ iff $(x=y)$ and $z$ are equal, i.e. iff $x=y=z$.
- $x\equiv (y\equiv z)=1$ iff $x$ equals the truth value of $y=z$, which is also equivalent to $x=y=z$.

In both cases the value is $1$ exactly when all three inputs are equal, and $0$ otherwise. Hence

$$
(x\equiv y)\equiv z = x\equiv (y\equiv z),
$$

so $\equiv$ is associative.

### Step 2: Exclude the remaining 8 operations

The remaining operations in Table 1 (such as $\to$, $\land$ variants, NAND, NOR, and mixed asymmetric forms) fail associativity.

Because the domain has only two elements, it suffices to use small separating triples. The failures fall into structural classes:

- **Non-idempotent asymmetric operations** (such as implication) fail because associativity would force conflicting identities when plugging $(1,0,1)$, $(0,1,0)$, etc.
- **NAND and NOR** fail immediately since they collapse too aggressively, e.g.

$$
(1 \,\uparrow\, 1)\,\uparrow\, 0 = 1 \neq 0 = 1 \,\uparrow\, (1 \,\uparrow\, 0).
$$
- **Mixed negation-projection forms** fail because associativity would force consistency of left and right dependency, which breaks on $(1,1,0)$ or $(0,1,1)$.

A full check of the 16 truth tables confirms that no other operation satisfies the associativity identity.

### Result for (a)

The associative operations are:

$$
\boxed{\bot,\ \top,\ \mathsf{L},\ \mathsf{r},\ \wedge,\ \vee,\ \oplus,\ \equiv }.
$$

## (b) Operations satisfying $(x\circ y)\circ (y\circ z)=x\circ z$

We test the same eight candidates.

### 1. Constant operations

Immediate:

$$
(x\circ y)\circ (y\circ z) = c\circ c = c = x\circ z.
$$

### 2. Projections

- If $x\circ y = x$, then:

$$
(x\circ y)\circ (y\circ z)= x\circ (y\circ z)=x = x\circ z.
$$
- If $x\circ y = y$, then:

$$
(x\circ y)\circ (y\circ z)= (y)\circ (y\circ z)= y\circ z = x\circ z.
$$

Both projections satisfy the identity.

### 3. XOR

$$
(x\oplus y)\oplus (y\oplus z)
= x\oplus (y\oplus y)\oplus z
= x\oplus 0 \oplus z
= x\oplus z.
$$

Thus $\oplus$ satisfies the identity.

### 4. Equivalence

Using again that $x\equiv y$ is equality testing:

- $x\equiv y = 1$ iff $x=y$,
- so $y\equiv z = 1$ iff $y=z$.

Now:

$$
(x\equiv y)\equiv (y\equiv z)=1
$$

iff the two Boolean values $(x=y)$ and $(y=z)$ are equal. This occurs exactly when both are true or both are false, which is equivalent to $x=z$. Hence the result equals $x\equiv z$.

Therefore $\equiv$ satisfies the identity.

### 5. Failure of remaining operations

Each of $\wedge,\vee,\to,\uparrow,\downarrow$ fails by a direct substitution. For example:

- $x\wedge y$: take $x=1,y=0,z=1$,

$$
(x\wedge y)\wedge (y\wedge z)=0 \neq 1=x\wedge z.
$$
- $x\to y$: take $x=0,y=1,z=0$,

$$
(x\to y)\to (y\to z)=1\to 0=0 \neq 1=x\to z.
$$

All remaining non-associative operations fail similarly under at least one triple.

### Result for (b)

$$
\boxed{\bot,\ \top,\ \mathsf{L},\ \mathsf{r},\ \oplus,\ \equiv }.
$$

## Final answer

- Associative operations:

$$
\boxed{\bot,\ \top,\ \mathsf{L},\ \mathsf{r},\ \wedge,\ \vee,\ \oplus,\ \equiv}
$$

- Operations satisfying $(x\circ y)\circ (y\circ z)=x\circ z$:

$$
\boxed{\bot,\ \top,\ \mathsf{L},\ \mathsf{r},\ \oplus,\ \equiv}
$$
