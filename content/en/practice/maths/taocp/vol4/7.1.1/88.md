Let $t = |T|$. Let $N_t$ be the CI-net constructed in the proof of Theorem F for the free tree $\langle 63\rangle$, and let the execution time be the length of the longest directed path in the network when every module fires as soon as its inputs are available.

We analyze this delay by using the _actual recursive structure of Theorem F_, not as an external heuristic, but as an explicit decomposition of the construction.

### 1. Decomposition induced by Theorem F

The proof of Theorem F constructs $N_t$ recursively as follows.

At each stage, the underlying tree $T$ is split at a centroid vertex $c$, producing subtrees

$$
T_1, T_2, \dots, T_k, \quad |T_i| \le \frac{t}{2}.
$$

The CI-net for $T$ is then assembled from:

- CI-nets $N_{T_i}$ for each subtree $T_i$,
- a constant-size interface gadget at the centroid $c$,
- wiring that connects each $N_{T_i}$ to $c$.

Iterating this decomposition yields a recursion tree $\mathcal{R}(T)$ of height $O(\log t)$. Each node of $\mathcal{R}(T)$ corresponds to a CI-net module or interface gadget created at a particular recursive call.

### 2. Induced layering of modules

Assign each module in $N_t$ a **level** equal to its depth in the recursion tree $\mathcal{R}(T)$. Then:

- There are $O(\log t)$ levels.
- Each module belongs to exactly one level.
- All edges in the CI-net fall into two types:

1. **Intra-level edges**, connecting modules created within the same recursive stage.
2. **Inter-level edges**, connecting a subtree CI-net $N_{T_i}$ to the centroid gadget at a higher level.

Crucially, by construction of Theorem F, inter-level dependencies always go from deeper levels to shallower levels (information flows from subtrees toward centroids), so the induced dependency graph is acyclic and respects this level structure.

### 3. Bounding the length of a directed path

Let $P$ be any directed path in the execution DAG of $N_t$. Decompose $P$ according to the level structure.

#### (a) Number of level changes

Each time $P$ moves from one level to a higher level, it moves up one step in the recursion tree. Since the recursion tree has height $O(\log t)$, the path $P$ can traverse at most $O(\log t)$ distinct levels.

#### (b) Length of a segment within one level

Fix a level $d$. Consider the subgraph induced by modules created at level $d$. These modules correspond to disjoint pieces of the tree decomposition at that stage.

Although there may be $\Omega(t^2)$ total modules overall, at any fixed level:

- Each module corresponds to a distinct portion of the original tree,
- The total number of modules at level $d$ is $O(t)$,
- Any directed path restricted to level $d$ cannot visit more than $O(t)$ distinct modules, since there are only $O(t)$ of them.

Thus the contribution of any single level to the length of $P$ is $O(t)$.

### 4. Total delay bound

Combining the two observations:

- At most $O(\log t)$ levels are traversed,
- At most $O(t)$ steps occur within any fixed level.

Therefore,

$$
|P| \le O(t) \cdot O(\log t) = O(t \log t).
$$

Since $P$ was arbitrary, the depth (parallel time) of the network is $O(t \log t)$.
