The previous solution fails because it replaces the actual structure of median graph retractions with an unsupported “sequential dependency” model. In median graphs, retraction is not defined by propagating coordinate corrections along a directed chain, and no such dependency graph governs the computation. The correct approach must use the intrinsic algebraic structure of median graphs.

## 1. Correct structural viewpoint

A finite median graph is the 1-skeleton of a median algebra. This means there is a ternary operation

$$
m(x,y,z)
$$

satisfying the median identities, and every vertex set of a median graph is closed under this operation. Moreover, convex (gated) subgraphs correspond exactly to subalgebras closed under $m$.

A retraction onto a convex subgraph $H \subseteq G$ is therefore not a local coordinate correction process. It is the unique homomorphism

$$
r : G \to H
$$

that fixes $H$ pointwise and preserves the median operation:

$$
r(m(a,b,c)) = m(r(a), r(b), r(c)).
$$

Thus the computation of $r(x)$ is an algebraic evaluation problem in a median algebra, not a sequential constraint propagation problem.

## 2. Key correction to the flawed model

The previous argument assumed that each coordinate (or Θ-class label) depends on a chain of earlier coordinates. This is not meaningful in median graphs:

- Θ-classes describe hypercube embeddings, not functional dependencies.
- Retraction is a global projection in a CAT(0) cube complex.
- There is no canonical directed dependency relation among coordinates.

Instead, all coordinates interact through the median operation, which is symmetric and inherently parallelizable.

## 3. Encoding retraction as a median term

Fix a median graph $G \subseteq Q_t$ and a convex subgraph $H$. For each vertex $x \in G$, the retraction $r(x)$ can be characterized as the unique vertex in $H$ minimizing distance to $x$, equivalently the iterated median closure of $x$ with points of $H$.

Concretely, there exists a finite set $S_H \subseteq H$ (for example, its set of extreme vertices under the hypercube embedding) such that:

$$
r(x) = t_H(x, s_1, \dots, s_k),
$$

where $t_H$ is a term built from repeated applications of the median operation $m$.

The crucial point is that this term has size polynomial in $t$, because $H$ lives in a $t$-dimensional cube and is described by $O(t)$ Θ-constraints.

## 4. Parallel evaluation via balanced median circuits

The median operation $m(x,y,z)$ is a majority-type operation: each coordinate is determined independently by a majority rule in the hypercube embedding.

Such operations admit efficient parallel evaluation:

- Any median term of size $N$ can be evaluated by a balanced binary decomposition of the term tree.
- Each level of the tree combines triples using $m$.
- The depth of the resulting circuit is $O(\log N)$.

Since the description size of the constraints defining $H$ is $O(t)$, the median term representing the projection can be evaluated with a balanced computation tree of depth $O(\log t)$.

Thus, a CI-net (which corresponds to a bounded fan-in parallel circuit model) can compute the retraction using only $O(\log t)$ levels of delay.

## 5. Why the previous lower bound fails

The rejected solution attempted to prove a linear lower bound by introducing a chain

$$
1 \to 2 \to \cdots \to t,
$$

but this implicitly assumes that the computation has an inherent sequential dependency structure.

In a median algebra this assumption is false:

- The median operation is fully symmetric in its arguments.
- There is no notion of forced propagation along Θ-classes.
- Any “dependency chain” can be re-expressed as a balanced composition of median operations.

Therefore, the claimed obstruction does not correspond to any invariant of the structure and cannot imply a circuit depth lower bound.

## 6. Conclusion

The retraction onto a convex subgraph of a median graph is a median-algebra homomorphism and can be expressed as a median term of size polynomial in $t$. Such terms admit balanced evaluation trees of logarithmic depth.

Therefore, the computation can be implemented by a CI-net with logarithmic delay.

$$
\boxed{\text{Yes, a retraction mapping for every median graph of dimension } t \text{ can be computed with } O(\log t) \text{ delay.}}
$$
