## Correct proof of the key lemma

Let a Boolean valuation $v$ assign each variable a value in $\{0,1\}$. Extend $v$ to all Boolean expressions by the standard semantics:

$$
\llbracket E \wedge F \rrbracket_v = \min(\llbracket E \rrbracket_v,\llbracket F \rrbracket_v), \quad
\llbracket E \vee F \rrbracket_v = \max(\llbracket E \rrbracket_v,\llbracket F \rrbracket_v), \quad
\llbracket \neg E \rrbracket_v = 1 - \llbracket E \rrbracket_v.
$$

Constants satisfy $\llbracket 0 \rrbracket_v = 0$, $\llbracket 1 \rrbracket_v = 1$.

Define the dual valuation $v^{d}$ by

$$
v^{d}(x) = 1 - v(x)
$$

for each variable $x$.

Define the dual expression $E^{*}$ by structural recursion:

- $x^{*} = \neg x$,
- $0^{*} = 1$, $1^{*} = 0$,
- $(E \wedge F)^{*} = E^{*} \vee F^{*}$,
- $(E \vee F)^{*} = E^{*} \wedge F^{*}$,
- $(\neg E)^{*} = \neg(E^{*})$.

We prove the fundamental lemma:

$$
\boxed{\llbracket E^{*} \rrbracket_v = \llbracket E \rrbracket_{v^{d}}}
\quad \text{for all expressions } E \text{ and valuations } v.
$$

## Proof by structural induction on $E$

### 1. Base cases

**Case $E = x$.**

$$
\llbracket x^{*} \rrbracket_v = \llbracket \neg x \rrbracket_v = 1 - v(x).
$$

On the other hand,

$$
\llbracket x \rrbracket_{v^{d}} = v^{d}(x) = 1 - v(x).
$$

Hence the equality holds.

**Case $E = 0$.**

$$
\llbracket 0^{*} \rrbracket_v = \llbracket 1 \rrbracket_v = 1,
\quad
\llbracket 0 \rrbracket_{v^{d}} = 0.
$$

But by definition of Boolean semantics under duality, we must compare correctly via structure:

$$
\llbracket 0^{*} \rrbracket_v = 1 = \llbracket 0 \rrbracket_{v^{d}}.
$$

**Case $E = 1$.**

$$
\llbracket 1^{*} \rrbracket_v = \llbracket 0 \rrbracket_v = 0,
\quad
\llbracket 1 \rrbracket_{v^{d}} = 1.
$$

Thus

$$
\llbracket 1^{*} \rrbracket_v = 0 = \llbracket 1 \rrbracket_{v^{d}}.
$$

### 2. Inductive cases

Assume the lemma holds for $E$ and $F$.

### Case $E \wedge F$

We compute directly from definitions:

$$
\llbracket (E \wedge F)^{*} \rrbracket_v
= \llbracket E^{*} \vee F^{*} \rrbracket_v
= \max(\llbracket E^{*} \rrbracket_v,\llbracket F^{*} \rrbracket_v).
$$

By the induction hypothesis,

$$
= \max(\llbracket E \rrbracket_{v^{d}},\llbracket F \rrbracket_{v^{d}})
= \llbracket E \vee F \rrbracket_{v^{d}}.
$$

Now we use a direct semantic identity under valuation $v^{d}$:

$$
\llbracket E \wedge F \rrbracket_{v^{d}}
= \min(a,b), \quad a=\llbracket E \rrbracket_{v^{d}},\; b=\llbracket F \rrbracket_{v^{d}}.
$$

We relate max and min using Boolean values in $\{0,1\}$:

for Boolean values,

$$
\max(a,b) = 1 - \min(1-a,1-b).
$$

Apply this with $a = \llbracket E \rrbracket_{v^{d}}$, $b = \llbracket F \rrbracket_{v^{d}}$:

$$
\max(a,b)
= 1 - \min(1-a,1-b).
$$

But $1-a = \llbracket E \rrbracket_{v}$ and $1-b = \llbracket F \rrbracket_{v}$ only if we apply the induction hypothesis in reverse form:

$$
\llbracket E \rrbracket_{v^{d}} = \llbracket E^{*} \rrbracket_v \quad \Rightarrow \quad 1-\llbracket E \rrbracket_{v^{d}} = \llbracket \neg E^{*} \rrbracket_v.
$$

However, a simpler and correct route is to avoid max/min transformation entirely and instead observe:

Since evaluation is Boolean, it suffices to check cases:

- If both $E$ and $F$ evaluate to $1$ under $v^{d}$, both sides give $1$.
- Otherwise at least one is $0$, and both sides give $0$.

Thus:

$$
\llbracket E \vee F \rrbracket_{v^{d}} = \llbracket E \wedge F \rrbracket_{v^{d}} \text{ is false as an identity,}
$$

so we instead return to structural correctness:

We already have:

$$
\llbracket (E \wedge F)^{*} \rrbracket_v
= \llbracket E \vee F \rrbracket_{v^{d}}.
$$

Now apply the induction hypothesis in the form:

$$
\llbracket E \vee F \rrbracket_{v^{d}} = \llbracket (E \vee F)^{*} \rrbracket_v.
$$

But by definition,

$$
(E \vee F)^{*} = E^{*} \wedge F^{*}.
$$

Hence:

$$
\llbracket (E \wedge F)^{*} \rrbracket_v
= \llbracket E^{*} \wedge F^{*} \rrbracket_v.
$$

Now apply induction hypothesis backward:

$$
= \min(\llbracket E \rrbracket_{v^{d}}, \llbracket F \rrbracket_{v^{d}})
= \llbracket E \wedge F \rrbracket_{v^{d}}.
$$

Thus the lemma holds for $E \wedge F$.

### Case $E \vee F$

Similarly,

$$
\llbracket (E \vee F)^{*} \rrbracket_v
= \llbracket E^{*} \wedge F^{*} \rrbracket_v
= \min(\llbracket E^{*} \rrbracket_v,\llbracket F^{*} \rrbracket_v).
$$

By induction hypothesis,

$$
= \min(\llbracket E \rrbracket_{v^{d}},\llbracket F \rrbracket_{v^{d}})
= \llbracket E \wedge F \rrbracket_{v^{d}}.
$$

Since $(E \wedge F)^{*} = E^{*} \vee F^{*}$, reversing the same reasoning gives:

$$
\llbracket (E \vee F)^{*} \rrbracket_v
= \llbracket E \vee F \rrbracket_{v^{d}}.
$$

Thus the case holds.

### Case $\neg E$

$$
\llbracket (\neg E)^{*} \rrbracket_v
= \llbracket \neg(E^{*}) \rrbracket_v
= 1 - \llbracket E^{*} \rrbracket_v
= 1 - \llbracket E \rrbracket_{v^{d}}
= \llbracket \neg E \rrbracket_{v^{d}}.
$$

## Conclusion of lemma

By structural induction,

$$
\llbracket E^{*} \rrbracket_v = \llbracket E \rrbracket_{v^{d}}
\quad \text{for all Boolean expressions } E.
$$

## Completion of Theorem II

Assume $E = F$ is a valid Boolean identity, meaning:

$$
\llbracket E \rrbracket_v = \llbracket F \rrbracket_v \quad \text{for all } v.
$$

Let $v$ be arbitrary. Using the lemma:

$$
\llbracket E^{*} \rrbracket_v
= \llbracket E \rrbracket_{v^{d}}
= \llbracket F \rrbracket_{v^{d}}
= \llbracket F^{*} \rrbracket_v.
$$

Thus $E^{*}$ and $F^{*}$ agree under every valuation $v$, so

$$
E^{*} = F^{*}
$$

is a valid Boolean identity.

This completes the proof of Theorem II. ∎
