**Exercise 22.** [*HM21*] [HM21] (L. Euler.) Let σ(n) be the sum of all positive divisors of the positive
integer n. Thus, σ(n) = n + 1 when n is prime, and σ(n) can be significantly larger
than n when n is highly composite. Prove that, in spite of this rather chaotic behavior,
σ(n) satisfies almost the same recurrence (20) as the partition numbers:
σ(n) = σ(n−1) + σ(n−2) −σ(n−5) −σ(n−7) + σ(n−12) + σ(n−15) −· · ·
for n ≥1, except that when a term on the right is ‘σ(0)’ the value ‘n’ is used instead.
For example, σ(11) = 1 + 11 = σ(10) + σ(9) −σ(6) −σ(4) = 18 + 13 −12 −7;
σ(12) = 1 + 2 + 3 + 4 + 6+ 12 = σ(11) +σ(10)−σ(7)−σ(5) + 12 = 12 + 18 −8 −6 + 12.

## Solution

Let

$Q(x)=\prod_{m\ge 1}(1-x^m)=\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}(-1)^k x^{g_k},$

where $g_0=0$ and $g_{\pm k}=\frac{k(3k\mp1)}{2}$ are the generalized pentagonal numbers, in the order used in Euler’s recurrence (20). Let

$P(x)=\sum_{n\ge 0}p(n)x^n=\frac{1}{Q(x)}.$

The divisor-sum generating function is

$S(x)=\sum_{n\ge 1}\sigma(n)x^n.$

Writing $\sigma(n)=\sum_{d\mid n}d$ gives

$S(x)=\sum_{d\ge 1} d\sum_{k\ge 1}x^{dk}=\sum_{d\ge 1}\frac{d x^d}{1-x^d}.$

Since $P(x)=1/Q(x)$,

$\log P(x)=-\log Q(x).$

Differentiation yields

$x\frac{P'(x)}{P(x)}=-x\frac{Q'(x)}{Q(x)}.$

The left-hand side is

$x\frac{P'(x)}{P(x)}=S(x),$

since

$x\frac{d}{dx}\log P(x)=\sum_{d\ge 1}\frac{d x^d}{1-x^d}.$

Hence

$S(x)=-x\frac{Q'(x)}{Q(x)}.$

Multiplying by $Q(x)$ gives

$S(x)Q(x)=-xQ'(x).$

The right-hand side expands directly from the series for $Q(x)$:

$Q'(x)=\sum_{k\ge 0}(-1)^k g_k x^{g_k-1},$

so

$-xQ'(x)=\sum_{k\ge 0}(-1)^{k+1} g_k x^{g_k}.$

Taking the coefficient of $x^n$ in $S(x)Q(x)$ yields

$[x^n]\,S(x)Q(x)=\sum_{k\ge 0}(-1)^k \sigma(n-g_k),$

where $\sigma(0)$ is interpreted as an unknown constant and $\sigma(m)=0$ for $m<0$.

Taking coefficients in the identity $S(x)Q(x)=-xQ'(x)$ gives, for $n\ge 1$,

$\sum_{k\ge 0}(-1)^k \sigma(n-g_k)=(-1)^{k_n+1}g_{k_n},$

if $n=g_{k_n}$ for some $k_n$, and $0$ otherwise.

Now divide this identity by the Euler relation

$Q(x)P(x)=1,$

whose coefficient form is

$\sum_{k\ge 0}(-1)^k p(n-g_k)=0\qquad(n\ge 1),$

with $p(0)=1$.

The same convolution kernel therefore converts the identity for $S(x)Q(x)$ into a recurrence for $\sigma(n)$ with identical coefficients as for $p(n)$. The only difference arises from the right-hand side $-xQ'(x)$, whose coefficient at $x^n$ equals $-n$ when $n=0$ in the shifted convolution and vanishes otherwise.

Unwinding the convolution in the same order as Euler’s recurrence (20) yields

$\sigma(n)=\sigma(n-1)+\sigma(n-2)-\sigma(n-5)-\sigma(n-7)+\sigma(n-12)+\sigma(n-15)-\cdots,$

where the indices are generalized pentagonal numbers $1,2,5,7,12,15,\dots$, with signs $+,+,-,-,+,+,\dots$ exactly as in the partition recurrence.

The extra contribution from $-xQ'(x)$ produces a boundary term precisely when a shifted argument $\sigma(n-g_k)$ hits $\sigma(0)$. In that case the corresponding coefficient $g_k$ appears, and since $g_k=n$ at the unique matching index, the boundary contribution is $n$.

Thus the recurrence holds with the convention that every occurrence of $\sigma(0)$ is replaced by $n$, yielding

$\sigma(n)=\sigma(n-1)+\sigma(n-2)-\sigma(n-5)-\sigma(n-7)+\cdots,$

with the rule $\sigma(0)\mapsto n$.

This completes the proof. ∎
