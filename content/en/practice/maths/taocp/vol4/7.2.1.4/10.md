## Solution

Represent a partition as a weakly decreasing sequence $a_1 \ge a_2 \ge \cdots \ge a_m \ge 1$, extended by $a_{m+1}=0$. A Ferrers diagram viewpoint identifies the partition with a left-justified array having $a_i$ cells in row $i$. Adding one cell produces a new partition exactly when the result remains weakly decreasing.

### (a) Tree of all partitions of all integers

Define a rooted tree with root $\epsilon$ (the empty partition). From a node $a_1a_2\cdots a_m$, form a child by adding a single cell to row $i$ for any index $i \ge 1$ such that the resulting sequence remains nonincreasing. Formally, for $1 \le i \le m$, row $i$ can be increased to $a_i+1$ exactly when $a_{i-1} > a_i$ (with $a_0=\infty$), and a new row $m+1$ of size $1$ is always admissible since $a_m \ge 1$ implies $a_m \ge 1 \ge 1 = a_{m+1}+1$ after creation.

Each such operation increases the sum $a_1+\cdots+a_m$ by $1$, so every node at depth $n$ is a partition of $n$. Conversely, every partition of $n$ has a unique path to $\epsilon$ by repeatedly deleting one cell from a Ferrers diagram, so every partition appears exactly once. This identifies the tree with Young’s lattice ordered by Ferrers diagram inclusion.

### (b) Tree of all partitions of a fixed integer $n$

The diagram for fixed $n$ is obtained by truncating the infinite tree at level $n$. Nodes are all partitions of integers $k \le n$, with edges still given by addition of a single cell. Leaves at depth $n$ are exactly the partitions of $n$. Every node at depth $k<n$ has children corresponding to all partitions of $k+1$ obtained by adding one cell, and no other nodes are present beyond depth $n$.

Thus this second tree is the induced subgraph consisting of all nodes at depth at most $n$ in the Young lattice.

### Lexicographic order and traversal

Let partitions be compared in lexicographic order on sequences $a_1a_2\cdots$, with higher weight on earlier parts. Among all children of a node $a_1a_2\cdots a_m$, adding a cell to row $1$ produces the lexicographically largest possible first coordinate increase, hence the smallest lexicographic continuation when generating in increasing lexicographic order is obtained by modifying earlier rows first.

More precisely, for two children obtained by adding a cell in rows $i<j$, the child from row $i$ has larger $a_i$ while all earlier parts remain unchanged, so it is lexicographically larger than any child produced by increasing a later row. Therefore, sorting children in increasing order of $i$ yields lexicographically increasing partitions.

A depth-first traversal that always explores children in increasing order of the row index of the added cell visits partitions in lexicographic order of their sequences $a_1a_2\cdots$.

This holds both for the infinite tree and for the truncated tree at level $n$, since lexicographic comparison depends only on the sequence structure and is preserved under restriction to fixed sum.

This completes the proof. ∎
