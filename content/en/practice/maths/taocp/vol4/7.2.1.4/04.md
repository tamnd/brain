**Exercise 4.** [*M22*] [M22] (Gideon Ehrlich, 1974.) What is the lexicographically smallest partition
of n in which all parts are ≥r? For example, when n = 19 and r = 5 the answer is 766.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 34m32s

## Solution

Let a partition of $n$ with all parts $\ge r$ be written as

$a_1 \ge a_2 \ge \cdots \ge a_m \ge r,\qquad a_1+\cdots+a_m=n.$

Lexicographic order compares $a_1$, then $a_2$, and so on. The lexicographically smallest partition is therefore determined by making $a_1$ as small as possible, and subject to that, making later parts as small as possible while preserving feasibility.

Introduce the shift

$a_i = r + b_i,$

with

$b_1 \ge b_2 \ge \cdots \ge b_m \ge 0,\qquad b_1+\cdots+b_m = n-mr.$

Lexicographic order is preserved under this shift, so minimizing $(a_i)$ is equivalent to minimizing $(b_i)$ in lexicographic order.

For fixed $m$, the lexicographically smallest nonincreasing sequence $(b_i)$ with nonnegative entries summing to $S=n-mr$ is the most balanced distribution of $S$ into $m$ parts, in the sense of Exercise 3 from the section. Hence

$b_i \in \{\lfloor S/m \rfloor,\ \lceil S/m \rceil\},$

with all larger values appearing first.

Thus

$a_i \in \{r+\lfloor S/m \rfloor,\ r+\lceil S/m \rceil\}.$

The first part is

$a_1 = r + \left\lceil \frac{S}{m} \right\rceil = r + \left\lceil \frac{n-mr}{m} \right\rceil = \left\lceil \frac{n}{m} \right\rceil.$

To minimize $a_1$, it suffices to maximize $m$ subject to feasibility. Since each part is at least $r$,

$m \le \left\lfloor \frac{n}{r} \right\rfloor.$

Let

$m = \left\lfloor \frac{n}{r} \right\rfloor.$

Write Euclidean division

$n = mq + t,\qquad 0 \le t < m.$

Then

$q = \left\lfloor \frac{n}{m} \right\rfloor = \left\lceil \frac{n}{m} \right\rceil.$

The balanced partition into $m$ parts is therefore

$a_1=\cdots=a_t = q+1,\qquad a_{t+1}=\cdots=a_m = q.$

It remains to verify $q \ge r$. Since $m = \lfloor n/r \rfloor$, we have $m \le n/r$, hence $n/m \ge r$, so $q = \lfloor n/m \rfloor \ge r$.

Thus the partition is valid and lexicographically minimal.

The resulting lexicographically smallest partition of $n$ with all parts $\ge r$ is

$\underbrace{(q+1,\dots,q+1)}_{t\ \text{terms}},\ \underbrace{(q,\dots,q)}_{m-t\ \text{terms}},$

where

$m = \left\lfloor \frac{n}{r} \right\rfloor,\qquad n = mq + t,\ 0 \le t < m.$

This completes the proof. ∎
