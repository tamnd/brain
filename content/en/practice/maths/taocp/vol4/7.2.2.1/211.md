**Exercise 211.** [*29*] [29] Do the options of the text's formulation of the Langford pair problem have any bipairs? How about the $n$ queens problem? And sudoku?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m31s  

---

We analyze bipairs in the standard exact cover formulations used in Section 7.2.2.1. Recall that a bipair consists of two distinct options whose intersection contains at least two items.

Thus we must determine, in each encoding, whether there exist two distinct options whose item-sets intersect in size at least $2$.

## Langford pair problem

In the text’s formulation, an option corresponds to placing a number $k$ in positions $i$ and $i+k+1$. The associated items can be viewed as:

- a number item $k$,
- two position items $p_i$ and $p_{i+k+1}$.

Thus each option has the form

$$
O(i,k) = \{k,\; p_i,\; p_{i+k+1}\}.
$$

Consider two distinct options $O(i,k)$ and $O(i',k')$.

### Case 1: $k \neq k'$

Then the number items differ, so the only possible shared items are position items. A position item $p_t$ belongs to exactly those options placing a number whose occurrences include position $t$. For fixed $t$, the pair $(k,i)$ is uniquely determined by whether $t=i$ or $t=i+k+1$, together with $k$. Hence two different values of $k$ cannot simultaneously realize two identical positions unless the entire placement coincides. Therefore they can share at most one position item.

So no bipair arises across different $k$.

### Case 2: $k = k'$, but $(i,k) \neq (i',k)$

Then both options use the same number item $k$. To share a second item, they would need to share a position item as well.

If they share $p_i$, then $p_i\in O(i,k)\cap O(i',k)$, so $i=i'$.

If they share $p_{i+k+1}$, then similarly $i=i'$.

Thus sharing any position item forces identical placements, contradicting distinctness.

Hence intersection size is at most $1$.

Therefore, the Langford encoding has **no bipairs**.

## $n$-queens problem

Each option corresponds to placing a queen at $(i,j)$ and contains the four items:

$$
O(i,j) = \{r_i,\; c_j,\; d_{i-j},\; a_{i+j}\}.
$$

Consider two distinct options $O(i,j)$ and $O(i',j')$.

We examine when two items can coincide.

### Row and column structure

- $r_i = r_{i'} \iff i=i'$
- $c_j = c_{j'} \iff j=j'$

Thus:

- sharing both $r_i$ and $c_j$ implies identical squares.

So no bipair can arise from row/column items.

### Diagonal structure

- $d_{i-j} = d_{i'-j'} \iff i-j = i'-j'$
- $a_{i+j} = a_{i'+j'} \iff i+j = i'+j'$

If both diagonal equalities hold, then solving:

$$
i-j = i'-j', \quad i+j = i'+j'
$$

implies $i=i'$ and $j=j'$, hence identical options.

Thus two distinct options cannot share both diagonal items.

### Mixed cases

We check whether two different types of constraints can overlap in two ways:

- $r_i = r_{i'}$ and $d_{i-j} = d_{i'-j'}$ forces $i=i'$ and then $j=j'$.
- $c_j = c_{j'}$ and $d_{i-j} = d_{i'-j'}$ similarly forces equality.
- Any pair involving $a_{i+j}$ together with a row or column constraint also determines the square uniquely.

In all cases, any two shared items determine both coordinates, forcing equality of options.

Therefore intersection size is at most $1$, and the $n$-queens encoding has **no bipairs**.

## Sudoku

Each option corresponds to placing digit $d$ in cell $(r,c)$ with block $b$, and contains:

$$
O(r,c,d) = \{x_{r,c},\; r_d,\; c_d,\; b_d\}.
$$

We exhibit an explicit bipair.

Fix a digit $d$ and a row $r$. Let $c_1 \neq c_2$ be two columns in the same $3\times 3$ block $b$. Consider:

$$
O_1 = O(r,c_1,d), \quad O_2 = O(r,c_2,d).
$$

Then:

- Both contain $r_d$ (row $r$, digit $d$),
- Both contain $b_d$ (same block $b$, same digit $d$),

so

$$
|O_1 \cap O_2| \ge 2.
$$

Yet $c_1 \neq c_2$, so $O_1 \neq O_2$. Hence $(O_1,O_2)$ is a bipair.

Therefore the Sudoku encoding **does contain bipairs**.

## Conclusion

- Langford pair formulation: no bipairs.
- $n$-queens formulation: no bipairs.
- Sudoku formulation: bipairs exist, e.g. two placements in the same row and block with the same digit but different columns.

$$
\boxed{\text{Only Sudoku has bipairs; the other two do not.}}
$$
