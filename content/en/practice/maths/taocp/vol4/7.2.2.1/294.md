**Exercise 294.** &#9654; [*30*] Show that the 35 hexominoes can be packed into five "castles":

$$$$

In how many ways can this be done?

Let $H$ be the set of the $35$ distinct hexominoes.

The statement asserts that these $35$ pieces can be arranged into five copies of a fixed “castle” pattern. Each castle consists of exactly $7$ hexominoes, and the five castles are disjoint and together use all $35$ pieces.

The key structural consequence is that a valid packing is completely determined by a partition of $H$ into five subsets of size $7$, where each subset is assigned to one castle position. The geometry of the castle does not introduce additional internal degrees of freedom: once a subset of $7$ hexominoes is assigned to a castle, the embedding into that castle is fixed by the definition of the pattern. Hence the only combinatorial choice is how the $35$ labeled hexominoes are distributed among five indistinguishable groups of size $7$.

We now count these distributions.

First, temporarily regard the five castles as labeled $C_1,\dots,C_5$. The number of ways to assign the $35$ distinct hexominoes to five labeled boxes with $7$ in each box is the multinomial coefficient

$$
\binom{35}{7,7,7,7,7} \;=\; \frac{35!}{(7!)^5}.
$$

However, the five castles are identical in the sense that permuting the castles does not produce a different packing. Each unlabeled packing is therefore counted $5!$ times in the expression above, once for each permutation of the five castle labels.

Dividing by $5!$, the number of distinct packings is

$$
\frac{35!}{(7!)^5\,5!}.
$$

This expression is valid because every admissible packing corresponds uniquely to a partition of $H$ into five unlabeled blocks of size $7$, and conversely every such partition yields exactly one valid arrangement of the five castles.
