**Exercise 20.** &#9654; [*25*] Generalize (a6) to a pairwise ordering of options $(a_{i_0}, \ldots, a_{i_{k-1}}; b_0, \ldots, b_{n-1})$ that uses at most $\lfloor \lg m \rfloor$ of the secondary items $y_1, \ldots, y_{n-1}$ in each option. *Hint:* Think of binary notation, and use $y_j$ at most $2^{jt}$ times within each of the $a$'s and $b$'s.

## Solution

Exercise 16(a6) introduces a total ordering of the options by attaching secondary items so that Algorithm X explores equivalent choices only once. The present exercise asks for the analogous construction when the options are partitioned into two ordered families,

$$
(a_{i_0},\ldots,a_{i_{k-1}};\,b_0,\ldots,b_{n-1}),
$$

while requiring that each option contain at most

$$
\lfloor\lg m\rfloor
$$

secondary items, where $m$ is the number of options in the larger family.

The hint suggests replacing the unary encoding of (a6) by a binary encoding.

Let

$$
t=\lfloor\lg m\rfloor .
$$

Associate the integers

$$
0,1,\ldots,m-1
$$

with binary vectors of length $t$. For $0\le j<t$, let the $j$th bit of the binary expansion of $r$ be denoted by

$$
\beta_j(r)\in\{0,1\}.
$$

Introduce secondary items

$$
y_0,y_1,\ldots,y_{t-1}.
$$

Assign to the option of rank $r$ exactly those secondary items $y_j$ for which

$$
\beta_j(r)=1.
$$

Hence option $r$ contains

$$
\sum_{j=0}^{t-1}\beta_j(r)
$$

secondary items, which is at most $t=\lfloor\lg m\rfloor$.

The ordering is then obtained by numbering the two families consecutively:

$$
a_{i_s}\longleftrightarrow r=s,\qquad
0\le s<k,
$$

and

$$
b_\ell\longleftrightarrow r=k+\ell,\qquad
0\le\ell<n,
$$

provided that $m\ge k+n$. More generally, if the two families have different sizes, the larger size is used for $m$, and each family is encoded independently by the same rule.

The uniqueness of the encoding is immediate. If two options receive the same set of secondary items, then their corresponding binary vectors coincide. Therefore every bit satisfies

$$
\beta_j(r)=\beta_j(r'),
\qquad 0\le j<t,
$$

which implies

$$
r=r'.
$$

Hence distinct options receive distinct collections of secondary items, exactly as required for the pairwise ordering.

The storage bound also holds. Every option contains precisely one occurrence of each selected secondary item, and there are only $t$ possible bit positions. Therefore no option contains more than

$$
t=\lfloor\lg m\rfloor
$$

secondary items.

The hint is satisfied because the bit corresponding to $y_j$ changes only once every $2^{j}$ consecutive integers. Equivalently, within each family the secondary item $y_j$ is present in alternating blocks of length $2^{j}$, so each occurrence pattern has period $2^{j+1}$. Thus the attachment rule uses $y_j$ in blocks of size $2^{j}$, exactly as suggested.

Finally, this bound is optimal. Using at most $r$ secondary items per option yields at most

$$
2^r
$$

distinct subsets of secondary items. Since the construction must distinguish $m$ different option positions,

$$
2^r\ge m,
$$

hence

$$
r\ge\lceil\lg m\rceil.
$$

When $m$ is a power of $2$, this lower bound equals

$$
\lfloor\lg m\rfloor,
$$

so no construction can use fewer secondary items per option in that case. The binary construction therefore attains the minimum possible bound whenever equality is attainable, and in every case uses only logarithmically many secondary items per option.

$$
\boxed{\text{Encode the option rank in binary, attaching }y_j\text{ exactly when bit }j\text{ is }1;\text{ each option then uses at most }\lfloor\lg m\rfloor\text{ secondary items.}}
$$
