**Exercise 165.** [*M30*] [M30] Consider an MCC problem in which we must choose 2 of 4 options to cover item 1, and 5 of 7 options to cover item 2; the options are all distinct.
- a) What's the size of the search tree if we branch first on item 1, then on item 2? Would it better to branch first on item 2, then on item 1?
- b) Generalize part (a) to the case when item 1 needs $p$ of $p+d$ options, while item 2 needs $q$ of $q+d$ options, where $q > p$ and $d > 0$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m13s  

---

## Setup

Item 1 is associated with 4 available options, and the task requires selecting exactly 2 of them. Item 2 is associated with 7 available options, and the task requires selecting exactly 5 of them. All options are distinct, and the two sets of options are disjoint, so choices for one item do not affect the availability of choices for the other item.

A search tree node corresponds to a partial choice, and branching corresponds to selecting subsets of the required size for the currently chosen item. A complete solution corresponds to a root-to-leaf path in which both items are satisfied.

For item 1, the number of possible complete choices is $\binom{4}{2}$. For item 2, the number of possible complete choices is $\binom{7}{5}$.

The size of the search tree is taken to mean the total number of nodes generated, including the root, all intermediate branching nodes, and all leaves.

## Solution

Branching first on item 1 produces $\binom{4}{2}$ children of the root, each representing a choice of 2 options among the 4 associated with item 1. Each such node then branches on item 2 into $\binom{7}{5}$ children, corresponding to choosing 5 options among the 7 associated with item 2.

The resulting tree therefore has one root node, $\binom{4}{2}$ nodes at the first level, and $\binom{4}{2}\binom{7}{5}$ leaves. The total number of nodes is

$$
1 + \binom{4}{2} + \binom{4}{2}\binom{7}{5}.
$$

Branching first on item 2 produces $\binom{7}{5}$ children of the root, and each of these branches into $\binom{4}{2}$ children for item 1. The total number of nodes is

$$
1 + \binom{7}{5} + \binom{7}{5}\binom{4}{2}.
$$

Since $\binom{4}{2}\binom{7}{5} = \binom{7}{5}\binom{4}{2}$, both expressions coincide, giving the same total number of nodes. The two search trees therefore have identical size, not only in number of leaves but in total number of nodes.

Because the branching factors differ, the only difference lies in the order in which widths appear at intermediate levels, not in the total number of nodes generated.

For the general case, item 1 requires selecting $p$ options from $p+d$ available, giving $\binom{p+d}{p}$ possibilities, and item 2 requires selecting $q$ options from $q+d$ available, giving $\binom{q+d}{q} = \binom{q+d}{d}$ possibilities.

If item 1 is processed first, the total node count is

$$
1 + \binom{p+d}{p} + \binom{p+d}{p}\binom{q+d}{q}.
$$

If item 2 is processed first, the total node count is

$$
1 + \binom{q+d}{q} + \binom{q+d}{q}\binom{p+d}{p}.
$$

The equality of multiplication shows these expressions are identical, so the search tree size is independent of the branching order.

## Verification

The branching construction produces exactly one level of internal nodes per item and one final level of leaves, since each item’s choice is completed before moving to the next item in either ordering. Every leaf corresponds to a pair consisting of a valid choice for item 1 and a valid choice for item 2, and no additional constraints couple the choices.

The multiplication of branching factors is commutative, so the number of leaves is invariant under the ordering of items. The total node count depends only on the sum of the root, the first-level nodes, and the leaves, and both orderings yield the same multiset of these quantities up to permutation.

## Conclusion

The two branching orders produce search trees of identical size. Neither ordering yields a smaller or larger tree in total node count under the stated assumptions. ∎
