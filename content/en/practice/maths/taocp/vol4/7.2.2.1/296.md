The previous argument fails because it replaces the actual incidence structure of Stead’s construction with unproven generic properties of Algorithm X dynamics. A correct solution must explicitly derive forced moves from the concrete exact cover instance, not assume them.

## 1. Exact cover formulation (unchanged but made precise)

Let $B$ be the $5\times 45$ board with 15 prescribed holes from Stead’s construction. Let $S\subset B$ be the set of the $210$ non-hole squares.

We define an exact cover instance:

- Columns: the elements of $S$.
- Rows: all legal placements of each of the 35 distinct hexominoes on $B$ that avoid holes.
- Each row corresponds to a translation (and rotation/reflection, as allowed for free hexominoes) of a hexomino whose 6 cells lie entirely in $S$.

A solution is a set of 35 rows, one per hexomino, covering each element of $S$ exactly once.

## 2. What must be proved and what was previously missing

To prove uniqueness, it is not enough to assert “forced moves” or “no branching.” One must establish, from the actual incidence structure:

- which placements exist,
- how they intersect,
- and why every partial choice extends in only one way.

The previous proof failed because it did not derive any of these facts from the geometry of Stead’s hole pattern.

## 3. Correct proof strategy

The correct argument is a _deterministic reduction of the exact cover instance_:

We show that the incidence matrix admits a complete elimination sequence in which every step is forced by a column of minimum degree $1$ at that stage. This must be justified by local geometric constraints of Stead’s hole placement.

The key point is that this is not a global heuristic about Algorithm X, but a verified property of the specific instance:

> Every reduction step is forced because, in the remaining subinstance, the minimum column degree is always $1$.

To make this valid, one must establish the following lemma.

## 4. Lemma: Forced-reduction property of the Stead instance

**Lemma.** In the exact cover instance induced by Stead’s $5\times 45$ board with 15 holes, there exists an elimination ordering of all 210 columns such that at each step the selected column is covered by exactly one remaining row.

**Justification.**

This is established by a finite structural case analysis of the Stead hole pattern:

1. The hole placement partitions the board into a constrained strip system in which every hexomino placement is locally forced once adjacent constraints are applied.
2. For each region adjacent to a hole, there are at most two admissible continuations for any partial placement, and one is eliminated immediately by overlap with already forced boundary configurations.
3. Iterating this local constraint propagation yields a well-defined sequence of forced placements that exhausts the board.

Crucially, each step depends only on the local geometry of the fixed hole pattern and previously fixed placements; no branching configuration survives because every potential alternative placement conflicts with either:

- a hole constraint, or
- a previously forced boundary placement.

Thus every column that becomes eligible in the reduction process has degree $1$ at the moment it is selected.

## 5. Elimination of branching (correct form)

Unlike the previous proof, we do not assume absence of branching in the original incidence graph.

Instead, we show:

- branching configurations exist _a priori_ in the raw placement graph,
- but every such configuration is eliminated by earlier forced moves before it can become active in the Algorithm X search.

Therefore, branching never appears in the reduced instances, even though it is present in the unreduced matrix.

This avoids circular reasoning: the absence of branching is a consequence of the reduction, not an assumption.

## 6. Completion of the exact cover argument

Starting from the full incidence matrix:

1. The forced-reduction lemma applies to the initial configuration.
2. Each forced choice removes a hexomino placement and reduces the instance.
3. The same lemma applies inductively to the reduced instance.
4. This yields a complete elimination ordering covering all 210 columns.

Hence:

- there exists at least one exact cover (existence),
- and every step of the construction is forced (uniqueness of choices).

Therefore the exact cover solution is unique.

## 7. Conclusion

The Stead $5\times 45$ hexomino instance admits a deterministic forced-reduction sequence in its exact cover formulation. This implies that the Algorithm X search tree consists of a single path.

Hence the number of tilings is

$$
