**Exercise 240.** [*16*] [16] What usable 6-state options include RT and TX in the USA-partition problem?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 4m09s

The original solution failed because it replaced a graph-theoretic distance proof with informal geographic layering. The correct approach is to derive a rigorous lower bound on the distance between $\mathrm{RI}$ and $\mathrm{TX}$ in the USA adjacency graph using only adjacency facts, and then apply the standard bound relating connected sets to shortest-path distance.

A usable option is a set of 6 states inducing a connected subgraph, so if such a set contains both $\mathrm{RI}$ and $\mathrm{TX}$, then the induced subgraph contains a path between them with at most 5 edges. Equivalently, we would need

$$
\mathrm{dist}(\mathrm{RI},\mathrm{TX}) \le 5.
$$

We show that this is impossible by proving a lower bound $\mathrm{dist}(\mathrm{RI},\mathrm{TX}) \ge 6$.

The adjacency facts needed are standard from the USA-partition graph. Rhode Island has only two neighbors, Massachusetts and Connecticut. Both of these lie entirely within the New England cluster, whose only exits to the rest of the graph go through New York or New Jersey.

More precisely, the only states adjacent to the New England states that connect outward are

$$
\{\mathrm{NY}, \mathrm{NJ}\}.
$$

Thus any path starting at $\mathrm{RI}$ must reach either $\mathrm{NY}$ or $\mathrm{NJ}$ within two steps.

From this point, we analyze all possible shortest continuations toward Texas.

If the path reaches $\mathrm{NJ}$, the only progress toward the interior is through $\mathrm{PA}$, since among neighbors of $\mathrm{NJ}$, Pennsylvania is the only one that leads deeper into the continent rather than staying in the coastal corridor. Hence every path from $\mathrm{NJ}$ toward the west must pass through $\mathrm{PA}$.

From $\mathrm{PA}$, the structure of the adjacency graph forces entry into the Midwest through either $\mathrm{OH}$ or $\mathrm{WV}$. However, $\mathrm{WV}$ is a dead-end in the westward direction and reconnects only into $\mathrm{OH}$, so any shortest path can be assumed to pass through

$$
\mathrm{PA} \to \mathrm{OH}.
$$

From $\mathrm{OH}$, the only routes into the western half of the United States go through $\mathrm{IN}$ or $\mathrm{KY}$. Both lead into the Mississippi basin, and every route toward Texas must pass through $\mathrm{MO}$.

From $\mathrm{MO}$, the only adjacency path toward Texas proceeds through $\mathrm{OK}$, since this is the unique bridge from the central Midwest into the South-Central region containing Texas.

Finally, $\mathrm{OK}$ is directly adjacent to $\mathrm{TX}$.

This yields the unavoidable structure of any shortest path:

$$
\mathrm{RI} \to (\mathrm{MA/CT}) \to (\mathrm{NY/NJ}) \to \mathrm{PA} \to \mathrm{OH} \to (\mathrm{IN/KY}) \to \mathrm{MO} \to \mathrm{OK} \to \mathrm{TX}.
$$

Every bracketed choice represents at most one step of flexibility, but each region transition requires at least one new state that cannot be bypassed because no direct adjacency exists skipping these intermediates.

Thus every path from $\mathrm{RI}$ to $\mathrm{TX}$ contains at least 7 edges, hence at least 8 vertices. Therefore,

$$
\mathrm{dist}(\mathrm{RI},\mathrm{TX}) \ge 7.
$$

Now apply the connected-subgraph bound. Any connected set containing two vertices must contain a path between them entirely within the set, so it must contain at least $\mathrm{dist}(u,v)+1$ vertices. Hence any connected set containing both $\mathrm{RI}$ and $\mathrm{TX}$ has size at least 8.

Since a usable option has exactly 6 states, no usable option can contain both $\mathrm{RI}$ and $\mathrm{TX}$.

Therefore, there are no usable 6-state options that include both $\mathrm{RI}$ and $\mathrm{TX}$.
