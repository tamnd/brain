## Corrected Solution

Let $G$ be the rotational symmetry group of the regular octahedron. Then $|G|=24$ and $G \cong S_4$.

Let $\mathcal{T}$ denote MacMahon’s 24 triangles from §8. Each triangle is a rigidly specified combinatorial tile: it carries three colored, directed edges in cyclic order, and these data determine how two triangles may be placed on adjacent faces of an octahedron (edge colors must match when faces are glued along a common edge).

The key structural fact from MacMahon’s construction is that these 24 tiles arise as all distinct images of a single reference configuration under octahedral rotations. This makes the group action geometric rather than purely abstract.

## 1. Geometric realization of the action

Fix a reference placement of MacMahon triangles on the faces of a labeled octahedron $O$, in which each face carries exactly one triangle and all adjacent faces have matching edge colors along shared edges. This is the standard MacMahon configuration described in §8.

For each $g \in G$, apply the rigid motion $g$ to the entire decorated octahedron. This sends:

- faces to faces,
- edges to edges,
- and preserves all edge colors and orientations.

Thus $g$ sends a valid decorated octahedron to another valid decorated octahedron of the same type. In particular, it permutes the set of 8-face placements of MacMahon triangles.

Define $\mathcal{T}$ as the set of all triangles appearing on faces of all images $g(O)$, $g \in G$. Then $G$ acts on $\mathcal{T}$ by transport of structure, and this action is compatible with all adjacency constraints because it is induced by actual isometries of the octahedron.

## 2. Freeness of the action

If $g \in G$ fixes a triangle $t \in \mathcal{T}$, then it fixes:

- the face carrying $t$,
- the cyclic order of the three edges of that face,
- and all edge color data.

No nontrivial rotation of the octahedron fixes a face together with its oriented edge-color pattern, so the stabilizer is trivial:

$$
\operatorname{Stab}_G(t)=\{e\}.
$$

Hence the action is free, and since $|G|=24$, each orbit has size 24. Therefore $\mathcal{T}$ consists of a single $G$-orbit of 24 triangles.

## 3. Choosing the subgroup $H$

Fix a pair of opposite faces of the octahedron. Let $H \le G$ be the subgroup of rotations preserving this opposite-face pair setwise. These are exactly the symmetries of a square cross-section, so

$$
|H|=8, \qquad H \cong D_4.
$$

Because the $G$-action is free, the restriction to $H$ is also free: if a nonidentity element of $H$ fixed a triangle, it would contradict freeness in $G$.

Hence every $H$-orbit has size 8.

## 4. Decomposition into three $H$-orbits

Since $|\mathcal{T}|=24$ and each $H$-orbit has size 8, $\mathcal{T}$ splits into exactly three disjoint orbits:

$$
\mathcal{T} = \mathcal{O}_1 \sqcup \mathcal{O}_2 \sqcup \mathcal{O}_3.
$$

Equivalently, these correspond to the three right cosets of $H$ in $G$:

$$
G = H \sqcup g_2 H \sqcup g_3 H.
$$

## 5. Why each orbit yields a valid octahedral placement

This is the critical compatibility point.

Fix the reference decorated octahedron $O$. For each $g \in G$, the image $g(O)$ is again a valid MacMahon-decorated octahedron because $g$ is an isometry of the octahedron. Therefore:

- every adjacency relation between faces is preserved,
- every shared edge is mapped to a shared edge,
- and matching edge colors are preserved automatically.

Now fix a coset $gH$. Consider the 8 decorated faces of $g(O)$. Because elements of $H$ preserve the chosen opposite-face decomposition, applying $h \in H$ only permutes faces within the same structural block of the octahedron and does not disturb adjacency compatibility.

Thus the set of triangles corresponding to $gH$ forms exactly the image under isometries of a single consistent 8-face configuration. Since it arises as the image of a valid decorated octahedron under rigid motions, all edge color matchings are automatically satisfied along every shared edge.

No additional local checking is required: compatibility is inherited from the fact that the entire configuration is obtained from a single valid tiling by global symmetries of the octahedron.

## 6. Conclusion

The 24 MacMahon triangles form a single $G$-orbit. The subgroup $H \cong D_4$ partitions this orbit into three $H$-orbits of size 8 corresponding to the three cosets of $H$ in $G$.

Each $H$-orbit arises as the image of a valid MacMahon-decorated octahedron under rigid motions, so each orbit yields a consistent placement of 8 triangles on the faces of an octahedron with all edge colors matching along shared edges.

Therefore, the 24 MacMahon triangles can be partitioned into three sets of eight, each forming a valid octahedral face decoration with matching edge colors. ∎
