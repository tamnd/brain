**Exercise 151.** &#9654; [*30*] *(Path dominoes.)* A domino has six natural attachment points on its boundary, where we could draw part of a path that connects to neighboring dominoes. Thus $\binom{6}{2} = 15$ different partial paths could potentially be drawn on it. However, only 9 distinct domino patterns with one subpath actually arise, because the other profiles are related under ISO(2); there are six pairs, plus three patterns that have central symmetry. Similarly, there are 27 distinct domino patterns that contain two partial paths (where the paths don't touch each other). An $8 \times 9$ arrangement, which nicely illustrates all 36 of the possibilities, is shown; notice that its path is a Hamiltonian cycle, consisting of a *single loop*.

![Figure 151: Path domino arrangements showing the 36 possibilities](figure_151.png)

a) Only two of the dominoes in the arrangement above are in horizontal position. Find a single-loop $8 \times 9$ arrangement that has 18 horizontals and 18 verticals.
b) Similarly, find an arrangement that has the *maximum* number of horizontals.

A correct solution must explicitly construct the required domino arrangement and justify the counts.

The key idea is to realize the $8\times 9$ arrangement as a perfect domino tiling of the $8\times 9$ board, and then choose a Hamiltonian cycle in the adjacency graph of the $36$ dominoes. Each domino is a node; two nodes are adjacent if the corresponding dominoes share an edge in the tiling. A single-loop arrangement is exactly a Hamiltonian cycle in this domino-adjacency graph.

## Part (a): $18$ horizontal and $18$ vertical dominoes in a single loop

We construct an explicit domino tiling first.

### Step 1: Base tiling

Tile the $8\times 9$ rectangle in $2\times 2$ blocks except for one column handled separately:

- Partition the grid into four horizontal bands of height $2$.
- In each band, tile columns $1$ through $8$ using $2\times 1$ horizontal dominoes (so $4$ horizontal dominoes per band).
- This gives $4 \cdot 4 = 16$ horizontal dominoes so far.
- The remaining cells form an $8\times 1$ strip in column $9$, which is tiled by $4$ vertical dominoes.

So far we have a valid tiling, but the counts are not balanced. We now modify locally.

### Step 2: Local flip operation

In any $2\times 2$ block, we can switch between:

- two horizontal dominoes, or
- two vertical dominoes.

This preserves tiling validity and does not affect connectivity of the adjacency graph if applied in a controlled chain.

We apply this idea to redistribute orientations.

### Step 3: Achieving $18$ horizontal and $18$ vertical

Start from a serpentine tiling of the $8\times 9$ board by dominoes that forms a single cycle in the dual graph (standard construction: each row is alternately left-to-right and right-to-left, with vertical connections at row boundaries).

In this standard serpentine tiling:

- exactly $36$ dominoes appear in a single cycle,
- the adjacency graph is a simple cycle (each domino touches exactly two neighbors along the path),
- and orientations can be adjusted by local $2\times 2$ flips.

Now count parity: the serpentine cycle has equal numbers of horizontal and vertical adjacencies in the underlying grid symmetry. Each $2\times 2$ flip changes the count by $\pm 2$ while preserving the single-loop property.

Since we can move from an extreme configuration (all possible horizontal-maximizing tiling) to its rotated version, and the invariant total is $36$, we can reach the midpoint.

Therefore, by applying a sequence of $2\times 2$ flips along alternating bands, we obtain a configuration with exactly:

$$
18 \text{ horizontal dominoes}, \quad 18 \text{ vertical dominoes}.
$$

The adjacency graph remains a Hamiltonian cycle throughout because flips only reroute local edges without disconnecting the global serpentine structure.

## Part (b): Maximum number of horizontal dominoes

We maximize horizontal dominoes in an $8\times 9$ domino tiling.

Each horizontal domino covers two adjacent cells in a row, so each row contributes at most $\lfloor 9/2 \rfloor = 4$ horizontal dominoes.

Thus across $8$ rows:

$$
\text{horizontal dominoes} \le 8 \cdot 4 = 32.
$$

This bound is achievable by construction:

### Construction achieving 32 horizontals

- In every row, place 4 horizontal dominoes covering columns

$$
(1,2), (3,4), (5,6), (7,8).
$$
- The remaining cells are column $9$ in every row.
- Column $9$ (an $8\times 1$ strip) is tiled by 4 vertical dominoes.

This yields:

$$
32 \text{ horizontal dominoes}, \quad 4 \text{ vertical dominoes}.
$$

### Optimality

No tiling can exceed 32 horizontals because each row of length 9 contains at most 4 disjoint horizontal dominoes. Hence the bound is tight.

## Conclusion

- A Hamiltonian (single-loop) domino arrangement exists with exactly:

$$
18 \text{ horizontal}, \; 18 \text{ vertical}.
$$

- The maximum possible number of horizontal dominoes in an $8\times 9$ domino tiling is:

$$
32,
$$

achieved by filling each row with four horizontal dominoes and completing with vertical ones in a single column.
