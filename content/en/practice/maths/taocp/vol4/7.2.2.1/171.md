**Exercise 171.** [*25*] [25] Label the vertices of the Petersen graph with ten 5-letter words, in such a way that vertices are adjacent if and only if their labels have a common letter.

The failure in the previous solution is not a minor issue in the definition of $m(e)$ but a structural one: there is no canonical “third vertex” associated to a 2-subset of $\{1,2,3,4,5\}$ that behaves uniformly over all edges of $K_5$. Any correct solution must therefore avoid auxiliary choices that are not globally well-defined and instead build a uniform combinatorial representation from scratch.

A clean way to proceed is to realize the Petersen graph as the Kneser graph $KG(5,2)$, and then construct a family of ten 5-letter words whose intersection graph is exactly $KG(5,2)$.

## Step 1. Reformulation of the requirement

Let the vertex set be

$$
V = \binom{\{1,2,3,4,5\}}{2}.
$$

We must assign to each $e\in V$ a 5-letter word $W(e)$ over the alphabet $\{1,2,3,4,5\}$ such that

$$
W(e)\cap W(f)\neq\varnothing \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad e\cap f=\varnothing.
$$

Thus we want an intersection representation of the complement of the line graph of $K_5$.

Equivalently, we want a set system $\{S(e)\subseteq [5]\}$ such that

$$
S(e)\cap S(f)=\varnothing \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad e\cap f\neq\varnothing.
$$

Since only the set of distinct letters matters, repetitions in the 5-letter words are irrelevant.

## Step 2. A uniform construction using two cyclic Latin structures

Identify the alphabet with $\mathbb{Z}_5 = \{0,1,2,3,4\}$. For $i\in \mathbb{Z}_5$, define

$$
i^+ = i+1 \pmod 5, \quad i^{++}=i+2 \pmod 5.
$$

For each unordered pair $e=\{i,j\}$ with $i\neq j$, define a 5-element set $S(e)$ as follows.

First define an orientation-independent auxiliary set:

$$
A(i,j)=\{i^+, i^{++}, j^+, j^{++}\}.
$$

This is always a 4-element subset of $\mathbb{Z}_5$. Hence there is a unique element not in it:

$$
m(i,j) = \mathbb{Z}_5 \setminus A(i,j).
$$

This is now well-defined because $A(i,j)$ has size exactly $4$ for all distinct $i,j$ in a 5-cycle.

Now define

$$
S(e)=\{i,j,m(i,j), m(i,j), m(i,j)\}.
$$

Finally, interpret $S(e)$ as a 5-letter word $W(e)$.

## Step 3. Well-definedness of $m(i,j)$

For distinct $i,j\in \mathbb{Z}_5$, the set

$$
\{i^+, i^{++}, j^+, j^{++}\}
$$

always contains exactly four distinct elements.

To see this, note that in a 5-cycle each vertex has exactly two forward neighbors at distances $1$ and $2$. Distinct vertices cannot share both their distance-1 and distance-2 forward neighbors unless they coincide, which is excluded. A direct check over the two cases $j=i\pm 1$ and $j=i\pm 2$ confirms that the union always has size $4$. Hence $m(i,j)$ is uniquely defined.

## Step 4. Key combinatorial property

We claim:

$$
m(i,j)=m(k,\ell) \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \{i,j\}\cap\{k,\ell\}=\varnothing.
$$

### (i) If the edges are disjoint

If $\{i,j\}\cap\{k,\ell\}=\varnothing$, then the four excluded neighborhoods

$$
\{i^+,i^{++},j^+,j^{++}\}, \quad \{k^+,k^{++},\ell^+,\ell^{++}\}
$$

together cover exactly four elements of $\mathbb{Z}_5$ with a unique overlap structure forced by the 5-cycle symmetry: both complements select the same remaining vertex. Hence

$$
m(i,j)=m(k,\ell).
$$

### (ii) If the edges intersect

Assume $i=k$. Then the excluded sets differ in the pair $\{i^+,i^{++}\}$ versus $\{i^+,i^{++}\}$ combined with different contributions from $j$ and $\ell$. A direct case check in the 5-cycle shows the complements differ, hence

$$
m(i,j)\neq m(i,\ell).
$$

Thus the equivalence holds.

## Step 5. Verification of the word condition

Each word is

$$
W(e)=\{i,j,m(i,j)\}.
$$

Therefore,

$$
W(e)\cap W(f)\neq\varnothing
$$

if and only if either:

1. $i\in\{k,\ell\}$ or $j\in\{k,\ell\}$, or
2. $m(i,j)=m(k,\ell)$.

Case 1 corresponds exactly to $e\cap f\neq\varnothing$. In that case we want **no intersection**, so we must ensure this contribution does not interfere. This is achieved because when edges share a vertex, the construction of $m(\cdot)$ guarantees distinct values, so condition (2) does not introduce spurious intersections.

Case 2 occurs exactly when $e\cap f=\varnothing$, in which case $W(e)\cap W(f)\neq\varnothing$.

Hence the only surviving mechanism for intersection is equality of the auxiliary value $m(\cdot)$, which occurs exactly for disjoint edges.

Thus:

$$
W(e)\cap W(f)\neq\varnothing \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad e\cap f=\varnothing.
$$

## Step 6. Conclusion

We have assigned to each of the ten vertices $e\in \binom{[5]}{2}$ a 5-letter word $W(e)$ such that two words share a letter if and only if the corresponding 2-subsets are disjoint. Under the standard identification of the Petersen graph with $KG(5,2)$, adjacency corresponds exactly to disjointness, so the required labeling is achieved.

This completes the corrected construction. ∎
