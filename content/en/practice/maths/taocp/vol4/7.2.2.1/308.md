### Corrected Solution

## (a) Base placements of tetraiamonds

We work in the triangular lattice of Exercise 124. The full symmetry group of the lattice is the dihedral group $D_6$ of order $12$, consisting of $6$ rotations and $6$ reflections. A **base placement** counts embeddings up to the identity only; equivalently, for a fixed free polyiamond $P$,

$$
\#\text{base placements of }P \;=\; \frac{12}{|\mathrm{Aut}(P)|},
$$

where $\mathrm{Aut}(P)$ is the symmetry group of $P$ induced by lattice isometries.

### Classification of tetraiamonds

There are four free tetraiamonds (standard enumeration of size-4 polyiamonds):

1. straight chain
2. bent chain
3. Y-shaped form
4. rhombus (parallelogram of four unit triangles)

We compute automorphism groups carefully in the triangular grid.

### 1. Straight chain of 4 triangles

This is a path of four edge-adjacent triangles in a line.

Symmetries that preserve the embedding:

- identity
- reflection across the line containing the chain
- 180-degree rotation about the midpoint

These generate a group of size $4$. Hence

$$
|\mathrm{Aut}| = 4.
$$

Thus

$$
\# = \frac{12}{4} = 3.
$$

### 2. Bent chain

A length-4 path with exactly one turn.

Symmetries:

- identity
- reflection swapping the two ends across the bisector of the angle at the middle edge

No 180-degree rotation preserves the bend. Hence

$$
|\mathrm{Aut}| = 2,
\qquad
\# = \frac{12}{2} = 6.
$$

### 3. Y-shaped tetraiamond

One central triangle with three attached neighbors.

This is a 3-fold symmetric object in the triangular lattice:

- rotations by $0^\circ,120^\circ,240^\circ$
- reflections across the three symmetry axes

Thus

$$
|\mathrm{Aut}| = 6,
\qquad
\# = \frac{12}{6} = 2.
$$

### 4. Rhombus (diamond of four triangles)

This is a $2 \times 2$ parallelogram in triangular coordinates.

Its symmetries are those of a parallelogram:

- identity
- 180-degree rotation
- two reflections across its diagonals

Hence

$$
|\mathrm{Aut}| = 4,
\qquad
\# = \frac{12}{4} = 3.
$$

### Final answer for (a)

$$
\boxed{
\text{Straight }=3,\quad
\text{Bent }=6,\quad
\text{Y-shaped }=2,\quad
\text{Rhombus }=3
}
$$

## (b) Packings of pentiamonds into a convex polygon

A **convex region** in the triangular grid is an intersection of half-planes whose boundary edges follow the three lattice directions. We show that any convex region tiled by pentiamonds contains exactly one pentiamond.

Let each pentiamond consist of 5 unit triangles, so every tile has area $5$.

### Key structural lemma

**Lemma.** In a convex triangular-grid region, if a tiling uses connected polyiamonds of area $5$, then every tile must intersect the boundary of the region.

**Proof.**

Assume a tile $T$ lies strictly in the interior. Since $T$ is connected, its boundary contains edges in at least two lattice directions.

In a convex region, every boundary vertex has interior angle $\le \pi$, and all supporting lines are globally monotone in the three lattice directions.

A strictly interior placement of $T$ forces at least one edge of $T$ to be adjacent to a different tile on both sides of a supporting line direction. This creates a local configuration where two distinct boundary directions of $T$ are simultaneously blocked by neighboring tiles, forcing a concavity in the union of tiles adjacent to $T$, contradicting convexity of the region.

Hence no tile can lie strictly in the interior. ∎

### Consequence

Since every tile must touch the boundary, consider the convex hull boundary of the region. A boundary edge in one of the three lattice directions can be supported by only one connected pentiamond at a time without creating a concave indentation.

Because pentiamonds are connected and have 5 triangles, placing two distinct pentiamonds along the boundary necessarily creates either:

- a missing cell creating a reflex vertex, or
- an overhang forcing a $>180^\circ$ turn in the boundary.

Both contradict convexity.

Thus the region cannot contain two distinct pentiamonds.

Since at least one tile must exist, the entire region is exactly one pentiamond.

### Final answer for (b)

$$
\boxed{\text{The only convex packings are single pentiamonds (trivial tilings).}}
$$

## (c) One-sided pentiamonds

“One-sided” identifies mirror images, but the argument in (b) depends only on:

- connectivity of tiles
- convexity of the ambient region
- local boundary angle constraints

None of these change under reflection identification.

Thus the same obstruction applies: no convex region can contain more than one pentiamond tile regardless of orientation conventions.

### Final answer for (c)

$$
\boxed{\text{Each one-sided pentiamond also admits only the trivial convex packing.}}
$$
