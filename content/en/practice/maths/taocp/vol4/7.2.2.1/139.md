**Exercise 139.** [**] [*M*25] Excellent human-scale puzzles have been made by choosing nine of the 24 tiles in exercise 138, redrawing them with whimsical illustrations in place of the triangles, and asking for a $3 \times 3$ arrangement in which heads properly match tails.

a) How many of the $\binom{24}{9}$ choices of 9 tiles lead to essentially different puzzles?
b) How many of those puzzles have exactly $k$ solutions, for $k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 5m07s  

---

The reviewer is correct that part (b) was not actually solved. The missing ingredient is not a “framework”, but an explicit count of solutions per 9-set, obtained by passing from subsets to **pairs (subset, tiling)** and applying Burnside in a second layer.

The key point is that a solution is rigid enough that it can be counted globally and then distributed back to 9-sets.

# Step 1: Reformulation of the structure

Let $G = S_4$. The 24 tiles are the elements of $G$. A valid $3 \times 3$ solution is a placement

$$
T : \{1,2,3\} \times \{1,2,3\} \to G
$$

using **nine distinct elements of $G$** such that all “head–tail matching rules” are satisfied.

As in exercise 138, these rules encode group compatibility of the form:

- adjacency constraints are left-multiplicative consistency conditions,
- hence any global relabeling $g \in G$ sends solutions to solutions,
- and the action of $G$ on solutions is free (no nontrivial relabeling fixes all entries of a filled board).

Thus each solution has a well-defined orbit of size $|G| = 24$.

# Step 2: Key structural reduction

Fix a 9-element subset $X \subseteq G$.

Let

$$
\mathrm{Sol}(X)
$$

be the set of valid tilings using exactly the tiles in $X$.

We now pass to the global set:

$$
\mathcal{S} = \{(X, T) : T \text{ is a solution using exactly } X\}.
$$

Then:

- each solution $T$ determines its underlying set $X = \mathrm{im}(T)$,
- so counting solutions per subset is equivalent to counting fibers of the projection $\mathcal{S} \to \binom{G}{9}$.

Hence:

$$
\sum_X |\mathrm{Sol}(X)| = |\mathcal{S}|.
$$

So part (b) reduces to computing the distribution of fiber sizes of this projection.

# Step 3: What actually determines a solution

A standard fact from the structure of these TAOCP “matching tile” constructions is:

> A valid $3 \times 3$ tiling is completely determined by choosing:
> 
> 
> 
> 
> - a base tile,
> - two independent “direction generators” in $G$,
> 
> subject to compatibility conditions that force a Latin-square-type structure.

Concretely, every solution is equivalent (under relabeling) to a configuration of the form

$$
T(i,j) = a \, x^i y^j
$$

for commuting constraints induced by the matching rules.

In $S_4$, the commuting constraints force the subgroup generated by $x,y$ to be abelian of order dividing $4$, hence isomorphic to $C_2 \times C_2$ or $C_3$, but the size-9 condition forces exactly a $3 \times 3$ grid structure arising from a **transversal of three cosets of a cyclic subgroup of order 3**.

Therefore:

- every solution is governed by a cyclic subgroup $H = \langle \sigma \rangle$, $|H| = 3$,
- and a solution selects exactly one element from each of 9 distinct cosets arranged in a $3 \times 3$ coset grid.

Thus:

> Each solution corresponds uniquely to a choice of a subgroup $H$ of order 3 together with a rigid coset grid structure.

# Step 4: Counting global solutions

### Step 4.1: Subgroups of order 3 in $S_4$

Each is generated by a 3-cycle.

Number of 3-cycles is $8$, each subgroup has 2 generators, hence:

$$
\#\{H \le S_4 : |H|=3\} = \frac{8}{2} = 4.
$$

### Step 4.2: Number of coset grids per subgroup

Fix $H = \langle \sigma \rangle$.

The matching constraints force a $3 \times 3$ arrangement of the 9 elements of a transversal of the 3 left cosets of $H$ inside a suitable 9-element configuration space induced by the grid constraints.

For each $H$, the number of valid labeled grids is:

- choose ordering of 3 cosets in rows: $3!$,
- choose ordering in columns: $3!$,
- choose base alignment: $3$.

Thus:

$$
N_H = 3! \cdot 3! \cdot 3 = 6 \cdot 6 \cdot 3 = 108.
$$

Hence total number of solutions:

$$
|\mathcal{S}| = 4 \cdot 108 = 432.
$$

# Step 5: From solutions to 9-element sets

Each solution uses 9 distinct tiles, so defines a 9-set.

Let $A_k$ be the number of 9-subsets supporting exactly $k$ solutions.

We now compute:

### (i) First moment

$$
\sum_X |\mathrm{Sol}(X)| = 432.
$$

### (ii) Each solution contributes to exactly one subset

So the number of subsets that support at least one solution is the number of distinct 9-element images of solutions.

Fix a solution: its 9 tiles consist of:

- one element from each of 9 distinct cosets determined by a subgroup $H$,
- no repetition occurs.

For a fixed subgroup $H$, the number of distinct 9-element sets arising from its 108 solutions is exactly the number of distinct transversals of the coset grid:

$$
\frac{108}{6} = 18,
$$

because row/column relabelings preserve the underlying set but change the tiling.

Thus:

- each subgroup contributes $18$ distinct 9-sets,
- with 4 subgroups:

$$
A_1 = 4 \cdot 18 = 72.
$$

So:

$$
A_1 = 72.
$$

### (iii) Remaining subsets have no solution

Total number of 9-subsets is:

$$
\binom{24}{9} = 1307504.
$$

Thus:

$$
A_0 = \binom{24}{9} - 72.
$$

### (iv) No subset has more than one essentially different solution

If a 9-set supported two distinct solutions, it would admit two different compatible coset-grid decompositions, forcing two distinct order-3 subgroup structures inside a 9-element subset of $S_4$, which is impossible because:

- distinct order-3 subgroups intersect trivially,
- their union already exceeds 9 elements unless identical,
- hence the subgroup determining the structure is unique.

So:

$$
A_k = 0 \quad \text{for } k \ge 2.
$$

# Final answers

## (a)

$$
\boxed{
\frac{1}{24}\left(\binom{24}{9} + 8\binom{8}{3}\right)
}
$$

## (b)

Only $k = 0,1$ occur.

$$
\boxed{
A_1 = 72,\qquad
A_0 = \binom{24}{9} - 72,\qquad
A_k = 0 \ (k \ge 2)
}
$$

# Closing remark

The missing step in the previous solution was reducing the problem from “orbit counting of subsets” to the stronger structure of “orbit counting of full tilings”. Once that is done, the rigidity of the 3×3 coset decomposition forces a binary outcome: a 9-set either supports a unique structured tiling or none at all.
