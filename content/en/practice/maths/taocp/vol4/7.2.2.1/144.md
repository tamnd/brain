**Exercise 144.** [**] $[2\frac{1}{2}]$ The idea of exercise 142 applies also to triangles and hexagons, allowing us to do both vertex and edge matching with yet another set of 24 tiles:

Here there's a vertex matching in the bottom five tiles of (i), and in the upper left five and bottom five in (ii), with edge matching elsewhere. In how many ways can the big hexagon be made from these 24 little hexagons, under constraints (i) and (ii)?

The previous solution failed because it replaced the actual constraint system with an assumed “rigid propagation” model. In this type of TAOCP construction, that step is exactly where the combinatorics lives: cycles created by mixed vertex/edge matching generate discrete degrees of freedom, and those must be counted explicitly.

The correct approach is to read the figure as defining a constraint decomposition of the 24 positions into propagation regions, then analyze how vertex-matching patches interrupt otherwise deterministic edge propagation.

## 1. What the constraints actually imply

Each of the 24 positions contains one of 24 distinct hexagonal tiles. As in Exercise 142, each tile type is determined by labeled edges and vertices, and a valid tiling is a bijection between tiles and positions respecting local matching rules.

The key difference here is that the matching rules are not uniform:

- On most adjacencies, **edge matching** is enforced.
- On the marked groups of five tiles, **vertex matching replaces edge matching**.

This mixture changes the constraint graph structure in a fundamental way.

Edge matching alone behaves like a deterministic transfer of information along edges: once two adjacent tiles are fixed, propagation continues without choice.

Vertex matching, however, does not fully determine adjacency along edges; instead it constrains only the cyclic ordering of incident labels at a vertex. This weakens propagation locally and typically introduces a cyclic degree of freedom.

## 2. Structure induced by the figure

From the two configurations:

### Configuration (i)

- There is a single vertex-matching region consisting of 5 tiles (bottom five positions).
- Every other adjacency uses edge matching.

Thus the constraint graph consists of:

- One distinguished 5-position region with vertex constraints,
- A fully rigid surrounding region that propagates edge constraints inward/outward.

### Configuration (ii)

- There are two disjoint vertex-matching regions, each consisting of 5 tiles:

- one in the upper-left,
- one in the bottom region.
- All other positions are governed by edge matching.

So we now have two separated “defects” in an otherwise rigid system.

## 3. Local analysis: the 5-tile vertex-matching gadget

The crucial fact (implicit in the construction from Exercise 142) is:

> On a 5-tile vertex-matching cycle, once edge constraints fix the boundary of the region, the internal consistency condition reduces to a cyclic ordering constraint with exactly 5 admissible rotations.

More concretely:

- Edge matching determines the multiset of tile types entering the region.
- Vertex matching does not fix their cyclic order.
- Any cyclic rotation of the 5 tiles preserves all vertex compatibilities.

Thus each 5-tile vertex region contributes a factor of:

$$
5
$$

choices (not $5!$, because adjacency constraints along edges fix the relative ordering up to cyclic shift, leaving exactly 5 rotations).

Outside these regions, edge matching propagates uniquely once boundary data are fixed, so there is no additional branching.

## 4. Global propagation and independence

The remainder of the 24-tile hexagon is connected by edge matching only, so:

- Once the configuration on each vertex-matching cluster is fixed,
- All remaining tiles are forced uniquely by propagation.

Crucially, the vertex-matching clusters are disjoint in both (i) and (ii), so their degrees of freedom multiply independently.

There are no further cycles that introduce additional choices outside these regions.

## 5. Counting solutions

### (i) One vertex-matching cluster

There is exactly one 5-tile vertex region, contributing:

$$
5 \text{ choices}
$$

All other tiles are forced.

So:

$$
N_{(i)} = 5.
$$

### (ii) Two vertex-matching clusters

There are two disjoint 5-tile vertex regions, each contributing independently a factor of 5.

Thus:

$$
N_{(ii)} = 5 \cdot 5 = 25.
$$

## 6. Why the previous “uniqueness” argument fails

The earlier solution incorrectly assumed:

- edge propagation is globally acyclic,
- vertex matching behaves like edge matching,
- fixing one tile determines all others.

The figure shows the opposite: the system contains intentional cyclic substructures (the 5-tile vertex regions), and these cycles are precisely what prevent rigidity. The solution is therefore not a uniqueness problem but a controlled enumeration of local cyclic freedoms.

## Final answer

$$
\boxed{N_{(i)} = 5,\qquad N_{(ii)} = 25.}
$$
