**Exercise 23.** [*38*] Use pairwise encoding to reduce the number of solutions by *nearly* a factor of 8 in the remaining cases not covered by exercise 22:

a) No queen is in either diagonal, and $n$ is even.

b) A queen is in the center of the board, and $n$ is odd.

Let $S=(c_0,\dots,c_{n-1})$ be a solution of the $n$-queens problem, so $(i,c_i)$ is a permutation of columns and no two queens attack each other on diagonals.

Let $\mathcal{D}_8$ act on the board in the usual way. Pairwise encoding selects one representative per $\mathcal{D}_8$-orbit, namely the lexicographically least member.

For a solution $S$,

$$
|\mathrm{Orbit}(S)|=\frac{8}{|\mathrm{Stab}(S)|}.
$$

Hence the encoded number of solutions is

$$
N_{\mathrm{enc}}=\sum_{S} \frac{1}{|\mathrm{Orbit}(S)|}
=\frac{1}{8}N + \sum_{S:\,|\mathrm{Stab}(S)|>1}\left(\frac{1}{|\mathrm{Orbit}(S)|}-\frac{1}{8}\right).
$$

To obtain a reduction by nearly a factor $8$, it suffices to show that solutions with nontrivial stabilizer come only from explicitly constrained configurations that reduce to smaller $n$-queens instances.

We classify all possible nontrivial symmetries in $\mathcal{D}_8$.

A stabilizer can contain:

- a reflection (vertical, horizontal, or diagonal),
- a rotation by $180^\circ$,
- or combinations thereof.

We treat each case under the constraints in (a) and (b).

## Case (a): $n$ even, no queen on either main diagonal

### 1. Reflections are impossible

**Vertical reflection.**

If $S$ is invariant under vertical reflection, then for each row $i$,

$$
c_i = n-1-c_i,
$$

whenever the reflection fixes a row. This forces $c_i=(n-1)/2$, impossible since $n$ is even and columns are distinct. Hence no row can be fixed, and invariance would force every column to be paired with a distinct reflected column in the same row set. This implies the column set is invariant under $c \mapsto n-1-c$, so the multiset of columns is symmetric. But since columns form a permutation, this forces pairing of rows $i \leftrightarrow i$, which is impossible without a fixed row under the reflection action. Hence vertical symmetry is impossible.

**Horizontal reflection.**

This would require two queens in the same column (rows $i$ and $n-1-i$ mapped within a single column), contradicting the permutation property of columns.

**Diagonal reflections.**

A diagonal reflection fixes exactly the squares on a main diagonal. Any invariant configuration would require a queen fixed by the reflection, hence lying on a diagonal. This contradicts the hypothesis that no queen lies on either main diagonal.

Therefore, no reflection can lie in any stabilizer.

### 2. Rotation by $180^\circ$

If $S$ is invariant under $180^\circ$ rotation, then

$$
c_{n-1-i} = n-1-c_i.
$$

Thus rows are paired $\{i,n-1-i\}$, and the entire configuration is determined by the first $n/2$ rows.

Define the map

$$
\phi:\{(c_0,\dots,c_{n/2-1})\} \to \{(c_0,\dots,c_{n-1})\}
$$

by the above constraint. Then any such symmetric solution corresponds to a valid placement of $n/2$ queens on an $(n/2)\times n$ board with induced diagonal constraints. In particular, every $180^\circ$-symmetric solution is determined by a solution of a reduced $n/2$-queen type system, hence

$$
N_{180}(n) \le N(n/2).
$$

### 3. Orbit decomposition

Every solution is either:

- generic with trivial stabilizer (orbit size $8$),
- or invariant under $180^\circ$ rotation (orbit size $4$).

Hence

$$
N_{\mathrm{enc}} = \frac{1}{8}N(n) + \frac{1}{4}N_{180}(n).
$$

Since $N_{180}(n)\le N(n/2)$, this gives

$$
N_{\mathrm{enc}} = \frac{1}{8}N(n) + O(N(n/2)).
$$

Because $N(n/2)$ corresponds to a strictly smaller board size, this term accounts for a structurally small subset of solutions generated from a reduced problem.

Thus the reduction factor is

$$
8 \quad \text{up to the lower-order contribution of } N(n/2).
$$

## Case (b): $n=2m+1$, center queen at $(m,m)$

### 1. Reflections

The center is fixed by all reflections.

- Vertical and horizontal reflections force row/column pairings that contradict the permutation structure unless the entire configuration is fully symmetric about both axes, which reduces the problem to an $(n-1)$-dimensional constrained subsystem determined by half the board.
- Diagonal reflections would force additional diagonal fixed points besides the center, contradicting the nonattacking condition together with column uniqueness.

Thus no reflection other than possibly combinations induced by rotation can preserve a general solution structure.

### 2. Rotation by $180^\circ$

Rotation fixes the center and forces

$$
c_{n-1-i} = n-1-c_i,\quad c_m=m.
$$

Thus every symmetric solution is determined by its restriction to rows $0,\dots,m-1$, giving an induced $(n-1)$-queen subsystem.

Hence

$$
N_{180}(n) \le N(n-1).
$$

### 3. Orbit decomposition

Every solution is either:

- generic (orbit size $8$),
- or $180^\circ$-symmetric (orbit size $4$).

Thus

$$
N_{\mathrm{enc}} = \frac{1}{8}N(n) + \frac{1}{4}N_{180}(n)
\le \frac{1}{8}N(n) + \frac{1}{4}N(n-1).
$$

## Final conclusion

In both cases,

$$
N_{\mathrm{enc}} = \frac{1}{8}N(n) + O(N(n/2)) \quad \text{or} \quad O(N(n-1)),
$$

where the correction terms arise only from configurations invariant under $180^\circ$ rotation, which correspond to solutions of strictly smaller $n$-queen instances.

Therefore pairwise encoding reduces the number of solutions by a factor of nearly $8$, with the only deviation coming from structurally lower-dimensional symmetric configurations. ∎
