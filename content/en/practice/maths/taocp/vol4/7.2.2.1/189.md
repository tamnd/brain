[Section 7.2.2.1: Dancing Links](../)

**Exercise 189.** [*HM31*] Let $\rho_n = \hat{s}_n - g_{\varpi_n}$ (see (86)). We'll prove that $|\rho_n| = O(e^{-n/\ln^2 n}\varpi_n)$, by applying the saddle point method to $R(z) = \sum_n \rho_n z^n/n! = e^{e^z} \int_0^\infty e^{-t} dt$. The idea is to show that $|R(z)|$ is rather small when $z = \xi e^{i\theta}$, where $\xi e^\xi = n$ as in 7.2.1.5–(2g).

a) Express $|e^{e^z}|$ and $|e^{-t}|$ in terms of $x$ and $y$ when $z = x + iy$.

b) If $0 \le \theta \le \frac{\pi}{2}$, $y = \xi \sin\theta \le \frac{\pi}{2}$, $0 < c_1 \le \cos\frac{y}{2}$, prove $|R(\xi e^{i\theta})| = O(\exp(\xi^2 - c_1\xi^2))$.

c) If $0 \le \theta \le \frac{\pi}{2}$, $y = \xi \sin\theta \ge \frac{\pi}{2}$, $0 < c_2 \le \frac{y}{\pi}$, prove $|R(\xi e^{i\theta})| = O(\exp(\xi^2 - c_2\xi^2/\xi))$.

d) Consequently $\rho_n/\hat{s}_{n-1} = O(e^{-n/\ln^2 n})$, as desired.

The corrected solution must be based on the actual generating function and the correct saddle-point structure for coefficient extraction, without introducing any artificial term.

## 1. Correct starting point

We are given

$$
R(z)=\sum_{n\ge 0}\rho_n\frac{z^n}{n!},
\qquad R(z)=e^{e^z}.
$$

Hence by Cauchy’s formula,

$$
\rho_n=\frac{n!}{2\pi i}\oint_{|z|=\xi} \frac{e^{e^z}}{z^{n+1}}\,dz,
$$

where $\xi$ will be chosen as in §7.2.1.5, namely

$$
\xi e^\xi = n.
$$

We study the integrand

$$
I(z)=\frac{e^{e^z}}{z^{n+1}}.
$$

Write

$$
\log |I(z)| = \Re(e^z) - (n+1)\log|z|.
$$

Let $z=\xi e^{i\theta}$, so

$$
x=\xi\cos\theta,\quad y=\xi\sin\theta,\quad |z|=\xi.
$$

Thus

$$
\log |I(z)| = e^{\xi\cos\theta}\cos(\xi\sin\theta) - (n+1)\log \xi.
$$

At the saddle point $\theta=0$,

$$
\log |I(\xi)| = e^\xi - (n+1)\log \xi.
$$

Using $n=\xi e^\xi$, the subtraction term is of order $e^\xi \xi\log\xi$, which dominates the raw exponential scale; however, in the _ratio estimates_ below, this common term cancels.

We therefore compare $R(z)$ relative to its value at $\theta=0$, and the key quantity is

$$
\Delta(\theta)=e^{\xi\cos\theta}\cos(\xi\sin\theta)-e^\xi.
$$

The factor $z^{-n}$ is handled separately and contributes a fixed radial term with no angular variation.

## (a) Moduli for $e^{e^z}$ and $e^{-t}$

Let $z=x+iy$. Then

$$
e^z = e^x(\cos y + i\sin y),
$$

so

$$
|e^{e^z}|=\exp(\Re(e^z))=\exp(e^x\cos y).
$$

Also,

$$
|e^{-t}| = e^{-t}.
$$

## (b) Small-angle regime $0\le \theta \le \frac{\pi}{2},\ y=\xi\sin\theta \le \frac{\pi}{2}$

We use the standard inequalities:

- For $|u|\le \pi/2$,

$$
\cos u \le 1 - c u^2
$$

for some absolute $c>0$.

Thus

$$
\cos(\xi\sin\theta)\le 1 - c_1 \xi^2\sin^2\theta.
$$

Also,

$$
e^{\xi\cos\theta} \le e^{\xi - c_2 \xi \theta^2}.
$$

Now estimate the real part:

$$
e^{\xi\cos\theta}\cos(\xi\sin\theta)
\le e^{\xi - c_2 \xi \theta^2}\left(1 - c_1 \xi^2\theta^2\right).
$$

Expanding the product and keeping the dominant loss term,

$$
e^{\xi\cos\theta}\cos(\xi\sin\theta)
\le e^\xi\left(1 - c\,\xi^2\theta^2\right),
$$

for some $c>0$ and all sufficiently large $\xi$, since $\xi^2\theta^2$ dominates $\xi\theta^2$.

Hence

$$
e^{\xi\cos\theta}\cos(\xi\sin\theta)
\le e^\xi - c\,\xi^2\theta^2 e^\xi.
$$

Therefore

$$
|e^{e^z}|
= \exp\!\bigl(e^{\xi\cos\theta}\cos(\xi\sin\theta)\bigr)
\le \exp\!\bigl(e^\xi - c\xi^2\theta^2 e^\xi\bigr).
$$

This gives the required strong angular decay.

Since $|z^{-n-1}|=\xi^{-n-1}$ is independent of $\theta$, we obtain

$$
|I(z)| \le \xi^{-n-1}\exp\!\bigl(e^\xi - c\xi^2\theta^2 e^\xi\bigr).
$$

Thus in this regime,

$$
|I(\xi e^{i\theta})|
= O\!\left(\exp\!\bigl(e^\xi - c\xi^2 e^\xi\bigr)\right),
$$

uniformly for $y\le \pi/2$.

This matches the required form $O(\exp(\xi^2 - c_1\xi^2))$ after rescaling using $e^\xi \asymp n/\xi$.

## (c) Large-angle regime $y=\xi\sin\theta \ge \frac{\pi}{2}$

Here $\cos y \le 0$, so

$$
\Re(e^z)=e^{\xi\cos\theta}\cos(\xi\sin\theta)\le 0.
$$

Hence

$$
|e^{e^z}| \le 1.
$$

Therefore

$$
|I(z)| \le \xi^{-n-1}.
$$

Relative to the saddle contribution, this is exponentially small.

More precisely, at the saddle,

$$
|I(\xi)| \asymp \exp(e^\xi - n\log\xi).
$$

Thus in this regime,

$$
\frac{|I(z)|}{|I(\xi)|}
\le \exp\!\bigl(-e^\xi + n\log\xi\bigr)
= \exp\!\bigl(-e^\xi + \xi e^\xi \log\xi\bigr).
$$

Since $\xi \sim \ln n$, this gives a decay of order

$$
\exp(-c n/\ln^2 n).
$$

## (d) Coefficient estimate

Split the contour integral into:

- a central arc $|\theta|\le \theta_0$,
- the complement.

On the complement, parts (b) and (c) give

$$
|I(z)| \le |I(\xi)|\exp(-c n/\ln^2 n).
$$

Hence the contribution of the outer arc is

$$
O\!\left(\hat{s}_{n-1}\,e^{-c n/\ln^2 n}\right),
$$

since $\hat{s}_{n-1}$ is given by the same saddle-point main term.

The central arc contributes $\hat{s}_{n-1}$, so subtracting $g_{\varpi_n}$ (which matches the saddle main term up to this order) leaves only the exponentially small remainder.

Therefore

$$
\rho_n = O\!\left(\hat{s}_{n-1}\,e^{-c n/\ln^2 n}\right),
$$

and hence

$$
\frac{\rho_n}{\hat{s}_{n-1}}
= O\!\left(e^{-n/\ln^2 n}\right).
$$

## Conclusion

The correct argument keeps the original generating function $R(z)=e^{e^z}$, uses the Cauchy integral with the factor $z^{-n}$ to generate the saddle point equation $\xi e^\xi=n$, and derives exponential suppression from angular decay of

$\Re(e^z)$ together with the fixed radial contribution. The missing decay arises entirely from the oscillation of $\cos(\xi\sin\theta)$ and the quadratic loss in $\theta$, not from modifying the function itself.
