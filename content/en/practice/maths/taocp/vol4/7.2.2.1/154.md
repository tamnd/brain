**Exercise 154.** [*M30*] (C. R. J. Singleton, 1982.) After twelve days of Christmas, the person who sings a popular carol has received twelve partridges in pear trees, plus eleven pairs of humming birds, . . . , plus one set of twelve drummers drumming, from his or her true love. Therefore an "authentic" partridge puzzle should try to pack $(n+1-k)$ squares of size $k \times k$, for $1 \le k \le n$, into a box that contains $n^2 = n \cdot 1^2 + (n-1) \cdot 2^2 + \cdots + 1 \cdot n^2$ cells. For which values of $n$ is $P(n)$ a perfect square?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 2m14s  

---

## Setup

Let

$P(n)=n\cdot 1^2+(n-1)\cdot 2^2+\cdots+1\cdot n^2.$

Reindexing with $k=1,2,\ldots,n$ gives

$P(n)=\sum_{k=1}^n (n+1-k)k^2.$

The task is to determine all positive integers $n$ for which $P(n)$ is a perfect square.

## Solution

Expand the sum:

$P(n)=(n+1)\sum_{k=1}^n k^2-\sum_{k=1}^n k^3.$

Use the standard identities

$\sum_{k=1}^n k^2=\frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}, \qquad \sum_{k=1}^n k^3=\frac{n^2(n+1)^2}{4}.$

Substitution yields

$P(n)=(n+1)\cdot \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}-\frac{n^2(n+1)^2}{4}.$

Factor $n(n+1)^2$:

$P(n)=n(n+1)^2\left(\frac{2n+1}{6}-\frac{n}{4}\right).$

Compute the bracket:

$\frac{2n+1}{6}-\frac{n}{4}=\frac{4(2n+1)-6n}{24}=\frac{8n+4-6n}{24}=\frac{2n+4}{24}=\frac{n+2}{12}.$

Thus

$P(n)=\frac{n(n+1)^2(n+2)}{12}.$

Since $(n+1)^2$ is a perfect square,

$P(n)\text{ is a square} \iff \frac{n(n+2)}{12}\text{ is a square}.$

Let

$\frac{n(n+2)}{12}=t^2$

for some integer $t\ge 0$. Then

$n(n+2)=12t^2.$

Rewrite the left-hand side:

$n(n+2)=(n+1)^2-1.$

Hence

$(n+1)^2-1=12t^2,$

so

$(n+1)^2-12t^2=1.$

Let $x=n+1$ and $y=t$. Then

$x^2-12y^2=1.$

This is the Pell equation with fundamental solution $(x_1,y_1)=(7,2)$ since

$7^2-12\cdot 2^2=49-48=1.$

All positive solutions are generated by

$x+y\sqrt{12}=(7+2\sqrt{12})^k,\quad k\ge 0.$

Since $n=x-1$, all solutions for $n$ are given by

$n=(x_k-1)\quad \text{where } x_k \text{ satisfies } x_k^2-12y_k^2=1.$

The first values are:

- $k=0$: $x=1$, $n=0$
- $k=1$: $x=7$, $n=6$
- $k=2$: $x=97$, $n=96$

Thus all positive $n$ for which $P(n)$ is a perfect square are exactly those for which $n+1$ is the $x$-component of a solution of

$x^2-12y^2=1.$

Therefore

$\boxed{n=6,\,96,\,1350,\,\ldots}$

## Verification

From $x^2-12y^2=1$, setting $x=n+1$ gives

$n(n+2)=12y^2,$

and substituting into

$P(n)=\frac{n(n+1)^2(n+2)}{12}$

yields

$P(n)=(n+1)^2y^2,$

which is a perfect square.

Conversely, if $P(n)$ is a square, then $\frac{n(n+2)}{12}$ must be a square because $(n+1)^2$ is a square factor in $P(n)$, which forces $n(n+2)$ to equal $12t^2$ and hence $(n+1)^2-12t^2=1$.
