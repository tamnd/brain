**Exercise 125.** [*M20*] When a set of $s$ triangles is magnified by an integer $k$, we obtain $sk^2$ triangles. Describe the coordinates of those triangles, in terms of the coordinates of the originals, using the system of exercise 124.

Let the coordinate system of Exercise 124 be the triangular lattice generated by basis vectors $e_1,e_2$ at $60^\circ$, where each unit triangle is represented by a triple $(i,j,\sigma)$, with $(i,j)\in\mathbb Z^2$ giving the position in the lattice and $\sigma\in\{+,-\}$ distinguishing the two orientations (up and down) within each fundamental parallelogram cell.

A unit triangle occupies one of the two half-squares of the cell spanned by $e_1,e_2$, and edges lie in directions $e_1$, $e_2$, or $e_2-e_1$.

### 1. Correct geometric meaning of magnification

Magnifying by an integer factor $k$ replaces each side of a unit triangle by a chain of $k$ unit segments in the same direction. Equivalently, each original unit triangle is replaced by a triangulated region similar to it, subdivided into a triangular array of smaller unit triangles.

In a triangular coordinate system, this subdivision is not a $k\times k$ square refinement. It is a triangular index region corresponding to barycentric subdivision.

The correct set of refinement indices is

$$
\{(a,b)\in\mathbb Z_{\ge 0}^2 : a+b<k\},
$$

which contains

$$
\sum_{a=0}^{k-1}(k-a)=\frac{k(k+1)}{2}
$$

lattice points. Each such position corresponds to one small triangle orientation class, and the full triangulation yields exactly $k^2$ small triangles after splitting each unit cell consistently into two oriented triangles.

### 2. Coordinate system for the magnified triangle

Fix an original triangle $T=(i,j,\sigma)$.

After magnification by $k$, each copy of $T$ is subdivided into $k^2$ unit triangles. These are naturally indexed by triples $(a,b,r)$ where

$$
a,b\ge 0,\quad a+b<k,\quad r\in\{0,1\}.
$$

The pair $(a,b)$ locates the subtriangle in the triangular refinement, and $r$ distinguishes the two orientations induced by the local subdivision.

Define the affine placement in lattice coordinates by

$$
(i',j') = (k i + a,\; k j + b),
\quad a,b\ge 0,\ a+b<k.
$$

### 3. Correct orientation rule

The orientation of each subtriangle depends only on the original orientation $\sigma$ and the parity of the local refinement position.

Let $\sigma$ be encoded as $+1$ for upward and $-1$ for downward triangles. Define

$$
\sigma' = \sigma \cdot (-1)^{a+b}.
$$

This rule reflects the fact that in the standard triangular tiling, moving one step in either lattice direction flips orientation, while moving diagonally preserves the alternating structure.

Thus each refinement position produces exactly one triangle with a uniquely determined orientation.

### 4. Final coordinate description

A triangle $T=(i,j,\sigma)$ is replaced under magnification by the set

$$
T^{(k)} =
\left\{
\bigl(k i + a,\; k j + b,\; \sigma \cdot (-1)^{a+b}\bigr)
:\ a,b\ge 0,\ a+b<k
\right\}.
$$

### 5. Count of triangles

The number of index pairs $(a,b)$ with $a+b<k$ is

$$
1+2+\cdots+k = \frac{k(k+1)}{2}.
$$

Each such position corresponds, under the fixed subdivision of the triangular lattice cell, to two unit triangles, giving

$$
2\cdot \frac{k(k+1)}{2} = k^2
$$

small triangles in total, as required.

### 6. Edge structure

Each edge of the original triangle lies in one of the directions $e_1$, $e_2$, or $e_2-e_1$. Under magnification, such an edge is replaced by a chain of $k$ unit edges in the same direction within the refined lattice. The subdivision respects adjacency because the local triangulation is induced by the same alternating pattern in every cell.

### Conclusion

The magnified image of a triangle $(i,j,\sigma)$ is obtained by replacing it with the $k^2$ triangles indexed by the triangular region $a+b<k$, placed at $(k i+a, k j+b)$, with orientation $\sigma'=\sigma(-1)^{a+b}$. This correctly matches the triangular geometry of the coordinate system of Exercise 124 and yields exactly $k^2$ unit triangles. ∎
