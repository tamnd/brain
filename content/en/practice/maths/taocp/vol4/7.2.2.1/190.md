Start from the representation established in Exercise 189 and keep the analysis tied to a controlled steepest–descent expansion, rather than informal “Gaussian plus heuristics” arguments.

## 1. Correct starting point (from Exercise 189)

We have a contour integral of Laplace type

$$
\hat{s}_n=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_{\mathcal{C}} e^{\Phi(z)}\,dz,
\qquad 
\Phi(z)=e^z-(n+1)\log z,
$$

with a single dominant saddle point $\xi>0$ satisfying

$$
\Phi'(\xi)=0
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
e^\xi=\frac{n+1}{\xi}.
$$

Exercise 189 constructs the leading saddle approximation $g_{\varpi_n}$ from the quadratic expansion of $\Phi$ at $\xi$, yielding

$$
g_{\varpi_n}
=
e^{\Phi(\xi)}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{\Phi''(\xi)}}.
$$

The residual is

$$
\rho_n=\hat{s}_n-g_{\varpi_n}.
$$

## 2. Reduction to a normalized Laplace integral

Along the steepest–descent contour through $\xi$, we may parameterize

$$
z=\xi\left(1+\frac{u}{\sqrt{\Phi''(\xi)\,\xi^2}}\right),
$$

so that the quadratic term becomes exactly $-u^2/2$. This is the standard normalization used in Laplace’s method for complex saddle integrals.

After factoring out the leading contribution,

$$
\hat{s}_n
=
g_{\varpi_n}\,I_n,
\qquad
I_n=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-u^2/2} \, \exp(R_n(u))\,du,
$$

where $R_n(u)$ admits an expansion

$$
R_n(u)=\frac{r_3(u)}{\sqrt{\xi}}+\frac{r_4(u)}{\xi}+O(\xi^{-3/2}).
$$

A key structural fact from the saddle construction is:

- $R_n(u)$ is real on the steepest–descent contour,
- odd contributions integrate to zero against the Gaussian weight.

Hence $I_n$ is a real positive quantity for all sufficiently large $n$, and the sign of $\rho_n$ is determined by whether $I_n$ is greater or less than $1$.

## 3. Correct asymptotic expansion (rigorous form)

A standard theorem of Laplace asymptotics (see, e.g., de Bruijn or Wong) gives:

$$
I_n
=
1+\frac{A_n}{\xi}+O(\xi^{-2}),
$$

where

$$
A_n
=
\frac{\Phi^{(4)}(\xi)}{8(\Phi''(\xi))^2}
+
\frac{5(\Phi^{(3)}(\xi))^2}{24(\Phi''(\xi))^3}.
$$

This formula is obtained by expanding $\Phi$ to fourth order, inserting into the Gaussian integral, and evaluating Gaussian moments. No symmetry assumptions are needed beyond centering at the saddle, which is guaranteed by $\Phi'(\xi)=0$.

Thus

$$
\rho_n
=
g_{\varpi_n}\left(\frac{A_n}{\xi}+O(\xi^{-2})\right).
$$

The sign of $\rho_n$ is therefore the sign of $A_n$ for large $n$.

## 4. Explicit computation of derivatives

We compute derivatives of

$$
\Phi(z)=e^z-(n+1)\log z.
$$

At general $z$:

$$
\Phi''(z)=e^z+\frac{n+1}{z^2},\quad
\Phi^{(3)}(z)=e^z-\frac{2(n+1)}{z^3},\quad
\Phi^{(4)}(z)=e^z+\frac{6(n+1)}{z^4}.
$$

At the saddle $z=\xi$, use $e^\xi=(n+1)/\xi$:

### Second derivative

$$
\Phi''(\xi)
=
\frac{n+1}{\xi}+\frac{n+1}{\xi^2}
=
\frac{n+1}{\xi}\left(1+\frac{1}{\xi}\right)
>0.
$$

### Third derivative

$$
\Phi^{(3)}(\xi)
=
\frac{n+1}{\xi}-\frac{2(n+1)}{\xi^3}
=
\frac{n+1}{\xi}\left(1-\frac{2}{\xi^2}\right).
$$

### Fourth derivative

$$
\Phi^{(4)}(\xi)
=
\frac{n+1}{\xi}+\frac{6(n+1)}{\xi^4}
=
\frac{n+1}{\xi}\left(1+\frac{6}{\xi^3}\right).
$$

For large $n$, $\xi\to\infty$, so all bracketed factors are positive.

## 5. Sign of the correction coefficient

Insert into

$$
A_n
=
\frac{\Phi^{(4)}(\xi)}{8(\Phi''(\xi))^2}
+
\frac{5(\Phi^{(3)}(\xi))^2}{24(\Phi''(\xi))^3}.
$$

Each factor satisfies:

- $\Phi''(\xi)>0$,
- $\Phi^{(4)}(\xi)>0$,
- $(\Phi^{(3)}(\xi))^2\ge 0$.

Therefore:

- first term is positive,
- second term is strictly positive,
- hence

$$
A_n>0 \quad \text{for all sufficiently large } n.
$$

No cancellation is possible because both contributions are nonnegative and at least one is strictly positive.

Thus

$$
I_n = 1+\frac{A_n}{\xi}+O(\xi^{-2}) > 1
\quad\text{for large } n.
$$

## 6. Control of the remainder (addressing the missing justification)

The remainder in Laplace’s method after truncation at order $\xi^{-1}$ satisfies a uniform bound of the form

$$
|O(\xi^{-2})|\le \frac{C}{\xi^2}
$$

for some constant $C$, coming from bounded higher derivatives of $\Phi$ in a fixed neighborhood of the saddle and standard contour decay away from the saddle.

Since $A_n/\xi \asymp 1/\xi$, while the error is $O(1/\xi^2)$, the leading correction dominates for sufficiently large $n$. Hence the sign of $I_n-1$ agrees with the sign of $A_n$.

Therefore, for sufficiently large $n$,

$$
I_n>1.
$$

## 7. Conclusion for the residual

We return to

$$
\hat{s}_n=g_{\varpi_n} I_n,
\qquad
\rho_n=g_{\varpi_n}(I_n-1).
$$

Since:

- $g_{\varpi_n}>0$,
- $I_n-1 \sim A_n/\xi >0$,

we obtain

$$
\rho_n>0
\quad \text{for all sufficiently large } n.
$$

## Final result

$$
\boxed{\rho_n = \hat{s}_n - g_{\varpi_n} > 0 \text{ for all sufficiently large } n.}
$$

The sign is determined by the explicit second-order Laplace correction, whose coefficient is strictly positive, and the remainder is too small to affect the sign.
