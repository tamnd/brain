**Exercise 158.** [*25*] [25] Another variation of the partridge puzzle when $2 \le n \le 7$ asks for the *smallest rectangular area* that will contain $k$ nonoverlapping squares of size $k \times k$ for $1 \le k \le n$. For example, here are solutions for $n = 2$, 3, and 4:

![Figure: smallest rectangular area solutions for n=2,3,4](figure_158.png)

(To show optimality for $n = 4$ one must prove that rectangles of sizes $6 \times 17$, $8 \times 13$, $5 \times 21$, and $7 \times 15$ are too small.) Solve this puzzle for $n = 5$, 6, and 7.

## Solution

For a fixed $k$, each of the $k$ squares has side length $k$, hence area $k^2$. Any rectangle containing $k$ nonoverlapping such squares must therefore have area at least $k \cdot k^2 = k^3$.

Let the containing rectangle have side lengths $x$ and $y$. Each square has side length $k$, so along each side of the rectangle, squares can only be placed in a grid aligned in multiples of $k$. Let $x = a k$ and $y = b k$ for integers $a, b \ge 1$, since each square occupies an interval of length $k$ in both directions and boundaries must align to avoid overlap and wasted partial slots. Then at most $ab$ squares can be placed, and the condition that all $k$ squares fit implies

$ab \ge k.$

The area of the rectangle is

$xy = (ak)(bk) = k^2 ab \ge k^2 \cdot k = k^3.$

Equality holds exactly when $ab = k$. In this case, the rectangle is tiled by an $a \times b$ array of $k \times k$ squares with no unused slots, so $k$ squares fit and the area is minimal. If $ab > k$, then $xy = k^2 ab > k^3$, so no such configuration is optimal.

Thus the minimal area is $\boxed{k^3}$, and all optimal rectangles correspond precisely to factorisations $k = ab$ with dimensions $(ak) \times (bk)$.

For $n = 5, 6, 7$, the optimal rectangles for each $k$ are as follows.

For $k = 5$, the only factorisations are $1 \cdot 5$ and $5 \cdot 1$, giving rectangles

$5 \times 25,\quad 25 \times 5.$

For $k = 6$, the factorisations are $1 \cdot 6$, $2 \cdot 3$, $3 \cdot 2$, and $6 \cdot 1$, giving rectangles

$6 \times 36,\quad 12 \times 18,\quad 18 \times 12,\quad 36 \times 6.$

For $k = 7$, the factorisations are $1 \cdot 7$ and $7 \cdot 1$, giving rectangles

$7 \times 49,\quad 49 \times 7.$

This completes the determination of all optimal rectangles for $n = 5, 6, 7$. ∎
