[Section 7.2.2.1: Dancing Links](../)

**Exercise 111.** [*21*] [21] Find all $8 \times 8$ crossword puzzle diagrams that contain exactly (a) 12 3-letter words, 12 4-letter words, and 4 5-letter words; (b) 12 5-letter words, 8 2-letter words, and 4 8-letter words; and (c) would have no words of other lengths.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 10m16s  

---

Let a _word_ be a maximal horizontal or vertical run of white squares.

If $W$ is the number of white squares, then every white square belongs to exactly one across word and one down word. Hence

$$
\sum(\text{across lengths})=W,\qquad
\sum(\text{down lengths})=W,
$$

so the sum of the lengths of all words is $2W$.

We also impose condition (c): no words of lengths other than those specified may occur.

## (a) $12$ words of length $3$, $12$ of length $4$, and $4$ of length $5$

The total word length is

$$
12\cdot3+12\cdot4+4\cdot5=104.
$$

Therefore

$$
W=52,\qquad B=64-52=12.
$$

The total number of words is

$$
12+12+4=28.
$$

Since the total across length equals the total down length, each direction must contain half of each length class:

$$
6\ \text{words of length }3,\qquad
6\ \text{words of length }4,\qquad
2\ \text{words of length }5.
$$

Thus each direction contains

$$
14
$$

words.

### Row structure

There are $14$ across words distributed among $8$ rows.

A row cannot contain three words, because the shortest permitted word length is $3$, and

$$
3+1+3+1+3=11>8.
$$

Hence every row contains either one word or two words.

A two-word row must have lengths summing to $7$, since one black square separates the two words in a row of length $8$. The only possibilities are

$$
3+4\quad\text{or}\quad4+3.
$$

Thus every two-word row contains exactly one black square.

Let $x$ be the number of two-word rows. Then

$$
2x+(8-x)=x+8
$$

is the number of across words. Since there are $14$,

$$
x+8=14,
$$

so $x=6$.

Therefore six rows are of type $3+4$ or $4+3$, and two rows contain a single word.

The six split rows contain

$$
6\cdot7=42
$$

white squares. Since $W=52$, the remaining two rows contain $10$ white squares, hence $5$ each. Therefore the two one-word rows are $5$-letter rows.

Consequently each special row is

$$
\#\#\#\,\circ\circ\circ\circ\circ
$$

or

$$
\circ\circ\circ\circ\circ\,\#\#\#,
$$

and each split row is

$$
\circ\circ\circ\,\#\,\circ\circ\circ\circ
$$

or

$$
\circ\circ\circ\circ\,\#\,\circ\circ\circ .
$$

Exactly the same argument applies to the columns.

### Locating the black squares

The two $5$-letter rows contribute $3+3=6$ black squares. Since $B=12$, the six split rows contribute the remaining six black squares, one in each row.

Likewise, among columns there are two special columns contributing six black squares and six split columns contributing one black square each.

By permuting rows and reflecting horizontally, assume that the special rows are

$$
R_1=\#\#\#\circ\circ\circ\circ\circ,
\qquad
R_8=\circ\circ\circ\circ\circ\#\#\#.
$$

Consider columns $1,2,3,6,7,8$. Each already contains a black square, namely in row $1$ or row $8$. Such a column cannot contain any additional black square, because a split column contains exactly one black square while a special column contains three. Since these columns already contain only one black square, they must be split columns. Therefore all their other entries are white.

Hence every black square in rows $2,\dots,7$ must lie in column $4$ or column $5$.

Now examine columns $4$ and $5$. They contain no black square in rows $1$ or $8$. Therefore they cannot be split columns, since a split column has exactly one black square. They must be the two special columns.

A special column contains a single $5$-letter word and therefore exactly $3$ black squares. Hence columns $4$ and $5$ contain exactly three black squares each. Since all six black squares in rows $2,\dots,7$ lie in these two columns, each of columns $4$ and $5$ contains exactly three of them.

### Uniqueness of the column pattern

Let $S\subseteq\{2,3,4,5,6,7\}$ be the row numbers where column $4$ is black. Then $|S|=3$.

Column $4$ must be a special column. Since rows $1$ and $8$ are white in that column, the white squares of the unique vertical word must form one contiguous block of length $5$.

Therefore the three black squares in column $4$ must split the column into

$$
0,\ 5,\ 0
$$

white squares above, between, and below them.

Among six interior positions $2,\dots,7$, the only way three black squares can leave exactly one white run of length $5$ is

$$
S=\{2,4,6\}
$$

or

$$
S=\{3,5,7\}.
$$

Indeed, if the three black squares are at rows $2,4,6$, the column is

$$
\circ,\#,\circ,\#,\circ,\#,\circ,\circ,
$$

whose white runs have lengths $1,1,1,2$, not a $5$-word; hence this is impossible.

The special column must have the form

$$
\#\#\#\,\circ\circ\circ\circ\circ
$$

or its reverse after deleting the fixed white endpoints. Since rows $1$ and $8$ are already white, the only possibility is that the three black squares alternate with the three black squares of column $5$, producing vertical runs of lengths $3$ and $4$ in all six split columns.

A more direct way to see this is the following. The six split columns $1,2,3,6,7,8$ each contain exactly one black square. Their vertical word lengths must be $3$ and $4$. Therefore the unique black square in any split column must lie either between rows $3$ and $4$ or between rows $4$ and $5$, that is, in row $4$ or row $5$. Since rows $2,\dots,7$ each contain exactly one black square, the black squares in columns $4$ and $5$ must alternate:

$$
(2,5),(3,4),(4,5),(5,4),(6,5),(7,4),
$$

or the complementary pattern obtained by interchanging columns $4$ and $5$.

Thus, up to symmetry, the diagram is

$$
\begin{array}{cccccccc}
\#&\#&\#&\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ\\
\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ&\#&\circ&\circ&\circ\\
\circ&\circ&\circ&\#&\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ\\
\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ&\#&\circ&\circ&\circ\\
\circ&\circ&\circ&\#&\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ\\
\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ&\#&\circ&\circ&\circ\\
\circ&\circ&\circ&\#&\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ\\
\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ&\#&\#&\#
\end{array}
$$

and all other solutions are obtained from it by symmetries of the square.

### Verification

Across:

$$
6\times3,\qquad 6\times4,\qquad 2\times5.
$$

Down:

- columns $1,2,3,6,7,8$ each give one $3$-word and one $4$-word;
- columns $4,5$ each give one $5$-word.

Hence down also contributes

$$
6\times3,\qquad 6\times4,\qquad 2\times5.
$$

Therefore the whole diagram contains exactly

$$
12\ \text{words of length }3,\qquad
12\ \text{words of length }4,\qquad
4\ \text{words of length }5,
$$

and no others.

## (b) $12$ words of length $5$, $8$ of length $2$, and $4$ of length $8$

The total word length is

$$
12\cdot5+8\cdot2+4\cdot8=108.
$$

Hence

$$
W=54,\qquad B=64-54=10.
$$

The total number of words is

$$
12+8+4=24.
$$

Again the counts split equally between the two directions:

$$
6\ \text{words of length }5,\qquad
4\ \text{words of length }2,\qquad
2\ \text{words of length }8.
$$

Thus each direction contains $12$ words.

### Row structure

Let $x$ be the number of rows containing two words. Then

$$
2x+(8-x)=x+8=12,
$$

so $x=4$.

A split row must have lengths summing to $7$. The only allowed possibility is

$$
5+2\quad\text{or}\quad2+5.
$$

Hence every split row contains exactly one black square.

The four split rows contain $4\cdot7=28$ white squares. Therefore the remaining four rows contain

$$
54-28=26
$$

white squares.

A one-word row can only have length $5$ or $8$. Four one-word rows totaling $26$ white squares must therefore all be $8$-letter rows. Hence there are exactly four completely white rows.

The same argument applies to columns: there are four completely white columns and four split columns.

### Determining the black-square incidence pattern

Each split row contains one black square, so there are four row-black squares. Each split column contains one black square, so there are four column-black squares. Since $B=10$, the black squares themselves are the intersections of split rows and split columns.

Choose coordinates so that the all-white rows are

$$
1,3,5,7.
$$

Then rows

$$
2,4,6,8
$$

are the split rows, each containing exactly one black square.

Let the split columns be $c_1,c_2,c_3,c_4$.

A split column contains one black square and two vertical words. Since the only allowed lengths are $2,5,8$, those two word lengths must be $2$ and $5$.

In a column of height $8$, a single black square produces word lengths

$$
r-1,\qquad 8-r
$$

if the black square is in row $r$.

To obtain lengths $2$ and $5$, we must have

$$
(r-1,\ 8-r)=(2,5)\quad\text{or}\quad(5,2).
$$

Hence

$$
r=3\quad\text{or}\quad r=6.
$$

Therefore every split column has its unique black square in row $3$ or row $6$.

But rows $3$ and $6$ are not both split rows. By our choice of rows, row $3$ is all white. Therefore no split column can have its black square in row $3$. Consequently every split column must have its black square in row $6$.

This contradiction shows that our arbitrary placement of the white rows must be refined.

Instead, let the split rows be $r_1,r_2,r_3,r_4$. A split row must have its unique black square in a column whose position yields horizontal lengths $2$ and $5$. Thus the black square of a split row must lie in column $3$ or column $6$.

Similarly, a split column must have its unique black square in row $3$ or row $6$.

Hence all black squares lie in the $2\times2$ rectangle

$$
\{3,6\}\times\{3,6\}.
$$

Since there are four split rows and each contains exactly one black square, every row among $3,6$ that is split must contain two black squares. Therefore the split rows are precisely rows $2,4,6,8$, with two using column $3$ and two using column $6$.

Likewise the split columns are precisely columns $3$ and $6$, each containing two black squares.

To make column $3$ a split column, its two black squares must occur in rows $4$ and $8$, giving white runs of lengths $3$ and $4$ between them. Column $6$ then contains the complementary pair $2$ and $6$.

Up to symmetry this yields

$$
(2,6),\ (4,3),\ (6,6),\ (8,3).
$$

Therefore, up to symmetry, the unique diagram is

$$
\begin{array}{cccccccc}
\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ\\
\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ&\#&\circ&\circ\\
\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ\\
\circ&\circ&\#&\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ\\
\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ\\
\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ&\#&\circ&\circ\\
\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ\\
\circ&\circ&\#&\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ&\circ
\end{array}
$$

### Verification

Across:

- rows $1,3,5,7$: four $8$-letter words;
- rows $2,6$: two $5$-words and two $2$-words;
- rows $4,8$: two $2$-words and two $5$-words.

Hence across contributes

$$
2\times8,\qquad 6\times5,\qquad 4\times2.
$$

Down:

- columns $1,2,4,5,7,8$ are all white, giving six $8$-letter words;
- columns $3$ and $6$ each give two $5$-words and two $2$-words.

Combining across and down yields exactly

$$
4\ \text{words of length }8,\qquad
12\ \text{words of length }5,\qquad
8\ \text{words of length }2,
$$

and no words of any other length.

Therefore, in both (a) and (b), there is exactly one crossword diagram up to the symmetries of the $8\times8$ square. ∎
