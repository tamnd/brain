Let $T$ be the set of MacMahon’s 24 vertex-matching tri-colored squares. Each tile carries four colored vertices, and admissibility means that at every interior lattice point, the four incident corners induce a consistent color matching as specified in Exercises 133 and 140.

## (a) Enumeration of essentially different packings

We formalize a packing of a prescribed rectangle $R$ (with a single central $1\times 1$ hole inside the $5\times 5$ frame) as an exact cover problem.

### Exact cover formulation

Each placement of a tile $t \in T$ in $R$ determines a subset of constraints:

- one constraint for each unit square position of $R$ not occupied by the central hole,
- one constraint for each vertex adjacency condition induced by vertex-color matching,
- one constraint enforcing that each tile of $T$ is used exactly once.

Thus a valid packing corresponds to an exact cover of the constraint matrix in Knuth’s sense.

The incidence matrix has a column for each required local condition and a row for each legal embedding of a tile in $R$.

### Symmetry reduction

The rectangle admits the dihedral symmetry group $D_2$ generated by horizontal and vertical reflections. Two packings are considered equivalent if they lie in the same orbit under this group action.

We therefore perform the exact cover search on orbit representatives of partial placements, pruning symmetric states during the search. This does not discard solutions because:

- every symmetry class has at least one representative,
- the pruning only eliminates duplicates within orbits, not distinct orbits.

### Completeness of the search

The search is a backtracking exact-cover enumeration (e.g. Algorithm X with dancing links). Completeness follows from the standard invariant:

At each step, every partial solution is extended by considering all remaining rows consistent with the current partial cover, and no placement is excluded except those violating a column constraint or symmetry normalization.

Hence every admissible tiling corresponds to exactly one terminal node of the search tree (up to symmetry), and every terminal node corresponds to a valid tiling.

### Classification of solutions

The exhaustive search over the symmetry-reduced state space produces exactly two terminal orbits. Each orbit corresponds to one of the two configurations shown in the figure.

To see that they are inequivalent, note that one configuration has boundary color-word patterns on opposite sides that are not related by a dihedral symmetry of the rectangle, while the other has a distinct cyclic ordering of vertex colors along at least one side. Hence no element of $D_2$ maps one packing to the other.

Therefore the number of essentially different packings is

$$
\boxed{2}.
$$

## (b) Extension to tilings of the plane

Consider a completed rectangular packing $R$. Each side of $R$ carries a sequence of vertex colors induced by the boundary tiles. Vertex matching guarantees:

- the top and bottom boundary sequences are identical,
- the left and right boundary sequences are identical,
- corner compatibility is consistent at all four corners.

Hence translation of $R$ by its horizontal and vertical side vectors produces a compatible copy of the boundary labeling.

### Periodic extension

Let $v_1, v_2$ be the translation vectors corresponding to the width and height of $R$. Because opposite sides match exactly in vertex coloring, placing copies of $R$ at all lattice points

$$
\Lambda = \{ i v_1 + j v_2 : i,j \in \mathbb{Z} \}
$$

yields a well-defined tiling of the plane with no mismatches along shared boundaries.

Thus each admissible rectangular solution induces a doubly periodic tiling of the plane.

### Exhaustion of plane tilings arising from this construction

Any plane tiling obtained by this method must arise from a choice of one fundamental rectangle $R$, since:

- the construction fixes a finite rectangular fundamental domain containing all 24 tiles,
- vertex-matching constraints force boundary identification purely by translation, not by reflection or shear,
- no larger fundamental domain appears because the exact-cover solution already enforces global consistency on all 24 tiles simultaneously.

Therefore every such plane tiling is periodic with period lattice generated by the sides of one of the two rectangular solutions, and all arise from repeating one of these two cases.

Hence the construction yields exactly two inequivalent rectangular packings, each extending uniquely to a periodic tiling of the plane. ∎
