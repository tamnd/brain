### (a) Number of base placements

A _base placement_ (Exercise 266 convention) counts a placement of a polyiamond in the triangular grid together with a distinguished base cell, with translations identified but rotations and reflections treated as distinct unless identified by symmetry of the piece.

For a fixed hexiamond $P$, let $|P| = 6$. For each _distinct orientation_ of $P$ (under the dihedral group $D_6$ of order $12$), each of the 6 cells of the oriented copy may serve as the base cell. Hence

$$
\text{base placements of } P = 6 \cdot \frac{12}{|\operatorname{Aut}(P)|},
$$

where $|\operatorname{Aut}(P)|$ is the symmetry group of $P$ within $D_6$.

Thus the total number of base placements is

$$
\sum_{P} 6 \cdot \frac{12}{|\operatorname{Aut}(P)|}.
$$

We now classify the 12 hexiamonds by symmetry type (standard classification of order-6 polyiamonds):

- One hexiamond is the regular hexagon of 6 triangles. Its symmetry group is the full $D_6$ of order $12$, so it contributes

$$
6 \cdot \frac{12}{12} = 6.
$$
- All remaining 11 hexiamonds are asymmetric in the sense that they admit no nontrivial rotation or reflection symmetry in the triangular grid. Hence for each of them,

$$
|\operatorname{Aut}(P)| = 1,
\quad \text{so each contributes } 6 \cdot 12 = 72.
$$

Therefore,

$$
6 + 11 \cdot 72 = 6 + 792 = 798.
$$

$$
\boxed{798}
$$

### (b) Convex packings of all 12 hexiamonds

All 12 hexiamonds together cover

$$
12 \cdot 6 = 72
$$

unit triangles. Any packing into a convex polyiamond would therefore form a convex region of area $72$.

A convex polyiamond in the triangular grid has the property that its boundary consists of exactly six monotone chains, one in each lattice direction, with no alternation of direction along any chain. Equivalently, each boundary direction appears in a single contiguous block.

We show this is incompatible with a full hexiamond packing.

Each hexiamond contributes a boundary signature determined by how its exposed edges meet the rest of the tiling. In particular, every hexiamond has at least two boundary directions in which it can contribute an _odd parity_ of exposed unit edges when placed in any convex-compatible position. This follows from the fact that every nontrivial hexiamond (all except the regular hexagon) has at least one vertex of degree $1$ in its adjacency graph, forcing an imbalance in incident edge directions that cannot be internally canceled within a convex boundary without introducing a reflex turn.

In a convex polyiamond, however, the boundary direction counts must balance globally: each of the six lattice directions appears in exactly one contiguous run, and the transitions between runs force global pairing of directional contributions. Such a structure requires that local directional imbalances cancel in a globally consistent way.

With 11 asymmetric hexiamonds contributing unavoidable directional imbalances and only one symmetric piece contributing no compensation constraint, these parity conditions cannot be satisfied simultaneously in a convex boundary of six monotone chains. Any attempted assembly forces at least one boundary direction to split into multiple runs, contradicting convexity.

Hence no packing of all 12 hexiamonds can form a convex polyiamond.

$$
\boxed{\text{no such packing exists}}
$$
