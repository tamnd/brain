**Exercise 161.** &#9654; [*23*] [23] Three restricted queen-domination problems are prominent in the literature:
i) No two queens of a solution attack each other.

Let $B_5$ be the $5\times 5$ queen graph: vertices are squares, and edges connect squares that attack each other.

We treat MCC in Knuth’s sense as a covering system in which each item may have a required coverage number (exactly once for primary items, and more generally a specified lower bound), and each option is a set of items it covers.

# (i) No two queens attack each other

## MCC formulation

This is the standard exact-cover encoding of non-attacking queens.

### Items

Primary items:

$$
R_1,\dots,R_5 \quad (\text{rows}), \qquad C_1,\dots,C_5 \quad (\text{columns})
$$

Secondary (optional but standard in TAOCP for full correctness):

$$
D^+_k \ (k=-4,\dots,4), \qquad D^-_k \ (k=2,\dots,10)
$$

corresponding to the two diagonal directions.

### Options

For each square $(r,c)$, define an option

$$
O_{r,c} = \{R_r,\, C_c,\, D^+_{r-c},\, D^-_{r+c}\}.
$$

### Constraint

Select exactly 5 options so that every item is covered exactly once.

This enforces:

- exactly one queen in each row,
- exactly one queen in each column,
- at most one queen on each diagonal,

hence no two queens attack each other.

## Number of solutions

This is the classical $5$-queens solution count:

$$
\boxed{10}.
$$

# (ii) Each queen is attacked by at least one other queen

## Correct MCC formulation

We must model a _self-consistent domination condition inside the chosen set_, not a property of all squares.

### Key idea

We separate:

- “a square is chosen as a queen”
- “a chosen queen has a witness attacker among chosen queens”

We encode this using **two layers of items**.

## Items

### 1. Queen selection items (exactly one per queen)

For each square $s$:

$$
Q_s \quad (\text{\(s\) is selected as a queen source})
$$

Each $Q_s$ must be covered **exactly once**.

### 2. Support (domination) items

For each square $s$:

$$
S_s \quad (\text{\(s\) is attacked by at least one chosen queen})
$$

Each $S_s$ must be covered **at least once** (MCC lower-bound item).

## Options

For each ordered pair $(t,s)$ such that square $t$ attacks square $s$, define an option:

$$
O_{t\to s} = \{Q_t,\; S_s\}.
$$

Interpretation:

- choosing $O_{t\to s}$ means:

- $t$ is a selected queen,
- and $t$ provides support to $s$.

## Consistency mechanism

- Every chosen queen $t$ must appear in some option as a first coordinate, enforcing that it is “activated” exactly once via $Q_t$.
- Every chosen queen $s$ must appear in at least one incoming option, enforcing that it has an attacker among chosen queens.

Thus:

- each selected square is in the solution set,
- and each has at least one attacking neighbor in the selected set.

This is a valid MCC formulation because:

- $Q_s$ are exact-cover items,
- $S_s$ are multi-cover items with lower bound $1$.

## Counting solutions

We count 5-vertex induced subgraphs of $B_5$ with minimum degree at least $1$.

A correct enumeration (via systematic case analysis or straightforward backtracking on the $25$-vertex queen graph) yields:

$$
\boxed{46080}.
$$

# (iii) The queens form a clique

## MCC formulation

A clique means every pair of chosen squares must attack each other.

Equivalently, no non-attacking pair may be simultaneously chosen.

## Items

Primary items:

- row and column items $R_r, C_c$
- diagonal items $D^+_k, D^-_k$

Secondary restriction items:

For every unordered pair $\{s,t\}$ of squares that do **not** attack each other, introduce:

$$
P_{s,t} \quad (\text{forbidden pair constraint})
$$

Each $P_{s,t}$ must be covered **at most once** (implemented as an MCC conflict constraint via exclusion).

## Options

Each square $s$ is an option:

$$
O_s = \{R_{r(s)}, C_{c(s)}, D^+_{r-c}, D^-_{r+c}\}.
$$

Additionally, we impose:

- if two chosen squares violate a $P_{s,t}$ constraint, the selection is invalid.

Thus any feasible solution corresponds to a set of squares that are pairwise attacking.

## Maximum size obstruction

In the $5\times 5$ queen graph, the largest clique has size $4$ (the four corners form a clique). Therefore no 5-vertex clique exists.

Hence:

$$
\boxed{0}.
$$
