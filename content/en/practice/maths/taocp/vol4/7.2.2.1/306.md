**Exercise 306.** &#9654; [*30*] (S. Grabarchuk, 1996.) In how many ways can the ten windmill dominoes be arranged so that the 20 large squares define a snake-in-the-box cycle, in the sense of exercise 172(b), and so do the 20 small squares? (For example, arrangements like

![Figure: Example windmill domino arrangements for exercise 306](figure_306_examples.png)

satisfy one snake-in-the-box condition but not the other.)

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m25s  

---

## Setup

Let $G_L$ be the graph whose vertices are the $20$ large squares in the $4 \times 5$ rectangle, with edges joining orthogonally adjacent squares. A snake-in-the-box cycle in the large layer is a Hamiltonian cycle in $G_L$.

Let $G_S$ be the graph whose vertices are the $20$ small squares. By the $45^\circ$ rotation and scaling construction, adjacency in the small layer connects centers of original squares that are diagonally adjacent in the $4 \times 5$ grid. Thus two vertices are adjacent in $G_S$ exactly when their corresponding large squares differ by a diagonal move in the original grid.

Each windmill domino occupies two adjacent vertices in $G_L$ and two adjacent vertices in $G_S$, independently. Therefore an arrangement induces a Hamiltonian cycle in $G_L$ and independently a Hamiltonian cycle in $G_S$.

The task is to determine whether there exist such arrangements and, if so, how many.

## Solution

In $G_S$, every edge joins two diagonally adjacent squares of the $4 \times 5$ grid. Such squares always have the same parity in the standard checkerboard coloring of the grid. Hence every edge of $G_S$ preserves parity, and $G_S$ decomposes into two connected components, each consisting of $10$ vertices.

A cycle that covers all $20$ vertices would have to cross between these two parity classes. No edge of $G_S$ connects the two classes, so no cycle in $G_S$ can include vertices from both components. Therefore $G_S$ contains no Hamiltonian cycle.

Since a valid windmill arrangement requires a snake-in-the-box cycle in the small layer, and no such cycle exists in $G_S$, no arrangement of windmill dominoes can satisfy the condition.

Hence the number of valid arrangements is

$\boxed{0}$

## Verification

The parity argument uses only that diagonal adjacency preserves $(i+j) \bmod 2$ on the integer grid coordinates. This is immediate since both moves $(1,1)$ and $(1,-1)$ preserve parity. The $4 \times 5$ grid contains both parity classes, so a Hamiltonian cycle would require edges between classes, contradicting the absence of such edges.

Therefore no Hamiltonian cycle exists in the small-layer graph, so no paired construction is possible.

This completes the proof. ∎
