### Key correction to part (b)

The previous solution failed because it treated all $1\times 1\times 2$ mirror-pair bricks as interchangeable. They are not. Each pair originates from a specific cube type, and those types determine the boundary face colors. Therefore, different placements of different pairs generally produce different external color patterns, even if the geometric tiling is the same.

So the classification problem is not “count domino tilings,” but “count labeled domino tilings up to symmetry of the box.”

## Structure of the 30 cubes (from Exercise 146)

The 30 cubes form 15 distinct mirror pairs. Each pair is a fixed $1\times 1\times 2$ brick with:

- a fixed internal matching face,
- fixed exterior face colors determined by that specific pair,
- no interchangeability between different pairs.

Thus we have 15 distinct domino types.

## (a) Construction of a $2 \times 3 \times 5$ brick

As in the proposed solution, tile the box by aligning all dominoes along the $x$-direction.

Index the box by

$$
(x,y,z)\in \{1,2\}\times\{1,2,3\}\times\{1,2,3,4,5\}.
$$

For each fixed $(y,z)$, place one domino covering

$$
(1,y,z)\quad \text{and}\quad (2,y,z).
$$

This produces exactly $3\cdot 5 = 15$ domino positions, matching the 15 mirror pairs.

Since each cube appears exactly once, all 30 cubes are used, and the construction is valid.

## (b) Catalog of essentially different bricks

### Step 1: What data determines a brick?

A completed brick is determined by:

1. A choice of assignment of the 15 distinct mirror pairs to the 15 domino positions of the $2\times 3\times 5$ box.
2. The geometric placement of each domino.
3. The orientation of each domino, which is fixed once the direction of pairing is chosen (here always along $x$).

Thus the only freedom is a permutation of the 15 distinct domino types across the 15 positions.

### Step 2: Effect of boundary colors

Each mirror pair has its own fixed exterior color pattern. Therefore:

- swapping two different pairs changes which colors appear at specific boundary locations,
- hence produces a generally different colored cuboid.

There is no mechanism that makes different permutations collapse to the same boundary coloring.

So distinct assignments of pairs produce distinct bricks, except when related by rigid symmetries of the box.

### Step 3: Symmetry of the $2\times 3\times 5$ box

The symmetry group of a rectangular box with three distinct side lengths consists only of independent $180^\circ$ rotations about the coordinate axes.

Thus the symmetry group has size

$$
|G| = 2^3 = 8.
$$

These symmetries act by permuting the 15 domino positions, and two configurations related by such a symmetry are considered the same brick.

### Step 4: Enumeration

We now count:

- total ways to assign 15 distinct mirror pairs to 15 positions:

$$
15!
$$

- quotient by rigid symmetries of the box:

$$
|G| = 8.
$$

No further identifications occur, because:

- all domino types are distinct,
- no additional algebraic or color symmetry identifies different assignments,
- each symmetry of the box acts faithfully on the set of configurations.

Therefore the number of essentially different bricks is

$$
\frac{15!}{8}.
$$

## Final answer

### (a)

The $2\times 3\times 5$ brick is formed by placing each of the 15 mirror pairs as a $1\times 1\times 2$ domino aligned in the $x$-direction, filling the entire box.

### (b)

Each essentially different brick corresponds to a permutation of the 15 distinct mirror pairs assigned to the 15 domino positions, modulo rigid symmetries of the box. The symmetry group has 8 elements, so the catalog size is

$$
\boxed{\frac{15!}{8}}.
$$
