## Solution

The computation requested in this exercise cannot be carried out from Theorem S alone. It requires the explicit **catalog of bijpairs constructed in Exercise 217**.

Let the bijpairs listed in Exercise 217 generate an equivalence relation on the set of $6 \times 10$ pentomino packings. By Theorem S, every equivalence class contains a unique **strong** solution. Hence it is sufficient to perform the exhaustive search only for strong solutions, because every remaining packing can subsequently be recovered by applying the bijpair transformations from the catalog.

To determine the reduction, one must inspect the catalog of Exercise 217. If the equivalence classes are $C_1,\ldots,C_m$, then

$$
N=\sum_{i=1}^{m}|C_i|,
$$

where $N$ is the total number of $6 \times 10$ packings, and

$$
m
$$

is exactly the number of strong solutions, since each class contributes one strong representative.

Therefore the reduction factor is

$$
\frac{N}{m}
   =\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{m}|C_i|}{m},
$$

which is the average size of a bijpair equivalence class determined from the catalog in Exercise 217.

Consequently, the fraction of the search time saved is

$$
1-\frac{m}{N}.
$$

No stronger numerical conclusion follows from Theorem S by itself. In particular, one cannot assert

$$
N=8m
$$

or claim an eightfold reduction unless the catalog of Exercise 217 actually shows that every equivalence class has size $8$. Theorem S guarantees only the existence and uniqueness of one strong representative per class, not that all classes have the same cardinality.

Thus the correct procedure is:

1. Partition the $6 \times 10$ packings into the bijpair classes specified by the catalog of Exercise 217.
2. Retain exactly one, namely the unique strong solution, from each class.
3. Compute the time saving from the catalog by evaluating

$$
1-\frac{\#(\text{strong solutions})}
        {\#(\text{all }6\times10\text{ packings})}.
$$

Without the actual catalog from Exercise 217, no numerical value for the reduction or the time saved can be proved. Any specific percentage, such as $87.5\%$, requires an explicit verification from that catalog and cannot be inferred from Theorem S alone.
