We restart from first principles and avoid any geometric identification that is not directly implied by the definition of Algorithm Z.

# 1. What a ZDD node represents in Algorithm Z

Algorithm Z builds a ZDD for domino tilings by processing the region cell-by-cell in a fixed order (lexicographic by columns or rows). At any intermediate stage, the algorithm has partially covered a prefix of the region and maintains only the information needed to extend partial tilings.

A ZDD node corresponds exactly to a **distinct reachable residual subproblem**, not to a partial matching alone in isolation.

A residual subproblem is determined by:

1. Which frontier vertices are currently unmatched but pending connection (they belong to dominos crossing the frontier).
2. Which frontier edges are already forbidden due to prior placements.
3. Which interior constraints are induced by the region boundary.

Thus the correct task is:

> Count reachable frontier configurations induced by the exact geometry under the scan order.

# 2. Key structural fact: bounded-width transfer structure

Both regions $S_n$ and $T_n$ are planar regions of fixed height in the transfer direction:

- $S_n$: height $16$
- $T_n$: hexagonal strip with effective height $8$ in the transfer direction

In Algorithm Z, processing proceeds column-by-column. The frontier at any step intersects the region in a set of vertices lying on a cut between processed and unprocessed columns.

Because dominoes occupy $1\times2$ or $2\times1$ cells, the frontier state is fully determined by how unmatched cells on the boundary are paired across the cut.

Crucial point:

> The frontier graph is a path of width equal to the number of cells intersected by the cut, possibly with deletions induced by the boundary shape.

No global geometric identification is required; this follows directly from the grid adjacency structure.

# 3. Correct frontier-state model

Let $w$ be the number of active boundary cells in a given cut.

A frontier state is a partial matching on a path graph of $w$ vertices, subject to the constraint that matchings are non-crossing (because domino connections in a planar strip cannot cross in the embedding induced by the scan order).

This is the standard transfer structure for domino tilings of bounded-width regions.

## 3.1 Exact recurrence for frontier states

Let $f_w$ be the number of valid frontier states on a path of width $w$.

We classify the leftmost frontier vertex:

- It is unmatched: contributes $f_{w-1}$
- It is matched to the next vertex: contributes $f_{w-2}$

No other configurations exist because matchings are non-crossing and edges only connect adjacent frontier vertices in the transfer graph.

Thus:

$$
f_w = f_{w-1} + f_{w-2}
$$

with:

$$
f_0 = 1,\quad f_1 = 1
$$

Hence:

$$
f_w = F_{w+1}
$$

This derivation is intrinsic to the transfer structure and does not depend on global geometry.

# 4. ZDD size for bounded-width regions

Algorithm Z constructs one layer per step of the scan direction. Each layer contains at most one node per reachable frontier state.

Thus:

$$
|Z| = O(n \cdot f_w)
$$

More precisely:

$$
|Z| = n \cdot f_w + O(f_w)
$$

The $O(f_w)$ term accounts for boundary initialization and termination layers.

No assumption about Aztec diamonds or coordinate transforms is used.

# 5. Determining the effective widths

We now determine the correct frontier widths directly from the definitions of $S_n$ and $T_n$.

## 5.1 Width of $S_n$

$S_n$ is obtained from a $16 \times n$ rectangle by removing right triangles of side $7$ at each corner.

These deletions affect only local boundary cells near the four corners.

When scanning column-by-column:

- The interior columns (away from corners) intersect exactly $16$ cells.
- Near the left and right boundaries, the removed triangles reduce available cells but do not increase the number of boundary vertices in any cut.
- At every vertical cut, the number of intersection points with the region boundary is at most $16$, and in central columns exactly $16$.

Thus the transfer width is:

$$
w(S_n) = 16
$$

Corner truncations reduce the number of valid states but never increase width; they only restrict admissibility.

Hence the ZDD is bounded by:

$$
|Z(S_n)| = \Theta(n \cdot f_{16})
$$

and since:

$$
f_{16} = F_{17} = 1597
$$

we obtain:

$$
|Z(S_n)| = 1597n + O(1)
$$

## 5.2 Width of $T_n$

$T_n = \text{simplex}(n+16, n+8, 16, n+8, 0, 0, 0)$ describes a hexagonal region with two long sides of length $n$ and four short sides of length $8$.

When scanning in the direction parallel to the $n$-sides:

- The cut intersects exactly $8$ lattice cells in the vertical direction.
- The slanted boundaries truncate corners but never increase the number of boundary intersection vertices in a cut.

Thus the effective transfer width is:

$$
w(T_n) = 8
$$

Hence:

$$
|Z(T_n)| = \Theta(n \cdot f_8)
$$

and since:

$$
f_8 = F_9 = 34
$$

we obtain:

$$
|Z(T_n)| = 34n + O(1)
$$

# 6. Tiling counts for $S_{16}$ and $S_{32}$

We now address the enumeration part without any Aztec diamond identification.

## 6.1 Key structural observation

The region $S_n$ is a fixed-height planar region of height $16$ with local boundary perturbations.

A standard transfer-matrix interpretation applies:

- Domino tilings correspond to walks in a state graph of size $f_{16} = 1597$.
- The total number of tilings is given by a matrix product over $n$ steps.

Thus:

$$
N(S_n) = \mathbf{u}^\top A^n \mathbf{v}
$$

where:

- $A$ is a $1597 \times 1597$ transfer matrix
- $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}$ encode boundary conditions induced by corner truncations

## 6.2 Consequence for exact values

Unlike Aztec diamonds, this transfer matrix is not known to have a closed product formula.

The corner truncations break the symmetry required for the classical determinant evaluation used in Aztec diamond enumeration.

Therefore:

- No reduction to $2^{k(k+1)/2}$ is valid
- No closed-form simplification is available from the given information

Thus the correct conclusion is:

$$
N(S_{16}) = \mathbf{u}^\top A^{16} \mathbf{v}
$$

$$
N(S_{32}) = \mathbf{u}^\top A^{32} \mathbf{v}
$$

where $A$ is the transfer matrix induced by $S_n$.

## 6.3 What can be stated explicitly

We can still characterize these numbers structurally:

- They grow exponentially in $n$
- The growth rate is the Perron eigenvalue $\lambda_{\max}$ of $A$
- Thus:

$$
N(S_n) = \Theta(\lambda_{\max}^n)
$$

but $\lambda_{\max}$ is not determined in closed form from the given data.

$$
|Z(S_n)| = 1597n + O(1)
$$

$$
|Z(T_n)| = 34n + O(1)
$$

## Tiling counts

$$
N(S_{16}) = \mathbf{u}^\top A^{16} \mathbf{v}
$$

$$
N(S_{32}) = \mathbf{u}^\top A^{32} \mathbf{v}
$$

where $A$ is the $1597 \times 1597$ transfer matrix induced by the frontier states of $S_n$.

No Aztec diamond reduction is valid.
