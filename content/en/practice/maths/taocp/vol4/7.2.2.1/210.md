**Exercise 210.** [*21*] [21] A "bitriple" $(\alpha, \beta; \alpha', \beta', \gamma')$ is analogous to a bipair, but with (92) replaced by $\alpha + \beta + \gamma = \alpha' + \beta' + \gamma'$. How can we modify an exact cover problem so that it excludes all solutions in which options $\alpha'$, $\beta'$, and $\gamma'$ are simultaneously present?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m53s  

---

We start from a standard exact cover instance in which solutions are sets of options, each option covering some items, all primary items must be covered exactly once, and secondary items may be covered at most once.

We are given three specific options $\alpha', \beta', \gamma'$, and we want to modify the instance so that no solution contains all three simultaneously, while leaving all other solutions unchanged in feasibility.

## Construction

Introduce two new secondary items $V_1$ and $V_2$.

Replace each of the three options $\alpha', \beta', \gamma'$ by two new options:

$$
\alpha'_1 = \alpha' \cup \{V_1\}, \qquad \alpha'_2 = \alpha' \cup \{V_2\},
$$

$$
\beta'_1 = \beta' \cup \{V_1\}, \qquad \beta'_2 = \beta' \cup \{V_2\},
$$

$$
\gamma'_1 = \gamma' \cup \{V_1\}, \qquad \gamma'_2 = \gamma' \cup \{V_2\}.
$$

All other options and items remain unchanged. The items $V_1, V_2$ are declared secondary.

No additional primary items are introduced.

## Correctness

### 1. Any valid solution of the modified instance forbids selecting all three originals

Assume a solution selects options corresponding to all three original options $\alpha', \beta', \gamma'$. In the modified instance, each of these choices must be realized by selecting one of its two variants, so the solution selects three options, each carrying either $V_1$ or $V_2$.

Thus we obtain a multiset of three labels from $\{V_1, V_2\}$. By the pigeonhole principle, at least one of $V_1$ or $V_2$ appears at least twice, meaning that secondary item would be covered more than once.

This violates the exact cover condition that every secondary item may be covered at most once. Hence no valid solution can contain all of $\alpha', \beta', \gamma'$ simultaneously.

### 2. Any solution that does not contain all three remains feasible

Consider any solution of the original instance that does not contain all three of $\alpha', \beta', \gamma'$. We show it can be lifted to a solution of the modified instance.

For each selected option among $\alpha', \beta', \gamma'$, choose its variant carrying $V_1$ or $V_2$ so that no conflict arises:

- If at most two of the three options are selected, assign distinct labels to the selected ones when needed.
- If only one is selected, choose either variant arbitrarily.

Since at most two of the modified options are chosen, we can always assign distinct secondary items when necessary, ensuring that neither $V_1$ nor $V_2$ is covered more than once.

All other items are covered exactly as in the original solution, since the modifications only add secondary items and do not alter primary-item coverage.

Thus every original valid solution that does not contain the forbidden triple corresponds to at least one valid solution in the modified instance.

### 3. Preservation of exact cover structure

The modification does not introduce any new primary items and does not force selection of any option. Each original option remains optional, now represented by two equivalent variants. The only change in feasibility arises from the interaction through secondary items $V_1, V_2$.

Therefore, the solution space is unchanged except for the exclusion of simultaneous selection of $\alpha', \beta', \gamma'$.

## Conclusion

By introducing two shared secondary items and splitting each of the three options into two labeled variants, we ensure that any solution selecting all three original options would violate the secondary-item constraint, while all other solutions remain valid up to harmless duplication of variants.

This correctly modifies the exact cover instance to exclude exactly those solutions containing $\alpha', \beta', \gamma'$ simultaneously. ∎
