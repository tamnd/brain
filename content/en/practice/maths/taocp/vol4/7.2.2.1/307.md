**Exercise 307.** [*M21*] If a $(3m+1) \times (3n+2)$ box is packed with $3mn+2m+n$ straight trominoes and one domino, where must the domino be placed?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m36s  

---

## Solution

Color the board by assigning to each cell $(i,j)$ the value

$$
\chi(i,j) \equiv i+j \pmod 3,
$$

with values in ${0,1,2}$.

A straight tromino, whether $1\times 3$ or $3\times 1$, occupies three consecutive cells in a line. Along any horizontal or vertical line, the values of $\chi$ increase or decrease by $1 \pmod 3$ at each step, so each straight tromino covers exactly one cell of each residue class $0,1,2$. Every tromino therefore contributes equally to all three color classes.

The board is $(3m+1)\times(3n+2)$, so its total number of cells is

$$
(3m+1)(3n+2)=9mn+6m+3n+2.
$$

The tiling uses $3mn+2m+n$ trominoes and one domino. The trominoes cover

$$
3(3mn+2m+n)=9mn+6m+3n
$$

cells, leaving exactly $2$ cells for the domino.

Let $C_r$ be the number of cells with color $r$. Since each tromino contributes one cell to each class, the differences between $C_0,C_1,C_2$ are determined entirely by the full board.

For a fixed row $i$, the sequence $\chi(i,j)$ for $j=0,1,\dots,3n+1$ contains $n$ complete blocks of length $3$, each contributing one of each residue, plus two extra cells at $j=3n$ and $j=3n+1$. These two cells have residues

$$
\chi(i,3n)\equiv i,\qquad \chi(i,3n+1)\equiv i+1 \pmod 3.
$$

Hence row $i$ contributes $n+1$ cells to residue $i$, $n+1$ cells to residue $i+1$, and $n$ cells to residue $i+2$.

Among the rows $i=0,1,\dots,3m$, there are $m+1$ rows with $i\equiv 0$, and $m$ rows with $i\equiv 1$, and $m$ rows with $i\equiv 2$. Summing contributions over residue classes yields

$$
C_0=C_1=3mn+2m+n+1,\qquad C_2=3mn+2m+n.
$$

Thus $C_0$ and $C_1$ each exceed $C_2$ by $1$.

After removing the domino, the remaining uncovered cells must be partitioned into trominoes, so each residue class must appear equally often in the remaining region. Therefore the domino must remove exactly one cell of residue $0$ and one cell of residue $1$.

Adjacent cells differ in residue by $\pm 1 \pmod 3$. Hence a domino can cover residues $(0,1)$, $(1,2)$, or $(2,0)$ depending on position, but only pairs of type $(0,1)$ are admissible, since the other two types would remove a cell from the unique minimal class $C_2$ or increase the imbalance.

Therefore the domino must occupy two adjacent cells $(i,j)$ and $(i',j')$ such that

$$
\{\chi(i,j),\chi(i',j')\}=\{0,1\}.
$$

Equivalently, the domino must lie on an edge whose endpoints are colored $0$ and $1$, that is, either

$$
(i',j')=(i,j+1)\ \text{with}\ i+j\equiv 0 \pmod 3,
$$

or

$$
(i',j')=(i+1,j)\ \text{with}\ i+j\equiv 0 \pmod 3.
$$

This characterizes all valid placements of the domino.

$$
\boxed{\text{The domino must cover two adjacent cells whose }(i+j\bmod 3)\text{ colors are }0\text{ and }1.}
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
