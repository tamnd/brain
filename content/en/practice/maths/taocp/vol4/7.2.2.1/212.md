**Exercise 212.** &#9654; [*M21*] [M21] If the primary items of an exact cover problem have been linearly ordered, we can say that the bipair $(\alpha, \beta; \alpha', \beta')$ is *canonical* if (i) the smallest item in all four options appears in $\alpha$ and $\alpha'$; and (ii) option $\alpha$ is lexicographically smaller than option $\alpha'$, when their items have been listed in ascending order.

a) Prove that Theorem 8 applies to exact coverings that are strong according to this definition of canonicity. *Hint:* Show that it's a special case of the text's definition.

b) Does such an ordering justify the choices made in (93)?

## Corrected Solution

Let $B$ denote the set of all bipairs in the text’s formulation, and let $C \subseteq B$ denote the subset of canonical bipairs as defined in the exercise. The key point is that $C$ is not an arbitrary subset, but a complete set of representatives: every bipair in $B$ has a canonical representative in $C$.

## (a)

We prove that Theorem 8 applies to exact coverings that are strong under the notion of canonical bipairs.

### Step 1: Relation between bipairs and canonical bipairs

Take any bipair

$$
(\alpha, \beta; \alpha', \beta') \in B.
$$

Let $m$ be the smallest primary item appearing in any of the four options $\alpha, \beta, \alpha', \beta'$.

By definition of bipairs in the text, the roles of $\alpha$ and $\beta$, and independently of $\alpha'$ and $\beta'$, come from symmetric choices of options, and the bipair structure does not depend on the ordering of items within each option.

We now construct a canonical bipair associated with it:

1. **Ensuring condition (i): smallest item placement.**

If $m \in \alpha$ and $m \in \alpha'$, we keep the bipair as is.

Otherwise, we swap within each pair so that the option containing $m$ is placed in the first position of each side. Since each side represents an unordered choice of options, this reindexing does not change the underlying bipair.

After this step, we obtain an equivalent bipair satisfying condition (i).
2. **Ensuring condition (ii): lexicographic normalization.**

List the items in each option in increasing order. If $\alpha$ is lexicographically larger than $\alpha'$, swap the two sides:

$$
(\alpha, \beta; \alpha', \beta') \mapsto (\alpha', \beta'; \alpha, \beta).
$$

This does not change the bipair, only its orientation.

After these two steps, we obtain a canonical bipair in $C$.

Thus, every bipair in $B$ has at least one canonical representative in $C$.

### Step 2: Logical consequence

Assume an exact cover instance is strong with respect to canonical bipairs, meaning no canonical bipair can occur.

Suppose, for contradiction, that a bipair exists in $B$. By Step 1, this bipair induces a canonical bipair in $C$. Hence a canonical bipair would occur, contradicting strength with respect to canonical bipairs.

Therefore, no bipair in $B$ can occur. This is exactly the condition required in Theorem 8.

### Conclusion for (a)

Strength with respect to canonical bipairs implies strength with respect to all bipairs in the sense of the text. Therefore the hypothesis of Theorem 8 is satisfied, and Theorem 8 applies.

## (b)

We now justify the choices made in (93).

The key issue in (93) is that the algorithm must avoid exploring symmetric or duplicated configurations that arise from multiple representations of the same underlying bipair structure.

The canonicality conditions achieve exactly this.

### Step 1: Role of the smallest primary item

Condition (i) forces a deterministic anchoring: among all options involved in a bipair, the smallest primary item must lie in both $\alpha$ and $\alpha'$. This ensures that every bipair is represented in a form where a unique minimal element plays a distinguished role.

This aligns with the selection strategy in (93), where branching is organized around the least primary item. Any valid bipair can be rewritten so that this item is the controlling element of both options, so restricting attention to this case does not omit solutions.

### Step 2: Lexicographic ordering

Condition (ii) removes the remaining symmetry between the two sides of a bipair. Without this requirement, each bipair would appear twice, once as

$$
(\alpha, \beta; \alpha', \beta')
\quad \text{and once as} \quad
(\alpha', \beta'; \alpha, \beta).
$$

The lexicographic rule selects exactly one of these, ensuring a unique representative.

This matches the implicit assumption in (93) that options are processed in a fixed deterministic order, preventing redundant exploration of symmetric cases.

### Step 3: Justification of (93)

Because every bipair has exactly one canonical representative, restricting the algorithm to canonical bipairs is equivalent to considering all bipairs without duplication.

Thus the ordering assumptions justify the selection rules in (93): they enforce a unique representative structure compatible with the branching strategy based on the smallest primary item and prevent symmetric repetition of search states.

(a) Every bipair induces a canonical bipair, so forbidding canonical bipairs forbids all bipairs. Hence Theorem 8 applies.
