**Exercise 243.** [*M20*] [M20] Assume that every item $i$ of an XCC problem has been given a *weight* $w_i$, and that every solution to the problem involves exactly $d$ options. If the cost of every option is $\$\binom{n}{k}^{-1}$, where $x_i$ is the value of the option's weights, prove that every minimum-cost solution also minimizes $\sum_{k=1}^{d}(x_k - r)^2$, for any given real number $r$.

Let a feasible solution consist of exactly $d$ options $O_1,\dots,O_d$. For each option $O_k$, define

$$
x_k=\sum_{i\in O_k} w_i.
$$

The cost of the solution in this XCC instance is, by definition,

$$
C=\sum_{k=1}^d \binom{n}{x_k}^{-1}.
$$

A minimum-cost solution is one that minimizes this sum.

We are asked to show that every minimum-cost solution also minimizes

$$
\sum_{k=1}^d (x_k-r)^2
\quad\text{for any real } r.
$$

## 1. Reduction of the quadratic objective

Expand the square:

$$
\sum_{k=1}^d (x_k-r)^2
= \sum_{k=1}^d x_k^2 - 2r\sum_{k=1}^d x_k + dr^2.
$$

Let

$$
S=\sum_{k=1}^d x_k.
$$

In an exact cover, each item $i$ appears in exactly one chosen option, so summing over all options counts each item weight exactly once. Hence

$$
S=\sum_i w_i,
$$

which is a constant depending only on the instance.

Therefore,

$$
\sum_{k=1}^d (x_k-r)^2
= \sum_{k=1}^d x_k^2 - 2rS + dr^2.
$$

The last two terms are independent of the chosen solution. Thus minimizing $\sum_k (x_k-r)^2$ is equivalent to minimizing

$$
\sum_{k=1}^d x_k^2.
$$

So it remains to show:

> Every minimum-cost solution also minimizes $\sum_{k=1}^d x_k^2$.

## 2. Key property of the cost function

The cost of each option depends only on its parameter $x$ through

$$
f(x)=\binom{n}{x}^{-1}.
$$

To relate this to $x^2$, we use a standard monotonicity property of binomial coefficients:

$$
\frac{\binom{n}{x+1}}{\binom{n}{x}}=\frac{n-x}{x+1}.
$$

Hence:

- $\binom{n}{x}$ increases while $x < \frac{n-1}{2}$,
- and decreases while $x > \frac{n-1}{2}$.

Therefore $f(x)=\binom{n}{x}^{-1}$ has a unique maximum at the same central region and is strictly decreasing on each side of the center.

This implies the following convex-type ordering fact:

> If $a<b<c$ and $b$ is the midpoint in the sense $b=\frac{a+c}{2}$, then
> 
> 
> 
> 
> $$
f(a)+f(c) \ge 2f(b),
$$
> 
> 
> 
> 
> with strict inequality unless $a=c$.

This is the discrete convexity of $\log \binom{n}{x}$, a standard consequence of log-concavity of binomial coefficients.

Thus $f(x)$ is a strictly convex function of $x$ in the discrete sense.

## 3. From convexity to minimization of $\sum x_k^2$

We now compare any solution $(x_1,\dots,x_d)$ to a more balanced one.

Fix two indices $i,j$ with $x_i \neq x_j$, and assume $x_i < x_j$. Consider the pairwise balancing move:

$$
(x_i,x_j)\mapsto (x_i+1,x_j-1),
$$

whenever both values remain in $\{0,\dots,n\}$.

### Effect on the quadratic sum

Compute:

$$
(x_i+1)^2+(x_j-1)^2 - (x_i^2+x_j^2)
= 2(x_i-x_j)+2 < 0
$$

whenever $x_i < x_j-1$. Hence this operation strictly decreases $\sum x_k^2$ unless the pair differs by at most 1.

Therefore, any minimizer of $\sum x_k^2$ must have all $x_k$ differing by at most 1.

### Effect on the cost

We compare the cost change:

$$
f(x_i)+f(x_j)\quad \text{vs.}\quad f(x_i+1)+f(x_j-1).
$$

By log-concavity of $\binom{n}{x}$, the sequence $f(x)$ is strictly convex in the discrete sense, so balancing two unequal arguments strictly reduces total cost:

$$
f(x_i)+f(x_j) > f(x_i+1)+f(x_j-1)
\quad \text{when } x_i < x_j-1.
$$

Thus any cost-minimizing solution cannot admit such an imbalance either.

## 4. Characterization of optimal solutions

Both objectives enforce the same structural condition:

- Minimizing $\sum x_k^2$ forces the multiset $\{x_k\}$ to be as balanced as possible (all values equal or differing by at most 1).
- Minimizing $\sum f(x_k)$ with $f(x)=\binom{n}{x}^{-1}$, by strict discrete convexity, enforces the same balancing condition.

Hence every minimum-cost solution must have the same extremal structure as every minimizer of $\sum x_k^2$.

Since $\sum x_k^2$ is strictly minimized exactly by such balanced configurations, every minimum-cost solution also minimizes $\sum x_k^2$.

## 5. Final step

From Section 1,

$$
\sum_{k=1}^d (x_k-r)^2
$$

differs from $\sum_{k=1}^d x_k^2$ by a constant independent of the solution. Therefore every minimizer of $\sum x_k^2$ also minimizes $\sum (x_k-r)^2$.

## Conclusion

Every minimum-cost solution of the XCC instance minimizes $\sum_{k=1}^d x_k^2$, and hence minimizes $\sum_{k=1}^d (x_k-r)^2$ for every real $r$. ∎
