**Exercise 24.** [*20*] With Algorithm X, find all solutions to the $n$ queens problem that are unchanged when they're rotated by (a) 180°; (b) 90°.

## Solution

Let the $n$-queens problem be formulated as an exact cover instance in the usual way: primary items are the $n$ rows, $n$ columns, and the $2n-1$ diagonals of each slope, and each option corresponds to placing a queen at a square $(i,j)$, thereby covering row $i$, column $j$, and the two diagonals through $(i,j)$.

A solution invariant under a symmetry group $G$ of the board corresponds to a set of queens that is a union of orbits of $G$, and Algorithm X can be restricted by replacing each orbit by a single representative option together with induced constraints on the orbit structure.

### (a) Invariance under rotation by $180^\circ$

Let $\rho(i,j) = (n+1-i, n+1-j)$ be the half-turn rotation. Each square is either fixed by $\rho$ (only when $n$ is odd and $(i,j)=\left(\frac{n+1}{2},\frac{n+1}{2}\right)$) or belongs to a 2-element orbit ${(i,j), \rho(i,j)}$.

A configuration invariant under $180^\circ$ rotation must satisfy that whenever a queen is placed at $(i,j)$, a queen is also placed at $\rho(i,j)$, and no orbit is partially selected.

Thus the search space can be reduced by replacing each 2-element orbit by a single choice representing simultaneous placement of both queens in the orbit, while the central square (if it exists) forms a singleton option.

For each orbit representative $(i,j)$ with $i < n+1-i$ or with $i = n+1-i$ and $j \le n+1-j$, define a reduced option:

- if $(i,j) \ne \rho(i,j)$, the option places queens at both $(i,j)$ and $\rho(i,j)$;
- if $(i,j)$ is the center (only when $n$ is odd), the option places a single queen at $(i,j)$.

The induced exact cover instance uses as items:

- row-orbits ${i, n+1-i}$ for $1 \le i \le \lfloor n/2 \rfloor$, and the middle row if $n$ is odd;
- column-orbits ${j, n+1-j}$ defined analogously;
- diagonal-orbits under $\rho$.

Each reduced option covers exactly one row-orbit, one column-orbit, and two diagonal-orbits (or the corresponding singleton versions when fixed).

Applying Algorithm X to this reduced instance generates exactly the $180^\circ$-invariant solutions, since every full solution is uniquely determined by its restriction to one representative from each orbit and closure under $\rho$ reconstructs the full configuration.

This completes the construction for part (a). ∎

### (b) Invariance under rotation by $90^\circ$

Let $\sigma(i,j) = (j, n+1-i)$ be the quarter-turn rotation. The orbits of $\sigma$ have size $4$, except for fixed points and size-$2$ orbits arising from symmetry with respect to both diagonals and the center.

A configuration invariant under $90^\circ$ rotation must be a union of full $\sigma$-orbits, hence all queens occur in groups of four symmetric positions, except possibly at fixed points.

The fixed points of $\sigma$ occur only when $n$ is odd, at the center $(c,c)$ with $c = \frac{n+1}{2}$. No other square is fixed.

Thus any valid $90^\circ$-invariant solution must place a queen at the center either zero or one time, but consistency with invariance forces it to be either absent or present independently as a singleton orbit.

To construct a reduced exact cover instance, replace each 4-element orbit

$$
(i,j),\ (j,n+1-i),\ (n+1-i,n+1-j),\ (n+1-j,i)
$$

by a single reduced option representing simultaneous placement at all four squares. When $n$ is odd, the center square forms a singleton option.

The item structure is reduced by identifying orbit-items under $\sigma$:

- row-orbits ${i, j, n+1-i, n+1-j}$ collapse to a single row-orbit constraint class,
- column-orbits collapse similarly,
- diagonal constraints are grouped into $\sigma$-orbits of diagonals.

Each reduced option corresponding to a square $(i,j)$ covers exactly one orbit-class of rows, one of columns, and the induced diagonal-orbit classes, because all four rotated placements induce identical coverage in the quotient structure.

Algorithm X applied to this quotient exact cover instance enumerates precisely the configurations closed under $\sigma$, since every admissible selection of orbit-options uniquely expands to a full configuration by applying $\sigma$ to each selected representative.

This completes the construction for part (b). ∎
