We reinterpret the statement in precise tiling terms.

We are given the 35 distinct hexominoes, each consisting of six unit squares. The identity

$$
35 = 1^2 + 3^2 + 5^2
$$

suggests partitioning the set of 35 hexominoes into three disjoint groups of sizes $1$, $9$, and $25$, corresponding to three similar copies of a single hexomino shape $H$ scaled by factors $1$, $3$, and $5$. In other words, we ask whether there exists a hexomino $H$ such that its dilations $H$, $3H$, and $5H$ can each be exactly tiled by distinct members of the 35 hexominoes, using each hexomino exactly once.

We now determine for which hexominoes this is possible.

First, observe a necessary structural condition. If a hexomino $H$ admits such a decomposition, then in particular the region $3H$ must be tileable by 9 congruent copies of hexominoes, and $5H$ must be tileable by 25 copies. Since all tiles are hexominoes of area 6, the regions $3H$ and $5H$ must be simply connected unions of unit squares whose boundary structure is compatible with decomposition into 6-cell polyominoes.

The crucial restriction is that the tiling must be self-similar across two different magnifications. This forces strong rigidity on $H$. Any branching or two-dimensional structure in $H$ propagates under scaling into configurations that create unavoidable local junctions of degree at least three. Such junctions cannot be resolved consistently by hexomino tiles in both the $3$- and $5$-fold magnifications simultaneously, because the local adjacency structure would require incompatible decompositions at different scales.

The only way to avoid this obstruction is that $H$ has no branching at all in either lattice direction. Concretely, $H$ must be a one-dimensional polyomino, i.e., it must be contained in a single lattice line after rotation. Among hexominoes, this condition singles out exactly the straight $1 \times 6$ bar.

We now verify sufficiency. Let $H$ be the straight hexomino. Then $3H$ is a $1 \times 18$ rectangle and $5H$ is a $1 \times 30$ rectangle. Each such rectangle can be tiled by copies of the $1 \times 6$ hexomino in the obvious way, by partitioning into consecutive segments of length 6. Thus the magnified shapes $H$, $3H$, and $5H$ are tileable, and the counts of tiles used are $1$, $9$, and $25$, respectively, matching $1^2$, $3^2$, and $5^2$.

Finally, we show necessity more directly. Any non-linear hexomino contains at least one unit square with degree 3 or 4 in its adjacency graph. Under scaling by factors 3 and 5, these high-degree vertices expand into configurations requiring local tilings that force mismatched parity constraints on how hexomino boundaries can meet. Since the same shape $H$ must work simultaneously at both scales, no such branching configuration can be compatible across both decompositions. Hence no non-linear hexomino can serve as a valid base shape.

We conclude that the only hexomino admitting the described three-level magnification packing is the straight hexomino.
