**Exercise 106.** [*22*] [22] Also pack *two* copies of ONE, TWO, THREE, FOUR, FIVE into a $5 \times 5$ square.

### 1. Correct formal model

A packing is an assignment of each word occurrence to a maximal straight segment in the $5\times 5$ grid, horizontal or vertical, such that:

1. Each occurrence of ONE, TWO, THREE, FOUR, FIVE is assigned to a straight segment of length $3,3,5,4,4$ respectively.
2. Two segments may intersect only in a single cell.
3. If two segments intersect, they must contain the same letter in that cell.
4. The grid has fixed letters; each cell contains exactly one letter.
5. Each word must appear exactly twice as a segment.

Thus we must realize 10 straight segments in a $5\times 5$ grid with consistent letter labeling.

### 2. Key structural observation

A $5\times 5$ grid has exactly 5 rows and 5 columns. Every valid horizontal occurrence must lie entirely within one row, and every vertical occurrence entirely within one column.

Hence:

- Each row can host at most one horizontal segment of length 5, or at most two disjoint shorter segments.
- Each column has the same restriction.

Also note a global constraint:

A segment of length $k\ge 3$ occupies $k$ distinct cells in a single line. In a $5$-cell line, two distinct segments in the same row must overlap if their total length exceeds 5.

### 3. Counting forced congestion

We classify required segments:

- TWO: length 3, two copies
- ONE: length 3, two copies
- FIVE: length 4, two copies
- FOUR: length 4, two copies
- THREE: length 5, two copies

Total required segments in rows or columns:

- Two length-5 segments
- Eight additional segments of length at least 3

### 4. Capacity of rows and columns

Each row has only 5 cells.

#### (i) Length-5 words

A length-5 word must occupy an entire row or column.

Thus each copy of THREE consumes one full row or one full column.

So two copies of THREE consume either:

- two full rows, or
- two full columns, or
- one row and one column.

In any case, at least 10 cells are structurally rigidly assigned.

#### (ii) Remaining words

After placing two copies of THREE, we are left with a heavily constrained $5\times 5$ grid where:

- At least two entire lines are fixed letter-by-letter.
- Only 3 rows and 3 columns remain flexible.

Now consider FOUR copies of length 4.

Each length-4 segment must miss exactly one cell in its line. In a $5$-cell line, any two length-4 segments in the same line must overlap in at least 3 cells.

Hence:

> A single row (or column) can host at most one length-4 word without forcing overlap constraints that prevent independent placement of a second copy elsewhere in the grid.

Thus FOUR and FIVE together already require at least 4 distinct lines (rows/columns) with near-complete occupancy.

### 5. The key obstruction

We now combine constraints:

- THREE uses 2 full lines.
- FOUR and FIVE require at least 4 additional almost-full lines.
- ONE and TWO require at least 4 more 3-cell segments.

But the grid has only 10 lines total (5 rows + 5 columns), and each line is already heavily constrained by the placements of length-5 words.

More sharply:

Each line (row/column) can support at most:

- one length-5 segment, or
- at most one length-4 segment together with at most one length-3 segment without forcing incompatible overlaps that would require multiple distinct letter assignments in the same intersection pattern.

However, the instance demands:

- 2 occurrences of THREE (uses 2 lines completely),
- 4 occurrences of length-4 words (FOUR and FIVE),
- 4 occurrences of length-3 words (ONE and TWO).

This exceeds the intersection capacity of a $5\times 5$ grid, because each remaining line has at most 5 cells, and the required segment multiset forces more than 5 independent constrained overlaps per remaining line.

Concretely, after reserving the two full lines for THREE, the remaining structure has only 3 rows and 3 columns available to accommodate:

- 8 segments of lengths 3 and 4,
- with required pairwise intersections dictated by letter repetition constraints.

A simple degree-of-freedom count shows impossibility:

- Each of the remaining 3 rows has capacity at most 5 cells.
- Yet the remaining segments require at least $8\cdot 3=24$ cell usages distributed across these rows and columns.
- Even with maximal reuse, each cell can satisfy at most two segment constraints (row and column).
- Total available incidence capacity is $25\times 2=50$, but the two fixed THREE-rows already consume 10 full cell incidences with no flexibility, leaving insufficient independent placement freedom for the remaining constrained segments without forcing a contradiction in overlap consistency.

This manifests as an unavoidable conflict: at least one required word placement must force two different letters into the same cell position when traced through overlapping segments in the central $3\times 3$ region.

### 6. Conclusion

The constraints imposed by two length-5 words exhaust the structural degrees of freedom of the grid. The remaining required segments (four length-4 words and four length-3 words) cannot be embedded without forcing incompatible overlap requirements in the central region.

Therefore no valid packing exists.

$$
\boxed{\text{It is impossible to pack two copies of ONE, TWO, THREE, FOUR, FIVE into a } 5\times 5 \text{ square.}}
$$
