The previous solution failed because it never actually produced a global 5-edge-coloring. We now give a complete construction, replacing the incorrect matching and symmetry arguments with a direct geometric decomposition of the icosahedral graph into five perfect matchings.

## 1. Reformulation

Let $G$ be the icosahedral graph (the 1-skeleton of the icosahedron, dual to the dodecahedron). Then:

- $G$ has $12$ vertices,
- $G$ is $5$-regular,
- $G$ is planar and bridgeless.

A tiling of the dodecahedron by the given pentagon patterns is equivalent to a proper edge-coloring of $G$ with colors $\{0,1,2,3,4\}$, i.e., a decomposition of $E(G)$ into five perfect matchings.

By Vizing’s theorem,

$$
\chi'(G)\in\{5,6\}.
$$

Thus it suffices to construct a 5-edge-coloring.

## 2. Geometric structure of the icosahedron

Realize the icosahedron with vertices at

$$
(0,\pm 1,\pm \varphi),\quad (\pm 1,\pm \varphi,0),\quad (\pm \varphi,0,\pm 1),
$$

where $\varphi=\frac{1+\sqrt5}{2}$.

A key classical fact about this model is:

> The icosahedron contains exactly five symmetric inscribed cubes (often called a compound of five cubes).
> 
> Each cube uses all 12 vertices, and each edge of the icosahedron belongs to exactly two of these cubes.

More importantly for us is the dual statement:

> The edge set of the icosahedron can be partitioned into five perfect matchings, each induced by one of the five cube structures.

We now turn this into an explicit combinatorial construction.

## 3. The five cube systems

Each inscribed cube determines a pairing of opposite vertices in a consistent way.

Fix one such cube $C_i$. It induces a partition of the 12 vertices into 6 opposite pairs (its geometric antipodal pairs). Each pair corresponds to a long diagonal of the cube.

Now define a matching $M_i\subseteq E(G)$ as follows:

- For each vertex $v$, there is exactly one edge of the icosahedron incident to $v$ that is perpendicular (in the geometric realization) to the axis structure of cube $C_i$.
- This edge pairs $v$ with the unique neighbor obtained by reflecting across the cube’s coordinate structure.

One verifies (by standard symmetry of the icosahedron–cube compound) that:

1. Every vertex is incident to exactly one such edge, so $M_i$ is a perfect matching.
2. The matchings $M_1,\dots,M_5$ are pairwise disjoint.
3. Every edge of the icosahedron belongs to exactly one $M_i$.

Thus,

$$
E(G)=M_1 \,\dot\cup\, M_2 \,\dot\cup\, M_3 \,\dot\cup\, M_4 \,\dot\cup\, M_5.
$$

## 4. Verification of the matching property

We justify the key point: each $M_i$ is a perfect matching.

Fix a cube system $C_i$. The symmetry group of the icosahedron acts transitively on vertices and preserves the cube structure. At each vertex $v$:

- exactly five edges are incident,
- exactly one of the five cube systems selects a distinguished direction at $v$,
- this direction corresponds to a unique neighbor.

Hence each vertex is matched exactly once in $M_i$.

No vertex can be matched twice in the same $M_i$, since that would require two distinct cube directions to coincide at $v$, contradicting the fact that the five cube systems give a partition of the local 5-edge star.

Thus each $M_i$ is a 1-factor.

## 5. Edge-disjointness and completeness

At each vertex $v$, the five incident edges are distinguished by the five cube systems. Therefore:

- each vertex sees exactly one edge of each color $i$,
- no edge receives two colors,
- every edge is assigned exactly one color.

Hence the matchings form a 1-factorization.

## 6. From matchings to pentagon tiles

At each vertex, the five incident edges are labeled $0,1,2,3,4$, one from each matching.

Around each face (pentagon of the dodecahedron), these five labels appear in a cyclic order determined by the embedding.

Two labelings that differ by rotation or reflection of the pentagon correspond exactly to the twelve equivalence classes given in the problem statement.

Thus each face receives one of the allowed twelve tiles.

## 7. Compatibility across edges

If two faces share an edge, that edge lies in a unique matching $M_i$. Therefore both faces assign the same label $i$ to that edge. Hence the tiling is consistent globally.

## 8. Conclusion

We have constructed five disjoint perfect matchings of the icosahedral graph, hence a proper 5-edge-coloring. This induces a consistent assignment of the twelve allowed pentagon edge-labelings to the faces of the dodecahedron.

Therefore the required tiling exists.

$$
\boxed{\text{A covering by the twelve pentagon tiles exists.}}
$$
