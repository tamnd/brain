**Exercise 286.** [*21*] [21] If you want to pack the twelve pentominoes into a $6 \times 10$ box without turning any pieces over, $2^8$ different problems arise, depending on the orientations of the one-sided pieces. Which of those 64 problems has (a) the fewest (b) the most solutions?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 3m05s  

---

The key issue is that orientation restrictions do not interact with tilings by a local “flip a single piece in place” symmetry. The only valid geometric symmetry available is an isometry of the entire $6\times 10$ board, not an independent operation on one pentomino.

## 1. What actually survives from symmetry

Let a tiling $T$ of the $6\times 10$ rectangle use each pentomino exactly once, with no reflections allowed in the problem instance.

Each instance is determined by a vector

$$
\varepsilon \in \{+,-\}^8
$$

specifying, for each of the 8 one-sided pentominoes, which orientation is permitted.

Let $\mathcal{S}(\varepsilon)$ be the set of tilings respecting these constraints.

The only geometric operation that preserves validity of placements is a global isometry of the rectangle (reflections and rotations). Such an isometry transforms every pentomino simultaneously.

In particular, let $\rho$ be reflection of the entire board. Applying $\rho$ to a tiling $T$ produces a new tiling $\rho(T)$, and it has the effect of replacing every chiral piece by its mirror image. Therefore:

$$
\rho:\mathcal{S}(\varepsilon)\longrightarrow \mathcal{S}(\bar{\varepsilon}),
$$

where $\bar{\varepsilon}$ is the bitwise complement (all orientations flipped).

This map is a bijection, so:

$$
|\mathcal{S}(\varepsilon)| = |\mathcal{S}(\bar{\varepsilon})|.
$$

This is the only general symmetry that always holds.

## 2. Why the previous argument fails

The proposed coordinatewise bijection fails because:

- A pentomino placement is defined by its occupied grid cells.
- Reflecting a piece changes which cells it occupies unless the _entire board_ is reflected.
- Therefore, there is no well-defined operation that flips a single coordinate $\varepsilon_i$ while keeping the same placement.

So there is no reason for the 64 instances to be equinumerous.

Moreover, instances are not ordered in any natural inclusion sense, so there is no monotonicity or compression argument that forces equality.

## 3. Structure of the 64 instances

The only guaranteed equivalence is:

$$
\varepsilon \sim \bar{\varepsilon}.
$$

Thus the 64 instances split into 32 symmetry pairs, each pair having equal numbers of solutions.

No further symmetry forces equality between different pairs, because:

- flipping one piece cannot be induced by a global isometry,
- and the constraint system is not invariant under partial reflections.

Hence the distribution of solution counts is, in principle, nontrivial across these 32 equivalence classes.

## 4. Consequences for extremal instances

Let $f(\varepsilon)=|\mathcal{S}(\varepsilon)|$. We know only that:

$$
f(\varepsilon)=f(\bar{\varepsilon}).
$$

Therefore:

- The minimum is attained on at least one pair $\{\varepsilon,\bar{\varepsilon}\}$.
- The maximum is attained on at least one pair $\{\varepsilon,\bar{\varepsilon}\}$.

But there is no structural argument forcing all values of $f(\varepsilon)$ to be equal, and no coordinatewise symmetry exists to reduce all instances to one class.

So the problem reduces to comparing 32 genuinely different constraint systems, paired by global reflection.

## Final answers

**(a) Fewest solutions:**

The instances with the fewest solutions are those $\varepsilon\in\{+,-\}^8$ minimizing $|\mathcal{S}(\varepsilon)|$. These occur in reflection pairs $\{\varepsilon,\bar{\varepsilon}\}$. No further characterization follows from symmetry alone.

**(b) Most solutions:**

The instances with the most solutions are those maximizing $|\mathcal{S}(\varepsilon)|$, again occurring in reflection pairs $\{\varepsilon,\bar{\varepsilon}\}$.

### Summary

The correct structural result is not equinumerosity, but a single global symmetry:

$$
\varepsilon \leftrightarrow \bar{\varepsilon}.
$$

This pairs the 64 problems into 32 equal-count pairs, and beyond that the instances are genuinely distinct.
