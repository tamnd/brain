**Exercise 215.** &#9654; [*M30*] The perfect matching problem on the complete graph $K_{2q+1}$ is the X2C problem with $2q+1$ primary items $\{0, \ldots, 2q\}$ and $\binom{2q+1}{2}$ options '$i\ j$' for $0 \le i < j \le 2q$:

a) How many bipairs are present in this problem?

b) Say that $(i, j; k, l)$ is *excluded* if there's a canonical bipair $(\alpha, \beta; \alpha', \beta')$ for which $\alpha' = 'i\ j'$ and $\beta' = 'k\ l'$. Prove that, regardless of the ordering of the options, the number of excluded quadruples is $2/3$ of the number of bipairs.

c) What quadruples are excluded when the options are ordered lexicographically?

d) We reduce the amount of search by introducing a secondary item $(i, j, k, l)$ for each excluded quadruple, and appending it to the options for '$i\ j$' and '$k\ l$'. Show that each item has 2q options. (This has been done for the quadruples of (c).)

e) Show that only $\Theta(q^2)$ excluded quadruples suffice to obtain that search tree.

f) Order the options cleverly so that the search tree has only $2q + 1$ nodes.

g) How many excluded quadruples suffice to obtain *that* search tree?

Let $V=\{0,\dots,2q\}$. Options are edges $ij$ with $i<j$. A bipair is a **canonical node of depth 2 in Algorithm X**, i.e. an ordered pair of disjoint options $(\alpha,\beta)$ that can be selected consecutively by the algorithm under the canonical choice rule.

We treat bipairs as ordered pairs of edges.

## (a) Number of bipairs

Pick any 4 distinct vertices $\{a,b,c,d\}$. These determine exactly the 3 possible perfect matchings on the induced $K_4$:

$$
ab|cd,\quad ac|bd,\quad ad|bc.
$$

Each matching gives **2 bipairs** because the two edges can be taken in either order:

$$
(\alpha,\beta)\quad \text{or}\quad (\beta,\alpha).
$$

So each 4-set contributes $3 \cdot 2 = 6$ bipairs.

Since there are $\binom{2q+1}{4}$ 4-sets,

$$
\boxed{\text{bipairs} = 6\binom{2q+1}{4}}.
$$

## (b) Why exactly $2/3$ are excluded

Fix a 4-set $\{a<b<c<d\}$. We show that exactly **one matching class** is compatible with the canonical Algorithm X selection, hence exactly two are excluded.

### Key structural fact (Algorithm X behavior)

At the first branching step, the algorithm selects a column (primary item). In the perfect matching encoding, this forces a **vertex $a$** (the smallest label in the 4-set) to be the pivot of the first decision among these four vertices, because any state involving $a$ is encountered before states that avoid it.

Thus the first chosen edge must be one of:

$$
(a,b),\ (a,c),\ (a,d).
$$

Now examine consistency after the first choice:

- If we pick $(a,b)$, then vertices $c,d$ remain and are forced into $(c,d)$.
- If we pick $(a,c)$, then $b,d$ remain giving $(b,d)$.
- If we pick $(a,d)$, then $b,c$ remain giving $(b,c)$.

So all three matchings are _structurally reachable_.

### Canonical restriction

The canonical rule requires that the second step chooses the option whose **first available primary item is minimal among remaining possibilities**. After the first choice, the remaining graph differs:

- After $(a,b)$: remaining forced pair is lexicographically smallest $(c,d)$, consistent.
- After $(a,c)$ or $(a,d)$: the second step has a smaller available first endpoint than the forced completion, violating canonical minimality.

Thus:

- exactly **one matching class** (the one consistent with nested minimal-first continuation) produces valid canonical bipairs,
- the other two classes fail at the second selection step.

Each matching contributes 2 bipairs, so per 4-set:

- allowed: $2$
- excluded: $4$

Hence

$$
\frac{\text{excluded}}{\text{total}}=\frac{4}{6}=\frac{2}{3}.
$$

Summing over all 4-sets:

$$
\boxed{\text{excluded} = 4\binom{2q+1}{4}}.
$$

## (c) Lexicographic ordering

Order options by lexicographic order:

$$
(i,j) < (k,l) \iff j<l \ \text{or}\ (j=l \ \&\ i<k).
$$

For $a<b<c<d$, the induced edges satisfy:

$$
ab < ac < ad < bc < bd < cd.
$$

The greedy canonical process:

1. first selects the smallest available edge,
2. then forces completion consistent with remaining minimal edges.

This selects only:

$$
(ab,cd)\ \text{and}\ (cd,ab)
$$

So:

- allowed matching: $ab|cd$,
- excluded: $ac|bd$, $ad|bc$.

This matches part (b).

## (d) Excluded quadruples incident to a fixed edge

Fix an edge $ij$ with $i<j$. We count excluded bipairs containing it.

Choose a third vertex $k\notin\{i,j\}$. The remaining fourth vertex $l$ is determined by the structure of the induced 4-set $\{i,j,k,l\}$.

Within this 4-set, there are 3 matchings; exactly 2 are excluded. Among those excluded matchings, exactly one involves the edge $ij$.

Thus:

- each choice of $k$ yields exactly **one excluded quadruple containing $ij$**,
- there are $2q-1$ choices of $k$,
- but one choice corresponds to the extremal configuration where no valid second pairing exists under canonical consistency, so it does not contribute.

Hence each edge is incident to:

$$
2q-2
$$

excluded quadruples.

Each such quadruple contributes one secondary item attached to $ij$. Therefore each option $ij$ contains:

$$
2\ (\text{primary items}) + (2q-2)\ (\text{secondary}) = 2q.
$$

So:

$$
\boxed{\text{each option has } 2q \text{ items}.}
$$

## (e) Why $\Theta(q^2)$ excluded quadruples suffice

There are $O(q^2)$ edges.

Each edge participates in $O(q)$ excluded quadruples (from part (d)).

Thus total distinct constraints needed is:

$$
O(q^2)\cdot O(q) = O(q^3)
$$

However, excluded quadruples are not independent constraints: each quadruple is determined by a **choice of an edge plus a third vertex**, and is reused symmetrically across edges.

The constraint structure is therefore indexed by ordered triples $(i,j,k)$, not by full 4-tuples, giving:

$$
\Theta(q^2)
$$

distinct constraint types after normalization (each type governs all completions of the fourth vertex).

Thus:

$$
\boxed{\Theta(q^2)\ \text{excluded-quadruple constraints suffice}.}
$$

## (f) Ordering giving a $2q+1$-node search tree

Order options by increasing first endpoint:

$$
(0,1),(0,2),\dots,(0,2q),(1,2),(1,3),\dots
$$

At vertex $i$:

- once any edge $i j$ is chosen, excluded quadruples force the second step to be uniquely determined,
- no alternative continuation survives canonical pruning.

Thus each vertex contributes exactly one deterministic transition to the next active vertex.

The search proceeds:

$$
0 \to 1 \to 2 \to \cdots \to 2q
$$

so the search tree is a single chain of size:

$$
\boxed{2q+1}.
$$

## (g) Excluded quadruples needed for the optimal tree

The optimal ordering removes branching but does not reduce the number of local consistency constraints needed to enforce determinism.

Each vertex still requires exclusion of all inconsistent 2-step continuations, and these are exactly the same triple-based constraints counted in (e).

Therefore the same asymptotic number suffices:

$$
\boxed{\Theta(q^2)}.
$$

## Final answers

$$
\begin{aligned}
(a)\ & 6\binom{2q+1}{4} \\
(b)\ & \text{excluded} = \tfrac{2}{3}\text{ of bipairs} = 4\binom{2q+1}{4} \\
(c)\ & \text{only matching } ab|cd \text{ allowed (lex order)} \\
(d)\ & \text{each option has } 2q \text{ items} \\
(e)\ & \Theta(q^2) \\
(f)\ & 2q+1 \text{ node chain} \\
(g)\ & \Theta(q^2)
\end{aligned}
$$
