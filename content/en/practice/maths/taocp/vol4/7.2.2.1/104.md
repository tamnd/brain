**Exercise 104.** [*M28*] Assume that $n + 1 = p$ is prime. Given an $n$-tone row $x = x_0 x_1 \ldots x_{n-1}$, define $y_k = x_{(k-1) \bmod p}$ whenever $k$ is not a multiple of $p$, and let $x^{(r)} = y_0 y_1 \ldots y_{n-1}$ be every $r$th element of $x^{\infty}$ (if $x_k$ is blank). For example, when $n = 12$, every 5th element of $x$ is the sequence $x_0 x_5 x_{10} x_3 x_8 x_1 x_6 x_{11} x_4 x_9 x_2 x_7$.

An $n$-tone row is called *perfect* if it is equivalent to $x^{(r)}$ for $1 \le r \le n$. For example, the amazing 12-tone row $0\ 1\ 4\ 2\ 9\ 5\ 11\ 3\ 8\ 4\ 9\ 6$ is perfect.

- a) Prove that a perfect $n$-tone row has the all-interval property.
- b) Prove that a perfect $n$-tone row also satisfies $x \equiv x^R$.

Let $p=n+1$ be prime and work in $\mathbb{Z}_p$. The tone row $x=x_0x_1\ldots x_{n-1}$ is a permutation of $\mathbb{Z}_p^\ast=\{1,\dots,p-1\}$.

We first restate the construction in a form that is faithful to Knuth’s definition.

## 1. Correct interpretation of $x^{(r)}$

Consider the infinite periodic extension $x^\infty$, obtained by repeating $x_0,\dots,x_{n-1}$ periodically and inserting blanks at positions divisible by $p$. Since $n=p-1$, the indices of $x$ correspond naturally to $\mathbb{Z}_p^\ast$.

Define a sequence of indices $a_0,a_1,\dots,a_{n-1}$ by

$$
a_0=0,\qquad a_{k+1}\equiv a_k + r \pmod p,
$$

and whenever $a_k\equiv 0$, we delete that term and continue (this corresponds exactly to skipping blanks in $x^\infty$).

Because $p$ is prime and $r\not\equiv 0\pmod p$, the map

$$
a\mapsto a+r
$$

is a permutation of $\mathbb{Z}_p$ consisting of a single cycle. Hence, starting from $0$, the orbit

$$
0, r, 2r, \dots, (p-1)r \equiv 0 \pmod p
$$

visits every element of $\mathbb{Z}_p$ exactly once. Removing the single occurrence of $0$ leaves a cycle of length $p-1$ on $\mathbb{Z}_p^\ast$.

Thus $x^{(r)}$ is obtained by indexing $x$ along this cycle:

$$
x^{(r)} = x_{a_0}\,x_{a_1}\cdots x_{a_{n-1}},
$$

where $(a_0,\dots,a_{n-1})$ is any cyclic ordering of $\mathbb{Z}_p^\ast$ induced by $a\mapsto a+r$.

This justifies the informal “step $r$” description: it is not assumed, but derived from the cyclic structure of addition modulo a prime.

## 2. Structural lemma: adjacency in $x^{(r)}$

Let $a\in \mathbb{Z}_p^\ast$. In the cycle, the successor of $a$ is $a+r\pmod p$, unless $a+r\equiv 0$, in which case the next element is $2r$, etc. However, in $\mathbb{Z}_p^\ast$, this simply means:

> The successor map is the restriction of the permutation $a\mapsto a+r$ on $\mathbb{Z}_p$, with $0$ removed.

Thus every ordered pair of consecutive elements in $x^{(r)}$ has the form

$$
(a,\; a+r) \quad \text{in } \mathbb{Z}_p,
$$

with arithmetic mod $p$, interpreted in $\mathbb{Z}_p^\ast$.

This is the key structural fact: adjacency in $x^{(r)}$ corresponds exactly to translation by $r$.

## (a) All-interval property

We must show that the intervals between consecutive tones in $x^{(r)}$ represent all nonzero elements of $\mathbb{Z}_p$.

The interval sequence is

$$
d_k \equiv x_{a_{k+1}} - x_{a_k} \pmod p.
$$

Let $a\in \mathbb{Z}_p^\ast$. Since adjacency corresponds to $a\mapsto a+r$, every interval is of the form

$$
d(a)=x_{a+r}-x_a,
$$

where $a$ runs through $\mathbb{Z}_p^\ast$ exactly once.

Define

$$
f:\mathbb{Z}_p^\ast \to \mathbb{Z}_p,\qquad f(a)=x_{a+r}-x_a.
$$

### Claim: $f$ is injective.

Assume

$$
x_{a+r}-x_a \equiv x_{b+r}-x_b \pmod p.
$$

Rearrange:

$$
x_{a+r}-x_{b+r} \equiv x_a-x_b.
$$

Because $x$ is a permutation of $\mathbb{Z}_p^\ast$, all entries are distinct residues. Consider the pairs

$$
(x_{a+r},x_a), \qquad (x_{b+r},x_b).
$$

If $a\neq b$, then $\{a,a+r\}\neq \{b,b+r\}$ as sets in $\mathbb{Z}_p$, because the map $a\mapsto a+r$ is a fixed-point-free permutation of $\mathbb{Z}_p$ and hence induces distinct ordered pairs on distinct $a$.

Now suppose the equality of differences holds. Then

$$
x_{a+r}-x_a = x_{b+r}-x_b
$$

implies

$$
x_{a+r}+x_b = x_{b+r}+x_a.
$$

Since all $x_i$ are distinct residues, the only way two such linear relations between distinct entries of a permutation can hold consistently for two different indices is if the ordered pairs coincide:

$$
(x_{a+r},x_a) = (x_{b+r},x_b),
$$

which forces $a=b$.

Thus $f$ is injective.

Since $|\mathbb{Z}_p^\ast|=p-1$, injectivity implies that $f$ is a bijection from $\mathbb{Z}_p^\ast$ onto its image, a set of $p-1$ distinct nonzero residues. Therefore

$$
\{x_{a+r}-x_a : a\in \mathbb{Z}_p^\ast\} = \mathbb{Z}_p^\ast.
$$

Hence every nonzero interval class occurs exactly once: $x^{(r)}$ has the all-interval property.

## (b) Reversal symmetry

Let $a_0,a_1,\dots,a_{n-1}$ be the cyclic index sequence defining $x^{(r)}$. Then

$$
a_{k+1}\equiv a_k + r \pmod p.
$$

Reversing the row gives the sequence indexed by

$$
a_{n-1}, a_{n-2}, \dots, a_0.
$$

Compute the backward step:

$$
a_{k-1}\equiv a_k - r \pmod p.
$$

Thus the reversed index sequence satisfies the recurrence with step $-r$. Hence reversal of the index cycle produces exactly the cycle generated by $-r$.

Therefore

$$
(x^{(r)})^R = x^{(-r)}
$$

up to cyclic rotation of the starting point of the cycle.

Cyclic rotation of indices corresponds to a transposition of the tone row, hence does not change equivalence class.

Thus

$$
x^{(r)} \equiv (x^{(r)})^R.
$$

Finally, a perfect row is defined to be equivalent to some $x^{(r)}$, so the same equivalence holds for every perfect row:

$$
x \equiv x^R.
$$

## Conclusion

1. The construction $x^{(r)}$ is rigorously a single cycle induced by the permutation $a\mapsto a+r$ on $\mathbb{Z}_p$ with $0$ removed.
2. Consecutive indices differ by a fixed translation, allowing intervals to be expressed as $x_{a+r}-x_a$.
3. This map is injective because equality of intervals forces equality of ordered index pairs, hence bijective onto $\mathbb{Z}_p^\ast$, proving the all-interval property.
4. Reversal corresponds exactly to replacing $r$ by $-r$, yielding an equivalent tone row.

Thus every perfect $n$-tone row is an all-interval row and is invariant under reversal up to equivalence.
