**Exercise 234.** [*M20*] The minimum-cost placement of $n$ nonattacking queens when the cost of a queen in cell $(i,j)$ is $8d(i,j)^2$, the *square* of its distance from the center?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m05s  

---

## Solution

Let the board be the $n \times n$ grid with rows and columns indexed by $1,2,\dots,n$, and let the center coordinate be $c = \frac{n+1}{2}$. For a queen placed at $(i,j)$, the squared distance from the center is

$d(i,j)^2 = (i-c)^2 + (j-c)^2.$

A placement of $n$ nonattacking queens is equivalent to choosing a permutation $\pi$ of ${1,2,\dots,n}$, where the queens occupy the squares $(i,\pi(i))$ for $i=1,\dots,n$.

The total cost is

$\sum_{i=1}^n 8d(i,\pi(i))^2 = 8\sum_{i=1}^n \bigl((i-c)^2 + (\pi(i)-c)^2\bigr).$

This separates into two sums,

$8\sum_{i=1}^n (i-c)^2 + 8\sum_{i=1}^n (\pi(i)-c)^2.$

Since $\pi$ is a permutation of ${1,\dots,n}$, the multiset ${\pi(1),\dots,\pi(n)}$ equals ${1,\dots,n}$, so

$\sum_{i=1}^n (\pi(i)-c)^2 = \sum_{k=1}^n (k-c)^2.$

Substituting gives

$8\sum_{i=1}^n (i-c)^2 + 8\sum_{i=1}^n (i-c)^2 = 16\sum_{k=1}^n (k-c)^2.$

The expression depends only on $n$ and not on the permutation $\pi$, so every nonattacking placement of $n$ queens has identical cost.

Therefore every feasible solution to the $n$-queens problem is simultaneously optimal for both minimization and maximization of the given cost function.

For a standard closed form, with $c = (n+1)/2$,

$\sum_{k=1}^n (k-c)^2 = \frac{n(n^2-1)}{12},$

so the common value of the objective function is

$16 \cdot \frac{n(n^2-1)}{12} = \frac{4n(n^2-1)}{3}.$

Hence the minimum and maximum coincide and equal

$\boxed{\frac{4n(n^2-1)}{3}}.$

This completes the proof. ∎
