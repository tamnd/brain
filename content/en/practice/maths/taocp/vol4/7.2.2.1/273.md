**Exercise 273.** [*25*] [25] All twelve pentominoes fit into a $3 \times 20$ box only in two ways, shown in (36).
a) How many ways are there to fit *eleven* of them into that box?
b) In how many solutions to (a) are the five holes nonadjacent, kingwise?
c) In how many ways can eleven pentominoes be packed into a $3 \times 19$ box?

### Corrected Solution

Let the two complete tilings of the $3\times 20$ rectangle by the twelve pentominoes be the only ones given in the statement.

Each pentomino covers 5 unit squares, and each tiling uses all 12 pentominoes, covering all 60 squares.

## (a) Number of ways to place eleven pentominoes in $3\times 20$

Start from either of the two complete tilings of the $3\times 20$ rectangle.

Delete exactly one pentomino. This produces a configuration of 11 pentominoes covering $55$ squares of the board.

There are $12$ choices of the deleted pentomino in each of the two tilings, so this produces $2\cdot 12=24$ configurations.

It remains to show that all 24 configurations are distinct.

Suppose two deletions produced the same placement of 11 pentominoes. Then the set of occupied squares is identical, hence its complement (the missing 5 squares) is identical. But in a tiling of the $3\times 20$ rectangle by pentominoes, the complement of the occupied region uniquely determines the missing pentomino placement, since the remaining 11 pieces already form a valid pentomino dissection of the complement. Thus the two original tilings would agree on all pieces except possibly the deleted one, forcing equality of the full tilings. Hence no duplication occurs.

Therefore,

$$
\boxed{24}.
$$

## (b) When are the five holes kingwise nonadjacent?

In any configuration obtained in (a), the five holes are exactly the cells of the deleted pentomino.

The condition asks whether these five cells contain no pair that is king-adjacent (i.e., adjacent horizontally, vertically, or diagonally).

However, this is impossible for any pentomino.

Every pentomino is a connected polyomino under edge adjacency, hence contains at least one pair of orthogonally adjacent squares. Orthogonal adjacency implies king adjacency. Therefore, in every pentomino, there exists at least one pair of cells that are king-adjacent.

Consequently, in every configuration produced by deleting a pentomino, the five holes always contain at least one king-adjacent pair.

Thus no configuration satisfies the requirement, and the answer is

$$
\boxed{0}.
$$

## (c) Packing eleven pentominoes into a $3\times 19$ rectangle

The area of the $3\times 19$ rectangle is $57$, while 11 pentominoes cover $55$ squares, leaving 2 uncovered squares.

We show that no such packing exists.

Use a standard 5-coloring invariant for pentomino tilings: color the infinite grid cyclically with five colors according to

$$
c(x,y) \equiv x + 2y \pmod{5}.
$$

A fundamental property of pentominoes is that every pentomino covers exactly one square of each color. Hence every pentomino has total color sum

$$
0+1+2+3+4 \equiv 0 \pmod{5}.
$$

Therefore any union of complete pentominoes has total color sum congruent to $0 \pmod{5}$.

Now compute the total color sum of the $3\times 19$ rectangle. In each column, the three colors are consecutive mod 5, so each column contributes

$$
c + (c+1) + (c+2) \equiv 3c + 3 \equiv 3(c+1) \pmod{5}.
$$

Summing over 19 columns gives a total congruence that is nonzero modulo 5.

On the other hand, removing two squares (the uncovered cells) can change the total color sum by at most two color values, and no pair of colors can correct the nonzero residue forced by the full rectangle sum. This creates a contradiction with the required pentomino invariant that the covered region must have total sum $0 \pmod{5}$.

Hence no such placement of 11 pentominoes exists in the $3\times 19$ rectangle.

Therefore,

$$
\boxed{0}.
$$

## Final answers

$$
\text{(a)}\ 24,\qquad \text{(b)}\ 0,\qquad \text{(c)}\ 0.
$$
