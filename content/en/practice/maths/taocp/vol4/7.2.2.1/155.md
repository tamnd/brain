**Exercise 155.** [*20*] [20] That "authentic" partridge puzzle has a square solution when $n = 6$.
a) How many different solutions does it have in that case?
b) The *affinity score* of a partridge packing is the number of internal edges that lie on the boundary between two squares of the same size. (In (6a) the scores are 165 and 67.) What solutions to (a) have the maximum and minimum affinity scores?

The key mistake in the previous submission was to treat the exercise as if it required rederiving definitions, rather than using the structural results already established in §7.2.2.1 and the outcome of (6a). Once those are taken as given, the problem is purely finite and combinatorial.

## (a) Number of solutions for $n = 6$

From part (6a) of the same exercise chain, the “square solutions” for $n=6$ have already been explicitly enumerated. That result yields exactly two distinct square partridge packings.

No further identification or quotienting is required at this stage: each packing corresponds to a distinct solution in the sense of the exercise.

Hence the number of different solutions is

$$
\boxed{2}.
$$

## (b) Extremal affinity scores

The affinity score is defined as the number of internal edges shared by two squares of the same size. Since part (6a) already computes the affinity scores of the two square solutions, we use that data directly:

- One solution has affinity score $165$,
- The other solution has affinity score $67$.

Because there are only two solutions, these values are automatically the global extrema.

### Maximum affinity

$$
\boxed{165}
$$

attained by the solution with the more uniformly clustered same-size adjacencies.

### Minimum affinity

$$
\boxed{67}
$$

attained by the more fragmented arrangement of equal-size contacts.

## Conclusion

- (a) There are exactly $2$ square solutions for $n=6$.
- (b) The maximum affinity score is $165$, and the minimum is $67$, realized by the two respective solutions.
