## Solution

Let a solution of the prime square problem be an arrangement of numbers forming an $n \times n$ square that satisfies the constraints stated in the text. The product associated with a solution is the product of all entries in the square.

Two solutions have the same product if and only if the multisets of entries appearing in the two squares are identical, since multiplication in $\mathbb{Z}$ depends only on multiplicities of factors.

The constraints of the prime square problem do not determine a unique placement of a given admissible multiset up to transpose. The exact cover formulation underlying the problem is invariant under independent permutations of rows and columns that preserve feasibility. Such permutations generally produce solutions that are not related by transposition, since transposition is a single specific involution exchanging rows and columns, while independent row and column relabelings form a strictly larger symmetry class.
