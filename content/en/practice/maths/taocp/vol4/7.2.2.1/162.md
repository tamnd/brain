**Exercise 162.** [*24*] [24] Say that a $\mathcal{Q}_n$ is an $n \times n$ array of $n$ nonattacking queens. Sometimes a $\mathcal{Q}_m$ contains a $\mathcal{Q}_n$ for $m < n$; for example, eight of the possible $\mathcal{Q}_5$'s contain a $\mathcal{Q}_4$, and the $\mathcal{Q}_7$ illustrated here contains both a $\mathcal{Q}_3$ and a $\mathcal{Q}_5$.

What is the smallest $n$ such that at least one $\mathcal{Q}_n$ contains (a) two $\mathcal{Q}_4$'s? (b) three $\mathcal{Q}_4$'s? (c) four $\mathcal{Q}_4$'s? (d) five $\mathcal{Q}_4$'s? (e) two $\mathcal{Q}_5$'s? (f) three $\mathcal{Q}_5$'s? (g) four $\mathcal{Q}_5$'s? (h) two $\mathcal{Q}_6$'s? (i) three $\mathcal{Q}_6$'s?

A $\mathcal{Q}_n$ corresponds to a permutation $\pi$ of ${1,\dots,n}$, with queens at $(i,\pi(i))$. Thus each row contains exactly one queen, and each column contains exactly one queen.

A $\mathcal{Q}_m$ contained in a given $\mathcal{Q}_n$ means a selection of $m$ queens that still form a nonattacking configuration, i.e., no two share a row or column. Since the original configuration already has exactly one queen per row and column, any choice of $m$ distinct rows automatically yields $m$ queens in distinct columns. Hence every subset $S \subseteq {1,\dots,n}$ with $|S|=m$ determines a valid $\mathcal{Q}_m$, namely

$$
\{(i,\pi(i)) : i \in S\}.
$$

Different row subsets produce different sets of queens, so the number of distinct $\mathcal{Q}_m$ subconfigurations in a given $\mathcal{Q}_n$ is exactly

$$
\binom{n}{m}.
$$

Therefore, the problem reduces to finding the smallest $n$ such that

$$
\binom{n}{m} \ge k.
$$

We now evaluate each case.

### (a)–(d): $\mathcal{Q}_4$

We compute:

$$
\binom{4}{4}=1,\quad \binom{5}{4}=5,\quad \binom{6}{4}=15.
$$

Thus the smallest $n$ with $\binom{n}{4}\ge k$ for $k \le 5$ is $n=5$.

- (a) $k=2$: $n=5$
- (b) $k=3$: $n=5$
- (c) $k=4$: $n=5$
- (d) $k=5$: $n=5$

### (e)–(g): $\mathcal{Q}_5$

$$
\binom{5}{5}=1,\quad \binom{6}{5}=6,\quad \binom{7}{5}=21.
$$

Thus the smallest $n$ with $\binom{n}{5}\ge k$ for $k \le 6$ is $n=6$.

- (e) $k=2$: $n=6$
- (f) $k=3$: $n=6$
- (g) $k=4$: $n=6$

### (h)–(i): $\mathcal{Q}_6$

$$
\binom{6}{6}=1,\quad \binom{7}{6}=7,\quad \binom{8}{6}=28.
$$

Thus the smallest $n$ with $\binom{n}{6}\ge k$ for $k \le 7$ is $n=7$.

- (h) $k=2$: $n=7$
- (i) $k=3$: $n=7$

### Final answers

- (a) $5$
- (b) $5$
- (c) $5$
- (d) $5$
- (e) $6$
- (f) $6$
- (g) $6$
- (h) $7$
- (i) $7$
