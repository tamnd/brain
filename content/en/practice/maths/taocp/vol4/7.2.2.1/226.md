[Section 7.2.2.1: Dancing Links](../)

**Exercise 226.** [*M30*] Assume that $a_1 \ldots a_{2n}$ is a Langford pairing, and let $a'_k = a_{2n+1-k}$ so that $a'_1 \ldots a'_{2n}$ is the reverse of $a_1 \ldots a_{2n}$. Are there any obvious relations between the sums

$$\Sigma_1 = \textstyle\sum_{k=1}^{2n} k a_k, \quad \Sigma_2 = \textstyle\sum_{k=1}^{2n} k a_k^2, \quad \Sigma_3 = \textstyle\sum_{k=1}^{2n} k^2 a_k, \quad \Sigma_4 = \textstyle\sum_{k=1}^{2n} k^2 a_k^2$$

What about the analogous sums $S = \sum_{k=1}^{2n} k a_k^3$ and $S' = \sum_{k=1}^{2n} k(a_k^2)^2$?

## Setup

Let $a_1,\ldots,a_{2n}$ be a Langford pairing on ${1,\ldots,n}$, so each value $k \in {1,\ldots,n}$ occurs exactly twice among $a_1,\ldots,a_{2n}$. Define the reversed sequence $a'_k = a_{2n+1-k}$ for $1 \le k \le 2n$.

Define

$$
\Sigma_1 = \sum_{k=1}^{2n} k a_k,\quad
\Sigma_2 = \sum_{k=1}^{2n} k a_k^2,\quad
\Sigma_3 = \sum_{k=1}^{2n} k^2 a_k,\quad
\Sigma_4 = \sum_{k=1}^{2n} k^2 a_k^2.
$$

For the reversed sequence define the analogous quantities

$$
\Sigma_1' = \sum_{k=1}^{2n} k a'_k,\quad
\Sigma_2' = \sum_{k=1}^{2n} k (a'_k)^2,\quad
\Sigma_3' = \sum_{k=1}^{2n} k^2 a'_k,\quad
\Sigma_4' = \sum_{k=1}^{2n} k^2 (a'_k)^2.
$$

Since $a'_k = a_{2n+1-k}$, all relations reduce to reindexing sums over $a_k$.

The total multiplicities give

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{2n} a_k = 2\sum_{t=1}^n t = n(n+1),
$$

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{2n} a_k^2 = 2\sum_{t=1}^n t^2 = \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{3},
$$

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{2n} a_k^3 = 2\sum_{t=1}^n t^3 = 2\left(\frac{n(n+1)}{2}\right)^2,
$$

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{2n} a_k^4 = 2\sum_{t=1}^n t^4.
$$

## Solution

Start with $\Sigma_1'$. Substituting $a'_k = a_{2n+1-k}$ gives

$$
\Sigma_1' = \sum_{k=1}^{2n} k a_{2n+1-k}.
$$

Set $t = 2n+1-k$, so $k = 2n+1-t$. The index $t$ also runs from $1$ to $2n$, hence

$$
\Sigma_1' = \sum_{t=1}^{2n} (2n+1-t)a_t.
$$

Expanding yields

$$
\Sigma_1' = (2n+1)\sum_{t=1}^{2n} a_t - \sum_{t=1}^{2n} t a_t
= (2n+1)n(n+1) - \Sigma_1.
$$

For $\Sigma_2'$, the same substitution gives

$$
\Sigma_2' = \sum_{k=1}^{2n} k a_{2n+1-k}^2
= \sum_{t=1}^{2n} (2n+1-t)a_t^2.
$$

Thus

$$
\Sigma_2' = (2n+1)\sum_{t=1}^{2n} a_t^2 - \Sigma_2
= (2n+1)\frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{3} - \Sigma_2.
$$

For $\Sigma_3'$, write

$$
\Sigma_3' = \sum_{k=1}^{2n} k^2 a_{2n+1-k}.
$$

With $t = 2n+1-k$,

$$
\Sigma_3' = \sum_{t=1}^{2n} (2n+1-t)^2 a_t.
$$

Expanding $(2n+1-t)^2$ gives

$$
(2n+1)^2 - 2(2n+1)t + t^2.
$$

Therefore

$$
\Sigma_3'
= (2n+1)^2\sum a_t - 2(2n+1)\sum t a_t + \sum t^2 a_t,
$$

so

$$
\Sigma_3' = (2n+1)^2 n(n+1) - 2(2n+1)\Sigma_1 + \Sigma_3.
$$

For $\Sigma_4'$,

$$
\Sigma_4' = \sum_{k=1}^{2n} k^2 a_{2n+1-k}^2
= \sum_{t=1}^{2n} (2n+1-t)^2 a_t^2,
$$

and expanding again yields

$$
\Sigma_4'
= (2n+1)^2\sum a_t^2 - 2(2n+1)\Sigma_2 + \Sigma_4.
$$

Substituting $\sum a_t^2 = \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{3}$ gives the explicit form.

For the cubic-weight sum,

$$
S = \sum_{k=1}^{2n} k a_k^3,
\qquad
S' = \sum_{k=1}^{2n} k (a'_k)^3.
$$

Then

$$
S' = \sum_{k=1}^{2n} k a_{2n+1-k}^3
= \sum_{t=1}^{2n} (2n+1-t)a_t^3,
$$

so

$$
S' = (2n+1)\sum a_t^3 - S
= (2n+1)\cdot 2\left(\frac{n(n+1)}{2}\right)^2 - S.
$$

For the fourth-power analogue,

$$
S'_{\text{(4)}} = \sum_{k=1}^{2n} k (a_k^2)^2 = \sum_{k=1}^{2n} k a_k^4,
$$

and reversing gives

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{2n} k a_{2n+1-k}^4
= (2n+1)\sum a_t^4 - \sum k a_k^4,
$$

so the same linear relation holds between the forward and reversed fourth-power sums.
