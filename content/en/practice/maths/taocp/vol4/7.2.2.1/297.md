**Exercise 297.** [*46*] (P. Torbijn, 1989.) Can the 35 hexominoes be packed into six $6 \times 6$ squares?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 3m39s

The previous solution fails because it tried to manufacture a symmetry constraint on a single configuration. Once that is removed, there is no remaining valid invariant in the argument. The correct way to repair the solution is to restart from what would actually be required for an impossibility proof: a legitimate invariant that every placement must satisfy and that is violated by the combined contribution of all 35 hexominoes. No such invariant is produced by the modular-residue argument given.

We now re-express the problem in the correct algebraic framework and show where the obstruction attempt breaks down.

Label the six $6\times 6$ boards as a single region $R$. We are placing 35 distinct hexominoes, each covering exactly 6 cells, so the total occupied area is $210$, leaving exactly 6 empty cells in $R$, which has size $216$.

A standard way to attempt a TAOCP-style obstruction is to assign weights to cells and compare the total weight of the region with the sum of weights contributed by the pieces. Any valid invariant must have the form

$$
\sum_{(i,j)\in R} w(i,j) \;=\; \sum_{k=1}^{35} \sum_{(i,j)\in P_k} w(i,j),
$$

and must hold for every placement.

The earlier solution implicitly tried to use residue classes modulo $3$, but did so only at the level of aggregate symmetry, not at the level of a true invariant. The key issue is that uniformity of a grid under a coloring does not imply any constraint on individual polyominoes unless the weighting is preserved under translations and compatible with the board structure.

### What actually works and what does not

A natural attempt is to use the $3\times 3$ residue coloring of the grid. Each $6\times 6$ board has exactly 4 cells in each residue class, so any valid placement must satisfy a global constraint on the total occupancy of each residue class.

However, this constraint is extremely weak. It only says that the _sum over all pieces plus holes_ is uniform:

$$
x_{ab} + u_{ab} = 24.
$$

This does not constrain how individual hexominoes contribute to the vector $(x_{ab})$, because there is no reason that each piece should respect any fixed distribution across residue classes.

The earlier argument attempted to strengthen this into a statement about symmetry of the _set of all hexominoes_. That is invalid: invariance of the set under rotations and reflections does not induce any constraint on a single chosen selection of placements.

### Why symmetry cannot be used here

The key logical error was the implication

$$
\text{set of pieces is symmetric} \;\Rightarrow\; \text{each valid packing is symmetric}.
$$

This is false in general combinatorial packing problems. Symmetry of the _instance_ does not enforce symmetry of the _solution_. There is no averaging argument available unless one explicitly constructs an action on the set of all packings and proves closure, which is not done here.

Thus there is no justification for concluding that

$$
x_{00}=x_{01}=\cdots=x_{22}.
$$

Once that step is removed, the modular $3$-residue argument collapses entirely, because it produces no contradiction from the correct constraints:

$$
\sum x_{ab} = 210,\quad \sum u_{ab} = 6,
$$

and nothing more.

### Exhaustion of linear invariants

One might try to salvage the approach by searching for a linear invariant (a weighting of cells modulo $m$) that is preserved by every $6\times 6$ board but not by the multiset of hexominoes. However, any such invariant must factor through the periodic structure of the $6\times 6$ rectangle. Because $6$ is divisible by $2$ and $3$, all standard periodic colorings (mod $2$, mod $3$, or their products) produce only global balancing conditions on the region, not constraints that interact rigidly with the full set of free hexominoes.

In particular, no invariant arises that forces incompatibility between the 35 pieces and the 6 holes using only residue-class or dihedral symmetry considerations. The earlier proof incorrectly assumed such a constraint existed.

### Conclusion

After removing the invalid symmetry step, the argument provides no obstruction to the packing. The residue-class computation is correct but too weak to imply impossibility, and no legitimate invariant is derived from it.

Therefore the corrected statement is:

The proposed method does not establish that the 35 hexominoes cannot be packed into six $6\times 6$ squares. The symmetry argument is invalid, and the remaining modular residue conditions are insufficient to derive a contradiction.
