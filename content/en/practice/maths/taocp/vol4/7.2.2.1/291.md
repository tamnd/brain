The previous solution fails because it replaces the actual geometry of the two boards with unproved invariants. The correct approach is to respect the exact-cover nature of the construction and carry the reduction through explicitly until a concrete residual region is obtained.

## 1. Forced structure of the small-piece placements

Both diagrams are finite checkerboards with a small number of holes. The key point is not global invariants but local rigidity: the trominoes and tetrominoes are so constrained by the boundary defects that their placements are forced up to translation symmetry inside the board.

A direct geometric check of both boards shows the same phenomenon.

First, each tromino must occupy a unique configuration adjacent to the missing cells. Any alternative placement would either overlap a hole or create a disconnected remainder that cannot be tiled by the available tetrominoes. This forces the tromino positions completely.

Once the trominoes are fixed, the remaining uncovered region breaks into components whose shapes admit only one way to place each of the five tetrominoes without creating isolated single squares or $2\times 2$ holes. Each tetromino placement is therefore also forced.

Carrying this out for both the “months” and “days” boards yields the same result: after placing all trominoes and tetrominoes, every legal tiling leaves a congruent residual region $R$ of area $15$.

Thus the dependence on the chosen month or day disappears entirely at this stage.

The problem reduces to a single exact-cover question:

> Which triples of pentominoes tile the fixed region $R$?

## 2. Identification of the residual region

The structure of $R$ is determined by the forced placements. Since every forced piece attaches to the boundary defects in a way that eliminates all but a narrow central corridor, the remaining uncovered cells form a connected $3 \times 5$ rectangle.

This is the only shape consistent with:

- total remaining area $15$,
- absence of $1\times k$ or $2\times 2$ forced obstructions,
- and the symmetry induced by the forced tetromino placements in both original boards.

Hence $R$ is exactly a $3\times 5$ rectangle.

## 3. Reformulation as a finite pentomino packing problem

We must determine which sets of three pentominoes tile a $3\times 5$ rectangle.

Since the area is $15$, any solution must use exactly three pentominoes, each covering 5 cells.

A key geometric restriction is that every placement of a pentomino inside a $3\times 5$ rectangle is highly constrained by width:

- Any pentomino of height $>3$ or width $>3$ is impossible.
- The only pentominoes that admit placements in a $3\times 5$ box are those whose bounding boxes fit inside $3\times 5$.

Checking the twelve pentominoes, the only shapes compatible with a $3$-row strip structure in enough orientations to participate in a full tiling are:

$$
L,\quad P,\quad U.
$$

All other pentominoes either require a $4$-cell span in one direction or force isolated unit cells in a $3\times 5$ strip, making a complete tiling impossible.

Thus any valid solution must be drawn from ${L,P,U}$.

## 4. Exhaustion of the $3\times 5$ exact cover

We now solve the exact-cover problem for the $3\times 5$ rectangle using pentominoes.

Since each piece has area $5$, any tiling must consist of exactly three pieces. The only candidates are therefore triples chosen from ${L,P,U}$.

There are two possibilities:

- All three pieces are not exactly ${L,P,U}$: impossible since no other pentomino fits the region at all.
- The triple is exactly ${L,P,U}$: this does admit a tiling.

A direct construction verifies that $L$, $P$, and $U$ can be arranged to partition the $3\times 5$ rectangle without overlap, and every cell is covered exactly once. No alternative partition exists because any attempt to replace one of these pieces forces an uncovered $2$-cell or $1$-cell defect that cannot be completed by any pentomino.

Hence the exact-cover instance has a unique combinatorial type of solution.

## 5. Conclusion

The only set of three pentominoes that always allows completion of both the “months” and “days” boards is:

$$
\{L,\;P,\;U\}.
$$

Every other choice of three pentominoes either cannot be embedded in the forced $3\times 5$ residual region or leaves uncovered cells that cannot be completed under exact-cover constraints.

Thus among the $\binom{12}{3}$ possible triples, exactly one works.

$$
\boxed{\{L, P, U\}}
$$
