**Exercise 230.** [*20*] Explain how Algorithm X$^8$, which minimizes the sum of option costs, can also be used to *maximize* that sum, in problems like that of Fig. 74.

## Solution

Let $S$ be a solution of the exact cover instance, so $S$ is a set of options whose union covers each item exactly once. In the situations considered in Fig. 74, every solution covers each item exactly once and therefore consists of a fixed number $|S| = k$ of options, independent of the particular solution $S$.

Let $c(O)$ denote the cost of an option $O$, and let the objective of Algorithm $X^8$ be to minimize

$$
C(S) = \sum_{O \in S} c(O).
$$

To convert this into a maximization problem, choose a constant $K$ such that $K \ge c(O)$ for every option $O$. Define a transformed cost function

$$
c'(O) = K - c(O).
$$

For any solution $S$, the transformed total cost is

$$
C'(S) = \sum_{O \in S} c'(O)
= \sum_{O \in S} (K - c(O))
= \sum_{O \in S} K - \sum_{O \in S} c(O)
= kK - C(S).
$$

Since $k$ is the same for every solution $S$, the quantity $kK$ is constant across all feasible solutions. Therefore, for any two solutions $S_1$ and $S_2$,

$$
C'(S_1) < C'(S_2) \;\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\; kK - C(S_1) < kK - C(S_2)
\;\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\; C(S_1) > C(S_2).
$$

Thus minimizing $C'(S)$ is equivalent to maximizing $C(S)$. Algorithm $X^8$ applied with costs $c'(O)$ therefore produces exactly the solutions that maximize the original sum of option costs.

This completes the proof. ∎
