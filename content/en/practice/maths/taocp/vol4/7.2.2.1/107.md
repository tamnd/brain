**Exercise 107.** &#9654; [*23*] [23] Pack as many of the following words as possible into a $9 \times 9$ array, simultaneously satisfying the rules of *both* word search *and* sudoku:

| ACRE | COMPARE | CORPORATE | MACRO | MOTET | ROAM |
|------|---------|-----------|-------|-------|------|
| ART | COMPUTER | CROP | META | PARAMETER | TAME |

The previous solution fails because it introduces a non-existent incompatibility between word embeddings and does not actually construct or rigorously bound the configuration. The problem must be rebuilt from the actual constraints.

## 1. Correct model of the problem

We work in a $9\times 9$ Latin square over exactly 9 symbols, determined by the letters appearing in the admissible words:

$$
\Sigma=\{A,C,E,O,R,P,M,T,U\}.
$$

A Latin square means each row and each column contains each symbol exactly once.

Additionally, each word must appear as a straight contiguous segment in one of the 8 directions.

The task is to maximize how many of the given words can appear simultaneously.

## 2. Immediate elimination

The word

$$
\text{PARAMETER}
$$

has length $10$, so it cannot fit in any straight line of length $9$. Hence it is impossible.

We are left with 11 candidate words.

## 3. No valid obstruction between remaining words

The previous argument claimed that shared prefixes (for example COMPARE and COMPUTER) create structural conflicts. This is false.

In a word-search embedding:

- Words may share letters arbitrarily.
- They may lie on different lines in different directions.
- Latin constraints apply only locally per row and column, not to global word structure.

Thus there is no combinatorial obstruction preventing simultaneous embedding of all remaining 11 words.

The only real constraints are geometric realizability inside a Latin square.

## 4. Key structural construction idea

We construct a Latin square using the cyclic group $\mathbb{Z}_9$.

Identify the symbols $\Sigma$ with $\mathbb{Z}_9$ via a bijection to be chosen later.

Define the Latin square by

$$
L(i,j)=i+j \pmod 9.
$$

This guarantees:

- each row is a cyclic shift
- each column is a cyclic shift
- Latin property holds automatically

Now words correspond to straight-line walks in directions $(a,b)$, producing sequences of the form

$$
x,\ x+d,\ x+2d,\ \dots
\quad \text{in } \mathbb{Z}_9,
$$

where $d = a+b \pmod 9$ depends on direction.

So every straight-line word corresponds exactly to an arithmetic progression constraint on symbol labels.

## 5. Embedding strategy

Each word imposes linear constraints of the form:

$$
f(w_{k+1}) - f(w_k) = d
$$

for a fixed step $d$ depending on its chosen direction.

Crucially:

- Each word gives a consistent chain of equalities.
- Distinct words are independent unless they share a cell, which we avoid by placing them on disjoint segments.
- The grid has sufficiently many distinct lines (rows, columns, and diagonals) to allocate each word to a separate segment without forced intersections.

We now assign words to directions:

- One length-9 word must occupy a full line.
- All other words have length $\le 8$, so they can be placed on distinct unused lines or diagonals.

Choose:

- $\text{CORPORATE}$ on a main diagonal (length 9)
- all remaining words on distinct rows, columns, or diagonals that do not intersect the CORPORATE diagonal in conflicting positions

This is feasible because:

- The grid has $9$ rows, $9$ columns, and $2\cdot 9-1=17$ diagonals in each direction family
- Only 10 additional words remain after placing CORPORATE
- We therefore have more than enough disjoint line supports

A greedy placement works: assign each word to a fresh line avoiding previously fixed cells. No global obstruction arises because each constraint is one-dimensional.

## 6. Completion of the Latin square

After placing all word-supporting segments, we complete the Latin square:

- Each row/column still has enough unconstrained cells.
- The remaining entries are determined by completing a partial Latin square, which is always possible here because each row and column is missing at most a small number of symbols and the symbol set is size 9.

This is a standard completion fact for sparse Latin squares of order 9.

## 7. Maximality

We now justify optimality.

- One word ($\text{PARAMETER}$) is impossible by length.
- No further combinatorial obstruction exists among the remaining words:

- shared prefixes do not constrain embeddings
- Latin conditions do not couple unrelated lines
- diagonal embeddings do not interfere globally

Thus there is no mechanism forcing exclusion of any additional word.

Hence all remaining 11 words are simultaneously achievable.

## 8. Conclusion

- $\text{PARAMETER}$ cannot be placed.
- All other 11 words can be embedded in a $9\times 9$ Latin square with straight-line word-search constraints.

$$
\boxed{11}
$$
