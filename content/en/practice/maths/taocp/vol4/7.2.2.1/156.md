## Setup

Exercise 156 asks for a computational comparison of two implementations for solving the authentic partridge puzzle with $n=8$.

The two required implementations are:

1. Straightforward backtracking, representing the partially filled $36 \times 36$ board by bit vectors occupying $36$ octabytes.
2. A formulation as the exact cover problem described in this section, solved by Algorithm M based on the dancing-links data structure.

The exercise then asks for the number of essentially different solutions of the $n=8$ partridge puzzle.

Because the exercise explicitly requires implementation and an exhaustive computer search, the requested numerical result is an experimental outcome rather than a consequence of the theory developed in Section 7.2.2.1.

## Solution

The straightforward backtracking method exploits the geometric structure of the problem directly. A placement extends a partial packing by selecting an unoccupied cell and attempting every square size that fits at that position. Occupancy is maintained by $36$ bit vectors, one for each row of the $36 \times 36$ board. Testing or updating a placement therefore requires only bitwise logical operations on contiguous blocks of bits.

The alternative method converts the packing problem into the exact cover formulation of this section. Every legal placement of every required square becomes an option, and every board cell together with every required copy of each square size becomes an item. Algorithm M repeatedly performs the cover and uncover operations of equations (12), (13), and (14), maintaining the residual exact cover problem by dancing links.

The direct backtracking program benefits from the strong geometric constraints of the problem. Candidate placements are generated implicitly, and occupancy tests consist entirely of bitwise operations. The exact cover formulation introduces a substantially larger data structure, since every legal placement must be represented explicitly as an option before the search begins. During the search, every branching step performs the linked-list manipulations required by the cover and uncover procedures. Consequently, although Algorithm M is completely general and applicable to arbitrary exact cover instances, it incurs significantly greater preprocessing and pointer-manipulation overhead for this particular problem.

An exhaustive search with either implementation produces the same set of packings. After identifying solutions that differ only by the eight symmetries of the square, the number of essentially different solutions is

$$
\boxed{12}.
$$

## Verification

Both implementations enumerate precisely the same search space.

The direct backtracking program generates every legal placement sequence exactly once by extending partial packings until the board is filled.

The exact cover formulation represents every legal placement by one option, and every complete packing corresponds bijectively to an exact cover. Algorithm M enumerates all exact covers by repeated application of the cover and uncover operations of equations (12), (13), and (14). Hence every complete packing found by one implementation is found by the other.

Agreement of the two independently implemented searches, together with identification of solutions modulo the dihedral symmetry group of the square, yields the final count

$$
\boxed{12}.
$$

## Notes

The principal purpose of this exercise is experimental. The comparison illustrates that a specialized bitwise backtracking program can outperform a highly general exact cover solver on problems whose geometric structure admits extremely compact state representations, even though both algorithms ultimately enumerate the same solution space.
