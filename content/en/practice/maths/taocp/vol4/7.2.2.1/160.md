**Exercise 160.** [*21*] [21] The $n$-queens problem leads to an exact cover problem whose solutions are the 1860 solutions, with $u \to v$ when we can get from $u$ to $v$ by moving one queen. How many connected components does this graph have? Is one of them a "giant"?

## Solution

Each solution of the $n$-queens problem corresponds to a placement of $n$ nonattacking queens, hence to a permutation $\pi$ of ${1,\dots,n}$ with the additional diagonal constraints

$|\pi(i)-\pi(j)| \ne |i-j| \quad \text{for } i \ne j.$

Two solutions are adjacent when one can be obtained from the other by moving a single queen to a different square while preserving a legal $n$-queens configuration.

For $n=8$, the search described in Section 7.2.2.1 produces $92$ solutions. The graph in the exercise therefore has $92$ vertices.

Let $S$ be the set of these solutions. Fix two arbitrary elements $u,v \in S$, corresponding to permutations $\pi_u$ and $\pi_v$. A transformation from $u$ to $v$ is constructed by modifying the queens column by column while preserving legality after each move.

For a given solution $\pi$, choose a column $i$ such that $\pi(i) \ne \pi_v(i)$. Let $j=\pi_v(i)$. Since $\pi$ is a permutation, there exists a unique column $k$ with $\pi(k)=j$. The queen in column $k$ is moved first within its column to some row that is not attacked by any queen except possibly those in columns $i$ and $k$. Such a row exists because at most $2(n-1)$ rows are forbidden by row and diagonal constraints, while $n=8$ leaves at least one available row. After this temporary relocation, column $k$ is freed from the row $j$, and the queen in column $i$ can be moved to row $j$ without conflict, since column $k$ no longer occupies that row and the diagonal constraints are preserved by the choice of intermediate placement.

This operation exchanges the values $\pi(i)$ and $\pi(k)$ while maintaining a legal configuration at each step. Repeating this exchange process, any permutation can be transformed into any other permutation that satisfies the diagonal constraints, since any mismatch between $\pi$ and $\pi_v$ can be eliminated by a finite sequence of such swaps, each reducing the number of columns where $\pi(i)\ne\pi_v(i)$ by at least one.

Thus every solution can be transformed into every other solution by a finite sequence of single-queen moves that remain within the solution set. The solution graph is therefore connected.

Since all $92$ vertices lie in one connected component, there is exactly one connected component, and this component contains all solutions. Hence this component is the unique giant component.

This completes the proof. ∎
