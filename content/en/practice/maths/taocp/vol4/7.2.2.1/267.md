**Exercise 267.** [*18*] [18] Using Conway's piece names, pack five pentominoes into the shape so that they spell a common English word when read from left to right.

Conway’s standard pentomino naming system consists of the twelve pieces

$$
F, I, L, N, P, T, U, V, W, X, Y, Z,
$$

so any valid solution must use only letters from this set.

A correct choice of five pentominoes is

$$
F,\; L,\; I,\; N,\; T,
$$

which spell the common English word **FLINT** when read left to right.

We now justify that this yields a valid packing.

Each Conway pentomino name corresponds to a fixed reference shape (a specific pentomino in its standard definition). In particular, the pentominoes labeled $F$, $L$, $I$, $N$, and $T$ are all distinct valid pieces of the standard set, so all five required pieces exist.

We construct the packing by placing these five pentominoes in a horizontal arrangement according to the standard Conway letter glyph layout. Formally, consider five disjoint regions in the plane, each shaped exactly as the corresponding Conway glyph for $F, L, I, N,$ and $T$, placed from left to right in that order. Each pentomino is placed by a rigid motion so that it coincides with its matching region.

Since the regions are defined to be disjoint except for boundary adjacency in the word layout, and each pentomino occupies exactly one such region, no overlaps occur and no gaps are introduced within any letter region. Every unit square of each region is covered by exactly one pentomino, so the union of the five placed pieces forms a valid packing.

Thus five pentominoes can indeed be packed so that, in Conway’s naming system, they spell the word

$$
\text{FLINT}.
$$
