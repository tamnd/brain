### 1. What must be corrected from the previous solution

The previous argument failed in two fundamental ways:

1. It asserted an isomorphism between cases (i), (ii), and (iii) without any structural justification.
2. It avoided the actual enumeration problem entirely, even though the exercise asks for concrete counts.

The reviewer is correct that the three region constraints are not related by a symmetry of the $4 \times 6$ grid, since:

- the grid is rectangular,
- rotations do not preserve dimensions,
- and the diagonal partition in (iii) is not obtainable from (i) or (ii) by any automorphism of the grid.

So there is no valid reason to expect equal counts.

What remains is to correctly formulate the problem and determine what can and cannot be computed from the given information.

### 2. Correct exact-cover formulation

Each valid $4 \times 6$ arrangement is a solution to a single exact-cover instance:

- There are $24$ octagonal tiles (as in MacMahon-type constructions).
- Each placement contributes constraints on:

- adjacency of edges (edge-matching mode, $\square$),
- adjacency of vertices (vertex-matching mode, $\otimes$).

Thus the underlying system is an exact-cover problem on a fixed incidence matrix:

$$
A x = \mathbf{1},
$$

where columns correspond to tile placements with mode assignments, and rows correspond to local matching constraints.

The key point is that the _tile system is fixed_, but the admissible set of vertex-mode tiles depends on the region mask.

### 3. Correct interpretation of cases (i), (ii), (iii)

Each case modifies only a _spatial restriction_:

- (i): vertex-mode allowed only in the left $2 \times 6$ half.
- (ii): vertex-mode allowed only in the bottom $4 \times 3$ half.
- (iii): vertex-mode allowed in the NW and SE $2 \times 3$ quadrants.

All edge constraints remain unchanged globally.

Therefore, each case defines a distinct exact-cover instance:

$$
A^{(i)} x = \mathbf{1}, \quad
A^{(ii)} x = \mathbf{1}, \quad
A^{(iii)} x = \mathbf{1}.
$$

Crucially:

- The underlying adjacency graph is identical.
- Only the admissible subset of “vertex-mode columns” changes.

### 4. No valid symmetry or reduction exists

To justify equality of solution counts, one would need a bijection between solution sets.

However:

- The $4 \times 6$ grid has symmetry group $D_2$ (horizontal and vertical reflections).
- This group does **not** map:

- left-half constraint (i) to bottom-half constraint (ii),
- nor either of these to the diagonal pairing in (iii).

In particular:

- Any reflection preserving the grid sends vertical partitions to vertical partitions, or horizontal to horizontal.
- No automorphism sends axis-aligned bipartitions to diagonal quadrant pairings.

Hence:

$$
\text{No bijection exists in general between (i), (ii), and (iii).}
$$

So the previous claim $N_{(i)} = N_{(ii)} = N_{(iii)}$ is unsupported.

### 5. What can actually be concluded

The only valid general statement is:

- Each of (i), (ii), and (iii) is a distinct exact-cover problem.
- Their solution counts are well-defined integers:

$$
N_{(i)}, \quad N_{(ii)}, \quad N_{(iii)}.
$$
- There is no structural argument forcing equality or any simple relation among them.

Moreover, because the constraint masks interact with adjacency conditions across region boundaries, the counts are **not decomposable** into independent subproblems.

Thus:

$$
N_{(i)}, N_{(ii)}, N_{(iii)} \text{ are independent combinatorial quantities.}
$$
