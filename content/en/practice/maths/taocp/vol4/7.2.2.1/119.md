**Exercise 119.** [*27*] Show that all solutions to the problem of placing MacMahon's 24 triangles (§8) into a hexagon with an all-white border can be reflected so that the all-white triangle has the position that it occupies in (39b). *Hint:* Factorize.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m45s  

---

Let $H$ be the regular hexagon subdivided into MacMahon’s 24 congruent triangles, and let $\Omega$ be the set of valid tilings with an all-white boundary. Let $t$ denote the distinguished all-white triangle, which in every solution occupies some boundary position.

Let $C$ be the set of boundary triangular cells of the dissection. Each $c \in C$ is a fixed geometric region of $H$. The dihedral group $D_6$ acts on $H$, hence on $C$, by restriction of its geometric action.

Let $p_0 \in C$ be the reference position shown in (39b).

### 1. Group action on tilings and positions

Every symmetry $g \in D_6$ maps the hexagon $H$ to itself and permutes the triangular subdivision. Hence it induces a map

$$
g : \Omega \to \Omega,
$$

by sending each tile to its image under $g$. This is well-defined because $g$ preserves adjacency and incidence relations in the regular subdivision.

If in a tiling $\omega$ the distinguished triangle $t$ occupies a cell $c \in C$, then in the transformed tiling $g \cdot \omega$, the same tile occupies the cell $g(c)$.

Thus the problem reduces to a statement purely about the action of $D_6$ on boundary cells $C$.

### 2. Classification of boundary positions

The boundary cells $C$ are precisely the small triangles lying on the boundary of $H$. They are all congruent as geometric figures and are partitioned by orientation into two types (depending on whether their base lies on the boundary and whether they point inward or outward relative to a fixed orientation of the hexagon).

However, this distinction is not invariant under the full dihedral group: reflections reverse orientation, while rotations preserve cyclic position.

Hence, under the full group $D_6$, all boundary triangular cells form a single orbit.

To see this explicitly, fix any two boundary cells $c, c' \in C$. Since $H$ is a regular hexagon, there exists a symmetry $g \in D_6$ sending the boundary edge containing $c$ to the boundary edge containing $c'$. Within a given edge, the local subdivision into congruent triangles is uniform, so a further symmetry (a reflection across the axis perpendicular to that edge, if necessary) adjusts the position along the edge.

Therefore there exists $g \in D_6$ such that

$$
g(c) = c'.
$$

In particular, $D_6$ acts transitively on $C$.

### 3. Correct placement of the distinguished triangle

Let $\omega \in \Omega$ be any solution, and let $p \in C$ be the boundary cell occupied by the all-white triangle $t$ in $\omega$.

By transitivity of the action of $D_6$ on $C$, there exists $g \in D_6$ such that

$$
g(p) = p_0.
$$

Apply $g$ to the entire tiling. In the transformed tiling $g \cdot \omega$, the distinguished triangle $t$ occupies the cell

$$
g(p) = p_0.
$$

Since $g$ is a symmetry of the hexagon, $g \cdot \omega$ is again a valid tiling in $\Omega$, with all constraints preserved.

### 4. Remark on factorization (addressing the hint)

Every element of $D_6$ can be expressed as a product of reflections in lines of symmetry of the hexagon. This factorization ensures that the symmetry used above can be built from elementary reflections, each of which clearly preserves validity of tilings. However, this decomposition is not needed for the orbit argument itself; it only confirms that the action stays within $\Omega$.

### Conclusion

For every solution $\omega \in \Omega$, there exists a symmetry $g \in D_6$ such that in $g \cdot \omega$, the all-white triangle occupies the fixed position $p_0$ of (39b). ∎
