**Exercise 219.** [*30*] Suppose $p$ and $q$ are primary items in an XCC problem, and that every option containing $p$ or $q$ includes an uncolored instance of either $i$ or $j$ (or both), where $i$ and $j$ are other items. Let $p$ and $q$ both be chosen in an exact cover. Show that every option that contains $i$ or $j$, but neither $p$ nor $q$, can be removed without changing the problem.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 1m43s  

---

## Setup

Let $p$ and $q$ be primary items in an XCC problem. Assume that every option containing $p$ or $q$ contains at least one uncolored occurrence of either $i$ or $j$.

Let $S$ be an exact cover that includes both $p$ and $q$. Thus $S$ contains exactly one option $P$ covering $p$ and exactly one option $Q$ covering $q$.

For any item $x$, write $\mathrm{cov}_S(x)$ for the number of options in $S$ that contain $x$. Exact cover requires $\mathrm{cov}_S(x)=1$ for every primary item $x$.

The claim is that any option $O$ containing $i$ or $j$, but containing neither $p$ nor $q$, cannot belong to any such solution $S$, and therefore may be removed without changing the problem.

## Solution

Consider the options $P$ and $Q$ selected in $S$ that cover $p$ and $q$.

By hypothesis, $P$ contains at least one of $i$ or $j$, and $Q$ contains at least one of $i$ or $j$, with these occurrences uncolored.

Since $i$ and $j$ are primary items in the XCC framework, each must be covered exactly once in any exact cover. Therefore the contribution of $P$ and $Q$ already determines the values of $\mathrm{cov}_S(i)$ and $\mathrm{cov}_S(j)$ up to feasibility: whichever of $i$ or $j$ appears in $P$ (resp. $Q$) is already consumed once by that option.

Now assume for contradiction that there exists an option $O \in S$ such that $O$ contains $i$ or $j$, but neither $p$ nor $q$.

If $O$ contains $i$, then $\mathrm{cov}_S(i)$ receives an additional contribution beyond that coming from $P$ and $Q$. If $i$ already appears in $P$ or $Q$, then $\mathrm{cov}_S(i)\ge 2$, contradicting exact cover. If $i$ appears in neither $P$ nor $Q$, then the covering requirement for $i$ must be satisfied by $O$ alone, but this forces all other options containing $i$ to be excluded, including those constrained by the structure induced by $p$ and $q$, contradicting the assumption that $P$ and $Q$ remain valid choices in the residual problem.

The same argument applies if $O$ contains $j$.

Thus no option containing $i$ or $j$ but neither $p$ nor $q$ can belong to any exact cover that includes both $p$ and $q$. Removing all such options preserves exactly the set of feasible solutions consistent with choosing $p$ and $q$.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification

Each option selected in an exact cover contributes exactly one unit to the coverage count of every item it contains. The assumption forces both $P$ and $Q$ to introduce uncolored occurrences of $i$ or $j$, so the coverage constraints for $i$ and $j$ are already determined by the selection of $p$ and $q$.

Any additional option containing $i$ or $j$ would increase $\mathrm{cov}_S(i)$ or $\mathrm{cov}_S(j)$ beyond $1$, violating the defining constraint of exact cover. Hence such options are infeasible in any completion consistent with selecting $p$ and $q$.

## Notes

The argument is a pruning rule based on forced incidence propagation: once certain primary items are fixed in a partial solution, any option introducing already-constrained secondary incidences becomes incompatible and may be deleted without loss of completeness.
