**Exercise 228.** [*M30*] The Langford pairings for $n = 16$ that minimize $\Sigma_1$ turn out to be precisely the 12,016 pairings that minimize $\Sigma_1$; and their reversals turn out to be precisely the 12,016 pairings that *maximize* both $\Sigma_2$ and $\Sigma_1$. Is this surprising, or what?

### Corrected solution

Let $A=(a_1,\dots,a_{2n})$ be a Langford pairing for $n=16$. Each symbol $k\in\{1,\dots,n\}$ occurs exactly twice.

Define the reversal $A'=(a'_1,\dots,a'_{2n})$ by

$$
a'_i = a_{2n+1-i}.
$$

Define

$$
\Sigma_1(A)=\sum_{i=1}^{2n} i\,a_i,\qquad 
\Sigma_2(A)=\sum_{i=1}^{2n} i\,a_i^2.
$$

## 1. Effect of reversal

### $\Sigma_1$

$$
\Sigma_1(A')=\sum_{i=1}^{2n} i\,a_{2n+1-i}.
$$

Let $j=2n+1-i$. Then $i=2n+1-j$, so

$$
\Sigma_1(A')=\sum_{j=1}^{2n} (2n+1-j)a_j
=(2n+1)\sum_{j=1}^{2n} a_j-\sum_{j=1}^{2n} j a_j.
$$

Since each $k$ appears exactly twice,

$$
\sum_{j=1}^{2n} a_j = 2\sum_{k=1}^n k = n(n+1),
$$

which is constant over all Langford pairings. Hence

$$
\Sigma_1(A')=(2n+1)n(n+1)-\Sigma_1(A).
$$

Thus reversal transforms $\Sigma_1$ by an affine reflection.

### $\Sigma_2$

Similarly,

$$
\Sigma_2(A')=\sum_{i=1}^{2n} i\,a_{2n+1-i}^2
=\sum_{j=1}^{2n} (2n+1-j)a_j^2.
$$

So

$$
\Sigma_2(A')=(2n+1)\sum_{j=1}^{2n} a_j^2-\sum_{j=1}^{2n} j a_j^2.
$$

Since each $k$ appears twice,

$$
\sum_{j=1}^{2n} a_j^2 = 2\sum_{k=1}^n k^2,
$$

also constant. Hence

$$
\Sigma_2(A')=(2n+1)\cdot 2\sum_{k=1}^n k^2-\Sigma_2(A).
$$

Thus reversal also transforms $\Sigma_2$ by an affine reflection.

## 2. Consequences

For each $i\in\{1,2\}$, there exists a constant $C_i$ (depending only on $n$) such that

$$
\Sigma_i(A') = C_i - \Sigma_i(A).
$$

Hence reversal is an involutive bijection on Langford pairings satisfying:

- $A$ minimizes $\Sigma_i$ if and only if $A'$ maximizes $\Sigma_i$,
- $A$ maximizes $\Sigma_i$ if and only if $A'$ minimizes $\Sigma_i$.

In particular, reversal exchanges the minimizers and maximizers of each statistic separately.

## 3. What can and cannot be concluded

Let

$$
\mathcal{M}_1 = \arg\min \Sigma_1,\qquad \mathcal{M}_2 = \arg\min \Sigma_2.
$$

From the reversal identities alone we obtain only:

$$
A\in \mathcal{M}_1 \;\Longleftrightarrow\; A' \in \arg\max \Sigma_1,
$$

$$
A\in \mathcal{M}_2 \;\Longleftrightarrow\; A' \in \arg\max \Sigma_2.
$$

There is no structural reason, and no derivation from the reversal symmetry, that forces

$$
\mathcal{M}_1 = \mathcal{M}_2.
$$

Likewise, nothing implies that the maximizers of $\Sigma_1$ and $\Sigma_2$ coincide.

These are independent optimization problems on the same finite set.

## 4. Interpretation of the exercise statement

The exercise asserts a special fact for $n=16$:

- The minimizers of $\Sigma_1$ form a set of size $12016$,
- the reversals of these pairings are exactly the pairings that simultaneously maximize both $\Sigma_1$ and $\Sigma_2$.

What is genuinely structural is only this:

- reversal converts minimization of each statistic into maximization of the same statistic,
- so any statement about “reversals of minimizers” automatically translates into a statement about maximizers.

What is _not_ structural is the interaction between $\Sigma_1$ and $\Sigma_2$. Their extremal sets are unrelated a priori.

## 5. Conclusion

The correct logical content is:

- Reversal induces affine reflections of both $\Sigma_1$ and $\Sigma_2$.
- Therefore it bijects minimizers and maximizers of each statistic separately.
- The coincidence, for $n=16$, between:

- reversals of $\Sigma_1$-minimizers, and
- simultaneous maximizers of both $\Sigma_1$ and $\Sigma_2$,

is not a consequence of reversal symmetry.

It reflects an additional, non-obvious degeneracy of the Langford solution space at $n=16$, where two distinct quadratic-weighted optimization criteria happen to select compatible extremal configurations.

## Final remark

What is surprising is not the existence of the reversal symmetry, which is unavoidable, but the alignment of extremal structures of two different objective functions on a highly constrained combinatorial set.
