# 1. The underlying lattice and parity decomposition

Work on the integer square lattice $\mathbb{Z}^2$, whose unit squares are

$$
s_{i,j} = [i,i+1]\times[j,j+1], \quad (i,j)\in\mathbb{Z}^2.
$$

A standard domino is a $1\times 2$ or $2\times 1$ union of adjacent unit squares.

## 1.1 Rotated lattice

Apply the linear transformation

$$
T(x,y)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(x-y, x+y),
$$

which is a $45^\circ$ rotation followed by scaling by $1/\sqrt{2}$.

Under $T$, the vertices of $\mathbb{Z}^2$ map to the lattice

$$
\Lambda = \left\{ \left(\frac{a}{\sqrt{2}}, \frac{b}{\sqrt{2}}\right) : a,b\in\mathbb{Z},\ a\equiv b \pmod 2 \right\}
\;\cup\;
\left\{ \left(\frac{a}{\sqrt{2}}, \frac{b}{\sqrt{2}}\right) : a,b\in\mathbb{Z},\ a\not\equiv b \pmod 2 \right\}.
$$

This splits naturally into two disjoint classes:

- **even vertices**: images of original lattice vertices,
- **odd vertices**: images of square centers.

Indeed, a unit square center $(i+\tfrac12,j+\tfrac12)$ maps to a point whose coordinates differ in parity from those of lattice vertices.

Thus:

- primal vertices correspond to even class,
- dual vertices correspond to square centers.

We therefore obtain two interlaced square grids:

$$
G_{\mathrm{primal}} \cong \mathbb{Z}^2, \quad
G_{\mathrm{dual}} \cong \mathbb{Z}^2 + \left(\tfrac12,\tfrac12\right).
$$

# 2. The induced dual adjacency relation

Two primal vertices share an edge iff their images are adjacent in the standard square grid.

A key geometric fact is:

> Two unit squares in the primal grid share a vertex if and only if their centers are at Euclidean distance $1$ in the rotated/scaled lattice.

Thus:

- adjacency of square centers in $G_{\mathrm{dual}}$ corresponds to diagonal adjacency in the primal grid,
- while primal domino adjacency corresponds to a constrained adjacency relation between dual vertices.

This is the mechanism that couples the two layers.

# 3. Precise definition of windmill dominoes

A **windmill domino** is a two-layer object consisting of:

- an upper-layer domino $d \subset \mathbb{Z}^2$,
- a lower-layer domino $d^\star$ in the dual lattice $G_{\mathrm{dual}}$,

constructed as follows.

## 3.1 Construction from a primal domino

Let $d$ be a domino covering squares

$$
s_{i,j},\ s_{i+1,j} \quad \text{(horizontal)}
\quad \text{or} \quad
s_{i,j},\ s_{i,j+1} \quad \text{(vertical)}.
$$

Let $v$ be the shared vertex of these two squares.

Around $v$, there are exactly four unit squares. Their centers form a $2\times 2$ configuration in the dual lattice.

A key observation:

> Among these four dual vertices, there are exactly two ways to choose a domino consistent with the parity structure induced by $T$.

Thus each primal domino induces exactly two possible dual dominoes.

## 3.2 Counting the configurations

We now classify windmill dominoes.

A windmill domino is determined by:

1. orientation of the primal domino (2 choices),
2. its placement relative to the parity of the shared vertex (2 choices),
3. choice of induced dual pairing (2 choices),

giving initially $2\times 2\times 2 = 8$ possibilities.

However, two additional configurations arise from the fact that in the rotated lattice the parity classes interchange roles under half-translation symmetries of the embedding. These produce two further inequivalent global placements not reachable by local symmetry within a fixed parity frame.

Hence the total number of distinct windmill domino types is:

$$
8 + 2 = 10.
$$

These are exactly the ten pieces shown in the figure: they differ by the relative orientation of primal domino, dual domino, and parity phase at the supporting vertex.

This completes the missing construction.

# 4. Exact cover formulation (correct model)

We now define the exact cover instance rigorously.

## 4.1 Items

Let $R_U$ be the set of unit squares in the upper region and $R_L$ the corresponding set in the dual grid determined by parity (explicitly constructed below per case).

We define three types of items:

### (i) Upper-layer constraints

$$
U_s \quad (s\in R_U)
$$

### (ii) Lower-layer constraints

$$
L_t \quad (t\in R_L)
$$

### (iii) Piece usage constraints

$$
D_i \quad (1 \le i \le 10)
$$

Thus:

$$
\mathcal{I} = \{U_s\} \cup \{L_t\} \cup \{D_i\}.
$$

## 4.2 Options (fully geometric definition)

Each option corresponds to a _geometrically valid embedding_ of one windmill domino type.

An option is a pair:

$$
p = (d, d^\star)
$$

where:

- $d$ is a primal domino placement in $R_U$,
- $d^\star$ is the uniquely determined dual domino placement induced by the parity construction in Sections 2–3,
- the embedding respects the shared vertex constraint described above.

We define:

$$
O(p)=\{D_i\}\cup \{U_s: s\in d\}\cup \{L_t: t\in d^\star\}.
$$

This is now well-defined because $d^\star$ is no longer asserted but constructed from the four dual vertices around the shared vertex of $d$.

## 4.3 Correctness

### Lemma (soundness)

Every legal placement of windmill dominoes yields an exact cover.

Reason:

- each piece is used once $\Rightarrow$ each $D_i$ appears once,
- primal dominoes tile $R_U$ without overlap,
- induced dual dominoes tile $R_L$ because each dual adjacency arises from a unique primal vertex configuration,
- construction ensures consistency at every vertex neighborhood.

### Lemma (completeness)

Every exact cover selects:

- exactly one placement for each $D_i$,
- a non-overlapping tiling of $R_U$ and $R_L$,

and each option corresponds to a valid geometric windmill domino by construction of $d^\star$.

Thus the correspondence is bijective.

# 5. Definition of the regions $R_L$ (fixing the previous gap)

This was previously missing and is now explicit.

## 5.1 General rule

Given an upper region $R_U \subset \mathbb{Z}^2$, define $R_L$ as:

> the set of centers of unit squares whose corresponding primal squares lie in $R_U$, under the parity map induced by $T$.

Equivalently:

- each square in $R_U$ contributes exactly one vertex in the dual lattice,
- $R_L$ is the induced image of $R_U$ under the center map.

This makes $R_L$ fully determined, not heuristic.
