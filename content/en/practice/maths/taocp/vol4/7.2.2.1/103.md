**Exercise 103.** [*M28*] Musical pitches in the Western system of "equal temperament" are the notes whose frequency is $440 \cdot 2^{n/12}$ cycles per second, for some integer $n$. The *pitch class* of such a note is $n \bmod 12$, and seven of the twelve possible pitch classes are conventionally designated by letters:

$$0 = \mathrm{A}, \quad 2 = \mathrm{B}, \quad 3 = \mathrm{C}, \quad 5 = \mathrm{D}, \quad 7 = \mathrm{E}, \quad 8 = \mathrm{F}, \quad 10 = \mathrm{G}.$$

The other classes are named by appending sharp ($\sharp$) or flat ($\flat$) signs, to go up or down by 1; thus $1 = \mathrm{A}\sharp = \mathrm{B}\flat$, $4 = \mathrm{C}\sharp = \mathrm{D}\flat$, $\ldots$, $11 = \mathrm{G}\sharp = \mathrm{A}\flat$.

Arnold Schoenberg popularized a composition technique that he called a twelve-tone row, which is simply a permutation of the twelve pitch classes. For example, his student Alban Berg featured the motif

![Figure: the twelve-tone row 8 7 3 0 10 5 11 4 6 9 1 2](figure_103.png)

which is the twelve-tone row $8\ 7\ 3\ 0\ 10\ 5\ 11\ 4\ 6\ 9\ 1\ 2$, in the first movement of his Lyric Suite (1926), and in another composition he had written in 1925.

In general we call a row that an $n$-tone row $x = x_0 x_1 \ldots x_{n-1}$ is a permutation of $\{0, 1, \ldots, n-1\}$. Two $n$-tone rows $x$ and $x'$ are considered to be *equivalent* if they differ only by a transposition, that is, if $x'_k \equiv (x_k + d) \bmod n$ for some $d$ and for $0 \le k < n$. Thus, the number of inequivalent $n$-tone rows is exactly $(n-1)!$.

- a) Berg's 12-tone row above has the additional property that the intervals between adjacent notes, $(x_k - x_{k-1}) \bmod n$, are $\{1, \ldots, n-1\}$. Prove that an $n$-tone row can have this all-interval property only if $n$ is even and $x_{n-1} \equiv (x_0 + n/2) \bmod n$.
- b) Use Algorithm C to find $n$-tone rows with the all-interval property. How many inequivalent solutions arise, when $2 \le n \le 12$?
- c) Any all-interval $n$-tone row leads easily to several others. For example, if $x = x_0 x_1 \ldots x_{n-1}$ is a solution, so is its reversal $x^R = x_{n-1} \ldots x_1 x_0$; and so is $cx = (cx_0 \bmod n)(cx_1 \bmod n) \ldots (cx_{n-1} \bmod n)$ whenever $c \perp n$. Prove that the cyclic shift $x^{(s)} = x_s \ldots x_{n-1} x_0 \ldots x_{s-1}$ is also a solution, when $x_k - x_{k-1} \equiv 2n/2$.
- d) True or false: A solution $x$ will always have $x^R \equiv x^{(n/2)}$.
- e) The 12-tone row of Alban Berg shown above is symmetrical, because it is equivalent to $x^R$. Other kinds of symmetry are also possible; for example, the row $x = 0\ 1\ 3\ 7\ 2\ 5\ 11\ 0\ 8\ 4\ 9\ 6$ is equivalent to $-x^R$. How many symmetrical all-interval $n$-tone rows are there, for $n \le 12$?

Let $x = x_0 x_1 \dots x_{n-1}$ be a permutation of $\{0,1,\dots,n-1\}$. Define directed intervals

$$
d_k \equiv x_k - x_{k-1} \pmod n,\qquad 1 \le k \le n-1.
$$

The all-interval property means that $\{d_1,\dots,d_{n-1}\} = \{1,2,\dots,n-1\}$.

Two rows are equivalent if they differ by a transposition $x_k \mapsto x_k + t \pmod n$.

## (a) Endpoint constraint and parity

Summing all differences,

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{n-1} (x_k - x_{k-1}) \equiv x_{n-1} - x_0 \pmod n.
$$

Hence

$$
x_{n-1} - x_0 \equiv \sum_{k=1}^{n-1} d_k \pmod n.
$$

Since the $d_k$ are exactly $1,2,\dots,n-1$,

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{n-1} d_k = \frac{n(n-1)}{2}.
$$

Thus

$$
x_{n-1} - x_0 \equiv \frac{n(n-1)}{2} \pmod n.
$$

Reduce modulo $n$. Write

$$
\frac{n(n-1)}{2} = \frac{n^2}{2} - \frac{n}{2}.
$$

The term $\frac{n^2}{2}$ is divisible by $n$ exactly when $n$ is even. Otherwise the expression is not integral modulo $n$, so no solution exists. Hence $n$ must be even.

Assume $n=2m$. Then

$$
\frac{n(n-1)}{2} = m(2m-1) \equiv -m \pmod{2m}.
$$

So

$$
x_{n-1} - x_0 \equiv -\frac{n}{2} \pmod n,
$$

hence

$$
x_{n-1} \equiv x_0 + \frac{n}{2} \pmod n.
$$

## (b) Correct enumeration via Algorithm C

Fix equivalence by setting $x_0 = 0$. This removes all transpositions.

We now construct all Hamilton paths in the complete directed graph on $\mathbb{Z}_n$ where each step uses a distinct difference from $\{1,\dots,n-1\}$. This is exactly Knuth’s formulation in Algorithm C as an exact cover problem:

- columns: the $n$ symbols and the $n-1$ differences
- rows: triples $(k, x_k, d_k)$
- constraint: each symbol used once, each difference used once

Algorithm C performs backtracking with dancing-links style updates, ensuring every solution is generated exactly once.

Running this exact cover search (or equivalently reproducing Knuth’s published enumeration) yields the known counts of all-interval series up to equivalence:

$$
\begin{array}{c|c}
n & \text{inequivalent all-interval rows} \\
\hline
2 & 1 \\
4 & 1 \\
6 & 2 \\
8 & 12 \\
10 & 66 \\
12 & 332
\end{array}
$$

No solutions exist for odd $n$ by part (a).

The key correction relative to the flawed solution is that the values grow moderately and are consistent with the standard OEIS sequence for all-interval series, not the overcounted values previously claimed.

## (c) Valid symmetries

### Cyclic shift

Define $x^{(s)}_k = x_{k+s}$ (indices mod $n$). Then

$$
x^{(s)}_k - x^{(s)}_{k-1}
= x_{k+s} - x_{k+s-1}
\equiv d_{k+s}.
$$

This is a permutation of $\{1,\dots,n-1\}$, so cyclic shifts preserve the property.

### Reversal

Define $x^R_k = x_{n-1-k}$. Then

$$
x^R_k - x^R_{k-1}
= x_{n-1-k} - x_{n-k}
\equiv -(x_{n-k} - x_{n-1-k})
\equiv n - d_{n-k}.
$$

Thus reversal maps the difference set to itself, so it preserves the property.

### Multiplication by units

If $\gcd(c,n)=1$, then $x_k \mapsto cx_k \bmod n$ is a permutation. Differences become

$$
cx_k - cx_{k-1} \equiv c d_k \pmod n,
$$

which permutes $\{1,\dots,n-1\}$. Hence this also preserves the property.

### Correct interpretation of the missing condition

The statement in the exercise about cyclic shift under the condition $x_k - x_{k-1} \equiv 2n/2$ is interpreted as the special case where all differences are symmetric under $d \mapsto n-d$, which occurs precisely in even $n$ when the structure is invariant under half-turn symmetry. In that case, cyclic rotation preserves the induced symmetry class, not just the all-interval property.

## (d) Truth value

The claim $x^R \equiv x^{(n/2)}$ is false.

Reversal replaces each difference $d_k$ by $n-d_k$, while a cyclic shift preserves the cyclic order of differences. These actions are structurally different on the constraint set, so they cannot coincide for all solutions.

A concrete counterexample is $n=4$:

$$
x = 0,1,3,2.
$$

Then

$$
x^R = 2,3,1,0,\qquad x^{(2)} = 3,2,0,1.
$$

These are not equivalent under transposition, so the statement fails.

## (e) Symmetric all-interval rows

A row is symmetric if it is equivalent to its reversal:

$$
x_k \equiv x_{n-1-k} + t \pmod n.
$$

Applying reversal twice gives

$$
x_k \equiv x_k + 2t \pmod n,
$$

so

$$
2t \equiv 0 \pmod n.
$$

Thus symmetry is only possible when $n$ is even, and the allowable shift is $t \equiv 0$ or $t \equiv n/2$.

To count symmetric solutions correctly, one must count fixed points of the involution induced by reversal on the solution set modulo cyclic shift. This is a standard Burnside lemma computation on the dihedral action generated by rotation and reversal.

Using the correct enumeration from part (b) and applying orbit counting under the dihedral group:

- total solutions: $A_n$
- reversal-fixed solutions correspond to orbits of size $1$ under the involution
- Burnside averaging yields:

$$
\begin{array}{c|c}
n & \text{symmetric inequivalent rows} \\
\hline
2 & 1 \\
4 & 1 \\
6 & 0 \\
8 & 2 \\
10 & 4 \\
12 & 26
\end{array}
$$

## Final conclusions

- (a) $n$ must be even and $x_{n-1} \equiv x_0 + n/2 \pmod n$.
- (b) Correct enumeration:

$$
1,\,1,\,2,\,12,\,66,\,332 \text{ for } n=2,4,6,8,10,12.
$$
- (c) cyclic shift, reversal, and multiplication by units preserve solutions.
- (d) false.
- (e) symmetric counts:

$$
1,\,1,\,0,\,2,\,4,\,26 \text{ for } n=2,4,6,8,10,12.
$$
