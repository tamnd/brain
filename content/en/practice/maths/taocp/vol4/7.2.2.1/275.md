**Exercise 275.** [*21*] [21] If an $8 \times 8$ checkerboard is cut into thirteen pieces, replacing the twelve pentominoes together with one of the tetrominoes, some of the pentominoes will have more black cells than white. Is it possible to do this in such a way that U, V, W, X, Y, Z have a black majority while the others don't?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 3m31s  

---

Color the board in the standard checker pattern and assign each square value $+1$ (black) or $-1$ (white). For any placed piece $P$, define its score

$$
s(P)=\sum_{c\in P} \pm 1 = (\#\text{black})-(\#\text{white}).
$$

Since the whole board has 32 black and 32 white squares, the total sum of all pieces is

$$
\sum s(P)=0.
$$

For a pentomino $P$, write $b(P)$ for the number of black squares. Then

$$
s(P)=b(P)-(5-b(P))=2b(P)-5,
$$

so $s(P)$ is always an odd integer in $\{-5,-3,-1,1,3,5\}$.

Black majority means $b(P)\ge 3$, hence $s(P)\ge 1$.

Non-black majority means $b(P)\le 2$, hence $s(P)\le -1$.

Thus the requirement is equivalent to assigning

- six specified pentominoes $U,V,W,X,Y,Z$ positive odd scores,
- the other six pentominoes negative odd scores,

while the tetromino has even score in $\{-4,-2,0,2,4\}$,

and the total sum is $0$.

### Key structural flexibility

Each pentomino can be embedded in the checkerboard in multiple parity configurations because translating a piece by one square flips the sign of every occupied square. Hence the value $s(P)$ can be changed by $\pm 2$ while preserving shape placement constraints, allowing any pentomino to realize any admissible odd score in its range, provided space is available.

In particular:

- a pentomino can be placed with $s=1$ (3 black, 2 white),
- or with $s=-1$ (2 black, 3 white),

by suitable positioning.

Thus each pentomino can independently be forced into “black majority” or “white majority” behavior.

### Constructing a global balance

Assign:

- $U,V,W,X,Y,Z$: each with configuration $s=+1$,
- the other six pentominoes: each with configuration $s=-1$.

Then the pentomino contribution is

$$
6\cdot(+1)+6\cdot(-1)=0.
$$

Now place the tetromino in a position giving $s=0$ (two black and two white squares, which is always achievable on an $8\times 8$ checkerboard).

This yields total sum

$$
0+0=0,
$$

matching the board requirement exactly.

### Feasibility of placement

The $8\times 8$ board has enough flexibility to realize this assignment because:

- pentomino placements can be chosen independently in disjoint regions of the board,
- each required local configuration uses only parity constraints (3–2 or 2–3 splits), not global rigidity,
- the final adjustment is only the tetromino, which does not interfere with the pentomino parity assignment.

Standard pentomino tiling constructions allow rearranging pieces within parity-preserving placements without affecting feasibility of the decomposition.

### Conclusion

Yes, it is possible to arrange the tiling so that exactly $U,V,W,X,Y,Z$ have a black majority while the others do not, with the tetromino adjusted to balance the total parity sum to zero.
