**Exercise 133.** [*21*] (P. A. MacMahon, 1921.) A set of 24 *square* tiles can be constructed, analogous to the triangular tiles of (§8), if we restrict ourselves to just three colors. For example, they can be arranged in a $4 \times 6$ rectangle as shown, with all-white border. In how many ways can this be done?

## Solution

The 24 tiles arise from the same construction as in §8: each tile corresponds to a coloring of the four sides of a unit square by three colors, identified under rotations. Since there are $3^4$ raw colorings and the cyclic group of order $4$ acts by rotation, Burnside’s lemma gives

$$
\frac{1}{4}\bigl(3^4 + 3 + 9 + 3\bigr) = \frac{96}{4} = 24,
$$

so the set of tiles is in bijection with all edge-colorings of a square up to rotation.

A placement of tiles in the $4\times 6$ rectangle is equivalent to assigning a color to each edge of the grid, with the condition that adjacent squares agree on their shared edge. Indeed, once all grid edges are colored, each unit square inherits a cyclic sequence of four colors; since every such sequence occurs in the tile set (up to rotation), exactly one tile (with suitable rotation) matches that square. Conversely, any tiling determines a unique coloring of all edges. Thus tilings are in bijection with edge-colorings of the grid satisfying the boundary condition.

The rectangle has $4\times 6$ cells, hence a grid with $5\times 7$ vertices. The number of horizontal edges is $(4+1)\cdot 6 = 30$, and the number of vertical edges is $4\cdot (6+1) = 28$, so the total number of edges is

$$
30 + 28 = 58.
$$

The boundary condition requires all outer edges to be white. The boundary consists of the top and bottom horizontal edges, $2\cdot 6 = 12$ edges, and the left and right vertical edges, $2\cdot 4 = 8$ edges, for a total of $20$ fixed edges.

Hence the number of free (interior) edges is

$$
58 - 20 = 38.
$$

Each interior edge may independently be assigned any of the three colors, and every such assignment yields a valid tiling as explained above. Therefore the number of tilings is

$$
3^{38}.
$$

$$
\boxed{3^{38}}
$$
