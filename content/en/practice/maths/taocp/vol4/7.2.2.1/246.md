Let $G=(V,E)$ be a simple undirected graph and let $U\subseteq V$ with $|U|=l$. All edges are undirected, but we measure costs in terms of ordered pairs as in the exercise.

### 1. Correct interior cost definition

The interior cost is defined as the number of ordered pairs $(u,v)\in U\times U$, $u\neq v$, that are not adjacent. The total number of ordered pairs is $l(l-1)$. Each undirected edge inside $U$ contributes two ordered adjacent pairs, hence $2|E(U)|$ ordered pairs are adjacent.

Therefore

$$
I(U)=l(l-1)-2|E(U)|.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
2|E(U)|=l(l-1)-I(U).
$$

This corrects the error in the previous solution.

### 2. Exterior cost

Let

$$
X(U)=|\{(u,v)\in E : u\in U,\ v\in V\setminus U\}|
$$

counting ordered crossing incidences (equivalently, each crossing edge contributes one unit, consistent with summing degrees over $U$).

We use the standard identity:

$$
\sum_{u\in U}\deg(u)=2|E(U)|+X(U),
$$

since each internal edge is counted twice and each crossing edge once.

Hence

$$
X(U)=\sum_{u\in U}\deg(u)-2|E(U)|.
$$

Substitute $2|E(U)|=l(l-1)-I(U)$:

$$
X(U)=\sum_{u\in U}\deg(u)-l(l-1)+I(U).
$$

### 3. Structural dependence of the objectives

For fixed $l$, the term $l(l-1)$ is constant, but $\sum_{u\in U}\deg(u)$ is not. It depends on which vertices are chosen, unless the graph is regular.

Thus:

- $I(U)$ depends only on internal edge density in $U$,
- $X(U)$ depends both on internal structure and on how $U$ intersects high-degree or low-degree vertices of $G$.

There is no affine equivalence between minimizing $I(U)$ and minimizing $X(U)$ over all $l$-subsets.

### 4. Effect on the partitioning problem in (118)

The key issue in the exercise is whether a cost function discourages “unnatural” components such as the AZ–ND–OK connection.

#### (a) Minimizing squared population

The squared population objective depends only on vertex weights and enforces balance of sizes. It is blind to adjacency structure, so it cannot distinguish geographically disconnected or weakly connected configurations except indirectly through the imposed partition constraints. This explains why anomalous components can appear.

#### (b) Minimizing exterior cost

Minimizing $X(U)$ penalizes edges crossing the boundary of each component. This directly encourages _edge coherence_: vertices inside a component tend to have many internal edges and few external edges.

In the USA graph setting, a configuration such as a component connecting AZ with ND and OK without passing through intermediate geographically connected states typically requires many boundary crossings, since such long-range groupings tend to cut through dense local adjacency structure. Therefore exterior-cost minimization tends to discourage exactly the type of “teleporting component” seen in the left-hand partition of (118).

However, this objective is still not perfect. It can favor isolating low-degree or sparsely connected regions if that reduces the cut size, even when such regions are not geographically coherent.

Overall, minimizing exterior cost produces partitions that are substantially more consistent with graph connectivity than squared population minimization.

#### (c) Minimizing interior cost (relation to Exercise 244)

From Exercise 244, interior cost measures lack of internal adjacency:

$$
I(U)=l(l-1)-2|E(U)|.
$$

Minimizing $I(U)$ is equivalent to maximizing internal edge density.

This encourages each component to be internally dense. In the USA graph, this tends to favor grouping states that are strongly interconnected and penalizes sparse internal structure. However, it can also produce counterintuitive effects: a region may split off if it is internally sparse even when it is geographically natural, or it may group distant vertices if that increases internal edge count.

Thus interior-cost minimization does not reliably align with geographic intuition; it emphasizes density rather than boundary coherence.

### 5. Conclusion

- The corrected identity is

$$
X(U)=\sum_{u\in U}\deg(u)-l(l-1)+I(U).
$$

- Interior cost and exterior cost are not equivalent optimization criteria.
- Minimizing exterior cost is significantly more aligned with graph connectivity and therefore would reduce anomalies like the AZ–ND–OK component, though it does not eliminate all pathologies.
- Minimizing interior cost emphasizes internal density and does not directly control boundary structure, so it is less effective for producing “reasonable-looking” geographic partitions.
- The squared population objective remains fundamentally different because it ignores adjacency entirely, which is the primary source of the bizarre component phenomenon in (118).
