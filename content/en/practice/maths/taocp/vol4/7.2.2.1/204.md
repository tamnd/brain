**Exercise 204.** [*M25*] [M25] True or false: Node $\alpha\alpha'$ is dominant in $T \oplus T'$ if and only if $\alpha$ is dominant in $T$ and $\alpha'$ is dominant in $T'$. *Hint:* Express $\deg(\alpha\alpha')$ in terms of $\deg(\alpha)$ and $\deg(\alpha')$.

## Solution

Let $T$ and $T'$ be the given structures, and let $\deg_T(\alpha)$ and $\deg_{T'}(\alpha')$ denote the degrees of nodes $\alpha$ in $T$ and $\alpha'$ in $T'$ respectively. Let $\deg_{T \oplus T'}(\alpha\alpha')$ denote the degree of the combined node in $T \oplus T'$.

By the construction of the operation $T \oplus T'$, adjacency at $\alpha\alpha'$ arises independently from the adjacency contributions of $\alpha$ in $T$ and $\alpha'$ in $T'$, so each incident relation at $\alpha\alpha'$ is inherited from exactly one of the two components. Hence each neighbor of $\alpha\alpha'$ corresponds uniquely to either a neighbor of $\alpha$ in $T$ or a neighbor of $\alpha'$ in $T'$, and no overlap occurs between these two classes. Therefore

$\deg_{T \oplus T'}(\alpha\alpha') = \deg_T(\alpha) + \deg_{T'}(\alpha').$

Let $\Delta(T)$ and $\Delta(T')$ denote the maximum degrees in $T$ and $T'$. A node is dominant in a structure precisely when its degree attains the maximum degree of that structure.

If $\alpha$ is dominant in $T$ and $\alpha'$ is dominant in $T'$, then

$\deg_T(\alpha) = \Delta(T), \qquad \deg_{T'}(\alpha') = \Delta(T'),$

and thus

$\deg_{T \oplus T'}(\alpha\alpha') = \Delta(T) + \Delta(T').$

No node in $T \oplus T'$ has degree exceeding $\Delta(T) + \Delta(T')$, since for any $\beta \in T$ and $\beta' \in T'$,

$\deg_{T \oplus T'}(\beta\beta') = \deg_T(\beta) + \deg_{T'}(\beta') \le \Delta(T) + \Delta(T').$

Hence $\alpha\alpha'$ is dominant in $T \oplus T'$.

Conversely, if $\alpha\alpha'$ is dominant in $T \oplus T'$, then for all $\beta \in T$ and $\beta' \in T'$,

$\deg_T(\alpha) + \deg_{T'}(\alpha') \ge \deg_T(\beta) + \deg_{T'}(\beta').$

Fixing $\beta' = \alpha'$, one obtains

$\deg_T(\alpha) \ge \deg_T(\beta),$

so $\alpha$ is dominant in $T$. Fixing $\beta = \alpha$, one obtains

$\deg_{T'}(\alpha') \ge \deg_{T'}(\beta'),$

so $\alpha'$ is dominant in $T'$.

Therefore $\alpha\alpha'$ is dominant in $T \oplus T'$ if and only if $\alpha$ is dominant in $T$ and $\alpha'$ is dominant in $T'$. ∎
