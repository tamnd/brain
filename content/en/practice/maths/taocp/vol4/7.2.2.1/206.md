**Exercise 206.** [*29*] [29] If $T$ is minimally dominant and $\deg(\text{root}(T)) \le \deg(\text{root}(T'))$, show that it's easy to describe the tree $T \oplus T'$.

We restate the construction in terms of the only structure that is actually used in Section 7.2.2.1: the organization of children of a minimally dominant tree by nondecreasing degree.

A tree $T$ is minimally dominant if, at every node, its children are arranged in a canonical order determined by the dominance relation, and in particular the children of the root form an ordered list in nondecreasing order of degree. This ordering is stable: inserting a new subtree can only affect the position in this list, not the internal structure of existing subtrees.

The operation $T \oplus T'$ is defined at the level of root-children: it combines the two root-ordered structures into a single minimally dominant tree by merging these ordered lists according to the dominance order.

Now assume

$$
\deg(\mathrm{root}(T)) \le \deg(\mathrm{root}(T')).
$$

This inequality implies that, at the level of root comparison used to order children of a minimally dominant tree, the subtree $T$ does not have higher priority than $T'$. Therefore, when forming the combined root-children list, $T'$ cannot appear before $T$ in the canonical ordering determined by degree.

Let $r = \mathrm{root}(T)$. Since $T$ is minimally dominant, its children already appear in increasing order of degree, say

$$
C(T) = (A_1, A_2, \dots, A_k).
$$

Similarly, write $T'$ as a single rooted subtree to be inserted into this ordered structure.
