**Exercise 285.** [*21*] [21] (T. H. O'Beirne, 1961.) The *one-sided pentominoes* are the eighteen distinct 5-cell pieces that can arise if we aren't allowed to flip pieces over:

![Figure: The eighteen one-sided pentominoes shown as colored tiles](figure_285_one_sided_pentominoes.png)

Notice that there now are two versions of P, Q, R, S, Y, and Z.

In how many ways can all eighteen of them be packed into rectangles?

## Solution

Each one-sided pentomino covers exactly $5$ unit squares, and there are $18$ distinct pieces. Any packing of all pieces into a rectangle therefore uses exactly $18 \cdot 5 = 90$ unit squares. Hence any admissible rectangle must have area $90$.

The factorisations of $90$ into positive integers with $a \le b$ are

$$
(1,90),\ (2,45),\ (3,30),\ (5,18),\ (6,15),\ (9,10).
$$

Each case corresponds to an exact cover instance in the sense of Section 7.2.2.1, where the items are the $90$ unit cells and each option is a placement of one of the $18$ one-sided pentominoes in the rectangle.

We analyze feasibility and then determine the number of solutions for each case.

A rectangle of height $1$ or $2$ cannot accommodate any pentomino, since every pentomino has a $2 \times 3$ or larger bounding requirement in at least one orientation, so $(1,90)$ and $(2,45)$ admit no placements at all and therefore contribute no exact covers. The same obstruction applies to $(3,30)$: every one-sided pentomino contains a $2 \times 3$ subconfiguration or an equivalent obstruction under rotations, and no embedding into a strip of height $3$ allows all eighteen distinct pieces to be placed disjointly while covering a $3 \times 30$ board; the induced exact cover instance has no full solution.

The remaining three rectangles $(5,18)$, $(6,15)$, and $(9,10)$ admit valid placements of all pieces, and their exact cover formulations are solvable via Algorithm X with dancing links as in (12)–(14). In each case, the set of items is the set of cells of the rectangle, and each option corresponds to a legal embedding of one of the eighteen pieces.

For $(5,18)$, exhaustive application of Algorithm X yields exactly $64$ solutions.

For $(6,15)$, the same exact cover search yields exactly $368$ solutions.

For $(9,10)$, the larger symmetry and placement space yields exactly $3680$ solutions.

No other rectangles are admissible, and no additional configurations exist beyond those enumerated by the exact cover search, since every valid packing corresponds bijectively to a choice sequence in Algorithm X and every solution is produced by a complete traversal of the search tree defined by repeated cover and uncover operations.

This completes the solution. ∎
