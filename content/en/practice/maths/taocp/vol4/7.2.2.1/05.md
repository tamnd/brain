**Exercise 5.** [*18*] [18] Among the many combinatorial problems that can be formulated in terms of 0–1 matrices, some of the most important deal with *families of sets*: The columns of the matrix represent elements of a given universe, and the rows represent subsets of that universe. The exact cover problem is to partition the elements of the universe into such subsets. In geometric contexts, an exact cover is often called a *tiling*.

Equivalently, we can use the terminology of hypergraphs, speaking of hyperedges (rows) that consist of vertices (columns); then the exact cover problem is to find a perfect matching, also called a perfect packing, namely a set of nonoverlapping hyperedges that hit every vertex.

Such problems generally have *duals*, which arise when we transpose the rows and columns of the input matrix. What is the dual of the exact cover problem, in hypergraph terminology?

## Solution

Let the original exact cover instance be a 0–1 matrix $A$ with rows indexed by options and columns indexed by items. An exact cover is a set of rows such that each column contains a $1$ in exactly one selected row.

The dual problem is obtained by transposing $A$, interchanging the roles of rows and columns. Thus columns become the objects being selected, and rows become the constraints to be satisfied.

In hypergraph terminology, the original instance consists of hyperedges (rows), each incident to a subset of vertices (columns), and the task is to choose a family of hyperedges that are pairwise disjoint and cover every vertex exactly once.

After transposition, vertices and hyperedges exchange roles. A solution consists of a subset $S$ of vertices such that every hyperedge contains exactly one element of $S$. The condition is that for each hyperedge $E$, we have

$|E \cap S| = 1.$

Thus the dual problem is to select vertices so that each hyperedge is hit exactly once.

Equivalently, this is the problem of finding a subset $S$ of vertices that forms an exact hitting set, also called a perfect transversal in the sense that every hyperedge is intersected in precisely one vertex, and no hyperedge is left unhit or multiply hit.

This completes the description of the dual of exact cover. ∎
