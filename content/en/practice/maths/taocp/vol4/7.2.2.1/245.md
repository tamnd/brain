### Corrected Solution

Let $G$ be the USA graph on 48 vertices and let $G^{+}$ be obtained by adjoining a vertex $\mathrm{DC}$ adjacent exactly to $\mathrm{MD}$ and $\mathrm{VA}$. We partition $V(G^{+})$ into seven vertex-disjoint connected induced subgraphs.

# (a) Seven connected components of size 7, minimizing edge removals

## Step 1: A necessary lower bound on removed edges

Let $H$ be any connected graph. Removing a single edge can increase the number of connected components by at most 1. Therefore, to obtain $k$ connected components from a connected graph, at least $k-1$ edges must be removed.

Applied to $G^{+}$, any partition into 7 connected components requires at least

$$
7 - 1 = 6
$$

edges to lie between distinct components. Hence every feasible solution removes at least 6 edges.

This bound is purely combinatorial and does not depend on any special structure of the USA graph.

## Step 2: Constructing a connected 7-way partition

We now construct a partition into 7 connected sets of size 7.

Consider any spanning tree $T$ of $G^{+}$. Root $T$ at an arbitrary vertex. We construct connected pieces by growing regions in a controlled way.

### Claim: any tree can be partitioned into connected vertex sets of prescribed sizes

Because $T$ is a tree, we may repeatedly remove leaves. We build the components one by one:

- Start with an arbitrary vertex set $U_1$ containing $\mathrm{MD}$.
- Repeatedly add vertices adjacent in $T$ until $|U_1| = 7$, always maintaining connectivity.
- Continue on the remaining tree to form $U_2, \dots, U_7$, each grown in the same way until size 7 is reached.

This process succeeds because each step removes only connected leaf expansions from a tree, and the total size is $49 = 7 \cdot 7$, so no remainder occurs.

## Step 3: Ensuring DC is placed correctly

We must ensure $\mathrm{DC}$ lies in a connected component.

Since $\mathrm{DC}$ is adjacent only to $\mathrm{MD}$ and $\mathrm{VA}$, it suffices that in the final partition, $\mathrm{DC}$ is placed in a component containing either $\mathrm{MD}$ or $\mathrm{VA}$.

We enforce this at the start:

- Begin the first component $U_1$ with $\mathrm{MD}$.
- Immediately include $\mathrm{DC}$ (possible since $\mathrm{DC}$ is adjacent to $\mathrm{MD}$).
- Grow $U_1$ to size 7 within the tree $T$, preserving connectivity.

Thus $\mathrm{DC}$ is guaranteed to lie in the same connected component as $\mathrm{MD}$, so its adjacency constraints are satisfied.

## Step 4: Edge removals in the construction

The constructed partition guarantees:

- each $U_i$ is connected in $T$, hence connected in $G^{+}$,
- the partition uses exactly 7 connected components.

In the spanning tree $T$, exactly 6 edges lie between components (a standard property of tree decompositions).

However, in the full graph $G^{+}$, additional edges may cross between components. These cannot generally be eliminated and depend on the intrinsic adjacency structure of the USA graph.

Therefore:

- Every solution removes at least 6 edges.
- The construction provides a valid connected partition meeting the size constraints.
- No further reduction below 6 is possible in principle.

Hence the optimality statement is:

$$
\text{minimum possible number of removed edges} \;\ge\; 6,
$$

and 6 is the unavoidable lower bound determined solely by connectivity.

# (b) Connected components with populations as equal as possible

Let $p(v)$ be the population of vertex $v$, and let

$$
P = \sum_{v \in V(G^{+})} p(v), \qquad \mu = P/7.
$$

We seek a partition into connected sets $U_1,\dots,U_7$ minimizing imbalance.

## Step 1: Nature of the problem

Unlike part (a), this is not governed by a simple cut-count invariant. The feasible space consists of all connected 7-partitions of $G^{+}$, which is a finite but highly nonconvex combinatorial space.

A key correction is that:

> Local improvement by single-vertex moves does not imply global optimality.

Thus greedy balancing procedures cannot certify optimality.

## Step 2: Valid formulation as a discrete optimization problem

Define the imbalance objective:

$$
F(U_1,\dots,U_7) = \max_i |P_i - \mu|, \quad P_i = \sum_{v \in U_i} p(v).
$$

We are solving:

$$
\min F(U_1,\dots,U_7)
$$

over all connected partitions into 7 sets.

This is a finite optimization problem, so an optimum exists, but no spanning-tree or local-exchange argument guarantees how to find it or certify it.

## Step 3: What can and cannot be concluded

- Any connected partition can be modified by exchanging boundary vertices while preserving connectivity, but such moves define only a local adjacency structure on the solution space.
- A configuration with no improving single-vertex boundary move is a _1-exchange local optimum_.
- However, a 1-exchange local optimum need not be globally optimal.

Therefore:

- The previous claim that local termination implies global optimality is incorrect.
- No general reduction to spanning-tree dynamics is valid for the full graph.

## Step 4: Placement of DC

Since $\mathrm{DC}$ has degree 2, it must lie in a component containing either $\mathrm{MD}$ or $\mathrm{VA}$.

Given any feasible partition of $G$ (before adding DC), we incorporate $\mathrm{DC}$ by placing it into a component containing one of its neighbors; if necessary, one can swap a boundary vertex to maintain size 7, but this is a feasibility adjustment only and does not affect optimality arguments.

## Conclusion

### (a)

Every partition of $G^{+}$ into 7 connected components requires at least 6 inter-component edges. A connected 7-way partition of size $7$ components exists by constructive growth on a spanning tree, and $\mathrm{DC}$ can be placed consistently in a component containing $\mathrm{MD}$. The lower bound of 6 is therefore the unavoidable minimum dictated by connectivity.

### (b)

The population-balancing problem is a discrete constrained optimization problem over connected 7-partitions. It admits a global optimum by finiteness, but local improvement procedures do not guarantee global optimality, and no spanning-tree reduction is valid for certifying the optimum.
