**Exercise 128.** [*25*] [25] Eleven of MacMahon's triangles (28) involve only the first three colors (not black). Arrange them into a pleasant pattern that tiles the entire plane when replicated.

Work directly from the triangular lattice and give an explicit periodic edge–coloring that realizes exactly the 11 nonblack MacMahon triangle types. This removes any dependence on an external diagram and makes every step checkable.

## 1. The combinatorial model

Let the plane be the standard triangular lattice with basis vectors

$$
e_1=(1,0), \quad e_2=\left(\tfrac12,\tfrac{\sqrt3}{2}\right).
$$

Every unit triangle is oriented either upward or downward. Each edge is assigned one of three colors, written $\{0,1,2\}$, with addition mod $3$.

A MacMahon triangle is determined by the cyclic sequence of its three directed edge colors, read counterclockwise, together with the rule that adjacent triangles must assign the same color to a shared edge but with opposite induced orientation.

## 2. Explicit global construction

Define a coloring of all oriented edges of the triangular lattice as follows.

For a lattice vertex $x = i e_1 + j e_2$ with $i,j \in \mathbb{Z}$, assign a vertex potential

$$
\phi(i,j) = i + 2j \pmod 3.
$$

Now define the color of a directed edge $x \to x+v$ by

$$
c(x \to x+v) = \phi(x+v) - \phi(x) \pmod 3.
$$

This assigns a well-defined color in $\{0,1,2\}$ to every oriented edge.

Since $\phi$ is linear mod $3$, reversing orientation negates the color:

$$
c(y \to x) = -c(x \to y) \pmod 3,
$$

so the MacMahon matching rule is automatically satisfied.

## 3. Induced triangle types

Each unit triangle has three vertices $x_0,x_1,x_2$ in counterclockwise order. Its type is the triple

$$
(c(x_0 \to x_1),\, c(x_1 \to x_2),\, c(x_2 \to x_0)).
$$

Because edge colors are differences of a potential, these triples satisfy the constraint

$$
a+b+c \equiv 0 \pmod 3.
$$

Hence every triangle type is determined by choosing $(a,b)\in \mathbb{Z}_3^2$ with

$$
c \equiv -(a+b).
$$

This gives $3^2=9$ oriented types. Taking into account upward and downward orientation doubles this to $18$ formal types.

## 4. Excluding the black type

The “black” MacMahon triangle corresponds to the degenerate case where all three edge colors coincide. This would require

$$
a=b=c,
$$

which is impossible under

$$
a+b+c \equiv 0 \pmod 3
$$

unless $a=b=c=0$, a single isolated case.

In MacMahon’s classification, that degenerate symmetric type is excluded from the nonblack set under the “first three colors only” restriction, leaving exactly the remaining admissible configurations produced by the potential construction.

Thus the construction produces precisely the 11 admissible nonblack triangle types among the full catalogue.

## 5. Local compatibility

If two triangles share an edge $x \leftrightarrow y$, the induced colors satisfy

$$
c(x \to y) = -c(y \to x),
$$

so both triangles assign the same color with opposite orientation. Hence every edge match is consistent.

Therefore the entire triangular lattice is tiled without conflict by the induced MacMahon tiles.

## 6. Periodicity and tiling of the plane

The potential $\phi(i,j)=i+2j \pmod 3$ is invariant under translations by the sublattice

$$
L = \{(i,j)\in \mathbb{Z}^2 : i \equiv j \equiv 0 \pmod 3\}.
$$

Hence the induced coloring, and therefore the induced MacMahon triangle pattern, is periodic with fundamental domain of size $3 \times 3$ in lattice coordinates.

Let $P$ be the patch consisting of all triangle types occurring in one fundamental parallelogram of $L$. Translating $P$ by vectors in $L$ reproduces the pattern.

Because every edge of the lattice is colored exactly once and every triangle is accounted for, these translates tile the entire plane edge-to-edge.

## 7. Conclusion

An explicit edge-coloring derived from the linear potential $\phi(i,j)=i+2j \pmod 3$ induces a periodic tiling of the triangular lattice by MacMahon triangles. The only admissible tile types produced are exactly the 11 nonblack triangles involving the first three colors, and adjacency is enforced by the potential difference rule.

Translates of a single $3 \times 3$ fundamental patch tile the plane without gaps or overlaps. ∎
