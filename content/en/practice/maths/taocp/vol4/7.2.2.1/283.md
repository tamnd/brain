We restart the argument from the actual combinatorial constraint imposed by the tatami condition and deduce the structure of admissible fences.

Let a fence consist of copies of the fixed tile $P$ placed on the square grid as in Exercise 282, with one distinguished inner copy $P_0$, and a family of outer copies, each of which must touch $P_0$ and collectively surround it as in the original definition. We now impose the tatami condition: at no grid vertex may four tile edges meet. Equivalently, at every lattice point, the local configuration of tile boundaries has vertex degree at most $3$.

The key point is to translate this local restriction into a global constraint on how outer copies may meet each other.

Consider the adjacency structure of the outer copies, where two copies are adjacent if they share a positive-length edge segment. As in Exercise 282, the fence can be represented by a planar graph embedded in the grid, whose vertices correspond to “junctions” where copies meet and whose edges correspond to adjacency relations along full tile edges.

In the unconstrained setting of Exercise 282, such a junction could in principle have degree $4$, corresponding to four tile edges meeting at a common grid vertex. The tatami condition eliminates exactly this possibility. Hence every vertex in the adjacency graph of outer tiles has degree at most $3$.
