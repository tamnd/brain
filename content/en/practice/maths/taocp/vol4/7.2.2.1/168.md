**Exercise 168.** &#9654; [*15*] [15] An MCC problem might have two identical options $o$, whose items are allowed to occur more than once. In such cases we might want the second copy of $o$ to be in the solution only if the first copy is also present. How can that be achieved?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 4m46s  

---

The core issue in the flawed solution is that it tries to create a directional dependency using only symmetric item-capacity constraints. In MCC, this is impossible unless the asymmetry is built directly into the structure of options and primary coverage, not via informal “linking” logic.

We restart from the specification:

We are given two identical options $o^{(1)}$ and $o^{(2)}$, both corresponding to the same multiset of items $S$, and we want to enforce

$$
o^{(2)} \in \mathcal{S} \;\Rightarrow\; o^{(1)} \in \mathcal{S}.
$$

The only available mechanisms are items with upper bounds and options that cover items.

## Correct construction

We introduce a new primary item $p$ with upper bound $1$, and a new auxiliary item $x$ with upper bound $1$.

We modify the MCC instance as follows:

- Replace the first copy of the option by

$$
o^{(1)} := S \cup \{p\}.
$$
- Replace the second copy by

$$
o^{(2)} := S \cup \{x\}.
$$
- Introduce a third option

$$
r := \{p, x\}.
$$

Finally, declare $p$ to be a primary item that must be covered exactly once, while $x$ is secondary with upper bound $1$.

## Key idea

The role of $r$ is to couple the two auxiliary items $p$ and $x$, while $p$ is the only item that enforces a necessary choice among the three options.

The crucial asymmetry is that:

- $p$ can only be covered by $o^{(1)}$ or $r$,
- $x$ can only be covered by $o^{(2)}$ or $r$,
- but $p$ is mandatory.

Thus any feasible solution must make a consistent choice that respects the coupling through $r$.

## Correctness proof

We show that $o^{(2)}$ cannot be selected without $o^{(1)}$.

### Case 1: $o^{(2)} \in \mathcal{S}$

If $o^{(2)}$ is selected, then $x$ is used once, so no other option may use $x$. In particular, $r$ is forbidden.

Since $p$ is a primary item with upper bound $1$, it must still be covered exactly once. The only remaining option that covers $p$ is $o^{(1)}$. Hence $o^{(1)}$ must be selected.

Thus,

$$
o^{(2)} \in \mathcal{S} \Rightarrow o^{(1)} \in \mathcal{S}.
$$

### Case 2: $o^{(2)} \notin \mathcal{S}$

Then $x$ is free. We may choose either $o^{(1)}$ alone or $r$, depending on how $p$ is satisfied. This does not violate any constraint.

## Why this fixes the original errors

1. **No conditional legality is used.**

All constraints are standard MCC constraints: items with upper bounds and options covering them.
2. **No inconsistent option rewriting occurs.**

Both copies of $o$ still cover the same set $S$; only auxiliary items are added.
3. **The implication is structural, not informal.**

The dependency arises because selecting $o^{(2)}$ consumes $x$, which disables the only alternative way to avoid forcing $o^{(1)}$, namely via $r$, leaving $o^{(1)}$ as the only feasible completion of the required coverage of $p$.

## Conclusion

The implication $o^{(2)} \Rightarrow o^{(1)}$ is enforced by introducing two coupled auxiliary items and a bridging option, with a mandatory primary item breaking symmetry. This yields a valid MCC construction using only standard constraints, without any external or conditional mechanisms.
