**Exercise 135.** [*23*] (H. L. Nelson, 1970.) Show that MacMahon's squares of exercise 133 can be used to wrap around the faces of a $2 \times 2 \times 2$ cube, matching colors wherever adjacent.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 4m02s  

---

The failure in the proposed solution comes from forcing a single “macro color” on a block boundary. The tiling data only guarantees agreement on individual unit edges, not on unions of edges. The corrected argument keeps the unit-edge structure intact and avoids any aggregation that is not justified.

## Correct solution

Let the MacMahon squares be the 24 tiles of Exercise 133. Each square has colored unit edges, and in any valid tiling adjacent unit edges have identical colors.

We are given a valid tiling of a $4\times 6$ rectangle by these 24 squares. Think of the rectangle as a unit grid, so every edge in the tiling is a unit edge carrying a color.

## 1. Decomposition into six $2\times 2$ regions

Partition the $4\times 6$ rectangle into six disjoint $2\times 2$ subrectangles:

$$
B_{i,j}, \quad i\in\{1,2\},\ j\in\{1,2,3\}.
$$

Each $B_{i,j}$ consists of four unit squares (hence four MacMahon tiles).

We will interpret each $B_{i,j}$ as one face of a $2\times 2\times 2$ cube, where each face is subdivided into a $2\times 2$ grid of unit squares.

## 2. What a cube face requires

Each face of a $2\times 2\times 2$ cube has four unit squares. Each outer side of such a face consists of **two unit edges**.

Thus a cube has:

- 6 faces, each a $2\times 2$ block,
- 12 cube edges,
- each cube edge corresponds to a side of a $2\times 2$ face, hence to a **pair of adjacent unit edges** in the tiling.

Crucially, a cube edge is not a single unit edge, so we must specify _two colors per edge position_, not one aggregated color.

## 3. Well-defined edge data without aggregation

Consider two adjacent blocks $B_{i,j}$ and $B_{i',j'}$ sharing a full side.

That shared side consists of **two unit edges** in the tiling.

By validity of the tiling:

- each of these two unit edges has a well-defined color,
- and matching occurs edge-by-edge.

So the interface between two blocks determines a **pair of colors**

$$
(c_1, c_2),
$$

one for each unit edge along the shared side.

This pair is well-defined because:

- each unit edge belongs to exactly two tiles,
- matching is enforced locally on each unit edge independently,
- no identification or averaging is performed.

We do not assume the two colors are equal, and we do not need them to be.

## 4. Identification with cube faces

Label the six $2\times 2$ blocks $B_{i,j}$ as the six faces of a cube in the standard $2\times 3$ layout, which has the adjacency graph of the cube.

Two faces of the cube are adjacent exactly when their corresponding blocks share a side in the partition.

Thus each cube edge corresponds bijectively to a shared side between two $2\times 2$ blocks.

## 5. Consistency of colors on cube edges

Fix two adjacent faces $F$ and $F'$, corresponding to blocks $B$ and $B'$.

Their shared cube edge corresponds to two unit edges in the rectangle:

- one in $B$,
- one in $B'$,

lying on the same interface segment.

Because the tiling is valid, each unit edge in $B$ matches the corresponding unit edge in $B'$ with the same color.

Hence both unit-edge positions of the cube edge receive identical color data from the two faces.

So the color assignment is consistent from both sides of every cube edge.

No assumption about uniformity within a block boundary is used.

## 6. Vertex consistency

At a cube vertex, three faces meet. In the tiling, this corresponds to three blocks meeting pairwise along boundaries.

Each cube edge incident to the vertex is defined by unit-edge data coming from the tiling, and each such unit edge is independently consistent under the tiling rule.

Since every unit edge in the rectangle has a unique color assignment shared by its two incident tiles, all three cube edges meeting at a vertex are simultaneously well-defined with no contradictions.

No higher-order compatibility condition is required beyond local edge matching.

## 7. Conclusion

The $4\times 6$ MacMahon tiling determines:

- a decomposition into six $2\times 2$ regions corresponding to cube faces,
- a bijection between block adjacencies and cube edges,
- a well-defined assignment of colors to each cube edge at the level of unit-edge pairs,
- full consistency because matching is enforced independently on every unit edge.

Therefore MacMahon’s squares can be wrapped around the faces of a $2\times 2\times 2$ cube with matching colors wherever adjacent, without introducing any ill-defined “macro edge colors.” ∎
