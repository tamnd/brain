**Exercise 232.** [*20*] The costs supplied to Algorithm X$^8$ must be integers. Does it matter that $d(i,j)$ in the 16 queens problem of Fig. 74 is never an integer. Is it OK to use $\lfloor 8d(i,j) \rfloor$ instead of $8d(i,j)$ for the cost of placing a queen in cell $(i,j)$?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 4m07s  

---

Let $S$ be a placement of 16 queens, one per row and column, and let

$$
C(S)=\sum_{(i,j)\in S} d(i,j).
$$

## 1. Does it matter that $d(i,j)$ is never an integer?

It does not matter.

Algorithm $X^8$ uses the costs only through **exact addition and exact comparison of total costs**. Nothing in the combinatorial structure of the 16-queens problem depends on the values being integers; what matters is that all feasible solutions are compared by the same additive objective function.

Thus we may regard $d(i,j)$ as real numbers and compute

$$
C(S)\in \mathbb{R}.
$$

Multiplying all costs by a positive constant, such as 8, gives

$$
C_8(S)=\sum_{(i,j)\in S} 8d(i,j)=8C(S),
$$

and this preserves ordering exactly:

$$
C(S) < C(T) \;\Longleftrightarrow\; C_8(S) < C_8(T).
$$

Hence the fact that $d(i,j)$ (and therefore $8d(i,j)$) is not an integer is irrelevant to the mathematical correctness of the optimization problem. It only reflects that Algorithm $X^8$ is stated in a model where costs are _represented_ as integers.

So the correct conclusion is: **no, it does not matter conceptually**; integrality is a representational convenience, not a structural requirement of the 16-queens cost model.

## 2. Is it OK to use $\lfloor 8d(i,j)\rfloor$ instead of $8d(i,j)$?

No.

Define the modified cost

$$
C'(S)=\sum_{(i,j)\in S} \lfloor 8d(i,j)\rfloor.
$$

For each cell,

$$
\lfloor 8d(i,j)\rfloor = 8d(i,j) - \varepsilon_{ij}, \quad 0 \le \varepsilon_{ij} < 1.
$$

Summing over the 16 queens in a placement $S$,

$$
C'(S)=8C(S)-\varepsilon(S),
\quad \text{where } 0 \le \varepsilon(S) < 16.
$$

Now the key point is that $\varepsilon(S)$ depends on the _entire placement_ and is not constant across feasible solutions.

For two solutions $S,T$,

$$
C'(S) - C'(T)
= 8\bigl(C(S)-C(T)\bigr) - \bigl(\varepsilon(S)-\varepsilon(T)\bigr).
$$

Even if $C(S) < C(T)$, it is possible for $\varepsilon(S)$ to be sufficiently larger than $\varepsilon(T)$ that

$$
C'(S) > C'(T).
$$

Thus the transformation does **not preserve the ordering of feasible solutions**, since it introduces a non-uniform perturbation that accumulates differently for different placements.

## Conclusion

- The non-integrality of $d(i,j)$ is not a mathematical obstacle: the optimization is well-defined over real costs, and scaling by 8 preserves all comparisons exactly.
- Replacing $8d(i,j)$ by $\lfloor 8d(i,j)\rfloor$ is not valid, because it destroys exact additivity up to a constant and can reverse the ordering between feasible solutions.

Therefore:

$$
\boxed{\text{Integrality of } d(i,j) \text{ does not matter, but flooring does.}}
$$
