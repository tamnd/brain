**Exercise 183.** [*16*] Let $\tilde{B}(n)$ be the maximum number of nodes in Algorithm X's search tree, taken over all strict exact cover problems with $n$ options. What is $\limsup_{n\to\infty} \tilde{B}(n)^{1/n}$?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 6m31s  

---

The previous solution fails because it does not correctly relate the branching factor to the decrease in the number of options. In Algorithm X, these two quantities are coupled, and this coupling determines the correct exponential growth rate.

## 1. Correct structural observation

Let an instance have $n$ options. At any node of Algorithm X’s search tree, choose an item $i$. Let $k(i)$ be the number of options containing $i$.

Algorithm X branches over those $k(i)$ options.

Now fix one branch, corresponding to choosing an option $O$ containing $i$. The key fact is:

- All $k(i)$ options containing $i$ are deleted immediately (they all intersect in item $i$).
- Hence at least $k(i)$ options are removed in this step.

Therefore, if the current subproblem has $m$ options, then along any branch created by choosing item $i$,

$$
m \;\mapsto\; m - k(i) \quad \text{at least.}
$$

So every branching step satisfies:

- branching factor = $k(i)$,
- decrease in problem size $\ge k(i)$.

## 2. Recurrence for the worst-case search tree

Let $\tilde{B}(n)$ be the maximum number of nodes over all strict exact cover instances with $n$ options.

From the above observation, in the worst case we obtain the recurrence

$$
\tilde{B}(n) \;\le\; \max_{k \ge 1} \; k \, \tilde{B}(n-k).
$$

This is because if an item appears in $k$ options, then:

- there are $k$ recursive subproblems,
- each subproblem has at most $n-k$ options remaining.

## 3. Exponential rate analysis

Assume an exponential form $\tilde{B}(n) \approx r^n$. Substituting into the recurrence gives

$$
r^n \approx k \, r^{n-k},
\quad \text{so} \quad
r^k \approx k.
$$

Thus the exponential growth rate $r$ must satisfy

$$
r = k^{1/k}.
$$

We maximize over all possible branching factors $k$. The function

$$
k^{1/k}
$$

is maximized at $k = 3$, giving

$$
\max_{k \ge 1} k^{1/k} = 3^{1/3}.
$$

Hence,

$$
\limsup_{n \to \infty} \tilde{B}(n)^{1/n} \le 3^{1/3}.
$$

## 4. Matching lower bound construction

We construct strict exact cover instances that achieve this growth.

For each group $r = 1,2,\dots$, introduce a fresh item $i_r$ and three options:

$$
O_{r,1} = \{i_r\}, \quad
O_{r,2} = \{i_r\}, \quad
O_{r,3} = \{i_r\}.
$$

To make the instance strict and prevent cross-interaction between groups, ensure:

- each $i_r$ appears only in these three options,
- no other item appears in more than one group,
- all additional items are private to their options.

Then:

- choosing item $i_r$ yields exactly $k=3$ options,
- selecting any one option deletes all three options in that group,
- no other group is affected.

Thus each group contributes a factor of exactly 3 branching into 3 independent recursive subproblems of size reduced by 3 options.

Let $n = 3h$. Then the search tree satisfies

$$
\tilde{B}(n) \;\ge\; 3^h.
$$

So

$$
\tilde{B}(n) \;\ge\; 3^{n/3}.
$$

Taking $n$-th roots,

$$
\limsup_{n \to \infty} \tilde{B}(n)^{1/n} \ge 3^{1/3}.
$$

## 5. Conclusion

We have both bounds:

$$
\tilde{B}(n)^{1/n} \to 3^{1/3}.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\boxed{\limsup_{n \to \infty} \tilde{B}(n)^{1/n} = 3^{1/3}}.
$$
