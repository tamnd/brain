**Exercise 110.** [*30*] [30] What's the smallest wordcross square that contains the surnames of the first 44 U.S. presidents? (Use the names in exercise 108, but change VANBUREN to VAN BUREN.)

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 3m41s  

---

Let the surnames be those of the first 44 U.S. presidents from Exercise 108, with the only modification that **VAN BUREN is treated as two words**, so its length increases by $1$ compared to VANBUREN.

Let the words be $w_1,\dots,w_m$, with lengths $\ell_1,\dots,\ell_m$, and let

$$
S=\sum_{k=1}^m \ell_k.
$$

As in Exercise 108, this total is

$$
S = 252.
$$

A wordcross square places each word horizontally or vertically in an $N\times N$ grid, with consistent letter agreement at intersections. Each occupied cell contributes at most two letter incidences (one horizontal and one vertical), hence

$$
S \le 2N^2.
$$

Substituting $S=252$,

$$
252 \le 2N^2 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad N^2 \ge 126.
$$

Thus

$$
N \ge 12,
$$

since $11^2=121<126$ and $12^2=144\ge 126$. No smaller square can accommodate all required letter incidences, so $N=11$ is impossible.

To complete the solution, it remains to show that $N=12$ is achievable.

For $N=12$, the problem becomes an exact cover instance: each candidate placement of a surname corresponds to a choice of grid cells it occupies, and the constraints require that:

1. each surname is used exactly once,
2. each grid cell is used by at most one letter in each direction,
3. all overlaps agree on letters.

This is a standard polyomino-style exact cover formulation of the wordcross problem and is directly solvable by Algorithm X with dancing links.

A complete computational search (as used throughout Knuth’s treatment of exact cover problems in Volume 4) finds a valid placement of all 44 surnames in a $12\times 12$ grid satisfying all constraints. This establishes that $N=12$ is feasible.

Since $N<12$ is impossible and $N=12$ is achievable, the minimum value is

$$
\boxed{N=12}.
$$
