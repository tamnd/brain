**Exercise 238.** [*24*] [24] Find $3\times n$ arrays filled with distinct 3-digit and $n$-digit primes, for $3 \le n \le 7$, having the minimum and maximum possible product.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m30s  

---

Let $A$ be a $3\times n$ array whose $3n$ entries are distinct primes, each either a 3-digit prime or an $n$-digit prime. The product depends only on the multiset of chosen primes, so the problem reduces to selecting a set $S$ of $3n$ distinct admissible primes minimizing or maximizing

$$
\prod_{p\in S} p.
$$

The key structure is the ordering between the two allowed classes.

- If $n \ge 4$, every 3-digit prime lies in $[100,999]$ and every $n$-digit prime lies in $[10^{n-1},10^n-1]$, hence every $n$-digit prime is strictly larger than every 3-digit prime.
- If $n=3$, both classes coincide and the admissible set is simply the set of all 3-digit primes.

Let $P_3$ denote the set of 3-digit primes, ordered increasingly as $a_1<a_2<\cdots$, and let $P_n$ denote the set of $n$-digit primes ordered increasingly as $b_1<b_2<\cdots$.

## Minimum product

We compare admissible replacements.

For any $n \ge 3$, all admissible primes are positive, so replacing any element by a smaller admissible prime strictly decreases the product.

Since $P_3$ contains all admissible primes when $n=3$, and contains the smallest admissible primes when $n\ge 4$, the $3n$ smallest admissible primes are always the first $3n$ elements of $P_3$.

Indeed:

- For $n=3$, admissible set is $P_3$.
- For $n\ge 4$, every element of $P_3$ is smaller than every element of $P_n$, so the smallest admissible primes are exactly those in $P_3$.

Hence, in all cases,

$$
\min \prod = \prod_{k=1}^{3n} a_k,
$$

where $a_1,\dots,a_{3n}$ are the $3n$ smallest 3-digit primes.

This is attainable since $3n \le 21$ for $3 \le n \le 7$, and $P_3$ contains 143 elements.

## Maximum product

Again, we use monotonic replacement: replacing any chosen prime by a larger admissible prime strictly increases the product.

### Case $n=3$

All admissible primes are 3-digit primes, so we must choose $3n=9$ primes from $P_3$. The maximum product is obtained by the nine largest 3-digit primes:

$$
\max \prod = \prod_{k=1}^{9} a_{143-k+1}.
$$

### Case $n \ge 4$

Now every $n$-digit prime is larger than every 3-digit prime. Therefore any 3-digit prime in a configuration can be replaced by an unused $n$-digit prime and strictly increase the product. Hence an optimal configuration uses only $n$-digit primes.

We must select $3n$ distinct elements from $P_n$, and the product is maximized by choosing the largest ones:

$$
\max \prod = \prod_{k=1}^{3n} b_{m-k+1},
$$

where $b_1<\cdots<b_m$ are all $n$-digit primes.

There are far more than $3n$ $n$-digit primes for $n\le 7$, so this selection is feasible.

## Final answers

For $3 \le n \le 7$:

### Minimum product (all $n$)

$$
\boxed{\min \prod = \prod_{k=1}^{3n} p_k},
$$

where $p_1<p_2<\cdots$ are the 3-digit primes.

### Maximum product

$$
\boxed{
\max \prod =
\begin{cases}
\prod_{k=1}^{9} p_{143-k+1}, & n=3,\\[6pt]
\prod_{k=1}^{3n} q_{m-k+1}, & 4 \le n \le 7,
\end{cases}}
$$

where $p_k$ are the 3-digit primes and $q_k$ are the $n$-digit primes in increasing order.

This completes the corrected solution. ∎
