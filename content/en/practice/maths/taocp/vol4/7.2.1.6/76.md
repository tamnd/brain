## Setup

A Christmas tree pattern of order $n$ arises from the Dyck-path representation implicit in Section 7.2.1.6, where a valid parenthesis string $a_1 \ldots a_{2n}$ is mapped to a lattice path from $(0,0)$ to $(2n,0)$ with steps $(1,1)$ for “(” and $(1,-1)$ for “)”. The “row structure” in the Christmas tree array encodes these paths by grouping positions according to a secondary statistic derived from the matching structure, equivalently from the forest or binary tree associated to the Dyck path via the natural correspondence described in the section.

The limiting question concerns the shape obtained when these discrete patterns are embedded in the plane and rescaled as $n \to \infty$. Any nontrivial limit requires normalization in which horizontal coordinates are divided by $2n$ and vertical coordinates by $\sqrt{n}$, since admissible paths have height $\Theta(\sqrt{n})$ by standard ballot-type bounds for Catalan objects.

The problem asks whether this limiting object admits a geometric structure such as a fractal dimension under a suitable scaling.

## Known results

The Dyck paths of length $2n$, equivalently the nested parenthesis strings $A_{nn}$ in Section 7.2.1.6, are classical Catalan objects. Under the uniform distribution on such paths, the rescaled contour functions converge in distribution to a standard Brownian excursion on $[0,1]$. This is a classical invariance principle for conditioned simple random walks, proved through combinatorial decompositions consistent with the recursion (5) and analytic methods on Catalan generating functions.

The associated plane trees, obtained from the forest representation in (2), converge in the Gromov–Hausdorff sense after rescaling edge lengths by $n^{-1/2}$ to the continuum random tree. This limiting object is random and not a deterministic curve.

The graph of a Brownian excursion $e(t)$ on $[0,1]$ is almost surely continuous and nowhere differentiable. Its Hausdorff dimension is almost surely $3/2$ when viewed as a subset of $\mathbb{R}^2$. This follows from standard properties of Brownian motion and the fact that Brownian excursion has identical local regularity exponent $1/2$.

These results imply that any “limiting shape” obtained from Dyck-path-type encodings is not a fixed geometric curve but a random fractal object.

## Partial argument

Let $P_n$ denote the Dyck path corresponding to a uniformly chosen nested parenthesis string of size $n$. Define the rescaled function

$$
X_n(t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} P_n(2nt), \quad 0 \le t \le 1.
$$

Tightness of ${X_n}_{n \ge 1}$ in $C[0,1]$ follows from the ballot theorem bounds implicit in (7), which control maximal deviation of partial sums. Finite-dimensional distributions converge to those of Brownian excursion because the recursion (5) encodes the same decomposition as the Markov property of conditioned random walks.

Therefore $X_n \Rightarrow e(t)$ in distribution in $C[0,1]$, where $e(t)$ is Brownian excursion. Any geometric embedding of the Christmas tree pattern that is continuous in the Dyck path representation inherits this weak convergence after rescaling.

The Hausdorff dimension of the limiting graph is determined by local oscillation. Over an interval of width $\delta$, Brownian excursion varies on the order of $\delta^{1/2}$. Covering the graph requires approximately $\delta^{-3/2}$ squares of side $\delta$, yielding dimension $3/2$ through the standard box-counting computation. No deterministic limit curve exists, since fluctuations persist at all scales.

## Status

The limiting object of properly normalized Christmas tree patterns exists in a probabilistic sense as the Brownian excursion and associated continuum random tree. This limit is random rather than deterministic, so the notion of a single “limiting shape” as a geometric figure does not apply.

The fractal dimension of the limiting graph is almost surely $3/2$ under the Brownian excursion scaling limit, while the underlying tree metric limit is the continuum random tree of Hausdorff dimension $2$.

The original question about a deterministic fractal shape has no positive resolution because the limit is not a fixed object but a distribution on fractal curves.
