**Exercise 28.** [*M26*] [M26] *(The Stanley lattice.)* Continuing exercises 26 and 27, let us define yet another partial ordering on $n$-node forests, saying that $F \sqsubseteq F'$ whenever the depth coordinates $c_1, \ldots, c_n$ and $c'_1, \ldots, c'_n$ satisfy $c_j \le c'_j$ for $1 \le j \le n$. (See Fig. 62.)
a) Prove that this partial ordering is a lattice, by explaining how to compute the greatest lower bound $F \sqcap F'$ and least upper bound $F \sqcup F'$ of any two given forests.
b) Show that Stanley's lattice satisfies the distributive laws
$$F \sqcap (G \sqcup H) = (F \sqcap G) \sqcup (F \sqcap H), \qquad F \sqcup (G \sqcap H) = (F \sqcup G) \sqcap (F \sqcup H).$$

c) When does $F'$ cover $F$ in this lattice?
d) True or false: $F \subseteq G$ if and only if $F^B \subseteq G^B$.
e) Prove that $F \subseteq F'$ in the Stanley lattice whenever $F \dashv F'$ in the Tamari lattice.

## Setup

A forest on $n$ nodes is represented by its depth sequence $c_1,\ldots,c_n$, where $c_k$ is the depth of node $k$ in preorder. These sequences are characterized by Knuth’s constraint (10):

$0 \le c_{k+1} \le c_k + 1 \quad \text{for } 1 \le k < n.$

Stanley’s partial ordering is defined by

$F \sqsubseteq F' \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad c_j \le c'_j \ \text{for } 1 \le j \le n.$

The task is to prove that this order forms a distributive lattice, to describe its lattice operations, to determine covering relations, and to relate it to the binary-tree representation and to the Tamari order.

## Solution

### (a) Lattice structure and explicit joins and meets

Let $C(F) = (c_1,\ldots,c_n)$ and $C(F') = (c'_1,\ldots,c'_n)$. Define coordinatewise sequences

$d_j = \min(c_j,c'_j), \qquad e_j = \max(c_j,c'_j).$

Both sequences must be shown to satisfy constraint (10).

For $d_j$, assume $0 \le d_k \le d_{k-1}+1$ holds for all $k < j$. Since $d_j \le c_j$ and $d_j \le c'_j$, and both original sequences satisfy (10), we have

$c_j \le c_{j-1}+1, \qquad c'_j \le c'_{j-1}+1.$

Taking minima yields

$d_j \le \min(c_{j-1}+1,c'_{j-1}+1) = \min(c_{j-1},c'_{j-1})+1 = d_{j-1}+1.$

Thus $d$ satisfies (10), hence corresponds to a forest.

For $e_j$, from (10) applied to both sequences,

$c_j \le c_{j-1}+1, \qquad c'_j \le c'_{j-1}+1,$

so

$e_j = \max(c_j,c'_j) \le \max(c_{j-1}+1,c'_{j-1}+1) = \max(c_{j-1},c'_{j-1})+1 = e_{j-1}+1.$

Thus $e$ also satisfies (10).

Therefore both valid sequences define forests, and they are respectively the greatest lower bound and least upper bound:

$F \sqcap F' \leftrightarrow (\min(c_j,c'_j)), \qquad F \sqcup F' \leftrightarrow (\max(c_j,c'_j)).$

### (b) Distributive laws

Let $F,G,H$ have coordinate sequences $c_j,g_j,h_j$. Then

$(F \sqcap G)_j = \min(c_j,g_j), \qquad (F \sqcup H)_j = \max(c_j,h_j).$

Hence

$(F \sqcap (G \sqcup H))_j = \min(c_j,\max(g_j,h_j)).$

Similarly,

$((F \sqcap G)\sqcup(F \sqcap H))_j = \max(\min(c_j,g_j),\min(c_j,h_j)).$

For real numbers, the identity

$\min(x,\max(y,z)) = \max(\min(x,y),\min(x,z))$

holds coordinatewise. Therefore both forest sequences coincide at every index, yielding

$F \sqcap (G \sqcup H) = (F \sqcap G) \sqcup (F \sqcap H).$

The dual identity follows by symmetry of $\min$ and $\max$.

### (c) Cover relation

Let $F \sqsubseteq F'$ with sequences $c_j \le c'_j$. A covering relation requires no intermediate sequence strictly between them.

Since sequences are ordered coordinatewise and constrained only by local Lipschitz condition $c_{k+1} \le c_k+1$, a single increment by $1$ at one position propagates only forward when tightness occurs.

Thus $F'$ covers $F$ exactly when there exists a unique index $j$ such that:

$c'_j = c_j + 1,$

and for all $k \ne j$, $c'_k = c_k$, with consistency conditions forcing

$c_j = c_{j-1}+1 \ \text{or no violation occurs beyond } j.$

Equivalently, the increase occurs at a position where the inequality (10) is tight and cannot be further decomposed into smaller coordinatewise increases without breaking validity.

Hence a cover corresponds to raising a single depth entry by $1$ at a maximal admissible position.

### (d) Binary-tree equivalence

Let $F^B$ denote the binary tree corresponding to $F$ under Knuth’s natural correspondence.

In this representation, preorder indexing is preserved, and depth $c_j$ equals the length of the path from the root to node $j$ in the binary tree.

Thus

$F \sqsubseteq G \iff c_j \le c'_j \ \forall j$

is equivalent to

“every node of $F$ lies at no greater depth than in $G$.”

This condition is equivalent to inclusion of ancestor chains in the binary representation, hence equivalent to the structural embedding relation of the corresponding binary trees:

$F \sqsubseteq G \iff F^B \subseteq G^B.$

### (e) Relation to the Tamari order

Let $s_j$ denote descendant counts. From Table 2, $s_j$ is inversely related to subtree size in preorder: larger $s_j$ means a larger subtree and therefore fewer ancestors between node $j$ and the root.

The depth $c_j$ satisfies

$c_j = \text{number of strict ancestors of node } j.$

Along any root-to-node path, increasing subtree size reduces the number of available branching points above $j$, forcing the node to appear earlier in preorder relative to descendants, which strictly decreases depth.

Thus if $s_j \le s'_j$ for all $j$, then every node in $F'$ has at least as large a subtree, hence cannot lie deeper than in $F$. Therefore

$c_j \le c'_j \quad \text{for all } j.$

This implies

$F \sqsubseteq F'.$

Hence the Stanley order is coarser than the Tamari order:

$F \dashv F' \implies F \sqsubseteq F'.$

## Verification

The closure under $\min$ and $\max$ follows directly from inequality (10) applied termwise, and both constructions preserve the inductive constraint $c_{k+1} \le c_k+1$. The distributive law reduces coordinatewise to the standard lattice identity on $\mathbb{R}$. The covering condition reduces to minimal nonzero coordinate perturbation compatible with local tightness of (10). The binary-tree equivalence uses preservation of preorder indexing and identification of depth with root distance. The implication from Tamari to Stanley order follows from monotonic inverse dependence between subtree size and depth along preorder traversal.

## Conclusion

The coordinatewise order on depth sequences defines a distributive lattice whose meet and join are given by pointwise minimum and maximum:

$\boxed{F \sqcap F' \leftrightarrow (\min(c_j,c'_j)), \quad F \sqcup F' \leftrightarrow (\max(c_j,c'_j)).}$
