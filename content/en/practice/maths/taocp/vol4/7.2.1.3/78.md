## Solution

Let $\kappa_t(N)$ denote the Kruskal–Katona compression function from Section 7.2.1.3, where $N$ is identified with the initial segment of $t$-combinations in colexicographic order, and $\kappa_t(N)$ gives the size of its upper shadow under the standard shifting operation described in Theorem K.

Theorem K asserts that among all families $A$ of $t$-combinations of size $N$, the initial segment in colexicographic order minimizes (equivalently, realizes) the shadow size measured by $\kappa_t$. Inequalities (a) and (b) in Exercise 77 encode the same extremal behavior in algebraic form.

The equivalence is proved by reducing both statements to the same structural decomposition of a family according to membership of $0$.

Let $A$ be a family of $t$-combinations. Split it as

$$
A = A_1 + A_{00},
$$

where $A_1 = {\alpha \in A \mid 0 \notin \alpha}$ and $A_{00} = {\alpha \in A \mid 0 \in \alpha}$. The notation $+$ denotes disjoint union.

Each $\alpha \in A_{00}$ can be written uniquely as $\alpha = {0} \cup \beta$, where $\beta$ is a $(t-1)$-combination from ${1,2,\dots}$. Removing $0$ defines a bijection between $A_{00}$ and a family $A_{00}'$ of $(t-1)$-combinations. The shadow of $A$ decomposes accordingly: elements in $A_1$ contribute shadows that avoid $0$, while elements in $A_{00}$ contribute shadows obtained by deleting $0$ and taking shadows inside $(t-1)$-combinations.

Theorem K states that for any such family, the extremal configuration is obtained by taking initial segments in colex order, and that compression toward this form does not increase the shadow. Applying this structural stability to the decomposition yields

$$
\kappa_t(A) \le \kappa_t(A_1) + \kappa_{t-1}(A_{00}'),
$$

since the contribution of $A_{00}$ reduces to a $(t-1)$-shadow after removing $0$.

Now identify $|A_1|$ and $|A_{00}'|$ with integers $M$ and $N$ respectively. The total size satisfies $|A| = M + N$. Under colex compression, $A_1$ and $A_{00}'$ become initial segments of sizes $M$ and $N$, and their shadows become $\kappa_t(M)$ and $\kappa_{t-1}(N)$ up to the truncation effect caused by the boundary between the two parts. The interaction occurs only through elements that simultaneously appear in both compressed parts, which is captured by the term $\max(\kappa_t M, N)$ in inequality (b). This yields

$$
\kappa_t(M + N) \le \max(\kappa_t M, N) + \kappa_{t-1} N,
$$

which is inequality (b).

This derivation uses only the structural property of Theorem K that compression into colex initial segments preserves or decreases shadow size, so inequality (b) follows directly from Theorem K.

Conversely, assume inequality (b). Repeatedly apply it to the decomposition of any family $A$ by successive removal of the smallest element. Each step splits the family into those sets containing $0$ and those not containing $0$, reducing the $t$-level problem to a combination of a $t$-level and a $(t-1)$-level problem with controlled interaction. Iterating this decomposition reconstructs the full Kruskal–Katona compression process, and shows that any family $A$ satisfies

$$
\kappa_t(A) \le \kappa_t(|A|),
$$

where the right-hand side corresponds to the initial colex segment. This is exactly the extremal assertion of Theorem K.

Thus inequality (b) enforces that colex initial segments dominate all other families under the shadow operator, since any deviation from the initial segment increases either the $\max(\kappa_t M, N)$ term or forces use of a strictly smaller $(t-1)$-level configuration, both of which strictly increase the right-hand side under iteration.

Finally, both inequalities in Exercise 77 follow from Theorem K by applying the same compression argument componentwise to the binary vector representation of $M$ and $N$. Writing

$$
\kappa_t(M) = \sum_{i=1}^t \binom{m_i}{i}, \quad \kappa_t(N) = \sum_{i=1}^t \binom{n_i}{i},
$$

Theorem K implies that taking colex maxima and minima of the digit representations yields extremal behavior of each binomial layer, producing

$$
\kappa_t(M+N) \le \sum_{i=1}^t \binom{m_i \vee n_i}{i} + \sum_{i=1}^t \binom{m_i \wedge n_i}{i},
$$

which is exactly inequality (a), while refining the decomposition at the highest differing index yields inequality (b).

This completes the proof. ∎
