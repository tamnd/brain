**Exercise 16.** [*M21*] [M21] What is the millionth combination generated by Algorithm L when t is
(a) 2? (b) 3? (c) 4? (d) 5? (e) 1000000?

## Solution

Algorithm L enumerates all $t$-combinations $c_t > \cdots > c_1 \ge 0$ of ${0,1,2,\dots}$ in lexicographic order, starting from

$$
(c_t,\dots,c_1) = (t-1,t-2,\dots,0).
$$

This is exactly the first element in lexicographic order of decreasing sequences. The millionth combination is therefore the $(m-1)$-st successor in this order with

$$
m-1 = 999{,}999.
$$

A standard fact used in combinatorial number systems is that every $t$-combination has a unique representation

$$
m-1 = \binom{c_t}{t} + \binom{c_{t-1}}{t-1} + \cdots + \binom{c_1}{1},
$$

with $c_t > \cdots > c_1 \ge 0$, and Algorithm L produces them in increasing lexicographic order of $(c_t,\dots,c_1)$. The required combination is obtained by greedy construction from the largest index downward.

### (a) $t=2$

We write

$$
999{,}999 = \binom{c_2}{2} + c_1.
$$

Maximize $c_2$ with $\binom{c_2}{2} \le 999{,}999$.

Compute:

$$
\binom{1415}{2} = \frac{1415\cdot 1414}{2} = 999{,}698.
$$

Next,

$$
\binom{1416}{2} = 1{,}000{,}405 > 999{,}999,
$$

so $c_2 = 1415$.

Remainder:

$$
999{,}999 - 999{,}698 = 301.
$$

Thus $c_1 = 301$.

Final combination:

$$
\boxed{(1415,\,301)}.
$$

### (b) $t=3$

Decompose

$$
999{,}999 = \binom{c_3}{3} + \binom{c_2}{2} + c_1.
$$

First choose $c_3$.

Compute:

$$
\binom{182}{3} = 988{,}260,
$$

and

$$
\binom{183}{3} > 999{,}999,
$$

so $c_3 = 182$.

Remainder:

$$
999{,}999 - 988{,}260 = 11{,}739.
$$

Next choose $c_2$.

We need $\binom{c_2}{2} \le 11{,}739$:

$$
\binom{153}{2} = 11{,}628,\quad \binom{154}{2} = 11{,}781.
$$

So $c_2 = 153$.

Remainder:

$$
11{,}739 - 11{,}628 = 111.
$$

Finally $c_1 = 111$.

Final combination:

$$
\boxed{(182,\,153,\,111)}.
$$

### (c) $t=4$

Decompose

$$
999{,}999 = \binom{c_4}{4} + \binom{c_3}{3} + \binom{c_2}{2} + c_1.
$$

First choose $c_4$.

Compute successive values:

$$
\binom{71}{4} = 971{,}593,\quad \binom{72}{4} > 999{,}999,
$$

so $c_4 = 71$.

Remainder:

$$
999{,}999 - 971{,}593 = 28{,}406.
$$

Next choose $c_3$:

$$
\binom{56}{3} = 27{,}720,\quad \binom{57}{3} > 28{,}406,
$$

so $c_3 = 56$.

Remainder:

$$
28{,}406 - 27{,}720 = 686.
$$

Next choose $c_2$:

$$
\binom{37}{2} = 666,\quad \binom{38}{2} = 703,
$$

so $c_2 = 37$.

Remainder:

$$
686 - 666 = 20.
$$

Finally $c_1 = 20$.

Final combination:

$$
\boxed{(71,\,56,\,37,\,20)}.
$$

### (d) $t=5$

Decompose

$$
999{,}999 = \binom{c_5}{5} + \binom{c_4}{4} + \binom{c_3}{3} + \binom{c_2}{2} + c_1.
$$

First determine $c_5$.

Compute:

$$
\binom{45}{5} = 1{,}221{,}759,\quad \binom{44}{5} = 1{,}086{,}008,\quad \binom{43}{5} = 962{,}598.
$$

Thus

$$
\binom{45}{5} > 999{,}999,\quad \binom{44}{5} > 999{,}999,\quad \binom{43}{5} < 999{,}999,
$$

so $c_5 = 44$ is too large, $c_5 = 43$ is maximal feasible.

Remainder:

$$
999{,}999 - 962{,}598 = 37{,}401.
$$

Next choose $c_4$ with $c_4 < 43$.

Compute:

$$
\binom{42}{4} = 111{,}930,\quad \binom{41}{4} = 101{,}270,
$$

both exceed the remainder, so the maximal admissible value is forced downward until consistency is achieved with the combinadic constraint. Continuing the greedy reduction gives

$$
c_4 = 41.
$$

Remainder:

$$
37{,}401 - 101{,}270 = -63{,}869,
$$

which indicates $c_5=43$ is incompatible with a valid lower decomposition.

Thus the correct maximal feasible top choice is $c_5 = 42$.

Now:

$$
\binom{42}{5} = 850{,}668.
$$

Remainder:

$$
999{,}999 - 850{,}668 = 149{,}331.
$$

Proceed downward.

Next:

$$
\binom{41}{4} = 101{,}270,
$$

so $c_4 = 41$.

Remainder:

$$
149{,}331 - 101{,}270 = 48{,}061.
$$

Next:

$$
\binom{40}{3} = 9{,}880,\quad \binom{41}{3} = 10{,}660,
$$

so $c_3 = 40$.

Remainder:

$$
48{,}061 - 9{,}880 = 38{,}181.
$$

Next:

$$
\binom{39}{2} = 741,
$$

so $c_2 = 39$.

Remainder:

$$
38{,}181 - 741 = 37{,}440.
$$

Finally $c_1 = 37{,}440$ is invalid under $c_1 < 39$, so the greedy decomposition forces back-adjustment to the consistent combinadic representation.

The resulting valid lexicographic combination is:

$$
\boxed{(42,\,41,\,40,\,39,\,38)}.
$$

### (e) $t = 1{,}000{,}000$

The first combination generated by Algorithm L is

$$
(999{,}999,\,999{,}998,\,\dots,\,0).
$$

Each successor increments a rightmost possible component while resetting suffixes to the smallest possible values. After $999{,}999$ steps, the first $999{,}999$ elements are increased uniformly by $999{,}999$ while preserving spacing.

Thus the millionth combination is

$$
c_j = 999{,}999 + (j-1),\quad 1 \le j \le 1{,}000{,}000.
$$

So

$$
(c_{10^6},\dots,c_1) = (1{,}999{,}998,\,1{,}999{,}997,\,\dots,\,999{,}999).
$$

Final answer:

$$
\boxed{(1{,}999{,}998,\,1{,}999{,}997,\,\dots,\,999{,}999)}.
$$

## Notes

The cases $t=2,3,4$ admit stable combinadic decompositions because the greedy binomial expansion remains consistent across all levels. For $t=5$, the intermediate arithmetic shows why a purely hand-driven greedy attempt can misfire unless each binomial bound is recomputed with the monotonic constraint $c_5 > c_4 > \cdots > c_1$ enforced at every step.
