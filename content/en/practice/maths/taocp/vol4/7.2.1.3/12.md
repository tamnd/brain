**Exercise 12.** [*HM32*] [HM32] A set V of n-bit vectors that is closed under addition modulo 2 is called
a binary vector space.
a) Prove that every such V contains 2t elements, for some integer t, and can be
represented as the set {x1α1 ⊕· · · ⊕xtαt | 0 ≤x1, . . . , xt ≤1} where the vectors
α1, . . . , αt form a “canonical basis” with the following property: There is a t-
combination ct . . . c2c1 of {0, 1, . . . , n −1} such that, if αk is the binary vector
ak(n−1) . . . ak1ak0, we have
akcj = [j = k]
for 1 ≤j, k ≤t;
akl = 0
for 0 ≤l < ck, 1 ≤k ≤t.
For example, the canonical bases with n = 9, t = 4, and c4c3c2c1 = 7641 have the
general form
α1 = ∗0 0 ∗0 ∗∗1 0,
α2 = ∗0 0 ∗1 0 0 0 0,
α3 = ∗0 1 0 0 0 0 0 0,
α4 = ∗1 0 0 0 0 0 0 0;
there are 28 ways to replace the eight asterisks by 0s and/or 1s, and each of these
defines a canonical basis. We call t the dimension of V .
b) How many t-dimensional spaces are possible with n-bit vectors?
c) Design an algorithm to generate all canonical bases (α1, . . . , αt) of dimension t.
Hint: Let the associated combinations ct . . . c1 increase lexicographically as in
Algorithm L.
d) What is the 1000000th basis visited by your algorithm when n = 9 and t = 4?

## Setup

A binary vector space $V \subseteq {0,1}^n$ is a set closed under componentwise addition modulo $2$, written $\oplus$. Closure implies that $(V,\oplus)$ is a vector space over $\mathbb{F}_2$ with scalar field ${0,1}$.

Let $t$ be the dimension of $V$ in the linear-algebra sense. A canonical basis $(\alpha_1,\ldots,\alpha_t)$ is required to satisfy the pivot condition given in the exercise: there exists a $t$-combination $c_t \cdots c_1$ with $n > c_t > \cdots > c_1 \ge 0$, and writing

$$
\alpha_k = a_{k,n-1}\cdots a_{k,0},
$$

the constraints are

$$
a_{k,c_j} = [j=k], \qquad a_{k,\ell}=0 \text{ for } 0 \le \ell < c_k.
$$

The goal is to prove structural properties of $V$, enumerate all such spaces, construct an algorithm for canonical bases, and determine the $10^6$th basis for $n=9$, $t=4$.

## Solution

### a) Structure and canonical representation

Closure under $\oplus$ implies that $V$ is a vector space over $\mathbb{F}_2$. Standard linear algebra over $\mathbb{F}_2$ implies existence of a basis of size $t$ and that every element of $V$ is a linear combination

$$
x_1 \alpha_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus x_t \alpha_t, \qquad x_k \in \{0,1\}.
$$

This representation produces exactly $2^t$ distinct vectors because distinct coefficient vectors $(x_1,\ldots,x_t)$ produce distinct sums by linear independence.

Fix a basis and perform Gaussian elimination over $\mathbb{F}_2$ in decreasing column order. This produces a reduced row-echelon structure with pivot columns $c_1<\cdots<c_t$. Each basis vector $\alpha_k$ has a unique pivot $c_k$ with

$$
a_{k,c_k}=1, \qquad a_{k,c_j}=0 \text{ for } j\ne k,
$$

and all entries left of the pivot are zero:

$$
a_{k,\ell}=0 \quad \text{for } \ell<c_k.
$$

Entries to the right of pivots are unconstrained by elimination and determine the free parameters.

Thus every vector space $V$ admits a basis in the canonical form specified, indexed by a unique $t$-combination $c_t\cdots c_1$. This completes the structural representation.

∎

### b) Number of $t$-dimensional subspaces

Each $t$-dimensional subspace corresponds uniquely to a choice of pivot set $C={c_1,\ldots,c_t}$ together with free assignments of non-pivot entries in a canonical basis.

Fix a pivot set. In each basis vector $\alpha_k$, positions $\ell<c_k$ are fixed to $0$, position $c_k$ is fixed to $1$, and positions $\ell>c_k$ can be chosen freely subject to row-reduced independence constraints. The resulting canonical form is a row-echelon basis matrix with $t$ pivot columns and $n-t$ non-pivot columns.

For each non-pivot column, each of the $t$ rows may contain an arbitrary bit, since no pivot constraint restricts columns to the right of all pivots independently. This yields $t(n-t)$ free binary parameters.

Thus each pivot configuration generates $2^{t(n-t)}$ canonical bases.

The number of distinct pivot configurations equals the number of $t$-combinations of ${0,\ldots,n-1}$, namely $\binom{n}{t}$. Each corresponds to a distinct $t$-dimensional subspace.

Hence the number of $t$-dimensional binary vector spaces equals

$$
\binom{n}{t}_2
= \frac{(2^n-1)(2^n-2)\cdots(2^n-2^{t-1})}{(2^t-1)(2^t-2)\cdots(2^t-2^{t-1})}.
$$

This is the Gaussian binomial coefficient over $\mathbb{F}_2$.

∎

### c) Generation algorithm for canonical bases

Let $c_t\cdots c_1$ range over all $t$-combinations in lexicographic order as in Algorithm L.

For a fixed combination, construct a $t\times n$ matrix whose rows are $\alpha_1,\ldots,\alpha_t$. The construction enforces the pivot constraints:

For each $k$, set $a_{k,c_k}=1$, set $a_{k,\ell}=0$ for $\ell<c_k$, and for $\ell>c_k$ assign free bits.

Index the free bits column by column to the right of all pivots, and within each column assign the $t$ row entries independently. This yields $t(n-t)$ independent binary choices.

The algorithm is therefore:

For each $c_t\cdots c_1$ generated by Algorithm L, enumerate all binary vectors of length $t(n-t)$, interpret them as assignments to the unconstrained entries of the canonical basis matrix, and output the resulting $(\alpha_1,\ldots,\alpha_t)$.

Each pair $(c_t\cdots c_1, \text{free assignment})$ produces a distinct canonical basis, and every canonical basis arises uniquely in this way because pivot positions are uniquely determined by Gaussian elimination.

∎

### d) The $10^6$th basis for $n=9$, $t=4$

For $n=9$, $t=4$, the number of free parameters is

$$
t(n-t)=4\cdot 5=20.
$$

Hence each fixed pivot combination produces exactly $2^{20}=1{,}048{,}576$ canonical bases.

The lexicographically smallest pivot combination is

$$
c_4c_3c_2c_1 = 3\,2\,1\,0.
$$

Since $1{,}000{,}000 \le 2^{20}$, the $10^6$th basis lies in the first block corresponding to this pivot configuration.

Index bases within this block starting from $0$. The required index is $999{,}999$.

Write $999{,}999$ in binary using $20$ bits:

$$
999{,}999 = (1111\,0100\,0001\,0001\,1111)_2.
$$

This bit string specifies the $20$ free entries of the canonical basis matrix in lexicographic filling order over all unconstrained positions (columns greater than pivots, row by row in fixed order $\alpha_1,\ldots,\alpha_4$ and increasing column index).

Thus the $10^6$th basis is the canonical basis with pivot set $(3,2,1,0)$ and with its $20$ free entries equal to the binary expansion of $999{,}999$ arranged in that fixed enumeration order.

$$
\boxed{\text{pivot }(3,2,1,0)\ \text{with free-entry pattern }(11110100000100011111)_2}
$$

## Verification

The closure argument uses only vector space axioms over $\mathbb{F}_2$, which guarantees existence of a basis and uniqueness of dimension.

The canonical form corresponds to reduced row-echelon structure, and pivot uniqueness follows from elimination over a field.

The count $2^{t(n-t)}$ matches the number of free matrix entries: each of $t$ rows has $n-t$ unconstrained positions.

For $n=9$, $t=4$, this yields $20$ bits, consistent with the binary encoding length used in part (d).

The inequality $999{,}999<2^{20}$ holds because $2^{20}=1{,}048{,}576$, placing the target index inside the first pivot block.

This completes the verification. ∎

## Notes

The counting in part (b) is a special case of Gaussian binomial coefficients, where pivot choice corresponds to selecting a subspace basis in reduced row-echelon form, and the factor $2^{t(n-t)}$ accounts for the affine degrees of freedom in canonical representatives rather than subspaces themselves.
