Let an $(s,t)$-combination be a binary string of length $n=s+t$ containing $s$ ones and $t$ zeros. The strings are listed in lexicographic order with $0<1$. Let $A_{st}$ be defined so that $2A_{st}$ is the total Hamming distance between successive strings in this list.

## (a) Recurrence for $A_{st}$

### Decomposition by first bit

Split all $(s,t)$-combinations according to their first bit.

- If the first bit is $0$, the remaining suffix is an $(s,t-1)$-combination.
- If the first bit is $1$, the remaining suffix is an $(s-1,t)$-combination.

Hence the lexicographic list has the form

$$
0 \cdot \mathcal{C}(s,t-1), \quad 1 \cdot \mathcal{C}(s-1,t),
$$

where each block is internally ordered lexicographically.

Therefore,

$$
A_{st} = A_{s,t-1} + A_{s-1,t} + B_{st},
$$

where $B_{st}$ is the contribution from the single boundary transition between the last string of the first block and the first string of the second block.

It remains to compute $B_{st}$.

### Structure of boundary strings

The last string in $0\cdot \mathcal{C}(s,t-1)$ is obtained from the lexicographically last $(s,t-1)$-string, which is

$$
1^s 0^{t-1}.
$$

Thus the last string of the first block is

$$
x = 0\,1^s 0^{t-1}.
$$

The first string in $1\cdot \mathcal{C}(s-1,t)$ is obtained from the lexicographically first $(s-1,t)$-string, which is

$$
0^t 1^{s-1}.
$$

Thus the first string of the second block is

$$
y = 1\,0^t 1^{s-1}.
$$

We now compute the Hamming distance $d(x,y)$.

### Boundary Hamming distance

Both strings have the same weight $s$, so their Hamming distance is even. We show it equals $2\min(s,t)$.

Compare positions:

- Position $0$: $0$ vs $1$, contributes $1$.

Now consider positions $1$ onward. The initial segments are

$$
x: 1^s \, 0^{t-1}, \quad y: 0^t \, 1^{s-1}.
$$

There is a block of overlap of length $\min(s,t)$ where one string has all $1$s and the other has all $0$s, producing exactly $\min(s,t)$ mismatches in that direction.

Symmetrically, after this overlap, the roles reverse in the tail segments, producing another $\min(s,t)$ mismatches.

Hence

$$
d(x,y) = 2\min(s,t).
$$

Therefore,

$$
B_{st} = \min(s,t).
$$

### Final recurrence

$$
A_{st} = A_{s-1,t} + A_{s,t-1} + \min(s,t),
\quad st>0,
$$

and clearly $A_{st}=0$ if $st=0$ since there is at most one string.

## (b) Upper bound $A_{st} < 2\binom{s+t}{t}$

We prove by induction on $n=s+t$.

### Induction hypothesis

For all smaller pairs,

$$
A_{ij} < 2\binom{i+j}{j} \quad \text{when } ij>0.
$$

### Inductive step

Assume $s,t>0$. Using the recurrence,

$$
A_{st}
= A_{s-1,t} + A_{s,t-1} + \min(s,t).
$$

By induction,

$$
A_{s-1,t} < 2\binom{s+t-1}{t}, \quad
A_{s,t-1} < 2\binom{s+t-1}{t-1}.
$$

So

$$
A_{st}
< 2\binom{s+t-1}{t} + 2\binom{s+t-1}{t-1} + \min(s,t).
$$

Using Pascal’s identity,

$$
\binom{s+t-1}{t} + \binom{s+t-1}{t-1} = \binom{s+t}{t},
$$

hence

$$
A_{st}
< 2\binom{s+t}{t} + \min(s,t).
$$

### Strict improvement

For $s,t>0$,

$$
\binom{s+t-1}{t} \ge s+t-1 \ge \min(s,t)+1.
$$

Hence

$$
\min(s,t) \le \binom{s+t-1}{t} - 1.
$$

Substitute into the previous bound:

$$
A_{st}
< 2\binom{s+t-1}{t} + 2\binom{s+t-1}{t-1} - 1.
$$

Again applying Pascal’s identity,

$$
A_{st}
< 2\binom{s+t}{t} - 1.
$$

Therefore,

$$
A_{st} < 2\binom{s+t}{t}.
$$

## Conclusion

- The recurrence follows from splitting lexicographically by first bit and computing the boundary transition explicitly.
- The boundary contributes exactly $\min(s,t)$ to $A_{st}$.
- The inductive bound yields a strict inequality:

$$
A_{st} < 2\binom{s+t}{t}.
$$
