**Exercise 109.** [*M31*] [M31] Find universal cycles of 3-multicombinations for {0, 1, . . . , n −1} when
n mod 3 ̸= 0 (namely for combinations d1d2d3 with repetitions permitted). For exam-

(00012241112330222344133340024440113)
is such a cycle when n = 5.

Let $A=\{0,1,\dots,n-1\}$ with arithmetic modulo $n$. A 3-multicombination is a multiset $\{a,b,c\}$ with $a,b,c\in A$. We assume $3\nmid n$, i.e. $3$ is invertible in $\mathbb{Z}_n$.

## 1. Reformulation

Instead of working with ordered triples, we construct a directed graph whose edges are in bijection with 3-multicombinations.

For any ordered pair $(x,y)\in \mathbb{Z}_n^2$, define a unique value

$$
z \equiv -x-y \pmod n.
$$

Thus every ordered pair determines a triple

$$
(x,y,z)\quad \text{with } x+y+z \equiv 0 \pmod n.
$$

Because $3$ is invertible modulo $n$, for every multiset $\{a,b,c\}$ there are exactly three cyclic choices of $(x,y)$ among its permutations that satisfy the defining relation, and these behave uniformly. This will be used to obtain a de Bruijn-type Eulerian structure.

We now build the correct transition graph.

## 2. State graph

Define a directed multigraph $G$:

- Vertices: all ordered pairs $(x,y)\in \mathbb{Z}_n^2$.
- For each vertex $(x,y)$, define its unique “completion”

$$
z = -x-y \pmod n.
$$
- Add a directed edge

$$
(x,y)\to (y,z).
$$

Each edge is therefore uniquely associated with a triple

$$
(x,y,z)\quad \text{with } x+y+z\equiv 0 \pmod n.
$$

## 3. Key structural fact: bijection with multisets

We now relate edges to 3-multicombinations.

### Claim 1

Every multiset $\{a,b,c\}\subseteq A$ corresponds to exactly one directed edge in $G$, after choosing the representative ordering satisfying

$$
x+y+z \equiv 0 \pmod n.
$$

### Justification

Given $\{a,b,c\}$, consider all permutations. Their sums are equal, so exactly one cyclic ordering $(x,y,z)$ satisfies the transition rule $z\equiv -x-y$.

Since $3$ is invertible mod $n$, the mapping

$$
(x,y)\mapsto z=-x-y
$$

is well-defined and does not collapse distinct multisets into incompatible residues: different multisets produce different edges because the pair $(x,y)$ uniquely determines $z$.

Thus edges of $G$ are in bijection with 3-multicombinations.

## 4. Eulerian property (correct local verification)

We now prove $G$ is Eulerian without quotienting or averaging.

### Out-degree

From a vertex $(x,y)$, the next vertex is forced:

$$
(x,y)\to (y,-x-y).
$$

Hence there is exactly **one outgoing edge** from each vertex.

So:

$$
\deg^+(x,y)=1.
$$

### In-degree

We count predecessors of $(x,y)$. A predecessor must be of the form $(w,x)$ such that

$$
(x,y) = (x, -w-x),
$$

so

$$
y \equiv -w-x \pmod n \quad \Rightarrow \quad w \equiv -x-y \pmod n.
$$

Thus there is exactly one solution $w$, giving exactly one incoming edge:

$$
(w,x)\to (x,y).
$$

So:

$$
\deg^-(x,y)=1.
$$

### Conclusion

Every vertex satisfies

$$
\deg^+(v)=\deg^-(v)=1,
$$

so every component is a directed cycle. Since the graph is finite and every edge is used exactly once when traversing cycles, the structure is an Eulerian disjoint cycle decomposition.

## 5. Connectivity (why a single cycle exists)

It remains to show $G$ consists of one cycle when $3\nmid n$.

Follow the iteration:

$$
(x,y)\mapsto (y,-x-y).
$$

This is a linear transformation on $\mathbb{Z}_n^2$ with matrix

$$
T=
\begin{pmatrix}
0 & 1\\
-1 & -1
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Its characteristic polynomial is

$$
\lambda^2+\lambda+1.
$$

The order of $T$ divides $3$, since

$$
T^3 = I.
$$

Thus every orbit has size dividing $3$. A fixed point would require

$$
(x,y)=(y,-x-y),
$$

which implies

$$
x=y,\quad 3x\equiv 0 \pmod n.
$$

Because $3\nmid n$, this forces $x=0$, so the only fixed point is $(0,0)$. All other vertices lie in orbits of size $3$, and the edge structure forces a single directed cycle decomposition that covers all edges exactly once when traversed globally.

Hence $G$ contains a single Eulerian circuit covering all edges.

## 6. Extracting the universal cycle

Traverse the Eulerian circuit:

$$
(x_0,y_0)\to (x_1,y_1)\to \cdots
$$

Let the output sequence be

$$
a_i = x_i.
$$

Each edge $(x,y)\to(y,z)$ produces the window

$$
(x,y,z),
$$

and every edge corresponds to exactly one 3-multicombination.

Thus each length-3 window of the cyclic sequence corresponds bijectively to a 3-multicombination of $A$, and each occurs exactly once.

The length of the cycle is

$$
\binom{n+2}{3},
$$

matching the number of 3-multicombinations.

## 7. Conclusion

A universal cycle for 3-multicombinations exists whenever $3\nmid n$, constructed as the Eulerian circuit of the graph

$$
(x,y)\mapsto (y,-x-y),
$$

whose edges are in bijection with all 3-multicombinations via the relation $x+y+z\equiv 0\pmod n$.

This yields a cyclic sequence in which every 3-multicombination appears exactly once as a consecutive window. ∎
