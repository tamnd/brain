**Exercise 101.** [*M25*] [M25] If f(x1, . . . , xn) is a Boolean formula, let F(p) be the probability that
f(x1, . . . , xn) = 1 when each variable xj independently is 1 with probability p.
a) Calculate G(p) and H(p) for the Boolean formulas g(w, x, y, z) = wxz∨wyz∨xy¯z,
h(w, x, y, z) = ¯wyz ∨xyz.
b) Show that there is a monotone Boolean function f(w, x, y, z) such that F(p) =
G(p), but there is no such function with F(p) = H(p). Explain how to test this
condition in general.

### Part (a)

Each variable $w,x,y,z$ is independent with $\Pr(x_j=1)=p$.

#### Computation of $G(p)$

$$
g(w,x,y,z)=wxz \lor wyz \lor x\bar y z.
$$

Factor $z$:

$$
g = z \cdot (wx \lor wy \lor x\bar y).
$$

Hence

$$
G(p)=\Pr(g=1)=\Pr(z=1)\Pr(wx \lor wy \lor x\bar y)=p \cdot \Pr(A),
$$

where $A = wx \lor wy \lor x\bar y$.

Condition on $(x,y)$:

- $x=0,y=0$: $A=0$
- $x=0,y=1$: $A=w$, so probability $p$
- $x=1,y=0$: $A=1$
- $x=1,y=1$: $A=w$, so probability $p$

Thus

$$
\Pr(A)=(1-p)^2\cdot 0 + (1-p)p\cdot p + p(1-p)\cdot 1 + p^2 \cdot p.
$$

Compute:

$$
\Pr(A)=p^2(1-p)+p(1-p)+p^3 = p.
$$

Therefore

$$
G(p)=p \cdot p = p^2.
$$

$$
\boxed{G(p)=p^2}
$$

A monotone realization is $f(w,x,y,z)=wx$, since $\Pr(wx=1)=p^2$.

#### Computation of $H(p)$

$$
h(w,x,y,z)=\bar w y z \lor x y z = yz(\bar w \lor x).
$$

Independence gives

$$
H(p)=\Pr(yz)\Pr(\bar w \lor x)=p^2 \cdot (1-\Pr(w=1,x=0)).
$$

$$
\Pr(w=1,x=0)=p(1-p),
$$

so

$$
\Pr(\bar w \lor x)=1-p(1-p)=1-p+p^2.
$$

Thus

$$
H(p)=p^2(1-p+p^2)=p^2-p^3+p^4.
$$

$$
\boxed{H(p)=p^2-p^3+p^4}
$$

### Part (b)

#### Existence for $G(p)$

The function $f(w,x,y,z)=wx$ is monotone and satisfies

$$
F(p)=p^2=G(p).
$$

#### Nonexistence for $H(p)$

Let $f$ be a monotone Boolean function on four variables, and let $A \subseteq \{0,1\}^4$ be its set of satisfying inputs. Monotonicity means $A$ is upward closed: if $a \in A$ and $b \ge a$ coordinatewise, then $b \in A$.

Let $m_k$ be the number of **minimal true assignments** of Hamming weight $k$. Then every satisfying assignment is generated uniquely by extending a minimal one, so the number of satisfying assignments of weight $k$, denoted $c_k$, satisfies

$$
c_k = \sum_{j \le k} m_j \binom{4-j}{k-j}.
$$

For any monotone function, all $m_j \ge 0$.

#### Step 1: Convert $H(p)$ to weight-count form

We express

$$
H(p)=p^2-p^3+p^4
$$

in the basis $p^k(1-p)^{4-k}$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
(1-p)^4 &= 1-4p+6p^2-4p^3+p^4 \\
p(1-p)^3 &= p-3p^2+3p^3-p^4 \\
p^2(1-p)^2 &= p^2-2p^3+p^4 \\
p^3(1-p) &= p^3-p^4 \\
p^4 &= p^4.
\end{aligned}
$$

Solving

$$
H(p)=\sum_{k=0}^4 c_k p^k(1-p)^{4-k}
$$

gives

$$
(c_0,c_1,c_2,c_3,c_4)=(0,0,1,1,1).
$$

So a function with this probability polynomial must satisfy:

- exactly one satisfying assignment of weight 2,
- one of weight 3,
- the all-ones assignment.

#### Step 2: Apply monotonicity constraint

Since $c_2=1$, there is exactly one satisfying assignment of weight 2; call it $S$. Because the function is monotone, $S$ must be a minimal true assignment.

A minimal assignment of size 2 generates all supersets obtained by adding one element. In a 4-variable universe, such a set has exactly

$$
\binom{4-2}{1}=2
$$

distinct supersets of size 3.

Therefore monotonicity forces

$$
c_3 \ge 2.
$$

But from $H(p)$,

$$
c_3 = 1,
$$

a contradiction.

Hence no monotone Boolean function can realize $H(p)$.

$$
\boxed{\text{No monotone } f \text{ satisfies } F(p)=H(p).}
$$

### General test

For a Boolean function on $n$ variables, define $c_k$ as the number of satisfying assignments of Hamming weight $k$. A monotone Boolean function exists with these weights if and only if there exist integers $m_k \ge 0$ such that

$$
c_k = \sum_{j \le k} m_j \binom{n-j}{k-j}.
$$

To test monotone representability:

1. Compute the coefficients $c_k$ of $F(p)$ in the basis $p^k(1-p)^{n-k}$.
2. Solve the triangular system above for $m_k$.
3. The function is monotone representable if and only if all $m_k \ge 0$.

### Final results

$$
\boxed{G(p)=p^2}, \qquad \boxed{H(p)=p^2-p^3+p^4}
$$

- $G(p)$: realizable by monotone function $wx$.
- $H(p)$: not realizable due to violation of upward-closure constraints.
