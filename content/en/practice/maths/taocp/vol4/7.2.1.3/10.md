**Exercise 10.** &#9654; [*21*] [21] The “World Series” of baseball is traditionally a competition in which the
American League champion (A) plays the National League champion (N) until one of
them has beaten the other four times. What is a good way to list all possible scenarios
AAAA, AAANA, AAANNA, . . . , NNNN? What is a simple way to assign consecutive
integers to those scenarios?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m09s

## Solution

Let $A$ denote a win by the American League champion and $N$ a win by the National League champion. A complete scenario is a finite word over ${A,N}$ in which the process stops at the first time one letter appears four times.

Every scenario has a unique final symbol, namely the winner’s fourth win. If the total length is $k$, then $4 \le k \le 7$, since at most three wins by the loser can occur before termination.

### Structure of an $A$-winning scenario

Assume $A$ wins in $k$ games. Then the final symbol is $A$, and the prefix of length $k-1$ contains exactly three $A$’s and $k-4$ occurrences of $N$.

Write the positions of the first three $A$’s among the first $k-1$ positions as

$1 \le c_1 < c_2 < c_3 \le k-1.$

The final $A$ occurs at position $k$. The remaining positions are filled with $N$.

Every such choice produces a valid scenario. No restriction is violated, since the total number of $N$’s is $k-4 \le 3$, so $N$ cannot reach four wins before termination.

Hence the $A$-winning scenarios of length $k$ are in bijection with $(k-1,3)$-combinations.

The counts follow:

$$
k=4:\ \binom{3}{3}=1,
\quad
k=5:\ \binom{4}{3}=4,
\quad
k=6:\ \binom{5}{3}=10,
\quad
k=7:\ \binom{6}{3}=20.
$$

### Structure of an $N$-winning scenario

By symmetry, if $N$ wins in $k$ games, then the final symbol is $N$, and the prefix of length $k-1$ contains exactly three $N$’s and $k-4$ occurrences of $A$.

Thus $N$-winning scenarios of length $k$ are also in bijection with $(k-1,3)$-combinations, giving the same counts:

$$
1,4,10,20.
$$

### Enumeration scheme

Order scenarios first by winner, then by length $k$ increasing from $4$ to $7$, and within each class use lexicographic order on the position sets ${c_1,c_2,c_3}$.

For $A$-winning scenarios, this corresponds to lexicographic order of combinations

$$
(c_3,c_2,c_1)
\quad \text{with} \quad
k-1 \ge c_3 > c_2 > c_1 \ge 1.
$$

Each triple determines the word uniquely by placing $A$ in positions $c_1,c_2,c_3,k$ and $N$ elsewhere.

The same construction applies to $N$-winning scenarios.

This produces a single ordered list of all scenarios:

$$
AAAA,\ AAANA,\ AAANNA,\ \ldots,\ NNNN.
$$

### Integer assignment

Assign consecutive integers by lexicographic rank within the above ordering.

For an $A$-winning scenario ending in length $k$, define its code as

$$
\bigl(0,\ k,\ (c_1,c_2,c_3)\bigr),
$$

and for an $N$-winning scenario,

$$
\bigl(1,\ k,\ (c_1,c_2,c_3)\bigr),
$$

where $0$ corresponds to $A$-winning and $1$ to $N$-winning.

Order these tuples lexicographically, with $(k,c_1,c_2,c_3)$ ordered by increasing $k$ and then lexicographically in $(c_1,c_2,c_3)$.

The rank function obtained from this ordering is a bijection onto

$$
\{0,1,\dots,69\},
$$

since the total number of scenarios is

$$
2\bigl(1+4+10+20\bigr)=70.
$$

Thus each scenario receives a unique consecutive integer equal to its lexicographic rank.

This completes the construction and the assignment. ∎
