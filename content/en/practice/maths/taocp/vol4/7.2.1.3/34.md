## Setup

An (s, t)-combination is represented in index-list form $c_t \cdots c_2 c_1$ with

$$
n = s + t,\qquad n > c_t > \cdots > c_1 \ge 0.
$$

A genlex listing is produced by Algorithm L, where each transition is determined by finding the largest index $j$ such that $c_{j+1} > c_j + 1$, increasing $c_j$, and resetting $c_{j-1},\ldots,c_1$ to their minimal values.

A transition is called imperfect when some component change has magnitude $2$, that is, some $c_i \leftarrow c_i \pm 2$ occurs between successive combinations. Exercise 33 identifies listings that are near-perfect in the sense that imperfect transitions are rare; this exercise asks how to construct listings that minimize their number.

The goal is to choose, among all genlex-compatible orderings (i.e., orderings that respect the same set of local successor rules as Algorithm L allows), a scheme that minimizes the number of imperfect transitions when $s$ and $t$ are not large.

## Solution

Imperfect transitions arise exactly from long carry chains in Step L3. When scanning from $c_1$ upward, if $c_1 = 0$, then $c_2 = 1$, and more generally $c_i = i-1$ holds for a maximal prefix, then the algorithm must propagate the reset $c_i \leftarrow i-1$ through this entire prefix until it reaches the first index $j$ such that

$$
c_{j+1} > c_j + 1.
$$

After visiting a combination, the next state differs from the previous one by increasing $c_j$ by $1$ and resetting all lower components to their minimal values. The reset induces a large change whenever the prefix length $j-1$ is large, and in index representation this is the only mechanism that produces jumps of size $2$ in some coordinate, namely when a component is forced to drop from a value near its upper bound to its minimum in a single transition of the lexicographic control structure.

Thus, minimizing imperfect transitions is equivalent to minimizing the total number of long reset cascades in Step L3.

A cascade of length at least $2$ occurs exactly when there are at least two consecutive equalities of the form $c_i = i-1$ at the moment the increase is triggered. Therefore, the problem reduces to controlling how often the structure of a combination forces long initial segments of the form

$$
(c_1, c_2, \ldots, c_k) = (0,1,\ldots,k-1).
$$

In the pure lexicographic genlex order, these long forced prefixes occur systematically near the boundaries of blocks of fixed higher indices. The mechanism is rigid: once $c_{j+1}$ reaches its minimum allowable value relative to $c_j$, the algorithm must restart a full prefix of minimal values, producing repeated long resets.

To suppress these events, the construction must maximize the available slack at every stage where a reset decision is made. For each state $c_t \cdots c_1$, define the slack at position $i$ by

$$
\Delta_i = c_{i+1} - c_i - 1,\qquad (1 \le i < t),
$$

with the convention $c_{t+1} = n$. A long cascade occurs precisely when $\Delta_1 = \cdots = \Delta_{j-1} = 0$ for a large $j-1$. Therefore, minimizing imperfect transitions requires keeping the vector $(\Delta_1,\ldots,\Delta_{t-1})$ as spread out as possible over the entire listing.

The construction achieving this within the genlex framework is obtained by modifying the tie-breaking behavior of Step L3. Instead of always choosing the smallest valid $j$ (which maximizes prefix length and therefore maximizes cascade size), one selects among all valid indices $j$ the index that maximizes $\Delta_j$ at the moment of transition. Equivalently, one chooses the rightmost position where increasing $c_j$ does not immediately force a maximal reset chain.

This modification changes only the control structure of the genlex traversal, not the set of admissible transitions in the Johnson graph of combinations. Each step still corresponds to a single increase of one coordinate followed by resetting a prefix, but the choice of where to break is biased toward positions where the gap to the next coordinate is largest.

This greedy selection has the effect that long prefixes of forced equalities are broken as early as possible. Consequently, whenever a reset occurs, the number of forced decrements in the prefix is minimized. Since each imperfect transition corresponds to a reset cascade of length at least $2$, minimizing cascade length minimizes the number of such transitions.

When $s$ and $t$ are not large, the slack distribution remains sufficiently nondegenerate that this greedy rule prevents repeated long cascades from concentrating in a small region of the listing. The resulting scheme differs from the pure lexicographic order only at boundary configurations where multiple indices satisfy the condition in Step L3, and each such modification replaces a potential long cascade by a shorter one or by a single-step adjustment.

Therefore the construction is obtained by running Algorithm L with the modified rule: in Step L3, choose $j$ maximizing $c_{j+1} - c_j$ among all admissible choices. This produces a genlex-compatible listing in which every possible cascade is locally minimized, hence the total number of imperfect transitions is as small as the genlex framework permits.

This completes the construction of schemes that are as near as possible to perfect in the sense of minimizing transitions of the form $c_j \leftarrow c_j \pm 2$. ∎

## Verification

Each transition in Algorithm L changes exactly one index $c_j$ by $+1$ and resets a prefix to minimal values. A change of magnitude $2$ in some coordinate occurs only when a reset skips over an intermediate admissible value, which happens exactly when a prefix of length at least $2$ is forced to restart simultaneously.

Maximizing $c_{j+1} - c_j - 1$ ensures that the chosen $j$ is the earliest point where further incrementing would not immediately force a longer prefix reset. Therefore any alternative choice of $j$ either preserves the same cascade length or increases it, since it moves the reset boundary into a region where more consecutive equalities $c_i = i-1$ already hold.

Thus every modification reduces or preserves the length of every cascade induced by Step L3. Since each imperfect transition corresponds to a cascade of length at least $2$, the number of such transitions cannot increase under the rule, and it is minimized among all genlex-compatible choices.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Notes

The construction can be viewed as a greedy smoothing of the Johnson graph traversal induced by lexicographic generation. The lexicographic order is extremal in the opposite direction: it maximizes prefix stability and therefore concentrates large resets. The modified rule distributes resets more evenly, producing a listing closer to a revolving-door Gray code while still remaining within the genlex framework.
