**Exercise 71.** [*M20*] [M20] How many t-cliques can a million-edge graph have?

## Solution

Let $m = 10^6$. A $t$-clique is a complete subgraph on $t$ vertices, so every $t$-clique contributes $\binom{t}{2}$ edges contained inside it. The task is to maximize the number of $K_t$ subgraphs among all graphs with exactly $m$ edges.

Let $G$ be an extremal graph on $m$ edges that maximizes the number of $t$-cliques. Standard edge-symmetrization applies: if two vertices $u,v$ satisfy $N(u)\subseteq N(v)$ and $uv\notin E(G)$, then adding the edge $uv$ and removing some edge incident to $v$ cannot decrease the number of $t$-cliques containing unaffected vertices, while potentially increasing those involving $v$. Repeating such transformations concentrates edges into a single highly dense region, and any optimal configuration must be a threshold graph, hence a union of a clique and isolated vertices. This is the Zykov compression principle specialized to edge constraints.

Thus the extremal structure is a complete graph on some $r$ vertices (possibly with isolated vertices), with all $m$ edges contained inside that clique. The number of $t$-cliques is then $\binom{r}{t}$, where $r$ is the largest integer satisfying

$$
\binom{r}{2} \le 10^6.
$$

We solve for $r$. Since

$$
\binom{1414}{2} = \frac{1414 \cdot 1413}{2},
$$

compute

$$
1414 \cdot 1413 = 1414(1400+13) = 1\,979\,600 + 18\,382 = 1\,997\,982,
$$

so

$$
\binom{1414}{2} = 998\,991 \le 10^6.
$$

For $r=1415$,

$$
1415 \cdot 1414 = 1414(1400+15) = 1\,979\,600 + 21\,210 = 2\,000\,810,
$$

hence

$$
\binom{1415}{2} = 1\,000\,405 > 10^6.
$$

Therefore $r=1414$.

With all $m$ edges placed inside $K_{1414}$, any $t$-clique must lie entirely within these $1414$ vertices, since any vertex outside has degree $0$ and cannot belong to a clique of size at least $2$. Hence the number of $t$-cliques is exactly $\binom{1414}{t}$ when $t \le 1414$, and $0$ otherwise.

No other distribution of the $10^6$ edges can produce more $t$-cliques: moving an edge from a smaller dense region into the largest existing clique increases the number of completed $t$-vertex subsets, since $\binom{x}{t}$ is convex in $x$ for integer $x \ge t$, and repeated compression yields a single clique as the unique maximizer.

The maximum number of $t$-cliques in a million-edge graph is therefore

$$
\boxed{\binom{1414}{t} \text{ for } t \le 1414,\quad 0 \text{ for } t \ge 1415.}
$$
