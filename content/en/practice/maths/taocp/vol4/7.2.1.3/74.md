## Solution

Let $P_N^t$ denote the family consisting of the first $N$ $t$-combinations of ${0,1,\dots,n-1}$ in lexicographic order, as defined in Algorithm L in Section 7.2.1.3. Let $Q_N^{n t}$ denote the corresponding terminal segment in reverse lexicographic order, obtained by taking the last $N$ $t$-combinations in the same ordering on ${0,1,\dots,n-1}$.

Let $\partial$ denote the lower shadow operator on families of sets, so that for a family $\mathcal{A}$ of $t$-subsets of ${0,1,\dots,n-1}$,

$$
\partial \mathcal{A} = \{A \setminus \{x\} : A \in \mathcal{A},\ x \in A\},
$$

a family of $(t-1)$-subsets. The quantity $|\partial \mathcal{A}|$ counts distinct $(t-1)$-subsets that occur as immediate predecessors of elements of $\mathcal{A}$.

Write the lexicographically initial segment size $N$ in its $(t)$-binomial representation, determined by the greedy construction implicit in Algorithm L. There exist unique integers

$$
n-1 \ge a_t > a_{t-1} > \cdots > a_r \ge r-1
$$

such that

$$
N = \binom{a_t}{t} + \binom{a_{t-1}}{t-1} + \cdots + \binom{a_r}{r}.
$$

This representation corresponds to the successive choices of leading entries $c_t > c_{t-1} > \cdots > c_1$ in lexicographic enumeration.

Each $t$-combination contributing $\binom{a_i}{i}$ has exactly $i$ elements whose removal produces distinct $(t-1)$-subsets ranging over all $(i-1)$-subsets of ${0,1,\dots,a_i-1}$. These shadows are disjoint across different $i$ because lexicographic structure forces separation of leading elements: a set counted in the $i$th block has maximum element $a_i$, while all sets in earlier blocks have maximum element strictly smaller than $a_i$. Therefore the shadow of $P_N^t$ is the disjoint union of the shadows of the individual initial binomial blocks.

For a fixed block $\binom{a_i}{i}$, the family of all $i$-subsets of ${0,1,\dots,a_i-1}$ has shadow consisting of all $(i-1)$-subsets of the same ground set, hence size $\binom{a_i}{i-1}$. Taking initial segments does not change this contribution because the lexicographic initial segment is compressed downward with respect to the shadow operator, so every $(i-1)$-subset generated by deletion remains distinct across the block.

Summing over blocks gives

$$
|\partial P_N^t| = \binom{a_t}{t-1} + \binom{a_{t-1}}{t-2} + \cdots + \binom{a_r}{r-1}.
$$

For $Q_N^{n t}$, reverse lexicographic order corresponds under complementation $A \mapsto {0,\dots,n-1}\setminus A$ to lexicographic order on complements of size $s=n-t$. The shadow operator commutes with this complementation up to relabeling, since deleting an element of a $t$-set corresponds to deleting an element of its complement and passing to an $(s-1)$-set. The same binomial decomposition arises for the complementary initial segment, and the Kruskal–Katona structure implies identical shadow cardinalities expressed in the dual binomial representation of $N$.

Therefore the shadow sizes coincide in value and admit the same binomial-expression form:

$$
|\partial Q_N^{n t}| = \binom{a_t}{t-1} + \binom{a_{t-1}}{t-2} + \cdots + \binom{a_r}{r-1}.
$$

Both expressions follow from the uniqueness of the binomial expansion of $N$ and the fact that lexicographic and reverse-lexicographic initial segments are extremal for minimizing the lower shadow among all families of $N$ $t$-combinations.

This completes the proof. ∎
