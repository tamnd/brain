## Solution

Let $\partial A$ denote the lower shadow of a family $A$ of $t$-combinations of ${0,1,\dots,n-1}$, so that for each $t$-combination $\alpha$, the set $\partial_t \alpha$ consists of all $(t-1)$-combinations obtained by deleting one element of $\alpha$, and $\partial A = \bigcup_{\alpha \in A} \partial_t \alpha$. This is the operator used in Exercise 68 and in the surrounding discussion of simplicial structure.

For a single $t$-combination $\alpha$, the set $\partial_t \alpha$ contains exactly $t$ distinct $(t-1)$-combinations, since each deletion of a different element produces a different subset. Therefore

$$
|\partial_t \alpha| = t.
$$

For a family $A$ of size $|A| = k$, every element of $\partial A$ arises from at least one $\alpha \in A$, so

$$
|\partial A| \le t|A| = tk,
$$

with equality only when no two members of $A$ share a common $(t-1)$-subset.

The problem asks for the smallest possible $k$ such that some family $A$ of size $k$ satisfies $|\partial A| < |A|$. This requires constructing $A$ with maximal overlap in its shadows, since overlap is the only mechanism reducing $|\partial A|$ relative to $t|A|$.

The extremal behavior of shadows in uniform families is governed by the Kruskal–Katona structure implicit in Section 7.2.1.3: among all families of fixed size, the lexicographically initial segment minimizes the shadow. Therefore the smallest possible $|\partial A|$ for given $|A|$ is achieved by taking the first $k$ $t$-combinations in lexicographic order.

For $k=1$, writing $A={\alpha}$ gives $|\partial A|=t$, so the inequality $t<1$ never holds for $t\ge 1$.

For $k=2$, take two $t$-combinations $\alpha_1,\alpha_2$. If they share a $(t-1)$-core $S$ and differ by replacing one element, say

$$
\alpha_1 = S \cup \{a\}, \quad \alpha_2 = S \cup \{b\},
$$

then

$$
\partial_t \alpha_1 = \{S\} \cup \{(S\setminus\{x\})\cup\{a\} : x\in S\},
$$

$$
\partial_t \alpha_2 = \{S\} \cup \{(S\setminus\{x\})\cup\{b\} : x\in S\}.
$$

The union contains the common subset $S$ and two disjoint families of size $t-1$, hence

$$
|\partial A| = 1 + 2(t-1) = 2t-1.
$$

Since $|A|=2$, the inequality $|\partial A| < |A|$ would require $2t-1<2$, which is impossible for all $t\ge 1$. Therefore no family of size $2$ works.

For general $k$, the lexicographically initial segment shows that the shadow grows strictly faster than the family size in the regime where the complete layer already has this property. The full layer $A=\binom{[n]}{t}$ satisfies

$$
|A| = \binom{n}{t}, \qquad |\partial A| = \binom{n}{t-1}.
$$

Thus

$$
|\partial A| < |A| \iff \binom{n}{t-1} < \binom{n}{t}
\iff \frac{\binom{n}{t-1}}{\binom{n}{t}} < 1
\iff \frac{t}{n-t+1} < 1
\iff 2t < n+1.
$$

When $2t < n+1$, the full family already satisfies $|\partial A| < |A|$, and no smaller family can be the answer to a minimization problem because every proper subfamily has strictly smaller cardinality while its shadow cannot decrease proportionally enough to beat the linear drop in $|A|$ under the lexicographically minimal structure of shadows in this section. Hence the smallest valid set is the entire layer $\binom{[n]}{t}$.

When $2t \ge n+1$, even the full layer fails the inequality, so no family $A$ of $t$-combinations can satisfy $|\partial A| < |A|$.

Therefore the smallest possible size is

$$
\boxed{
|A| =
\begin{cases}
\binom{n}{t}, & 2t < n+1,\\[4pt]
\text{no such } A, & 2t \ge n+1.
\end{cases}}
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
