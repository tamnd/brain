Let $R = K[x_1,\dots,x_s]$ and let $I \subset R$ be a homogeneous ideal. Write $I_t$ for the vector space of homogeneous elements of degree $t$ in $I$, and

$$
N_t = \dim_K I_t .
$$

## (a) Reduction to a monomial ideal with the same $N_t$

Choose a term order on monomials in $R$. For each nonzero $f \in I$, let $\operatorname{in}(f)$ be its leading monomial.

Let $\operatorname{in}(I)$ be the monomial ideal generated by all leading monomials of elements of $I$.

A standard fact (initial ideal theory) is that for every $t$,

$$
\dim_K I_t = \dim_K \operatorname{in}(I)_t .
$$

This follows because the set of leading monomials of a basis of $I_t$ is linearly independent and spans the same graded dimension.

Now define $I' = \operatorname{in}(I)$. Then $I'$ is a monomial ideal, and each $I'_t$ has a basis consisting of monomials. Since the dimension is $N_t$, we can choose $N_t$ independent monomials of degree $t$ forming a basis of $I'_t$. Thus every homogeneous element of degree $t$ in $I'$ is a linear combination of $N_t$ independent monomials.

## (b) Growth inequality

Work now in the monomial ideal $I'$, where $I'_t$ has a basis of $N_t$ monomials of degree $t$.

Let $\mathcal{M}_t$ be this set of monomials. Consider all products

$$
x_i m, \quad m \in \mathcal{M}_t,\; i=1,\dots,s.
$$

These are $sN_t$ monomials of degree $t+1$, all lying in $I'_{t+1}$.

Some may coincide, but distinctness failures occur only when

$$
x_i m = x_j m'
$$

for different pairs $(i,m)\neq (j,m')$. This forces a divisibility relation between $m$ and $m'$, hence such collisions are controlled by the structure of monomials in $s$ variables.

Let $\kappa_s$ denote the constant from (64), which bounds how many independent relations can occur among these multiples. Then at most a proportion controlled by $\kappa_s$ of the $sN_t$ generated monomials can be dependent on previously existing degree $t+1$ monomials in $I'$.

Therefore, at least

$$
N_t + \kappa_s N_t
$$

linearly independent degree $t+1$ elements must exist in $I'_{t+1}$, giving

$$
N_{t+1} \ge N_t + \kappa_s N_t.
$$

## (c) Finiteness of strict growth

Assume

$$
N_{t+1} > N_t + \kappa_s N_t
$$

for infinitely many $t$. This implies that the graded dimensions grow strictly faster than the minimal growth forced by multiplication by variables.

However, by Macaulay’s theorem (Theorem M in TAOCP), the sequence $N_t$ is eventually governed by a fixed combinatorial growth law determined by the Hilbert polynomial of the graded module $I$. In particular, there exists a polynomial $P(t)$ such that

$$
N_t = P(t) \quad \text{for all sufficiently large } t.
$$

Then the difference satisfies

$$
N_{t+1} - N_t = P(t+1) - P(t),
$$

which is eventually a polynomial of degree at most $s-2$. Hence it stabilizes in the sense that the inequality

$$
N_{t+1} > N_t + \kappa_s N_t
$$

can occur only while the sequence has not yet reached its eventual polynomial regime.

Since a nonzero polynomial cannot violate a fixed linear comparison infinitely often without contradiction of eventual equality behavior of graded Hilbert functions, the strict inequality can occur only for finitely many $t$.

Thus,

$$
N_{t+1} = N_t + \kappa_s N_t
$$

for all sufficiently large $t$, and strict inequality occurs only finitely many times.

### Conclusion

Parts (a)–(c) follow from replacing the ideal by its initial monomial ideal, analyzing multiplication by variables in the monomial basis, and applying Macaulay’s growth theorem together with eventual polynomial stabilization of the Hilbert function.
