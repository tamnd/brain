**Exercise 64.** [*25*] [25] (*Magic Fifteen.*) Two players alternately select digits from 1 to 9, using no digit twice; the winner, if any, is the first to get three digits that sum to 15. What's a good strategy for playing this game?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m15s  

---

The key step is to make the reduction to tic-tac-toe completely explicit and then use the known optimal play for that game.

We begin with the Lo Shu magic square

$$
\begin{matrix}
8 & 1 & 6 \\
3 & 5 & 7 \\
4 & 9 & 2
\end{matrix}
$$

which has the defining property that every row, column, and main diagonal consists of three distinct digits whose sum is $15$. A standard verification shows that these are exactly the eight winning triples for the “sum to 15” condition. Conversely, any three distinct digits from $1$ to $9$ that sum to $15$ must correspond to one of these eight lines in the square. Thus there is a bijection between winning triples in the original game and straight lines in the $3\times 3$ grid.

Now reinterpret the game as follows. Each digit corresponds to a cell of a $3\times 3$ tic-tac-toe board via the magic square placement. A move consists of claiming an unclaimed cell. A player wins exactly when they claim three cells forming a line in this grid. Therefore the game is exactly tic-tac-toe under a relabeling of the cells.

Once this equivalence is established, the problem reduces to giving optimal play for tic-tac-toe. The structure of optimal play in tic-tac-toe is well known and can be derived by systematic case analysis using symmetry.

The center cell is the most valuable because it participates in four winning lines, whereas a corner participates in three and an edge in only two. Therefore the first player’s optimal opening move is to take the center. After this, the correct general principle is to always block any immediate threat and to prefer creating multiple simultaneous threats, that is, forks.

With perfect play from both sides, tic-tac-toe is a draw. The second player can always respond to prevent the first player from creating a fork, and every attempt by either player to create a forced win can be countered. Therefore, although the first player has the advantage of tempo and should take the center to maximize options, neither side can force a win.

Translating back to the original game, a good strategy is simply to play tic-tac-toe optimally on the magic square labeling: take the center on the first move, respond to immediate winning threats, block forks whenever they arise, and otherwise prefer moves that create the most potential winning lines, namely corners over edges. Under perfect play, the result is a draw, meaning neither player can force three digits summing to $15$, but optimal play ensures you never lose and maximizes your chance of creating a fork if the opponent makes an error.
