[Section 7.2.2.2: Satisfiability](../)

**Exercise 188.** [*HM25*] [HM25] Analyze random 1SAT, the case $k = 1$: What are $S_{1,n}$ and $\hat{S}_{1,n}$?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m31s  

---

## Solution

For random $k$SAT, let $P_{k,n}$ denote the stopping time defined in the section, and let

$$
S_{k,n}=E P_{k,n}.
$$

The hatted quantities correspond to the model in which clauses are selected with replacement, whereas the unhatted quantities correspond to the model in which the possible clauses are selected without replacement. For $k=1$, the possible clauses are the $2n$ unit clauses

$$
1,\bar{1},2,\bar{2},\ldots,n,\bar{n}.
$$

A formula becomes unsatisfiable exactly when both literals of some variable have appeared. Therefore $P_{1,n}>m$ precisely when the first $m$ selected clauses contain no complementary pair.

For the model without replacement, the first $m$ clauses form a uniformly chosen $m$-subset of the $2n$ literals. A conflict-free subset is obtained by choosing the $m$ variables that occur and then choosing one of the two signs for each chosen variable. Hence the number of conflict-free subsets is

$$
\binom{n}{m}2^m,
$$

while the total number of possible subsets is

$$
\binom{2n}{m}.
$$

Therefore, for $0\le m\le n$,

$$
q_m=\Pr(P_{1,n}>m)=\frac{\binom{n}{m}2^m}{\binom{2n}{m}}.
$$

For $m>n$, the pigeonhole principle forces a complementary pair, so $q_m=0$. Since the expectation of a positive integer-valued random variable satisfies

$$
E P_{1,n}=\sum_{m\ge0}\Pr(P_{1,n}>m),
$$

we obtain

$$
S_{1,n}
=
\sum_{m=0}^{n}
\frac{\binom{n}{m}2^m}{\binom{2n}{m}}.
$$

Thus

$$
\boxed{
S_{1,n}
=
\sum_{m=0}^{n}
\frac{2^m\binom{n}{m}}{\binom{2n}{m}}
}
$$

For the model with replacement, the first $m$ clauses are independent selections from the $2n$ possible literals. We count the sequences that contain no complementary pair.

Suppose exactly $r$ variables occur among the first $m$ clauses. The variables can be chosen in

$$
\binom nr
$$

ways. For each chosen variable, one sign is selected, giving

$$
2^r
$$

possible sign choices. The $m$ positions must then be filled with these $r$ chosen literals, and every chosen literal must occur at least once. The number of such surjective assignments is

$$
\sum_{j=0}^{r}(-1)^j\binom rj(r-j)^m
$$

by inclusion-exclusion on the missing literals.

Hence the number of conflict-free length-$m$ sequences is

$$
\sum_{r=0}^{\min(n,m)}
\binom nr 2^r
\sum_{j=0}^{r}(-1)^j\binom rj(r-j)^m .
$$

The total number of length-$m$ sequences of unit clauses is

$$
(2n)^m.
$$

Therefore

$$
\hat q_m
=
\Pr(\hat P_{1,n}>m)
=
\frac1{(2n)^m}
\sum_{r=0}^{\min(n,m)}
\binom nr 2^r
\sum_{j=0}^{r}(-1)^j\binom rj(r-j)^m .
$$

Using the same tail-sum identity for expectations gives

$$
\hat S_{1,n}
=
\sum_{m\ge0}\hat q_m,
$$

and therefore

$$
\boxed{
\hat S_{1,n}
=
\sum_{m\ge0}
\frac1{(2n)^m}
\sum_{r=0}^{\min(n,m)}
\binom nr 2^r
\sum_{j=0}^{r}(-1)^j\binom rj(r-j)^m
}
$$

The unhatted expression terminates at $m=n$ because no conflict-free set can contain more than one literal from each of the $n$ variables. The hatted expression has infinitely many terms because repeated selection allows arbitrarily long conflict-free prefixes.

This completes the proof.

∎
