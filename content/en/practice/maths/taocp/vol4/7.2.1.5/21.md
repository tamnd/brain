**Exercise 21.** [*M27*] [M27] How many partitions of $\{1, \ldots, n\}$ are self-conjugate?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 5m10s  

---

## Solution

The factor $\frac{1}{m!}$ is irrelevant, so it is removed from both sides. The inequality becomes

$$
\sum_{p} x_{p_1}^{a_1}\cdots x_{p_m}^{a_m}
\;\ge\;
\sum_{p} x_{p_1}^{b_1}\cdots x_{p_m}^{b_m},
$$

where the sum ranges over all permutations $p$ of ${1,\ldots,m}$.

For a vector $x=(x_1,\ldots,x_m)$ with nonnegative components, assume without loss of generality that

$$
x_1 \ge x_2 \ge \cdots \ge x_m \ge 0.
$$

### Sufficiency: $\alpha \succeq \beta \Rightarrow$ inequality

The relation $\alpha \succeq \beta$ is generated by a sequence of covering moves of the form

$$
(\ldots,a_i,\ldots,a_j,\ldots)\;\to\;(\ldots,a_i-1,\ldots,a_j+1,\ldots)
\quad (i<j,\ a_i>a_j).
$$

It suffices to prove the inequality for one such move, since transitivity preserves the result.

Fix such a move and write $\alpha'$ for the modified exponent vector. The difference between the two symmetric sums decomposes into contributions over pairs of permutations that differ only by exchanging the images of positions $i$ and $j$.

For any fixed assignment of values to all positions except $i,j$, the relevant terms reduce to

$$
x_u^{a_i}x_v^{a_j} + x_u^{a_i-1}x_v^{a_j+1}
$$

on one side and

$$
x_u^{a_i-1}x_v^{a_j+1} + x_u^{a_i}x_v^{a_j}
$$

distributed over permutations, so it suffices to compare the two monomials under consistent placement of $u,v$.

Since $x_1 \ge \cdots \ge x_m$, every occurrence splits into cases with $u \ge v$. In that case,

$$
x_u^{a_i}x_v^{a_j} \ge x_u^{a_i-1}x_v^{a_j+1}
$$

because

$$
\frac{x_u^{a_i}x_v^{a_j}}{x_u^{a_i-1}x_v^{a_j+1}} = \frac{x_u}{x_v} \ge 1.
$$

Each paired comparison is therefore nonnegative, and summing over all permutation classes preserves the inequality.

Any chain of covering steps preserves the inequality, so the result holds whenever $\alpha \succeq \beta$.

### Necessity: inequality for all $x$ implies $\alpha \succeq \beta$

Assume the inequality holds for all nonnegative $x$. Let $a^{\downarrow}$ and $b^{\downarrow}$ denote the sequences $(a_i)$ and $(b_i)$ sorted in nonincreasing order.

For $t>1$, define

$$
x_1=\cdots=x_k=t,\quad x_{k+1}=\cdots=x_m=1.
$$

For any exponent vector $\gamma=(\gamma_1,\ldots,\gamma_m)$, each term $x_{p_1}^{\gamma_1}\cdots x_{p_m}^{\gamma_m}$ contributes a power of $t$ equal to the sum of those $\gamma_i$ assigned to the first $k$ positions. The largest exponent of $t$ occurring in the symmetric sum equals

$$
\max_{S \subseteq \{1,\ldots,m\},\,|S|=k} \sum_{i\in S} \gamma_i,
$$

which equals the sum of the $k$ largest parts of $\gamma$.

Hence the left-hand side grows like $t^{A_k}$ and the right-hand side like $t^{B_k}$, where

$$
A_k = a_1^{\downarrow} + \cdots + a_k^{\downarrow}, \quad
B_k = b_1^{\downarrow} + \cdots + b_k^{\downarrow}.
$$

If the inequality holds for all $t$, then for each $k$ the exponent must satisfy

$$
A_k \ge B_k.
$$

Both $\alpha$ and $\beta$ have total sum $n$, so equality holds at $k=m$. The inequalities

$$
a_1^{\downarrow}+\cdots+a_k^{\downarrow}
\ge
b_1^{\downarrow}+\cdots+b_k^{\downarrow}
\quad (1\le k \le m)
$$

characterize majorization, hence $\alpha \succeq \beta$.

This completes the proof. ∎
