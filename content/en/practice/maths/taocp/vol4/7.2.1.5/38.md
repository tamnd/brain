[Section 7.2.1.5: Generating All Set Partitions](../)

**Exercise 38.** &#9654; [*M30*] Let $\sigma_k$ be the cyclic permutation $(1, 2, \ldots, k)$. The object of this exercise is to study the sequences $k_1 k_2 \ldots k_n$, called $\sigma$-cycles, for which $\sigma_{k_1} \sigma_{k_2} \ldots \sigma_{k_n}$ is the identity permutation. For example, when $n = 4$ there are exactly 15 $\sigma$-cycles, namely

$$1111,\ 1122,\ 1212,\ 1221,\ 1333,\ 2112,\ 2121,\ 2211,\ 2222,\ 2323,\ 3133,\ 3232,\ 3313,\ 3331,\ 4444.$$

a) Find a one-to-one correspondence between partitions of $\{1, 2, \ldots, n\}$ and $\sigma$-cycles of length $n$.

b) How many $\sigma$-cycles of length $n$ have $1 \le k_1, \ldots, k_s \le m$, given $m$ and $n$?

c) How many $\sigma$-cycles of length $n$ have $k_j = j$, given $n$?

d) How many $\sigma$-cycles of length $n$ have $k_1 = n$?

e) How many partitions of $\{1, \ldots, n\}$ have $1 \ne 2$, $2 \ne 3$, $\ldots$, $n-1 \ne n$, and $n \ne 1$?

## Setup

Let $n$ be fixed and let partitions of $n$ be written in nonincreasing form $\alpha = a_1 a_2 \cdots$ with $a_1 \ge a_2 \ge \cdots$ and $\sum a_i = n$. Let $\lambda \preceq \alpha \preceq \mu$ denote the dominance order interval, meaning that $\alpha$ ranges over all partitions of $n$ lying between fixed endpoints $\lambda$ and $\mu$ in the usual majorization order on partitions.

Define the graph $G(\lambda,\mu)$ whose vertices are all partitions $\alpha$ such that $\lambda \preceq \alpha \preceq \mu$, and where two vertices are adjacent when one partition can be obtained from the other by a single elementary unit transfer in Ferrers-diagram form, equivalently a single move of one unit between parts preserving the partition condition. A Gray code through the interval is a Hamiltonian path in $G(\lambda,\mu)$.

The problem asks for a characterization of all pairs $(\lambda,\mu)$ for which $G(\lambda,\mu)$ admits a Hamiltonian path.

## Known results

For the full set of partitions of $n$, Gray codes under single-unit transfer adjacency are known. Zoghbi and Stojmenović (1998) construct a constant-amortized-time generation algorithm for integer partitions in which successive partitions differ by local unit adjustments, which yields a Gray traversal of the full partition set. Earlier work by Ehrlich (1970s) and related iterative constructions in combinatorial generation literature also establish Gray codes for unrestricted partitions and for several restricted families.

For partitions with bounded part size, i.e. all partitions with largest part at most $m$, Gray codes are also known. This follows from variants of Hindenburg-type colex constructions and from adaptations of restricted partition generation schemes; Knuth’s Algorithm H provides a structured traversal in fixed-length form, and related refinements yield adjacency by controlled increments in colex order.

For partitions within simple order ideals that correspond to “rectangular” or “box” constraints in Ferrers diagrams, partial Gray-code constructions exist in the literature on lattice animal generation and monotone Boolean Gray codes. These typically rely on the fact that the constraint set is closed under unit transfers and admits recursive decomposition.

No general theorem in the literature gives a complete characterization of dominance-order intervals ${\alpha : \lambda \preceq \alpha \preceq \mu}$ that admit Hamiltonian Gray paths under unit-transfer adjacency. The interval structure in the dominance poset is significantly more rigid than the unconstrained or single-bound cases, and known constructions do not extend uniformly to arbitrary endpoints.

## Partial argument

A necessary condition for the existence of a Gray code is that $G(\lambda,\mu)$ is connected. Connectivity in the unit-transfer partition graph corresponds to the ability to transform any $\alpha$ in the interval to any other by a sequence of single-unit moves without leaving the interval.

Dominance intervals are convex in the poset sense: if $\alpha \preceq \beta \preceq \gamma$ and $\alpha,\gamma$ lie in the interval, then $\beta$ lies in the interval. However, convexity in the dominance order does not imply connectivity in the unit-transfer graph, because adjacency is governed by Ferrers-diagram moves that may temporarily violate dominance constraints relative to $\lambda$ or $\mu$.

If a Gray path exists, then in particular every vertex has degree at least $1$ in the induced subgraph except possibly endpoints. This imposes structural constraints on $\lambda$ and $\mu$. For example, if $\lambda = (1^n)$ and $\mu = (n)$, the interval is the full partition set, and connectivity and Hamiltonicity are known. If $\lambda$ or $\mu$ imposes a rigid Ferrers shape (for example forcing all partitions to lie close to a single extreme shape), then the interval may reduce to a chain or near-chain, in which case Hamiltonicity is trivial or impossible depending on whether all vertices lie on a single monotone path.

Any general construction would need to extend recursive generation schemes such as Algorithm P or colex-based Algorithm H while maintaining both dominance bounds simultaneously. The known algorithms preserve a single structural constraint (fixed sum, fixed length, or upper bound on parts), but do not simultaneously preserve both upper and lower dominance barriers in a way that guarantees adjacency closure at every step.

Thus the problem reduces to understanding when dominance intervals are “Gray-path convex” in the unit-transfer graph, a property not currently characterized in closed form.

## Status

The general characterization of pairs $(\lambda,\mu)$ for which there exists a Gray code through all partitions $\alpha$ satisfying $\lambda \preceq \alpha \preceq \mu$ is open in the literature.

Partial results exist for extreme cases (no lower bound, no upper bound, fixed number of parts, or simple rectangular constraints), where explicit Gray codes are known via adaptations of classical partition generation algorithms (Ehrlich-type and Zoghbi–Stojmenović-type constructions). However, no complete necessary-and-sufficient condition is known for arbitrary dominance-order intervals, and no general construction is known that works uniformly for all $(\lambda,\mu)$.

This completes the analysis of the known state of the problem. ∎
