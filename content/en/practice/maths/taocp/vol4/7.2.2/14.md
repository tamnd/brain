**Exercise 14.** [**] [M25] If exercise 12 has $T(n)$ toroidal solutions, show that $Q(mn) \ge Q(m)^2 T(n)$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 4m09s  

---

Let $x_1,\dots,x_m$ and $y_1,\dots,y_m$ be two solutions of the $m$-queens problem. Thus each is a permutation of ${1,\dots,m}$, and in the solution indexed by $x$ (respectively $y$) there is exactly one queen in each row and column, with the usual non-attacking diagonal condition.

Let $S \subseteq {1,\dots,n}^2$ be a fixed toroidal solution of size $n$. Thus $|S|=n$, and for any two distinct $(i,j),(i',j')\in S$ we have both

$$
i-j \not\equiv i'-j' \pmod n, \qquad i+j \not\equiv i'+j' \pmod n.
$$

We partition the $mn\times mn$ board into $m^2$ blocks of size $n\times n$. For $a,b\in{1,\dots,m}$, denote by $B_{a,b}$ the block consisting of rows $(a-1)n+1,\dots,an$ and columns $(b-1)n+1,\dots,bn$.

We now define a placement of queens. For each $k\in{1,\dots,m}$, place a translated copy of $S$ inside the block $B_{x_k,y_k}$, mapping each $(i,j)\in S$ to the global square

$$
((x_k-1)n+i,\ (y_k-1)n+j).
$$

No other blocks contain queens. Since $x$ and $y$ are permutations, the blocks $B_{x_k,y_k}$ are all distinct in both coordinates, so we obtain exactly $m$ disjoint copies of $S$, hence a total of $mn$ queens.

We verify the non-attacking conditions.

### Row and column conditions

Fix a row of the global board. It lies in a unique block-row $a$. Since $x_1,\dots,x_m$ is a permutation, there is exactly one $k$ with $x_k=a$, hence exactly one occupied block in this block-row. Inside that block, each row contains exactly one point of $S$, so the global row contains exactly one queen. The same argument with $y_1,\dots,y_m$ shows each column contains exactly one queen.

### Diagonal conditions within a block

Inside a fixed block $B_{x_k,y_k}$, two queens come from the same translated copy of $S$. Since $S$ is a toroidal solution, it has no repeated values of $i-j$ or $i+j$ modulo $n$, hence in particular no two queens in the same block attack each other diagonally in the ordinary sense. Thus there are no diagonal conflicts within a single block.

### Diagonal conditions across different blocks

Let two queens lie in distinct blocks $B_{x_k,y_k}$ and $B_{x_\ell,y_\ell}$ with $k\ne \ell$. Write their coordinates as

$$
P = ((x_k-1)n+i,\ (y_k-1)n+j), \qquad
Q = ((x_\ell-1)n+i',\ (y_\ell-1)n+j'),
$$

with $(i,j),(i',j')\in S$.

We first consider the major diagonal condition. Assume for contradiction that $P$ and $Q$ lie on the same major diagonal, so

$$
(x_k-1)n+i-(y_k-1)n-j = (x_\ell-1)n+i'-(y_\ell-1)n-j'.
$$

Rearranging gives

$$
(x_k-y_k-x_\ell+y_\ell)n = (i'-j')-(i-j).
$$

The left-hand side is a multiple of $n$. The right-hand side satisfies

$$
-(n-1) \le (i'-j')-(i-j) \le n-1,
$$

since $i,i',j,j'\in{1,\dots,n}$. The only multiple of $n$ in this range is $0$, hence both sides must be $0$. Therefore

$$
x_k-y_k = x_\ell-y_\ell, \qquad i-j = i'-j'.
$$

The second equality implies that two distinct elements of $S$ have the same value of $i-j$ as integers, contradicting the toroidal condition (which in particular implies distinctness modulo $n$ and hence distinctness in ${1,\dots,n}$).

The same argument applies to the minor diagonal condition

$$
(x_k-1)n+i+(y_k-1)n+j = (x_\ell-1)n+i'+(y_\ell-1)n+j',
$$

which leads to

$$
(x_k+y_k-x_\ell-y_\ell)n = (i'+j')-(i+j).
$$

Again the right-hand side lies in $[-(2n-2),2n-2]$, forcing it to be $0$, hence $i+j=i'+j'$, contradicting the toroidal property of $S$.

Thus no two queens in different blocks attack each other diagonally.

### Counting

The construction depends on:

- a choice of two $m$-queens solutions, giving $Q(m)^2$ possibilities,
- a choice of a toroidal $n$-queens solution, giving $T(n)$ possibilities.

Each such choice produces a distinct $mn$-queens solution by the above construction. Therefore

$$
Q(mn) \ge Q(m)^2 T(n).
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
