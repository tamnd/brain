[Section 7.2.2: Backtracking](../)

**Exercise 9.** [**] [21] Can a $4n$-queen placement have $4n$ queens on "white" squares?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 6m25s  

---

We restart from the correct structural observation and complete the argument without introducing any invalid independence claims.

Let the board be a $4n \times 4n$ chessboard, colored in the usual way so that a square $(i,j)$ is white if and only if $i+j$ is even.

Assume, for contradiction, that there is a nonattacking placement of $4n$ queens, one in each row, and that every queen lies on a white square.

### Step 1: parity structure of the placement

Let the queen in row $i$ be in column $x_i$. Since all queens are on white squares, we have

$$
i + x_i \equiv 0 \pmod 2,
$$

so

$$
x_i \equiv i \pmod 2.
$$

Thus even rows contain queens only in even columns, and odd rows only in odd columns.

### Step 2: decomposition into two independent subboards

Define the even index sets

$$
E = \{2,4,\dots,4n\}, \quad O = \{1,3,\dots,4n-1\}.
$$

Because $x_i \equiv i \pmod 2$, the restriction of the placement gives bijections

$$
x(E) = E, \quad x(O) = O.
$$

Now define two induced configurations:

For $i=2a \in E$, define a placement on a $2n \times 2n$ board by

$$
( a,\, y_a ), \quad \text{where } y_a = \frac{x_{2a}}{2}.
$$

For $i=2a-1 \in O$, define

$$
( a,\, z_a ), \quad \text{where } z_a = \frac{x_{2a-1}+1}{2}.
$$

Each of $y$ and $z$ is a permutation of ${1,\dots,2n}$, and the nonattacking condition is preserved within each class because scaling by $2$ (and shifting by $1$) preserves equalities of the form

$$
|x_i - x_j| = |i - j|.
$$

Hence each parity class gives a valid nonattacking $(2n)$-queen placement.

### Step 3: preservation of the “all-white” property under reduction

Crucially, the color condition is preserved under this reduction.

In the even class, squares are $(2a,2y_a)$, so their parity condition becomes

$$
2a + 2y_a \equiv 0 \pmod 2 \quad \Rightarrow \quad a + y_a \equiv 0 \pmod 2.
$$

Similarly, in the odd class, squares are $(2a-1,2z_a-1)$, and again

$$
(2a-1)+(2z_a-1)=2(a+z_a-1) \equiv 0 \pmod 2,
$$

so

$$
a + z_a \equiv 0 \pmod 2.
$$

Thus each induced $(2n)\times(2n)$ queen placement also lies entirely on white squares of its smaller board (with the induced coloring).

So the same hypothesis is preserved at size $2n$.

### Step 4: iteration

We may repeat the same construction.

Starting from a $4n \times 4n$ board, we obtain two independent $(2n)\times(2n)$ placements, each consisting only of white squares. Reapplying the same argument to each of these yields four independent $(n)\times(n)$ placements, and continuing iteratively, after $k$ steps we obtain $2^k$ independent nonattacking queen placements on $(4n)/2^k \times (4n)/2^k$ boards, each consisting only of white squares.

Choose $k$ such that $(4n)/2^k = 2$. This is possible since $4n$ is divisible by $4$. We arrive at a nonattacking placement of $2$ queens on a $2 \times 2$ board, with both queens on white squares.

### Step 5: final contradiction

The $2 \times 2$ chessboard has only two white squares, namely $(1,1)$ and $(2,2)$. However, these two squares lie on the same diagonal, so two queens placed on them attack each other.

Hence it is impossible to place $2$ nonattacking queens on a $2 \times 2$ board at all, let alone on white squares.

This contradicts the conclusion of the iterative reduction.

### Conclusion

The original assumption is false. Therefore, no nonattacking placement of $4n$ queens on a $4n \times 4n$ board can have all $4n$ queens on white squares.

$$
\boxed{\text{No, such a placement is impossible.}}
$$
