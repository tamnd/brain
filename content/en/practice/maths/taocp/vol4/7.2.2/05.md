**Exercise 5.** [**] [20] Reformulate Algorithm B as a *recursive* procedure called $\textit{try}(l)$, having global variables $n$ and $x_1, \ldots x_n$, to be invoked by saying '$\textit{try}(1)$'. Can you imagine why the author of this book decided not to present the algorithm in such a recursive form?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m27s  

---

The recursive formulation introduces a procedure $\textit{try}(l)$ with global variables $n$ and $x_1,\ldots,x_n$. When invoked as $\textit{try}(1)$, it operates as follows. If $l = n + 1$, the sequence $x_1 x_2 \ldots x_n$ is visited. Otherwise, for each $t \in D_l$ in turn, the condition $P_l(x_1,\ldots,x_{l-1}, t)$ is tested; if it holds, then $x_l \leftarrow t$ is performed and $\textit{try}(l+1)$ is called. After returning from the recursive call, control proceeds to the next candidate value of $t$, so the next successful assignment to $x_l$ overwrites the previous one.

The absence of explicit recursion in the book reflects the emphasis on an iterative control structure that mirrors a machine-level backtrack mechanism. The stack of active levels is represented implicitly by the variable $l$, avoiding procedure-call overhead and fixed recursion depth mechanisms. This form also makes the flow of assignments and backtracking steps explicit in terms of the Algorithm B states, which is important for analyzing resource usage and for implementation on systems such as MIX where call-return conventions would obscure the underlying state transitions. ∎
