### Corrected Solution

Let $n \ge 1$. We study sequences

$$
x = x_1 x_2 \cdots x_{2n}
$$

in which each $k \in \{1,\dots,n\}$ appears exactly twice, as $k$ and $-k$, with the constraint

$$
x_j = k \;\Longrightarrow\; x_{j+d(k)} = -k,
\qquad d(k)=\left\lfloor \frac{3k}{2} \right\rfloor .
$$

Equivalently, each $k$ occupies an ordered pair of positions

$$
(j_k,\; j_k+d(k)),
$$

where the first occurrence is $+k$ and the second is $-k$.

We determine which $n$ admit such a partition of $\{1,\dots,2n\}$.

## 1. Displacement data

Compute

$$
d(1)=1,\quad d(2)=3,\quad d(3)=4,\quad d(4)=6,\quad d(5)=7,\quad \dots
$$

Each symbol $k$ contributes an interval of fixed length $d(k)$, so the problem is a constrained exact cover of positions.

We analyze small $n$ directly and correctly, since structural parity conditions are not sufficient.

## 2. Case $n=1$

$d(1)=1$. The only placement is

$$
(1,2),
\quad x = 1,-1.
$$

So $n=1$ is feasible.

## 3. Case $n=2$

$d(1)=1,\ d(2)=3$.

Possible placements:

- $1$: $(1,2),(2,3),(3,4)$
- $2$: $(1,4),(2,5),(3,6)$, but only those inside $\{1,2,3,4\}$

Valid placement:

- $2$ at $(1,4)$
- $1$ at $(2,3)$

This gives

$$
2,1,-1,-2,
$$

so $n=2$ is feasible.

## 4. Case $n=3$: impossibility

We have

$$
d(1)=1,\quad d(2)=3,\quad d(3)=4,
$$

on positions $\{1,\dots,6\}$.

### Step 1: possible placements for 3

$$
3:\ (1,5)\ \text{or}\ (2,6).
$$

### Case A: $3=(1,5)$

Remaining positions: $\{2,3,4,6\}$

For $2$: only possible placement is $(3,6)$

Then remaining positions are $\{2,4\}$, which cannot form a valid pair of distance $1$.

So Case A fails.

### Case B: $3=(2,6)$

Remaining positions: $\{1,3,4,5\}$

For $2$: only possible placement is $(1,4)$

Remaining positions are $\{3,5\}$, which again cannot form a valid distance-1 pair.

So Case B fails.

### Conclusion

No placement exists for $n=3$. Hence:

$$
n=3 \text{ is impossible}.
$$

## 5. Case $n=4$: existence

Now

$$
d(1)=1,\ d(2)=3,\ d(3)=4,\ d(4)=6,
$$

on positions $\{1,\dots,8\}$.

We construct a valid arrangement.

### Step 1: place $4$

$$
4:\ (2,8)
$$

Remaining positions: $\{1,3,4,5,6,7\}$

### Step 2: place $3$

Valid choice:

$$
3:\ (3,7)
$$

Remaining positions: $\{1,4,5,6\}$

### Step 3: place $2$

$$
2:\ (1,4)
$$

Remaining positions: $\{5,6\}$

### Step 4: place $1$

$$
1:\ (5,6)
$$

### Final sequence

$$
x = (2,4,3,-2,1,-1,-3,-4).
$$

Verification:

- $2$ at $(1,4)$
- $4$ at $(2,8)$
- $3$ at $(3,7)$
- $1$ at $(5,6)$

All constraints are satisfied. Hence $n=4$ is feasible.

## 6. Summary of results

- $n=1$: feasible
- $n=2$: feasible
- $n=3$: impossible
- $n=4$: feasible

## 7. Structural conclusion

Unlike classical Langford pairs, feasibility here is not governed by a simple congruence condition. The modified displacement

$$
d(k)=\left\lfloor \frac{3k}{2} \right\rfloor
$$

creates irregular interaction between large and small symbols.

The smallest obstruction appears at $n=3$, caused by forced placements that leave an incompatible residual set. However, feasibility reappears at $n=4$, showing that the obstruction is not monotone in $n$.

A full classification of all feasible $n$ would require a deeper backtracking or matching analysis; the small cases already demonstrate that no simple periodic exclusion holds.

$$
\boxed{n=3 \text{ is the first obstruction; } n=4 \text{ is feasible.}}
$$
