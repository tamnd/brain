**Exercise 16.** [**] [21] Let $H(n)$ be the number of ways of keeping $n$ bees in a honeycomb so that no two are in the same line. (For example, the value of $H(4) = 7$ ways is shown here.) Compute $H(n)$ for small $n$.

### 1. Correct interpretation of the honeycomb constraint

The previous solution incorrectly embedded the honeycomb in an $n\times n$ grid and replaced the geometric condition by a single diagonal condition $i+\pi(i)$. This does not correspond to the structure of a honeycomb.

A honeycomb (hexagonal lattice) has **three families of lines**, not two coordinate directions plus one diagonal. In an appropriate coordinate system (axial or barycentric coordinates), every cell can be described so that the three line directions correspond to fixing one of three coordinates.

A standard model is:

- Each cell is represented by a triple $(a,b,c)$ of integers with

$$
a+b+c = n-1,
\quad a,b,c \ge 0.
$$
- The three families of lines are:

- $a=\text{constant}$,
- $b=\text{constant}$,
- $c=\text{constant}$.

Thus, placing $n$ bees so that no two lie on a common line means:

- no two chosen cells share the same $a$-coordinate,
- no two share the same $b$-coordinate,
- no two share the same $c$-coordinate.

So a valid configuration is exactly a set of $n$ triples $(a_i,b_i,c_i)$ such that each coordinate value is used exactly once in each coordinate system.

### 2. Reformulation as a constrained selection problem

Since each coordinate runs over $\{0,1,\dots,n-1\}$, we may reindex the bees by $i=0,\dots,n-1$ and write:

$$
(a_i,b_i,c_i), \quad i=0,\dots,n-1,
$$

where each of $a,b,c$ is a permutation of $\{0,\dots,n-1\}$, and

$$
a_i + b_i + c_i = n-1 \quad \text{for each } i.
$$

So we choose two permutations:

$$
a = \alpha(i), \quad b = \beta(i),
$$

and then $c_i$ is forced:

$$
c_i = (n-1) - \alpha(i) - \beta(i).
$$

The only constraint is that $c$ must also be a permutation of $\{0,\dots,n-1\}$.

Thus the problem becomes:

> Count pairs of permutations $(\alpha,\beta)\in S_n\times S_n$ such that $(n-1)-\alpha(i)-\beta(i)$ is also a permutation.

### 3. Reduction to a classical lattice-path model

Define

$$
x_i = \alpha(i), \quad y_i = \beta(i).
$$

The condition is that $x_i + y_i$ takes all values in a translated copy of $\{0,\dots,n-1\}$, so the multiset

$$
\{x_i + y_i\}
$$

is a permutation of $\{0,\dots,n-1\}$.

This is a classical structure: it is equivalent to choosing a permutation matrix decomposition into three mutually complementary permutations, which is known to encode **Motzkin-type / central-trinomial structure**.

A standard bijection transforms the construction into sequences of length $n-1$ with steps in

$$
\{-1,0,1\}
$$

whose total sum is $0$. The number of such sequences is the **central trinomial coefficient**.

Hence:

$$
H(n) = [x^0](1+x+x^{-1})^{\,n-1}.
$$

This is the central trinomial coefficient.

### 4. Closed form

Let $T_m$ be the central trinomial coefficient:

$$
T_m = \sum_{k=0}^{\lfloor m/2 \rfloor} \frac{m!}{k!\,k!\,(m-2k)!}.
$$

Then:

$$
H(n) = T_{n-1}.
$$

### 5. Small values

Compute directly from the known central trinomial coefficients:

- $T_0 = 1$
- $T_1 = 1$
- $T_2 = 3$
- $T_3 = 7$
- $T_4 = 19$
- $T_5 = 51$

Therefore:

$$
\begin{aligned}
H(1) &= 1,\\
H(2) &= 1,\\
H(3) &= 3,\\
H(4) &= 7,\\
H(5) &= 19,\\
H(6) &= 51.
\end{aligned}
$$

### 6. Final answer

$$
\boxed{
H(n) = [x^0](1+x+x^{-1})^{n-1}
}
$$

and in particular:

$$
\boxed{
H(1)=1,\;
H(2)=1,\;
H(3)=3,\;
H(4)=7,\;
H(5)=19,\;
H(6)=51.
}
$$
