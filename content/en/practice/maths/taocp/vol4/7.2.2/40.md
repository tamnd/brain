**Exercise 40.** &#9654; [*15*] $[15]$ Why do you think sequential data structures such as (16)–(23) weren't featured in Section 2.2.2 of this series of books (entitled "Sequential Allocation")?

## Solution

Section 2.2.2 (“Sequential Allocation”) is concerned with representing linear or quasi-linear collections in contiguous memory, together with the elementary operations that such representations support efficiently, such as indexing, sequential traversal, and simple insertion or deletion at the end of a structure. The emphasis is on static storage discipline and on the tradeoff between contiguity and flexibility in growth.

The structures numbered (16)–(23) are introduced in a different context, namely as auxiliary mechanisms for controlling combinatorial search, rather than as primary storage allocation schemes. In backtracking, the central requirement is not merely to store a sequence of items, but to maintain a changing partial solution $x_1, \ldots, x_l$ together with rapidly testable constraints $P_l$, and to support frequent advancement and reversal of decisions.

Sequential allocation models are poorly matched to this regime because the dominant operations are paired updates: an assignment at level $l$ must be undone exactly when the algorithm returns to level $l-1$. This imposes a strict LIFO discipline on modifications of state, which is naturally represented by an implicit or explicit stack rather than by static sequential allocation rules. The relevant data structures therefore behave as snapshots of evolving state rather than as permanently allocated linear records.

The same structures (16)–(23), even when they include arrays, linked lists, or stack-like mechanisms, are used in backtracking primarily as dynamic control instruments for maintaining candidate sets, conflict tables, or undoable updates. Their correctness depends on invariants tied to the search tree, not on properties of memory layout. Consequently, their essential feature is reversibility of updates rather than sequential placement in memory.

For this reason, Section 2.2.2 does not treat these structures as part of sequential allocation theory. The theory there is about how objects occupy storage over time in a linear memory model, while the structures used in backtracking are defined by how information is transformed during traversal of a combinatorial tree. The same physical representations appear, but their role is algorithmic rather than allocative, so they are developed later in the context where the governing structure is the search process itself.
