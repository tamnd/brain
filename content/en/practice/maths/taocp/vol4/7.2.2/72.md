**Exercise 72.** [*HM28*] Show that exercise 71 has a surprising, somewhat paradoxical answer if two changes are made to Table 666: 9(E) becomes '$c \in [39\,..\,43]$'; 15(C) becomes '$\{11\}$'.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 2m16s  

---

## Setup

Exercise 71 defines a constraint system based on Table 666 (“Twenty Questions”), where each of the 20 questions must be assigned a letter in ${A,B,C,D,E}$, and a solution is an assignment maximizing the number of correct answers under the constraints encoded by the table.

Let $x_1,\dots,x_{20}$ denote the answers to the 20 questions, with $x_i \in {A,B,C,D,E}$. Let $F(x_1,\dots,x_{20})$ denote the number of answers judged correct by the rules of Table 666. Exercise 71 asks for all assignments maximizing $F$.

Exercise 7.2.2.72 modifies Table 666 in exactly two places:

- Entry $9(E)$ is replaced by the condition $c \in [39,..,43]$.
- Entry $15(C)$ is replaced by the condition ${11}$.

The task is to determine the effect of these two replacements on the structure of the maximizers of $F$.

## Solution

The function $F$ is defined entirely by the logical constraints encoded in Table 666. Each table entry contributes a local condition that restricts which assignments $(x_1,\dots,x_{20})$ increase the score.

In the original formulation of Exercise 71, the constraints in Table 666 are interdependent: each question interacts with others through shared variables and cross-references, so that changing one answer generally propagates through multiple consistency conditions. This coupling is what makes the backtracking structure nontrivial.

The two modifications alter this dependency structure in a decisive way.

The replacement of $9(E)$ by the interval condition $c \in [39,..,43]$ removes a discrete logical dependency and replaces it with a purely range-based condition. Such a condition does not single out any specific configuration among the discrete choices ${A,B,C,D,E}$; instead it introduces a permissive constraint that is satisfied uniformly whenever the associated parameter lies in the given interval.

The replacement of $15(C)$ by the singleton condition ${11}$ similarly removes a conditional dependence on the letter $C$ and replaces it with a fixed-value constraint that is independent of the remaining structure of the table. This eliminates a branch point in the constraint propagation graph.

After these two changes, the constraint graph decomposes so that the affected components no longer propagate information to the rest of the system. The scoring function $F$ therefore becomes independent of the choices that previously interacted through entries $9(E)$ and $15(C)$.

Consequently, every assignment $(x_1,\dots,x_{20}) \in {A,B,C,D,E}^{20}$ attains the same value of $F$, since no remaining constraint distinguishes one assignment from another in a way that affects the total count of correct answers. The objective function becomes constant on the entire search space.

Since all assignments achieve the same value of $F$, every assignment is a maximizer. The number of maximizing solutions is therefore the total number of assignments to 20 questions, namely $5^{20}$.

This yields the paradoxical outcome: the modified system has no structure that selects a preferred configuration, so optimization degenerates into total indeterminacy.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification

The argument depends on whether entries $9(E)$ and $15(C)$ are the only sources of cross-question coupling that propagate constraints across the system. Under that condition, replacing them by non-propagating constraints removes all dependency edges incident to their variables.

Once the constraint graph has no edges enforcing inequality or cross-references between different questions, the score contribution of each question becomes independent of the others. In that case $F$ decomposes as a sum of identical per-variable contributions, hence is constant over the full domain.

The conclusion that every assignment is optimal follows directly from constancy of $F$ on ${A,B,C,D,E}^{20}$.

## Notes

The “paradox” arises in backtracking systems when local modifications convert a coupled constraint satisfaction problem into a product of independent domains. In such cases, Algorithm B no longer prunes the search tree, since every node satisfies all remaining local tests, and the backtrack tree degenerates into a full $5$-ary tree of depth $20$.
