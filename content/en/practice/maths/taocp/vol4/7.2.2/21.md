**Exercise 21.** &#9654; [*M25*] *[M25]* If $x = x_1 x_2 \ldots x_{2n}$, let $x^D = (-x_{2n}) \ldots (-x_2)(-x_1) = -x^R$ be its dual. a) Show that if $x$ is odd and $x$ solves Langford's problem $(n)$, we have $x_k = n$ for some $k \le \lfloor n/2 \rfloor$ if and only if $x_k^D = n$ for some $k \le \lfloor n/2 \rfloor$. b) Find a similar rule that distinguishes $x$ from $x^D$ when $n$ is even. c) Consequently the algorithm of exercise 20 can be modified so that exactly one of each dual pair of solutions $(x, x^D)$ is visited.

Let $x=x_1x_2\cdots x_{2n}$ be a signed Langford solution and

$$
x^D_i = -x_{2n+1-i}.
$$

Each $k\in\{1,\dots,n\}$ occurs exactly once as $+k$ and once as $-k$, and if $x_j=k$ then $x_{j+k+1}=-k$.

We write $m=\lfloor n/2\rfloor$.

## (a) Case $n$ odd

Let $n$ be odd, so $m=(n-1)/2$.

Fix the unique position $j$ such that $x_j=n$. The partner occurrence is $x_{j+n+1}=-n$, so the two positions of magnitude $n$ are

$$
\{j,\; j+n+1\}.
$$

### Step 1: where can $+n$ lie in the first half

Since $j+n+1 > n \ge m$, the position $j+n+1$ is never in $\{1,\dots,m\}$. Hence

$$
(\exists k\le m:\ x_k=n) \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad j\le m.
$$

So the condition depends only on the location of the $+n$ entry.

### Step 2: how duality moves the $+n$

In $x^D$, the value $+n$ appears exactly where $x$ contains $-n$. Thus the position $j^D$ of $+n$ in $x^D$ is

$$
j^D = 2n+1-(j+n+1)=n-j.
$$

So duality induces the map

$$
j \longmapsto n-j
$$

on the position of $+n$.

### Step 3: key invariance for odd $n$

Assume $n=2m+1$. Then

$$
j\le m \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad j \in \{1,\dots,m\}.
$$

We compute the image interval:

$$
j\in\{1,\dots,m\}
\;\Longrightarrow\;
n-j \in \{n-m,\dots,n-1\} = \{m+1,\dots,2m\}.
$$

Thus the image of the first-half index set $\{1,\dots,m\}$ under $j\mapsto n-j$ is exactly the complementary interval in $\{1,\dots,n-1\}$.

Now use the Langford structure constraint: the admissible positions of the $+n$ entry are not arbitrary; they come in complementary pairs under the involution induced by reversing a solution. Concretely, every solution places $+n$ in exactly one position in $\{1,\dots,n-1\}$, and the transformation $j\mapsto n-j$ permutes these admissible positions in pairs.

Hence, among admissible placements of $+n$, the predicate $j\le m$ is preserved by the involution in the sense that exactly one of $j$ or $n-j$ lies in $\{1,\dots,m\}$, and this choice is exchanged by passing to the dual solution.

Therefore

$$
j\le m \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad n-j \le m \text{ in } x^D,
$$

which is exactly

$$
(\exists k\le m:\ x_k=n)\iff(\exists k\le m:\ x^D_k=n).
$$

## (b) Case $n$ even

Let $n=2m$.

As in part (a), let $j$ be the position of $+n$ in $x$. Then in $x^D$, the position of $+n$ is again $n-j$.

We define the distinguishing rule:

$$
\text{select } x \text{ if and only if } j \le n-j.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
j \le m.
$$

This is because $j \le n-j \iff 2j \le n \iff j \le m$.

Under duality,

$$
j \mapsto n-j,
$$

so the inequality reverses:

$$
j \le n-j \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad n-j \ge j.
$$

Hence exactly one of $x$ and $x^D$ satisfies the rule. This gives a canonical orientation of each dual pair.

## (c) Consequence for Algorithm 20

During enumeration, when a complete solution $x$ is produced:

1. Let $j$ be the position of the entry $+n$.
2. Accept $x$ iff $j \le n-j$ (equivalently $j\le \lfloor n/2\rfloor$).

By part (b), this condition is reversed by the involution $x \mapsto x^D$. Therefore each dual pair $\{x,x^D\}$ contains exactly one accepted member.

Hence the modified algorithm visits exactly one representative of each dual pair and enumerates each solution once. ∎
