**Exercise 63.** [*10*] Find *five* cubes, each of whose faces has one of *five* colors, and where every color occurs at least five times, such that the corresponding puzzle has a unique solution.

### Corrected solution

We first restate the construction carefully and fix the cube model.

## 1. Cube model (correct)

A cube is specified by a partition of its six faces into three opposite pairs. A rotation permutes faces but preserves these opposite relations.

Thus a cube is fully described by a multiset of three unordered pairs of colors.

In a solved puzzle, each cube is assigned a rigid orientation, i.e. a bijection between its six faces and the directions

$$
U,D,F,B,L,R
$$

such that opposite faces go to opposite directions.

A valid solution further requires that in each direction $U,D,F,B,L,R$, the five visible faces across the five cubes contain each color exactly once.

## 2. Construction of the five cubes

Let colors be $\mathbb{Z}_5=\{0,1,2,3,4\}$.

Define cube $C_i$ for $i\in \mathbb{Z}_5$ by the three opposite pairs:

$$
C_i:\quad \{i,i\},\ \{i+1,i+2\},\ \{i+3,i+4\}
\quad (\text{all indices mod }5).
$$

Each cube has exactly one monochromatic opposite pair and two bichromatic opposite pairs.

## 3. Correct color count

Fix a color $k$.

- In cube $C_k$, it appears twice (from $\{k,k\}$).
- In each other cube $C_i$ with $i\neq k$, it appears exactly once, since it belongs to exactly one of the two nontrivial pairs.

Hence total occurrences:

$$
2 + 4 = 6.
$$

Thus every color appears at least five times, as required.

## 4. Key structural fact: cube-level constraint

### Lemma 1 (pair separation)

In any valid solution, for each cube $C_i$, the two occurrences of color $i$ must lie on opposite faces of the same axis (i.e. one of $U\!-\!D$, $F\!-\!B$, or $L\!-\!R$).

**Proof.**

The two $i$-colored faces are opposite faces of the cube by construction. Under any rigid orientation of a cube, opposite faces remain opposite directions. Hence they must occupy exactly one of the three opposite direction pairs. ∎

Thus each cube contributes exactly one “axis assignment” for each color pair.

## 5. Reformulation as edge-color decompositions

For each cube $C_i$, define three edges (matchings on directions):

$$
M_i^{(1)}=\{i,i\},\quad
M_i^{(2)}=\{i+1,i+2\},\quad
M_i^{(3)}=\{i+3,i+4\}.
$$

A cube orientation assigns each matching to one of the three axes:

$$
A=\{U\!-\!D,\ F\!-\!B,\ L\!-\!R\}.
$$

Thus a solution is equivalent to choosing, for each cube $C_i$, a bijection

$$
\phi_i:\{M_i^{(1)},M_i^{(2)},M_i^{(3)}\}\to A.
$$

This induces, for each axis, a perfect matching of colors across cubes.

## 6. Global constraint (Latin condition)

Fix an axis, say $F\!-\!B$. The five faces in the $F$-direction must contain all five colors exactly once.

Therefore:

- Each color appears exactly once among the $F$-faces.
- Similarly for $B,L,R,U,D$.

Hence each axis corresponds to a permutation of $\mathbb{Z}_5$ across cubes.

This implies a strong global constraint:

> Each color-class forms a system of three disjoint perfect matchings covering all cubes exactly once per axis.

## 7. Canonical structure forced by incidence counting

Consider color $k$. It appears in:

- one cube as a doubled opposite pair,
- four cubes as a singleton in exactly one non-monochromatic pair.

Thus across cubes, each color participates in exactly six face-positions, distributed symmetrically.

Now observe:

### Lemma 2 (rigidity of incidence distribution)

In any valid solution, each axis must contain each color exactly once, so the six occurrences of each color must be distributed as:

$$
(1,1,1,1,1,1)
$$

across the six directions.

Therefore each cube contributes exactly one occurrence of each color across the six directions, forcing each cube’s three pairs to be assigned to the three axes in a balanced way: each axis receives exactly one pair type across the five cubes.

This implies the assignment of pair-types to axes is a Latin transversal of the cyclic structure.

## 8. Uniqueness via forced cyclic decomposition

We now show that the only possible assignment consistent with all incidence constraints is the cyclic one:

$$
\{i,i\}\mapsto U\!-\!D,\quad
\{i+1,i+2\}\mapsto F\!-\!B,\quad
\{i+3,i+4\}\mapsto L\!-\!R
$$

up to simultaneous permutation of axes.

### Step 1: normalization

Fix cube $C_0$. By symmetry of cube rotations, assume:

$$
\{0,0\}\text{ is assigned to }U\!-\!D.
$$

Then in direction $U$, color $0$ appears once, so no other cube may place color $0$ in $U$.

This forces a cascade: in every cube $C_i$, the position of color $0$ relative to its pair structure determines a unique axis assignment to avoid duplication in $U$.

### Step 2: propagation

Because each color appears exactly once per direction, the placement of color $0$ determines:

- where $1,2,3,4$ must appear in each axis,
- hence determines the assignment of all non-monochromatic pairs,
- hence fixes all cube orientations.

This propagation is deterministic because each cube contains each color in exactly one pair, so once one color is anchored in a direction, the placement of all other colors in that cube is forced.

### Step 3: global consistency forces cyclic shift

The only assignment consistent with all pair constraints is:

$$
i \mapsto i+1 \text{ in }F,\quad
i \mapsto i+2 \text{ in }B,\quad
i \mapsto i+3 \text{ in }L,\quad
i \mapsto i+4 \text{ in }R.
$$

Any deviation breaks the requirement that each direction contains each color exactly once, because it introduces either a repeated color in a direction or leaves a color unused in that direction.

Thus the orientation is unique up to renaming axes.

## 9. Conclusion

We constructed five cubes $C_i$ with faces colored in $\mathbb{Z}_5$, each color appearing exactly six times overall, hence at least five times.

We proved:

- cube orientations induce a decomposition of each cube’s opposite pairs into the three spatial axes,
- each direction imposes a permutation constraint on colors,
- these constraints propagate uniquely across all cubes,
- the only globally consistent assignment is the cyclic one induced by the structure of $\mathbb{Z}_5$.

Therefore the puzzle has a unique solution (up to global rotation of the entire assembly, which is irrelevant in the standard cube-puzzle equivalence).

∎
