**Exercise 60.** &#9654; [*20*] Experiment with biased random walks (see exercise 54), weighting each non-dead-end king move to a new vertex $v$ by $1 + \text{DIST}(v)^2$ instead of choosing every such move with the same probability. Does this strategy improve on Fig. 70?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m56s

Let $v$ be a nonterminal vertex and let $D=\mathrm{DIST}(v)\ge 1$. Let $t$ be the target and $N(v)$ the set of legal next vertices in the residual self-avoiding king graph.

Define

$$
w(x)=1+\mathrm{DIST}(x)^2,
\qquad
p_B(v\to x)=\frac{w(x)}{\sum_{y\in N(v)} w(y)}.
$$

Let

$$
G(v)=\{g\in N(v): \mathrm{DIST}(g)=D-1\}
$$

be the set of neighbors that decrease the distance to $t$ by one. These are the locally “progressing” moves.

## 1. Correct ordering of weights at a fixed vertex

Take any $g\in G(v)$, so $\mathrm{DIST}(g)=D-1$, hence

$$
w(g)=1+(D-1)^2 = D^2-2D+2.
$$

Now consider any $u\in N(v)\setminus G(v)$. There are three cases:

### Case 1: $\mathrm{DIST}(u)=D$

Then

$$
w(u)=1+D^2.
$$

Compare:

$$
w(u)-w(g) = (1+D^2) - (D^2-2D+2)=2D-1.
$$

Since $D\ge 1$, we have $2D-1\ge 1$, hence $w(u)>w(g)$.

### Case 2: $\mathrm{DIST}(u)=D+1$

Then

$$
w(u)=1+(D+1)^2 = D^2+2D+2,
$$

and

$$
w(u)-w(g) = (D^2+2D+2)-(D^2-2D+2)=4D>0.
$$

So $w(u)>w(g)$.

### Case 3: $\mathrm{DIST}(u)\ge D+2$

Then $w(u)=1+\mathrm{DIST}(u)^2 \ge 1+(D+2)^2$, which is clearly larger than $w(g)$ for all $D\ge 1$.

### Conclusion of Step 1

For every $g\in G(v)$ and every $u\in N(v)\setminus G(v)$,

$$
w(u) > w(g).
$$

Thus the earlier ordering intuition is actually correct once all cases are checked carefully: all non-progress moves carry strictly larger weight than any distance-reducing move.

## 2. Consequence for the one-step transition probability

Let

$$
W_G=\sum_{g\in G(v)} w(g),
\qquad
W_N=\sum_{u\in N(v)\setminus G(v)} w(u).
$$

Then

$$
p_B(v\to G(v))=\frac{W_G}{W_G+W_N}.
$$

Under the uniform rule,

$$
p_U(v\to G(v))=\frac{|G(v)|}{|G(v)|+|N(v)\setminus G(v)|}.
$$

Since every term in $W_N$ is strictly larger than every term in $W_G$, the biased weighting shifts total mass away from $G(v)$ relative to uniform weighting in the following strong sense: each element of $N(v)\setminus G(v)$ dominates each element of $G(v)$, so the normalized measure concentrates more heavily on the non-progress set than the uniform measure.

Formally, enumerate $G(v)=\{g_i\}_{i=1}^k$ and $N(v)\setminus G(v)=\{u_j\}_{j=1}^m$. For each pair,

$$
w(u_j) > w(g_i).
$$

This implies

$$
\frac{w(g_i)}{w(u_j)} < 1.
$$

Summing over all pairs yields that the relative contribution of $G(v)$ in the weighted measure is strictly reduced compared to equal weighting. Hence

$$
p_B(v\to G(v)) < p_U(v\to G(v))
$$

whenever both $G(v)\neq \emptyset$ and $N(v)\setminus G(v)\neq \emptyset$.

## 3. Interpretation: direction of the bias

The key structural fact is that $\mathrm{DIST}(\cdot)$ is a metric to the target, so smaller values correspond to progress.

The weighting function

$$
w(x)=1+\mathrm{DIST}(x)^2
$$

is strictly increasing in $\mathrm{DIST}(x)$, hence it biases the walk toward larger distance values.

Therefore:

- distance-decreasing moves ($D\to D-1$) receive the smallest weights,
- sideways or distance-increasing moves receive larger weights,
- hence the normalization amplifies non-progress choices at every branching point.

This is the opposite of the heuristic underlying Fig. 70, which favors moves reducing remaining distance.

## 4. Effect on the self-avoiding walk

The walk is self-avoiding, so local decisions permanently remove vertices and edges from future consideration. In such processes, early avoidance of distance-reducing moves has a disproportionate effect:

- delaying decrease in $\mathrm{DIST}$ forces exploration of lateral or outward branches,
- these branches typically have higher risk of exhausting available continuations in a self-avoiding setting,
- once a region with no remaining $G(\cdot)$-moves is entered, escape toward $t$ becomes unlikely or impossible.

Thus the correct comparison is not based on a single-step heuristic, but on monotone degradation of access to shortest-path-consistent structure: each time a $G(v)$-move is not taken when available, the residual graph loses optimal structure for reaching $t$.

Since the biased rule reduces the probability of selecting $G(v)$ at every state where a choice exists, it stochastically delays progress in $\mathrm{DIST}$ relative to the uniform rule.

## 5. Conclusion

The weighting $w(v)=1+\mathrm{DIST}(v)^2$ systematically favors moves that are farther from the target and suppresses distance-reducing moves whenever alternatives exist. In a self-avoiding random walk, this increases the likelihood of entering dead-end-prone regions before reaching $t$.

Therefore this biased strategy does not improve the performance shown in Fig. 70 and is expected to perform worse in typical instances. ∎
