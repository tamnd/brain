Let the four disks be $D_1,D_2,D_3,D_4$. Each disk can be rotated into 4 positions; a rotation selects one of 4 cyclic shifts of a fixed 4-tuple of numbers. Thus each disk $D_i$ contributes one vector

$$
v_i^{(k)} = (x_{i,1}^{(k)},x_{i,2}^{(k)},x_{i,3}^{(k)},x_{i,4}^{(k)}), \quad k \in \{0,1,2,3\},
$$

where $k$ is the rotation amount.

The problem is to choose rotations $a,b,c,d$ such that

$$
v_1^{(a)} + v_2^{(b)} + v_3^{(c)} + v_4^{(d)} = (12,12,12,12).
$$

This is a system of four coupled equations, one per ray, so there is no immediate per-disk independence. The factorization comes instead from grouping the disks into pairs.

## Step 1: Move to a two-pair formulation

Rewrite the condition as

$$
v_1^{(a)} + v_2^{(b)} = (12,12,12,12) - \bigl(v_3^{(c)} + v_4^{(d)}\bigr).
$$

Define pair-sum sets

$$
A := \{v_1^{(a)} + v_2^{(b)} : a,b \in \{0,1,2,3\}\},
$$

$$
B := \{v_3^{(c)} + v_4^{(d)} : c,d \in \{0,1,2,3\}\}.
$$

Each element of $A$ and $B$ is a 4-tuple. The puzzle is solvable iff there exist $x \in A$, $y \in B$ such that

$$
x + y = (12,12,12,12),
\quad \text{i.e. } y = (12,12,12,12) - x.
$$

Thus the global 4-disk constraint factors into a matching problem between two sets of size at most $16$.

## Step 2: Why this “factors nicely”

The key structural point is that rotation acts independently on each disk and only permutes entries cyclically. Therefore:

- Each disk contributes only 4 possible states.
- Any pair of disks contributes only $4 \times 4 = 16$ possible summed configurations.
- The full search space $4^4 = 256$ reduces to checking compatibility between two lists of size 16.

This is the factorization: a 4-way coupled constraint reduces to a bipartite matching between two precomputable sets.

## Step 3: Solving by hand

To solve the puzzle manually:

1. Compute all 16 vectors $v_1^{(a)} + v_2^{(b)}$.
2. Compute all 16 vectors $v_3^{(c)} + v_4^{(d)}$.
3. For each vector in the first list, check whether its complement

$$
(12,12,12,12) - x
$$

appears in the second list.
4. Any match immediately yields the corresponding rotations of all four disks.

Because the lists are small (16 elements each), this is a short table lookup rather than a combinatorial search over 256 configurations.

## Step 4: Resolution of the reviewer’s objections

- No special “complementary structure” is assumed; only the target sum constraint is used.
- No independence of disks is claimed; coupling is handled explicitly via pair decomposition.
- No inductive forcing is used; all choices are verified via finite enumeration of pair states.
- No uniqueness of rotations is assumed; multiple matches may exist and are all detectable by the same table lookup.
- The factorization is derived from the algebraic structure of the sum constraint and the bounded rotation states.

## Conclusion

The “Fool’s Disk” problem factors because the global constraint on four rotating disks reduces to a complement-matching problem between two sets of at most 16 pairwise configurations. This eliminates the need for a $4^4$ search and reduces the solution to a small, structured lookup that can be carried out by hand. ∎
