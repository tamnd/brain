[Section 7.2.2: Backtracking](../)

**Exercise 17.** [**] [15] Quick (a student noticed that the loop in step L2 of Algorithm L can be changed from 'while $x_j < 0$' to 'while $x_j \ne 0$', because $x_l$ cannot be positive at

that point of the algorithm. So he decided to eliminate the minus signs and just set $x_{l+k+1} \leftarrow k$ in step L3. Was it a good idea?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m11s  

---

The change is not valid. In Algorithm L, the negative values of $x_j$ are not cosmetic; they encode state information that distinguishes whether a position has already been committed in a way that must be undone or complemented later in the construction. The condition that justifies replacing “$x_j < 0$” by “$x_j \ne 0$” relies on the invariant that positive entries never occur in that part of the algorithm, and that invariant itself depends on the signed representation.

Eliminating the minus signs and replacing the assignment by $x_{l+k+1} \leftarrow k$ destroys the distinction between the first placement of a value and its paired placement enforced by (7). The algorithm then loses the ability to represent whether a number has been used in its required complementary position, so the state no longer encodes partial Langford configurations correctly. As a result, the modified procedure can produce arrays that satisfy superficial nonzero conditions while failing the pairing constraint, and it can also block necessary backtracking information that was previously carried by sign.
