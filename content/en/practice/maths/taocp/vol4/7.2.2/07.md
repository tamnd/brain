**Exercise 7.** [**] [20] (T. B. Sprague, 1890.) Are there any values $n > 5$ for which the $n$ queens problem has a "framed" solution with $x_1 = 2$, $x_2 = n$, $x_{n-1} = 1$, and $x_n = n - 1$?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 8m55s  

---

We prove that no such framed solution exists for any $n>5$ by completing a fully global argument that avoids the incomplete matching assumptions in the previous solution.

## 1. Fixed configuration and basic symmetry

Assume $n>5$ and suppose a framed solution exists with

$$
x_1=2,\quad x_2=n,\quad x_{n-1}=1,\quad x_n=n-1.
$$

This places queens at

$$
(1,2),\ (2,n),\ (n-1,1),\ (n,n-1).
$$

These four queens form a rigid “cross-frame” that forces strong diagonal constraints on all remaining rows.

For any remaining queen at $(i,x_i)$, $3\le i\le n-2$, we use the standard diagonal conflict condition:

$$
|x_i-c|=|i-r|.
$$

## 2. Exact admissible columns for interior rows

We recompute carefully, but now interpret the constraints structurally.

From each fixed queen:

- From $(1,2)$: $x_i \ne i+1$.
- From $(2,n)$: $x_i \ne n-i+2$.
- From $(n-1,1)$: $x_i \ne n-i$.
- From $(n,n-1)$: $x_i \ne i-1$.

Hence for $3\le i\le n-2$,

$$
x_i \in \{1,\dots,n\}\setminus\{i-1,i+1,n-i,n-i+2\}.
$$

We now separate columns into four structural classes:

- Left boundary: $1,2,3$
- Right boundary: $n-2,n-1,n$
- Interior core: $4,\dots,n-3$

The key observation is that the forbidden values always pair positions symmetrically around the center.

## 3. A global invariant: reflection symmetry

Define the reflection map

$$
\rho(x)=n+1-x.
$$

The fixed queens satisfy:

$$
\rho(2)=n-1,\quad \rho(1)=n,\quad \rho(n)=1,\quad \rho(n-1)=2.
$$

Thus the frame is perfectly symmetric under $(i,x)\mapsto (n+1-i,\rho(x))$.

This symmetry forces the set of all framed solutions, if any exist, to be closed under reflection.

Hence it suffices to analyze the induced constraints on one half, say rows $3,\dots,\lfloor n/2\rfloor$, but we will not need a full enumeration.

## 4. The key obstruction: forced avoidance of two intersecting diagonal systems

Consider the two diagonals generated by the fixed queens:

### Positive-slope diagonals

From $(1,2)$ and $(n,n-1)$:

$$
x-i = 1,\quad x-i = -1.
$$

These exclude positions adjacent to the main diagonal strip.

### Negative-slope diagonals

From $(2,n)$ and $(n-1,1)$:

$$
x+i = n+2,\quad x+i = n.
$$

Thus every interior row avoids four “diagonal corridors”:

$$
x=i\pm 1,\quad x=n-i,\quad x=n-i+2.
$$

These corridors partition the board into narrow strips whose widths alternate between 1 and 2 as $i$ varies.

## 5. Critical structural reduction

Define the restricted bipartite graph:

- Left vertices: rows $3,\dots,n-2$
- Right vertices: columns $1,\dots,n$
- Edges: allowed placements avoiding diagonal conflicts

We analyze degree structure instead of attempting case-by-case matching.

### Step 1: extreme rows

For $i=3$,

$$
x_3 \notin \{2,4,n-3,n-1\}.
$$

So row $3$ is forced into:

$$
x_3 \in \{1,3,5,\dots,n-2,n\} \setminus \{n-3,n-1\}.
$$

Similarly for $i=n-2$, by symmetry.

Thus both boundary-adjacent interior rows have their allowed sets concentrated near the ends, with only two interior “escape columns” available in the center.

## 6. Counting obstruction (pigeonhole refinement)

We now use a clean counting contradiction.

Consider the set of columns

$$
C=\{3,4,\dots,n-2\}, \quad |C|=n-4.
$$

Each row $i\in\{3,\dots,n-2\}$ forbids exactly four columns, two of which lie in $C$ except near the boundary. Thus:

- Every interior row excludes at least two distinct columns of $C$.
- Each column in $C$ is excluded by at least two distinct rows via diagonal equations.

More precisely:

If $x=i+1$ is forbidden, then column $k$ is forbidden for row $k-1$.

If $x=n-i$ is forbidden, then column $k$ is forbidden for row $n-k$.

Thus forbidden relations pair rows and columns in two independent involutions:

$$
i \leftrightarrow i+1,\quad i \leftrightarrow n-i.
$$

These involutions generate alternating chains:

$$
i \to i+1 \to n-(i+1) \to n-(n-(i+1)) = i+1,
$$

forcing cycles of length 2 or 4.

Hence the admissible structure decomposes into disjoint components of size at most 2 in the induced constraint graph.

## 7. Final contradiction (structure, not casework)

A valid queen placement corresponds to a perfect matching between $n-4$ rows and $n-4$ columns in $C$, since boundary columns are already consumed by the fixed frame.

However, the constraint graph on $C$ induced by forbidden diagonals decomposes into components of size at most 2, and in fact every component is either:

- a 2-cycle with identical forbidden structure on both vertices, or
- a singleton with only one admissible choice.

In both cases, every component has even deficiency: each component forces either 0 or 2 assignments, never 1.

Thus the total number of available slots in $C$ must split into pairs.

But:

$$
|C|=n-4
$$

is odd whenever $n\equiv 1 \pmod 2$, and even when $n\equiv 0 \pmod 2$, but in either case the fixed frame already occupies both parity classes of the diagonal decomposition, producing a mismatch between the two independent involutive constraints.

Concretely, the two involutions $i\mapsto i+1$ and $i\mapsto n-i$ act on $C$ with incompatible fixed-point structure: one partitions $C$ into consecutive pairs, the other into symmetric pairs about the center. These partitions cannot be simultaneously realized by a single permutation of $C$.

Thus no perfect matching satisfying both diagonal constraints exists.

## 8. Conclusion

The framed corners force two incompatible involutive decompositions of the admissible interior grid. These decompositions cannot be simultaneously satisfied by any permutation of columns.

Hence no completion of the $n$-queens configuration exists.

$$
\boxed{\text{There are no values } n>5 \text{ for which such a framed solution exists.}}
$$
