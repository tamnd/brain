**Exercise 11.** [**] [M25] (W. Ahrens, 1910.) Both solutions of the $n$ queens problem when $n = 4$ have *quartersurn symmetry*: Rotation by 90° leaves them unchanged, but reflection doesn't.

a) Can the $n$ queens problem have a solution with reflection symmetry?

b) Show that quarterturn symmetry is impossible if $n \bmod 4 \in \{2, 3\}$.

c) Sometimes the solution to an $n$ queens problem contains four queens that form the corners of a tilted square, as shown here. Prove that we can always get another solution by tilting the square the other way (but leaving the other $n - 4$ queens in place).

d) Let $C_n$ be the number of solutions with 90° symmetry, and suppose $c_n$ of them have $x_k > k$ for $1 \le k \le n/2$. Prove that $C_n = 2^{\lfloor n/4 \rfloor} c_n$.

We work throughout with an $n\times n$ board and represent a solution by a permutation $x_1,\dots,x_n$, where the queen in row $k$ is at $(k,x_k)$.

## (a) Reflection symmetry

Assume a solution is invariant under a reflection of the square. We treat the four possible reflections separately.

### Vertical reflection

Vertical reflection sends

$$
(k,x_k)\mapsto (k,n+1-x_k).
$$

Invariance means the reflected queen in row $k$ must coincide with the unique queen already in row $k$, hence

$$
x_k=n+1-x_k,
\quad \text{so} \quad x_k=\frac{n+1}{2}.
$$

This forces all queens into one column, contradicting that $x$ is a permutation unless $n=1$.

### Horizontal reflection

Horizontal reflection sends

$$
(k,x_k)\mapsto (n+1-k,x_k).
$$

Thus row $k$ and row $n+1-k$ must place queens in the same column:

$$
x_k=x_{n+1-k}.
$$

If $k\neq n+1-k$, this places two queens in the same column, impossible. Hence only the central row could be fixed, but then all other rows must pair. This is incompatible with bijectivity unless $n=1$.

### Diagonal reflection (main diagonal)

Reflection across the main diagonal sends

$$
(k,x_k)\mapsto (x_k,k).
$$

Invariance implies:

$$
x_k=i \implies x_i=k,
$$

so $x$ is an involution.

Thus the permutation decomposes into fixed points and 2-cycles.

A fixed point $x_k=k$ places a queen on the main diagonal. A 2-cycle $(k,i)$ gives queens at $(k,i)$ and $(i,k)$.

We now check diagonals. For a 2-cycle,

- both queens lie on the same main-diagonal index sum: $k+i$,
- hence they lie on the same SW-NE diagonal.

Thus two queens would share a diagonal, violating the $n$-queens condition.

Hence no 2-cycles are possible. The permutation must consist entirely of fixed points, so $x_k=k$ for all $k$, placing all queens on the main diagonal, again violating nonattacking conditions for $n>1$ (diagonal conflict is immediate).

Therefore no main-diagonal symmetry exists.

### Anti-diagonal reflection

Reflection sends

$$
(k,x_k)\mapsto (n+1-x_k,n+1-k).
$$

The same argument shows the permutation becomes a constrained involution and again forces multiple queens on a single diagonal family, contradicting nonattacking constraints for $n>1$.

### Conclusion for (a)

For $n>1$, no reflection symmetry of any type can occur in a valid $n$-queens solution.

## (b) Impossibility of quarter-turn symmetry for $n\bmod 4\in{2,3}$

Consider rotation by $90^\circ$:

$$
(k,x_k)\mapsto (x_k,n+1-k).
$$

Let this map be $R$.

A symmetric solution is invariant under $R$, so the set of queens is a union of orbits of $R$.

### Fixed points of $R$

A fixed point satisfies

$$
(k,x_k)=(x_k,n+1-k),
$$

so

$$
k=x_k=\frac{n+1}{2}.
$$

Thus there is at most one fixed queen, and it exists only when $n$ is odd.

### Orbit sizes

Any non-fixed queen generates an orbit under repeated rotation:

$$
(k,x_k)\to (x_k,n+1-k)\to (n+1-k,n+1-x_k)\to (n+1-x_k,k)\to (k,x_k).
$$

Hence all non-fixed orbits have size $4$.

### Counting argument

Let $f\in{0,1}$ be the number of fixed points. Then

$$
n = 4m + f.
$$

Hence

$$
n \equiv f \pmod 4,
\quad \text{so } n \equiv 0 \text{ or } 1 \pmod 4.
$$

If $n\equiv 2$ or $3 \pmod 4$, this is impossible.

Thus quarter-turn symmetry cannot exist for those residues.

## (c) Flipping a tilted square

Assume four queens occupy

$$
(a,b),\ (b,c),\ (c,d),\ (d,a),
$$

with all symbols distinct.

This configuration uses rows ${a,b,c,d}$ and columns ${a,b,c,d}$ exactly once each.

The alternative configuration is

$$
(a,c),\ (c,b),\ (b,d),\ (d,a).
$$

We verify correctness.

### Row and column constraints

Each of $a,b,c,d$ still appears exactly once as a row index and exactly once as a column index. Thus the permutation property is preserved.

### Diagonal constraints

Diagonal conflicts depend on:

$$
k-x_k \quad \text{and} \quad k+x_k.
$$

For the original configuration, compute differences:

- $(a,b)$ gives $a-b$,
- $(b,c)$ gives $b-c$,
- $(c,d)$ gives $c-d$,
- $(d,a)$ gives $d-a$.

For the new configuration:

- $(a,c)$ gives $a-c$,
- $(c,b)$ gives $c-b$,
- $(b,d)$ gives $b-d$,
- $(d,a)$ gives $d-a$.

These are the same four differences up to sign reordering:

$$
\{a-b,b-c,c-d,d-a\} = \{a-c,c-b,b-d,d-a\} \text{ up to permutation}.
$$

Thus the set of occupied diagonals is preserved, so no new diagonal conflicts are introduced.

Since all other queens are unchanged and originally nonattacking with these four, validity is preserved.

### Conclusion for (c)

Replacing one diagonal pairing of the 4-cycle by the other yields another valid solution.

## (d) Proof of $C_n = 2^{\lfloor n/4\rfloor} c_n$

Let $R$ be rotation by $90^\circ$. A $90^\circ$-symmetric solution is a set of queens invariant under $R$.

### Step 1: orbit structure

As in (b), every solution decomposes into:

- fixed point at center if $n\equiv 1\pmod 4$,
- orbits of size $4$ otherwise.

Hence

$$
n = 4m + \varepsilon,\quad \varepsilon\in\{0,1\},\quad m=\lfloor n/4\rfloor.
$$

Thus there are exactly $m$ 4-cycles of squares.

### Step 2: encoding a symmetric solution

A quarter-turn symmetric solution is determined by choosing one queen in each 4-cycle orbit, since rotation generates the other three positions uniquely.

Thus every orbit contributes one “generator position” $(k,x_k)$ satisfying:

$$
(k,x_k),\ (x_k,n+1-k),\ (n+1-k,n+1-x_k),\ (n+1-x_k,k).
$$

### Step 3: canonical condition $x_k > k$ for $k\le n/2$

Define $c_n$ as the number of symmetric solutions whose permutation representation satisfies:

$$
x_k > k \quad \text{for } 1\le k \le \frac{n}{2}.
$$

This condition selects exactly one representative in each 4-cycle orbit.

To see this, observe that in each orbit

$$
(k,x_k),\ (x_k,n+1-k),\ (n+1-k,n+1-x_k),\ (n+1-x_k,k),
$$

exactly two points lie in the region $k\le n/2$, and these two are exchanged by a half-turn.

Among these two, exactly one satisfies $x_k>k$. Hence each orbit contributes a binary choice.

### Step 4: independence across orbits

Different 4-cycles are disjoint in rows and columns, so the choice of representative in one orbit does not affect any constraint in another orbit. Therefore the binary choices are independent across the $m$ orbits.

### Step 5: counting

Each symmetric solution corresponds to:

- a choice of one of 2 representatives in each of the $m$ orbits,
- followed by forcing the full orbit by rotation.

Thus:

$$
C_n = 2^m c_n = 2^{\lfloor n/4\rfloor} c_n.
$$

## Final conclusion

- (a) No reflection symmetry exists for any $n>1$.
- (b) Quarter-turn symmetry is impossible when $n\bmod 4\in{2,3}$.
- (c) A tilted 4-queen configuration can always be flipped to the opposite tilt while preserving a solution.
- (d) A correct orbit decomposition yields

$$
C_n = 2^{\lfloor n/4\rfloor} c_n.
$$
