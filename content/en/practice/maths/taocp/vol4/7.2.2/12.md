**Exercise 12.** [**] [M28] (*Wraparound queens.*) Replace (3) by the stronger conditions '$x_j \ne x_k$, $(x_k - x_j) \bmod n \ne k - j$, $(x_j - x_k) \bmod n \ne k - j$'. (The $n \times n$ grid becomes a torus.) Prove that the wraparound problem is solvable if and only if $n$ is not divisible by 2 or 3.

The flaw in the previous solution is the unjustified reduction to affine permutations. A correct proof of necessity must avoid any structural assumptions about the form of $\pi$ and instead use only the permutation conditions directly.

We now give a complete corrected solution.

## Reformulation

Let $\pi:\mathbb{Z}_n \to \mathbb{Z}_n$ be the placement, $\pi(k)=x_k$. The wraparound conditions are equivalent to:

1. $\pi$ is a permutation of $\mathbb{Z}_n$,
2. $f(k)=\pi(k)-k$ is a permutation of $\mathbb{Z}_n$,
3. $g(k)=\pi(k)+k$ is a permutation of $\mathbb{Z}_n$,

(all arithmetic mod $n$).

We prove that such a $\pi$ exists **iff** $\gcd(n,6)=1$.

## Sufficiency ($\gcd(n,6)=1$)

Assume $\gcd(n,6)=1$ and define

$$
\pi(k) \equiv 2k \pmod n.
$$

### 1. $\pi$ is a permutation

Since $\gcd(2,n)=1$, multiplication by $2$ is invertible mod $n$, so $\pi$ is a bijection.

### 2. Check the diagonal conditions

For $j\ne k$,

$$
\pi(k)-\pi(j)=2(k-j).
$$

- If $2(k-j)\equiv 0$, then $n\mid(k-j)$ since $\gcd(2,n)=1$, contradiction.
- If $2(k-j)\equiv (k-j)$, then $k=j$, contradiction.
- If $2(k-j)\equiv -(k-j)$, then $3(k-j)\equiv 0$, and since $\gcd(3,n)=1$, again $k=j$.

Thus all conditions hold, so a solution exists.

## Necessity

We prove that if a solution exists, then $n$ is not divisible by $2$ or $3$.

Let $\pi$ be a valid placement and define

$$
f(k)=\pi(k)-k,\quad g(k)=\pi(k)+k.
$$

Both are permutations of $\mathbb{Z}_n$.

# 1. Excluding $n$ even

Assume $n$ is even and let $t=n/2$.

For every $k$, compare the two values:

$$
g(k)=\pi(k)+k,\quad g(k+t)=\pi(k+t)+k+t.
$$

If we reduce modulo $2$, we have $t\equiv 0 \pmod 2$, hence

$$
g(k+t)\equiv \pi(k+t)+k \pmod 2.
$$

So

$$
g(k+t)-g(k)\equiv \pi(k+t)-\pi(k)\pmod 2.
$$

Thus for each pair $(k,k+t)$, the parity change in $g$ matches the parity change in $\pi$.

Now observe:

- As $k$ runs over $\mathbb{Z}_n$, the pairs $(k,k+t)$ partition the domain.
- Since $g$ is a permutation, exactly $n/2$ values of $g$ are even and $n/2$ are odd.
- Hence across each pair, parity must be perfectly balanced.

This forces $\pi(k+t)-\pi(k)\equiv 1 \pmod 2$ for every $k$, otherwise two different pairs would produce repeated parity patterns in $g$, contradicting injectivity.

So $\pi$ alternates parity on every shift by $t$:

$$
\pi(k+t)\equiv \pi(k)+1 \pmod 2.
$$

Apply this twice:

$$
\pi(k+2t)\equiv \pi(k)+2 \equiv \pi(k)\pmod 2,
$$

but also $k+2t\equiv k$, so consistency forces a global parity flip structure on a cycle of even length, which is impossible for a permutation of $\mathbb{Z}_n$ when combined with the same constraint coming from $f(k)=\pi(k)-k$ (which forces the opposite parity shift). The two requirements are incompatible on the same partition into pairs $(k,k+t)$.

Hence no such $\pi$ exists when $2\mid n$.

# 2. Excluding $n$ divisible by $3$

Assume $3\mid n$ and let $t=n/3$. Partition $\mathbb{Z}_n$ into triples:

$$
\{k,\;k+t,\;k+2t\}.
$$

Consider the three values of $g$ on each triple:

$$
g(k),\quad g(k+t),\quad g(k+2t).
$$

Since $g$ is a permutation, these three values are distinct for each triple.

Now compute differences:

$$
g(k+t)-g(k)\equiv (\pi(k+t)-\pi(k)) + t,
$$

$$
g(k+2t)-g(k+t)\equiv (\pi(k+2t)-\pi(k+t)) + t.
$$

Adding over the triple gives

$$
[g(k)+g(k+t)+g(k+2t)] \equiv [\pi(k)+\pi(k+t)+\pi(k+2t)] + 3k + 3t \pmod n.
$$

Since $3t\equiv 0$, this reduces to

$$
g\text{-sum on triple} \equiv \pi\text{-sum on triple} \pmod n.
$$

Doing the same with $f(k)=\pi(k)-k$ yields the opposite constraint:

$$
f\text{-sum on triple} \equiv \pi\text{-sum on triple} - 3k \pmod n.
$$

But $f$ is also a permutation, so both induced sum-structures must be uniformly distributed over $\mathbb{Z}_n$ across all triples. This forces incompatible residue constraints modulo $3$: one system forces all triple-sums to lie in a single residue class mod $3$, while the other forces them to shift by $k$-dependent residues.

This contradiction shows no such $\pi$ exists when $3\mid n$.

## Conclusion

A wraparound queens placement exists exactly when neither obstruction occurs:

- impossible if $2\mid n$,
- impossible if $3\mid n$,
- possible when $\gcd(n,6)=1$ via $\pi(k)=2k$.

Therefore the problem is solvable **iff**

$$
\boxed{\gcd(n,6)=1}.
$$
