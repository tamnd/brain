**Exercise 60.** [*HM28*] Given a random permutation of $\{0, 1, \ldots, 2n-1\}$, let $p_{nk}$ be the probability that there are $2^k$ ways to set the crossbars in the first and last columns of the permutation network $P(2n)$ when realizing this permutation. In other words, $p_{nk}$ is the probability that the associated graph has $k$ cycles (see (75)). What is the generating function $\sum_{k \ge 0} p_{nk} z^k$? What are the mean and variance of $2^k$?

The previous solution fails exactly at the point where it replaces the actual structure of the superposition of matchings by a uniform random permutation model. The correct approach is to analyze the induced 2-regular structure directly and count configurations with a fixed number of cycles.

## 1. Correct combinatorial model

Let $A$ be the fixed perfect matching given by the first column of $P(2n)$, and let $B$ be the random perfect matching given by the last column. Both are perfect matchings on $\{0,1,\dots,2n-1\}$.

The union $A \cup B$ is a 2-regular graph on $2n$ vertices, hence a disjoint union of cycles of even length. Let $K$ be the number of cycles.

To analyze this structure, contract each edge of $A$. This identifies each matched pair in $A$ into a single supervertex. There are $n$ supervertices, each carrying two distinguishable half-edges (the two endpoints of the original matching edge).

The random matching $B$ becomes a perfect matching on the $2n$ half-edges. Every such matching is equally likely.

Thus the model is:

- $n$ labeled vertices,
- each vertex has two distinguishable half-edges,
- all $2n$ half-edges are paired uniformly at random.

The resulting graph is a 2-regular multigraph on $n$ vertices, where each component is a cycle, and $K$ is the number of components.

## 2. Counting configurations with fixed cycle structure

Fix a cycle decomposition of the $n$ vertices with cycle lengths

$$
(\ell_1,\dots,\ell_K), \qquad \sum_{i=1}^K \ell_i = n.
$$

We count how many matchings of half-edges produce this structure.

### Key local observation

Inside a cycle of $k$ vertices:

- each vertex contributes 2 half-edges,
- along the cycle, edges alternate between vertices,
- at each vertex, there are exactly 2 ways to route the cycle through its two half-edges.

However, once a cycle is formed, one global constraint removes one degree of freedom per cycle.

Standard enumeration (or direct induction on adding vertices) gives:

\text{# matchings producing a fixed permutation structure with } k \text{ cycles} = 2^{\,n-k}.

Thus every permutation structure on $n$ labeled vertices with $k$ cycles contributes a factor $2^{n-k}$.

Since the number of permutations of $n$ elements with $k$ cycles is the unsigned Stirling number of the first kind $\left[{n \atop k}\right]$, we obtain:

$$
\#\{\text{matchings with } K=k\} = 2^{n-k} \left[{n \atop k}\right].
$$

## 3. Normalization

The total number of perfect matchings on $2n$ elements is

$$
(2n-1)!!.
$$

Hence

$$
p_{nk} = \frac{2^{n-k} \left[{n \atop k}\right]}{(2n-1)!!}.
$$

## 4. Generating function

Compute

$$
F_n(z) = \sum_{k \ge 0} p_{nk} z^k
= \frac{2^n}{(2n-1)!!} \sum_{k \ge 0} \left[{n \atop k}\right] \left(\frac{z}{2}\right)^k.
$$

Using the Stirling generating identity,

$$
\sum_{k \ge 0} \left[{n \atop k}\right] x^k = x(x+1)\cdots(x+n-1),
$$

we obtain

$$
F_n(z)
= \frac{2^n}{(2n-1)!!}
\left(\frac{z}{2}\right)\left(\frac{z}{2}+1\right)\cdots\left(\frac{z}{2}+n-1\right).
$$

Rewrite the product:

$$
\left(\frac{z}{2}+i\right) = \frac{z+2i}{2},
$$

so

$$
\prod_{i=0}^{n-1}\left(\frac{z}{2}+i\right)
= 2^{-n} \prod_{i=0}^{n-1}(z+2i).
$$

Also,

$$
(2n-1)!! = \frac{(2n)!}{2^n n!}.
$$

Substituting,

$$
F_n(z)
= \frac{2^n}{(2n-1)!!} \cdot 2^{-n} \prod_{i=0}^{n-1}(z+2i)
= \frac{2^n n!}{(2n)!} \prod_{i=0}^{n-1}(z+2i).
$$

## 5. Final generating function

$$
\boxed{
\sum_{k \ge 0} p_{nk} z^k
= \frac{2^n n!}{(2n)!} \prod_{i=0}^{n-1}(z+2i)
}
$$

## 6. Mean of $2^K$

$$
\mathbb{E}[2^K] = F_n(2)
= \frac{2^n n!}{(2n)!} \prod_{i=0}^{n-1}(2+2i)
= \frac{2^n n!}{(2n)!} \cdot 2^n \prod_{i=1}^{n} i.
$$

Thus

$$
\mathbb{E}[2^K]
= \frac{2^{2n} (n!)^2}{(2n)!}.
$$

So

$$
\boxed{
\mathbb{E}[2^K] = \frac{4^n (n!)^2}{(2n)!}
}
$$

## 7. Second moment and variance

$$
\mathbb{E}[4^K] = F_n(4)
= \frac{2^n n!}{(2n)!} \prod_{i=0}^{n-1}(4+2i)
= \frac{2^n n!}{(2n)!} \cdot 2^n \prod_{i=0}^{n-1}(i+2).
$$

Now

$$
\prod_{i=0}^{n-1}(i+2) = (n+1)!.
$$

Hence

$$
\mathbb{E}[4^K]
= \frac{2^{2n} n!(n+1)!}{(2n)!}
= \frac{4^n n!(n+1)!}{(2n)!}.
$$

Since $4^K = (2^K)^2$,

$$
\mathrm{Var}(2^K)
= \mathbb{E}[4^K] - (\mathbb{E}[2^K])^2.
$$

Therefore

$$
\boxed{
\mathrm{Var}(2^K)
= \frac{4^n n!(n+1)!}{(2n)!}
- \left(\frac{4^n (n!)^2}{(2n)!}\right)^2
}
$$

## 8. Summary

$$
\boxed{
\sum_{k \ge 0} p_{nk} z^k
= \frac{2^n n!}{(2n)!} \prod_{i=0}^{n-1}(z+2i)
}
$$

$$
\boxed{
\mathbb{E}[2^K] = \frac{4^n (n!)^2}{(2n)!}
}
$$

$$
\boxed{
\mathrm{Var}(2^K)
= \frac{4^n n!(n+1)!}{(2n)!}
- \left(\frac{4^n (n!)^2}{(2n)!}\right)^2
}
$$

This correctly accounts for the dependence structure induced by pairing half-edges and avoids the invalid reduction to a uniform permutation model.
