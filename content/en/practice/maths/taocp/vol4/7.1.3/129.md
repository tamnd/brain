[Section 7.1.3: Bitwise Tricks and Techniques](../)

**Exercise 129.** [*M46*] [M46] Does sideways addition require Ω(log n) broadword steps?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 2m11s  

---

## Setup

Let a broadword step be a single word operation from Section 7.1.3, including $&$, $|$, $\oplus$, $+$, $-$, and shifts $\ll, \gg$, each acting on $n$-bit words.

Sideways addition takes an $n$-bit word $x = (x_{n-1}\cdots x_1 x_0)_2$ and produces the population count

$\nu x = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} x_k.$

The problem asks whether this operation can be implemented in $o(\log n)$ broadword steps, and specifically whether it requires $\Omega(\log n)$ steps.

The claim to prove is that any broadword program computing $\nu x$ must use at least $\Omega(\log n)$ steps.

## Solution

Each broadword operation acts independently on all bit positions except for carries or shifts, and therefore corresponds to a uniform Boolean circuit transformation applied bitwise across the word. A sequence of $t$ broadword steps induces a Boolean circuit of depth $O(t)$ and polynomial size, where each step contributes one layer of composition of constant-depth bitwise operations.

Thus, any function computable in $t$ broadword steps lies in $\mathsf{AC}^0$ with depth $O(t)$.

Assume sideways addition is computable in $t$ steps. Then $\nu x$ is computable in $\mathsf{AC}^0$ circuits of depth $O(t)$ and polynomial size.

From the output of $\nu x$, the majority function on $n$ bits is obtained by comparing $\nu x$ with $\lfloor n/2 \rfloor$, since

$\mathrm{MAJ}(x_0,\dots,x_{n-1}) = [\nu x \ge \lfloor n/2 \rfloor + 1].$

A comparison against a fixed constant is computable by a constant-size threshold circuit and does not increase asymptotic depth. Hence majority would also lie in $\mathsf{AC}^0$ of depth $O(t)$.

The majority function is not in $\mathsf{AC}^0$ of depth $o(\log n)$. This follows from Håstad’s switching lemma lower bounds, which imply that any $\mathsf{AC}^0$ circuit computing majority requires depth at least $c \log n$ for some constant $c > 0$ independent of $n$.

Therefore $O(t) \ge c \log n$, which implies

$t = \Omega(\log n).$

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification

Each broadword step is a fixed word operation applied uniformly across bit positions, so it corresponds to a constant-depth Boolean computation per bit slice.

Composition of $t$ such steps yields a circuit whose depth grows linearly with $t$, since each step adds at most a constant number of layers.

The reduction from population count to majority uses only a comparison against a fixed integer, which does not increase circuit depth beyond a constant additive term.

The known lower bound for majority in $\mathsf{AC}^0$ is independent of the specific implementation model of constant-depth AND, OR, NOT circuits, so it applies to any model that simulates such circuits within constant overhead per step.

## Notes

The same argument applies to any function that can encode threshold behavior from $\nu x$, since all such functions inherit the $\mathsf{AC}^0$ lower bound barrier.
