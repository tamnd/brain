**Exercise 146.** &#9654; [*M20*] [M20] Prove Eq. (138), which relates the ρ and λ functions.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 56s

## Solution

Let $\rho(j)$ denote the largest power of $2$ dividing $j$, so $\rho(j)=2^{\nu_2(j)}$, where $\nu_2(j)$ is the 2-adic valuation of $j$. Let $\lambda(j)$ denote the largest power of $2$ not exceeding $j$, so $\lambda(j)=2^{\lfloor \log_2 j \rfloor}$ for $j\ge 1$.

Write $j$ in the form

$$
j = 2^{a} q,
$$

where $a=\nu_2(j)$ and $q$ is odd. Then $\rho(j)=2^a$ and $j/\rho(j)=q$.

Since $q$ is odd, its binary representation ends in $1$, hence $\lfloor \log_2 q \rfloor$ is the position of its highest 1-bit. Therefore

$$
\lfloor \log_2 j \rfloor
= \lfloor \log_2(2^a q) \rfloor
= a + \lfloor \log_2 q \rfloor.
$$

Exponentiating gives

$$
\lambda(j)
= 2^{\lfloor \log_2 j \rfloor}
= 2^{a + \lfloor \log_2 q \rfloor}
= 2^a \cdot 2^{\lfloor \log_2 q \rfloor}.
$$

Since $q=j/\rho(j)$, this becomes

$$
\lambda(j) = \rho(j)\,\lambda\!\left(\frac{j}{\rho(j)}\right).
$$

This is Eq. (138).

The factorization is valid because $\rho(j)$ extracts exactly the full power-of-two factor from $j$, leaving an odd quotient, and multiplication by $\rho(j)$ shifts binary representation left by $a$ positions, preserving the location of the highest 1-bit relative to the quotient.

This completes the proof. ∎
