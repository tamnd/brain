## Setup

Let Theorem P′ be the statement proved in Section 7.1.3 using Lemma B and the counting framework of broadword chains. The proof in the book relies on the structural property that certain intermediate expressions are constrained by equations of the form

$E(x) = 0,$

where $E(x)$ ranges over linear combinations (in the sense of the bitwise and arithmetic model used in Lemma B) of previously constructed values in the chain.

Lemma B is used only to bound the number of possible relevant shift amounts and memory addresses when such constraints force a value to equal $0$.

We are asked to prove Theorem P′ under the modification that Lemma B remains valid when every constraint of the form

$E(x) = 0$

is replaced by

$E(x) = \alpha_s$

for fixed constants $\alpha_s$.

Thus every zero-test constraint in the original argument becomes a fixed-constant test.

## Solution

The proof of Theorem P′ proceeds by induction on the length parameter $t$ of the broadword chain $(x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_f)$ and the associated sets $(U_t)$ of inputs that remain indistinguishable with respect to the partial computation up to time $t$.

In the original argument, Lemma B is invoked to show that when $t$ is small, only a limited number of shift amounts and memory addresses can be distinguished by constraints of the form $E(x)=0$. This restriction ensures that, for all $x \in U_t$, the state $Q(x,t)$ must lie in a bounded family determined independently of $x$.

Under the modified hypothesis, each constraint has the form

$E(x) = \alpha_s.$

Fix any such constraint. Define a shifted expression

$E'(x) = E(x) \oplus \alpha_s.$

Using identity (11) and the bitwise distributivity laws (6) and (7), the transformation $E(x)=\alpha_s \iff E(x)\oplus \alpha_s = 0$ holds for all 2-adic integers, since

$E(x)=\alpha_s \iff E(x)\oplus \alpha_s = \alpha_s \oplus \alpha_s = 0.$

Thus every occurrence of a constant equality constraint can be converted into a zero-equality constraint after a uniform rewriting of expressions by XOR with the corresponding constant.

This rewriting preserves the structure required by Lemma B because XOR with a fixed constant does not increase the set of admissible shift amounts or memory addresses: all occurrences of $E(x)$ in the chain are replaced uniformly by $E(x)\oplus \alpha_s$, and the dependence on $x$ is unchanged in each bit position due to (3).

Therefore the combinatorial bound provided by Lemma B applies verbatim to the transformed system, since every test of the form $E(x)=\alpha_s$ becomes a test of the form $E'(x)=0$ with $E'$ ranging over the same syntactic class of expressions used in the original proof.

The remainder of the proof of Theorem P′ depends only on the cardinality bounds on admissible states $Q(x,t)$ and the growth condition

$|U_t| \ge \frac{n}{2^{2t-1}},$

together with the fact that the program cannot force a distinguished register to equal its target value before the final stage. These arguments are unchanged because the transformation $E \mapsto E \oplus \alpha_s$ preserves equality relations and does not alter the branching structure of the computation tree indexed by $x$.

Hence every step in the original proof of Theorem P′ remains valid after replacing Lemma B by its generalized form.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification

Each constraint $E(x)=\alpha_s$ is equivalent to $E(x)\oplus \alpha_s=0$ by the involution property $a\oplus a=0$ from (11). The rewrite uses only bitwise XOR identities and does not introduce additional operations beyond those already permitted in the chain model.

The structural role of Lemma B is solely to bound the number of syntactically distinct relevant parameters under zero-equality constraints; after reduction to zero-equality, the same bound applies without modification.

No step of the induction defining $U_t$ or the state equivalence relation $Q(x,t)$ depends on the specific value $0$ rather than an arbitrary fixed constant, since all reasoning is invariant under uniform bitwise translation by $\alpha_s$.

## Notes

The key observation is that equality constraints in this model are affine-invariant under XOR. This reflects that the computation is carried out over the abelian group $({0,1}^\infty, \oplus)$ at each bit position, so constants can always be normalized away without changing combinatorial structure.
