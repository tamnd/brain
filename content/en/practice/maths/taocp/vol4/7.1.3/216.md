**Exercise 216.** &#9654; [*M26*] [M26] Given n-bit integers x1, . . . , xm ≥0, n ≥λm, compute in O(m) steps the
least y > 0 such that y /∈{a1x1+· · ·+amxm | a1, . . . , am ∈{0, 1}}, if λx takes unit time.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m06s  

---

The greedy argument is correct, so the only issue is to make the linear-time implementation precise under the stated model.

The task is to compute the smallest positive integer not representable as a subset sum of the form

$$
a_1x_1+\cdots+a_mx_m,\quad a_i\in\{0,1\},
$$

where the $x_i$ are $n$-bit integers and $\lambda x$ operations take unit time, with $n \ge \lambda m$.

## Correct solution

Let $S$ denote the set of subset sums of $x_1,\dots,x_m$. We compute the least $y>0$ not in $S$.

### Step 1: Sorting in $O(m)$ time

We first sort $x_1,\dots,x_m$ in nondecreasing order.

Because each $x_i$ is an $n$-bit integer and each operation on an $n$-bit word costs $O(1)$, we are in the standard word-RAM model with word size $\Theta(n)$. The condition $n \ge \lambda m$ implies that a word can store the index range $\{1,\dots,m\}$ and can also serve as a radix digit large enough to encode $m$ distinct buckets.

We use a least-significant-digit radix sort with base $B = 2^{\Theta(\lambda m)}$, chosen so that each digit fits in a constant number of machine words and extraction of a digit is a constant-time bit operation. Since each $x_i$ has at most $n$ bits and $n = O(\lambda m)$, each key has only $O(1)$ such large digits. Each radix pass therefore costs $O(m)$, and the number of passes is $O(1)$. Hence the total sorting time is $O(m)$.

Thus we obtain

$$
x_1 \le x_2 \le \cdots \le x_m
$$

in $O(m)$ time.

### Step 2: Greedy computation of the smallest unattainable sum

Initialize

$$
R \leftarrow 0.
$$

Process the sorted sequence. For each $i=1,\dots,m$:

If $x_i \le R+1$, set

$$
R \leftarrow R + x_i,
$$

otherwise stop and output $R+1$.

Finally output $R+1$.

### Step 3: Correctness

We maintain the invariant:

After processing $i$ elements, every integer in $\{0,1,\dots,R\}$ is representable as a subset sum of $\{x_1,\dots,x_i\}$, and no representable value exceeds $R$ without creating a gap.

Initially $R=0$, and only $0$ is representable, so the invariant holds.

Suppose it holds after $i-1$. If $x_i \le R+1$, then all sums up to $R+x_i$ are achievable: those up to $R$ already are, and those in $[x_i, R+x_i]$ are obtained by adding $x_i$ to all previously achievable sums. Hence $R$ updates to $R+x_i$ and the invariant persists.

If $x_i > R+1$, then no subset containing $x_i$ can produce $R+1$, since such sums are at least $x_i$, and subsets not containing $x_i$ are bounded by $R$. Thus $R+1 \notin S$, while all integers $\le R$ are representable, so $R+1$ is the least missing positive integer.

### Step 4: Running time

Sorting takes $O(m)$ time under the word-RAM radix scheme justified above. The greedy scan performs $O(1)$ work per element, hence $O(m)$ time.

Therefore the total complexity is

$$
O(m).
$$

### Final answer

$$
\boxed{y = R+1}
$$

computed by sorting the $x_i$ in linear time and applying the standard greedy prefix-sum argument.
