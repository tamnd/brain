## Solution

Let the black edges be those of a regular pentagon tiling of the hyperbolic plane, and let the gray lines be the internal subdivision shown in Fig. 14. Each pentagon is partitioned by the gray lines into $10$ triangles.

Each pentagon has $5$ vertices and $5$ edges. The subdivision indicated in the figure assigns exactly two triangular regions to each side of a pentagon, one adjacent to each endpoint of the side. This accounts for $2 \cdot 5 = 10$ triangles per pentagon, consistent with the statement.

Let $V$ denote the set consisting of all original pentagon vertices, all edge midpoints, and all pentagon centers. The gray lines connect only points in $V$ and decompose each pentagon into triangles whose vertices lie in $V$. Each such triangle has one vertex at a pentagon center, one vertex at a pentagon vertex, and one vertex at a midpoint of an incident edge. Every gray segment is one side of exactly two such triangles, one from each adjacent pentagon.

If the black pentagon edges are removed, adjacency between regions is determined solely by the gray lines, so the resulting decomposition consists entirely of triangles with vertices in $V$. Each original pentagon contributes $10$ such triangles, and these triangles glue across shared gray edges without introducing any additional polygonal faces.

The resulting decomposition of the hyperbolic plane is therefore the barycentric subdivision induced by the pentagonal tiling: a uniform triangulation in which every face is a triangle whose vertices alternate among pentagon centers, pentagon vertices, and edge midpoints, with incidence determined exactly by center-vertex-edge adjacency in the original tiling.

This completes the description of the decomposition defined by the gray lines alone. ∎
