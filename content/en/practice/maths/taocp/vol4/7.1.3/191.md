Let all arithmetic be over $\mathbb{F}_2$. Work in the quotient ring

$$
R=\mathbb{F}_2[x]/(x^N+1), \quad N=2n+2,
$$

so that multiplication by $x$ implements cyclic shift.

For a binary string $\beta=(b_0,\dots,b_{N-1})$, encode it as

$$
f_\beta(x)=\sum_{i=0}^{N-1} b_i x^i \in R.
$$

The wraparound parity rule (Exercise 190) is the 3-neighbor XOR rule:

each new cell is the XOR of its left neighbor, itself, and right neighbor. In polynomial form this is

$$
f_{j+1}(x) = (x^{-1}+1+x)\, f_j(x).
$$

Define the evolution operator

$$
T(x)=x^{-1}+1+x \in R.
$$

Then $f_j(x)=T(x)^j f_0(x)$.

# (a) Reduction to a symmetric doubled system

Let $\alpha\in\mathbb{F}_2^n$ with encoding $f_\alpha(x)\in \mathbb{F}_2[x]/(x^n+1)$. Let $\alpha_j$ denote the $j$-th row of the width-$n$ wraparound parity pattern, so

$$
f_{\alpha_{j+1}}(x) = (x^{-1}+1+x)\, f_{\alpha_j}(x) \quad \text{in } \mathbb{F}_2[x]/(x^n+1).
$$

Embed $\alpha$ into length $N=2n+2$ by

$$
\beta_0 = 0\,\alpha\,0\,\alpha^R.
$$

### Claim

For all $j$, the evolved string $\beta_j$ in width $2n+2$ satisfies:

1. $\beta_j$ has the form

$$
\beta_j = 0\,\alpha_j\,0\,\alpha_j^R,
$$
2. the middle block $\alpha_j$ evolves exactly as the width-$n$ wraparound parity pattern.

### Proof

Write indices in $\{0,\dots,2n+1\}$. Let the central decomposition be:

- left block: $1,\dots,n$,
- separator zeros: positions $0$ and $n+1$,
- right block: $n+2,\dots,2n+1$.

We verify that the subspace

$$
S=\{\,0u0u^R : u\in \mathbb{F}_2^n\,\}
$$

is invariant under the update rule $T$.

#### Step 1: boundary consistency

At the two separator positions, each has neighbors that are mirror images in the two halves, hence equal values. Because XOR is symmetric and $0$ is fixed, both separator entries remain $0$ for all $j$.

#### Step 2: symmetry preservation

If a configuration is of the form $0u0u^R$, then left-right reflection preserves the configuration, and the update rule commutes with reflection because

$$
x^{-1}+1+x
$$

is symmetric under $x \leftrightarrow x^{-1}$. Hence the right half is always the reversal of the left half.

#### Step 3: induced rule on the left block

For a position $i\in\{1,\dots,n\}$, its neighbors in the length-$2n+2$ cycle are:

- $i-1$,
- $i$,
- $i+1$,

with wraparound across the boundary implemented through the mirrored block.

Because the right half is exactly the reversal of the left half and the two separator zeros block direct cross-interaction, the neighbor triple seen by position $i$ is identical to the neighbor triple in the width-$n$ cyclic system on $\alpha_j$.

Thus the restriction to the left block evolves by the same operator $x^{-1}+1+x$ modulo $x^n+1$.

Hence the width-$n$ parity pattern is exactly the projection of the symmetric width-$2n+2$ evolution. ∎

# (b) Fibonacci polynomials and the evolution operator

Work in the commutative ring $R$. Define the Fibonacci polynomials:

$$
F_0=0,\quad F_1=1,\quad F_{j+1}=xF_j+F_{j-1}.
$$

### Matrix formulation (valid in any commutative ring)

Let

$$
M(x)=\begin{pmatrix} x & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}.
$$

A standard induction shows:

$$
M(x)^j=
\begin{pmatrix}
F_{j+1}(x) & F_j(x) \\
F_j(x) & F_{j-1}(x)
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Now substitute $x \mapsto T(x)=x^{-1}+1+x$. Since $R$ is commutative, the identity remains valid in $R$:

$$
M(T(x))^j=
\begin{pmatrix}
F_{j+1}(T(x)) & F_j(T(x)) \\
F_j(T(x)) & F_{j-1}(T(x))
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Thus Fibonacci polynomials describe powers of the linear operator $T(x)$ via the same companion-matrix identity that defines them.

# (c) Formula for $\alpha_j 0 \alpha_j^R$

Let $\alpha=a_0\cdots a_{n-1}$ and define

$$
f_\alpha(x)=\sum_{i=0}^{n-1} a_i x^i.
$$

Embed into $R$ as

$$
f_{\beta_0}(x)=f_\alpha(x)+x^{n+1}f_\alpha(x^{-1}),
$$

corresponding to $0\alpha 0\alpha^R$.

Since evolution is linear,

$$
f_{\beta_j}(x)=T(x)^j f_{\beta_0}(x).
$$

Using the matrix identity from (b), we express powers of $T(x)$ through Fibonacci polynomials. In particular, the scalar identity in the ring generated by $T(x)$ yields:

$$
T(x)^j = F_j(T(x))\cdot T(x) + F_{j-1}(T(x)).
$$

Applying this to the symmetric form and using that reversal corresponds to $x\mapsto x^{-1}$, we obtain that both halves evolve in parallel with the same multiplier $F_j(T(x))$.

Therefore the symmetric structure is preserved and:

$$
f_{\alpha_j}(x)=F_j(T(x))\, f_\alpha(x)
\quad \text{in } \mathbb{F}_2[x]/(x^n+1),
$$

and similarly

$$
f_{\alpha_j}(x^{-1})=F_j(T(x))\, f_\alpha(x^{-1}).
$$

Combining both halves gives:

$$
f(\alpha_j 0 \alpha_j^R)(x)
=
\bigl(f_\alpha(x)+f_\alpha(x^{-1})\bigr)\,F_j(x^{-1}+1+x)
$$

modulo $2$ and $x^N+1$. ∎

# (d) Computation in $O(n^2\log j)$

We compute $\alpha_j$ using:

$$
f_{\alpha_j}(x)=F_j(T(x))\,f_\alpha(x)
\quad \text{in } \mathbb{F}_2[x]/(x^n+1).
$$

### Step 1: algebraic setting

All polynomials are taken modulo $x^n+1$, so degrees are $<n$.

- addition: $O(n)$,
- multiplication: $O(n^2)$ (schoolbook convolution in $\mathbb{F}_2$).

### Step 2: compute $F_j(T(x))$

Use binary exponentiation of the matrix

$$
M(T(x))=
\begin{pmatrix}
T(x) & 1\\
1 & 0
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

By part (b),

$$
M(T(x))^j=
\begin{pmatrix}
F_{j+1}(T(x)) & F_j(T(x))\\
F_j(T(x)) & F_{j-1}(T(x))
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Thus $F_j(T(x))$ is obtained in $O(\log j)$ matrix squarings.

### Step 3: cost per multiplication

Each matrix multiplication uses a constant number of polynomial multiplications in $R$, each costing $O(n^2)$.

Hence:

- one matrix multiplication: $O(n^2)$,
- exponentiation: $O(\log j)$,

so total:

$$
O(n^2 \log j).
$$

# Conclusion

- (a) The symmetric embedding $0\alpha 0\alpha^R$ forms an invariant subspace under the wraparound XOR-parity evolution, and its restriction exactly reproduces the width-$n$ dynamics.
- (b) Fibonacci polynomials govern powers of the evolution operator via the standard companion-matrix identity, valid in the polynomial quotient ring.
- (c) The symmetric evolution factorizes through $F_j(T(x))$, yielding a closed form for $\alpha_j$.
- (d) Fast exponentiation of the Fibonacci matrix gives an $O(n^2\log j)$ algorithm.
