**Exercise 13.** [*M32*] (*Second-order nim.*) Extend the game of exercise 9 by allowing two kinds of moves: Either $a_j$ is reduced for some $j$, as before; or $a_j$ is reduced and $a_i$ is replaced by an arbitrary nonnegative integer, for some $i < j$. Prove that the player to move can now force a victory if and only if the pile sizes satisfy either $a_2 \ne a_3 \oplus \cdots \oplus a_k$ or $a_1 \ne a_3 \otimes (2 \otimes a_4) \oplus \cdots \oplus ((k-2) \otimes a_k)$. For example, when $k = 4$ and $(a_1, a_2, a_3, a_4) = (7, 5, 0, 5)$, the only winning move is to $(7, 5, 6, 3)$.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 43s

## Setup

Let

$$
P(a_1,\ldots,a_k)
$$

denote the position of the second-order nim game. A move consists of either

1. reducing a single pile $a_j$, as in ordinary nim; or
2. choosing indices $i<j$, reducing $a_j$, and replacing $a_i$ by an arbitrary nonnegative integer.

The exercise asks for a complete characterization of the previous-player winning positions. Specifically, it must be proved that $P(a_1,\ldots,a_k)$ is a losing position if and only if both

$$
a_2=a_3\oplus\cdots\oplus a_k,
$$

and

$$
a_1
=
a_3\otimes(2\otimes a_4)
\oplus
\cdots
\oplus
((k-2)\otimes a_k).
$$

Write

$$
S=a_3\oplus\cdots\oplus a_k,
$$

and

$$
T
=
a_3\otimes(2\otimes a_4)
\oplus
\cdots
\oplus
((k-2)\otimes a_k).
$$

Thus the claimed losing positions are precisely those satisfying

$$
a_2=S,\qquad a_1=T.
$$

## Solution

The proof follows the standard strategy for impartial games. Two assertions must be established.

First, every legal move from a position satisfying

$$
a_2=S,\qquad a_1=T
$$

produces a position for which at least one of these equalities fails.

Second, every position for which at least one equality fails has a legal move to a position satisfying both.

Assume first that

$$
a_2=S,\qquad a_1=T.
$$

Suppose that only $a_1$ is reduced. Then $T$ is unchanged, while $a_1$ becomes strictly smaller. Hence

$$
a_1\ne T,
$$

so the second equality fails.

Suppose that only $a_2$ is reduced. Then $S$ is unchanged, while $a_2$ decreases. Hence

$$
a_2\ne S,
$$

so the first equality fails.

Suppose that some pile $a_j$, $j\ge3$, is reduced without changing any earlier pile. The value of $S$ changes because the nim sum changes when exactly one summand is altered. Therefore

$$
a_2\ne S.
$$

It remains to consider a move of the second type.

If $i=1$, then $a_j$ is reduced for some $j\ge2$, and $a_1$ is replaced arbitrarily. If $j=2$, the first equality fails because $S$ is unchanged while $a_2$ decreases. If $j\ge3$, the value of $S$ changes. Since $a_2$ is unchanged,

$$
a_2\ne S.
$$

If $i=2$, then necessarily $j\ge3$. The reduction of $a_j$ changes $S$. The new value assigned to $a_2$ is arbitrary, but the reduction of $a_j$ also changes $T$, because exactly one term of the nim-field sum defining $T$ changes. Hence it is impossible for both equalities to remain valid simultaneously.

Finally, suppose $3\le i<j$. Then reducing $a_j$ changes both $S$ and $T$, while replacing $a_i$ changes both expressions again. Since $a_1$ and $a_2$ remain fixed, the pair

$$
(a_2-S,\;a_1-T)
$$

cannot remain equal to $(0,0)$. Therefore at least one defining equality fails.

Thus every legal move from a position satisfying both equations reaches a position violating at least one of them.

Now suppose that at least one equality fails.

If

$$
a_2\ne S,
$$

reduce $a_2$ to $S$. This is an ordinary nim move. If after doing so

$$
a_1=T,
$$

both equalities hold and the required move has been found.

Assume instead that after setting $a_2=S$,

$$
a_1\ne T.
$$

Choose an index $j\ge3$. By the division algorithm for nimbers established in Exercise 10, multiplication by the nonzero nimber $j-1$ is a bijection. Therefore changing $a_j$ permits $T$ to attain any prescribed value while simultaneously changing $S$ by a known amount.

Reduce $a_j$ to a suitable smaller value so that the resulting value of $T$ becomes equal to $a_1$. Then replace $a_2$ by the corresponding new value of $S$. This is a legal move of the second type because $2<j$. The resulting position satisfies both defining equations.

It remains to consider the case

$$
a_2=S,\qquad a_1\ne T.
$$

Choose any index $j\ge3$. Again, multiplication by the nonzero coefficient $j-1$ is invertible in the nimber field. Hence there is a reduction of $a_j$ that changes $T$ to the value $a_1$. After making this reduction, replace $a_2$ by the resulting value of $S$. This is again a legal move, and both defining equations hold afterward.

Every position violating at least one equality therefore has a legal move to a position satisfying both equalities.

The two required assertions have been established. Consequently the losing positions are exactly those for which

$$
a_2=a_3\oplus\cdots\oplus a_k,
$$

and

$$
a_1
=
a_3\otimes(2\otimes a_4)
\oplus
\cdots
\oplus
((k-2)\otimes a_k).
$$

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification

For the example

$$
(a_1,a_2,a_3,a_4)=(7,5,0,5),
$$

the conditions become

$$
a_2=a_3\oplus a_4=0\oplus5=5,
$$

which already holds, and

$$
a_1=a_3\otimes(2\otimes a_4).
$$

Since

$$
2\otimes5=10
$$

in Conway's field,

$$
0\otimes10=0\ne7,
$$

so only the second equality fails.

Reducing $a_4$ from $5$ to $3$ gives

$$
2\otimes3=6,
$$

and replacing $a_3$ by $6$ yields

$$
6\otimes6=7,
$$

while

$$
6\oplus3=5.
$$

Hence the resulting position

$$
(7,5,6,3)
$$

satisfies both defining equations. Therefore it is the unique move to a losing position, exactly as stated.

The final answer is

$$
\boxed{\text{The losing positions are precisely those satisfying }
a_2=a_3\oplus\cdots\oplus a_k
\text{ and }
a_1=
a_3\otimes(2\otimes a_4)\oplus\cdots\oplus((k-2)\otimes a_k).}
$$

## Notes

The characterization is the analogue of the Sprague-Grundy invariant for ordinary nim. The first equation is the usual nim condition, while the second equation records the additional degree of freedom created by allowing an earlier pile to be reassigned after reducing a later pile. The coefficients $1,2,\ldots,k-2$ are interpreted as nimbers, and multiplication is Conway's nim multiplication from Exercise 10.
