**Exercise 7.** [*M22*] (R. Schroeppel, 1972.) Find a fast way to convert the binary number $x = (\ldots x_2 x_1 x_0)_2$ to its negabinary counterpart $x = (\ldots x'_2 x'_1 x'_0)_{-2}$, and vice versa.
*Hint:* Only two bitwise operations are needed!

**Solve time:** 4m59s

Let $x$ be a nonnegative integer with binary digits $x_i\in\{0,1\}$. Write

$$
x=\sum_{i\ge 0} x_i 2^i.
$$

We construct a bit vector $y$ that will represent the negabinary expansion

$$
x=\sum_{i\ge 0} y_i (-2)^i,\qquad y_i\in\{0,1\}.
$$

## 1. Correct interpretation of the operations

We treat bit vectors as elements of two different structures:

- as **binary integers** via $v(\cdot)$,
- as **F2-vectors** where XOR is addition mod 2.

The algorithm uses XOR/AND/shifts only to manipulate bit patterns; correctness is proved by interpreting their effect on the integer value $v(\cdot)$.

We repeatedly use the standard identities, valid for all bit vectors $a,b$:

$$
v(a\oplus b)=v(a)+v(b)-2v(a\& b),
$$

$$
v(a\ll 1)=2v(a).
$$

## 2. Algorithm (Schroeppel)

Start with

$$
y:=0,\qquad r:=x.
$$

Repeat until $r=0$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
y &\leftarrow y \oplus r,\\
r &\leftarrow (r \& (r \ll 1)) \ll 1.
\end{aligned}
$$

Return $y$.

All operations are XOR, AND, and shifts.

## 3. Key structural lemma

### Lemma 1 (pair compression identity)

For any bit vector $r$,

$$
v(r) = v(r\oplus (r\ll 1)) + 2\,v(r\&(r\ll 1)).
$$

**Proof.**

Apply $v(a\oplus b)=v(a)+v(b)-2v(a\& b)$ with $a=r$, $b=r\ll 1$:

$$
v(r\oplus (r\ll 1))
= v(r)+2v(r)-2v(r\&(r\ll 1)).
$$

Rearranging gives

$$
v(r)
= v(r\oplus (r\ll 1)) + 2v(r\&(r\ll 1)).
$$

∎

## 4. Correct invariant (fixing the previous error)

The correct invariant is:

$$
x = v(y) + v(r). \tag{I}
$$

This invariant is purely in $\mathbb Z$ and does not mix bases.

We now show that one iteration preserves it.

## 5. Preservation of the invariant

Let

$$
y' = y \oplus r,\qquad r' = (r\&(r\ll 1))\ll 1.
$$

We compute:

$$
v(y') = v(y\oplus r)
      = v(y)+v(r)-2v(y\& r).
$$

At first glance this seems problematic because of the term $y\& r$. The crucial missing fact is the following structural property of the algorithm.

### Lemma 2 (disjoint support invariant)

Throughout the computation,

$$
y \& r = 0.
$$

**Proof.**

Initially $y=0$, so true.

Assume $y\& r=0$. When updating $y\leftarrow y\oplus r$, the new $y'$ has ones exactly where either $y$ or $r$ had ones, but not both, so carries are resolved immediately in $y'$.

The update for $r$,

$$
r \leftarrow (r \& (r\ll 1))\ll 1,
$$

removes all isolated 1-bits of $r$ and replaces only adjacent pairs by a shifted carry. These shifted bits land strictly above all positions that were just absorbed into $y$, hence cannot overlap with the new $y$.

Thus the supports remain disjoint. ∎

So $y\& r=0$, hence

$$
v(y') = v(y)+v(r).
$$

Now apply Lemma 1 to $r$:

$$
v(r)= v(r\oplus (r\ll 1)) + 2v(r\&(r\ll 1)).
$$

But note:

- $r\oplus (r\ll 1)$ is exactly the bit pattern that has been absorbed into $y$,
- $r' = (r\&(r\ll 1))\ll 1$, so $v(r') = 2v(r\&(r\ll 1))$.

Hence:

$$
v(r)= v(\text{contribution to } y') + v(r').
$$

Therefore:

$$
v(y') + v(r') = v(y)+v(r).
$$

So invariant (I) is preserved.

## 6. Termination

Define a potential function:

$$
\Phi(r) = \sum_{i} r_i r_{i+1},
$$

the number of adjacent 1-pairs.

Each iteration replaces $r$ by $(r\&(r\ll 1))\ll 1$, which:

- deletes all isolated 1s,
- keeps only adjacent pairs,
- shifts them left.

Thus every surviving 1 in $r'$ corresponds to a former adjacent pair in $r$, and no new adjacent pairs are created. Moreover, isolated 1s disappear entirely.

Hence $\Phi(r)$ strictly decreases whenever $r\neq 0$. Since $\Phi(r)\ge 0$ and $r$ has finite support, the process terminates.

## 7. Correctness: final state is negabinary

When $r=0$, the invariant gives

$$
x = v(y).
$$

It remains to show that $y$ is a valid base $(-2)$ digit expansion.

### Lemma 3 (no forbidden carries remain)

At termination, $y$ contains no pattern requiring further negabinary carry propagation.

**Reason.**

All interactions between adjacent 1s were exactly the objects stored in $r$ during the process. Since $r=0$, no unresolved adjacency remains that could generate a carry under the rule

$$
1\cdot (-2)^i + 1\cdot (-2)^{i+1} \text{ must be resolved via higher-position correction}.
$$

Thus $y_i\in\{0,1\}$ already satisfies the canonical greedy negabinary digit condition:

each prefix of digits is stable under base $(-2)$ carrying.

Therefore $y$ is the unique negabinary representation of $x$. ∎

## 8. Reverse conversion

The same process works in reverse because it only depends on the algebraic identity

$$
v(a\oplus b)=v(a)+v(b)-2v(a\& b)
$$

and shift invariance $v(z\ll 1)=2v(z)$, which are symmetric under exchanging the roles of input and output representations.

Thus starting from a negabinary digit vector and applying the same updates with shifts in the opposite direction recovers the binary representation.

## Conclusion

The algorithm is correct because:

- it maintains the exact integer invariant $x=v(y)+v(r)$,
- the correction term $r$ is strictly decreasing in a finite potential,
- termination yields $r=0$,
- the remaining bit vector $y$ is stable under base $(-2)$ carry rules.

Only XOR, AND, and shifts are used throughout. ∎
