Let the eight neighbors of $X$ be denoted $X_{NW}, X_N, X_{NE}, X_W, X_E, X_{SW}, X_S, X_{SE}$.

The 1957 definition is

$$
custer(X)= X \,\&\, \neg\bigl(X_{NW}\,\&\,X_N\,\&\,X_{NE}\,\&\,X_W\,\&\,X_E\,\&\,X_{SW}\,\&\,X_S\,\&\,X_{SE}\bigr).
$$

The proposed alternative is

$$
custer'(X)= X \,\&\, \neg(X_{NW}\,\&\,X_N\,\&\,\cdots\,\&\,X_{SE}).
$$

Applying De Morgan’s law gives

$$
custer'(X)
= X \,\&\, (\neg X_{NW} \,\mid\, \neg X_N \,\mid\, \neg X_{NE} \,\mid\, \neg X_W \,\mid\, \neg X_E \,\mid\, \neg X_{SW} \,\mid\, \neg X_S \,\mid\, \neg X_{SE}).
$$

Expression (157) is this De Morgan form.

Now compare this with the original 1957 formulation. The original form computes a single intermediate value

$$
A = X_{NW}\,\&\,X_N\,\&\,\cdots\,\&\,X_{SE},
\quad custer(X)= X \,\&\, \neg A.
$$

The alternative (157) removes this 8-way conjunction and replaces it with an 8-way disjunction of local complements.

The key difference is structural, not algebraic.

First, the original form forces a global 8-input conjunction across all neighbor words. In a word-at-a-time implementation this is a multi-operand reduction whose result is meaningful only after all eight operands are available simultaneously. This ties the computation to a single wide AND reduction stage producing $A$ as a full intermediate mask.

Second, in (157) each neighbor contributes independently through $\neg X_\ast$. These terms are local transformations of individual data fields and can be formed without combining all neighbors first. The aggregation step is then a single OR-reduction of precomputed values:

$$
\neg X_{NW} \mid \cdots \mid \neg X_{SE}.
$$

This separation improves modularity in a bitwise stencil computation: the negations of neighbor fields are reusable quantities. In typical applications of bitwise image or grid transformations, the same neighbor complements are often useful in multiple rules, whereas the composite value $A$ from the original definition is specific to this single predicate and has no reuse value.

Third, the OR-of-complements structure is more natural for incremental and parallel evaluation. The condition “at least one neighbor bit is 0” is expressed directly as a disjunction of simple tests, while the original form encodes the equivalent condition indirectly through a full conjunction followed by negation. In bitwise algorithm design, direct representation of local failure conditions as an OR of simple masks is generally preferred because it aligns with how word-level masks are combined and reused.

Thus (157) is preferable because it replaces a single global 8-way conjunction embedded under a negation with a decomposition into eight independent local complements combined by a single associative OR, improving structural locality and reuse of intermediate results in word-level bitwise computation, while preserving equivalence by De Morgan’s law.
