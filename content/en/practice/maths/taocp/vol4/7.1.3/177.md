## Solution

Let $W$ denote the set of white pixels of the given bitmap $X$, and let $G_W$ be the graph whose vertices are elements of $W$, with $u - v$ when $u$ and $v$ are king-move apart. Let $W'$ denote the set of white pixels after the shrinking transformation (161), and let $G'_W$ be the corresponding graph on $W'$.

Apply the shrinking transformation (161) to the full bitmap, ignoring color. Each pixel is processed by a fixed local rule depending only on its $N(i,j)$-neighborhood, so the transformation commutes with exchanging black and white pixels. If $X^c$ denotes the bitmap obtained from $X$ by interchanging black and white pixels, then applying (161) to $X^c$ produces exactly the bitmap $(X')^c$ obtained from $X'$ by the same color interchange.

Thus the graph $G_W$ of white pixels in $X$ is identical to the graph $G^c_B$ of black pixels in $X^c$, and $G'_W$ is identical to $(G^c)'_B$, the black-pixel graph after shrinking applied to $X^c$.

Exercise 176 establishes that for any bitmap, the number of connected components of the black-pixel graph after shrinking equals the number of components of the original black-pixel graph minus the number of isolated vertices of the original graph.

Applying this statement to $X^c$ yields that the number of connected components of $(G^c)'_B$ equals the number of components of $G^c_B$ minus the number of isolated vertices of $G^c_B$.

Since $G_W = G^c_B$ and $G'_W = (G^c)'_B$, this identity translates directly into the statement that the number of connected components of $G'_W$ equals the number of connected components of $G_W$ minus the number of isolated vertices of $G_W$.

This completes the proof. ∎
