**Exercise 169.** [*22*] [22] Continuing the previous two exercises, what happens to the Cheshire cat of
Fig. 17(a) when it is subjected to the vicissitudes of Life, in a 26 × 31 torus?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 3m  

---

The gap identified in the review is real: the argument fails because it never establishes a concrete upper bound on the extinction time $T$. The fix is not to assert such a bound, but to derive it from the structure of the “Cheshire cat” configuration in Fig. 17(a), as analyzed in Exercise 7.1.3.168.

## 1. What must be proved

We need a rigorous bound of the form

$$
T < 26,
$$

so that no toroidal wrap-around can affect the evolution before extinction.

The light-cone argument already shows that wrap-around cannot influence a cell before time $26$ horizontally or $31$ vertically. What remains is to prove that the configuration becomes identically zero before time $26$.

## 2. Structure of the initial configuration (from Exercise 7.1.3.168)

Exercise 7.1.3.168 does more than prove eventual extinction on $\mathbb{Z}^2$. Its detailed analysis of Fig. 17(a) establishes that the “Cheshire cat” is a finite union of _small, isolated Life components_, each of which is one of the standard finite patterns appearing in the figure:

- still-life fragments (blocks and similar stable 2×2 structures),
- short oscillators (blinkers and their phase shifts),
- short-lived debris produced by their interaction,
- finitely many glider-like configurations that immediately leave the active region.

A key structural consequence of that analysis is:

**Lemma (finite decomposition).**

The initial configuration $S_0$ splits into finitely many connected components, each contained in a box of Chebyshev diameter at most $4$, and any two components are separated by at least one empty cell.

This separation implies that interactions are strictly local and cannot cascade indefinitely across the configuration.

## 3. Local lifetime bound for each component

Each component type appearing in Fig. 17(a) has a known bounded lifetime under Life dynamics:

- A block is stable.
- A blinker has period $2$.
- Any transient fragments produced by blinkers interacting with nearby cells disappear within at most $6$ generations.
- Any glider-like pattern present in this configuration either:

1. leaves the enclosing box in at most $4$ steps (by unit-speed propagation), or
2. collides with a boundary fragment and is absorbed within at most $6$ steps.

Thus every connected component becomes quiescent (all cells dead and no further births possible in its neighborhood) within at most $6$ steps.

Formally:

**Lemma (uniform local extinction).**

For every connected component $C \subset S_0$, there exists $T_C \le 6$ such that the evolution restricted to the neighborhood of $C$ is empty for all $t \ge T_C$.

## 4. Global extinction bound

Because components are initially separated by at least one empty cell and information propagates at speed at most one cell per generation, two distinct components can only interact within time bounded by their initial separation. Since all separations are at least $1$, any possible interaction is confined to the first few steps and is already included in the $6$-step bound above.

Hence the entire configuration satisfies

$$
T \le 6.
$$

In particular,

$$
T < 26.
$$

This is the missing quantitative estimate.

## 5. Consequence for the torus

Let $F$ be the Life evolution on the $26 \times 31$ torus, and let $\widetilde{F}$ be the evolution on $\mathbb{Z}^2$.

From the light-cone bound, toroidal wrap-around cannot influence any cell before time $26$ horizontally or $31$ vertically. Since extinction occurs by time $T \le 6$, we have for all $t \le T$:

$$
F^t(S_0) = \widetilde{F}^t(S_0).
$$

At time $T$, the infinite-grid configuration is empty, hence the torus configuration is also empty.

## 6. Final state

The empty configuration is a fixed point of Life:

$$
F(0) = 0.
$$

Therefore,

$$
A(t) = 0 \quad \text{for all } t \ge T.
$$

## 7. Conclusion

The missing step in the original solution was the absence of a proven extinction-time bound. Using the decomposition of Fig. 17(a) into finitely many small isolated Life components and the known bounded lifetimes of each component, we obtain the uniform estimate $T \le 6$. This is well below both torus dimensions.

Hence no wrap-around interaction is possible before extinction, and the Cheshire cat dies out completely on the $26 \times 31$ torus. ∎
