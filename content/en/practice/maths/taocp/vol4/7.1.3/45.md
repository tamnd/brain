**Exercise 45.** &#9654; [*20*] (T. Rokicki, 1999.) Explain how to test if $x^{R_c} \mathbin{?} y^R$, without reversing $x$ and $y$.

The previous argument fails because it does not anchor the meaning of “?” in a precise model of word comparison, and therefore cannot justify that the simulated access preserves the actual evaluation order of the relation.

We restart from the definition that is standard in TAOCP contexts for this section: “?” denotes lexicographic comparison on words of equal length over an ordered alphabet (here binary words), with the most significant position first. That is, for words $u,v \in \{0,1\}^n$,

$$
u \;?\; v
$$

is determined by the first index $i$ (starting from $0$ as least significant or, equivalently, from $n-1$ as most significant depending on convention fixed by the section) where $u_i \neq v_i$, and the result depends only on that position. In TAOCP’s standard binary word interpretation, comparison is prefix-determined in a fixed linear order of positions, so any correct algorithm must reproduce that exact sequence of inspected positions.

We adopt the consistent convention used in such comparisons: evaluation proceeds from most significant to least significant bit, i.e., from index $n-1$ down to $0$. The correctness of the method depends on matching this order exactly.

## Step 1: eliminate the incorrect “forward streaming” model

The previous solution incorrectly iterated $i = 0,1,\dots,n-1$. This reverses the comparison order and therefore does not correspond to the semantics of lexicographic comparison. The correct simulation must iterate in the same order in which “?” examines symbols.

Thus the comparison must proceed over indices:

$$
i = n-1, n-2, \dots, 0.
$$

At each step we must be able to compute the corresponding symbols of $x^{R_c}$ and $y^R$ without materializing either word.

## Step 2: correct on-the-fly access to transformed words

Assume $n$ is a multiple of $c$. Write any index $i$ uniquely as

$$
i = bc + t, \quad 0 \le t < c.
$$

### Access to $x^{R_c}$

By definition of blockwise reversal,

$$
(x^{R_c})_{bc+t} = x_{bc + (c-1-t)}.
$$

Thus we can compute any symbol of $x^{R_c}$ in $O(1)$ time from $x$ without constructing the transformed word.

### Access to $y^R$

Full reversal satisfies

$$
(y^R)_i = y_{n-1-i}.
$$

Again, this is constant-time access.

## Step 3: correct simulation of the comparison

We now simulate lexicographic comparison exactly in the order it is defined.

For $i = n-1$ down to $0$:

1. Compute

$$
a_i = (x^{R_c})_i = x_{bc + (c-1-t)}, \quad i = bc+t.
$$
2. Compute

$$
b_i = (y^R)_i = y_{n-1-i}.
$$
3. If $a_i \neq b_i$, terminate immediately and return the result of comparing $a_i$ and $b_i$.
4. Otherwise continue.

If no difference is found, the words are equal.

## Step 4: correctness justification

The key point is that this procedure reproduces exactly the sequence of symbol comparisons that would occur if $x^{R_c}$ and $y^R$ were explicitly constructed.

This holds for two reasons:

1. **Exact correspondence of inspected positions.**

At each step $i$, the algorithm computes precisely $(x^{R_c})_i$ and $(y^R)_i$. No reordering is introduced beyond the fixed, correct index mappings.
2. **Prefix-determined nature of lexicographic comparison.**

Lexicographic comparison depends only on the first position (in most-significant-first order) where the two words differ. Since the algorithm inspects positions in exactly that order and evaluates the correct symbols at each position, the first detected mismatch is identical to that of the fully materialized words.

Therefore, early termination behavior is preserved: the algorithm stops at the same index where $x^{R_c}$ and $y^R$ would first differ.

## Step 5: conclusion

To test $x^{R_c} \;?\; y^R$ without reversing either word, we simulate the lexicographic comparison directly in the required order $n-1 \to 0$, computing symbols on demand via

$$
(x^{R_c})_{bc+t} = x_{bc+(c-1-t)}, \quad (y^R)_i = y_{n-1-i}.
$$

This yields an $O(n)$-time, $O(1)$-extra-space procedure that is exactly equivalent to comparing the explicitly reversed words, while avoiding all reversal operations.
