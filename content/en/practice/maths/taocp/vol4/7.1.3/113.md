The previous proof fails because it tries to _simulate_ 2-adic addition algorithmically via bounded carry propagation. That is unnecessary and incorrect. The correct approach is to use the fact that a broadword chain already consists of operations that are intrinsically well-defined on 2-adic integers.

## Corrected solution

Let $(x_0, x_1, \dots, x_r)$ be a broadword chain of width $n$. Each $x_i$ is obtained from earlier terms using one of the standard broadword operations: bitwise Boolean operations, shifts, addition, or subtraction, interpreted on $n$-bit words modulo $2^n$.

We construct the new chain by taking it unchanged:

$$
x'_i = x_i \quad \text{for all } 0 \le i \le r,
$$

so $r' = r$.

It remains to show that this chain is a strong broadword chain.

## Step 1: Interpreting operations in $\mathbb{Z}_2$

Each broadword operation extends naturally to the ring of 2-adic integers $\mathbb{Z}_2$:

- Bitwise operations $\&, \,|,\, \oplus$ act coordinatewise on binary expansions and therefore define continuous maps $\mathbb{Z}_2^k \to \mathbb{Z}_2$.
- Shifts correspond to multiplication or division by powers of 2 (the right shift is well-defined as a 2-adic operation on appropriate representatives).
- Addition and subtraction on $n$-bit words are reduction modulo $2^n$, but the same formulas define the standard 2-adic addition and subtraction on $\mathbb{Z}_2$. The difference is that 2-adic addition allows infinite carry propagation, which is well-defined as a limit and does not require termination.

Thus every primitive operation used in the chain defines a valid function on $\mathbb{Z}_2$, independent of truncation.

## Step 2: Validity of each step as a 2-adic chain

Each step of the original chain has the form

$$
x_i = f_i(x_{a_1}, \dots, x_{a_k})
$$

where $f_i$ is one of the allowed broadword operations.

Since each $f_i$ is also a well-defined operation on $\mathbb{Z}_2$, the same defining equations hold when all variables are interpreted as 2-adic integers. Therefore the sequence $(x'_i)$ satisfies exactly the same recurrence relations in $\mathbb{Z}_2$, making it a valid 2-adic chain.

No simulation of carry propagation is required, and no bounded termination argument is needed. The operations are defined algebraically, not procedurally.

## Step 3: Preservation of subsequence

Since we did not modify the sequence,

$$
(x_0, x_1, \dots, x_r)
$$

is trivially a subsequence of

$$
(x'_0, x'_1, \dots, x'_{r'}).
$$

## Step 4: Strongness

A strong broadword chain requires that the chain be a valid 2-adic chain for all $n$-bit choices of $x_0$.

Because the construction above interprets the same algebraic expressions in $\mathbb{Z}_2$, every choice of initial $x_0 \in \{0, \dots, 2^n-1\} \subset \mathbb{Z}_2$ produces a valid execution of the chain in $\mathbb{Z}_2$.

Hence the chain is strong.

## Conclusion

Taking $x'_i = x_i$ yields a chain of the same length with the original chain as a subsequence. All operations extend canonically to 2-adic integers, so the chain is simultaneously a broadword chain of width $n$ and a valid 2-adic chain.

Therefore a strong broadword chain is obtained with $r' = r = O(r)$.
