### (a) Cycle lengths are powers of 2 for shift-free broadword functions

Let $f : [0,2^n) \to [0,2^n)$ be an $n$-bit broadword function built from addition, subtraction, multiplication modulo $2^n$, and bitwise Boolean operations, with no shift instructions. Consider the iteration $x_{t+1} = f(x_t)$.

By finiteness, every orbit is eventually periodic. We prove that every cycle length is a power of $2$.

#### Step 1. Compatibility with reduction modulo $2^k$

Because no shift operations are present, every primitive operation is _bit-position local_: each output bit depends only on input bits at the same or lower significance through carries, but never moves information between bit positions.

Formally, if $x \equiv y \pmod{2^k}$, then for each allowed operation $\circ$,

$$
x \circ z \equiv y \circ z \pmod{2^k}, \quad z \circ x \equiv z \circ y \pmod{2^k},
$$

and similarly for bitwise Boolean operations. Hence congruence modulo $2^k$ is preserved by every primitive operation and therefore by $f$.

Thus $f$ induces a well-defined map

$$
f_k : \mathbb{Z}/2^k\mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z}/2^k\mathbb{Z}, \quad f_k(x \bmod 2^k) = f(x) \bmod 2^k.
$$

#### Step 2. Induction hypothesis

We prove by induction on $k$ that every cycle length of $f_k$ is a power of $2$.

For $k=1$, the state space has two elements, so every cycle has length $1$ or $2$, hence a power of $2$.

Assume the claim holds for $k-1$. Let $C$ be a cycle of $f_k$ of length $T$, and let $\pi$ be reduction modulo $2^{k-1}$. Then $\pi(C)$ is a cycle of $f_{k-1}$, so its length is $2^a$ for some $a$. Hence $T$ is a multiple of $2^a$, so write

$$
T = 2^a s.
$$

#### Step 3. Structure of the lifted dynamics

Define

$$
g = f_k^{\,2^a}.
$$

Then for any $x \in C$,

$$
g(x) \equiv x \pmod{2^{k-1}}.
$$

Thus $g$ maps each fiber

$$
\{x, x + 2^{k-1}\}
$$

into itself, so on each fiber it is a permutation of a set of size $2$. Therefore the restriction of $g$ to any fiber has cycle length $1$ or $2$.

#### Step 4. Bounding the full cycle length

Since $g = f_k^{2^a}$, we have

$$
f_k^{2^a s}(x) = x.
$$

Minimality of $T$ implies $s$ is the minimal positive integer such that $g^s(x)=x$. Because $g$ acts on a 2-element set, this minimal $s$ is either $1$ or $2$. Hence

$$
T = 2^a \quad \text{or} \quad T = 2^{a+1}.
$$

In either case, $T$ is a power of $2$.

#### Step 5. Conclusion

By induction, every cycle length of $f_n = f$ is a power of $2$. ∎

### (b) A length-3 broadword chain realizing period $p$

Fix $p \in \{1,\dots,n\}$. Let

$$
M = 2^p - 1,
$$

so that $M$ selects the lowest $p$ bits.

We construct an $n$-bit broadword function using three sequential broadword steps whose dynamics contains a cycle of length $p$.

#### Step 1. Define the three-step chain

Given $x \in [0,2^n)$, define:

1. **Masking step**

$$
x_1 = x \,\&\, M.
$$

1. **Shift and wrap step (working only on low $p$ bits)**

$$
x_2 = ((x_1 \ll 1) \;\&\; M)\; \mid\; \big((x_1 \gg (p-1)) \;\&\; 1\big).
$$

1. **Final cleanup step**

$$
x_3 = x_2.
$$

We define $f(x) = x_3$. This is a composition of three broadword steps (AND, shift, shift+OR), hence a chain of length $3$.

#### Step 2. Invariant subspace

Let

$$
S = \{x : x \& \sim M = 0\},
$$

the set of words whose only nonzero bits lie in the lowest $p$ positions.

For every $x \in S$, step 1 ensures $x_1 = x$. Step 2 produces a value still in $S$ because the shift is masked by $M$, and the wrapped bit is inserted only into the lowest $p$ bits. Hence $S$ is forward-invariant under $f$.

Thus the dynamics of $f$ restricted to $S$ is exactly a cyclic left shift on $p$-bit words.

#### Step 3. Existence of a $p$-cycle

Consider the state

$$
x_0 = 1.
$$

Inside the invariant subsystem $S$, this corresponds to a single $1$ in the least significant bit.

Under $f$, the update is:

$$
(b_{p-1}\dots b_1 b_0) \mapsto (b_{p-2}\dots b_0 b_{p-1}),
$$

a cyclic rotation of $p$ bits.

Therefore the orbit of $x_0$ has length exactly $p$, since the single $1$-bit returns to its original position only after $p$ rotations.

#### Step 4. Conclusion

For every $p \le n$, there exists an $n$-bit broadword chain of length $3$ whose induced function has a cycle of length $p$. ∎
