**Exercise 21.** [*22*] [22] Construct the *reverse* of Gosper's hack: Show how to compute $x$ from $y$.

Start from a correct form of Gosper’s hack and analyze its bit structure.

## 1. Correct forward construction

Let $x \ge 0$. Define

$$
c = x \,\&\, (-x),
$$

the least significant 1-bit of $x$. Write $c = 2^k$, where $k = \operatorname{ctz}(x)$.

Then

$$
r = x + c.
$$

This addition clears the bit at position $k$ in $x$ and produces a carry that propagates through the contiguous block of 1s immediately above it. As a consequence:

- bits below position $k$ in $r$ are all $0$,
- bit $k$ in $r$ is $1$,
- $r$ agrees with $x$ above the carried region.

Now define the displacement term

$$
s = \frac{r \oplus x}{c} \gg 2.
$$

This is well-defined because $r \oplus x$ is supported exactly on the finite region affected by the carry, and division by $c=2^k$ shifts that region down so that bit position $k$ becomes position $0$. The additional shift by $2$ moves this encoded block to positions starting at $k+2$.

Finally,

$$
y = r \;|\; (s \ll (k+2)).
$$

This shows the essential structure:

- $r$ occupies all bit positions below $k+2$,
- the extra term $s$ occupies only positions $\ge k+2$,
- hence the two parts do not interfere.

## 2. Key structural facts for inversion

From the forward construction:

1. $c = 2^k$, where $k = \operatorname{ctz}(x)$.
2. $r = x + 2^k$, hence:

- bits $0,1,\dots,k-1$ of $r$ are all $0$,
- bit $k$ of $r$ is $1$.
3. The extra term is placed entirely at positions $\ge k+2$.

Therefore:

$$
r = y \bmod 2^{k+2}.
$$

So once $k$ is known, $r$ is the lower $k+2$ bits of $y$, and then $x = r - 2^k$.

The only missing ingredient is how to recover $k$ from $y$.

## 3. Recovering $k$ from $y$

Observe that in $r = x + 2^k$, all bits below $k$ are zero, and bit $k$ is the lowest set bit of $r$.

Since the correction term occupies only bits $\ge k+2$, it cannot affect any bit position below $k+2$. Hence the lowest set bit of $y$ is exactly at position $k$. Therefore:

$$
k = \operatorname{ctz}(y),
\qquad c = y \,\&\, (-y).
$$

This is the crucial recovery step that was missing in the previous attempt.

## 4. Reconstruction of $r$

Let

$$
k = \operatorname{ctz}(y).
$$

Since all contributions from the shifted term lie at positions $\ge k+2$, the lower $k+2$ bits of $y$ come entirely from $r$. Thus:

$$
r = y \bmod 2^{k+2} = y \,\&\, \bigl((1 \ll (k+2)) - 1\bigr).
$$

This uniquely determines $r$, because no higher-bit interaction occurs with the correction term.

## 5. Recovering $x$

From the forward relation $r = x + 2^k$, we immediately obtain:

$$
x = r - 2^k.
$$

Substituting $c = 2^k$, this is:

$$
x = r - c.
$$

## 6. Final inverse algorithm

Given $y$:

1. Compute

$$
k = \operatorname{ctz}(y), \quad c = 1 \ll k.
$$
2. Recover

$$
r = y \,\&\, \bigl((1 \ll (k+2)) - 1\bigr).
$$
3. Compute

$$
x = r - c.
$$

## 7. Correctness argument

- The decomposition $y = r \,|\, (s \ll (k+2))$ ensures that all bits below $k+2$ belong exclusively to $r$.
- The structure of $r = x + 2^k$ guarantees that its lowest set bit is exactly at position $k$, so $k = \operatorname{ctz}(y)$.
- Once $k$ is known, both $c$ and $r$ are uniquely determined.
- Finally, subtracting $c$ inverts the carry construction.

Thus the reverse of Gosper’s hack is correctly obtained by extracting the trailing-zero position of $y$, reconstructing the truncated intermediate value $r$, and undoing the single-bit increment.
