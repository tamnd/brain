[Section 7.1.3: Bitwise Tricks and Techniques](../)

**Exercise 206.** [*20*] [20] The perfect shuffle (175) is sometimes called an “outshuffle,” by comparison
with the “inshuffle” that takes z →
y ‡ x = (y31x31 . . . y1x1y0x0)2; the outshuffle
preserves the leftmost and rightmost bits of z, but the inshuffle has no fixed points.
Can an inshuffle be performed as efficiently as an outshuffle?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m59s  

---

A clean way to repair the argument is to fix a single formal model for all permutations and avoid applying the half-swap map to “positions” informally. Once this is done, the correct relationship turns out to be simpler than the flawed conjugation claim.

## 1. Model: bit positions as the underlying set

Work on the index set

$$
\{0,1,\dots,63\}.
$$

Write a 64-bit word $z$ as

$$
z = (a_{31}\dots a_0\, b_{31}\dots b_0),
$$

meaning:

- position $k$, $0 \le k \le 31$, holds $b_k$,
- position $32+k$, $0 \le k \le 31$, holds $a_k$.

Thus:

$$
b_k \leftrightarrow k, \qquad a_k \leftrightarrow 32+k.
$$

All shuffles are permutations of this same index set.

## 2. Define the half-swap permutation correctly

Define $\tau$ as a permutation of indices:

$$
\tau(k) =
\begin{cases}
k+32, & 0 \le k \le 31,\\
k-32, & 32 \le k \le 63.
\end{cases}
$$

This is a genuine permutation of $\{0,\dots,63\}$ and satisfies $\tau^2 = \mathrm{id}$.

It swaps the two halves of the word.

## 3. Define outshuffle and inshuffle as index permutations

### Outshuffle $\sigma_{\mathrm{out}}$

$$
\sigma_{\mathrm{out}}(k)=2k \quad (0 \le k \le 31),
$$

$$
\sigma_{\mathrm{out}}(32+k)=2k+1 \quad (0 \le k \le 31).
$$

This yields

$$
(b_0,a_0,b_1,a_1,\dots,b_{31},a_{31}).
$$

### Inshuffle $\sigma_{\mathrm{in}}$

$$
\sigma_{\mathrm{in}}(k)=2k+1 \quad (0 \le k \le 31),
$$

$$
\sigma_{\mathrm{in}}(32+k)=2k \quad (0 \le k \le 31).
$$

This yields

$$
(a_0,b_0,a_1,b_1,\dots,a_{31},b_{31}).
$$

## 4. Correct relationship between inshuffle and outshuffle

We compute $\sigma_{\mathrm{out}} \circ \tau$.

### Case 1: $k \in \{0,\dots,31\}$ (originally $b_k$)

$$
\tau(k)=k+32,
$$

$$
\sigma_{\mathrm{out}}(\tau(k))=\sigma_{\mathrm{out}}(32+k)=2k+1.
$$

So:

$$
(\sigma_{\mathrm{out}} \circ \tau)(k)=2k+1.
$$

This matches $\sigma_{\mathrm{in}}(k)$.

### Case 2: $k = 32+i$ (originally $a_i$)

$$
\tau(32+i)=i,
$$

$$
\sigma_{\mathrm{out}}(\tau(32+i))=\sigma_{\mathrm{out}}(i)=2i.
$$

So:

$$
(\sigma_{\mathrm{out}} \circ \tau)(32+i)=2i,
$$

which matches $\sigma_{\mathrm{in}}(32+i)$.

### Conclusion

For all indices,

$$
\sigma_{\mathrm{in}} = \sigma_{\mathrm{out}} \circ \tau.
$$

So the inshuffle is obtained by:

1. swapping the two halves of the input, then
2. performing the outshuffle.

No conjugation is involved, and no action of $\tau$ on output positions is required.

## 5. Efficiency on MMIX

The map $\tau$ is a fixed block permutation:

$$
(a_{31}\dots a_0\, b_{31}\dots b_0)
\;\longleftrightarrow\;
(b_{31}\dots b_0\, a_{31}\dots a_0),
$$

implementable by a constant number of MMIX operations using shifts, masks, and or-combines as in Section 7.1.3.

The outshuffle is already known to be implementable in linear word time with a fixed sequence of instructions independent of word size.

Therefore the inshuffle costs:

- one $\tau$ (constant time),
- one outshuffle (same cost as usual),

hence the same asymptotic and constant-factor efficiency as the outshuffle.

## Final conclusion

The inshuffle is not a conjugate of the outshuffle under a half-swap; instead it is simply the outshuffle applied after swapping the two halves of the input:

$$
\sigma_{\mathrm{in}} = \sigma_{\mathrm{out}} \circ \tau.
$$

Thus an inshuffle can be performed as efficiently as an outshuffle on MMIX, with only constant additional overhead.
