Construct a min-Cartesian tree $T$ on indices $1,\dots,n$ and then apply Algorithm V for LCA. The RMQ problem is reduced to LCA on this tree.

## 1. Construction of the Cartesian tree in $O(n)$

Define a rooted ordered binary tree $T$ with vertex set $\{1,\dots,n\}$ such that:

- The inorder traversal of $T$ is $1,2,\dots,n$.
- Each node $v$ has key $A_v$.
- The heap property holds: for every parent $u$ and child $v$, $A_u \le A_v$.

This is the standard **min-Cartesian tree**.

### Stack construction

Process indices $i = 1,2,\dots,n$ in increasing order. Maintain a stack $S$ of indices such that the values $A$ are strictly increasing from bottom to top.

For each $i$:

1. Let $last = \text{pop all } j \in S \text{ with } A_j > A_i$.
2. If $S$ is nonempty, let $p$ be the new top of $S$. Set $i$ as the right child of $p$.
3. If $last \neq \emptyset$, let the last popped element become the left child of $i$ (the last popped one is the nearest greater element to the left in the structural sense of Cartesian trees).
4. Push $i$ onto $S$.

At the end, the bottom of the stack is the root.

### Complexity

Each index is pushed and popped at most once, so the construction is $O(n)$.

## 2. Structural properties of the Cartesian tree

### Lemma 1 (Heap property)

For every parent-child relation $u \to v$, we have $A_u \le A_v$.

**Proof.**

A node becomes parent only when it survives popping, meaning it is the nearest index with smaller or equal value encountered during construction. Any child is attached after being popped due to a larger value, so parent values are always smaller. ∎

### Lemma 2 (Inorder property)

An inorder traversal of $T$ visits vertices in the order $1,2,\dots,n$.

**Proof.**

Nodes are inserted in increasing index order. The stack construction ensures that all nodes popped from the stack when inserting $i$ become part of the left subtree of $i$, and any remaining stack element becomes its ancestor on the right spine. This enforces that all indices left of $i$ appear in its left subtree and all indices right of $i$ appear in its right subtree. Hence inorder is $1,\dots,n$. ∎

### Lemma 3 (Interval subtree property)

For any interval $[i,j]$, the nodes of the Cartesian tree whose indices lie in $[i,j]$ form a connected subtree.

**Proof.**

By Lemma 2, all indices are arranged in inorder. Removing nodes outside $[i,j]$ leaves a contiguous inorder segment. In any binary tree with inorder $1,\dots,n$, a contiguous interval corresponds to a connected subtree induced by the path structure, since no edge can cross the interval boundary without violating inorder continuity. Hence the induced subgraph is connected. ∎

## 3. RMQ–LCA equivalence

### Lemma 4 (Root is interval minimum)

Let $T[i,j]$ be the subtree induced by indices in $[i,j]$. The root of this subtree is the index $k(i,j)$ such that

$$
A_{k(i,j)} = \min(A_i,\dots,A_j).
$$

**Proof.**

Let $r$ be the root of the Cartesian tree. Since the heap property holds, $r$ is the global minimum of $A$, and recursively each subtree root is the minimum in its inorder interval.

We prove by induction on interval length.

- Base case: length $1$ is trivial.
- Inductive step: let $t$ be the minimum in $[i,j]$. In a Cartesian tree, removing the global minimum splits the inorder sequence into left and right parts, and no element smaller than $t$ can lie in either subtree. Thus $t$ becomes the root of the induced subtree.

Therefore the minimum in $[i,j]$ is exactly the root of the induced subtree. ∎

### Lemma 5 (LCA characterization)

For any $i \le j$, the least common ancestor of $i$ and $j$ in $T$ is the root of the subtree induced by $[i,j]$.

**Proof.**

By Lemma 3, all nodes in $[i,j]$ form a connected subtree. The unique node whose removal separates $i$ and $j$ into different components in a rooted tree is exactly the root of this induced subtree. Hence it is the LCA of $i$ and $j$. ∎

### Theorem (RMQ = LCA)

For all $1 \le i \le j \le n$,

$$
k(i,j) = \mathrm{LCA}_T(i,j).
$$

Combining Lemma 4 and Lemma 5 gives

$$
A_{\mathrm{LCA}(i,j)} = \min(A_i,\dots,A_j).
$$

Thus RMQ reduces to LCA.

## 4. Application of Algorithm V

Algorithm V from the preceding exercises preprocesses any rooted tree on $n$ vertices in $O(n)$ time to build the tables $(\pi,\beta,\alpha,\tau)$, enabling constant-time LCA queries.

Apply Algorithm V to the Cartesian tree $T$. Since:

- Construction of $T$ takes $O(n)$,
- Preprocessing for LCA takes $O(n)$,

the total preprocessing time is $O(n)$.

Each RMQ query $(i,j)$ is answered by computing:

$$
k(i,j) = \mathrm{LCA}_T(i,j),
$$

in $O(1)$ time.

## Conclusion

A min-Cartesian tree built in $O(n)$ time has the property that the minimum element in any interval $[i,j]$ is exactly the least common ancestor of $i$ and $j$. Applying Algorithm V to this tree yields $O(n)$ preprocessing and $O(1)$ RMQ queries.
