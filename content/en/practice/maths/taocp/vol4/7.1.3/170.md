**Exercise 170.** &#9654; [*21*] [21] What result does the Guo–Hall thinning automaton produce when given a
solid black rectangle of M rows and N columns? How long does it take?

The previous solution fails because it replaces the actual Guo–Hall thinning rules with a uniform erosion model. The correct analysis must work from the _local deletion conditions_ and then use symmetry only as a constraint, not as a dynamics substitute.

## 1. Correct model of Guo–Hall thinning

The Guo–Hall algorithm is a parallel iterative thinning procedure. Each iteration consists of two subiterations. In each subiteration, a black pixel $p$ is deleted only if a set of local conditions holds, involving:

- the number of black neighbors in its 8-neighborhood,
- the number of 0→1 transitions in the ordered neighborhood,
- directional constraints that differ between the two subiterations.

Two facts are crucial:

1. Deletions are **not uniform on the boundary**.
2. Boundary pixels can survive an iteration even if they lie on the outer frame.

Thus any argument assuming “one full layer is removed per step” is invalid.

## 2. What symmetry actually implies

A filled $M \times N$ rectangle is invariant under:

- reflection across its vertical centerline,
- reflection across its horizontal centerline.

The Guo–Hall rules depend only on the local 8-neighborhood and are rotation-reflection consistent. Therefore:

- the set of black pixels remains symmetric under both reflections at every iteration.

However, symmetry only implies:

- if a pixel is deletable, its reflected counterparts are also deletable,
- it does **not** imply that all boundary pixels are deletable simultaneously.

So symmetry constrains the _form_ of the result, but not the _rate_ or _pattern_ of deletion.

## 3. True qualitative evolution

Consider pixels by their geometric position.

### 3.1 Interior region

Any pixel with distance at least 2 from the boundary has a full 8-neighborhood of black pixels. Such pixels:

- fail the endpoint conditions,
- are never deleted until the boundary approaches them.

So erosion propagates inward.

### 3.2 Non-central non-axis pixels

Any pixel not on the vertical or horizontal midlines has an asymmetric configuration: one direction (toward the nearest side) is strictly closer than others.

Repeated application of thinning rules eventually deletes such pixels because:

- they become boundary pixels of a shrinking shape,
- they satisfy endpoint and connectivity removal conditions in one of the subiterations.

Thus all off-axis structure is eliminated.

### 3.3 Central axes stability

Now consider the two symmetry axes:

- vertical center line (if $N$ is odd, column $j_0$)
- horizontal center line (if $M$ is odd, row $i_0$)

A pixel on the vertical center line has:

- symmetric left/right neighborhoods,
- two opposing boundary directions at equal distance.

This produces two key effects under Guo–Hall conditions:

- it fails endpoint deletion tests more often than generic boundary pixels,
- deletion would locally break symmetry and create connectivity violations in one of the subiterations.

The same holds for the horizontal center line.

Therefore both axes are **stable under thinning once the shape has sufficiently narrowed around them**.

## 4. Final configuration

Because the process preserves both horizontal and vertical symmetry and deletes all non-central pixels, the only possible fixed point compatible with symmetry and connectivity is:

$$
\{(i, j_0) : 1 \le i \le M\} \;\cup\; \{(i_0, j) : 1 \le j \le N\},
$$

where

$$
i_0 = \frac{M+1}{2}, \quad j_0 = \frac{N+1}{2}
$$

(with integer interpretation: one or two central lines depending on parity).

### Case breakdown

- If both $M$ and $N$ are odd: a cross centered at $(i_0, j_0)$.
- If one dimension is even: the corresponding “center line” becomes a 2-pixel-wide strip, but thinning reduces it to a 1-pixel representative line depending on tie-breaking; in all cases the result remains a single-pixel-wide discrete medial structure.

### Key structural fact

The algorithm does **not** collapse the rectangle into a single horizontal or vertical segment. Neither direction is privileged, so both medial directions persist.

Thus the result is the **discrete medial axis of the rectangle**, which is a cross-shaped 1-pixel skeleton.

## 5. Why the earlier “layer peeling” model fails

The incorrect recurrence

$$
M^{(t+1)} = M^{(t)} - 2,\quad N^{(t+1)} = N^{(t)} - 2
$$

assumes simultaneous deletion of all boundary pixels.

In Guo–Hall thinning:

- boundary pixels are tested independently under directional subiterations,
- only subsets satisfying strict local predicates are removed,
- corners and edges do not behave uniformly.

So width and height do not decrease in lockstep.

## 6. Running time

Each iteration scans all pixels and evaluates constant-size neighborhood conditions:

$$
\Theta(MN) \text{ per iteration.}
$$

The number of iterations is bounded by the maximum distance from the boundary to the medial axis, which is:

$$
\Theta(\max(M, N)).
$$

Reason: each iteration can advance the effective boundary inward by at least one pixel in some direction, and no pixel survives until it reaches the stable medial structure.

Thus total running time is:

$$
\Theta(MN \cdot \max(M, N)).
$$

## Final answer

A solid $M \times N$ rectangle under Guo–Hall thinning converges to its discrete medial axis:

- a vertical and horizontal center line forming a cross (possibly adjusted by parity),
- always 1-pixel thick.

The process takes:

$$
\Theta(MN \cdot \max(M, N))
$$

time.
