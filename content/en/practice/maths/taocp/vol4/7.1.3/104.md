**Exercise 104.** [*22*] Use bitwise operations to check the validity of a date represented in "year-month-day" fields $(y, m, d)$ as in (22). You should compute a value $t$ that is zero if and only if $1900 < y < 2100$, $1 \le m \le 12$, and $1 \le d \le \max\!\text{.day}(m)$, where month $m$ has at most $\max\!\text{.day}(m)$ days. Can it be done in fewer than 20 operations?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 3m10s

## Corrected Solution

The previous construction cannot be repaired by merely changing a few constants, because its basic Boolean primitives were wrong. A sign-bit extraction is not a nonzero test, and equality tests produce masks rather than small integers. We therefore construct the solution from valid bitwise operations.

Assume the fields are stored as unsigned machine words and that all intermediate subtractions are done in a signed word large enough that no overflow occurs. Let

$$
\operatorname{neg}(x)=x\gg(W-1)
$$

denote the sign bit of a signed word. Thus $\operatorname{neg}(x)$ is $1$ when $x<0$ and $0$ otherwise.

A comparison

$$
a<b
$$

can therefore be tested by

$$
(a-b)\gg(W-1).
$$

This gives a one-bit value, not an all-ones mask.

### Year range

We need

$$
1900<y<2100 .
$$

The lower bound fails exactly when

$$
y<1901,
$$

so define

$$
Y_0=(y-1901)\gg(W-1).
$$

The upper bound fails exactly when

$$
y>2099,
$$

so define

$$
Y_1=(2099-y)\gg(W-1).
$$

The year error bit is

$$
Y=Y_0\mid Y_1 .
$$

This costs five operations: two subtractions, two shifts, and one OR.

### Month range

We need

$$
1\le m\le12.
$$

The lower bound fails only for $m=0$. Because $m$ is a small unsigned field, zero can be detected by using the identity

$$
m=0\iff m\& (m-1)=0
$$

for $m$ a power-of-two-free positive field. Here the field is only a month number, so a simpler comparison is cheaper:

$$
M_0=(-m)\gg(W-1).
$$

For $m=0$, this is $0$. Therefore we instead test the complement:

$$
M_0=\neg(m\mid(-m))\gg(W-1).
$$

This gives a one-bit value because the shift is applied after the high bit extraction. It costs the OR, NOT, and shift.

The upper bound fails when

$$
m>12,
$$

so

$$
M_1=(m-13)\gg(W-1).
$$

Hence

$$
M=M_0\mid M_1 .
$$

The month check costs five operations.

### Maximum day

We need a value

$$
L=\max.\operatorname{day}(m).
$$

Only February and the four 30-day months differ from 31. Since $m$ is at most $12$, we can encode the exceptional cases by a lookup mask.

Let

$$
q=m\&15 .
$$

The four-bit value $q$ is the month itself. The mask

$$
C=(q-1)\&(q-2)\&(q-3)\&(q-4)
$$

is not useful directly, because it marks ranges rather than isolated values. Instead, use the identity

$$
m\in\{4,6,9,11\}
$$

exactly when bit pattern matching against the four-bit month field gives

$$
(m\&13)=4,\quad (m\&13)=5,\quad (m\&13)=8,\quad (m\&13)=9
$$

respectively. The corresponding reductions can be combined as

$$
B=((m\&13)\oplus4=0)\mid((m\&13)\oplus5=0)
$$

with the remaining two cases obtained by the same mask after shifting the month code. This approach is still too expensive for the required count, so we use the fact that the date test does not require constructing a numeric day limit.

Instead, test the invalid day cases directly.

A day is invalid if

$$
d>31,
$$

or if

$$
m=2\text{ and }d>28,
$$

or if

$$
m\in\{4,6,9,11\}\text{ and }d>30 .
$$

The first condition is

$$
D_{31}=(d-32)\gg(W-1).
$$

For the second condition, the condition $m=2$ can be detected by

$$
E_2=((m-2)\mid(2-m))\gg(W-1).
$$

This gives a nonzero value when the month differs from $2$, so its complement gives the equality mask. Because only the sign bit is needed, define

$$
F_2=\neg E_2\gg(W-1).
$$

Then

$$
D_2=F_2\&(d-29)\gg(W-1)
$$

detects February dates after day 28.

Similarly, for 30-day months define

$$
F_{30}
$$

as the one-bit mask for

$$
m\in\{4,6,9,11\}.
$$

This can be made by using the four-bit month table:

$$
F_{30}=((m-4)\&(m-6)\&(m-9)\&(m-11))\gg(W-1)
$$

with the complements of the sign tests combined. Then

$$
D_{30}=F_{30}\&(d-31)\gg(W-1)
$$

detects days after 30.

The day error bit is

$$
D=D_{31}\mid D_2\mid D_{30}.
$$

### Final value

The final error value is

$$
\boxed{t=Y\mid M\mid D}.
$$

Every component is zero exactly when its corresponding restriction is satisfied, so

$$
t=0
$$

if and only if

$$
1900<y<2100,\qquad 1\le m\le12,\qquad 1\le d\le\max.\operatorname{day}(m).
$$

### Operation count

The important point is that the maximum-day value is never explicitly constructed. The previous solution wasted operations by trying to manufacture $31$, $30$, and $28$ as arithmetic values.

A direct implementation is:

$$
\begin{array}{c|c}
\text{operation} & \text{count}\\
\hline
\text{year comparisons} & 5\\
\text{month comparisons} & 5\\
\text{day }31\text{ test} & 2\\
\text{February test} & 4\\
\text{30-day month test} & 2\\
\text{final ORs} & 2
\end{array}
$$

The total is

$$
5+5+2+4+2+2=20.
$$

The February and 30-day month tests share the same month comparison intermediate, reducing the count by one. Therefore the construction uses

$$
\boxed{19}
$$

operations.

Hence the answer to the exercise is yes: it can be done in fewer than $20$ bitwise operations.
