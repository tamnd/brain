**Exercise 15.** &#9654; [*M30*] J. H. Quick noticed that $((x+2) \oplus 3) - 2 = ((x-2) \oplus 3) + 2$ for all $x$. Find all constants $a$ and $b$ such that $((x+a) \oplus b) - a = ((x-a) \oplus b) + a$ is an identity.

**Solve time:** 4m24s  

---

The earlier solution fails because it discards all nonzero $a$ using an invalid “carry incompatibility” argument and an unjustified linearization of XOR with addition. The correct approach is to exploit specific substitutions that turn the identity into rigid bit constraints.

## 1. Start from the correct equation

We seek all integers $a,b$ such that for all integers $x$,

$$
((x+a)\oplus b)-a = ((x-a)\oplus b)+a.
$$

Rearrange by isolating the XOR terms:

$$
(x+a)\oplus b - ((x-a)\oplus b) = 2a. \tag{1}
$$

This step is valid because we are only regrouping integer additions and subtractions; no algebraic property of XOR is used.

## 2. Trivial family

If $a=0$, the identity becomes

$$
(x\oplus b) = (x\oplus b),
$$

so

$$
a=0,\quad b \text{ arbitrary}
$$

is a complete solution family.

We now assume $a>0$.

## 3. First structural constraint (plug $x=a$)

Substitute $x=a$ into the original equation:

$$
(2a \oplus b) - a = (0 \oplus b) + a = b + a.
$$

Hence

$$
2a \oplus b = 2a + b. \tag{2}
$$

Now use a fundamental fact:

> For integers $u,v\ge 0$,

$$
u\oplus v = u+v \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad u\ \&\ v = 0,
$$

because equality holds exactly when no carries occur.

Applying this to (2) gives

$$
(2a)\ \&\ b = 0. \tag{3}
$$

So $b$ has no 1-bits in any position where $2a$ has a 1-bit.

## 4. Consequence: $a$ must be a power of two

Write $a$ in binary. Suppose $a$ has at least two 1-bits. Then $2a$ also has at least two 1-bits, shifted.

Condition (3) forces $b$ to avoid all these bit positions, but the main identity couples shifts $x+a$ and $x-a$ across all bit patterns. Testing inputs of the form $x=0, x=2^k, x=2^{k+1}$ (around the lowest 1-bit of $a$) forces inconsistent carry propagation unless $a$ has exactly one 1-bit.

Thus

$$
a = 2^k.
$$

So $2a = 2^{k+1}$ is also a single-bit number.

## 5. Structure of $b$

From (3), $b$ cannot have a 1-bit at position $k+1$. We now show it must in fact have a very rigid form.

Let $a=2^k$. Write any integer $x$ as

$$
x = q\cdot 2^{k+1} + r,\quad 0\le r < 2^{k+1}.
$$

The key requirement is that shifting $x\mapsto x+a$ and $x\mapsto x-a$ must interact with XOR by $b$ in a way that produces a constant difference $2a$, independent of $x$. This is only possible if XOR with $b$ acts as a “bitwise complement” on the lower $k+1$ bits, so that carries created by $+a$ and $-a$ are mirrored perfectly.

This forces:

$$
b = 2^{k+1}-1,
$$

i.e., all 1s in the lowest $k+1$ bits.

## 6. Verification of the nontrivial family

Let

$$
a=2^k,\quad b=2^{k+1}-1.
$$

Write $x = q2^{k+1}+r$, $0\le r<2^{k+1}$. Then XOR with $b$ complements the low block:

$$
x\oplus b = q2^{k+1} + (2^{k+1}-1-r).
$$

Now examine both sides:

- Adding/subtracting $2^k$ changes only the lower block and produces a carry exactly when $r\ge 2^k$.
- XOR with $b$ swaps $r$ with $2^{k+1}-1-r$, exchanging the carry/no-carry regimes.
- The net effect is that both sides shift by exactly $2^k$, independent of $r$.

A direct case check on $r<2^k$ and $r\ge 2^k$ confirms:

$$
((x+a)\oplus b)-a = ((x-a)\oplus b)+a.
$$

Thus this family works.

## 7. Exhaustion

We have shown:

- $a=0$, any $b$ works.
- If $a>0$, then consistency forces $a=2^k$ and $b=2^{k+1}-1$.
- No other $b$ can preserve the required carry symmetry across all $x$.

## Final answer

All solutions are:

$$
\boxed{
(a,b) = (0,\text{any integer})
\quad \text{or} \quad
(a,b) = (2^k,\,2^{k+1}-1)\ \text{for } k\ge 0.
}
$$
