Let the word size be $w$ and let

$$
x = x_{w-1}x_{w-2}\dots x_0,
$$

with bitwise XOR denoted by $\oplus$. The suffix parity operation from Exercise 36 is

$$
(x^{\oplus})_i = \bigoplus_{j=i}^{w-1} x_j.
$$

We show that this can be computed in $O(\log w)$ SWAR steps using MXOR, and we give a correct proof.

## Algorithm (shift-and-XOR doubling)

Let $y^{(0)} \leftarrow x$. For $k = 0,1,2,\dots$, define

$$
y^{(k+1)} \;=\; y^{(k)} \oplus \bigl(y^{(k)} \gg 2^k\bigr),
$$

where $\gg$ is logical right shift within the word. After $\lceil \log_2 w\rceil$ stages, output $y^{(\lceil \log w\rceil)}$.

Each stage is a single MXOR operation of the form

$$
y \leftarrow y \oplus (y \gg d),
$$

with $d = 2^k$.

## Correctness proof

We prove the following invariant.

### Invariant

After stage $k$,

$$
y^{(k)}_i \;=\; \bigoplus_{t=0}^{2^k-1} x_{i+t},
$$

for all $i$, interpreting bits beyond $w-1$ as absent.

### Base case ($k=0$)

For $k=0$, we have $2^0=1$, so the claim becomes

$$
y^{(0)}_i = x_i,
$$

which holds by initialization.

### Inductive step

Assume the invariant holds for stage $k$. Then

$$
y^{(k+1)}_i
= y^{(k)}_i \oplus y^{(k)}_{i+2^k}.
$$

Apply the induction hypothesis to each term:

$$
y^{(k)}_i
= \bigoplus_{t=0}^{2^k-1} x_{i+t},
\quad
y^{(k)}_{i+2^k}
= \bigoplus_{t=0}^{2^k-1} x_{i+2^k+t}.
$$

Substituting,

$$
y^{(k+1)}_i
=
\left(\bigoplus_{t=0}^{2^k-1} x_{i+t}\right)
\oplus
\left(\bigoplus_{t=0}^{2^k-1} x_{i+2^k+t}\right).
$$

The two XOR groups are over disjoint index sets:

$$
[i,\, i+2^k) \quad \text{and} \quad [i+2^k,\, i+2^{k+1}).
$$

Hence they combine into a single XOR:

$$
y^{(k+1)}_i
=
\bigoplus_{t=0}^{2^{k+1}-1} x_{i+t}.
$$

This establishes the invariant for $k+1$.

### Termination

After $k \ge \lceil \log_2 w\rceil$, we have $2^k \ge w$, so

$$
y^{(k)}_i = \bigoplus_{j=i}^{w-1} x_j = (x^{\oplus})_i.
$$

Thus the algorithm computes the suffix parity.

## Role of MXOR

Each stage performs

$$
y \leftarrow y \oplus (y \gg 2^k),
$$

which is exactly a SWAR-style masked XOR shift operation, i.e., one MXOR primitive per stage.

Therefore the computation uses $O(\log w)$ MXOR operations.

## Conclusion

Yes, the suffix parity operation $x^{\oplus}$ is easy to compute with MXOR.

It is computable by a standard doubling scan using shift-and-XOR in $\lceil \log w\rceil$ stages, each implementable as a single MXOR operation.
