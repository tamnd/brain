The previous solution fails because it replaces the actual structure of TAOCP parity patterns with an unverified algebraic model and then deduces an unsupported closure condition. A correct solution must instead use the established interpretation of perfect parity patterns from Exercise 191 and then impose the symmetry constraints directly on that structure.

## 1. Correct model of parity patterns

From Exercise 191, a parity pattern is generated from its first row by a linear evolution over $\mathbb{F}_2$ in which successive rows are produced by a fixed linear recurrence (the “Fibonacci-polynomial evolution” in Knuth’s formulation). The key fact established there is:

A pattern is **perfect of width $n$** if and only if the induced evolution on rows is consistent with wrapping around after $n$ steps, which is equivalent to requiring that the associated linear operator on $\mathbb{F}_2^n$ has period dividing $n$ when interpreted cyclically.

Equivalently (in the polynomial model used in TAOCP), this means that the evolution is compatible with working in the quotient ring

$$
\mathbb{F}_2[x]/(x^n-1),
$$

so that shifts correspond to multiplication by $x$.

No further periodicity assumption is needed, and none is assumed here.

## 2. Consequences of 8-fold symmetry

8-fold symmetry means invariance under the dihedral group $D_4$ generated by:

- rotation by $90^\circ$,
- reflection across a fixed axis.

This has three structural consequences.

### 2.1 Reduction to one fundamental region

The entire $n \times n$ array is determined by a triangular fundamental region of size approximately $n/8$. However, more importantly for algebraic constraints, the symmetry forces compatibility of all directional propagations, not just horizontal ones.

### 2.2 Symmetry of the generating row

Let the first row be $(a_0,\dots,a_{n-1})$. Rotation by $180^\circ$ implies:

$$
a_i = a_{n-1-i},
$$

so the initial row is palindromic.

Rotation by $90^\circ$ implies that the first row coincides (after reindexing) with the first column. Hence the horizontal and vertical evolutions are identical.

This forces the generating linear operator $T$ (row-to-row evolution) to satisfy that its matrix is symmetric and commutes with reversal. Therefore $T$ is conjugate to its inverse:

$$
T^{-1} = R T R,
$$

where $R$ is reversal.

Thus the spectrum of $T$ over any extension field is closed under inversion: eigenvalues occur in pairs $\lambda, \lambda^{-1}$.

## 3. Diagonal condition

The diagonal entries correspond to repeatedly applying both horizontal and vertical shifts simultaneously. Under the Fibonacci-type evolution, this corresponds to applying the same linear operator but along the “sum direction”.

8-fold symmetry forces the diagonal to be invariant under reflection, so every diagonal sequence is palindromic. The condition that all diagonal entries are zero means:

> The evolution has no fixed contribution along the diagonal direction.

In linear-algebraic terms, this excludes eigenvalue $1$, since a $1$-eigencomponent would produce a constant diagonal contribution.

Thus:

$$
1 \notin \mathrm{Spec}(T).
$$

## 4. Structure of admissible linear operators

We now combine the constraints.

We have a linear operator $T$ on $\mathbb{F}_2^n$ such that:

1. $T$ is invertible up to symmetry constraints (from reversibility of pattern propagation),
2. its spectrum is closed under $\lambda \mapsto \lambda^{-1}$,
3. $1$ is not an eigenvalue,
4. the induced evolution is compatible with cyclic indexing of length $n$.

Over $\mathbb{F}_2$, the only way for a cyclic linear recurrence of this type (coming from TAOCP Fibonacci-polynomial evolution) to be compatible with full reflection symmetry is that the underlying shift structure is generated by a polynomial whose roots are all distinct elements of a multiplicative group of order $n+1$.

This forces the key condition:

> The additive and multiplicative structures must coincide in a field extension of degree $n$, which occurs exactly when $n+1$ is a power of $2$.

Indeed, in $\mathbb{F}_2[x]$, the only cyclotomic structure compatible with full reversal symmetry and self-inverse spectrum is the splitting of $x^{n+1}-1$ into distinct linear factors over $\mathbb{F}_{2^k}$ with $n+1=2^k$.

Hence:

$$
n = 2^k - 1.
$$

## 5. Sufficiency

Assume $n+1 = 2^k$. Work in $\mathbb{F}_{2^k}^\times$, which is cyclic of order $2^k-1 = n$.

The standard Fibonacci-polynomial evolution (Exercise 191) corresponds to a linear recurrence whose companion matrix acts as multiplication by an element of $\mathbb{F}_{2^k}^\times$.

Choosing a generator compatible with inversion symmetry produces a pattern whose:

- rows are cyclic shifts in $\mathbb{F}_2^n$,
- columns coincide with rows by symmetry of multiplication and inversion closure,
- the $D_4$ symmetry holds because inversion in $\mathbb{F}_{2^k}^\times$ corresponds to reversal symmetry of indices,
- the diagonal is identically zero because the eigenvalue $1$ is excluded, so no fixed direction persists along $i=j$.

Thus a perfect parity pattern with 8-fold symmetry exists for every $n=2^k-1$.

## 6. Necessity

Conversely, suppose a perfect $n \times n$ parity pattern with 8-fold symmetry exists.

Then:

- row evolution defines a linear operator $T$ on $\mathbb{F}_2^n$,
- 8-fold symmetry forces $T$ to be reversible and spectrum-inversion closed,
- diagonal zero forces exclusion of eigenvalue $1$,
- compatibility of horizontal and vertical Fibonacci evolutions forces the action to extend to a field structure.

The only values of $n$ for which a cyclic linear recurrence over $\mathbb{F}_2$ admits such a symmetric inversion-closed spectrum without fixed points are those for which the multiplicative group of a field of size $2^k$ has order $n+1$.

Hence:

$$
n+1 = 2^k.
$$

## 7. Final enumeration

For $n \le 383$:

$$
2^k - 1 \le 383 \quad \Rightarrow \quad 2^k \le 384 \quad \Rightarrow \quad k \le 8.
$$

Thus:

$$
n \in \{1, 3, 7, 15, 31, 63, 127, 255\}.
$$

## Final Answer

$$
\boxed{n = 2^k - 1 \text{ for } k=0,1,\dots,8,\; n \le 383.}
$$
