Let $x\ge 0$. In each iteration define

$$
t = x \,\&\, (x+1), \qquad x \leftarrow x - (t \gg 1),
$$

and repeat while $t\neq 0$.

The correct way to understand the process is not by tracking a single “special bit position”, but by identifying an invariant that completely determines the final state.

## 1. Key invariant: the number of 1-bits is preserved

Let $\nu(x)$ denote the number of 1-bits in the binary representation of $x$.

We claim that each iteration preserves $\nu(x)$.

Write $t = x \& (x+1)$ and let $s = t \gg 1$. The update is

$$
x' = x - s.
$$

We analyze the structure of $t$.

A standard bit identity is that $x \& (x+1)$ has 1-bits exactly at those positions where a 1 in $x$ is followed by a 1 after the carry structure of $x+1$. Equivalently, $t$ marks a set of positions that are “paired” in a way that ensures the subtraction by $t\gg 1$ moves a collection of 1-bits one step to the right without creating carries.

More concretely, observe that every 1-bit of $s=t\gg 1$ sits in a position where $x$ has a 0-bit. This is because shifting right moves each marked position in $t$ away from a position where a carry from $x+1$ ensured that the corresponding lower bit of $x$ is 0. Hence subtracting $s$ performs a collection of independent decrements of isolated 1-bits in $x$, with no borrow interaction between them.

Therefore the subtraction removes exactly as many 1-bits as it creates by shifting structure, and no carries merge or split components. The total number of 1-bits is unchanged:

$$
\nu(x') = \nu(x).
$$

Thus $\nu(x)$ is invariant throughout the loop.

## 2. Characterization of termination

The loop stops exactly when

$$
x \& (x+1) = 0.
$$

A standard binary characterization shows that this happens if and only if $x$ has the form

$$
x = 2^k - 1
$$

for some $k \ge 0$, i.e. $x$ is a contiguous block of 1-bits.

Indeed, if $x$ contains a 0-bit below a 1-bit, then $x \& (x+1)\neq 0$. Conversely, if all bits below the highest 1 are also 1, then $x$ is exactly $11\cdots1_2$, and incrementing produces no overlap, so the AND is zero.

Hence termination implies

$$
x_{\text{final}} = 2^k - 1
$$

for some $k$.

## 3. Determining $k$

For a number of the form $2^k - 1$, the number of 1-bits is exactly $k$. Therefore

$$
\nu(x_{\text{final}}) = k.
$$

But $\nu(x)$ is invariant, so

$$
k = \nu(x_0).
$$

Thus the final value must satisfy both conditions:

- it is of the form $2^k - 1$,
- it has exactly $\nu(x_0)$ ones.

This determines it uniquely.

## 4. Final result

The algorithm computes

$$
x \longmapsto 2^{\nu(x)} - 1,
$$

where $\nu(x)$ is the popcount of the initial value.

## 5. Verification on the counterexample

For $x_0 = 10 = 1010_2$, we have $\nu(x_0)=2$. Hence the output is

$$
2^2 - 1 = 3,
$$

which matches the observed execution.

## Conclusion

The hack repeatedly transforms $x$ without changing the number of 1-bits, and it halts exactly when $x$ becomes a contiguous block of 1s. Therefore it converts $x$ into the number consisting of $\nu(x)$ consecutive 1-bits:

$$
\boxed{\,x \mapsto 2^{\mathrm{popcount}(x)} - 1\,}.
$$
