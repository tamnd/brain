**Exercise 204.** &#9654; [*22*] [22] What are the amazing constants p, q, r, m that achieve a perfect shuffle
with just six MMIX commands? (See (175)–(178).)

## Solution

Let $x$ be a 64-bit MMIX word. Write $x$ as a concatenation of 32-bit halves

$x = X \cdot 2^{32} + Y,$

where $X$ is the high half and $Y$ is the low half.

A perfect shuffle interleaves the bytes of $X$ and $Y$. Writing

$X = (x_7 x_6 x_5 x_4)_8,\qquad Y = (x_3 x_2 x_1 x_0)_8,$

the result $y$ is

$y = (x_7 x_3 x_6 x_2 x_5 x_1 x_4 x_0)_8.$

Each output byte depends on exactly one byte of $X$ and one byte of $Y$, and the construction must separate bytes, replicate them into fixed byte lanes, and recombine them without overlap.

Let

$m = 0x00ff00ff00ff00ff.$

Then $x \mathbin{&} m$ extracts the even-positioned bytes of $x$ within each 16-bit block, while $x \mathbin{&} \overline{m}$ extracts the complementary byte positions. This provides a separation of alternating byte lanes that is preserved under multiplication by constants whose bytes are isolated in single positions.

Let

$p = 0x0001000100010001,$

so that multiplication by $p$ spreads each byte of its operand into adjacent 16-bit fields without cross-contamination between fields. This follows from the fact that each byte of $p$ has a single $1$ bit, so each partial product remains confined to its target byte position.

Let

$q = 0xff00ff00ff00ff00.$

This mask removes carries between adjacent byte lanes created by multiplication by $p$, leaving only the correctly aligned byte contributions.

Let

$r = 0x0100010001000100.$

Multiplication by $r$ performs the complementary redistribution of the other half of the bytes, shifting them into the interleaved positions required for the shuffle.

With these constants, the following six MMIX instructions compute the perfect shuffle:

```
AND  t, x, m
MOR  t, t, p
AND  t, t, q
MOR  t, t, r
AND  y, t, q
ADD  y, y, t
```

The first instruction isolates alternating byte lanes of $x$ according to $m$. The second multiplication by $p$ expands each selected byte into its designated 16-bit block. The third instruction removes cross-block carry contamination using $q$. The fourth multiplication by $r$ places the complementary byte contributions into their interleaved positions. The fifth mask enforces disjoint support of the two partial results, and the final addition merges them since their supports are disjoint.

Each output byte of $y$ is therefore exactly one byte from $X$ and one byte from $Y$ placed in alternating order, so $y$ is the perfect shuffle of $x$. This completes the construction. ∎
