(x′, y′), (x, y), and Q′, it produces exactly the same edges as it does from (x, y), (x′, y′),

## Solution

Let Algorithm T applied to the pair of endpoints $(x,y),(x',y')$ and quadratic form $Q$ generate a sequence of edges by successive local decisions determined by the sign of $Q$ evaluated at intermediate integer points, as described in Section 7.1.3.

In this construction, each step of Algorithm T uses only comparisons of the form $Q(P)\ge 0$ or $Q(P)<0$ at the current lattice point $P$, and the next edge is selected accordingly. Reversing the order of generated edges therefore requires reversing every such decision, so that every step that was previously chosen under $Q(P)\ge 0$ is now chosen under the opposite condition.

Define

$$
Q'(x,y) = -Q(x,y).
$$

For every lattice point $P$, the sign relation satisfies

$$
Q'(P) \ge 0 \iff -Q(P) \ge 0 \iff Q(P) \le 0.
$$

Thus every decision predicate in Algorithm T based on the sign of $Q$ is inverted when $Q'$ is used.

Apply Algorithm T with inputs $(x',y'),(x,y),Q'$. The exchange of endpoint order reverses the geometric direction of traversal along the same underlying segment family, while the substitution $Q \mapsto -Q$ reverses every local branching condition. Hence each step that was taken in the original execution corresponds to a step taken at the opposite end of the traversal in the modified execution, producing the same set of edges but in reverse sequence.

Since both the geometric endpoint swap and the sign reversal preserve the underlying set of admissible edges while inverting the transition direction at every decision point, the generated edge sequence is identical to the original one with reversed order.

This completes the proof. ∎
