**Exercise 67.** [*M31*] If $u(x)$ is a polynomial of degree $< n$, represented as in exercise 65, discuss the computation of $v(x) = u(x)^2 \bmod (x^n + x^m + 1)$, when $0 < m < n$ and both $m$ and $n$ are odd. *Hint:* This problem has an interesting connection with perfect shuffling.

Work in $R=\mathbb{F}_2[x]/(x^n+x^m+1)$, with $0<m<n$, $m,n$ odd. Let

$$
u(x)=\sum_{i=0}^{n-1} u_i x^i.
$$

In characteristic $2$,

$$
u(x)^2=\sum_{i=0}^{n-1} u_i x^{2i}.
$$

Thus it suffices to reduce each monomial $x^{2i}$ to its unique representative of degree $<n$ in $R$, and then XOR the contributions.

## 1. Reduction system

The defining relation is

$$
x^n = x^m + 1.
$$

Hence for any exponent $e \ge n$,

$$
x^e = x^{e-n}x^n = x^{e-n}(x^m+1)=x^{e-n+m}+x^{e-n}.
$$

This is a valid identity in $R$, but it is not yet a canonical form rule because the exponents $e-n$ or $e-n+m$ may still be $\ge n$. Therefore we define a _full reduction process_.

## 2. Full reduction of a monomial

Define a reduction operation on formal $\mathbb{F}_2$-linear combinations of monomials:

If $e<n$, keep $x^e$ unchanged.

If $e\ge n$, replace $x^e$ by

$$
x^{e-n} + x^{e-n+m}.
$$

Apply this rule repeatedly to every resulting term whose exponent is still $\ge n$, until all exponents lie in $\{0,1,\dots,n-1\}$.

### Termination

Each application replaces an exponent $e$ by exponents $e-n$ and $e-n+m$, both strictly smaller than $e$ since $n>m>0$. Hence every branch of the प्रक्रिया strictly decreases exponents, so the process terminates.

### Well-definedness

The rule is linear over $\mathbb{F}_2$, and each step is an identity in the quotient ring. Therefore the final XOR-sum of all produced monomials is independent of the order of reductions and equals the canonical representative of $x^e$ in $R$.

## 3. Expansion of $x^{2i}$

Let $e_0=2i$. Define a multiset process:

- Start with $\{e_0\}$.
- While there exists $e \ge n$ in the multiset, replace $e$ by

$$
e-n \quad \text{and} \quad e-n+m.
$$

Let $S(i)\subseteq \{0,1,\dots,n-1\}$ be the set of all exponents that appear after full reduction of $x^{2i}$, counted modulo $2$ (i.e., duplicates cancel).

Then in $R$,

$$
x^{2i} \equiv \sum_{k\in S(i)} x^k.
$$

Equivalently, $S(i)$ is the set of all endpoints of reduction paths starting at $2i$, where each step replaces

$$
e \mapsto e-n \quad \text{or} \quad e \mapsto e-n+m
\quad \text{when } e\ge n.
$$

## 4. Final expression for $v(x)$

Since squaring is linear in $\mathbb{F}_2$,

$$
v(x)=u(x)^2 \bmod (x^n+x^m+1)
$$

is obtained by superposition of the reductions of each $x^{2i}$:

$$
v(x)=\sum_{i=0}^{n-1} u_i \left(\sum_{k\in S(i)} x^k\right).
$$

Thus the coefficient form is

$$
v_k = \bigoplus_{i:\, k\in S(i)} u_i.
$$

## 5. Structure of the computation

The map $i \mapsto S(i)$ is a linear transformation of the $n$-dimensional vector space over $\mathbb{F}_2$. Computation of $v$ therefore reduces to propagating each input bit $u_i$ along a binary branching reduction tree induced by

$$
e \to e-n \quad \text{and} \quad e \to e-n+m.
$$

This is the correct completion of the reduction: it accounts for all secondary and higher reductions of terms like $x^{t+m}$, since those are handled automatically by continuing the same rule until all exponents fall below $n$.

## 6. Connection to perfect shuffling (structure)

Each reduction step subtracts $n$ (a global “cut”) and optionally adds $m$ (a fixed offset). Thus the evolution of exponents behaves like a controlled binary branching walk with a fixed offset parameter $m$. When interpreted on indices modulo $2n$, this corresponds to a structured interleaving of positions, which is the same combinatorial mechanism underlying perfect shuffle permutations: doubling creates a split, while the trinomial correction introduces a fixed offset between branches.

## Conclusion

The correct computation of $v(x)$ is obtained by:

1. Squaring via $u(x)^2=\sum u_i x^{2i}$.
2. Fully reducing each $x^{2i}$ using the iterative rule

$$
x^e \mapsto x^{e-n}+x^{e-n+m} \quad (e\ge n),
$$

until all exponents are $<n$.
3. XOR-aggregating all resulting contributions.

This yields a complete and correct description of $v(x)$ in canonical degree $<n$ form in $\mathbb{F}_2[x]/(x^n+x^m+1)$.
