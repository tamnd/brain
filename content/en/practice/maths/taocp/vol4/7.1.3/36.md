**Exercise 36.** [*20*] Given $x = (x_{63} \ldots x_1 x_0)_2$, suggest efficient ways to calculate the quantities
i) $x^R = (x_{63}^{\oplus} \ldots x_1^{\oplus} x_0^{\oplus})_2$, where $x_k^{\oplus} = x_k \oplus \cdots \oplus x_1 \oplus x_0$ for $0 \le k < 64$;
ii) $x^L = (x_{63}^{\oplus} \ldots x_1^{\oplus} x_0^{\oplus})_2$, where $x_k^L = x_k \wedge \cdots \wedge x_1 \wedge x_0$ for $0 \le k < 64$.

## Solution

Let the initial word be $x = (x_{63}\ldots x_0)_2$. We assume 64-bit fixed-width arithmetic, so a left shift by $k$ introduces zeros in positions $< k$ and discards bits beyond position $63$.

### (i) Computation of $x^R$

Define a sequence of words $x^{(0)}, x^{(1)}, \ldots, x^{(6)}$ by

$$
x^{(0)} = x,\qquad x^{(t+1)} = x^{(t)} \oplus \bigl(x^{(t)} \ll 2^t\bigr), \quad 0 \le t \le 5.
$$

We prove by induction on $t$ that after stage $t$, each bit $x^{(t)}_k$ equals the XOR of the original bits in the interval

$$
x_k \oplus x_{k-1} \oplus \cdots \oplus x_{\max(0,\,k-2^t+1)}.
$$

For $t=0$ the statement holds since no combination has been applied.

Assume the statement holds for $t$. Then in stage $t+1$,

$$
x^{(t+1)} = x^{(t)} \oplus (x^{(t)} \ll 2^t).
$$

At position $k$, this gives

$$
x^{(t+1)}_k = x^{(t)}_k \oplus x^{(t)}_{k-2^t},
$$

with the convention that $x^{(t)}_{j}=0$ for $j<0$.

By the induction hypothesis, $x^{(t)}_k$ is the XOR of the block

$[k-2^t+1,,k]$, while $x^{(t)}_{k-2^t}$ is the XOR of the block

$[k-2^{t+1}+1,,k-2^t]$. The two blocks are disjoint, hence their XOR is the XOR over their union, which is

$[k-2^{t+1}+1,,k]$. This preserves the induction claim.

After $t=6$, since $2^6=64$, every index $k$ satisfies $k-63 \le 0$, so each position contains the XOR of all bits from $0$ to $k$. Thus the desired word is obtained as

$$
x^R = x \oplus (x \ll 1) \oplus (x \ll 2) \oplus (x \ll 4) \oplus (x \ll 8) \oplus (x \ll 16) \oplus (x \ll 32),
$$

implemented by successive doubling shifts.

Hence $x^R$ is computed by the loop

$$
\text{for } k \in \{1,2,4,8,16,32\}: \quad x \leftarrow x \oplus (x \ll k).
$$

### (ii) Computation of $x^L$

Define a sequence $y^{(0)}, y^{(1)}, \ldots, y^{(6)}$ by

$$
y^{(0)} = x,\qquad y^{(t+1)} = y^{(t)} \wedge \bigl(y^{(t)} \ll 2^t\bigr), \quad 0 \le t \le 5.
$$

We prove by induction on $t$ that after stage $t$, each bit $y^{(t)}_k$ equals the AND of the original bits in the interval

$$
x_k \wedge x_{k-1} \wedge \cdots \wedge x_{\max(0,\,k-2^t+1)}.
$$

For $t=0$ the claim is immediate.

Assume it holds for $t$. At stage $t+1$,

$$
y^{(t+1)} = y^{(t)} \wedge (y^{(t)} \ll 2^t),
$$

so at position $k$,

$$
y^{(t+1)}_k = y^{(t)}_k \wedge y^{(t)}_{k-2^t}.
$$

By the induction hypothesis, $y^{(t)}_k$ is the AND over $[k-2^t+1,,k]$, while $y^{(t)}_{k-2^t}$ is the AND over $[k-2^{t+1}+1,,k-2^t]$. Since AND over a union of disjoint sets is the AND of the results, their combination yields the AND over

$[k-2^{t+1}+1,,k]$.

After $t=6$, the interval extends to $[0,k]$, so each position contains the prefix AND of all bits up to $k$. Thus

$$
x^L = x \wedge (x \ll 1) \wedge (x \ll 2) \wedge (x \ll 4) \wedge (x \ll 8) \wedge (x \ll 16) \wedge (x \ll 32),
$$

again implemented by successive updates.

## Final answers

$$
\boxed{
x^R:\; x \leftarrow x \oplus (x \ll 1) \oplus (x \ll 2) \oplus (x \ll 4) \oplus (x \ll 8) \oplus (x \ll 16) \oplus (x \ll 32)
}
$$

$$
\boxed{
x^L:\; x \leftarrow x \wedge (x \ll 1) \wedge (x \ll 2) \wedge (x \ll 4) \wedge (x \ll 8) \wedge (x \ll 16) \wedge (x \ll 32)
}
$$
