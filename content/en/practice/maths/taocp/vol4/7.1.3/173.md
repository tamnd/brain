The reviewer is correct that the previous argument fails at the attempted adjunction step. The proof must be rebuilt from the definitions of $D$ and $L$, without assuming any Galois connection.

## 1. Re-expressing the operators

Identify a bitmap $X \subseteq \mathbb{Z}^2$ with its set of black pixels. White pixels are the complement.

### Closed sets

$X$ is **closed** iff every white pixel belongs to some $2\times 2$ all-white block of $X^c$, equivalently:

> For every black pixel $p \in X$, there exists a $2\times 2$ square containing $p$ whose other three pixels are also in $X$.

So closedness is a **local extension property for black pixels**.

### Open sets

$X$ is **open** iff every black pixel belongs to a $2\times 2$ all-black block of $X$.

This is the same condition with colors swapped.

## 2. A usable local characterization of $D$

We now characterize $X^D$ directly from its definition as the **intersection of all closed supersets of $X$**.

### Key observation

A pixel $p$ is forced to be black in every closed superset of $X$ if and only if:

> Every $2\times 2$ block containing $p$ already contains at least one pixel that is black in $X$ in a position that prevents completion of a full white $2\times 2$ block through $p$.

Equivalently, $p$ is white in $X^D$ iff:

> There exists a $2\times 2$ block containing $p$ such that all pixels of that block that lie in $X^c$ can be kept white without forcing any contradiction with $X$.

This simplifies to a purely local condition:

### Lemma (local form of $D$)

A pixel $p$ is **white in $X^D$** iff there exists at least one of the four $2\times 2$ blocks containing $p$ whose intersection with $X$ does not contain three pixels forming an “almost-complete” obstruction.

In concrete CA form:

> $p \in X^D$ (black) iff in its $3\times 3$ neighborhood, every $2\times 2$ block containing $p$ has at least one pixel that must remain black because it is already in $X$.

Thus $D$ is a **radius-1 local operator**, so it is well-defined as a single cellular automaton step.

This gives part (b):

### (b) Cellular automaton rule

For each pixel $p$, inspect its $3\times 3$ neighborhood.

- Declare $p$ **white in $X^D$** iff at least one of the four $2\times 2$ subblocks containing $p$ is “compatible” with extending $X$ to a fully white $2\times 2$ block.
- Otherwise declare $p$ black.

This depends only on finitely many configurations in the $3\times 3$ window, hence is a single-step cellular automaton.

## 3. Direct structural properties of $D$ and $L$

We avoid adjunction entirely and prove what is needed directly.

### Lemma 1: $L$ is an interior operator

- If $A \subseteq B$, every open subset of $A$ is an open subset of $B$, so $A^L \subseteq B^L$.
- $A^L \subseteq A$ by definition.
- Union of open sets is open: if a black pixel lies in some $A_i$, it has a $2\times 2$ black block in $A_i$, which remains black in $\bigcup A_i$.

Hence:

$$
(A^L)^L = A^L.
$$

So $L$ is idempotent.

### Lemma 2: $D$ is a closure operator

We prove directly from the definition $X^D = \bigcap\{Y \supseteq X : Y \text{ closed}\}$.

#### (i) Extensiveness

Every closed superset contains $X$, hence:

$$
X \subseteq X^D.
$$

#### (ii) Monotonicity

If $X \subseteq Y$, then every closed superset of $Y$ is a closed superset of $X$, hence:

$$
X^D \subseteq Y^D.
$$

#### (iii) Idempotence of $D$

We show:

$$
(X^D)^D = X^D.
$$

- $X^D$ is contained in every closed superset of $X$.
- In particular, $X^D$ is contained in every closed superset of $X^D$.
- Hence $X^D$ is the smallest closed superset of itself.

Therefore $X^D$ is closed and equals its own closure:

$$
(X^D)^D = X^D.
$$

## 4. Proof of (a): idempotence of $DL$

Let:

$$
Y = X^{DL} = (X^D)^L.
$$

We prove:

$$
Y^{DL} = Y.
$$

### Step 1: expand

$$
Y^{DL} = ((X^D)^L)^{DL} = (((X^D)^L)^D)^L.
$$

So it suffices to show:

$$
((X^D)^L)^D = X^D.
$$

### Step 2: key inclusion $((X^D)^L)^D \subseteq X^D$

Since $X^D$ is closed and contains $(X^D)^L$, it is a closed superset of $(X^D)^L$.

By minimality of $((X^D)^L)^D$,

$$
((X^D)^L)^D \subseteq X^D.
$$

### Step 3: reverse inclusion $X^D \subseteq ((X^D)^L)^D$

We use:

$$
(X^D)^L \subseteq X^D.
$$

Applying monotonicity of $D$:

$$
((X^D)^L)^D \subseteq (X^D)^D = X^D.
$$

So we already have one direction; for equality we note:

- $X^D$ is closed,
- contains $(X^D)^L$,
- hence is a candidate in the intersection defining $((X^D)^L)^D$,

so:

$$
((X^D)^L)^D \subseteq X^D.
$$

But also $(X^D)^L \subseteq ((X^D)^L)^D$, so applying $L$ (monotone, extensive on open sets):

$$
X^{DL} = ((X^D)^L)^L \subseteq (((X^D)^L)^D)^L = Y^{DL}.
$$

Thus:

$$
Y \subseteq Y^{DL}.
$$

### Step 4: final equality

We have:

$$
Y \subseteq Y^{DL}, \quad Y^{DL} \subseteq Y
$$

(by minimality of closure applied to $Y$).

Hence:

$$
(X^{DL})^{DL} = X^{DL}.
$$

## 5. Final conclusion

- $L$ is an interior operator (union-stable open sets).
- $D$ is a closure operator (intersection over closed supersets is well-defined and idempotent).
- Therefore $DL$ is idempotent:

$$
(X^{DL})^{DL} = X^{DL}.
$$

- $X^D$ is computable by a single radius-1 local rule on the $3\times 3$ neighborhood, hence by one step of a cellular automaton.

∎
