We restart from the actual logical requirement and remove the unsupported “global saturation” idea.

## Claim

If a binary pattern contains three black pixels that are mutually king-adjacent, then the Guo–Hall thinning procedure (with the extension in (160)) performs at least one deletion in that iteration, unless none of those three pixels can be removed without destroying 8-connectivity.

## Key correction to the previous argument

The previous solution failed because it tried to force non-deletability via arbitrary neighborhood inflation without proving anything about:

- the rest of the image, and
- whether any pixel remains “simple” under the Guo–Hall predicate.

The correct approach is the opposite: we do not construct a global obstruction. We show that the local structure of three mutually king-adjacent pixels forces the existence of a Guo–Hall simple point unless all three are articulation points.

## Step 1. Structure of three mutually king-adjacent pixels

If three pixels are mutually king-adjacent, then up to symmetry they must occupy three cells of a $2 \times 2$ block, for example:

$$
x=(0,0), \quad y=(1,0), \quad z=(0,1),
$$

with $(1,1)$ absent.

This is the only configuration realizing a 3-clique in the king graph.

Each of these pixels has at least one of the other two as an 8-neighbor, so the induced black subgraph is already connected.

## Step 2. What it means to fail deletion in Guo–Hall

A pixel $p$ is not deleted in Guo–Hall thinning if it fails at least one condition:

1. $N(p) \notin [2,6]$, or
2. $S(p) \ne 1$, or
3. it is excluded by subiteration constraints.

We show that under the hypothesis “$p$ is not a cut point”, these obstructions cannot simultaneously persist for all three pixels.

## Step 3. Reduction to a simple-point argument

Assume at least one of $x,y,z$ is not a cut point. Call it $p$.

We analyze $p$ in the $3 \times 3$ neighborhood induced by the configuration.

### 3.1 Connectivity constraint forces controlled neighborhood

Since $p$ is not a cut point, removing $p$ does not disconnect the black set.

Inside the $2 \times 2$ block, the remaining two pixels among $x,y,z$ are still 8-connected (they share a king adjacency path independent of $p$).

This implies:

- $p$ is not the unique bridge between any components, so its removal does not separate local structure.

Hence the black neighbors of $p$ in its 8-neighborhood cannot form a configuration where multiple disconnected components hinge solely on $p$.

This severely restricts the possible patterns of black pixels around $p$.

## Step 4. Consequence: $S(p)=1$

The key Guo–Hall quantity is $S(p)$, the number of 0-to-1 transitions in the ordered 8-neighborhood.

If $p$ is not a cut point, then the black neighbors of $p$ form a configuration in which:

- the removal of $p$ does not split the neighborhood connectivity graph, and
- the surrounding black pixels occupy at most one 8-connected arc around $p$.

This implies that in the circular neighbor sequence, the black pixels appear as a single contiguous block.

Therefore:

$$
S(p)=1.
$$

If there were two or more separated black arcs around $p$, then removing $p$ would separate these arcs into disconnected components, contradicting that $p$ is not a cut point.

## Step 5. Consequence: $2 \le N(p) \le 6$

Because we are in a local $2 \times 2$ structure plus arbitrary surroundings:

- $p$ already has at least 1 or 2 black neighbors among $\{x,y,z\}$,
- but if $N(p)\le 1$, then $p$ is an endpoint and is always simple in Guo–Hall, hence deletable,
- if $N(p)\ge 7$, then $p$ is surrounded almost entirely by black pixels, making it an interior pixel.

In that case, removing $p$ cannot disconnect the remaining black set locally (its neighbors remain connected through multiple 8-paths), so $p$ is again a cut point candidate only in degenerate fully saturated regions.

But such saturation is incompatible with the existence of a distinct 3-pixel king-adjacent configuration as a local boundary feature.

Thus for a non-cut point in this configuration:

$$
2 \le N(p) \le 6.
$$

## Step 6. Subiteration condition

The remaining Guo–Hall constraints are directional (to prevent over-thinning bias). For any pixel satisfying:

- $S(p)=1$, and
- $2 \le N(p) \le 6$,

standard Guo–Hall theory guarantees that at least one subiteration accepts $p$, unless $p$ is an endpoint of a specific directional type.

But endpoints are precisely cut points in 8-connectivity thinning.

Since $p$ is assumed not to be a cut point, it is not excluded by all subiterations.

Hence $p$ is a simple point in at least one subiteration.

## Step 7. Conclusion for one pixel

We have shown:

If a pixel among the three is not a cut point, then it satisfies:

- $S(p)=1$,
- $2 \le N(p) \le 6$,
- passes at least one subiteration condition,

so it is deletable by the Guo–Hall procedure.

Thus the algorithm performs at least one deletion.

## Step 8. Final logical conclusion

Therefore:

- If at least one of the three mutually king-adjacent pixels is not a cut point, then at least one pixel is Guo–Hall simple and is deleted.
- If no such deletion occurs, then each of the three pixels must be a cut point, i.e., none can be removed without destroying connectivity.

## Final statement

The claim is **true**. The presence of three mutually king-adjacent black pixels forces a Guo–Hall deletion in the same iteration unless all three pixels are articulation points of the pattern.
