Let

$$
\alpha = (\alpha_k \cdots \alpha_0), \qquad \alpha_i \in \{0,1\}, \qquad \alpha_i \alpha_{i+1} \neq 11,
$$

and

$$
N(\alpha)=\sum_{i\ge 0}\alpha_i w_i,\qquad w_i = (-1)^i F_{i+2}.
$$

The fundamental identity is

$$
w_i = w_{i+1} + w_{i+2}.
$$

This is the only relation used in recursion (148). Everything else is a consequence of how this identity is applied locally under the constraint that digits remain in $\{0,1\}$ with no adjacent 1s.

## 1. What recursion (148) actually is

Recursion (148) defines successor $S(\alpha)$ and predecessor $P(\alpha)$ by scanning from the least significant position and applying one local rule until the representation becomes admissible.

It operates on triples of consecutive digits $(\alpha_i,\alpha_{i+1},\alpha_{i+2})$. The only situations that ever arise during addition or subtraction of $1=w_0$ are:

### Carry propagation form

A local configuration

$$
(\ldots,2,0,0,\ldots)
$$

at position $i$ is replaced using

$$
2w_i = w_i + w_i = (w_{i+1}+w_{i+2}) + w_i.
$$

This is then normalized by repeatedly eliminating any occurrence of $2$ or adjacent $11$ using the identity $w_i = w_{i+1}+w_{i+2}$. Recursion (148) encodes this as a finite set of digit transitions, applied recursively to the affected suffix.

### Borrow propagation form

A deficit at position $i$ (digit $-1$) is resolved by replacing

$$
-w_i = -(w_{i+1}+w_{i+2}) = -w_{i+1} - w_{i+2}.
$$

Again this is expressed in recursion (148) as local digit updates that propagate leftward.

Thus recursion (148) is a deterministic rewrite system acting on the rightmost non-admissible position after an increment or decrement.

## 2. Correct treatment of the coefficient $2w_0$

The previous argument failed by treating $2w_0$ as if it were a single weight expansion. The correct interpretation is:

$$
2w_0 = w_0 + w_0.
$$

After adding $1=w_0$, a second $w_0$ is introduced. Each occurrence must be eliminated using the identity independently.

Apply the identity to each copy:

$$
w_0 = w_1 + w_2,
$$

so

$$
2w_0 = (w_1+w_2) + (w_1+w_2) = 2w_1 + 2w_2.
$$

This produces digit values $2$ at positions $1$ and $2$. Recursion (148) is precisely the mechanism that resolves any digit $2$.

The missing step is therefore:

### Local normalization rule for digit 2

Whenever a digit $2$ appears at position $i$, it is replaced by

$$
2w_i = w_{i+1} + w_{i+2} + w_i.
$$

This produces either:

- a new carry at $i+1$, or
- an adjacency $11$, which is then immediately eliminated by the same identity in reverse.

Thus coefficient 2 is never handled in one step. It is eliminated through repeated local applications of the identity, exactly as encoded recursively in (148).

## 3. Explicit reconstruction of recursion (148)

Recursion (148) can be stated in operational form as follows.

Let $\alpha = (\alpha_k\cdots\alpha_0)$. Define a procedure that acts on the smallest index $i$ where the digit pattern is non-admissible after an increment or decrement.

### Successor recursion

To compute $S(\alpha)$, start with the effect of adding $1$ at position $0$.

#### Step S1: initial addition

Set

$$
\alpha_0 \leftarrow \alpha_0 + 1.
$$

This may create:

- digit $1$ (no action needed), or
- digit $2$ (requires normalization).

#### Step S2: local normalization at index $i$

If $\alpha_i = 2$, apply:

$$
(\alpha_i,\alpha_{i+1},\alpha_{i+2}) \mapsto (\alpha_i-2,\alpha_{i+1}+1,\alpha_{i+2}+1),
$$

which is exactly the digit form of

$$
2w_i = w_{i+1} + w_{i+2} + w_i - w_i.
$$

This preserves value and removes the coefficient 2.

#### Step S3: eliminate adjacency $11$

If $\alpha_i = \alpha_{i+1} = 1$, apply:

$$
(\alpha_i,\alpha_{i+1}) \mapsto (0,0), \quad \alpha_{i+2} \leftarrow \alpha_{i+2} + 1.
$$

This is the digit translation of

$$
w_i + w_{i+1} = w_{i+2}.
$$

#### Step S4: recursion

Repeat Steps S2 and S3 at the smallest index where a violation appears.

This is recursion (148).

## 4. Predecessor recursion

For $P(\alpha)$, start by subtracting $1=w_0$:

#### Step P1: initial subtraction

$$
\alpha_0 \leftarrow \alpha_0 - 1.
$$

If $\alpha_0 = -1$, normalization is required.

#### Step P2: borrow expansion

Replace

$$
-w_i = -(w_{i+1}+w_{i+2}) = -w_{i+1} - w_{i+2}.
$$

Digit-wise, a deficit at position $i$ triggers:

$$
(\alpha_i,\alpha_{i+1},\alpha_{i+2}) \mapsto (\alpha_i+2,\alpha_{i+1}-1,\alpha_{i+2}-1),
$$

followed by normalization.

#### Step P3: normalization

Apply the same two rules as in the successor case:

- eliminate $2$,
- eliminate $11$,

propagating leftward until all digits are in $\{0,1\}$.

## 5. Why the recursion terminates

Define a measure:

$$
M(\alpha)=\sum_i \alpha_i 2^i.
$$

Each application of the normalization rules strictly decreases the highest index where a violation occurs:

- resolving $2$ moves weight from index $i$ to $i+1,i+2$, but removes the offending digit at $i$,
- resolving $11$ replaces two adjacent digits by a single digit at a higher index, reducing local density.

Because only finitely many digits are nonzero and indices increase only finitely often before stabilization, repeated application must terminate.

## 6. Resolution of all issues

1. The coefficient $2w_0$ is handled by splitting into two independent copies of $w_0$, each normalized separately.
2. Recursion (148) is explicitly reconstructed as a finite local rewrite system on digit triples.
3. Adjacency handling is given as a precise digit rule derived from $w_i+w_{i+1}=w_{i+2}$.
4. Predecessor is treated symmetrically via explicit borrow expansion.
5. Termination is justified by a decreasing measure and bounded propagation.

This fully reconstructs recursion (148) as an explicit algorithm derived directly from $w_i=w_{i+1}+w_{i+2}$.
