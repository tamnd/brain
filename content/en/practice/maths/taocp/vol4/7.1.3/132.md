## (a)

The proof in the previous solution is correct, so we keep it brief.

For each $v\in V$, the bit $v$ of $\tau(U)$ is $1$ exactly when every factor $(u\in U?\rho_u:\delta_u)$ has a $1$ in position $v$.

- If $U$ is a clique, then for $u\in U$, every vertex in $U$ is adjacent to $u$, so $\rho_u$ contains all bits of $U$. For $u\notin U$, $\delta_u$ only forbids bit $u$, irrelevant for vertices in $U$. Hence $\tau(U)=\sigma(U)$.
- Conversely, if $\tau(U)=\sigma(U)$, then for any $u,v\in U$, the $v$-bit of $\tau(U)$ is $1$, so the factor $\rho_u$ must also have a $1$ in position $v$. Hence $u=v$ or $u\sim v$, so $U$ is a clique.

Thus,

$$
U \text{ is a clique} \iff \tau(U)=\sigma(U).
$$

## (b) Corrected proof

Assume

$$
\tau(U)=\sigma(T).
$$

We prove that $T$ is a clique.

### Step 1: Show $T\subseteq U$

Let $v\in T$. Then the $v$-bit of $\tau(U)$ is $1$. By definition,

$$
\tau(U)=\bigwedge_{0\le u<n}(u\in U?\rho_u:\delta_u).
$$

If $v\notin U$, then the factor corresponding to $u=v$ is $\delta_v$, and $\delta_v$ has a $0$ in position $v$. This would force the $v$-bit of $\tau(U)$ to be $0$, contradiction.

Hence $v\in U$, so

$$
T\subseteq U.
$$

### Step 2: Prove adjacency inside $T$

Take any $x,y\in T$. From Step 1, $x\in U$, so the factor $\rho_x$ appears in the definition of $\tau(U)$.

Since $y\in T$, the $y$-bit of $\tau(U)$ is $1$. Therefore every factor in the intersection, in particular $\rho_x$, has a $1$ in position $y$. Thus

$$
y\in \rho_x \quad\Rightarrow\quad x=y \text{ or } x\sim y.
$$

Since this holds for all $x,y\in T$, the set $T$ is a clique.

Hence,

$$
\tau(U)=\sigma(T)\implies T \text{ is a clique}.
$$

## (c)

We correct only the dependence on (b); the structure is otherwise valid.

From (a), any clique $U$ satisfies $\sigma(U)=\tau(U)\in C_n$.

### ($\Rightarrow$)

Let $U$ be a maximal clique. Then $\sigma(U)\in C_n$.

If $\sigma(U)$ were not maximal in $C_n$, there would exist $x\in C_n$ with

$$
\sigma(U)\subsetneq x.
$$

By definition of $C_n$, $x=\tau(W)$ for some $W$. By (b), $W$ is a clique. One can extract from $W$ a clique strictly containing $U$, contradicting maximality of $U$. Hence $\sigma(U)\in C_n^+$.

### ($\Leftarrow$)

Suppose $\sigma(U)\in C_n^+$. Since $\sigma(U)\in C_n$, there exists $W$ with

$$
\tau(W)=\sigma(U).
$$

By (b), $U$ is a clique.

If $U$ were not maximal, there would be a clique $T\supsetneq U$, implying

$$
\sigma(U)\subsetneq \sigma(T)\in C_n,
$$

contradicting maximality in $C_n$. Hence $U$ is maximal.

Therefore,

$$
U \text{ is a maximal clique} \iff \sigma(U)\in C_n^+.
$$

## (d)

The recurrence for $C_k$ is correctly derived:

$$
C_k=
\{x\&\delta_{k-1}:x\in C_{k-1}\}\;\cup\;\{x\&\rho_{k-1}:x\in C_{k-1}\}.
$$

Bitwise intersection is monotone:

$$
x\subseteq y \;\Rightarrow\; x\&a \subseteq y\&a.
$$

Hence descendants of non-maximal elements of $C_{k-1}$ are always contained in descendants of maximal ones, so non-maximal elements can be ignored.

### Algorithm

Start with

$$
C_0^+=\{2^n-1\}.
$$

For $k=1,\dots,n$:

1. For each $x\in C_{k-1}^+$, compute

$$
x\&\delta_{k-1},\quad x\&\rho_{k-1}.
$$
2. Remove duplicates.
3. Remove any element properly contained in another.

The result is $C_k^+$.

At $k=n$, $C_n^+$ is obtained, and by (c) these are exactly the bit encodings of all maximal cliques.
