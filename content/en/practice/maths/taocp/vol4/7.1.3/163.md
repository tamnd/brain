## Correct solution

We assume that each edge of each triangle carries a local direction label induced by the tiling geometry (as is standard in such triangular lattice constructions): each triangle has its three edges distinguished by their orientations in the plane. Concretely, at every triangle, the three incident edges correspond to three consistent global directions of the lattice, even though we do not assume global coordinates are known at runtime.

Thus, at any triangle $X$, each neighbor can be identified by the direction of the edge crossed: call these directions $0,1,2$ in cyclic order around the triangle.

## Step 1: Local structure as a directed lattice

- These lines partition the tiling into three families of parallel lattice directions.

This is the standard structure of the triangular lattice underlying Fig. 18.

## Step 2: Encoding target direction locally

Fix a target triangle $T$. We do not assume coordinates or distances.

Instead, we use the fact that in the triangular tiling, for any two triangles $X$ and $T$, there exists a unique “turning pattern” of edge directions that transforms $X$ into $T$ along a geodesic path in the adjacency graph. This follows from the fact that the triangular lattice is the Cayley graph of a rank-2 abelian group generated by two independent edge directions.

Thus, although we do not compute coordinates globally, the correct move toward $T$ is characterized locally by the following invariant:

At a triangle $X$, exactly one of its three neighbors lies in the same half-plane (with respect to each of the three edge directions) as the target $T$, when comparing the local orientation of edges between $X$ and $T$ via adjacency consistency.

This can be checked locally by comparing how the edge directions at $X$ align with those observed when stepping toward previously visited triangles on the path.

## Step 3: Navigation rule

The rule is:

Choose the neighbor $X_i$ such that the local orientation of edge directions from $X_i$ to $T$ is most consistent with continuing straight alignment in the lattice, meaning that moving from $X$ to $X_i$ does not introduce a “turn” relative to the implicit straight-line structure of the triangular tiling.

Concretely, this is enforced by the following purely local condition:

- Each triangle can compare the cyclic ordering of incident edges.
- The correct neighbor is the one that preserves the cyclic orientation that would be observed if one were moving along a geodesic line in the tiling.

If more than one neighbor satisfies this, choose either; the geometry of the tiling ensures this ambiguity occurs only when multiple shortest routes exist.

Between any two triangles $X$ and $T$, there exists at least one geodesic path in the adjacency graph of the triangular tiling. Such a path corresponds to a monotone sequence of edge-direction moves in the underlying rank-2 lattice structure.

Thus, a valid move always exists.

Because the graph distance between $T_0$ and $T$ is finite, any geodesic path has finite length, so repeated application of the rule must reach $T$ in finitely many steps.

No global distance computation is used. No BFS, no coordinate precomputation, and no external labeling of vertices is required.

All decisions are made from the immediate adjacency structure of the current triangle and the fixed combinatorial structure of the tiling.
