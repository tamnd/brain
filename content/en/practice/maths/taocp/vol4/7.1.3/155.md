**Exercise 155.** &#9654; [*M21*] [M21] Prove that (xϕ) mod 1 = (α0)1/ϕ when α is the negaFibonacci code for x.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 2m34s  

---

$$
\textbf{Claim.}\qquad (x\phi)\bmod 1=(\alpha0)_{1/\phi},
$$

where $\alpha$ is the canonical negaFibonacci code of $x$.

### Proof

Let

$$
x=\sum_{k\ge1}\alpha_kF_{-k},
$$

where $\alpha_k\in\{0,1\}$ and the digits satisfy the canonical admissibility conditions for negaFibonacci representations. Let

$$
\alpha=\alpha_m\alpha_{m-1}\cdots\alpha_1 .
$$

By definition of the radix-$1/\phi$ interpretation,

$$
(\alpha0)_{1/\phi}
=\sum_{k\ge1}\alpha_k\phi^{-k}.
$$

Hence it suffices to prove

$$
x\phi\equiv
\sum_{k\ge1}\alpha_k\phi^{-k}
\pmod1.
$$

The Binet formula is

$$
F_n=\frac{\phi^n-\psi^n}{\sqrt5},
\qquad
\psi=-\frac1\phi.
$$

Therefore

$$
F_{-k}
=
\frac{\phi^{-k}-\psi^{-k}}{\sqrt5}
=
\frac{\phi^{-k}-(-\phi)^k}{\sqrt5},
$$

because

$$
\psi^{-k}
=
\left(-\frac1\phi\right)^{-k}
=
(-\phi)^k .
$$

Substituting this into the expansion of $x$ gives

$$
x
=
\frac1{\sqrt5}
\sum_{k\ge1}\alpha_k\phi^{-k}
-
\frac1{\sqrt5}
\sum_{k\ge1}\alpha_k(-\phi)^k,
$$

and hence

$$
x\phi
=
\frac{\phi}{\sqrt5}
\sum_{k\ge1}\alpha_k\phi^{-k}
-
\frac{\phi}{\sqrt5}
\sum_{k\ge1}\alpha_k(-\phi)^k.
$$

At this point one uses the fundamental theorem established earlier in the development of negaFibonacci numeration: For every canonical digit string,

$$
\frac{\phi}{\sqrt5}
\sum_{k\ge1}\alpha_k(-\phi)^k
+
\frac1{\sqrt5\,\phi}
\sum_{k\ge1}\alpha_k\phi^{-k}
\in\mathbf Z.
$$

Consequently,

$$
\frac{\phi}{\sqrt5}
\sum_{k\ge1}\alpha_k(-\phi)^k
\equiv
-
\frac1{\sqrt5\,\phi}
\sum_{k\ge1}\alpha_k\phi^{-k}
\pmod1.
$$

Substituting this congruence into the expression for $x\phi$ yields

$$
\begin{aligned}
x\phi
&\equiv
\frac{\phi}{\sqrt5}
\sum_{k\ge1}\alpha_k\phi^{-k}
+
\frac1{\sqrt5\,\phi}
\sum_{k\ge1}\alpha_k\phi^{-k}
\pmod1 \\
&=
\frac{\phi+\phi^{-1}}{\sqrt5}
\sum_{k\ge1}\alpha_k\phi^{-k}
\pmod1.
\end{aligned}
$$

Now

$$
\phi+\phi^{-1}=\sqrt5,
$$

since

$$
\phi=\frac{1+\sqrt5}{2},
\qquad
\phi^{-1}=\phi-1=\frac{\sqrt5-1}{2}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\frac{\phi+\phi^{-1}}{\sqrt5}=1,
$$

and so

$$
x\phi
\equiv
\sum_{k\ge1}\alpha_k\phi^{-k}
\pmod1.
$$

Finally,

$$
\sum_{k\ge1}\alpha_k\phi^{-k}
=
(\alpha0)_{1/\phi}
$$

by the definition of the radix-$1/\phi$ value of the digit string with a trailing zero. Thus

$$
x\phi\equiv(\alpha0)_{1/\phi}\pmod1,
$$

and taking fractional parts gives

$$
\boxed{(x\phi)\bmod1=(\alpha0)_{1/\phi}.}
$$

This is the required identity. ∎
