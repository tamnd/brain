[Section 7.1.3: Bitwise Tricks and Techniques](../)

**Exercise 28.** [*16*] What does the operation $(x + 1) \,\&\, \bar{x}$ produce?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 58s  

---

## Solution

Let $x = (\ldots x_2 x_1 x_0)_2$ and let $k$ be the least index such that $x_k = 0$. Then $x_0 = x_1 = \cdots = x_{k-1} = 1$, and $x_k = 0$.

Write $x$ in the form

$x = u\,0\,1^k,$

where $u$ denotes the prefix bits $(\ldots x_{k+1})$.

Adding $1$ to $x$ flips the trailing block $1^k$ to $0^k$ and changes the $0$ at position $k$ to $1$, giving

$x+1 = u\,1\,0^k.$

The bitwise complement of $x$ is

$\bar{x} = \bar{u}\,1\,0^k,$

since complementation flips each bit, so $1^k$ becomes $0^k$ and $0$ becomes $1$.

Now compute the bitwise AND:

$(x+1)\,\&\,\bar{x} = (u\,1\,0^k)\,\&\,(\bar{u}\,1\,0^k).$

Bitwise AND is performed positionwise, so the prefix yields $u ,&, \bar{u} = 0$ by the identity $x ,&, \bar{x} = 0$ in (13). At position $k$, we have $1 ,&, 1 = 1$ by (11). At positions $0$ through $k-1$, we have $0 ,&, 0 = 0$.

Thus the result is the number with a single $1$ in position $k$ and $0$ elsewhere:

$(x+1)\,\&\,\bar{x} = (0\ldots010^k)_2 = 2^k.$

Since $k$ is the position of the least significant $0$-bit of $x$, this produces the power of $2$ corresponding to that bit position.

Therefore,

$\boxed{(x+1)\,\&\,\bar{x} \text{ is the number with a single 1 at the least significant 0-bit of } x.}$

This completes the solution. ∎
