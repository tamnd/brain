**Exercise 8.** &#9654; [*M22*] Given a finite set $S$ of nonnegative integers, the "minimal exclulant" of $S$ is defined to be

$$\mathrm{mex}(S) = \min\{k \mid k \ge 0 \text{ and } k \notin S\}.$$

Let $x \oplus S$ denote the set $\{x \oplus y \mid y \in S\}$, and let $S \oplus y$ denote $\{x \oplus y \mid x \in S\}$. Prove that if $x = \mathrm{mex}(S)$ and $y = \mathrm{mex}(T)$ then $x \oplus y = \mathrm{mex}((S \oplus y) \cup (x \oplus T))$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 41s

## Solution

Let

$U=(S\oplus y)\cup(x\oplus T).$

We prove that

$x\oplus y=\mathrm{mex}(U),$

which requires showing first that $x\oplus y\notin U$, and second that every nonnegative integer smaller than $x\oplus y$ belongs to $U$.

Since $x=\mathrm{mex}(S)$, we have

$x\notin S,$

and every integer $a$ with $0\le a<x$ satisfies $a\in S$. Likewise,

$y\notin T,$

and every integer $b$ with $0\le b<y$ satisfies $b\in T$.

Suppose that

$x\oplus y\in S\oplus y.$

Then there is some $s\in S$ such that

$s\oplus y=x\oplus y.$

Applying $\oplus y$ to both sides gives

$s=x,$

because $y\oplus y=0$. This contradicts $x\notin S$. Hence

$x\oplus y\notin S\oplus y.$

Similarly, suppose that

$x\oplus y\in x\oplus T.$

Then there is some $t\in T$ such that

$x\oplus t=x\oplus y.$

Applying $\oplus x$ to both sides gives

$t=y,$

which contradicts $y\notin T$. Therefore

$x\oplus y\notin U.$

It remains to prove that every integer $z$ satisfying

$0\le z<x\oplus y$

belongs to $U$. Write the binary expansions

$x=(\cdots x_2x_1x_0)_2,\qquad y=(\cdots y_2y_1y_0)_2.$

Let $i$ be the most significant position at which $z$ differs from $x\oplus y$. Since

$z<x\oplus y,$

the bit of $x\oplus y$ at position $i$ is $1$ and the bit of $z$ at position $i$ is $0$. Thus

$x_i\oplus y_i=1.$

There are two cases.

If

$x_i=1,\qquad y_i=0,$

define

$s=z\oplus y.$

For every position above $i$, $z$ and $x\oplus y$ have identical bits, so $s$ and $x$ have identical bits above $i$. At position $i$,

$s_i=z_i\oplus y_i=0\oplus0=0,$

whereas

$x_i=1.$

Therefore

$s<x.$

By the definition of $x=\mathrm{mex}(S)$, every nonnegative integer less than $x$ belongs to $S$, so

$s\in S.$

Hence

$z=s\oplus y\in S\oplus y\subseteq U.$

If instead

$x_i=0,\qquad y_i=1,$

define

$t=z\oplus x.$

Again, the bits above $i$ agree with those of $y$. At position $i$,

$t_i=z_i\oplus x_i=0\oplus0=0,$

while

$y_i=1.$

Therefore

$t<y.$

The definition of $y=\mathrm{mex}(T)$ gives

$t\in T.$

Consequently,

$z=t\oplus x\in x\oplus T\subseteq U.$

Every $z<x\oplus y$ lies in $U$, and $x\oplus y$ does not lie in $U$. Therefore

$\mathrm{mex}(U)=x\oplus y.$

This completes the proof.

$$
\boxed{x\oplus y=\mathrm{mex}((S\oplus y)\cup(x\oplus T))}
$$

∎
