**Exercise 31.** &#9654; [*20*] A mathematician named Dr. L. I. Presume decided to calculate the ruler function with a simple loop as follows: "Set $\rho \leftarrow 0$; then while $x \mathbin{\&} 1 = 0$, set $\rho \leftarrow \rho + 1$ and $x \leftarrow x \gg 1$." He reasoned that, when $x$ is a random integer, the average number of right shifts is the average value of $\rho$, which is 1; and the standard deviation is only $\sqrt{2}$, so the loop almost always terminates quickly. Criticize his decision.

## Solution

Let $\rho$ denote the number of executed iterations of the loop, so $\rho$ is the largest integer such that $x \mathbin{&} (2^{\rho}-1)=0$ and either $x=0$ or the bit in position $\rho$ of $x$ equals $1$. The loop therefore computes the 2-adic valuation $v_2(x)$, stopping precisely when the least significant 1-bit of $x$ is reached.

Dr. L. I. Presume’s argument assumes that $x$ is a “random integer” and treats the bits of $x$ as independent Bernoulli variables with parameter $1/2$. Under this assumption,

$\Pr(\rho \ge k) = 2^{-k},$

since $\rho \ge k$ holds exactly when the lowest $k$ bits of $x$ are all zero. This distribution gives

$\mathbb{E}[\rho] = \sum_{k \ge 1} \Pr(\rho \ge k) = \sum_{k \ge 1} 2^{-k} = 1,$

and

$\mathrm{Var}(\rho) = \sum_{k \ge 1} k^2 2^{-k} - 1 = 2,$

so the standard deviation is $\sqrt{2}$.

The first defect in the reasoning is that the model of a “random integer” is not well defined on the set of all integers. Any meaningful probabilistic interpretation must restrict attention to a finite set, such as the integers in ${0,1,\dots,2^w-1}$ for a fixed word size $w$. Without such a restriction, the notion of uniform randomness on all integers is undefined, so the expectation and deviation calculations have no formal basis.

Within a fixed $w$-bit model, the computation is well defined, but the behavior changes in a crucial corner case: when $x=0$, the condition $x \mathbin{&} 1=0$ remains true after every right shift, so the loop does not terminate. This single input contradicts the implicit assumption that termination is guaranteed.

Even when restricting to nonzero inputs, the inference “standard deviation $\sqrt{2}$ implies almost always terminates quickly” is not valid. The distribution of $\rho$ has exponential tail

$\Pr(\rho \ge k)=2^{-k},$

so while large values are rare, they are not excluded, and no finite concentration statement follows from the variance alone. In particular, a standard deviation bound does not control tail behavior strongly enough to justify a claim of “almost always” without an explicit inequality such as the geometric tail estimate above.

In a bounded word model, the loop runs at most $w$ iterations, so worst-case behavior is linear in the word size. This worst-case occurs when $x$ is divisible by $2^{w-1}$, and therefore cannot be dismissed in an algorithmic cost analysis.

The expectation being equal to $1$ does not imply that the loop is reliably fast for individual inputs; it only describes an average over a chosen distribution. Algorithmic suitability requires either a worst-case guarantee or a clearly specified probabilistic model with proven concentration bounds, neither of which is established in the given reasoning.

This completes the critique. ∎
