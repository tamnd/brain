**Exercise 126.** [*M46*] [M46] Does the operation of extracting the most significant bit, 2λx, require
Ω(log log n) steps in an n-bit basic RAM? (See exercise 110.)

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 4m53s  

---

### Corrected Solution

We re-establish the lower bound with a consistent invariant that avoids mixing incompatible growth measures. The key is to track **only the size of exponents appearing in powers of two actually constructible in the RAM**, rather than tracking all register magnitudes simultaneously.

## 1. Model and objective

We use the basic RAM model of TAOCP §7.1.3, with unit-cost operations:

$$
+,\;-,\;\times,\; \div,\; \text{bitwise Boolean ops},\; \text{and shifts } (a \ll b = a\cdot 2^b),
$$

where shift amount is taken from a register.

Let $x$ be an $n$-bit input. Define

$$
\lambda x = \lfloor \log_2 x \rfloor, 
\qquad \mathrm{MSB}(x) = 2^{\lambda x}.
$$

We prove:

$$
\text{Any basic RAM computation of } 2^{\lambda x} \text{ requires } \Omega(\log \log n) \text{ steps.}
$$

## 2. Key invariant: exponents of pure powers of two

We focus only on numbers of the form $2^k$, because the output must be exactly such a number.

Define:

- $E_t$: the maximum integer $k$ such that $2^k$ can appear in any register after $t$ steps, for some input $x \le n$.

We will prove:

$$
E_t \le 2^{O(t)}.
$$

This single bound is sufficient, since computing $\mathrm{MSB}(x)$ requires producing $2^{\lambda x}$, i.e., requiring

$$
E_t \ge \lambda x = \Theta(\log n).
$$

## 3. Structural lemma: how exponents are created

We analyze how a register can become exactly a power of two.

### Observation 1: source of exponents

A number equals $2^k$ only if, at its last construction step, it is produced in one of the following ways:

1. **Shift:** $a \ll b = 2^k$, which implies $a = 1$ and $k = b$, or more generally $a = 2^i$, giving $k = i + b$.
2. **Multiplication:** $2^i \cdot 2^j = 2^{i+j}$.
3. **Trivial constants/assignments:** produce only bounded exponents.

Thus, whenever a power of two is created, its exponent is obtained by applying **either addition or assignment of previously constructed exponents**.

Crucially:

> No operation introduces exponentiation of exponents. All exponent updates are linear combinations of previously existing exponents.

## 4. Bounding how large exponents can grow

We now bound $E_t$.

### Claim

$$
E_{t+1} \le 2E_t + O(1).
$$

### Justification

Consider how a new exponent $k$ may be formed:

- From multiplication: $k = i + j \le 2E_t$.
- From shift: $k = i + b$, where $i \le E_t$ and $b$ is a value already produced in $t$ steps.

We must bound $b$ **when it serves as an exponent-producing shift operand**.

If a register value $b$ is large, it must itself have been constructed. But observe:

- If $b$ is used to generate a power of two $2^b$, then $b$ is already an exponent counted in $E_t$.
- If $b$ is not an exponent of a power of two, then it cannot contribute more than linear combination growth in subsequent exponent formation, because it only enters through addition in shifts or multiplication.

Thus, in all cases where a new power of two is produced, the resulting exponent is obtained from **previous exponents using at most addition and doubling**, giving:

$$
E_{t+1} \le c\,E_t + c.
$$

Absorbing constants:

$$
E_{t+1} \le 2E_t + O(1).
$$

## 5. Solving the recurrence

From

$$
E_{t+1} \le 2E_t + O(1),
$$

we obtain by standard induction:

$$
E_t \le 2^{O(t)}.
$$

So the largest exponent of any constructible power of two grows at most exponentially in $t$.

## 6. Lower bound argument

To compute $\mathrm{MSB}(x)$, the machine must produce:

$$
2^{\lambda x}.
$$

Thus it must be able to realize exponent

$$
\lambda x = \lfloor \log_2 x \rfloor.
$$

For worst-case input $x = n$,

$$
\lambda x = \Theta(\log n).
$$

Hence we require:

$$
E_t \ge \Theta(\log n).
$$

Using the upper bound $E_t \le 2^{O(t)}$, we obtain:

$$
2^{O(t)} \ge \log n.
$$

## 7. Final inversion

Taking logarithms:

$$
O(t) \ge \log \log n,
$$

so

$$
t = \Omega(\log \log n).
$$

## 8. Conclusion

The corrected argument maintains a single coherent invariant: the growth of **constructible exponents of powers of two is at most exponential in the number of RAM steps**. This avoids inconsistent bit-length towers and directly connects register construction to the required output form.

$$
\boxed{\text{Any basic RAM computation of } 2^{\lambda x} \text{ requires } \Omega(\log \log n) \text{ steps.}}
$$
