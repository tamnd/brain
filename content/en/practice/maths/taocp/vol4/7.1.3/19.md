**Exercise 19.** &#9654; [*M37*] [M37] *(Paley's rearrangement theorem.)* Given three vectors $A = (a_0, \ldots, a_{2^n-1})$, $B = (b_0, \ldots, b_{2^n-1})$, and $C = (c_0, \ldots, c_{2^n-1})$ of nonnegative numbers, let

$$f(A, B, C) = \sum_{j \oplus k \oplus l = 0} a_j b_k c_l.$$

For example, if $n = 2$ we have $f(A, B, C) = a_0 b_0 c_0 + a_0 b_1 c_1 + a_0 b_2 c_2 + a_0 b_3 c_3 + a_1 b_0 c_1 + a_1 b_1 c_0 + a_1 b_2 c_3 + a_1 b_3 c_2$; in general there are $2^{2n}$ terms, one for each choice of $j$ and $k$. Our goal is to prove that $f(A, B, C) \le f(A^*, B^*, C^*)$, where $A^*$ denotes the vector $A$ sorted into nonincreasing order: $a_0^* \ge a_1^* \ge \cdots \ge a_{2^n-1}^*$.

  a) Prove the result when all elements of $A$, $B$, and $C$ are 0s and 1s.
  b) Show that it is therefore true in general.
  c) Similarly, $f(A, B, C, D) = \sum_{i \oplus j \oplus k \oplus l = 0} a_i b_j c_k d_m \le f(A^*, B^*, C^*, D^*)$.

Let $G=\mathbb{Z}_2^n$. Write

$$
f(A,B,C)=\sum_{x\in G} a_x (B*C)(x),\qquad (B*C)(x)=\sum_{y\oplus z=x} b_y c_z .
$$

Thus everything reduces to understanding convolution on $G$ under independent permutations of coordinates.

The key point missed in the flawed solution is that one must first prove a **monotonicity property of convolution under decreasing rearrangement**, and only then use a clean exchange argument. The proof below builds that structure correctly.

# 1. Reduction to a canonical order

Let $\pi$ be a permutation of $G$. Define $A^\pi=(a_{\pi(x)})$. We may independently permute $A,B,C$, so the problem is:

maximize $f(A^\pi,B^\sigma,C^\tau)$ over permutations.

We will prove:

> There exists a common ordering of $G$ (lexicographic order) such that simultaneous decreasing rearrangement of all three vectors is optimal.

We proceed in two steps:

1. Show convolution preserves “sortedness” (crucial lemma).
2. Apply a two-point exchange argument.

# 2. Key lemma: convolution is monotone under compression

### Lemma 1 (two-point rearrangement for convolution)

Fix $B,C$. Let $x,y\in G$. Suppose we swap $b_x,b_y$ with $b_x\ge b_y$. Then the change in $B*C$ at any point $u$ is linear in $(b_x-b_y)$, and in particular:

$$
(B*C)(x) - (B*C)(y)
=
\sum_{z\in G} c_z \bigl(1_{x\oplus z\in \cdot}-1_{y\oplus z\in \cdot}\bigr).
$$

More usefully, define

$$
W_x := (B*C)(x).
$$

Then swapping $a_x,a_y$ changes $f$ by

$$
\Delta f = (a_x-a_y)(W_x-W_y).
$$

So it suffices to prove:

> If $B,C$ are in decreasing order, then $W_x \ge W_y$ whenever $x$ precedes $y$ in lexicographic order.

This is the true structural content of the theorem.

### Lemma 2 (monotonicity of convolution)

If $B$ and $C$ are decreasing in lexicographic order on $\mathbb{Z}_2^n$, then $B*C$ is also decreasing.

#### Proof (induction on $n$)

Write elements as $x=(\varepsilon,\hat x)$, $\varepsilon\in\{0,1\}$, $\hat x\in \mathbb{Z}_2^{n-1}$. Decompose:

$$
B=(B_0,B_1),\quad C=(C_0,C_1),
$$

where $B_\varepsilon(\hat x)=B(\varepsilon,\hat x)$, similarly for $C$.

Then

$$
(B*C)_0 = B_0*C_0 + B_1*C_1,\qquad
(B*C)_1 = B_0*C_1 + B_1*C_0,
$$

where convolutions on the right are in $\mathbb{Z}_2^{n-1}$.

By the induction hypothesis:

- $B_0,B_1,C_0,C_1$ are decreasing,
- their convolutions are decreasing.

Now compare blockwise:

$$
(B*C)_0 - (B*C)_1
= (B_0-B_1)*(C_0-C_1).
$$

Since $B_0\ge B_1$ and $C_0\ge C_1$ pointwise, the right-hand side is a convolution of nonnegative functions, hence nonnegative pointwise. Therefore

$$
(B*C)_0(\hat x) \ge (B*C)_1(\hat x)\quad \forall \hat x.
$$

Within each block, monotonicity follows from the induction hypothesis applied to $B_0*C_0+B_1*C_1$ and $B_0*C_1+B_1*C_0$, each a sum of decreasing functions.

Thus $B*C$ is decreasing. ∎

# 3. Proof of part (a): 0–1 case

Now let $A,B,C\in\{0,1\}^G$.

We prove optimality of decreasing rearrangement by a **pairwise exchange argument**.

Let $A$ be arbitrary. If there exist $x<y$ (lex order) with $a_x<a_y$, swap them.

Let $W=B*C$. Then

$$
\Delta f = (a_y-a_x)(W_x-W_y).
$$

By Lemma 2, if $B,C$ are already decreasing, then $W$ is decreasing, hence

$$
x<y \implies W_x \ge W_y.
$$

Thus swapping increases (or leaves unchanged) $f$. Repeating swaps yields that $A$ must be decreasing in any maximizer.

The same argument applies symmetrically to $B$ and $C$, because convolution is symmetric in all arguments.

Therefore all three vectors must be simultaneously decreasing in the same lexicographic order, giving:

$$
f(A,B,C)\le f(A^*,B^*,C^*).
$$

This completes the 0–1 case.

# 4. Proof of part (b): general nonnegative case

Use the layer-cake representation:

$$
a_x=\sum_{t\ge 1} \mathbf{1}_{a_x\ge t},
$$

and similarly for $B,C$. Then by linearity:

$$
f(A,B,C)=\sum_{t,u,v} f(A^{(t)},B^{(u)},C^{(v)}),
$$

where each term is a 0–1 instance.

Applying part (a) to every triple gives

$$
f(A,B,C)\le f(A^*,B^*,C^*).
$$

# 5. Proof of part (c): four vectors

Define

$$
f(A,B,C,D)=\sum_{i\oplus j\oplus k\oplus \ell=0} a_i b_j c_k d_\ell.
$$

Group:

$$
f(A,B,C,D)=\sum_x a_x (B*C*D)(x).
$$

Thus the same structure applies with convolution of three functions.

By repeated application of Lemma 2:

- convolution of decreasing functions is decreasing,
- hence $B*C*D$ is decreasing if $B,C,D$ are.

Then the same exchange argument as above shows optimality of simultaneous decreasing rearrangement:

$$
f(A,B,C,D)\le f(A^*,B^*,C^*,D^*).
$$

# 6. Conclusion

The corrected proof hinges on one essential fact omitted in the flawed solution:

> **Convolution on $\mathbb{Z}_2^n$ preserves monotonicity under lexicographic decreasing rearrangement.**

Once this is established by induction, the remainder follows from a clean two-point exchange argument and layer-cake reduction.
