## Solution

Let the conic be given by the general quadratic equation

$$
C(x,y) \equiv ax^2 + bxy + cy^2 + dx + ey + f = 0,
$$

with rational coefficients and not all of $a,b,c$ zero.

A point of the conic is _$x$-critical_ when the projection onto the $x$-axis fails to be locally one-to-one, which occurs exactly at points where the tangent is vertical. For an implicit curve this is characterized by the simultaneous system

$$
C(x,y)=0, \qquad \frac{\partial C}{\partial y}(x,y)=0.
$$

Here

$$
\frac{\partial C}{\partial y} = bx + 2cy + e,
$$

so $x$-critical points satisfy a linear equation in $(x,y)$ together with a quadratic equation, hence form the intersection of a line and a conic. This system has at most two real solutions unless the conic contains a vertical line component, in which case the conic degenerates and is handled separately as a line or pair of lines.

Similarly, a point is _$y$-critical_ when the tangent is horizontal, characterized by

$$
C(x,y)=0, \qquad \frac{\partial C}{\partial x}(x,y)=0,
$$

where

$$
\frac{\partial C}{\partial x} = 2ax + by + d.
$$

Again this gives at most two points unless the conic degenerates with a horizontal line component.

Let $P$ be the finite set consisting of all $x$-critical and $y$-critical points of the conic, together with endpoints of any unbounded branches when the conic is viewed projectively. Since each condition above is obtained by solving a linear equation together with the quadratic $C(x,y)=0$, the set $P$ contains finitely many points, at most four in the nondegenerate case.

The real locus of the conic minus $P$ is a finite union of connected open arcs. On each such arc, neither $\partial C/\partial x$ nor $\partial C/\partial y$ vanishes simultaneously with $C$, so the implicit function theorem applies. On an arc where $\partial C/\partial y \neq 0$, the curve can be written locally as $y = y(x)$ with

$$
\frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{C_x}{C_y},
$$

and the sign of $C_y$ does not change on the arc because no $y$-critical point lies inside it. Hence $y(x)$ is monotone in $x$ on that arc. Symmetrically, on arcs where $\partial C/\partial x \neq 0$, the curve is monotone in $y$.

Between consecutive points of $P$ along the curve ordering, the sign pattern of $(C_x, C_y)$ is constant, so each resulting arc is monotone in at least one coordinate direction. Subdividing the conic at all points of $P$ therefore yields pieces each of which is either $x$-monotone or $y$-monotone.

Degenerate cases where $C$ factors into linear components produce straight-line segments, which are trivially monotone in both $x$ and $y$, so no further subdivision is required beyond separating the components.

Each monotone arc produced in this subdivision satisfies the hypotheses required for Algorithm T, since its projection onto at least one coordinate axis is injective and its endpoints are explicitly known. Therefore the conic can be partitioned into finitely many monotone parts on which Algorithm T applies.

This completes the proof. ∎
