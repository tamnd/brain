**Exercise 2.** [*16*] (H. S. Warren, Jr.) Are any of the following relations valid for all integers $x$ and $y$? (i) $x \oplus y \le x \mid y$; (ii) $x \mathbin{\&} y \le x \mid y$; (iii) $|x - y| \le x \oplus y$.

## Solution

Let $x = (\ldots x_2 x_1 x_0)_2$ and $y = (\ldots y_2 y_1 y_0)_2$, with bitwise definitions as in (1)–(3). All comparisons are in the usual integer order on two’s complement integers.

### (i) $x \oplus y \le x \mid y$

For every bit position $k \ge 0$, the Boolean relation $x_k \oplus y_k \le x_k \vee y_k$ holds, since the truth table of XOR and OR gives $0 \le 0$, $1 \le 1$, and $0 \le 1$ in the three relevant cases and never produces $1 > 0$.

Thus each binary digit of $x \oplus y$ is bounded above by the corresponding digit of $x \mid y$. Writing both numbers in binary expansion,

$x \oplus y = \sum_{k \ge 0} (x_k \oplus y_k) 2^k, \qquad x \mid y = \sum_{k \ge 0} (x_k \vee y_k) 2^k,$

and comparing termwise with nonnegative weights $2^k$, yields

$x \oplus y \le x \mid y.$

This holds for all integers $x,y$.

### (ii) $x \mathbin{&} y \le x \mid y$

For every bit position $k \ge 0$, the Boolean relation $x_k \wedge y_k \le x_k \vee y_k$ holds, since $1 \le 1$ and $0 \le 0,1$ in all cases.

Hence each bit of $x \mathbin{&} y$ is bounded above by the corresponding bit of $x \mid y$. Using binary expansions,

$x \mathbin{\&} y = \sum_{k \ge 0} (x_k \wedge y_k) 2^k, \qquad x \mid y = \sum_{k \ge 0} (x_k \vee y_k) 2^k,$

and comparing coefficients with nonnegative weights gives

$x \mathbin{\&} y \le x \mid y.$

This holds for all integers $x,y$.

### (iii) $|x - y| \le x \oplus y$

The relation fails for general integers.

Take $x = -1$ and $y = 1$. In two’s complement, $x = (\ldots 1111)_2$ and $y = (\ldots 0001)_2$. Then

$x \oplus y = (\ldots 1110)_2 = -2.$

Also,

$|x - y| = |-2| = 2.$

Thus the claimed inequality becomes

$2 \le -2,$

which is false in the usual integer order.

Therefore the relation does not hold for all integers $x,y$.

## Notes

The inequalities in (i) and (ii) follow from bitwise domination: in each position the Boolean value on the left never exceeds the corresponding value on the right, and binary expansion preserves this ordering because all weights $2^k$ are positive. The inequality in (iii) fails because XOR does not preserve absolute differences once negative two’s complement carries are interpreted globally rather than bitwise. ∎
