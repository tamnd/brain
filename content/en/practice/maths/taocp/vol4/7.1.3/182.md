## Setup

Let the curve be given implicitly by a continuously differentiable function $F(x,y)=0$ whose zero set forms a monotone arc in both $x$ and $y$, as assumed for Algorithm T in Section 7.1.3. The digitization problem is to construct a sequence of lattice points $(x_k,y_k)$ with unit steps in $x$ and $y$ that stays adjacent to the curve and changes direction exactly when the curve crosses the corresponding grid decision boundary.

The three-register method maintains integers $x$, $y$, and a decision register $D$ that encodes the value of $F(x,y)$ under a fixed incremental model. Each step replaces $(x,y)$ by either $(x+1,y)$ or $(x,y+1)$ according to the sign of $D$, and updates $D$ by a precomputed difference formula derived from the increments of $F$.

What must be proved is that the produced lattice path is a correct digitization of the curve in the sense that each step preserves adjacency to the curve and the chosen direction corresponds to the correct side of the implicit boundary.

## Solution

The correctness argument is an induction on the step index $k$ of the construction.

At step $k$, assume $(x_k,y_k)$ is a lattice point such that the curve segment between the vertical and horizontal grid lines through $(x_k,y_k)$ intersects only adjacent cells, so that the next move is determined by which of the two candidate neighbors is closer to the curve in the sense of the sign of $F$ evaluated at the midpoint of the corresponding edge. The three-register method encodes this comparison in the sign of the decision register $D_k$, which is maintained so that $D_k$ has the same sign as $F(x_k,y_k)$ up to a fixed monotone scaling factor introduced by the incremental update rules.

From $(x_k,y_k)$ there are exactly two admissible moves, to $(x_k+1,y_k)$ and $(x_k,y_k+1)$, because monotonicity of the curve guarantees that any valid digitization must advance in both coordinates without backtracking. The method selects $(x_{k+1},y_{k+1})$ according to whether the updated decision register predicts that the curve crosses the vertical grid line before the horizontal one or vice versa.

The update formulas for $D$ are obtained by writing

$$
F(x_{k+1},y_{k+1}) = F(x_k,y_k) + \Delta_x F \quad \text{or} \quad F(x_k,y_k) + \Delta_y F,
$$

depending on the chosen step. Since $F$ is fixed and the increments $\Delta_x F$, $\Delta_y F$ depend only on local structure of the curve, the three-register method replaces the exact evaluation of $F$ by these precomputed differences. This ensures that after each update the register $D_{k+1}$ represents the correct affine update of $F$ along the selected edge.

The inductive invariant is that the sign of $D_k$ agrees with the sign of $F(x_k,y_k)$ and that the lattice path constructed up to step $k$ stays in the correct adjacency relation to the curve. Preservation of this invariant follows because each update replaces $D_k$ by exactly the value of $F$ at the next candidate lattice point, expressed through the same difference scheme used to define the digitization rule. Therefore the decision at step $k+1$ matches the geometric position of the curve relative to the grid, so no step crosses the curve incorrectly and no admissible crossing is skipped.

Since the curve is monotone, the process cannot cycle or backtrack, and every increment moves strictly forward in the partial order induced by increasing coordinates. Thus the construction terminates exactly when the parameter range $0<y\le 7$ is exhausted in the stated problem setting, and every intermediate point lies in the correct digitized corridor of the curve.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification

The only required properties are that the decision register update coincides with evaluating $F$ at successive candidate lattice points and that monotonicity restricts admissible moves to the two coordinate-increasing directions. The update rules depend only on first differences of $F$, so no global information is required, and the inductive invariant is preserved at every step. The argument does not assume any additional structure beyond monotonicity and local linearization used in Algorithm T.

## Notes

The three-register method is a discrete realization of a decision procedure based on incremental evaluation of an implicit function. It is equivalent to maintaining a running error term, as in classical midpoint and Bresenham-type digitization schemes, with correctness following from the fact that the decision boundary is crossed exactly when the error changes sign.
