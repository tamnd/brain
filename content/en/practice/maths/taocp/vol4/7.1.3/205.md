**Exercise 205.** &#9654; [*22*] [22] How would you perfectly unshuffle on MMIX, going from w in (175) back to z?

The error in the previous solution is the misinterpretation of what the SWAR “compaction” produces and therefore the incorrect reconstruction step. Once the even and odd bit streams have been compacted, they are no longer sparse bit patterns that must be scattered back into place. They are ordinary 32-bit words representing the two original halves. The final operation is therefore a word concatenation, not a bit-level interleaving.

We now restart the argument cleanly.

## Structure of the shuffle in (175)

The perfect shuffle in (175) takes a 64-bit word

$$
z = (z_{63}\dots z_0)_2
$$

and interleaves the bits of its lower and upper 32-bit halves.

Write

$$
z = a \cdot 2^{32} + b,
$$

where $a$ contains bits $z_{63}\dots z_{32}$ and $b$ contains bits $z_{31}\dots z_0$.

The shuffle produces a word $w$ such that:

- bits of $b$ occupy even positions of $w$,
- bits of $a$ occupy odd positions of $w$.

Equivalently,

$$
w_{2i} = b_i,\qquad w_{2i+1} = a_i,\qquad 0 \le i < 32.
$$

Thus unshuffling must recover $a$ and $b$ from even and odd bits of $w$, then concatenate them.

## Step 1: Separate even and odd bits

Let the standard masks be

$$
m_0 = 0x5555555555555555,\qquad
m_1 = 0xAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAA.
$$

Define

$$
x = w \,\&\, m_0,\qquad y = w \,\&\, m_1.
$$

Then:

- $x$ contains the even-position bits of $w$ (i.e., the bits of $b$),
- $y$ contains the odd-position bits of $w$ (i.e., shifted bits of $a$).

At this stage both $x$ and $y$ are sparse representations.

## Step 2: SWAR compaction (inverse of bit spreading)

We now convert sparse bit fields into dense 32-bit words using the standard SWAR compression network.

### Compaction of even bits

Define:

$$
x_0 = x.
$$

Then apply:

$$
\begin{aligned}
x_1 &= (x_0 \,\&\, 0x3333333333333333) \;|\; ((x_0 \gg 1) \,\&\, 0x3333333333333333), \\
x_2 &= (x_1 \,\&\, 0x0F0F0F0F0F0F0F0F) \;|\; ((x_1 \gg 2) \,\&\, 0x0F0F0F0F0F0F0F0F), \\
x_3 &= (x_2 \,\&\, 0x00FF00FF00FF00FF) \;|\; ((x_2 \gg 4) \,\&\, 0x00FF00FF00FF00FF), \\
x_4 &= (x_3 \,\&\, 0x0000FFFF0000FFFF) \;|\; ((x_3 \gg 8) \,\&\, 0x0000FFFF0000FFFF), \\
x_5 &= (x_4 \,\&\, 0x00000000FFFFFFFF) \;|\; ((x_4 \gg 16) \,\&\, 0x00000000FFFFFFFF).
\end{aligned}
$$

Then $x_5$ is the 32-bit word $b$.

### Compaction of odd bits

Apply the same network to $y$. Although $y$ is shifted into odd positions, the same compression still gathers the corresponding bits into a dense word:

$$
y_5 = a.
$$

Thus:

- $x_5 = b$,
- $y_5 = a$.

## Step 3: Correct reconstruction (key correction)

The previous solution incorrectly attempted

$$
x_5 \;|\; (y_5 \ll 1),
$$

which is invalid because it assumes the data remains in interleaved form.

After compaction, both $x_5$ and $y_5$ are _dense 32-bit values_. No further bit scattering is required.

We simply reverse the original concatenation of $z$:

$$
z = (y_5 \ll 32) \;|\; x_5.
$$

Substituting the identifications:

- $y_5 = a$,
- $x_5 = b$,

we obtain:

$$
z = (a \ll 32) \;|\; b,
$$

which is exactly the original 64-bit word.

## Why no scatter operation is needed

The key correction is conceptual:

- Before compaction, bits are interleaved and sparse.
- The SWAR network removes spacing and produces dense 32-bit integers.
- After compaction, the bit positions no longer correspond to the original global index structure.

Thus:

- “scatter back into even/odd positions” would apply only if we stopped after masking,
- but compaction has already completed the inverse mapping from sparse bit fields to dense words.

Therefore the correct final step is word-level concatenation, not bit-level reconstruction.

## Correctness justification

1. **Masking step is exact**: $m_0$ and $m_1$ partition even and odd positions of $w$.
2. **SWAR compaction is bijective on its image**: each stage preserves information while progressively eliminating gaps between relevant bits.
3. **No structural loss occurs**: each of $x_5$ and $y_5$ is a full 32-bit reconstruction of one original half.
4. **Final recombination is correct inverse of concatenation**: since $z = (a \ll 32) | b$, the inverse is exactly $a,b$ extraction followed by concatenation.

## Final result

$$
\boxed{
z = (\,\text{compact}(w \,\&\, 0xAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAA) \ll 32\,)\;|\;\text{compact}(w \,\&\, 0x5555555555555555)
}
$$

This is a correct MMIX implementation of the perfect unshuffle, fully finite and bit-precise.
