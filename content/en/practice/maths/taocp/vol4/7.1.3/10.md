**Exercise 10.** [*HM40*] (*Nimbers*, also known as *Conway's field.*) Continuing exercise 8, define the operation $x \otimes y$ of "nim multiplication" recursively by the formula

$$x \otimes y = \mathrm{mex}\{(x \otimes j) \oplus (i \otimes y) \oplus (i \otimes j) \mid 0 \le i < x, 0 \le j < y\}.$$

Prove that $\oplus$ and $\otimes$ define a *field* over the set of all nonnegative integers. Prove also that if $0 \le x, y < 2^{2^n}$ then $x \otimes y < 2^{2^n}$, and $2^{2^n} \otimes y = 2^{2^n} y$. (In particular, this field contains subfields of size $2^{2^n}$ for all $n \ge 0$.) Explain how to compute $x \otimes y$ efficiently.

## Corrected solution

Let $\oplus$ be nim addition from the preceding exercise. It is already known that $(\mathbb N,\oplus)$ is an abelian group. Define nim multiplication by

$$
x\otimes y=\operatorname{mex}\{(x\otimes j)\oplus(i\otimes y)\oplus(i\otimes j):0\le i<x,\ 0\le j<y\}.
$$

We prove that this makes $\mathbb N$ a field.

The proof requires a careful induction principle.

### Lemma 1. Uniqueness of recursively defined operations

Suppose $f:\mathbb N^2\to\mathbb N$ satisfies

$$
f(x,y)=\operatorname{mex}
\{f(x,j)\oplus f(i,y)\oplus f(i,j):i<x,\ j<y\}.
$$

Then $f$ is uniquely determined.

#### Proof

Induct on $x+y$. For a fixed pair $(x,y)$, every value occurring on the right has smaller first argument or smaller second argument:

$$
i+y<x+y,\qquad x+j<x+y.
$$

Therefore all values needed to compute $f(x,y)$ have already been determined. Hence the mex is forced. ∎

We will repeatedly use this lemma. Whenever we prove an identity, we must show that the proposed new function satisfies the same recursion, with all smaller instances already established.

## 1. Basic properties of $\otimes$

### Zero

For $x=0$, the set in the definition is empty, so

$$
0\otimes y=\operatorname{mex}(\varnothing)=0.
$$

The definition is symmetric in $x,y$, so

$$
x\otimes y=y\otimes x.
$$

Hence

$$
x\otimes0=0.
$$

## 2. Distributivity

We prove

$$
(a\oplus b)\otimes c=(a\otimes c)\oplus(b\otimes c).
$$

The previous proof failed because it used distributivity as its own induction hypothesis without controlling the arguments. We instead use induction on the sum of the three arguments.

Assume distributivity has already been proved whenever the total of the three arguments is smaller than $a+b+c$.

Fix $b$, and define

$$
F(x,y)=(x\oplus b)\otimes y\oplus(b\otimes y).
$$

We prove

$$
F(x,y)=x\otimes y
$$

for $x=a,y=c$.

By Lemma 1 it is enough to show that $F$ satisfies the multiplication recursion.

For $i<a$ and $j<c$, we have

$$
i+j+b<a+c+b,
$$

so the induction hypothesis applies. Thus

$$
(i\oplus b)\otimes j
=
(i\otimes j)\oplus(b\otimes j).
$$

Therefore

$$
F(i,j)
=
((i\oplus b)\otimes j)\oplus(b\otimes j)
=
i\otimes j.
$$

Also,

$$
F(a,j)
=
(a\oplus b)\otimes j\oplus b\otimes j.
$$

Here the induction parameter is $a+b+j<a+b+c$, so

$$
(a\oplus b)\otimes j
=
(a\otimes j)\oplus(b\otimes j).
$$

Hence

$$
F(a,j)=a\otimes j.
$$

Similarly,

$$
F(i,c)=i\otimes c.
$$

The forbidden values for $F(a,c)$ are therefore

$$
F(a,j)\oplus F(i,c)\oplus F(i,j),
$$

which equal

$$
(a\otimes j)\oplus(i\otimes c)\oplus(i\otimes j).
$$

These are exactly the forbidden values defining

$$
a\otimes c.
$$

By uniqueness,

$$
F(a,c)=a\otimes c,
$$

so

$$
(a\oplus b)\otimes c
=
(a\otimes c)\oplus(b\otimes c).
$$

Thus multiplication distributes over addition.

## 3. Associativity

We prove

$$
(a\otimes b)\otimes c=a\otimes(b\otimes c)
$$

by induction on $a+b+c$.

Assume the result is known for all smaller triples.

Define

$$
F(x,y)=(x\otimes b)\otimes y.
$$

We prove that $F(a,c)$ satisfies the recursion for $a\otimes(b\otimes c)$.

For $i<a,j<c$,

$$
i+b+j<a+b+c,
$$

so associativity is already known for the triple $(i,b,j)$. Hence

$$
(i\otimes b)\otimes j=i\otimes(b\otimes j).
$$

Likewise,

$$
(a\otimes b)\otimes j=a\otimes(b\otimes j)
$$

and

$$
(i\otimes b)\otimes c=i\otimes(b\otimes c)
$$

by the induction hypothesis.

The forbidden values for $F(a,c)$ are

$$
F(a,j)\oplus F(i,c)\oplus F(i,j),
$$

which become

$$
a\otimes(b\otimes j)
\oplus
i\otimes(b\otimes c)
\oplus
i\otimes(b\otimes j).
$$

These are exactly the forbidden values in the recursion defining

$$
a\otimes(b\otimes c).
$$

Therefore, by uniqueness,

$$
(a\otimes b)\otimes c=a\otimes(b\otimes c).
$$

Together with symmetry, multiplication is a commutative associative operation.

## 4. Multiplicative inverses

We prove that every nonzero element has an inverse.

Fix $a>0$. We first show that the values

$$
a\otimes0,\ a\otimes1,\ldots,a\otimes(a-1)
$$

are distinct.

Suppose

$$
a\otimes r=a\otimes s
$$

with $r<s<a$.

In the recursion defining $a\otimes s$, choose $i=r$ and $j=s$. One of the forbidden values is

$$
(a\otimes r)\oplus(r\otimes s)\oplus(r\otimes s).
$$

Since every nimber added to itself is $0$,

$$
(a\otimes r)\oplus(r\otimes s)\oplus(r\otimes s)=a\otimes r.
$$

Thus the value $a\otimes r$ is forbidden when computing $a\otimes s$. Therefore

$$
a\otimes s\ne a\otimes r,
$$

a contradiction.

Hence the $a$ values are distinct.

It remains to show that they are exactly the nimbers below $a$. We prove by induction on $a$ that

$$
a\otimes r<a\qquad(r<a).
$$

For $a=1$ it is immediate.

Assume the claim for smaller first arguments. If $r<a$, every forbidden value in the definition of $a\otimes r$ has the form

$$
(a\otimes j)\oplus(i\otimes r)\oplus(i\otimes j),
$$

where $j<r$ and $i<a$.

By the induction hypothesis on $r$ and the second arguments, all values involving the smaller factor lie below $a$. Hence the mex cannot skip all of $0,1,\ldots,a-1$. Therefore

$$
a\otimes r<a.
$$

The $a$ distinct values $a\otimes r$ for $0\le r<a$ are therefore $a$ distinct elements of the set

$$
\{0,1,\ldots,a-1\}.
$$

They must be the whole set.

In particular, $1$ occurs. Hence there exists $b$ such that

$$
a\otimes b=1.
$$

Every nonzero element has an inverse, so

$$
(\mathbb N,\oplus,\otimes)
$$

is a field.

# 5. The finite subfields

Let

$$
M_n=2^{2^n}.
$$

We prove simultaneously:

1. If $x,y<M_n$, then

$$
x\otimes y<M_n.
$$

1. If $y<M_n$, then

$$
M_n\otimes y=M_ny.
$$

The two statements are proved together by induction on $n$.

For $n=0$,

$$
M_0=2.
$$

The two elements $0,1$ form the ordinary field of two elements.

Assume the result for $M=M_n$. Put

$$
M_{n+1}=M^2.
$$

First prove the special identity.

For $y<M$,

$$
M\otimes y
=
\operatorname{mex}
\{(M\otimes j)\oplus(i\otimes y)\oplus(i\otimes j):
i<M,j<y\}.
$$

By the induction hypothesis,

$$
i\otimes y<M,\qquad i\otimes j<M.
$$

Also, for $j<y<M$, the induction hypothesis gives

$$
M\otimes j=M j.
$$

Therefore the forbidden values are precisely

$$
Mj\oplus r,
$$

where $j<y$ and $r<M$.

These are the integers

$$
0,1,\ldots,My-1.
$$

Hence the mex is

$$
M y,
$$

so

$$
M\otimes y=My.
$$

Now let $x,y<M^2$. Since $M$ is a nim-power-of-two boundary, every such number has a unique decomposition

$$
x=a\oplus Mb,\qquad y=c\oplus Md
$$

with

$$
a,b,c,d<M.
$$

Using distributivity,

$$
x\otimes y
=
(a\oplus Mb)\otimes(c\oplus Md).
$$

Expanding,

$$
x\otimes y
=
a\otimes c
\oplus
a\otimes(Md)
\oplus
(Mb)\otimes c
\oplus
(Mb)\otimes(Md).
$$

The identity already proved gives

$$
a\otimes(Md)=M(a\otimes d),
$$

$$
(Mb)\otimes c=M(b\otimes c),
$$

and

$$
(Mb)\otimes(Md)=M^2(b\otimes d).
$$

By the induction hypothesis,

$$
a\otimes c<M,
$$

and all coefficients of the terms multiplied by $M$ are less than $M$. Therefore the product has no component beyond $M^2$, and

$$
x\otimes y<M^2=M_{n+1}.
$$

This proves closure.

Hence

$$
\{0,1,\ldots,2^{2^n}-1\}
$$

is a field of size

$$
2^{2^n}.
$$

# 6. Efficient computation

The recursive definition is inefficient because it requires a mex over $xy$ values. The subfield structure gives a faster algorithm.

Given $x,y$, choose the largest $n$ such that

$$
M=2^{2^n}\le\max(x,y).
$$

Write

$$
x=a\oplus Mb,\qquad y=c\oplus Md
$$

with smaller pieces. Then compute recursively

$$
a\otimes c,\quad a\otimes d,\quad b\otimes c,\quad b\otimes d.
$$

Combine them by

$$
x\otimes y
=
a\otimes c
\oplus
M(a\otimes d)
\oplus
M(b\otimes c)
\oplus
M^2(b\otimes d).
$$

At each level the size of the operands is reduced to a smaller subfield. The number of levels is the number of times one takes a square root of the relevant field size, which is logarithmic in the bit length. The recursive computation therefore avoids enumerating the mex set and gives a polynomial-time multiplication algorithm.

Thus nim addition and nim multiplication form Conway's field, the field contains subfields of all sizes $2^{2^n}$, and multiplication can be computed efficiently. $\square$
