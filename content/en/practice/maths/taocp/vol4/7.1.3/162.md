### Corrected solution

#### 1. Correct construction of the pentagrid

The pentagrid in Fig. 14 is the tiling of the hyperbolic plane by regular pentagons of type $\{5,4\}$, drawn in the Poincaré disk model.

Work in the unit disk

$$
D=\{z\in\mathbb{C} : |z|<1\},
$$

where hyperbolic lines are represented by Euclidean circles orthogonal to the boundary circle $|z|=1$, together with diameters.

The construction is not based on independent families of circles. It is generated by a reflection group.

Start with a single regular hyperbolic pentagon $P_0$ such that:

- all sides are hyperbolic geodesics (hence Euclidean circular arcs orthogonal to $|z|=1$),
- four pentagons meet at each vertex, so each interior angle is

$$
\frac{2\pi}{4}=\frac{\pi}{2}.
$$

Such a pentagon exists uniquely up to isometry in the hyperbolic plane.

Now proceed as follows:

1. Draw the five geodesic sides of $P_0$ in the Poincaré disk. Each side is an arc of a circle orthogonal to $|z|=1$.
2. Reflect $P_0$ in each of its sides. Each reflection is an isometry of the hyperbolic plane and maps geodesics to geodesics.
3. Continue reflecting all newly obtained pentagons in their sides.

The resulting orbit under the reflection group $[5,4]$ fills the disk with congruent regular pentagons. The union of all side images is the pentagrid.

This is the correct mechanism behind the figure: the pentagrid is the 1-skeleton of the $\{5,4\}$ tessellation.

#### 2. What circles are present?

Every edge in the pentagrid is a hyperbolic line. In the Poincaré disk model, this means:

- each edge lies on a Euclidean circle orthogonal to the unit circle $|z|=1$, or is a diameter of the disk.

Thus the circles appearing in the drawing are exactly:

1. **The boundary circle**

$$
|z|=1.
$$
2. **All geodesic carrier circles of the tiling edges**, i.e. the (infinitely many) Euclidean circles orthogonal to $|z|=1$ that arise as images of the sides of the initial pentagon under reflections in the group $[5,4]$. Diameters are included as limiting cases.

No other circles occur in the pentagrid drawing.

#### 3. Corrections to the earlier approach

- There are not five privileged families of circles. The edges are generated globally by the reflection group, not by fixed pencils.
- There are no concentric circles centered at the origin in the figure. Such circles are not geodesics and are not part of the pentagrid.
- Disjointness of “families” is irrelevant; the correct structure is a single orbit of geodesic edges under reflections.
- Completeness follows because the reflection group $[5,4]$ acts transitively on edges and generates all adjacent pentagons.

This fully describes both the construction and the exact types of circles present in the pentagrid.
