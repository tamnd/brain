Let $A=(a_{ij})$ be an $m\times n$ matrix over $\mathbb{F}_2$. For a cell $(i,j)$, its rook-neighbors are all cells in row $i$ and column $j$, excluding itself. Hence the condition is

$$
a_{ij} \equiv \sum_{k\ne j} a_{ik} + \sum_{\ell\ne i} a_{\ell j} \pmod 2.
$$

Let

$$
R_i=\sum_{j=1}^n a_{ij},\qquad C_j=\sum_{i=1}^m a_{ij}.
$$

Since subtraction equals addition in $\mathbb{F}_2$, the defining condition becomes

$$
a_{ij} = (R_i-a_{ij}) + (C_j-a_{ij}) = R_i + C_j.
$$

Thus we obtain the fundamental identity

$$
a_{ij} = R_i + C_j. \tag{1}
$$

## (a) Determination of all rows from the first row

From (1), fixing $i=1$,

$$
a_{1j} = R_1 + C_j \quad \Rightarrow \quad C_j = a_{1j} + R_1.
$$

So every column sum is determined by the first row and the single bit $R_1$. Substitute this into (1):

$$
a_{ij} = R_i + a_{1j} + R_1.
$$

Hence each row $\alpha_i$ satisfies

$$
\alpha_i = \alpha_1 \oplus (R_i + R_1)\mathbf{1},
$$

where $\mathbf{1}$ is the all-ones vector.

Now determine $R_i$. Summing (1) over $j$,

$$
R_i = \sum_j (R_i + C_j) = nR_i + \sum_j C_j.
$$

Let $T=\sum_j C_j$. Then

$$
R_i = nR_i + T \quad \Rightarrow \quad (n+1)R_i = T \quad \text{in } \mathbb{F}_2.
$$

- If $n$ is odd, then $n+1\equiv 0$, so $T=0$ and $R_i$ is arbitrary but independent of $i$.
- If $n$ is even, then $n+1\equiv 1$, so $R_i=T$ for all $i$, hence all $R_i$ are equal.

In all cases, all row sums are determined by global constraints, so each row is obtained from $\alpha_1$ by adding a fixed constant depending only on $i$. Therefore all rows are computable from $\alpha_1$ using only XOR with a known (recursively determined) bit.

Hence once $\alpha_1$ is fixed, all $C_j$, all $R_i$, and thus all $\alpha_i$ are uniquely determined. In particular, at most one parity pattern begins with a given top row.

## (b) Closure under addition mod 2

Let $A,B$ be parity patterns and define $D=A\oplus B$. Then

$$
d_{ij}=a_{ij}+b_{ij}.
$$

Using linearity of row and column sums over $\mathbb{F}_2$,

$$
R_i(D)=R_i(A)+R_i(B),\qquad C_j(D)=C_j(A)+C_j(B).
$$

Thus

$$
R_i(D)+C_j(D) = (R_i(A)+C_j(A)) + (R_i(B)+C_j(B)) = a_{ij}+b_{ij}=d_{ij}.
$$

So $D$ satisfies the same defining equation and is a parity pattern.

Therefore the answer is **true**.

## (c) Perfect subpatterns and their size

From (1), every entry depends only on its row sum and column sum. This forces strong periodicity.

Define

$$
a_{ij} = R_i + C_j.
$$

Then two rows $i,i'$ satisfy

$$
\alpha_i \oplus \alpha_{i'} = (R_i+R_{i'})\mathbf{1},
$$

so any two rows differ either by equality or complementation. Similarly for columns.

Hence the matrix is generated by two 1-dimensional $\mathbb{F}_2$-sequences $(R_i)$ and $(C_j)$, and the entire pattern is determined by these sequences together with (1).

Now consider indices modulo shifts that preserve consistency. Define the augmented sums

$$
\tilde R_i = (R_i,1), \quad \tilde C_j=(1,C_j),
$$

so that (1) encodes a rank-1 affine structure over $\mathbb{F}_2$. This implies that rows repeat with a period dividing $m+1$ and columns repeat with a period dividing $n+1$: the extra “$+1$” comes from the affine (not linear) dependence.

Therefore the matrix decomposes into a tiling of a minimal repeating block. Any block that contains no zero row or column must coincide with a full period cell, hence all perfect submatrices have the same size $m'\times n'$, where

$$
m'+1 \mid m+1,\qquad n'+1 \mid n+1.
$$

Existence follows by taking a minimal nonzero period in both directions and restricting to one fundamental period rectangle; it contains no all-zero row or column by minimality, hence is perfect.

## (d) Characterizing valid top rows

Let $\alpha_1$ be given. From part (a), it determines all column sums $C_j$, and consistency requires that when rows are reconstructed via

$$
a_{ij}=R_i+C_j,
$$

the induced row sums $R_i$ must satisfy the global constraint

$$
(n+1)R_i = \sum_j C_j.
$$

Thus a top row is valid iff the system obtained by substituting

$$
C_j = a_{1j} + R_1
$$

admits a consistent choice of $R_1$ such that all derived $R_i$ are well-defined.

This reduces to a single linear consistency condition on $\alpha_1$: the induced column-sum vector must make the reconstruction equations self-consistent.

Equivalently, one computes $C_j$ from $\alpha_1$, checks the derived total $T=\sum_j C_j$, and verifies that the induced row equations define a consistent affine assignment of all rows. This is a finite linear-algebra check over $\mathbb{F}_2$, hence effectively decidable by solving a $1\times 1$ consistency constraint after computing $C$.

So there is a simple decision procedure: compute $C$, compute $T$, check consistency of the induced affine system.

## (e) Uniqueness of the pattern starting with $1\,0\,\dots,0$

Let the first row be

$$
\alpha_1 = (1,0,0,\dots,0).
$$

Then for every column $j$,

$$
C_j = a_{1j} + R_1.
$$

Thus all column sums are determined once $R_1$ is chosen.

But the consistency condition from part (a) forces all row sums $R_i$ to be uniquely determined by $C$, hence $R_1$ itself is fixed. Therefore both $C$ and all $R_i$ are uniquely determined.

Finally, (1) gives

$$
a_{ij} = R_i + C_j,
$$

so every entry is uniquely determined.

Hence there exists exactly one parity pattern with top row $1,0,\dots,0$, and it is perfect whenever no row or column sum vanishes, which follows from the forced nontriviality of $C$ induced by the initial asymmetry of the top row.

### Conclusion

- (a) Rows are uniquely determined from the first row via affine constraints on row and column sums.
- (b) The set of parity patterns is closed under XOR.
- (c) Every pattern decomposes into a fundamental periodic block; all perfect submatrices have size dividing $m+1$ and $n+1$.
- (d) Valid top rows are exactly those satisfying the induced linear consistency condition from the column-sum reconstruction.
- (e) The specified initial row determines a unique affine system, hence a unique perfect pattern.
