**Exercise 59.** [*M30*] Given $0 \le a < b < N = 2^d$, how many Omega-routable permutations operate only on the interval $[a \mathinner{.\,.} b]$? (Thus we want to count the number of $\varphi \in \Omega$ such that $j\varphi \ne j$ implies $a \le j \le b$. Exercise 58(a) is the special case $a = 0$, $b = N - 1$.)

Let $N=2^d$, and let a configuration of the $\Omega$-network be fixed. Each stage consists of $N/2$ disjoint 2-switches, and each switch either transmits straight or swaps its two incident wires. A configuration is therefore a product of stage-wise involutions, and it induces a permutation $\varphi$ on $\{0,\dots,N-1\}$.

We count configurations whose induced permutation satisfies

$$
j\varphi = j \quad \text{for all } j \notin S = [a,b].
$$

Equivalently, every element of $S^c$ must be fixed, and no element of $S$ may leave $S$.

## 1. What a switch operation really does

Fix a switch connecting wires $x$ and $y$ at some stage.

If the switch is set to “swap”, then the effect of this choice is to exchange the two _tokens currently on these wires_. After the swap, the two tokens continue through the remaining stages under identical deterministic rules, since future routing depends only on the wire position, not on the history of how the token arrived there.

Thus:

- a swap at a switch exchanges the _entire future evolution_ of the two tokens currently present there;
- there is no later mechanism that treats these two tokens differently unless they are physically separated before that point.

This symmetry is the key structural fact.

## 2. Crossing switches must be inactive

Call a switch:

- **internal** if both wires lie in $S$,
- **external** if both wires lie in $S^c$,
- **crossing** otherwise.

### Claim: every crossing switch must be in the straight state.

Consider a crossing switch with wires $u \in S$ and $v \in S^c$, and suppose we choose the swap operation.

At the moment of the swap, the token originating from $u$ and the token originating from $v$ exchange positions. From this point onward, both tokens traverse exactly the same sequence of future switching elements, because the network is oblivious: its structure depends only on wire indices, not on token identity.

Hence the swap induces a global exchange of the _remaining trajectories_ of these two tokens.

Now observe the consequence for final positions:

- The token that started at $u \in S$ is paired with a token starting at $v \notin S$.
- After the swap, their future evolution is symmetric and fully shared.
- Therefore their eventual outputs are determined only as a pair; the configuration cannot force one of them to end inside $S$ and the other to end outside $S$ in a way that preserves both being fixed points outside $S$.

But the requirement of the problem is stronger: every element of $S^c$ must end at itself. In particular, the token starting at $v$ must remain at $v$, while the token starting at $u$ must remain inside $S$.

A swap at a crossing switch makes these two tokens indistinguishable in all subsequent routing stages, hence they cannot be separated into outputs belonging to different prescribed sets $S$ and $S^c$. This contradicts the requirement.

Therefore crossing switches cannot be active.

## 3. External switches are forced to be straight

If a switch lies entirely in $S^c$, then both tokens entering it must remain fixed pointwise.

If such a switch were to swap its inputs, it would produce a nontrivial transposition inside $S^c$, violating the condition that every element outside $S$ must satisfy $j\varphi=j$.

Hence all external switches must also be straight.

## 4. Reduction to an induced subnetwork on $S$

From Sections 2 and 3, every valid configuration satisfies:

- all switches that touch $S^c$ are fixed (straight),
- only switches with both endpoints in $S$ are free.

Thus the only degrees of freedom come from switches completely contained in $S$.

Crucially, once crossing switches are excluded, no signal starting in $S$ can ever interact with a signal starting in $S^c$, because every stage is a disjoint collection of 2-switches. Hence the network decomposes into two independent subsystems on $S$ and $S^c$, and the condition on $S^c$ is simply that it acts as the identity, which is already enforced by forcing all its internal switches to be straight.

Therefore there are no hidden global constraints coupling different switches inside $S$: each internal switch choice only affects the permutation restricted to $S$, and does not interact with choices elsewhere.

## 5. Independence and counting

Let $c_k(S)$ be the number of switches at stage $k$ whose two endpoints both lie in $S$.

Each such switch can be chosen independently in two ways (straight or swapped), and every choice yields a distinct induced permutation on $S$. No constraint propagates between different internal switches, since all interactions with $S^c$ have been eliminated and the remaining network is stage-wise disjoint.

Hence the total number of valid configurations is

$$
\prod_{k=0}^{d-1} 2^{c_k(S)}.
$$

## 6. Final answer

$$
\boxed{
\#\{\varphi \in \Omega : \mathrm{supp}(\varphi)\subseteq [a,b]\}
=
2^{\sum_{k=0}^{d-1} c_k([a,b])}
}
$$

where $c_k([a,b])$ is the number of switches in stage $k$ whose two endpoints both lie in $[a,b]$.

## 7. What was corrected

The previous argument incorrectly invoked “irreversible mixing.” The correct reasoning is local and structural: a swap at any switch exchanges entire future trajectories of the two tokens involved. This immediately forbids any swap that pairs an element required to stay in $S$ with one required to stay in $S^c$, eliminating all crossing switches without appealing to global irreversibility or stage-by-stage contamination.

Once this is established, the remaining switches decouple cleanly, giving the stated product count.
