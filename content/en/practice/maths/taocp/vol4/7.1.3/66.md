**Exercise 66.** &#9654; [*M26*] Suppose the polynomial $u(x)$ has been represented as an $n$-bit integer $u$ as in exercise 65, and let $v = u \oplus (u \ll \delta) \oplus (u \ll 2\delta) \oplus (u \ll 3\delta) \oplus \cdots$ for some integer $\delta$.

a) What's a simple way to describe the polynomial $v(x)$?

b) Suppose $n$ is large, and the bits of $u$ have been packed into 64-bit words. How would you compute $v$ when $\delta = 1$, using bitwise operations in 64-bit registers?

c) Consider the same question as (b), but when $\delta = 64$.

d) Consider the same question as (b), but when $\delta = 3$.

e) Consider the same question as (b), but when $\delta = 67$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m32s  

---

## Setup

Let $u(x)=\sum_{i=0}^{n-1} u_i x^i$ over GF$(2)$, and let $u$ be the corresponding integer whose binary expansion is $(u_{n-1}\ldots u_1 u_0)_2$ as in exercise 65. Bitwise addition $\oplus$ corresponds to addition in GF$(2)$, and left shift by $\delta$ corresponds to multiplication by $x^\delta$.

The definition

$v = u \oplus (u \ll \delta) \oplus (u \ll 2\delta) \oplus \cdots$

is interpreted in GF$(2)$ arithmetic as an infinite sum of shifted copies of $u$.

## Solution

### (a)

Each shift $u \ll k\delta$ corresponds to $x^{k\delta} u(x)$. Hence

$v(x)=u(x)\bigl(1+x^\delta+x^{2\delta}+\cdots\bigr).$

In GF$(2)$, the formal geometric series satisfies

$1+x^\delta+x^{2\delta}+\cdots=\frac{1}{1+x^\delta}.$

Therefore

$\boxed{v(x)=\frac{u(x)}{1+x^\delta}}$

in GF$(2)[[x]]$, equivalently

$v(x)(1+x^\delta)=u(x).$

Equivalently, coefficients satisfy

$v_k = \bigoplus_{j\ge 0} u_{k-j\delta}.$

### (b) $\delta=1$, 64-bit packed words

For $\delta=1$,

$v = u \oplus (u \ll 1) \oplus (u \ll 2) \oplus \cdots$

so each bit of $v$ is the prefix XOR of bits of $u$.

Thus for each position $k$,

$v_k = u_0 \oplus u_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus u_k.$

This can be computed within a 64-bit register using a standard prefix-XOR cascade. Starting from $v \leftarrow u$, the transformations

$v \leftarrow v \oplus (v \gg 1),$

$v \leftarrow v \oplus (v \gg 2),$

$v \leftarrow v \oplus (v \gg 4),$

$v \leftarrow v \oplus (v \gg 8),$

$v \leftarrow v \oplus (v \gg 16),$

$v \leftarrow v \oplus (v \gg 32)$

propagate XOR information across all distances $2^k$ inside the word. After the final step, each bit accumulates exactly the XOR of all lower bits in its 64-bit word, hence produces the required prefix XOR.

Across multiple 64-bit words, the same operation is applied wordwise after first carrying the prefix XOR from the previous word into the current one, since the definition of $v_k$ is global over the bit stream.

### (c) $\delta=64$

Here shifts move exactly one machine word. Writing $u$ as an array of 64-bit words $(U_0,U_1,\dots)$, the definition becomes

$V_i = U_i \oplus U_{i-1} \oplus U_{i-2} \oplus \cdots.$

Thus each word is a prefix XOR over words.

This is computed by

$V_0 = U_0,$

$V_i = V_{i-1} \oplus U_i \quad (i \ge 1).$

Each step is one word XOR, hence the computation is linear in the number of words, with no intra-word shifts required.

### (d) $\delta=3$

The expression

$v = u \oplus (u \ll 3) \oplus (u \ll 6) \oplus \cdots$

forms a geometric series in the shift operator $\ll 3$. Hence

$v = u \cdot (1 + S + S^2 + \cdots), \quad S(x)=x^3.$

Within a fixed 64-bit register, the same doubling method used for prefix sums applies, but with base shift 3 instead of 1. Starting with $v \leftarrow u$, repeated squaring of the shift effect gives

$v \leftarrow v \oplus (v \ll 3),$

$v \leftarrow v \oplus (v \ll 6),$

$v \leftarrow v \oplus (v \ll 12),$

$v \leftarrow v \oplus (v \ll 24),$

$v \leftarrow v \oplus (v \ll 48).$

Since any contribution beyond 63 positions leaves the 64-bit word, the cascade stabilizes after these shifts. The resulting word equals the sum of all $u \ll 3k$ terms that remain within the word, which matches the required restriction of the infinite definition to 64-bit arithmetic.

### (e) $\delta=67$

Write $\delta = 64 + 3$. Then

$u \ll (67k) = u \ll (64k + 3k).$

A shift by $64k$ moves data across words, while the residual shift $3k$ acts within each word.

First define, for each 64-bit word $U_i$, its intra-word transform

$\widetilde{U}_i = U_i \oplus (U_i \ll 3) \oplus (U_i \ll 6) \oplus \cdots,$

computed exactly as in part (d), restricted to 64-bit width.

This incorporates all shifts that remain inside a word under multiples of 3.

Next, the full shift by $67$ propagates these transformed words across word boundaries exactly as a shift by $64$ would, because the remaining $3k$ component has already been absorbed into $\widetilde{U}_i$. Thus the original definition becomes a word-level prefix XOR:

$V_i = \widetilde{U}_i \oplus \widetilde{U}_{i-1} \oplus \widetilde{U}_{i-2} \oplus \cdots.$

Hence the computation is performed in two stages: first apply the intra-word geometric accumulation for shift $3$, then perform a word-wise prefix XOR as in part (c).

## Verification

For part (a), multiplying $u(x)$ by $\sum_{k\ge 0} x^{k\delta}$ produces exactly the convolution that places $u_i$ into all positions $i+k\delta$, and XOR of these contributions yields the defining expression of $v$.

For part (b), each doubling step ensures that after processing shift $2^j$, all contributions from distances less than $2^{j+1}$ are included exactly once modulo 2, which yields prefix XOR on each 64-bit block.

For part (c), the recurrence $V_i = V_{i-1} \oplus U_i$ expands to $V_i = \bigoplus_{j=0}^i U_j$, matching the definition of shifts by multiples of 64 bits.

For part (d), repeated application of $v \leftarrow v \oplus (v \ll 3\cdot 2^j)$ accumulates all shifts $3k$ representable within 64 bits, since any larger shift leaves the word and contributes zero.

For part (e), decomposing $\delta$ into $64+3$ separates inter-word propagation from intra-word shifts, and the two-stage construction reconstructs exactly the contributions of all $u \ll 67k$ terms.

This completes the proof. ∎
