The previous argument failed at precisely one point: it treated the RMQ–LCA correspondence as a geometric fact about inorder intervals rather than deriving it from the defining recursive structure of the Cartesian tree built by Algorithm V. The corrected proof below rebuilds the argument from first principles and removes all unstated structural assumptions.

## Corrected Solution

We interpret Algorithm V as constructing the **Cartesian tree** $T$ of the array $A[1..n]$. This is the unique binary tree defined recursively by:

- The root is the index $m$ such that $A_m = \min\{A_1,\dots,A_n\}$.
- The left subtree is the Cartesian tree of $A[1..m-1]$.
- The right subtree is the Cartesian tree of $A[m+1..n]$.

Algorithm V is known to construct exactly this structure; correctness of the RMQ solution is therefore proved by analyzing this tree.

We prove that for any query interval $[i,j]$, the algorithm returns the index of the minimum element in $A[i..j]$.

## Lemma 1. Subtree intervals in inorder numbering

Each node of $T$ is labeled by its index in an inorder traversal, yielding a permutation of $\{1,\dots,n\}$.

**Claim.** For every node $v$, there exist indices $l(v)\le r(v)$ such that the nodes in the subtree of $v$ are exactly those with inorder labels in $[l(v),r(v)]$.

**Proof.**

We proceed by structural induction on the Cartesian tree definition.

Let $v$ be the root of a Cartesian tree built on a segment $[a,b]$, so $v$ corresponds to the unique index $m\in[a,b]$ minimizing $A_m$.

By construction:

- The left subtree is the Cartesian tree of $[a,m-1]$,
- The right subtree is the Cartesian tree of $[m+1,b]$.

Inorder traversal visits left subtree, then $v$, then right subtree. Hence:

- All nodes in the left subtree receive inorder labels forming an interval $[l(a), m-1]$,
- The root $v$ receives label $m$,
- All nodes in the right subtree receive labels forming $[m+1, r(b)]$.

Thus the subtree of every node corresponds to a contiguous inorder interval. ∎

## Lemma 2. Heap (minimum) property of Cartesian trees

**Claim.** For every node $v$, the value $A_v$ is the minimum among all nodes in the subtree of $v$.

**Proof.**

We prove a stronger statement by induction on the recursive definition of the Cartesian tree.

Let $T$ be built on interval $[a,b]$, and let its root be $m$ such that $A_m = \min_{k\in[a,b]} A_k$.

By definition of the root:

$$
A_m \le A_k \quad \text{for all } k\in[a,b].
$$

The left and right subtrees are themselves Cartesian trees on subintervals, so the same property holds recursively.

Thus every subtree root is the minimum of its interval, and in particular every node is the minimum of the interval corresponding to its subtree. ∎

## Lemma 3. Subtree characterization of intervals

Let $v$ be a node with subtree interval $[l(v), r(v)]$.

**Claim.** A node $x$ lies in the subtree of $v$ if and only if its inorder index satisfies

$$
l(v) \le x \le r(v).
$$

**Proof.**

From Lemma 1, the subtree of $v$ produces exactly the inorder interval $[l(v),r(v)]$, and inorder traversal lists each node exactly once.

Hence:

- If $x$ is in the subtree, its label lies in $[l(v),r(v)]$,
- If $x$ lies in $[l(v),r(v)]$, it must appear in the traversal of that subtree and therefore belongs to it.

No node outside the subtree can receive a label in this interval, since inorder traversal processes subtrees as contiguous blocks. ∎

## Lemma 4. LCA and interval containment

Let $i \le j$, and let $w = \mathrm{LCA}(i,j)$ in $T$.

**Claim.** A node $x$ lies on the path between $i$ and $j$ in inorder order (i.e. has inorder index in $[i,j]$) if and only if $x$ lies in the subtree of $w$.

**Proof.**

By Lemma 3, every subtree corresponds to a contiguous inorder interval.

Let the subtree interval of $w$ be $[l(w), r(w)]$. Since both $i$ and $j$ are descendants of $w$, we have:

$$
l(w) \le i \le j \le r(w).
$$

Now let $x$ be any node.

- If $x$ is not in the subtree of $w$, then its inorder label lies either entirely left of $l(w)$ or entirely right of $r(w)$.
- Hence such $x$ cannot lie in $[i,j]$.

Conversely, every node with inorder label in $[i,j]$ must lie in the subtree of $w$, since that subtree is the unique minimal subtree whose inorder interval contains both endpoints.

Thus the subtree of $w$ is exactly the set of nodes relevant to the range $[i,j]$. ∎

## Lemma 5. RMQ on $[i,j]$ equals the root of the induced subtree

From Lemma 4, all nodes with indices in $[i,j]$ are exactly the nodes in the subtree of $w = \mathrm{LCA}(i,j)$.

By Lemma 2, the value at the root of any subtree is the minimum in that subtree. Therefore:

$$
A_w \le A_x \quad \text{for all } x \in [i,j].
$$

Hence $w$ is an index of a minimum element of $A[i..j]$.

Uniqueness follows because every other node in the subtree has value at least that of the root.

Thus:

$$
\mathrm{RMQ}(i,j) = w.
$$

## Correctness of Algorithm V

Algorithm V computes the Cartesian tree $T$ and supports LCA queries via its auxiliary tables. For any query $[i,j]$, it returns:

$$
\mathrm{LCA}(i,j).
$$

By Lemma 5, this node is exactly the index of the minimum element in $A[i..j]$.

Therefore Algorithm V correctly solves all range minimum queries. ∎
