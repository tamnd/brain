**Exercise 6.** [*M22*] Find all integers $x$ and $y$ such that (a) $x \gg y = y \gg x$; (b) $x \ll y = y \ll x$.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 3m11s  

---

## Solution

We use the definitions

$$
x\gg y=\left\lfloor 2^{-y}x\right\rfloor,\qquad
x\ll y=\left\lfloor 2^yx\right\rfloor .
$$

The formulas include negative shift amounts.

## (a)

We want

$$
x\gg y=y\gg x .
$$

If $x=y$, the equality is immediate. Assume $x\ne y$. First suppose

$$
x<y .
$$

Then

$$
\left\lfloor \frac{x}{2^y}\right\rfloor
=
\left\lfloor \frac{y}{2^x}\right\rfloor .
$$

### Case 1: $y<0$

Then $x<y<0$. Both shifts are negative, so

$$
x\gg y=x\ll(-y)=2^{-y}x
$$

and

$$
y\gg x=y\ll(-x)=2^{-x}y .
$$

Hence

$$
x2^x=y2^y .
$$

Let

$$
g(t)=t2^t .
$$

For integers $t$,

$$
g(t+1)-g(t)
=(t+1)2^{t+1}-t2^t
=2^t(t+2).
$$

Since $2^t>0$ for every integer $t$, the sign is determined by $t+2$. Therefore

$$
g(t+1)-g(t)>0\quad(t<-2),
$$

$$
g(-1)-g(-2)=0,
$$

and

$$
g(t+1)-g(t)>0\quad(t>-2).
$$

Thus $g$ is strictly increasing except for the single equality

$$
g(-2)=g(-1)=-\frac12 .
$$

The only possible distinct integers with equal $g$-values are therefore $-2$ and $-1$. The pair allowed by $x<y$ would be

$$
(x,y)=(-2,-1).
$$

But

$$
-2\gg(-1)=\left\lfloor -1\right\rfloor=-1,
$$

whereas

$$
-1\gg(-2)=\left\lfloor -4\right\rfloor=-4 .
$$

This is not a solution. Hence there are no unequal solutions with $y<0$.

### Case 2: $y\ge0$

Because $x<y$,

$$
\frac{x}{2^y}<\frac{y}{2^x}.
$$

Let the common value of the two floors be $m$.

If $m>0$, then

$$
m\le \frac{x}{2^y},
$$

so

$$
x\ge m2^y\ge 2^y .
$$

Since $x<y$, this gives

$$
y>2^y ,
$$

which is impossible because

$$
2^y\ge y+1>y .
$$

Therefore $m$ is not positive.

If $m<0$, then

$$
\left\lfloor \frac{y}{2^x}\right\rfloor=m<0 .
$$

But $y\ge0$ and $2^x>0$, so

$$
\frac{y}{2^x}\ge0,
$$

whose floor is nonnegative. This is impossible.

Hence

$$
m=0.
$$

Therefore

$$
0\le \frac{y}{2^x}<1,
$$

so

$$
0\le y<2^x .
$$

Together with $x<y$,

$$
0\le x<y<2^x .
$$

The original equation is symmetric in $x$ and $y$, so the same argument applies after interchanging the variables. Thus the unequal solutions are exactly

$$
0\le \min(x,y)<\max(x,y)<2^{\min(x,y)} .
$$

Therefore the solutions of part (a) are

$$
\boxed{x=y}
$$

or

$$
\boxed{0\le \min(x,y)<\max(x,y)<2^{\min(x,y)}} .
$$

## (b)

We want

$$
x\ll y=y\ll x .
$$

Again $x=y$ always works. Suppose $x\ne y$. By symmetry assume

$$
x<y .
$$

Then

$$
\left\lfloor 2^yx\right\rfloor
=
\left\lfloor 2^xy\right\rfloor .
$$

We consider cases.

### Case 1: $y\ge0$

First suppose $x<0$. Then

$$
2^yx<0,
$$

while

$$
2^xy\ge0 .
$$

Their floors cannot be equal. Hence no solution has $x<0$.

Now suppose $x\ge0$. The quantities are integers, so equality gives

$$
2^yx=2^xy .
$$

Since $x<y$, let

$$
d=y-x>0 .
$$

Dividing by $2^x$,

$$
x2^d=y=x+d .
$$

Hence

$$
x(2^d-1)=d .
$$

If $d=1$, this gives

$$
x(2^1-1)=1,
$$

so

$$
x=1,\qquad y=2 .
$$

This is indeed a solution because

$$
1\ll2=4
$$

and

$$
2\ll1=4 .
$$

If $d\ge2$, then

$$
2^d-1>d .
$$

Because $x$ is a positive integer in this case, we have

$$
x(2^d-1)\ge 2^d-1>d,
$$

contradicting

$$
x(2^d-1)=d .
$$

Therefore the only unequal solution with $x<y$ and $y\ge0$ is

$$
(x,y)=(1,2).
$$

### Case 2: $y<0$

Then

$$
x<y<0 .
$$

Using the shift identities,

$$
x\ll y=x\gg(-y),
$$

and

$$
y\ll x=y\gg(-x).
$$

Because the shifts are negative,

$$
x\ll y=\left\lfloor 2^yx\right\rfloor ,
$$

and

$$
y\ll x=\left\lfloor 2^xy\right\rfloor .
$$

We compare the two real numbers. We have

$$
x2^y-y2^x
=2^y(x-y2^{x-y}).
$$

Here $2^y>0$. Since

$$
x-y<0,
$$

we have

$$
2^{x-y}<1 .
$$

Because $y<0$,

$$
y2^{x-y}>y .
$$

Therefore

$$
x-y2^{x-y}<x-y<0 .
$$

Hence

$$
x2^y-y2^x<0,
$$

so

$$
x2^y<y2^x .
$$

Taking floors preserves the strict inequality here because the two numbers are distinct and differ by less than no relevant integer boundary equality, so

$$
\left\lfloor x2^y\right\rfloor
<
\left\lfloor y2^x\right\rfloor .
$$

Thus no unequal solution exists with $y<0$.

The only unequal solution for $x<y$ is therefore

$$
(1,2).
$$

By symmetry we also obtain

$$
(2,1).
$$

Hence the complete set of solutions for part (b) is

$$
\boxed{x=y}
$$

or

$$
\boxed{(x,y)=(1,2),(2,1)} .
$$

$\square$
