**Exercise 9.** [*M26*] (*Nim.*) Two people play a game with $k$ piles of sticks, where there are $a_j$ sticks in pile $j$. If $a_1 = \cdots = a_k = 0$ when it is a player's turn to move, that player loses; otherwise the player reduces one of the piles by any desired amount, throwing away the removed sticks, and it is the other player's turn. Prove that the player to move can force a victory if and only if $a_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus a_k \ne 0$. [*Hint:* Use exercise 8.]

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 43s  

---

## Setup

We prove that the player to move has a forced victory exactly when

$a_1\oplus a_2\oplus\cdots\oplus a_k\ne0.$

Let

$A=a_1\oplus a_2\oplus\cdots\oplus a_k.$

A position with $A=0$ is called a zero position, and a position with $A\ne0$ is called a nonzero position. The proof has two parts: every move from a zero position gives a nonzero position, and from every nonzero position there exists a move to a zero position. These two facts imply the stated winning criterion.

## Solution

Suppose first that the player to move is given a position with

$a_1\oplus\cdots\oplus a_k=0.$

Consider any legal move. The move changes exactly one pile, say pile $j$, from $a_j$ to $a'_j$, where

$0\le a'_j<a_j.$

The new nim sum is

$a_1\oplus\cdots\oplus a_{j-1}\oplus a'_j\oplus a_{j+1}\oplus\cdots\oplus a_k.$

Using the original equality,

$a_1\oplus\cdots\oplus a_{j-1}\oplus a_j\oplus a_{j+1}\oplus\cdots\oplus a_k=0,$

so the new nim sum equals

$a_j\oplus a'_j.$

Since $a'_j<a_j$, their binary expansions differ in the most significant bit where $a_j$ has a $1$ and $a'_j$ has a $0$. Therefore

$a_j\oplus a'_j\ne0.$

Hence every move from a zero position produces a nonzero position.

Now suppose that the position is nonzero, so

$A=a_1\oplus\cdots\oplus a_k\ne0.$

Let the most significant bit in which $A$ has a $1$ be bit $m$. Since the $m$th bit of the xor is $1$, an odd number of the integers $a_1,\ldots,a_k$ have a $1$ in position $m$. Hence at least one pile, say pile $j$, satisfies

$a_j\text{ has bit }m\text{ equal to }1.$

Because all bits above $m$ in $A$ are zero, every bit above $m$ occurs an even number of times among the $a_i$. The number $a_j$ therefore has a binary value larger than the number obtained by changing its bit $m$ from $1$ to $0$ and toggling precisely the lower bits required by the xor operation. Define

$a'_j=a_j\oplus A.$

The bits above $m$ of $a'_j$ equal those of $a_j$, the bit $m$ changes from $1$ to $0$, and the lower bits may change arbitrarily. Consequently,

$a'_j<a_j.$

Thus replacing pile $j$ by $a'_j$ is a legal move.

After this move the new nim sum is

$a_1\oplus\cdots\oplus a_{j-1}\oplus a'_j\oplus a_{j+1}\oplus\cdots\oplus a_k.$

Substituting $a'_j=a_j\oplus A$ gives

$a_1\oplus\cdots\oplus a_k\oplus A.$

By the definition of $A$ this is

$A\oplus A=0.$

Therefore every nonzero position has a move to a zero position.

The two established properties imply the result. From a zero position every move gives the opponent a nonzero position, and from every nonzero position the player to move can move to a zero position. Thus the player who moves first from a nonzero position can always return the game to a zero position after each opponent move. Since the piles are finite and every move decreases the total number of sticks, the game eventually reaches the position with all piles empty, which is a zero position. The opponent is then required to move from that position and loses.

If the initial position is a zero position, every possible first move gives the opponent a nonzero position, so the opponent can maintain the strategy above and force the original player to lose.

Hence the player to move can force a victory if and only if

$a_1\oplus\cdots\oplus a_k\ne0.$

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification

The construction of the winning move uses the most significant nonzero bit of

$A=a_1\oplus\cdots\oplus a_k.$

At that bit, changing $a_j$ to $a_j\oplus A$ strictly decreases the chosen pile, so the move is legal. The resulting xor is

$A\oplus A=0,$

which confirms that the move reaches a zero position.

The converse argument uses only the fact that a legal move changes one pile. The old and new nim sums differ by

$a_j\oplus a'_j,$

and this value cannot be zero when $a_j\ne a'_j$. Therefore no legal move can preserve a zero position.
