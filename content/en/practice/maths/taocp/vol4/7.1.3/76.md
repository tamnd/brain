**Exercise 76.** [*27*] A *mapping network* is analogous to a sorting network or a permutation network, but it uses $2 \times 2$ mapping modules instead of comparators or crossbars, and it is supposed to be able to output all $n^n$ possible mappings of its $n$ inputs. Exercise 75, in conjunction with Fig. 12, shows that a mapping network for $n = 2^d$ exists with only $4d-2$ levels of delay, and with $n/2$ modules on each level; furthermore, this construction needs general $2 \times 2$ mapping modules (instead of simple crossbars) in only $d$ of those levels.

To within $O(n)$, what is the smallest number $G(n)$ of modules that are sufficient to implement a general $n$-element mapping network?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m13s  

---

Let $G(n)$ denote the minimum number of $2\times 2$ mapping modules sufficient to realize all functions $f:\{1,\ldots,n\}\to\{1,\ldots,n\}$.

We assume $n=2^d$, as in the given construction, and then extend to general $n$ by standard padding.

## 1. Upper bound from the construction

From Exercise 75 and Fig. 12, there exists a mapping network with:

- $4d-2$ levels,
- $n/2$ modules per level,
- general $2\times 2$ mapping modules used in exactly $d$ levels (this affects only constant factors, not asymptotics of the total count).

Thus the total number of modules is

$$
M = (4d-2)\cdot \frac{n}{2} = n(2d-1) = 2n\log_2 n - n.
$$

Hence

$$
G(n) \le 2n\log_2 n + O(n).
$$

## 2. Lower bound by information capacity

A general $2\times 2$ mapping module has at most $4$ internal states. Therefore a network with $M$ modules has at most $4^M$ global configurations.

Each configuration induces at most one function $\{1,\ldots,n\}\to\{1,\ldots,n\}$. Hence the number of realizable mappings is at most $4^M$.

Since there are exactly $n^n$ such mappings, we must have

$$
4^M \ge n^n.
$$

Taking binary logarithms,

$$
2M \ge n\log_2 n,
\qquad\text{so}\qquad
M \ge \frac{1}{2}n\log_2 n.
$$

Thus

$$
G(n) \ge \frac{1}{2}n\log_2 n.
$$

## 3. What can and cannot be concluded

Combining the bounds,

$$
\frac{1}{2}n\log_2 n \;\le\; G(n) \;\le\; 2n\log_2 n + O(n).
$$

This immediately implies

$$
G(n) = \Theta(n\log n),
$$

but it does **not** determine a precise leading constant.

The attempted refinement to

$$
G(n) = n\log_2 n + O(n)
$$

is unjustified, because the upper and lower bounds leave a factor-4 gap in the leading coefficient. No argument in the construction or in the counting bound forces these constants to coincide.

The phrase “to within $O(n)$” would require determining a constant $c$ such that

$$
G(n) = c\,n\log_2 n + O(n),
$$

but the available arguments only constrain $c$ to lie in

$$
\frac{1}{2} \le c \le 2.
$$

## 4. Final conclusion

The correct asymptotic information derivable from the given construction and counting argument is:

$$
\boxed{
\frac{1}{2}n\log_2 n \;\le\; G(n) \;\le\; 2n\log_2 n + O(n),
\quad\text{hence}\quad
G(n)=\Theta(n\log n).
}
$$

No sharper estimate of the form $c\,n\log n + O(n)$ is justified from the given data.
