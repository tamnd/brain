**Exercise 3.** [*M20*] If $x = (\bar{x}_{n-1} \ldots x_1 x_0)_2$ with $x_{n-1} = 1$, let $x^M = (\bar{x}_{n-1} \ldots \bar{x}_1 \bar{x}_0)_2$. Thus we have $0^M$, $1^M$, $2^M$, $3^M$, $\ldots = -1$, $0$, $1$, $0$, $3$, $2$, $1$, $0$, $7$, $6$, $\ldots$, if we let $0^M = -1$. Prove that $(x \oplus y)^M < |x - y| \le x \oplus y$ for all $x, y \ge 0$.

Let $n$ be the unique integer such that $2^{n-1} \le x < 2^n$, so $x_{n-1}=1$. Write all numbers in $n$-bit form by padding leading zeros; thus $x,y \in \{0,\dots,2^n-1\}$.

Recall that for $u>0$,

$$
u^M = (2^n-1)-u,
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
0^M = -1.
$$

We must prove

$$
(x \oplus y)^M < |x-y| \le x \oplus y.
$$

## 1. Upper bound: $|x-y| \le x \oplus y$

Let $k$ be the most significant bit position where $x$ and $y$ differ (if any). If $x=y$, the claim is trivial.

Assume $x \ne y$. Then the bit $k$ contributes $2^k$ to both $x \oplus y$ and the absolute difference.

Without loss of generality assume $x \ge y$. Write

$$
x = A + 2^k + a, \qquad y = A + b,
$$

with $0 \le a,b < 2^k$. Then

$$
|x-y| = 2^k + (a-b) \le 2^k + |a-b|.
$$

Also,

$$
x \oplus y = 2^k + (a \oplus b).
$$

By induction on $n$ applied to $a,b$ (which are $k$-bit numbers),

$$
|a-b| \le a \oplus b.
$$

Hence

$$
|x-y| \le 2^k + (a \oplus b) = x \oplus y.
$$

## 2. Key correction for the lower bound

We do **not** use the incorrect identity

$|x-y| + (x \oplus y) \ge 2^n$, which fails when $x=y$ or when the most significant bits cancel.

Instead we prove directly

$$
(x \oplus y)^M < |x-y|
$$

by splitting into cases.

## 3. Case analysis for $(x \oplus y)^M < |x-y|$

### Case 1: $x \oplus y = 0$

Then $x=y$, so

$$
|x-y| = 0,
\qquad (x \oplus y)^M = 0^M = -1.
$$

Thus

$$
(x \oplus y)^M = -1 < 0 = |x-y|.
$$

### Case 2: $x \oplus y > 0$

Let $k$ be the most significant bit where $x$ and $y$ differ. Then necessarily $0 \le k \le n-1$, and the $k$-th bit of $x \oplus y$ is $1$. Hence

$$
x \oplus y \ge 2^k.
$$

We compare magnitudes more carefully.

#### Step 1: lower bound on $|x-y|$

At bit $k$, the contribution to the difference is at least $2^k$, and all lower bits contribute at most $2^k-1$. Therefore,

$$
|x-y| \ge 2^k - (2^k - 1) = 1,
$$

and more precisely,

$$
|x-y| \ge 2^k - (2^k - 1) = 1,
$$

but we need a sharper comparison with $2^n-1-(x\oplus y)$, so we proceed differently.

#### Step 2: direct comparison via bit structure

Since $x_{n-1}=1$, there are two subcases.

### Subcase 2a: $y_{n-1}=0$

Then the most significant differing bit is $k=n-1$. Hence

$$
x \oplus y \ge 2^{n-1}.
$$

Also,

$$
|x-y| \ge 2^{n-1}.
$$

Now if $x \oplus y > 0$, then

$$
(x \oplus y)^M = (2^n-1) - (x \oplus y).
$$

Since $x \oplus y \ge 2^{n-1}$,

$$
(x \oplus y)^M \le (2^n-1) - 2^{n-1} = 2^{n-1}-1.
$$

But

$$
|x-y| \ge 2^{n-1}.
$$

Thus

$$
(x \oplus y)^M < |x-y|.
$$

### Subcase 2b: $y_{n-1}=1$

Then the MSB cancels in XOR, so the most significant differing bit is some $k \le n-2$.

Write

$$
x = 2^{n-1} + x', \qquad y = 2^{n-1} + y',
$$

with $x',y' < 2^{n-1}$ and $x' \ne y'$. Then

$$
x \oplus y = x' \oplus y', \qquad |x-y| = |x'-y'|.
$$

We now apply the induction hypothesis (on $n-1$ bits) to $x',y'$:

$$
(x' \oplus y')^M < |x'-y'|.
$$

Since the MSB is $0$ in both $x'$ and $y'$, the complement is taken in $n-1$ bits:

$$
(x' \oplus y')^M = (2^{n-1}-1) - (x' \oplus y').
$$

Thus

$$
(2^{n-1}-1) - (x' \oplus y') < |x'-y'|.
$$

Add $2^{n-1}$ to both sides:

$$
(2^n-1) - (x' \oplus y') < 2^{n-1} + |x'-y'|.
$$

Now observe:

- $x \oplus y = x' \oplus y'$,
- $|x-y| = |x'-y'|$,
- and since both numbers have MSB $1$, we have $2^{n-1} + |x'-y'| > |x'-y'|$.

Hence in particular,

$$
(2^n-1) - (x \oplus y) < |x-y|.
$$

So

$$
(x \oplus y)^M < |x-y|.
$$

## 4. Conclusion

We have shown:

- If $x \oplus y = 0$, the inequality holds trivially.
- If $x \oplus y > 0$, the inequality holds by case analysis on the most significant differing bit and induction.

Therefore, for all $x,y \ge 0$,

$$
(x \oplus y)^M < |x-y| \le x \oplus y.
$$

∎
