**Exercise 23.** [*20*] [20] Suppose g(k) ⊕2j = g(l). What is a simple way to find l, given j and k?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 3m32s

## Solution

Let $x = g(k)$ and $x' = x \oplus 2^j$. The condition states $g(l) = x'$.

The inverse relation (8) expresses the binary digits of an integer in terms of a Gray-coded string. If $x = (. . . a_2 a_1 a_0)_2$, then

$$
b_i = a_i \oplus a_{i+1} \oplus a_{i+2} \oplus \cdots
$$

for $i \ge 0$, where $k = (. . . b_2 b_1 b_0)_2$.

Apply this reconstruction to both $x$ and $x'$. Since $x'$ differs from $x$ only at position $j$, the suffix parity changes only for those indices $i \le j$.

For $i > j$, the defining sum for $b_i$ does not include $x_j$, hence

$$
b_i' = b_i \quad \text{for } i > j.
$$

For $i \le j$, the term $x_j$ appears in the suffix defining $b_i$, so the flip of $x_j$ contributes an extra $\oplus 1$:

$$
b_i' = b_i \oplus 1 \quad \text{for } 0 \le i \le j.
$$

Thus the binary representation of $l$ is obtained from that of $k$ by complementing exactly the bits $0,1,\dots,j$. Equivalently,

$$
l = k \oplus (2^{j+1} - 1).
$$

This mask has binary form consisting of $j+1$ trailing ones, so the operation flips precisely the same bit set as induced by the Gray-code perturbation.

Therefore,

\boxed{l = k \oplus (2^{j+1} - 1)}. $$ ∎
