**Exercise 24.** [*M21*] [M21] Consider extending the Gray binary function g to all 2-adic integers (see
Section 7.1.3). What is the corresponding inverse function g[−1]?

## Solution

Let $g$ be defined on 2-adic integers by the same bit relation as in (7),

$$
a_j=b_j\oplus b_{j+1},
$$

where $k=(\ldots b_2 b_1 b_0)_2$ and $g(k)=(\ldots a_2 a_1 a_0)_2$. The extension to $\mathbb{Z}_2$ preserves this identity for all $j\ge 0$ since 2-adic expansions remain infinite to the left.

The inverse problem consists of recovering $(b_j)$ from $(a_j)$ under

$$
a_j=b_j\oplus b_{j+1}.
$$

Fixing a coordinate $j$ and summing the relations for indices $0$ through $j$ in $\mathbb{F}_2$ eliminates the intermediate terms:

$$
a_0 \oplus a_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus a_j
= (b_0\oplus b_1)\oplus (b_1\oplus b_2)\oplus \cdots \oplus (b_j\oplus b_{j+1}).
$$

All interior terms $b_1,\ldots,b_j$ cancel pairwise, leaving

$$
a_0 \oplus \cdots \oplus a_j = b_0 \oplus b_{j+1}.
$$

In 2-adic arithmetic the sequence $(b_j)$ is not required to vanish for large $j$, so the system must be solved recursively instead of truncated. The defining relation rearranges to

$$
b_{j+1}=b_0 \oplus (a_0 \oplus \cdots \oplus a_j).
$$

Setting $j=0$ gives

$$
b_1=b_0\oplus a_0.
$$

Setting $j=1$ gives

$$
b_2=b_0\oplus a_0\oplus a_1.
$$

Induction on $j$ yields

$$
b_j=b_0\oplus (a_0\oplus a_1\oplus \cdots \oplus a_{j-1}).
$$

The free parameter $b_0$ corresponds to the choice of initial 2-adic digit, so the inverse of $g$ on $\mathbb{Z}_2$ is not single-valued unless a normalization is fixed. The standard Gray construction corresponds to the normalization $b_0=0$, since leading zeros in binary expansions represent the same 2-adic integer limit.

Under this convention,

$$
b_j = a_0 \oplus a_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus a_{j-1}, \quad j\ge 1,
$$

and $b_0=0$. Shifting indices yields the compact form

$$
b_j = a_0 \oplus a_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus a_j.
$$

Thus the inverse map is the prefix XOR transform on 2-adic bit sequences, defined by

$$
g^{-1}(l)=(\ldots b_2 b_1 b_0)_2 \quad \text{with} \quad b_j=\bigoplus_{i=0}^j a_i,
$$

where $(\ldots a_2 a_1 a_0)_2=l$.

Equivalently in shift notation over $\mathbb{Z}_2$,

$$
g^{-1}(l)=l \oplus (l\ll 1)\oplus (l\ll 2)\oplus \cdots,
$$

and convergence holds 2-adically because each bit position depends on finitely many shifts.

The inverse is therefore the prefix-parity operator on infinite binary expansions, extending the finite-length formula (10) to the 2-adic domain without modification other than infinite convergence. This completes the proof. ∎
