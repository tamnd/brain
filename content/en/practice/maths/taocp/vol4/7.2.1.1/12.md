**Exercise 12.** &#9654; [*25*] [25] The compositions of n are the sequences of positive integers that sum to n.
For example, the compositions of 4 are 1111, 112, 121, 13, 211, 22, 31, and 4. An integer
n has exactly 2n−1 compositions, corresponding to all subsets of the points {1, . . . , n−1}
that might be used to break the interval (0 . . n) into integer-sized subintervals.
a) Design a loopless algorithm to generate all compositions of n, representing each
composition as a sequential array of integers s1s2 . . . sj.
b) Similarly, design a loopless algorithm that represents the compositions implicitly
in an array of pointers q0q1 . . . qt, where the elements of the composition are
(q0 −q1)(q1 −q2) . . . (qt−1 −qt) and we have q0 = n, qt = 0. For example, the
composition 211 would be represented under this scheme by the pointers q0 = 4,
q1 = 2, q2 = 1, q3 = 0, and with t = 3.

## Solution

Let a composition of $n$ be represented by a subset $S \subseteq {1,2,\dots,n-1}$, where $j \in S$ indicates a break between positions $j$ and $j+1$ in the interval $(0,n)$. If $S = {q_1 < q_2 < \cdots < q_t}$, define $q_0 = 0$, $q_{t+1} = n$, and the associated composition is

$$
s_i = q_i - q_{i-1}, \quad 1 \le i \le t+1.
$$

This gives a bijection between compositions of $n$ and subsets of ${1,\dots,n-1}$.

The task reduces to generating all subsets of an $(n-1)$-set in loopless fashion and maintaining the induced partition under single-bit changes.

### (a) Sequential array representation

Maintain a binary array $x[1],\dots,x[n-1]$, where $x[j]=1$ if $j \in S$ and $x[j]=0$ otherwise. Also maintain the current composition in an array $s[1],\dots,s[m]$, where $m$ is the current number of parts.

Initialize $x[j]=0$ for all $j$, and the composition is $s[1]=n$ with $m=1$.

Use a loopless Gray code generator for binary $(n-1)$-tuples, producing at each step the index $k$ of the single bit that changes. Such a generator exists by the construction in Section 7.2.1.1, where successive tuples differ in exactly one coordinate and the changed coordinate can be identified in $O(1)$ time.

At each step, suppose bit $x[k]$ changes.

If $x[k]$ changes from $0$ to $1$, a new break is inserted at position $k$, splitting one part. Let $k$ lie in the unique interval endpoint range

$$
q_{i-1} < k < q_i.
$$

Then replace $s_i$ by two parts

$$
(k - q_{i-1}, \; q_i - k).
$$

If $x[k]$ changes from $1$ to $0$, a break is removed at position $k$, merging two adjacent parts. If $k = q_i$, then replace

$$
(s_i, s_{i+1}) \mapsto (s_i + s_{i+1}).
$$

Each update modifies at most two adjacent entries of $s$ and requires no scanning of the array beyond locating the affected part via stored endpoints.

This yields a loopless generation of all $2^{n-1}$ compositions.

### (b) Pointer representation

Maintain a doubly linked ordered list of breakpoints

$$
q_0, q_1, \dots, q_t
$$

with $q_0 = 0$ and $q_t = n$. The composition is defined by

$$
(q_1 - q_0), (q_2 - q_1), \dots, (q_t - q_{t-1}).
$$

Again maintain a loopless Gray code generator on $(n-1)$ bits, producing at each step a single index $k$.

Maintain also a boolean array indicating whether $k$ is currently a breakpoint, and for each existing breakpoint store its predecessor and successor pointers in the list.

At each step:

If $k$ is not a breakpoint, insert it. Let $q_i < k < q_{i+1}$. Create a new node $k$ between $q_i$ and $q_{i+1}$ by updating pointers

$$
q_i \rightarrow k, \quad k \rightarrow q_{i+1},
$$

and split the composition accordingly.

If $k$ is a breakpoint, delete it by bypassing the node $k$:

$$
q_{i-1} \rightarrow q_{i+1}, \quad q_{i+1} \rightarrow q_{i-1},
$$

and merge the two adjacent parts.

Each step changes exactly one link insertion or deletion and updates at most constant many pointers, hence the generation is loopless.

Both constructions generate all compositions of $n$ in bijection with the loopless generation of all subsets of ${1,\dots,n-1}$, with each step requiring $O(1)$ structural changes.

This completes the solution. ∎
