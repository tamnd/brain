## Solution

Let $x \in [0,1)$ have binary expansion

$$
x = \sum_{j \ge 1} x_j 2^{-j}, \qquad x_j \in \{0,1\},
$$

and let the Rademacher functions be

$$
r_j(x) = (-1)^{x_j}.
$$

Let $k \ge 0$ have binary expansion

$$
k = \sum_{j \ge 0} k_j 2^j, \qquad k_j \in \{0,1\}.
$$

Equation (17) defines the Walsh function in Rademacher form as

$$
w_k(x) = \prod_{j \ge 0} r_{j+1}(x)^{k_j}.
$$

Since $r_{j+1}(x)^{0} = 1$ and $r_{j+1}(x)^{1} = r_{j+1}(x)$, the product reduces to

$$
w_k(x) = \prod_{j \ge 0:\, k_j=1} r_{j+1}(x).
$$

Substituting $r_{j+1}(x) = (-1)^{x_{j+1}}$ gives

$$
w_k(x) = \prod_{j \ge 0:\, k_j=1} (-1)^{x_{j+1}}
= (-1)^{\sum_{j \ge 0} k_j x_{j+1}}.
$$

The exponent simplifies modulo $2$ since each $k_j \in {0,1}$, so repeated multiplication corresponds to addition in $\mathbb{Z}_2$. Hence

$$
w_k(x) = (-1)^{\sum_{j \ge 0} k_j x_{j+1}}.
$$

The Walsh function defined in binary-index form assigns sign $(-1)$ exactly when the binary inner product between $(k_0,k_1,\dots)$ and $(x_1,x_2,\dots)$ is $1$ modulo $2$. This reproduces the standard Walsh definition in terms of dyadic addition of binary digits.

This establishes the Rademacher-to-Walsh law (17). ∎
