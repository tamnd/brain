## Solution

Let $\Gamma_n = v_0, v_1, \dots, v_{2^n-1}$ be an $n$-bit Gray cycle. For each coordinate $j$ with $0 \le j \le n-1$, let $c_j$ denote the number of transitions in the cycle that flip bit $j$. Every transition flips exactly one bit, hence

$$
c_0 + c_1 + \cdots + c_{n-1} = 2^n.
$$

For each $j$, consider the projection $\pi_j$ that deletes the $j$th bit. The cycle $\Gamma_n$ projects under $\pi_j$ to a closed walk in the $(n-1)$-cube in which each vertex is visited exactly twice, once with $x_j = 0$ and once with $x_j = 1$. Every transition counted by $c_j$ collapses to a stationary step in the projection, while every other transition produces a move in the $(n-1)$-cube.

Hence the projected walk has exactly $2^n - c_j$ moves.

Fix $0 \le j \le n-1$ and consider the partition of the $n$-cube into the $2^j$ classes determined by the first $j$ coordinates. Each class contains $2^{n-j}$ vertices, and each class is visited exactly $2^{n-j}$ times in the cycle when projected onto the first $j$ coordinates. Between any two consecutive visits of the same class, the cycle must leave that class, which requires a transition in one of the coordinates $0,1,\dots,j-1$.

For a fixed class, there are $2^{n-j}$ visits arranged cyclically, hence at least $2^{n-j}$ exits from that class in the cyclic traversal. Each exit corresponds to a transition in one of the coordinates $0,1,\dots,j-1$. Summing over all $2^j$ classes yields at least

$$
2^j \cdot 2^{n-j} / 2^{n-j} = 2^j
$$

distinct transitions counted with multiplicity in the coordinates $0,1,\dots,j-1$. This gives

$$
c_0 + c_1 + \cdots + c_{j-1} \ge 2^j.
$$

For $j=n$, the inequality gives

$$
c_0 + c_1 + \cdots + c_{n-1} \ge 2^n.
$$

Since every step of the Gray cycle changes exactly one bit, the left-hand side equals the total number of steps, which is exactly $2^n$, hence equality holds at $j=n$.

This completes the proof. ∎
