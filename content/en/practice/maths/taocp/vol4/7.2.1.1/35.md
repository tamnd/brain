[Section 7.2.1.1: Generating All n-Tuples](../)

**Exercise 35.** [*HM23*] [HM23] The 2n × 2n Paley matrix Pn is obtained from Paley functions just as
the Walsh matrix Wn is obtained from Walsh functions. (See (20).) Find interesting
relations between Pn, Wn, and the Hadamard matrix Hn. Prove that all three matrices
are symmetric.

## Solution

Let $r_j(x)$ denote the Rademacher functions and let $w_k(x)$ denote the Walsh functions defined in equation (17) of the section, so that for $k = (\ldots b_2 b_1 b_0)_2$ one has

$$
w_k(x) = \prod_{j \ge 0} r_j(x)^{b_j}.
$$

The Paley functions are defined by $p_0(x)=1$ and

$$
p_k(x) = (-1)^{\lfloor 2x \rfloor k}\, p_{\lfloor k/2 \rfloor}(2x).
$$

Write $k = 2q + \varepsilon$ with $\varepsilon \in {0,1}$ and $q = \lfloor k/2 \rfloor$. Then

$$
(-1)^{\lfloor 2x \rfloor k} = (-1)^{\lfloor 2x \rfloor (2q+\varepsilon)}
= \left((-1)^{\lfloor 2x \rfloor}\right)^{\varepsilon}
= r_0(x)^{\varepsilon},
$$

since $(-1)^{2\lfloor 2x \rfloor q}=1$. Hence the defining recurrence becomes

$$
p_k(x) = r_0(x)^{b_0}\, p_{\lfloor k/2 \rfloor}(2x),
$$

where $b_0 = k \bmod 2$.

Apply the same relation to $p_{\lfloor k/2 \rfloor}(2x)$. Writing $\lfloor k/2 \rfloor = (\ldots b_2 b_1)_2$, the same computation gives

$$
p_{\lfloor k/2 \rfloor}(2x) = r_1(x)^{b_1}\, p_{\lfloor k/4 \rfloor}(4x),
$$

since $r_0(2x)=(-1)^{\lfloor 4x \rfloor}=r_1(x)$.

Iteration yields, after $t$ steps,

$$
p_k(x) = \left(\prod_{j=0}^{t} r_j(x)^{b_j}\right)\, p_{\lfloor k/2^{t+1} \rfloor}(2^{t+1}x).
$$

For sufficiently large $t$, $\lfloor k/2^{t+1} \rfloor = 0$, and $p_0(\cdot)=1$, so the residual factor disappears. This gives the closed form

$$
p_k(x) = \prod_{j \ge 0} r_j(x)^{b_j}.
$$

By equation (17), the Walsh function with the same binary expansion $k = (\ldots b_2 b_1 b_0)_2$ satisfies

$$
w_k(x) = \prod_{j \ge 0} r_j(x)^{b_j}.
$$

Thus the Paley functions coincide with the Walsh functions pointwise,

$$
p_k(x) = w_k(x).
$$

The Walsh matrix $W_n$ has entries $w_k(x)$ evaluated at dyadic points $x = j/2^n$, and the Paley matrix $P_n$ is defined analogously from $p_k(x)$, so entrywise equality implies $P_n = W_n$.

The Hadamard matrix $H_n$ is defined recursively by

$$
H_0 = (1), \qquad H_{n+1} =
\begin{pmatrix}
H_n & H_n \\
H_n & -H_n
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

The Walsh construction satisfies the same recursion because appending one Rademacher factor $r_0(x)^{b_0}$ exactly splits rows into unchanged and sign-flipped copies according to $b_0$. Since Paley functions match Walsh functions pointwise, the same recursion holds for $P_n$, so

$$
P_{n+1} =
\begin{pmatrix}
P_n & P_n \\
P_n & -P_n
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

With $P_0 = W_0 = H_0$, induction on $n$ gives $P_n = W_n = H_n$ for all $n$, where the equality is understood entrywise after ordering indices by binary expansion consistent with equation (17).

Symmetry follows from the defining product representation. For $W_n$, swapping indices $k$ and $x$ corresponds to exchanging the binary roles of coefficients $b_j$ in $k$ and evaluation digits in $x$, and the product $\prod r_j(x)^{b_j}$ is invariant under this interchange. The same representation holds for $P_n$, so both matrices are symmetric. The Hadamard recursion preserves symmetry because if $H_n$ is symmetric then

$$
H_{n+1}^\mathsf{T} =
\begin{pmatrix}
H_n^\mathsf{T} & H_n^\mathsf{T} \\
H_n^\mathsf{T} & -H_n^\mathsf{T}
\end{pmatrix}
=
H_{n+1}.
$$

Thus $P_n$, $W_n$, and $H_n$ coincide under the standard ordering and are all symmetric matrices. This completes the proof. ∎
