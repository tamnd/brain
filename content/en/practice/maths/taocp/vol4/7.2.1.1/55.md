The converse is true.

Let $Q_n$ be the $n$-cube, and let $M$ be an arbitrary perfect matching between the even-parity and odd-parity vertices of $Q_n$. The question is whether there exists a Hamiltonian cycle $C$ of $Q_n$ such that every other edge of $C$ is an edge of $M$.

If such a cycle exists, then numbering its vertices cyclically as

$$
(v_0,v_1,\ldots,v_{2^n-1}),
$$

the edges

$$
\{\{v_{2k},v_{2k+1}\}:0\le k<2^{n-1}\}
$$

are precisely the edges of $M$. Thus the exercise asks whether every perfect matching can be extended to a Hamiltonian cycle in this alternating fashion.

This was posed as a conjecture by Ruskey and Savage. It was proved by Jiří Fink in 2007: every perfect matching of the $n$-cube extends to a Hamiltonian cycle. Since every edge of a perfect matching joins vertices of opposite parity, any Hamiltonian cycle containing the matching necessarily alternates between matching edges and nonmatching edges. Consequently, after choosing an appropriate starting vertex and orientation of the cycle, the matching is exactly

$$
\{\{v_{2k},v_{2k+1}\}:0\le k<2^{n-1}\}.
$$

Therefore every perfect matching between the even- and odd-parity vertices of the $n$-cube does arise as one half of an $n$-bit Gray cycle.

Hence the answer to the converse question is **yes**. This statement is a theorem, although it was still an open problem when the original paper of Ruskey and Savage appeared. It was established by Fink, who proved that every perfect matching of $Q_n$ extends to a Hamiltonian cycle.
