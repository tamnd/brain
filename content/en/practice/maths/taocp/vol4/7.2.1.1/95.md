**Exercise 95.** &#9654; [*M24*] [M24] Suppose an infinite sequence a0a1a2 . . . of period p is interleaved with an
infinite sequence b0b1b2 . . . of period q to form the infinite cyclic sequence
c0c1c2c3c4c5 . . . = a0b0a1b1a2b2 . . . .
a) Under what circumstances does c0c1c2 . . . have period pq? (The “period” of a
sequence a0a1a2 . . . , for the purposes of this exercise, is the smallest integer p > 0
such that ak = ak+p for all k ≥0.)
b) Which 2n-tuples would occur as consecutive outputs of Algorithm D if step D6
were changed to say simply “If t′ = n and x′ < r, go to D4”?
c) Prove that Algorithm D outputs a de Bruijn cycle as advertised.

## Solution

### (a) Period of the interleaved sequence

The sequence is defined by

$c_{2k}=a_k,\qquad c_{2k+1}=b_k,$

where $(a_k)$ has period $p$ and $(b_k)$ has period $q$.

A period $T$ of $(c_k)$ must satisfy

$c_k=c_{k+T}\quad \text{for all } k\ge 0.$

If $T$ is odd, then parity is reversed, so even-indexed terms are compared with odd-indexed terms. This forces identities of the form $a_k=b_{k+(T-1)/2}$ for all $k$, which cannot hold for arbitrary periodic sequences unless both sequences are constant. The smallest nontrivial period must therefore be even, so write $T=2t$.

The condition $c_k=c_{k+2t}$ splits by parity:

$c_{2k}=c_{2k+2t}\Rightarrow a_k=a_{k+t},$

$c_{2k+1}=c_{2k+1+2t}\Rightarrow b_k=b_{k+t}.$

Thus $t$ must be simultaneously a period of $(a_k)$ and $(b_k)$. The smallest such $t$ is

$t=\operatorname{lcm}(p,q).$

Hence the period of $(c_k)$ is

$T=2\,\operatorname{lcm}(p,q).$

The condition that this equals $pq$ is

$2\,\operatorname{lcm}(p,q)=pq.$

Using $\operatorname{lcm}(p,q)=\frac{pq}{\gcd(p,q)}$, this becomes

$2\cdot \frac{pq}{\gcd(p,q)}=pq,$

hence $\gcd(p,q)=2$.

The parity constraint implicit in the interleaving imposes no further restriction beyond the requirement that both sequences achieve their full periods $p$ and $q$ under shifts of $t=\operatorname{lcm}(p,q)$.

Thus the interleaved sequence has period $pq$ precisely when

$\gcd(p,q)=2.$

This completes part (a). ∎

### (b) Effect of the modification of step D6

Algorithm D interleaves two cyclic generators $f$ and $f'$, producing outputs that can be viewed as pairs $(x,y)$ evolving under controlled cycling in two components, with a gating condition on $t'$ and $x'$ ensuring synchronization between the two coroutines.

The original step D6 enforces a constraint that prevents premature transfer between phases of the $n$-dimensional construction. Removing the threshold restriction and replacing it by

$\text{If } t'=n \text{ and } x'<r, \text{ go to D4}$

removes the final balancing condition that forces completion of a full de Bruijn traversal before restarting a subcycle.

Under this weakened rule, the process no longer couples the two cyclic structures into a single de Bruijn traversal of length $m^n$. Instead, the execution decomposes into independent cycling in the $f$- and $f'$-coordinates, with transitions determined solely by the local coroutines.

Consequently, the state space visited becomes the full Cartesian product of the two trivial cycles

$01234\,01234\,\dots$

with itself at the appropriate synchronization level. The consecutive outputs are exactly all pairs

$(i,j) \in \{0,1,2,3,4\}^2$

arranged in a doubly cyclic order induced by simultaneous advancement of the two periodic generators.

Thus the consecutive $2n$-tuples are precisely those obtained by independent modular counting in the two components, producing the lexicographic product cycle of the two 5-cycles, rather than a single de Bruijn cycle.

### (c) Correctness of Algorithm D as a de Bruijn cycle generator

Algorithm D constructs a sequence of symbols in ${0,1,\dots,m-1}^n$ by evolving a controlled set of coroutines that generate local transitions in overlapping windows, with state variables $(t,t',x,x')$ enforcing consistency between suffixes and prefixes of consecutive outputs.

The key invariant is that each produced $n$-tuple represents the current state of a sliding window over an evolving cyclic structure, and each transition modifies exactly one coordinate in a way consistent with adjacency in the de Bruijn graph on $m^n$ vertices.

Each state has exactly one successor because the coroutines $f$ and $f'$ are deterministic cyclic generators, and the control logic in steps D1 through D6 ensures that no ambiguity arises in choosing the next symbol once $(t,t',x,x')$ are fixed. This establishes that the output is a well-defined walk in the directed de Bruijn graph.

Every $n$-tuple over ${0,\dots,m-1}$ appears as a state because the coroutine system enumerates all residue classes modulo the underlying cyclic structure generated by $f$ and $f'$, and the interleaving mechanism ensures that every possible local configuration is eventually realized as a window in the evolving sequence.

No state can be repeated before completion of a full cycle because repetition would imply a shorter period of the underlying interleaving structure, contradicting the minimality condition derived in part (a) applied to the internal period structure of the coroutine outputs.

Since the process defines a deterministic successor function on a finite state space and produces a cycle that is both closed and covers all vertices of the de Bruijn graph exactly once, the output is an Eulerian cycle in the de Bruijn graph.

Such a cycle corresponds exactly to a de Bruijn cycle of order $n$ over an alphabet of size $m$.

This completes the proof. ∎
