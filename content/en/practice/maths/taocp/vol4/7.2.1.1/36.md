## Solution

Let $W_n = (w_{ij})_{0 \le i,j < 2^n}$ denote the Walsh matrix defined in Section 7.2.1.1, where the entry $w_{ij}$ equals the value of the Walsh function $w_i(x)$ evaluated at the dyadic point corresponding to index $j$, equivalently the value $(-1)^{\langle i,j\rangle}$ under the binary inner product induced by equation (7). The Walsh transform of a vector $(X_0,\ldots,X_{2^n-1})$ is the vector $(x_0,\ldots,x_{2^n-1})$ defined by

$$
x_i = \sum_{j=0}^{2^n-1} w_{ij} X_j.
$$

The recursive structure of Walsh functions follows from equation (7), since writing $i = 2i' + b$ and $j = 2j' + c$ with $b,c \in {0,1}$ gives

$$
w_{ij} = (-1)^{bc} w_{i'j'}.
$$

This yields the block decomposition

$$
W_{n+1} =
\begin{pmatrix}
W_n & W_n \\
W_n & -W_n
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Let $X^{(n)}$ denote the vector of length $2^n$. Partition it into two halves,

$$
X^{(n+1)} =
\begin{pmatrix}
A \\
B
\end{pmatrix},
$$

where $A = (X_0,\ldots,X_{2^n-1})$ and $B = (X_{2^n},\ldots,X_{2^{n+1}-1})$.

Multiplication by $W_{n+1}$ gives

$$
W_{n+1}
\begin{pmatrix}
A \\
B
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
W_n A + W_n B \\
W_n A - W_n B
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Define intermediate vectors

$$
U = A + B, \qquad V = A - B.
$$

Then the transform reduces to

$$
W_n U, \qquad W_n V,
$$

and recombination gives the output

$$
x_i =
\begin{cases}
(W_n U)_i & 0 \le i < 2^n, \\
(W_n V)_{i-2^n} & 2^n \le i < 2^{n+1}.
\end{cases}
$$

This yields a recursive algorithm in which each stage performs one vector split, one vector addition and subtraction for each index pair $(A_j,B_j)$, and two smaller Walsh transforms of size $2^{n-1}$.

For the cost analysis, let $T(n)$ denote the number of arithmetic operations required for a transform of size $2^n$. The decomposition produces $2^n$ additions and $2^n$ subtractions to form $U$ and $V$, and two recursive calls on size $2^{n-1}$. Hence

$$
T(n) = 2T(n-1) + 2^{n+1}.
$$

Unfolding the recurrence gives

$$
T(n) = 2^n T(0) + \sum_{k=1}^{n} 2^{n-k} 2^{k+1}
= 2^n + \sum_{k=1}^{n} 2^{n+1}
= 2^n + n \cdot 2^{n+1}.
$$

Thus the Walsh transform can be computed in

$$
T(n) = (2n+1)2^n
$$

additions and subtractions, matching the optimal order $O(2^n n)$ implied by the necessity of combining $2^n$ inputs across $n$ levels of binary decomposition.

The resulting procedure is the fast Walsh–Hadamard transform: at each recursion level, pairs $(A_j,B_j)$ are replaced by $(A_j+B_j, A_j-B_j)$, and the same operation is applied recursively to each half until singletons are reached. This completes the construction of an efficient Walsh transform algorithm. ∎
