**Exercise 58.** &#9654; [*21*] [21] Let α be the delta sequence of an n-bit Gray cycle, and obtain β from α by
changing q occurrences of 0 to n, where q is odd. Prove that ββ is the delta sequence
of an (n + 1)-bit Gray cycle.

## Solution

Let the (n)-bit Gray cycle have vertices

[
v_0,v_1,\ldots,v_{2^n-1},
]

with indices taken modulo (2^n), and let its delta sequence be

[
\alpha=\alpha_0\alpha_1\cdots \alpha_{2^n-1}.
]

Coordinates are numbered (0,1,\ldots,n-1). Put (m=2^n). The sequence (\beta) is obtained from (\alpha) by changing exactly (q) occurrences of (0) into (n), where (q) is odd.

We construct a path in the ((n+1))-bit cube by starting at

[
w_0=(v_0,0),
]

where the final coordinate is the new coordinate (n), and then following the transitions given by (\beta).

For (0\leq i\leq m), let (r_i) be the number of symbols among

[
\beta_0,\beta_1,\ldots,\beta_{i-1}
]

that are equal to (n). Thus (r_i) is the number of replaced zeros that have occurred before step (i).

After (i) steps, the coordinates (1,\ldots,n-1) have changed exactly as in the original cycle, because the only altered symbols were zeros. The new coordinate has value

[
r_i\bmod 2.
]

The old coordinate (0) has been changed fewer times than in the original cycle by exactly the number of replaced zeros. Therefore it is the corresponding coordinate of (v_i) with a flip whenever (r_i) is odd. Hence

[
w_i=(x_i,r_i\bmod2),
]

where (x_i) is obtained from (v_i) by toggling coordinate (0) exactly (r_i\bmod2) times.

We first show that the vertices

[
w_0,w_1,\ldots,w_{m-1}
]

are distinct. Suppose

[
w_i=w_j.
]

The last coordinates give

[
r_i\equiv r_j\pmod2.
]

Thus the same toggle has been applied to coordinate (0) in both (x_i) and (x_j). Since

[
x_i=x_j,
]

it follows that

[
v_i=v_j.
]

The original Gray cycle has no repeated vertices, so (i=j). Therefore the first traversal visits (2^n) distinct vertices.

Now consider the endpoint after the first copy of (\beta). The coordinates (1,\ldots,n-1) return to their starting values. In the original cycle, coordinate (0) would also return to its starting value, but the (q) replaced zeros prevented (q) flips of that coordinate. Since (q) is odd, coordinate (0) is complemented. The new coordinate is also complemented, because it has been flipped (q) times. Therefore

[
w_m=(v_0\text{ with coordinate }0\text{ complemented},1).
]

The two copies of (\beta) now traverse corresponding vertices. Because the same delta sequence is applied from (w_m) as from (w_0), for every (0\leq i<m),

[
w_{m+i}
]

is obtained from (w_i) by complementing coordinates (0) and (n).

It remains to prove that the two traversals do not overlap. Suppose, for contradiction, that

[
w_i=w_{m+j}
]

for some (0\leq i,j<m). Using the relation between the two traversals,

[
w_i=\operatorname{comp}_{{0,n}}(w_j).
]

Write the new-coordinate values of (w_i,w_j) as (r_i\bmod2) and (r_j\bmod2). Complementing coordinate (n) gives

[
r_i\equiv r_j+1\pmod2.
]

Now compare the first (n) coordinates. Since (x_i) is obtained from (v_i) by flipping coordinate (0) exactly (r_i\bmod2) times, and the right side has coordinate (0) complemented, equality gives that (v_i) is obtained from (v_j) by flipping coordinate (0) exactly

[
r_i+r_j+1\equiv 0\pmod2
]

times. Therefore

[
v_i=v_j.
]

Because the original cycle has no repeated vertices,

[
i=j.
]

But then the new-coordinate relation becomes

[
r_i\equiv r_i+1\pmod2,
]

which is impossible. Hence the two traversals are disjoint.

The first traversal therefore contains (2^n) vertices, and the second traversal contains another (2^n) vertices, for a total of

[
2^{n+1}
]

distinct vertices. Every step changes exactly one coordinate, and the sequence of changes is precisely

[
\beta\beta .
]

Finally, after the second traversal, coordinates (0) and (n) have each been complemented twice, so they return to their starting values. All other coordinates also return to their starting values. Thus the path closes.

Therefore (\beta\beta) is the delta sequence of an ((n+1))-bit Gray cycle.

(\square)

I replaced the flawed overlap argument with a contradiction based on the relationship between the original vertices and the two complemented coordinates.
