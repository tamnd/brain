## Solution

Let $A_l = {0,1,\dots,l-1}$ with $l$ even, and consider $n$-tuples $x = (x_1,\dots,x_n) \in A_l^n$. A Gray cycle in this setting is a cyclic ordering of all $l^n$ tuples such that consecutive tuples differ in exactly one coordinate by $\pm 1$ modulo $l$, and the last tuple is adjacent to the first in the same sense.

Theorem D in this section constructs such a cycle when $l$ is odd by a reflected recursive scheme whose closure depends on the ability to match endpoints consistently under reversal. When $l$ is even, the obstruction disappears because the 1-dimensional base cycle admits a perfect antipodal symmetry, and this allows the same recursive reflection mechanism to close globally without parity conflict.

Define a base cycle on $A_l$ by

$$
\Gamma_1 = (0,1,2,\dots,l-1),
$$

viewed cyclically, so that $i$ is adjacent to $i+1 \pmod l$. Since $l$ is even, the involution $i \mapsto i + \frac{l}{2} \pmod l$ partitions $A_l$ into disjoint antipodal pairs, and the cycle $\Gamma_1$ is invariant under reversal up to a cyclic shift of exactly $\frac{l}{2}$ positions. This symmetry is the structural property required for the recursive construction.

Assume recursively that a cyclic Gray sequence $\Gamma_{n-1}$ has been constructed on $A_l^{n-1}$ with the property that consecutive tuples differ in exactly one coordinate by $\pm 1 \pmod l$, and that $\Gamma_{n-1}$ closes cyclically. List its elements as

$$
\Gamma_{n-1} = (v_0, v_1, \dots, v_{l^{n-1}-1}),
$$

with indices taken modulo $l^{n-1}$.

For each prefix $v_k$, attach a copy of $\Gamma_1$, but reverse its direction depending on the parity of $k$. Define

$$
\Gamma_n =
(v_0,0,\dots,v_0,l-1),
(v_1,l-1,\dots,v_1,0),
(v_2,0,\dots,v_2,l-1),
\dots,
(v_{l^{n-1}-1},\ast),
$$

where the $n$th coordinate cycles through $A_l$ in increasing order when $k$ is even and in decreasing order when $k$ is odd.

Within each block corresponding to fixed $v_k$, successive tuples differ only in the $n$th coordinate by $\pm 1 \pmod l$, so adjacency holds internally. Between blocks, the last element of the $k$th block is

$(v_k, b_k)$ and the first element of the $(k+1)$st block is $(v_{k+1}, b_{k+1})$. By construction of alternating direction and the cyclic symmetry of $\Gamma_1$ for even $l$, the boundary values satisfy $b_k = b_{k+1}$ for all $k$, so consecutive blocks differ only in the first $n-1$ coordinates. The transition from $v_k$ to $v_{k+1}$ already satisfies the Gray condition by the inductive hypothesis, hence adjacency is preserved across block boundaries.

It remains to verify cyclic closure. The last block corresponds to $v_{l^{n-1}-1}$, and its direction is opposite that of $v_0$ because $l^{n-1}$ is even or odd exactly as needed to match the alternation pattern induced by the recursion. Since $\Gamma_1$ has even length, reversing it preserves endpoint compatibility without introducing a fixed-point mismatch, so the last tuple is adjacent to the first by the same coordinate-change argument used internally between blocks.

Thus $\Gamma_n$ is a cyclic ordering of all $l^n$ tuples in which successive tuples differ in exactly one coordinate by $\pm 1 \pmod l$, and the construction is valid for all $n \ge 1$ when $l$ is even. This completes the construction analogous to Theorem D with $l$ even. ∎
