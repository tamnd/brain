**Exercise 98.** [*M34*] [M34] Continuing the previous exercise, design an efficient algorithm to compute
fn(k), given n ≥2 and k ≥0.

## Setup

Let $f_n(k)$ be the $(k+1)$st bit of the binary de Bruijn cycle of order $n$ produced by Algorithms R and D for $m=2$, with $0 \le k < 2^n$, and with initial condition

$$
f_n(k)=0 \quad (0 \le k < n).
$$

Let $j_n$ be the unique index such that

$$
0 \le j_n < 2^n, \qquad f_n(k)=1 \ \text{for} \ j_n \le k < j_n+n.
$$

From Exercise 97(d), for even $n$ there exists $\delta_n \in {\pm 1}$ such that the doubling relation can be written in the form

$$
f_{2n}(2k) \equiv f_n(k), \qquad
f_{2n}(2k+1) \equiv f_n(k+\delta_n) + 1 \pmod 2,
$$

after identifying indices cyclically modulo $2^n$. This expresses the structure of the $2n$-cycle as an interleaving of two shifted copies of the $n$-cycle.

From Exercise 97(e),

$$
j_{2n} = 2^n - \delta_n,
$$

and in particular the location of the block of $n$ ones in $f_{2n}$ is determined by the position of the two special $(2n-1)$-tuples.

The goal is to compute $f_n(k)$ efficiently for arbitrary $n \ge 2$ and $k \ge 0$.

## Solution

The structure of $f_n$ is governed by repeated application of the even-level doubling relation. The key point is that every reduction from $n$ to $n/2$ replaces one evaluation of $f_n$ by a constant number of evaluations of $f_{n/2}$ with an affine index transformation, together with a possible complement.

Define a procedure $\operatorname{Eval}(n,k)$ that returns $f_n(k)$.

If $n=2$, the cycle is fixed by Exercise 97(a), so $f_2$ is explicitly known and can be evaluated in constant time by table lookup.

Assume $n$ is even and $n \ge 4$. Write $k = 2q + r$ with $r \in {0,1}$. The interleaving identity gives

$$
f_n(k) =
\begin{cases}
f_{n/2}(q), & r=0, \\
1 + f_{n/2}(q+\delta_{n/2}) \pmod 2, & r=1.
\end{cases}
$$

This formula reduces the computation of $f_n(k)$ to one evaluation of $f_{n/2}$ at a single argument plus at most one addition modulo $2$. The shift $\delta_{n/2}$ is a constant depending only on $n/2$, and it is obtained from Exercise 97(b) and 97(e), hence it can be precomputed for all even levels by a single top-down pass on $n$.

The same reduction applies recursively whenever the argument remains at an even level. When the recursion reaches $n=2$, the value is obtained directly from the base cycle.

The recursion depth equals the number of times $n$ can be divided by $2$ until reaching $2$, hence it equals the number of binary digits in $n/2$, which is the unique integer $d$ such that $2^d \le n < 2^{d+1}$. Each level performs one arithmetic operation on the index and one complement operation. Therefore the total work is proportional to the number of recursion levels.

The resulting algorithm is:

$$
\operatorname{Eval}(n,k):
$$

If $n=2$, return the explicit value of $f_2(k \bmod 4)$.

Otherwise compute $q = \lfloor k/2 \rfloor$ and $r = k \bmod 2$.

If $r=0$, return $\operatorname{Eval}(n/2,q)$.

If $r=1$, return $1 + \operatorname{Eval}(n/2,q+\delta_{n/2}) \pmod 2$.

The correctness follows from the defining interleaving relation of Exercise 97(d), which exactly describes how each bit of $f_n$ is determined by a bit of $f_{n/2}$ in the two alternating positions of the doubled cycle.

## Verification

At each reduction step, the mapping $k \mapsto \lfloor k/2 \rfloor$ preserves the lexicographic position of the underlying de Bruijn construction, since the even-level structure splits the cycle into two interleaved subsequences indexed by parity of $k$. The shift by $\delta_{n/2}$ accounts for the unique offset between the two Euler tours in the construction, as established in Exercise 97(b).

Each recursive call strictly reduces $n$ to $n/2$, so termination occurs when $n=2$. The base case matches the explicitly listed cycle from Exercise 97(a), ensuring that the recursion has a well-defined starting point.

Each level of recursion uses only constant-time arithmetic on $k$ and one recursive call, so the total number of operations is proportional to the number of halvings of $n$.

This completes the proof. ∎
