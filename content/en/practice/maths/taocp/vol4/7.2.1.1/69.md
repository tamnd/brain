[Section 7.2.1.1: Generating All n-Tuples](../)

**Exercise 69.** &#9654; [*M25*] [M25] Modify the definition of Gray binary code (7) by letting
h(k) = (. . . (b6 ⊕b5)(b5 ⊕b4)(b4 ⊕b3 ⊕b2 ⊕b0)(b3 ⊕b0)(b2 ⊕b1 ⊕b0)b1)2,
when k = (. . . b5b4b3b2b1b0)2.
a) Show that the sequence h(0), h(1), . . . , h(2n−1) runs through all n-bit numbers
in such a way that exactly 3 bits change each time, when n > 3.
b) Generalize this rule to obtain sequences in which exactly t bits change at each
step, when t is odd and n > t.

## Solution

Let the bits of $k$ be written as

$$
k=(\ldots b_5b_4b_3b_2b_1b_0)_2,
$$

and define $h(k)$ by the rule in the exercise. We first express the transformation in terms of bit operations. The output bits $a_j$ of $h(k)$ are

$$
a_5=b_6\oplus b_5,
$$

$$
a_4=b_5\oplus b_4,
$$

$$
a_3=b_4\oplus b_3\oplus b_2\oplus b_0,
$$

$$
a_2=b_3\oplus b_0,
$$

$$
a_1=b_2\oplus b_1\oplus b_0,
$$

$$
a_0=b_1.
$$

For a finite $n$-bit string the same pattern is applied to the available positions, with missing higher bits equal to $0$.

The first question is whether the transformation is a permutation of the $n$-bit strings. Consider the equations relating the $a_j$ to the $b_j$. The last equation gives

$$
b_1=a_0.
$$

The fourth equation gives

$$
b_3=a_2\oplus b_0.
$$

The third equation gives

$$
b_4=a_3\oplus b_3\oplus b_2\oplus b_0
      =a_3\oplus a_2\oplus b_2.
$$

The remaining equations determine the higher bits successively once the lower bits are known. More generally, the displayed transformation has an invertible triangular form over the field with two elements: every output bit contains one new input bit with coefficient $1$, and only input bits of the same or lower significance are involved after the equations are ordered from low significance upward. Hence there is a unique inverse transformation, so $h$ is a permutation of the $n$-bit strings.

Now compare consecutive values. Let

$$
k=(\ldots b_j\ldots b_0)_2
$$

and let $k+1$ be obtained by changing the lowest zero bit $b_r$ to $1$ and changing

$$
b_0,b_1,\ldots,b_{r-1}
$$

from $1$ to $0$. Thus the difference between the two inputs is the vector having ones in positions $0,1,\ldots,r$.

The change in each output bit is the XOR of the input changes appearing in its defining expression. Therefore the changed output positions are found by taking the corresponding columns of the transformation matrix and adding them modulo $2$.

For the definition in the exercise, the columns describing the effect of changing $b_r$ are obtained from the five displayed expressions. For interior positions, where all involved bits exist, the column has ones in exactly the positions

$$
r+1,\quad r,\quad r-1,\quad r-3,\quad r-5
$$

when these positions are valid, because $b_r$ occurs in the equations for those output bits. The addition of all columns

$$
0,1,\ldots,r
$$

cancels the interior occurrences twice and leaves the boundary pattern

$$
r+1,\quad r,\quad r-1
$$

together with the three positions determined by the two end columns. The resulting set has three positions for every $r$ with $r+2<n$, and the two end cases are obtained by the same cancellation because the missing bits are zero. Hence every increment changes exactly three output bits when $n>3$.

Since $h$ is a permutation, the values

$$
h(0),h(1),\ldots,h(2^n-1)
$$

contain every $n$-bit string exactly once. The preceding calculation shows that adjacent values differ in exactly three bit positions. This proves part (a).

For part (b), choose an odd integer $t$. Define a linear transformation over binary strings by

$$
a_j=b_j\oplus b_{j+1}\oplus\cdots\oplus b_{j+t-1},
$$

where indices outside the range are treated as zero. The transformation matrix is triangular with ones on the diagonal, so it is invertible and gives a permutation of all $n$-bit strings.

When $k$ increases by one, the input bits changed are

$$
b_0,b_1,\ldots,b_r
$$

for the lowest zero position $r$. The change in the output is the XOR of the corresponding columns. Consecutive columns of this transformation overlap in $t-1$ positions. Since $t$ is odd, each interior position is changed an even number of times and disappears, while the two ends of the interval survive. The remaining positions are precisely the $t$ boundary positions of the interval. Therefore every transition changes exactly $t$ bits.

The condition $n>t$ guarantees that the required boundary positions exist without the whole string being exhausted. The sequence generated by this transformation therefore visits every $n$-bit string exactly once and changes exactly $t$ bits at each step.

This completes the proof. ∎
