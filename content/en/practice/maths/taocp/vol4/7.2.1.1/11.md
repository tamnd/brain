**Exercise 11.** [*M22*] [M22] (H. J. Purkiss, 1865.) The two smallest rings of the Chinese ring puzzle
can actually be taken on or off the bar simultaneously. How many steps does the puzzle
require when such accelerated moves are permitted?

## Solution

Let $f,g,h$ be Boolean functions on $n$ variables $x_1,\dots,x_n$, and write the relation

$$
f \,\$\, g \,\$\, h
$$

to mean that for all assignments $x,y \in {0,1}^n$,

$$
f(x)=g(y)=1 \;\Rightarrow\; h(x\wedge y)=1,
$$

where $(x\wedge y)_i = x_i \wedge y_i$.

The relation fails exactly when there exist $x,y$ such that

$$
f(x)=1,\quad g(y)=1,\quad h(x\wedge y)=0.
$$

Hence the problem is equivalent to testing satisfiability of

$$
F(x,y) = f(x)\wedge g(y)\wedge \neg h(x\wedge y).
$$

The relation holds if and only if $F$ is identically $0$.

Let $B(f),B(g),B(h)$ denote the numbers of nodes in the reduced ordered BDDs of $f,g,h$. Each BDD is ordered, so every node queries variables in increasing index order.

Construct a product traversal over states consisting of a triple of BDD nodes

$$
(u,v,w),
$$

where $u$ is a node of $f$, $v$ of $g$, and $w$ of $h$. A state represents the set of partial assignments consistent with reaching these nodes under a common pair $(x,y)$.

At each step, let $i$ be the smallest variable index among the labels $V(u),V(v),V(w)$ that is not yet resolved along the current path. The traversal branches on the values of the relevant variables at level $i$.

If $i = V(u)$, the $x_i$ variable is resolved in $f$ and $u$ moves to its LO or HI successor according to $x_i$.

If $i = V(v)$, the $y_i$ variable is resolved in $g$ and $v$ moves to its LO or HI successor according to $y_i$.

If $i = V(w)$, the variable of $h$ is $z_i = x_i \wedge y_i$. The value of $z_i$ is determined by the pair $(x_i,y_i)$ already chosen along the traversal, so $w$ moves to its LO successor when $x_i \wedge y_i = 0$ and to its HI successor when $x_i \wedge y_i = 1$.

Sink states are interpreted in the standard way: a state is accepting when

$$
u=\top,\quad v=\top,\quad w=\bot,
$$

because this corresponds to $f(x)=1$, $g(y)=1$, and $\neg h(x\wedge y)=1$.

The search explores the reachable portion of the product graph of node triples. Memoization identifies repeated states $(u,v,w)$, since future behavior depends only on the current BDD nodes and not on the path by which they were reached. Every transition advances at least one of $u,v,w$ along the ordered variable indices, so no cycle occurs.

Each state $(u,v,w)$ is formed from a choice of one node in each BDD, so the number of states is bounded by

$$
|U|\cdot|V|\cdot|W| = B(f)\,B(g)\,B(h).
$$

Each state generates at most four transitions, corresponding to the four possible pairs $(x_i,y_i)\in{0,1}^2$, and each transition updates at most one node pointer per BDD according to its LO/HI structure.

Thus each state contributes $O(1)$ work, since computing $x_i\wedge y_i$ and following pointers in $w$ is constant time and BDD navigation is pointer chasing.

The traversal therefore runs in time proportional to the number of reachable product states, which is bounded by $O(B(f)B(g)B(h))$. The relation $f ,$, g ,$, h$ holds exactly when no accepting state $(\top,\top,\bot)$ is reachable, so the algorithm decides the relation within this bound.

This completes the proof. ∎
