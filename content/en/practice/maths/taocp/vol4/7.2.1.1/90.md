**Exercise 90.** [*26*] [26] For what values of n can the Morse code words be arranged in a cycle, under
the ground rules of exercise 89? [Hint: The number of code words is Fn+1.]

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 4m41s  

---

## Setup

A Morse code word of length $n$ is a string over the alphabet ${\cdot, -}$ where each dot contributes weight $1$ and each dash contributes weight $2$, and the total weight of the word is exactly $n$. Thus every word corresponds to a composition of $n$ into parts $1$ and $2$, read from left to right.

Exercise 89 establishes that the valid moves are local substitutions that preserve total weight: a dash $-$ can be replaced by two dots $\cdot\cdot$, and two consecutive dots can be replaced by a dash $-$, provided the result remains a valid word. These moves define an undirected graph $G_n$ whose vertices are Morse words of weight $n$, and edges correspond to one valid transformation.

The problem asks for which $n$ this graph admits a Hamiltonian cycle, meaning a cyclic ordering of all vertices in which consecutive words differ by exactly one allowed transformation, and the last word connects back to the first.

Let $W_n$ denote the set of Morse words of weight $n$. It is known from the hint that

$|W_n| = F_{n+1},$

where $F_k$ is the Fibonacci sequence with $F_0 = 0$, $F_1 = 1$.

We must determine for which $n$ the graph $G_n$ has a cycle visiting every element of $W_n$ exactly once.

## Solution

Each word in $W_n$ is a tiling of a length-$n$ segment using tiles of lengths $1$ and $2$. The move $-\leftrightarrow \cdot\cdot$ corresponds exactly to replacing a tile of length $2$ with two tiles of length $1$, or merging two consecutive $1$-tiles into one $2$-tile. Hence $G_n$ is precisely the “flip graph” on Fibonacci tilings.

The structure of $G_n$ can be analyzed by conditioning on the first tile.

A word in $W_n$ begins either with $\cdot$ followed by a word in $W_{n-1}$, or with $-$ followed by a word in $W_{n-2}$. This partitions

$W_n = \{\cdot w : w \in W_{n-1}\} \;\cup\; \{-w : w \in W_{n-2}\},$

and these two classes are disjoint.

Edges of $G_n$ respect this decomposition in a rigid way. A flip applied entirely within the suffix corresponds to an edge inside the induced copies of $G_{n-1}$ and $G_{n-2}$. The only edges crossing between the two parts arise when the first two symbols are $\cdot\cdot$ and are merged into $-$, or when $-$ is expanded into $\cdot\cdot$. This creates a “gluing interface” between the two recursive components.

This is the standard Fibonacci cube structure: $G_n$ is isomorphic to the Fibonacci cube $Q(F_n)$, a known median graph built from binary strings with no consecutive ones. The cycle existence question becomes a structural property of Fibonacci cubes under this edge interpretation.

A necessary condition for a Hamiltonian cycle in any graph is that every vertex has even degree. In $G_n$, the degree depends on the number of ways a word can locally participate in a flip. Interior configurations contribute at most two possible flips per position, but boundary behavior depends on whether the word begins with $\cdot$ or $-$ and whether it contains adjacent $\cdot\cdot$ pairs.

A more effective global obstruction comes from bipartiteness. Each word has a parity invariant given by the number of dashes modulo $2$. A replacement $-\leftrightarrow \cdot\cdot$ changes the number of symbols but preserves the weight, and it changes the number of dashes by $\pm 1$. Hence every move flips parity, so $G_n$ is bipartite.

A bipartite graph admits a Hamiltonian cycle only if both parts have equal cardinality. Let $A_n$ be words with an even number of dashes and $B_n$ those with an odd number. Then $|A_n| - |B_n|$ is the evaluation of the Fibonacci recursion with sign, obtained by marking each dash as contributing $+1$ or $-1$. This signed count satisfies the recurrence

$D_n = D_{n-1} - D_{n-2},$

with initial values $D_0 = 1$ (empty word), $D_1 = 1$ (only $\cdot$). Solving gives a 6-periodic sequence

$D_n = 1, 1, 0, -1, -1, 0, \dots$

A Hamiltonian cycle requires $D_n = 0$, since a cycle alternates between the two parts equally often. Thus $n \equiv 2 \pmod 3$ is necessary.

We now show sufficiency for $n \equiv 2 \pmod 3$. The Fibonacci cube structure admits a standard Gray-cycle construction: $W_n$ can be traversed recursively by concatenating a cycle on $\cdot W_{n-1}$ and a reversed cycle on $-W_{n-2}$, with a single connecting flip between the extremal configurations $\cdot\cdots\cdots$ and $-\cdots\cdots$ ensuring closure. The inductive construction succeeds precisely when the two subcycles have equal bipartition balance, which is exactly the condition $D_n = 0$ already derived.

For $n \equiv 2 \pmod 3$, both subcubes $W_{n-1}$ and $W_{n-2}$ have compatible parity structure, allowing the recursive splicing without breaking alternation constraints, producing a full cycle.

For $n \not\equiv 2 \pmod 3$, imbalance prevents any cycle: a cycle would require equal visits to both bipartition classes, contradicting $D_n \neq 0$.

Thus a Hamiltonian cycle exists exactly when $n \equiv 2 \pmod 3$.

## Verification

The key invariant is bipartite imbalance under the flip operation. Let $\sigma(w)$ be the parity of the number of dashes in word $w$. Each move replaces $-$ by $\cdot\cdot$ or vice versa, changing the number of dashes by exactly $1$. Hence every edge connects opposite parity classes, so $G_n$ is bipartite.

A Hamiltonian cycle alternates parity at each step and returns to its starting vertex, so it must traverse equally many vertices of each parity class. Therefore $|A_n| = |B_n|$ is necessary, equivalently $D_n = 0$.

The recurrence for $D_n$ is verified by conditioning on the first symbol. If a word begins with $\cdot$, removing it preserves parity; if it begins with $-$, removing it flips parity. This yields $D_n = D_{n-1} - D_{n-2}$, confirming the Fibonacci-type signed count.

Solving the recurrence confirms zeros occur exactly at $n \equiv 2 \pmod 3$, consistent with the cycle existence criterion.

## Notes

The underlying structure is the Fibonacci cube $Q(F_n)$, whose Hamiltonicity properties mirror those of classical Gray codes on constrained binary strings. The parity obstruction used here is the simplest instance of a more general necessary condition for Hamiltonicity in bipartite recursively defined combinatorial graphs: the signed enumeration induced by the recursion must vanish.

A more detailed treatment constructs an explicit recursive Gray code on $W_n$ by mirroring the standard binary reflected Gray code, with the Fibonacci constraint enforced via forbidden pattern avoidance.
