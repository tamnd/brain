## Solution

Let $\omega = e^{2\pi i/3}$ so that $\omega^3 = 1$ and $1+\omega+\omega^2 = 0$.

Write every $x \in [0,1)$ in base $3$ as

$$
x = \sum_{j \ge 0} x_j 3^{-(j+1)}, \quad x_j \in \{0,1,2\},
$$

choosing the expansion that is not eventually $2$.

For each $j \ge 0$, define the digit character

$$
r_j(x) = \omega^{x_j}.
$$

Each $r_j(x)$ takes values in ${1,\omega,\omega^2}$ and depends only on the $j$th ternary digit of $x$.

Let $k$ be a nonnegative integer with ternary expansion

$$
k = \sum_{j \ge 0} k_j 3^j, \quad k_j \in \{0,1,2\},
$$

where all but finitely many $k_j$ are zero.

Define the ternary Walsh function

$$
w_k(x) = \prod_{j \ge 0} r_j(x)^{k_j}.
$$

Since $r_j(x)^3 = 1$, the exponent $k_j$ is taken modulo $3$, and only finitely many factors differ from $1$, so the product is well-defined.

Equivalently,

$$
w_k(x) = \omega^{\sum_{j \ge 0} k_j x_j}.
$$

To establish orthonormality in $L^2[0,1]$, consider $w_k$ and $w_\ell$ with ternary digits $k_j$ and $\ell_j$. Their inner product is

$$
\int_0^1 w_k(x)\overline{w_\ell(x)}\,dx
= \int_0^1 \omega^{\sum_{j \ge 0} (k_j-\ell_j)x_j}\,dx.
$$

Partition $[0,1)$ into intervals of length $3^{-N}$ and use that on each such interval the first $N$ digits $(x_0,\dots,x_{N-1})$ are constant while the remaining digits vary independently and uniformly over ${0,1,2}$. The integral therefore factorizes as a product of digit averages:

$$
\int_0^1 w_k(x)\overline{w_\ell(x)}\,dx
= \prod_{j \ge 0} \left( \frac{1}{3}\sum_{t=0}^2 \omega^{(k_j-\ell_j)t} \right).
$$

For each fixed $j$, the factor equals $1$ if $k_j \equiv \ell_j \pmod 3$, since then $\omega^{(k_j-\ell_j)t}=1$ for all $t$. If $k_j \not\equiv \ell_j \pmod 3$, then $k_j-\ell_j \in {1,2}$ and

$$
\frac{1}{3}\sum_{t=0}^2 \omega^{(k_j-\ell_j)t}
= \frac{1}{3}(1+\omega^{m}+\omega^{2m}) = 0,
$$

since $1+\omega+\omega^2=0$.

The product is therefore $1$ when $k=\ell$ and $0$ otherwise, so

$$
\int_0^1 w_k(x)\overline{w_\ell(x)}\,dx = \delta_{k\ell}.
$$

The family ${w_k}_{k \ge 0}$ is thus an orthonormal system in $L^2[0,1]$.

Completeness follows by viewing $w_k$ as characters of the additive group of ternary digit sequences with finite support, which is dense in $L^2[0,1]$ under the standard identification of functions depending on finitely many ternary digits. Any such function is a finite linear combination of $w_k$, so the span is dense, hence the system is complete.
