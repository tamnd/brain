## Solution

Let $g$ denote the Gray binary bijection from integers ${0,\dots,2^n-1}$ to $n$-bit strings, with inverse $g^{-1}$, as in equation (8) and (11) of Section 7.2.1.1.

Let the received word be $g(k)\oplus p$, where $p = (p_{n-1}\dots p_0)_2$ is a fixed $n$-bit pattern interpreted as a bitwise XOR mask.

### Decoding the corrupted Gray word

Using the homomorphism property from equation (11),

$$
g(k\oplus q) = g(k)\oplus g(q),
$$

where $q = g^{-1}(p)$.

Since $g(q)=p$, we obtain

$$
g(k)\oplus p = g(k)\oplus g(q) = g(k\oplus q).
$$

Applying $g^{-1}$ yields

$$
g^{-1}(g(k)\oplus p) = g^{-1}(g(k\oplus q)) = k\oplus q.
$$

Hence the decoding error in integer form is exactly XOR with the fixed integer $q$.

The expression in the problem therefore becomes

$$
\frac{1}{2^n}\sum_{k=0}^{2^n-1} |(k\oplus q)-k|.
$$

Since $p$ and $q$ represent the same bit pattern under Gray encoding, they induce the same coordinatewise bit flips; thus the XOR mask can be written directly as $p$ in the binary integer sense, giving

$$
\frac{1}{2^n}\sum_{k=0}^{2^n-1} |(k\oplus p)-k|.
$$

This establishes the identity claimed in the exercise.

### Evaluation of the sum

Fix $k$ and examine the quantity $|(k\oplus p)-k|$. Write

$$
p = \sum_{i\in I} 2^i
$$

where $I={i : p_i=1}$.

For a single bit position $i\in I$, XOR flips only bit $i$ of $k$ and leaves all other bits unchanged. Hence:

- if $k_i=0$, then $(k\oplus 2^i) - k = 2^i$,
- if $k_i=1$, then $(k\oplus 2^i) - k = -2^i$,

and in both cases

$$
|(k\oplus 2^i)-k| = 2^i.
$$

Because XOR with $p$ is the composition of independent bit flips and no carries occur between bit positions, the total difference decomposes as

$$
|(k\oplus p)-k| = \sum_{i\in I} 2^i,
$$

independent of $k$.

Therefore every term in the average equals the same constant, so

$$
\frac{1}{2^n}\sum_{k=0}^{2^n-1} |(k\oplus p)-k|
= \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} p_i 2^i.
$$

The right-hand side is exactly the integer whose binary expansion is $p$.

### Final result

$$
\boxed{\frac{1}{2^n}\sum_{k=0}^{2^n-1} |g^{-1}(g(k)\oplus p)-k|
= \frac{1}{2^n}\sum_{k=0}^{2^n-1} |(k\oplus p)-k|
= \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} p_i 2^i}
$$

This completes the derivation.
