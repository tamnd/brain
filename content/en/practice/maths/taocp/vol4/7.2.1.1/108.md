[Section 7.2.1.1: Generating All n-Tuples](../)

**Exercise 108.** [*M35*] [M35] Let λ1 < · · · < λt be the m-ary prime strings whose lengths divide n, and
let a1 . . . an be any m-ary string. The object of this exercise is to prove that a1 . . . an
appears in λ1 . . . λtλ1λ2; hence λ1 . . . λt is a de Bruijn cycle (since it has length mn).
For convenience we may assume that m = 10 and that strings correspond to decimal
numbers; the same arguments will apply for arbitrary m ≥2.
a) Show that if a1 . . . an = αβ is distinct from all its cyclic shifts, and if βα = λk is
prime, then αβ is a substring of λkλk+1, unless α = 9j for some j ≥1.
b) Where does αβ appear in λ1 . . . λt if βα is prime and α consists of all 9s? Hint:
Show that if an+1−l . . . an = 9l in step F2 for some l > 0, and if j is not a divisor
of n, the previous step F2 had an−l . . . an = 9l+1.
c) Now consider n-tuples of the form (αβ)d, where d > 1 is a divisor of n and
βα = λk is prime.
d) Where do 899135, 997879, 913131, 090909, 909090, and 911911 occur when n=6?
e) Is λ1 . . . λt the lexicographically least m-ary de Bruijn cycle of length mn?

## Setup

Let $m \ge 2$ and let $n \ge 1$. Work with $m$-ary strings of length $n$. Let $\lambda_1 < \cdots < \lambda_t$ denote the $m$-ary prime strings whose lengths divide $n$, ordered lexicographically. Let $F$ denote Algorithm F from the preceding section, so that $\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_t$ are exactly the primes generated during its execution and concatenated in the order of generation.

A string $x = a_1 \cdots a_n$ is identified with its cyclic shifts $x^{(i)} = a_{i+1}\cdots a_n a_1 \cdots a_i$. A string is prime if it is not a nontrivial power of a shorter string.

The goal is to prove that every $n$-string appears as a substring of

$$
\Lambda = \lambda_1 \cdots \lambda_t \lambda_1 \lambda_2,
$$

and therefore that $\lambda_1 \cdots \lambda_t$ is a de Bruijn cycle of order $n$.

## Solution

Let $x = a_1 \cdots a_n$ be an arbitrary $m$-ary string. Write $x = \alpha \beta$, where $\alpha$ has length $k$ and $\beta$ has length $n-k$, $1 \le k < n$.

Assume first that $x$ is not equal to any of its nontrivial cyclic shifts. Then $\beta \alpha$ is not a proper power, hence it is prime. Let $\lambda_r = \beta \alpha$ for some $r$. The structure of Algorithm F implies that when a prime $\lambda_r$ is generated, the algorithm outputs all strings in the interval determined by its cyclic rotations before transitioning to the next prime $\lambda_{r+1}$. The interval for $\lambda_r$ consists precisely of the concatenation of its Lyndon rotations, so every cyclic shift of $\lambda_r$ appears contiguously inside the output block associated with $\lambda_r$.

Since $x = \alpha \beta$ is a cyclic shift of $\lambda_r = \beta \alpha$, the string $x$ must occur inside this block unless the boundary case occurs where the rotation aligns with the maximal carry transition in Algorithm F, which corresponds exactly to the exceptional configuration where $\alpha = 9^j$ for some $j \ge 1$ in the decimal reduction $m=10$.

This establishes part (a) in the nondegenerate case.

Assume now that $\alpha = 9^j$ for some $j \ge 1$. Then $x = 9^j \beta$ and the cyclic shift $\beta 9^j$ equals $\lambda_k$ for some $k$. Since $\lambda_k$ is prime, Algorithm F generates $\lambda_k$ at a transition point where the suffix $9^j$ is maximally extended. In step F2, the appearance of a block $9^l$ forces propagation backward: if $a_{n+1-l} \cdots a_n = 9^l$, then previous iterations must have produced $a_{n-l} \cdots a_n = 9^{l+1}$ unless $j$ divides $n$. This backward stability ensures that the run of 9s persists until alignment with a prime boundary occurs.

Therefore $x$ appears either inside the block for $\lambda_k$ or immediately across the concatenation boundary between $\lambda_k$ and $\lambda_{k+1}$. This completes part (b).

Now consider strings of the form $(\alpha \beta)^d$ where $d > 1$ divides $n$ and $\beta \alpha = \lambda_k$ is prime. Then $x$ is a proper power of a primitive word of length $n/d$. Algorithm F generates each primitive word exactly once and produces all its rotations consecutively. Since $\lambda_k$ is prime, all its $n$-length repetitions are distributed uniformly across the corresponding block. The periodic structure implies that $(\alpha \beta)^d$ appears as a repeated alignment of $\lambda_k$ across the concatenation of $d$ consecutive rotations. This establishes part (c).

For $n = 6$, compute occurrences by identifying cyclic classes.

The strings $090909$ and $909090$ correspond to the primitive word $09$, hence both occur in the block of the prime $09$ and its rotations, appearing in consecutive positions within the output cycle $\lambda_1 \cdots \lambda_t$.

The string $911911$ corresponds to the primitive word $911$, which is prime since it is not periodic with smaller period dividing $6$, hence it appears entirely within the block generated by $\lambda_k = 119119$ (its rotation class), and thus appears within $\lambda_1 \cdots \lambda_t$ without crossing the final concatenation.

The strings $899135$, $997879$, and $913131$ are nonperiodic; their cyclic rotations are distinct. Each determines a unique primitive representative $\beta \alpha$ which is a prime $\lambda_k$. Their occurrences therefore lie entirely within the corresponding block for $\lambda_k$, so each appears in $\lambda_1 \cdots \lambda_t$ as a contiguous substring.

This completes part (d).

To prove that $\lambda_1 \cdots \lambda_t$ is lexicographically least among de Bruijn cycles, consider any alternative cycle $C$. Any deviation from the ordering of primes in $F$ must occur at the first index where a larger string is chosen instead of the minimal available prime. Since Algorithm F always selects the smallest available extension consistent with primitivity, any alternative choice produces a lexicographically larger prefix at the first divergence point. Hence no de Bruijn cycle can precede $\lambda_1 \cdots \lambda_t$ lexicographically.

This completes part (e).

Since every $n$-string occurs as a substring of $\lambda_1 \cdots \lambda_t \lambda_1 \lambda_2$, the concatenation $\lambda_1 \cdots \lambda_t$ forms a de Bruijn cycle of length $m^n$. ∎

## Verification

Each case reduces to whether a string is primitive or a power of a shorter word. Primitive strings correspond exactly to outputs of Algorithm F, and each such output generates a full rotation class, ensuring coverage of all cyclic shifts. The exceptional $9^j$ case corresponds to maximal digit carry behavior in Algorithm F, which is the only situation where a rotation boundary can fall between generated blocks. Periodic strings $(\alpha \beta)^d$ are accounted for by replication of primitive cycles. The lexicographic minimality follows from the greedy minimal-choice property of Algorithm F, which prevents earlier divergence without increasing the prefix.

## Notes

The argument is a cycle concatenation interpretation of the Lyndon factorization underlying Algorithm F. The structure of primes as Lyndon words ensures that the concatenation of all Lyndon representatives ordered lexicographically produces the de Bruijn sequence, with the cyclic boundary correction accounting for wraparound coverage.
