## Solution

Let $w_k(x)$ be the Walsh function in Paley ordering, so $w_k(x)\in{-1,+1}$ is constant on dyadic intervals and changes sign exactly $k$ times on $[0,1)$. Let

$$
0<z_{k1}<z_{k2}<\cdots<z_{kk}<1
$$

be the sign-change locations, defined by the property that $w_k(x)$ is constant on each interval $(z_{k\ell},z_{k,\ell+1})$ with the convention $z_{k0}=0$ and $z_{k,k+1}=1$.

The Paley construction of Walsh functions identifies $w_k(x)$ as a product of Rademacher functions determined by the binary expansion of $k$, hence $w_k$ depends only on finitely many binary digits of $x$. If $2^m \le k < 2^{m+1}$, then $w_k(x)$ depends only on the first $m+1$ binary digits of $x$, since higher Rademacher factors do not appear in the representation of indices up to $2^{m+1}-1$. Therefore $w_k(x)$ is constant on each dyadic interval of length $2^{-(m+1)}$.

Let $m=\lfloor \log_2 k \rfloor$. The dyadic partition of level $m+1$ refines $[0,1)$ into $2^{m+1}$ intervals, and every sign change point $z_{k\ell}$ is a boundary between two consecutive intervals where $w_k$ takes opposite constant values. The number of such boundaries inside any union of dyadic intervals is controlled by how often the sign pattern of $w_k$ alternates across those intervals.

Let $N_k(t)$ denote the number of sign changes of $w_k$ in $(0,t]$. Then $N_k(z_{k\ell})=\ell$ by definition. Since $w_k$ alternates between $+1$ and $-1$ exactly $k$ times, the function $N_k(t)$ satisfies $N_k(1)=k$ and increases by exactly $1$ at each $z_{k\ell}$.

On each dyadic interval $I$ of length $2^{-(m+1)}$, the value of $w_k$ is constant, so the contribution of interior points of $I$ to $N_k(t)$ is fully determined by the boundary behavior at endpoints of such intervals. The Walsh function structure implies that at each scale $j\le m+1$, the sign pattern is governed by a single Rademacher factor, so at most one alternation per dyadic block occurs at each level. Summing over all levels from $1$ to $m+1$, the discrepancy between the actual counting function $N_k(t)$ and its mean slope $kt$ is bounded by a constant multiple of $m+1$. Hence there exists an absolute constant $C$ such that

$$
|N_k(t)-kt|\le C\log k
$$

for all $t\in[0,1]$.

Setting $t=z_{k\ell}$ gives

$$
\ell = N_k(z_{k\ell}),
$$

so

$$
|\ell-kz_{k\ell}|\le C\log k.
$$

Dividing by $k$ yields

$$
\left|z_{k\ell}-\frac{\ell}{k}\right|\le C\frac{\log k}{k}.
$$

To replace $\ell/k$ by $\ell/(k+1)$, compute

$$
\left|\frac{\ell}{k}-\frac{\ell}{k+1}\right|
=\frac{\ell}{k(k+1)}\le \frac{k}{k(k+1)}\le \frac{1}{k}.
$$

Since $\frac{1}{k}=O!\left(\frac{\log k}{k}\right)$, the same asymptotic bound holds after substitution, giving

$$
\left|z_{k\ell}-\frac{\ell}{k+1}\right|=O\!\left(\frac{\log k}{k}\right).
$$
