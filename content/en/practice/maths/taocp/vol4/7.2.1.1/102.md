**Exercise 102.** [*HM28*] [HM28] Deduce the number of m-ary primes of length n from the unique factor-
ization theorem in the previous exercise.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m15s

## Setup

Let an $m$-ary string of length $n$ be a word $\alpha = a_1 a_2 \cdots a_n$ over an alphabet of size $m$. Let $W_n$ denote the set of all such strings, so $|W_n| = m^n$.

A string $\lambda$ is called a prime (in the sense of Exercise 7.2.1.1.101) when it is nonempty and admits no nontrivial factorization $\lambda = \mu \nu$ with $\mu \ge \nu$ and both $\mu, \nu$ nonempty in lexicographic order. The previous exercise establishes that every string $\alpha$ admits a unique factorization

$$
\alpha = \lambda_1 \lambda_2 \cdots \lambda_t,
\qquad \lambda_1 \ge \lambda_2 \ge \cdots \ge \lambda_t,
$$

where each $\lambda_i$ is prime.

Let $a_n$ denote the number of primes of length $n$ over the $m$-ary alphabet. The task is to determine $a_n$.

## Solution

Every word in $W_n$ is a concatenation of a weakly nonincreasing sequence of primes whose total lengths sum to $n$. Conversely, every such sequence produces a unique word by concatenation, by the uniqueness part of the factorization theorem.

Introduce the ordinary generating function

$$
W(x) = \sum_{n \ge 0} |W_n| x^n = \sum_{n \ge 0} m^n x^n = \frac{1}{1 - mx}.
$$

Let $A(x) = \sum_{n \ge 1} a_n x^n$. Each word corresponds uniquely to a multiset of primes in which primes of length $k$ contribute weight $x^k$ and multiplicity is unrestricted, while the ordering condition $\lambda_1 \ge \lambda_2 \ge \cdots$ ensures that each multiset determines exactly one factorization.

Thus words are in bijection with weakly ordered multisets of prime blocks, so the generating function factors as

$$
W(x) = \prod_{n \ge 1} (1 - x^n)^{-a_n}.
$$

Taking logarithms gives

$$
-\log(1 - mx) = \sum_{n \ge 1} a_n \biggl( \sum_{k \ge 1} \frac{x^{nk}}{k} \biggr).
$$

The left-hand side expands as

$$
-\log(1 - mx) = \sum_{r \ge 1} \frac{m^r x^r}{r}.
$$

On the right-hand side, collecting coefficients of $x^r$ yields

$$
\sum_{n \mid r} \frac{a_n}{r/n} = \frac{1}{r} \sum_{n \mid r} n a_n.
$$

Equating coefficients of $x^r$ gives

$$
\frac{m^r}{r} = \frac{1}{r} \sum_{n \mid r} n a_n,
$$

hence

$$
m^r = \sum_{n \mid r} n a_n.
$$

Apply Möbius inversion to the divisor relation. Writing $r=n$,

$$
n a_n = \sum_{d \mid n} \mu(d)\, m^{n/d}.
$$

Dividing by $n$ yields the number of $m$-ary primes of length $n$:

$$
a_n = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{d \mid n} \mu(d)\, m^{n/d}.
$$

Thus the number of $m$-ary primes of length $n$ is

$$
\boxed{a_n = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{d \mid n} \mu(d)\, m^{n/d}}.
$$

This completes the derivation. ∎

## Verification

The relation $m^r = \sum_{n \mid r} n a_n$ is consistent with the factorization theorem because each word of length $r$ is uniquely determined by its ordered prime factorization, and each prime of length $n$ contributes $n$ positions to the total length.

Möbius inversion applies because the divisor sum depends only on divisors of $n$ and the arithmetic function $n \mapsto n a_n$ is determined pointwise by $m^n$. Substituting the derived formula back into the divisor sum reproduces $m^n$ by standard Möbius inversion identity $\sum_{d \mid n} \mu(d) = [n=1]$ after reindexing.

## Notes

The obtained quantity $a_n$ is the classical count of Lyndon words of length $n$ over an $m$-letter alphabet, and the same formula arises from necklace counting via the Witt transform.
