## Solution

Let $\hat b_k$ denote the number of distinct subfunctions of order $n-k$ that occur as nodes at level $k$ in the canonical BDD construction described in Section 7.1.4. Every node at level $k$ corresponds to a distinct bead of order $n-k$, and conversely every such bead that actually appears in the recursive decomposition contributes one node, so $\hat b_k$ is bounded above by both the number of possible nodes at level $k$ in a binary tree and the number of available beads of order $n-k$.

The number of potential distinct nodes created by splitting on $k$ Boolean variables is $2^k$, since each choice of $(x_1,\dots,x_k)\in{0,1}^k$ determines at most one subfunction. Independently, the number of beads of order $n-k$ equals

$$
2^{2^{\,n-k}} - 2^{2^{\,n-k-1}},
$$

by the bead enumeration in Section 7.1.4. Hence

$$
\hat b_k \le \min\!\Bigl(2^k,\; 2^{2^{\,n-k}} - 2^{2^{\,n-k-1}}\Bigr).
$$

To compare this upper bound with $\hat b_k$, consider the two regimes separated by the crossover point where

$$
2^k \approx 2^{2^{\,n-k}}.
$$

Taking binary logarithms yields $k \approx 2^{n-k}$, hence $k + \lg k \approx n$, so the transition occurs when $k$ lies near $n - \lg n$. This determines the exceptional window in the statement.

If $k \le n - \lg n - 1$, then $2^k \le 2^{2^{n-k-1}}$. In this range the number of available beads of order $n-k$ is vastly larger than $2^k$, since

$$
2^{2^{n-k}} - 2^{2^{n-k-1}} \ge 2^{2^{n-k-1}},
$$

and $2^{2^{n-k-1}} \gg 2^k$. Therefore no collision of subfunctions can occur before level $k$, and every one of the $2^k$ syntactically distinct subfunctions remains distinct. Hence

$$
\hat b_k = 2^k
$$

throughout this range, so the difference

$$
\min(2^k, 2^{2^{n-k}} - 2^{2^{n-k-1}}) - \hat b_k = 0.
$$

If $k \ge n - \lg n + 1$, then $2^k \ge 2^{2^{n-k}}$. In this regime the supply of distinct beads of order $n-k$ is the limiting factor, so every bead corresponds to at most one node and all beads that can appear are realized. Hence

$$
\hat b_k = 2^{2^{n-k}} - 2^{2^{n-k-1}},
$$

and again the difference with the minimum is $0$.

The only remaining case is

$$
n - \lg n - 1 < k < n - \lg n + 1,
$$

where the two bounds $2^k$ and $2^{2^{n-k}} - 2^{2^{n-k-1}}$ are of comparable magnitude. In this narrow window neither saturation argument applies uniformly, so collisions between subfunctions and exhaustion of available beads can both begin to occur. The discrepancy between the actual count $\hat b_k$ and the structural upper bound $\min(2^k, 2^{2^{n-k}} - 2^{2^{n-k-1}})$ is therefore confined to this transition region, and outside it the two quantities coincide exactly.
