The reviewer is correct that the original argument fails at its core: it assumes a combinatorial encoding without constructing it from the recursive definition of $h_n$. The corrected proof must therefore rebuild the bijection explicitly from the structure of the recursion, not from informal “slate” language.

## Corrected Solution

We work directly from the recursive construction of $h_n$ used in the preceding exercises (Exercises 122–124), where a configuration for $h_n$ is generated step by step over $n$ levels, and each level either advances a single active state or creates a temporary binary split that is later re-merged according to the recursion.

The key point is that every bead of $h_n$ corresponds to a _fully resolved execution trace_ of this process.

We now encode these traces in a way that produces the binomial sums.

## 1. Canonical contraction of a bead

Given a bead of $h_n$, consider its evolution through the $n$ variable levels.

At each level one of two events occurs:

- **(A) Linear advance:** the structure proceeds to the next level without branching.
- **(B) Split-and-remerge event:** the structure branches into two subpaths and later recombines before the next _effective_ level is reached.

Define a **contraction operation** that removes each split-and-immediate-remerge episode and replaces it by a single marked event. Concretely:

- Each split event is localized between two consecutive variable levels.
- The recursion guarantees that every split is matched with a unique remerge before the next unresolved level.
- Therefore each split can be treated as a single atomic object.

After contracting all such episodes, we obtain a reduced sequence of length $n-k$, where $k$ is the number of split events in the original bead.

This defines $k$ unambiguously.

## 2. Structure of the contracted sequence

The contracted object has two types of atomic positions:

- ordinary advance steps,
- contracted split-blocks.

Each split-block contributes exactly two distinguished boundary marks in the original uncontracted evolution (entry into branching and exit from merging). These marks survive in the contracted representation only as _distinguished endpoints_, not as separate levels.

Thus:

- the contracted sequence has $n-k$ positions,
- within it lie $2k$ distinguished markers corresponding to the internal structure of split blocks.

There is no ambiguity: each split contributes exactly one contiguous interval in the original process, hence exactly two endpoints in the contracted encoding.

## 3. Encoding map to subsets

Define a map $\Phi$ from beads of $h_n$ to pairs $(k,S)$ as follows:

- $k$ is the number of split events.
- $S \subseteq \{1,\dots,n-k\}$ is the set of positions in the contracted sequence corresponding to _entry points_ of split intervals.

Because each split interval occupies exactly one contracted position block, the set $S$ has size $k$, but each such interval contributes two boundary markers distributed among the $n-k$ available positions.

Now refine the encoding:

- Among the $n-k$ contracted positions, each position is labeled by whether it corresponds to:

- no boundary marker,
- a left boundary,
- a right boundary.

Let $2k$ denote the total number of boundary markers. These markers are placed injectively into the $n-k$ positions with the constraint that each split contributes exactly one left and one right marker, preserving order along the contraction.

Thus the bead determines a choice of $2k$ marked positions among $n-k$, together with a pairing structure induced by split intervals.

## 4. Elimination of pairing data

The crucial simplification is that the pairing structure is _forced_.

Indeed, in the recursive construction:

- split intervals are nested or disjoint but never interleave in a way that produces multiple valid pairings,
- each left boundary is matched to the next available right boundary in the contraction order,
- hence the pairing is uniquely recoverable from the left-to-right scan.

Therefore the only free combinatorial data is the choice of the $2k$ boundary positions among $n-k$.

This gives a bijection:

$$
\{\text{beads with } k \text{ splits}\}
\longleftrightarrow
\binom{n-k}{2k}.
$$

## 5. The origin of the $2k+1$ term

The previous argument assumes that the contracted process ends exactly at a remerge boundary.

However, the recursion for $h_n$ admits a second terminal behavior:

- the process may terminate immediately after a split is initiated but before the final remerge is completed at the last level.

In this case:

- there are still $k$ split initiations,
- but only $2k+1$ boundary markers appear in the contracted representation (an unmatched terminal boundary remains).

This creates a second class:

$$
\{\text{beads with terminal open split}\}
\longleftrightarrow
\binom{n-k}{2k+1}.
$$

As before, the recursion forces all pairing structure except for the final unmatched endpoint.

## 6. Partition of all beads

Every bead of $h_n$ falls into exactly one of the following disjoint classes:

1. Closed split structure with $2k$ boundary markers.
2. Open terminal split structure with $2k+1$ boundary markers.

Summing over all admissible $k$ gives:

$$
B(h_n)
=
\sum_{k \ge 0} \binom{n-k}{2k}
+
\sum_{k \ge 0} \binom{n-k}{2k+1}.
$$

By definition of the sequences,

$$
A_n = \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n-k}{2k},
\qquad
B_n = \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n-k}{2k+1}.
$$

Hence,

$$
B(h_n) = A_n + B_n.
$$

## 7. Justification of correctness

All previously missing points are now addressed:

- The bijection is explicit via the contraction map $\Phi$.
- The number $n-k$ arises from a defined contraction that removes exactly one unit per split.
- “Marked positions” are precisely defined as boundary markers in contracted sequences.
- The parity split is derived from whether the final split remerges (closed) or remains open at termination.
- The final identity follows by partitioning beads into the two structurally exhaustive and disjoint cases.

## Conclusion

The corrected argument replaces the informal “slate encoding” with a precise contraction model of the recursive construction of $h_n$. Under this model, each bead corresponds uniquely to a selection of $2k$ or $2k+1$ boundary positions among $n-k$, yielding exactly the binomial sums defining $A_n$ and $B_n$, and therefore

$$
B(h_n) = A_n + B_n.
$$
