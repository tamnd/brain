[Section 7.1.4: Binary Decision Diagrams](../)

**Exercise 64.** [*M21*] [M21] We can compute the median ⟨f1f2f3⟩of three Boolean functions by forming
f4 = f1 ∨f2,
f5 = f1 ∧f2,
f6 = f3 ∧f4,
f7 = f5 ∨f6.
Then B(f4) = O(B(f1)B(f2)), B(f5) = O(B(f1)B(f2)), B(f6) = O(B(f3)B(f4)) =
O(B(f1)B(f2)B(f3)); therefore B(f7) = O(B(f5)B(f6)) = O(B(f1)2B(f2)2B(f3)).
Prove, however, that B(f7) is actually only O(B(f1)B(f2)B(f3)), and the running
time to compute it from f5 and f6 is also O(B(f1)B(f2)B(f3)).

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m16s

## Solution

Let $f_4 = f_1 \vee f_2$, $f_5 = f_1 \wedge f_2$, $f_6 = f_3 \wedge f_4$, and $f_7 = f_5 \vee f_6$.

For any two Boolean functions $p,q$, the standard APPLY construction on ordered reduced BDDs builds the BDD for $p \circ q$ (with $\circ \in {\wedge,\vee}$) by processing pairs of nodes $(\alpha,\beta)$ where $\alpha$ is a node of $B(p)$ and $\beta$ is a node of $B(q)$. Each such pair determines at most one node in the result, because the recursive decomposition on the top variable yields uniquely determined LO and HI successors, and reduction merges identical triples $(V,\mathrm{LO},\mathrm{HI})$. Hence

$$
B(p \circ q) \le B(p)\,B(q).
$$

Applying this to $f_4$ and $f_5$ gives

$$
B(f_4) = O(B(f_1)B(f_2)), \qquad B(f_5) = O(B(f_1)B(f_2)).
$$

Next,

$$
B(f_6) = B(f_3 \wedge f_4) \le B(f_3)\,B(f_4) = O(B(f_1)B(f_2)B(f_3)).
$$

The naive estimate for $f_7 = f_5 \vee f_6$ yields

$$
B(f_7) \le B(f_5)\,B(f_6) = O(B(f_1)^2 B(f_2)^2 B(f_3)).
$$

This bound does not reflect the functional structure of $f_7$, which simplifies algebraically using distributivity:

$$
(f_1 \wedge f_2) \vee (f_3 \wedge (f_1 \vee f_2))
= (f_1 \wedge f_2) \vee (f_1 \wedge f_3) \vee (f_2 \wedge f_3).
$$

Thus $f_7$ is the majority function of $f_1,f_2,f_3$, written as a disjunction of three pairwise conjunctions.

Construct a BDD for $f_7$ by applying a three-way synchronous APPLY that processes triples of nodes $(\alpha,\beta,\gamma)$ from the BDDs of $f_1,f_2,f_3$. For each triple, the Shannon decomposition on the current variable index produces LO and HI children determined solely by the corresponding children of $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$, so the construction is well-defined. Reduction identifies identical triples $(V,\mathrm{LO},\mathrm{HI})$, so each reachable triple of nodes contributes at most one node in the resulting BDD.

The number of reachable triples is bounded by

$$
B(f_1)\,B(f_2)\,B(f_3),
$$

since each node of the constructed diagram corresponds to at most one triple from the Cartesian product of node sets of the three input BDDs. Hence

$$
B(f_7) = O(B(f_1)B(f_2)B(f_3)).
$$

The same triple-based APPLY evaluation computes all nodes of $f_7$ in time proportional to the number of generated triples, since each triple is processed once and each processing step performs only constant-time pointer manipulations and comparisons of variable indices. Therefore the running time is also

$$
O(B(f_1)B(f_2)B(f_3)).
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
