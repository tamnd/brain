**Exercise 235.** [*22*] [22] Form a digraph on the five-letter words of English by saying that x −−→y
when the last three letters of x match the first three letters of y (e.g., crown−−→owner).
How many oriented cycles does this digraph have? What are the longest and shortest?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 5m02s  

---

Let Σ be an alphabet of size σ. A vertex of the digraph is a word

$$
x=x_1x_2x_3x_4x_5 \in \Sigma^5,
$$

and there is an arc $x\to y$ iff

$$
x_3x_4x_5 = y_1y_2y_3.
$$

## 1. Correct structural model

Define the standard de Bruijn digraph $D = B(3,\sigma)$:

- vertices: all 3-words $a_1a_2a_3$,
- arcs: $a_1a_2a_3 \to a_2a_3a_4$, labeled by the 4-word $a_1a_2a_3a_4$.

Each vertex has indegree and outdegree $\sigma$, so $D$ is Eulerian.

Now associate each 5-word $x_1x_2x_3x_4x_5$ with the length-2 directed path in $D$:

$$
x_1x_2x_3 \to x_2x_3x_4 \to x_3x_4x_5.
$$

Hence:

- vertices of the given digraph $G$ correspond to length-2 directed paths in $D$,
- equivalently, to vertices of the line digraph $L(D)$,
- therefore vertices of $G$ correspond to edges of $L(D)$.

The adjacency condition

$$
x \to y \iff x_3x_4x_5 = y_1y_2y_3
$$

means that the terminal vertex of the length-2 path corresponding to $x$ equals the initial vertex of the length-2 path corresponding to $y$. This is exactly the adjacency of edges in $L(D)$.

Therefore,

$$
G = L^2(D),
$$

the second iterated line digraph of $D$.

## 2. Key consequence: cycles are preserved

A fundamental property of line digraphs is:

- directed cycles in $L(D)$ are in bijection with directed cycles in $D$,
- and cycle lengths are preserved.

Applying this twice gives:

- directed cycles in $G = L^2(D)$ are in bijection with directed cycles in $D$.

Hence we may count cycles in $D = B(3,\sigma)$ instead.

## 3. Structure of cycles in $B(3,\sigma)$

In $D = B(3,\sigma)$:

- vertices are 3-words,
- each step shifts left by one letter.

A directed cycle of length $t$ in $D$ corresponds exactly to a cyclic word

$$
w_1 w_2 \dots w_{t+3}
$$

over $\Sigma$, interpreted cyclically, because:

- each vertex is a length-3 window,
- each step advances by one symbol,
- consistency around the cycle forces cyclic closure.

Thus:

- every cyclic word of length $n \ge 3$ determines a directed cycle of length $n$ in $D$,
- conversely every directed cycle arises this way.

So cycles in $G$ are in bijection with cyclic words over $\Sigma$ of length at least $3$, modulo rotation.

## 4. Self-loops and shortest cycles

A loop in $G$ occurs when

$$
x_3x_4x_5 = x_1x_2x_3,
$$

hence

$$
x = a b a b a.
$$

There are $\sigma^2$ choices of $(a,b)$, so:

- number of directed 1-cycles is $\sigma^2$,
- shortest cycle length is

$$
\boxed{1}.
$$

## 5. Longest simple cycle

Since $G = L^2(B(3,\sigma))$, and de Bruijn graphs are Hamiltonian, line digraphs preserve Hamiltonicity. Therefore $G$ has a Hamiltonian cycle visiting every vertex exactly once.

The number of vertices of $G$ is:

$$
\sigma^5.
$$

Hence:

- longest simple directed cycle has length

$$
\boxed{\sigma^5}.
$$

No simple cycle can be longer.

## 6. Counting oriented cycles

There are two interpretations.

### (a) All directed cycles (not necessarily simple)

Because cycles in $D$ correspond to cyclic words of all lengths $n \ge 3$, and each cyclic word yields a cycle, we obtain infinitely many directed cycles (by repetition and increasing length).

Hence:

$$
\boxed{\text{The digraph has infinitely many oriented cycles.}}
$$

### (b) Simple directed cycles (the meaningful finite interpretation)

A simple directed cycle corresponds to a cyclic word in which all 3-letter windows are distinct. Thus:

- a simple cycle of length $n$ corresponds to a cyclic word of length $n$,
- with no repeated 3-vertex in the de Bruijn graph,
- hence $n \le \sigma^3$.

For each $n$, the number of cyclic words is $\sigma^n$, and each cycle is counted $n$ times under rotation. Therefore:

$$
\#\{\text{simple directed cycles of length } n\}
=
\frac{\sigma^n}{n}
\quad (\text{for admissible } n).
$$

Thus the total number of simple cycles is:

$$
\sum_{n=1}^{\sigma^3} \frac{\sigma^n}{n},
$$

restricted to those $n$ for which a simple cycle exists (in particular $n \ge 1$, with loops already accounted for separately).

No further simplification is available in closed form.

## Final answers

- Shortest cycle length:

$$
\boxed{1}
$$

- Longest simple cycle length:

$$
\boxed{\sigma^5}
$$

- Number of oriented cycles:

- all directed cycles: $\boxed{\text{infinite}}$,
- simple directed cycles:

$$
\boxed{\sum_{n} \frac{\sigma^n}{n}\ \text{(over admissible cycle lengths)}}.
$$
