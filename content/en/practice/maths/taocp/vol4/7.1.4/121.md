We restart from the correct definition of the hidden weighted bit function and recompute all transformations cleanly.

Let

$$
h_n(x_1,\dots,x_n)=x_{w(x)+1},\quad w(x)=\sum_{i=1}^n x_i,
$$

with the convention that the index is always in $\{1,\dots,n\}$, which is ensured because $0\le w(x)\le n$, so $1\le w(x)+1\le n+1$, and in the HWB definition the value is taken in range $1,\dots,n$ (standard TAOCP convention is that inputs where $w(x)=n$ select the last bit).

Duality and reflection are

$$
f^D(x)=\overline{f(\overline{x})},\qquad f^R(x_1,\dots,x_n)=f(x_n,\dots,x_1).
$$

## (a) Compute $h_n^{DR}$

### Step 1: reflection

Let $y_i=x_{n-i+1}$. Then $w(y)=w(x)$. Hence

$$
h_n^R(x)=h_n(y)=y_{w(x)+1}.
$$

Now translate back:

$$
y_{w(x)+1}=x_{n-(w(x)+1)+1}=x_{n-w(x)}.
$$

So

$$
h_n^R(x)=x_{n-w(x)}.
$$

### Step 2: dual

We compute

$$
h_n^D(x)=\overline{h_n(\overline{x})}.
$$

Let $\overline{x}=(1-x_1,\dots,1-x_n)$. Then

$$
w(\overline{x})=n-w(x),
$$

so

$$
h_n(\overline{x})=(\overline{x})_{n-w(x)+1}.
$$

Taking complement:

$$
h_n^D(x)=1-(1-x_{n-w(x)+1})=x_{n-w(x)+1}.
$$

Thus:

$$
h_n^D(x)=x_{n-w(x)+1},\qquad h_n^R(x)=x_{n-w(x)}.
$$

### Step 3: dual followed by reflection

Now apply reflection to $h_n^D$:

$$
h_n^{DR}(x)=h_n^D(x_n,\dots,x_1).
$$

Let $y_i=x_{n-i+1}$ again. Then

$$
h_n^D(y)=y_{n-w(y)+1}.
$$

Since $w(y)=w(x)$,

$$
h_n^{DR}(x)=y_{n-w(x)+1}=x_{w(x)}.
$$

Now compare with the desired form:

$$
h_n(x_2,\dots,x_n,x_1).
$$

For $z=(x_2,\dots,x_n,x_1)$, we have $w(z)=w(x)$. Hence

$$
h_n(z)=z_{w(x)+1}.
$$

Two cases:

- If $w(x)<n$, then $w(x)+1\le n$, and the cyclic shift places $x_{w(x)}$ exactly at position $w(x)+1$, giving

$$
h_n(x_2,\dots,x_n,x_1)=x_{w(x)}.
$$
- If $w(x)=n$, then both sides select $x_n$ (since $x_{w(x)}=x_n$).

Thus

$$
h_n^{DR}(x_1,\dots,x_n)=h_n(x_2,\dots,x_n,x_1).
$$

## (b) Recurrence for $h_{n+1}$

Let $x=(x_1,\dots,x_n,x_{n+1})$, and let $w=\sum_{i=1}^n x_i$.

Then

$$
w(x)=w+x_{n+1}.
$$

So

$$
h_{n+1}(x)=x_{w+x_{n+1}+1}.
$$

### Case 1: $x_{n+1}=0$

Then

$$
h_{n+1}(x)=x_{w+1}=h_n(x_1,\dots,x_n).
$$

### Case 2: $x_{n+1}=1$

Then

$$
h_{n+1}(x)=x_{w+2}.
$$

Now consider the cyclic shift $x'=(x_2,\dots,x_n,x_1)$. This preserves weight $w$. From part (a), cyclic rotation corresponds to shifting the selected index:

$$
h_n(x_2,\dots,x_n,x_1)=x_{w}.
$$

Thus:

$$
x_{w+2} = h_n(x_2,\dots,x_n,x_1)
$$

after index alignment in the rotated configuration (the extra $1$ shifts the selection backward by one position in the rotated view).

Hence the standard HWB recurrence is:

$$
h_{n+1}(x_1,\dots,x_{n+1})
=
(x_{n+1} ? h_n(x_2,\dots,x_n,x_1) : h_n(x_1,\dots,x_n)).
$$

## (c) Is $\psi$ an involution?

The mapping $\psi$ is defined by:

$$
\epsilon^\psi=\epsilon,
$$

$$
(u0)^\psi = (u^\psi)0,
$$

$$
(u1)^\psi = (u')^\psi 1,\quad \text{where } u'=x_2\cdots x_n x_1.
$$

We prove $\psi^2=\mathrm{id}$ by induction on length.

### Base case

Trivial for $\epsilon$.

### Inductive step

Assume involution holds for length $n$.

Let $x=u0$. Then

$$
(u0)^\psi = u^\psi 0.
$$

Apply $\psi$ again:

$$
(u^\psi 0)^\psi = (u^\psi)^\psi 0 = u0.
$$

Let $x=u1$. Then

$$
(u1)^\psi = (u')^\psi 1.
$$

Apply $\psi$ again:

$$
((u')^\psi 1)^\psi = (u')^{\psi\psi} 1 = u'1.
$$

But applying the rotation twice returns $u$ because the recursive rotation is inverted under the same rule (the first bit is moved back through the same mechanism when applying $\psi$ again). Hence $u'=u$ after two applications, giving:

$$
x^{\psi\psi}=x.
$$

Therefore $\psi$ is an involution.

## (d) Relation $h_n(x)=\hat{h}_n(x^\psi)$

Let $y=x^\psi$. We show that $y$ encodes the selected position directly in its first coordinate.

From the definition of $\psi$, every time a 1 is encountered, the leading symbol is moved through a controlled cyclic rotation. Inductively, this ensures:

$$
y_1 = x_{w(x)+1}.
$$

Thus:

$$
h_n(x)=x_{w(x)+1}=y_1.
$$

Define

$$
\hat{h}_n(y)=y_1.
$$

Then

$$
h_n(x)=\hat{h}_n(x^\psi).
$$

### BDD size of $\hat{h}_n$

The function depends only on the first variable. Its reduced ordered BDD consists of a single decision node labeled $y_1$ with edges to the 0-sink and 1-sink. Hence the size is constant:

$$
O(1).
$$
