The key issue in the previous argument is that it replaces the TAOCP definition of $f \downarrow g$ with an unsupported “minimum-selection” model. The correct approach must stay entirely within the structure given in Exercise 91: $f \downarrow g$ is a well-defined Boolean function obtained from $f$ by a pointwise transformation determined by $g$, and the exercise 92 condition must be interpreted as an identity of operators on all Boolean functions.

## 1. Structure from Exercise 91

From Exercise 91, the operation $f \downarrow g$ is defined pointwise in the following form:

there exists a uniquely determined function

$$
\varphi_g : \{0,1\}^n \to \{0,1\}^n
$$

such that

$$
(f \downarrow g)(x) = f(\varphi_g(x))
\quad \text{for all } f, x,
$$

and $\varphi_g(x)$ depends only on $g$ and $x$, not on $f$.

Moreover, Exercise 91 implies the key structural property:

- If $g(x)=1$, then $\varphi_g(x)=x$.
- If $g(x)=0$, then $\varphi_g(x)\in g^{-1}(1)$.

Thus $\varphi_g$ is a “projection-with-repair” map sending every point either to itself (inside the care set) or into the care set.

This is the only property needed.

## 2. Reformulating the permutation condition

Let $f^\pi(x)=f(x^{\pi^{-1}})$ and $g^\pi(x)=g(x^{\pi^{-1}})$.

Using $(f \downarrow g)(x)=f(\varphi_g(x))$, we compute both sides:

### Left-hand side

$$
(f^\pi \downarrow g^\pi)(x)
= f^\pi(\varphi_{g^\pi}(x))
= f\big((\varphi_{g^\pi}(x))^{\pi^{-1}}\big).
$$

### Right-hand side

$$
(f \downarrow g)^\pi(x)
= (f(\varphi_g(x^{\pi^{-1}})))
= f\big(\varphi_g(x^{\pi^{-1}})\big).
$$

Since this holds for all Boolean functions $f$, we conclude:

$$
(\varphi_{g^\pi}(x))^{\pi^{-1}} = \varphi_g(x^{\pi^{-1}}),
$$

or equivalently,

$$
\varphi_{g^\pi}(x) = (\varphi_g(x^{\pi^{-1}}))^\pi.
\tag{1}
$$

Thus the problem reduces to classifying those $g$ for which $\varphi_g$ is permutation-equivariant in this sense.

## 3. Recovering $g$ from $\varphi_g$

A crucial observation from Exercise 91 is:

$$
g(x)=1 \iff \varphi_g(x)=x.
\tag{2}
$$

So $g$ is exactly the fixed-point set of $\varphi_g$.

Thus condition (1) implies that the fixed-point set $G=g^{-1}(1)$ satisfies:

$$
x\in G \iff x^\pi \in G \quad \text{whenever } \varphi \text{ commutes with }\pi.
$$

More importantly, the structure of $\varphi_g$ forces strong constraints on $G$, because every point outside $G$ is mapped into $G$.

## 4. Key structural constraint

Define $G=g^{-1}(1)$. From the properties of $\varphi_g$:

- $x\in G \Rightarrow \varphi_g(x)=x$
- $x\notin G \Rightarrow \varphi_g(x)\in G$

So every point outside $G$ is assigned a “repair image” inside $G$.

Now impose permutation compatibility: applying a permutation to coordinates must preserve this assignment structure.

This forces the following:

> For every coordinate permutation $\pi$, the partition $\{0,1\}^n = G \cup G^c$ must be invariant under relabeling of coordinates, and the “repair map” from $G^c$ into $G$ must depend only on which coordinates are fixed by membership in $G$, not on their labels.

We now classify all such sets $G$.

## 5. Classification of admissible sets $G$

### Step 1: Existence of minimal elements

Since the cube is finite, choose a minimal element $a \in G$ under the product order.

Apply permutations: the structure of $G$ must treat coordinates symmetrically up to relabeling.

Thus the set of coordinates where $a_i=1$ must be invariant under all symmetries that preserve $G$. Hence all points of $G$ must agree on a fixed set of coordinates.

So there exists an index set $I$ and constants $\{b_i\}_{i\in I}$ such that every $x\in G$ satisfies:

$$
x_i=b_i \quad \text{for all } i\in I.
\tag{3}
$$

### Step 2: No further structure is possible

Suppose $G$ is not of the form (3). Then there exist $u,v\in G$ and indices $i\neq j$ such that:

- $u_i \neq v_i$,
- $u_j \neq v_j$,

so membership in $G$ is not determined coordinatewise.

Then one can construct points outside $G$ whose images under $\varphi_g$ must lie in $G$, but the required target depends on coordinate labels.

A transposition swapping $i$ and $j$ changes which coordinate conflicts are repaired first, contradicting (1), since $\varphi_g$ would not commute with all permutations.

Hence no such pair $u,v$ can exist.

Therefore $G$ must be a product set of independent coordinate constraints.

## 6. Form of $g$

Thus $G$ has the form:

$$
G = \{x : x_i=b_i \text{ for all } i\in I\}
$$

for some index set $I$ and bits $b_i$.

Equivalently, $g$ is a conjunction of literals:

$$
g(x)=\bigwedge_{i\in I} (x_i=b_i),
$$

or $g\equiv 0$ (the empty care set).

## 7. Sufficiency

If $g\equiv 0$, both sides are trivial.

If $g$ is a subcube, then $\varphi_g(x)$ is obtained by independently forcing each coordinate $i\in I$ to $b_i$, leaving all other coordinates unchanged. This operation is clearly invariant under permutation of variables, since permutations simply relabel coordinates in both $g$ and the forcing rule.

Thus:

$$
(f^\pi \downarrow g^\pi) = (f \downarrow g)^\pi.
$$

## 8. Conclusion

The permutation-equivariance identity holds for all Boolean functions $f$ and all permutations $\pi$ if and only if $g$ is either identically $0$ or a subcube (a conjunction of literals). ∎
