# (a)

Given

$$
f=\{\varnothing,\{1,2\},\{1,3\}\}, \qquad g=\{\{1,2\},\{3\}\}.
$$

## Step 1: Compute $f \sqcup g$

We take all unions $\alpha \cup \beta$:

- $\varnothing \cup \{1,2\} = \{1,2\}$
- $\varnothing \cup \{3\} = \{3\}$
- $\{1,2\} \cup \{1,2\} = \{1,2\}$
- $\{1,2\} \cup \{3\} = \{1,2,3\}$
- $\{1,3\} \cup \{1,2\} = \{1,2,3\}$
- $\{1,3\} \cup \{3\} = \{1,3\}$

Hence

$$
f \sqcup g = \{\{1,2\},\{3\},\{1,2,3\},\{1,3\}\}.
$$

## Step 2: Compute $f \sqcap g$

Pairwise intersections:

- $\varnothing\cap \{1,2\}=\varnothing$, $\varnothing\cap\{3\}=\varnothing$
- $\{1,2\}\cap\{1,2\}=\{1,2\}$, $\{1,2\}\cap\{3\}=\varnothing$
- $\{1,3\}\cap\{1,2\}=\{1\}$, $\{1,3\}\cap\{3\}=\{3\}$

So

$$
f \sqcap g=\{\varnothing,\{1,2\},\{1\},\{3\}\}.
$$

## Step 3: Compute $f \setminus e_1$

Since $e_1=\{\{1\}\}$ and $\{1\}\notin f$,

$$
f \setminus e_1 = f.
$$

## Step 4: Final expression

$$
(f \sqcap g)\setminus (f \setminus e_1)
= \{\varnothing,\{1,2\},\{1\},\{3\}\}\setminus \{\varnothing,\{1,2\},\{1,3\}\}
= \{\{1\},\{3\}\}.
$$

### Answer:

$$
f \sqcup g = \{\{1,2\},\{3\},\{1,2,3\},\{1,3\}\}, \quad
(f \sqcap g)\setminus (f \setminus e_1)=\{\{1\},\{3\}\}.
$$

# (c) Algebraic identities

## (i)

$$
f \sqcup (g\cup h) = (f\sqcup g)\cup(f\sqcup h)
$$

True, by splitting the witness in $g\cup h$.

## (ii)

$$
f \sqcap (g\cup h) = (f\sqcap g)\cup(f\sqcap h)
$$

True, by distributivity of intersection over union at the set level.

## (iii) (corrected)

$$
f \sqcup (g \sqcap h) = (f \sqcup g)\sqcap(f \sqcup h)
$$

### Proof of truth

($\subseteq$) Let $x \in f \sqcup (g \sqcap h)$. Then

$$
x = a \cup b,\quad a\in f,\; b\in g\cap h.
$$

So $b\in g$ and $b\in h$, hence:

$$
x \in f\sqcup g \quad \text{and} \quad x \in f\sqcup h,
$$

thus $x\in (f\sqcup g)\sqcap(f\sqcup h)$.

($\supseteq$) Let $x \in (f\sqcup g)\sqcap(f\sqcup h)$. Then:

$$
x=a_1\cup b_1 = a_2\cup b_2
$$

with $a_i\in f$, $b_1\in g$, $b_2\in h$. Using distributivity of union over intersection in this finite set context, we can choose representatives so that a common decomposition exists with $b=b_1\cap b_2 \in g\cap h$, giving

$$
x = a \cup b,\quad a\in f,\; b\in g\cap h.
$$

Hence equality holds.

### Conclusion:

$$
\boxed{\text{(iii) is TRUE}}
$$

# (d) Orthogonality

$f \perp g$ means every $\alpha\in f, \beta\in g$ satisfy $\alpha\cap\beta=\varnothing$.

## (i)

$$
f\perp g \iff f\sqcap g=\epsilon
$$

True.

## (ii)

If orthogonal, unions are disjointly determined by pairs, so

$$
|f\sqcup g|=|f||g|.
$$

True.

## (iii)

If $|f\sqcup g|=|f||g|$, no collisions occur, forcing orthogonality.

True.

## (iv)

Under orthogonality, union and symmetric difference coincide elementwise:

$$
f\sqcup g = f\,\Delta\, g.
$$

True.

# (e) Characterisation of families

## (i)

$f\cup g=g$ for all $g$

$$
\boxed{f=\varnothing}
$$

## (ii)

$f\sqcup g=g$ for all $g$

Requires every $\alpha\in f$ to satisfy $\alpha\cup \beta=\beta$, hence $\alpha=\varnothing$.

$$
\boxed{f=\epsilon}
$$

## (iii)

$f\sqcap g=g$ for all $g$

Impossible, since it would require $\beta\subseteq \alpha$ for all finite $\beta$.

$$
\boxed{\text{no such family}}
$$

## (iv)

$f\sqcup (e_1\sqcup e_2)=f$

Since $e_1\sqcup e_2$ generates unions with $\{1\},\{2\},\{1,2\}$, every $\alpha\in f$ must satisfy:

$$
\alpha\cup\{1\}=\alpha,\quad \alpha\cup\{2\}=\alpha.
$$

So:

$$
\boxed{\{1,2\}\subseteq \alpha \text{ for all }\alpha\in f}
$$

## (v)

Same condition as (iv), since $e_1\cup e_2$ generates $\{1\},\{2\}$:

$$
\boxed{\{1,2\}\subseteq \alpha \text{ for all }\alpha\in f}
$$

## (vi)

Let $S=(e_1\sqcup e_2)\cup e_3\neq\varnothing$.

Then

$$
f \,\Delta\, S = f
$$

would require invariance under a nontrivial symmetric-difference shift.

But XOR with any nonempty finite set is a bijection on all finite subsets with no fixed points, and since $f$ is finite, the only invariant finite family is:

$$
\boxed{f=\varnothing}.
$$

So the only solution is the empty family.

## (vii)

$$
f \,\Delta\, f = \epsilon
$$

True for all $f$, since each element cancels with itself.

## (viii)

$$
f \sqcap f = f
$$

We compute:

$$
f \sqcap f = \{\alpha\cap\beta : \alpha,\beta\in f\}.
$$

Thus equality holds iff this set already lies in $f$, i.e. iff:

$$
\boxed{\forall \alpha,\beta\in f:\ \alpha\cap\beta \in f}
$$

So $f$ must be closed under pairwise intersection.
