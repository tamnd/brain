**Exercise 159.** [*20*] [20] John Conway’s basic rule for Life, exercise 7.1.3–167, is a Boolean function
L(xNW, xN, xNE, xW, x, xE, xSW, xS, xSE).
What ordering of those nine variables will
make the BDD as small as possible?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 2m30s  

---

Let the eight neighbor variables be denoted collectively by $y_1,\dots,y_8$, and let

$$
s = y_1 + \cdots + y_8.
$$

Conway’s Life rule is

$$
L(x,y_1,\dots,y_8) =
\begin{cases}
1 & x=1 \text{ and } s \in \{2,3\},\\
1 & x=0 \text{ and } s = 3,\\
0 & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
$$

Thus there are two symmetric Boolean functions of $s$:

$$
f_1(s) = [s=2 \text{ or } 3], \quad f_0(s) = [s=3],
$$

and

$$
L = (x \wedge f_1(s)) \;\vee\; (\neg x \wedge f_0(s)).
$$

### 1. Structure of optimal OBDDs for the symmetric part

For any ordering of $y_1,\dots,y_8$, every reduced OBDD for a symmetric function of these variables is isomorphic to a counting diagram whose nonterminal nodes are uniquely determined by pairs

$$
(k,t),
$$

where $k$ is the number of variables already read and $t$ is the number of 1s among them. Two partial assignments are equivalent if and only if they yield the same $(k,t)$, since the residual function depends only on the remaining Hamming weight.

Hence any ordering of the eight neighbor variables yields the same minimal sub-OBDD size for each of $f_0$ and $f_1$, namely a linear counting structure with $O(8^2)$ nodes, and more importantly, a unique sequence of levels indexed by $k$.

Thus the only issue is how the variable $x$ interacts with these symmetric states.

### 2. Key observation: splitting induced by $x$

Once the variable $x$ is assigned, the problem becomes a single symmetric function:

- if $x=1$, we evaluate $f_1(s)$,
- if $x=0$, we evaluate $f_0(s)$.

Crucially, these are distinct Boolean functions of $s$. They are not identical at any level of the counting process, since for example at $s=2$,

$$
f_1(2)=1, \quad f_0(2)=0.
$$

Therefore, after $x$ is fixed, the residual OBDD is one of two distinct symmetric machines.

### 3. Why interleaving $x$ forces duplication

Consider any ordering in which $x$ is not placed entirely before or entirely after all neighbors. Then along some root-to-leaf path in the BDD, we encounter both:

- a stage where some subset of neighbors has been read before $x$,
- and later a stage where additional neighbors are read after $x$.

We analyze the critical point: the moment immediately before $x$ is tested.

At that point, the OBDD must represent the residual function as a function of the partial count $(k,t)$, but it does not yet know whether it will evaluate to $f_0$ or $f_1$. Therefore the residual function is not a single symmetric function of $(k,t)$, but a _pair of possibilities_:

$$
(f_0 \text{ on remaining inputs}), \quad (f_1 \text{ on remaining inputs}).
$$

These two cases are distinguishable at every continuation state, because there exists a completion of the remaining variables that separates them (e.g., completions with exactly two or three 1s).

Hence, before $x$ is read, each symmetric state $(k,t)$ must be duplicated into two inequivalent BDD nodes:

$$
(k,t, \text{pending } x=0), \quad (k,t, \text{pending } x=1).
$$

This duplication is forced by the reduced-OBDD equivalence rule: two nodes can only merge if their induced Boolean functions are identical on all extensions, but the two pending cases differ on extensions achieving $s=2$.

Therefore any ordering in which $x$ is not globally separated from the neighbors introduces an unavoidable factor-of-two blow-up in all intermediate counting levels prior to the occurrence of $x$.

### 4. Optimal placements of $x$

If $x$ is placed first, then immediately the computation splits into two independent symmetric OBDDs:

- one computing $f_0$,
- one computing $f_1$,

but both share the same counting structure for $s$. This yields exactly one copy of the symmetric counting DAG, with a single root decision on $x$.

If $x$ is placed last, the symmetric counting DAG is built once for the neighbors, and the final level is a single test on $x$ selecting between two terminals computed from the same shared structure.

In both cases, no duplication occurs in intermediate counting levels.

Thus both extreme placements achieve the same optimal sharing.

### 5. Optimality

From the duplication argument, any ordering that places $x$ between neighbor variables necessarily creates two inequivalent residual families of symmetric functions prior to the occurrence of $x$, forcing duplication of all counting states at those levels.

This strictly increases the number of BDD nodes compared with the boundary placements.

Since permutations of $y_1,\dots,y_8$ do not affect the counting structure, the only degree of freedom is the position of $x$, and only the boundary positions avoid duplication.

### 6. Conclusion

An optimal variable ordering is:

$$
x, y_1, y_2, \dots, y_8
\quad \text{or} \quad
y_1, y_2, \dots, y_8, x,
$$

where $y_1,\dots,y_8$ is any ordering of

$$
x_{NW}, x_N, x_{NE}, x_W, x_E, x_{SW}, x_S, x_{SE}.
$$

All such orderings yield the same minimal reduced BDD size, and any interleaving of $x$ with the neighbors produces a strictly larger BDD due to forced duplication of symmetric counting states before $x$ is resolved.
