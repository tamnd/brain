**Exercise 98.** &#9654; [*22*] [22] In addition to (70), explain how to define the vertices ENDPT(x) of G that
have degree ≤1. Also characterize PAIR(x, y), the components of size 2.

Let $G$ be the graph defined in (70), with adjacency predicate $E(u,v)$. We assume $G$ is simple, so $E(x,x)$ does not hold.

## 1. Definition of $\mathrm{ENDPT}(x)$

A vertex $x$ is an endpoint if it has at most one neighbor. In first-order logic, this means that it is impossible to find two distinct vertices both adjacent to $x$.

Thus we define

$$
\mathrm{ENDPT}(x)\;\equiv\;\neg \exists y \exists z \,\bigl(y \ne z \wedge E(x,y) \wedge E(x,z)\bigr).
$$

This directly states that $x$ does not have two different neighbors, hence $\deg(x)\le 1$.

An equivalent form is the functional version of adjacency:

$$
\mathrm{ENDPT}(x)\;\equiv\;\forall y \forall z \,\bigl(E(x,y)\wedge E(x,z)\rightarrow y=z\bigr).
$$

Both formulas are equivalent under standard first-order logic.

## 2. Characterization of $\mathrm{PAIR}(x,y)$

A pair $(x,y)$ forms a connected component of size $2$ exactly when:

1. $x \ne y$,
2. $x$ and $y$ are adjacent,
3. $x$ has no neighbor other than $y$,
4. $y$ has no neighbor other than $x$.

This is expressed as

$$
\mathrm{PAIR}(x,y)\;\equiv\; x \ne y \wedge E(x,y)
\wedge \forall z \,(E(x,z)\rightarrow z=y)
\wedge \forall z \,(E(y,z)\rightarrow z=x).
$$

## 3. Correctness of the characterization

If $\mathrm{PAIR}(x,y)$ holds, then $x$ is adjacent only to $y$, and $y$ is adjacent only to $x$. Hence the only vertices reachable from $x$ or $y$ are $x$ and $y$ themselves, so the connected component containing $x$ has exactly two vertices.

Conversely, if a connected component has exactly two vertices $x$ and $y$, then they must be adjacent (otherwise the component would split), and no other edges incident to either vertex can exist. Hence the above formula holds.

## 4. Summary

$$
\mathrm{ENDPT}(x)\;\equiv\;\neg \exists y \exists z \,(y \ne z \wedge E(x,y)\wedge E(x,z)),
$$

$$
\mathrm{PAIR}(x,y)\;\equiv\; x \ne y \wedge E(x,y)
\wedge \forall z (E(x,z)\rightarrow z=y)
\wedge \forall z (E(y,z)\rightarrow z=x).
$$

These correctly define vertices of degree at most $1$ and components of size $2$ in the graph $G$.
