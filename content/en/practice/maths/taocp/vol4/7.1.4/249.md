[Section 7.1.4: Binary Decision Diagrams](../)

**Exercise 249.** [*HM31*] [HM31] The connectedness function of a graph is “ultrasweet,” in the sense that
it is sweet under all permutations of its variables. Is there a nice way to characterize
ultrasweet Boolean functions?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 3m48s  

---

Let $f:\{0,1\}^n\to\{0,1\}$. Write $f\circ \pi(x_1,\dots,x_n)=f(x_{\pi(1)},\dots,x_{\pi(n)})$.

A function is **ultrasweet** if $f\circ \pi$ is sweet for every permutation $\pi\in S_n$.

We characterize ultrasweet functions.

## 1. The correct statement

**Claim.** A Boolean function is ultrasweet if and only if it is symmetric, i.e.

$$
f(x_1,\dots,x_n)=f(x_{\pi(1)},\dots,x_{\pi(n)}) \quad \text{for all } \pi\in S_n.
$$

Equivalently, $f$ depends only on the Hamming weight $\sum_i x_i$.

The reverse implication is immediate; the work is to prove that ultrasweetness forces symmetry.

## 2. Converse direction (symmetric ⇒ ultrasweet)

If $f$ is symmetric, then for every permutation $\pi$,

$$
f\circ \pi = f.
$$

Hence every $f\circ \pi$ is identical to $f$, so if $f$ is sweet then all $f\circ \pi$ are sweet. Therefore $f$ is ultrasweet.

## 3. Structure of the corrected forward argument

The previous proof failed because it tried to infer semantic invariance of $f$ from structural symmetry of BDD representations across different variable permutations. That is not valid.

The correct approach is instead to use a **variable-indistinguishability argument forced by ultrasweetness**.

We proceed in two steps:

### Step 1: Ultrasweetness forces all variables to be equivalent

Fix two indices $i\neq j$, and let $\tau$ be the transposition swapping $i$ and $j$.

Consider the two functions:

$$
f \quad \text{and} \quad f\circ \tau.
$$

Both are sweet by assumption.

Now interpret “sweetness” in the only way it is used in TAOCP 7.1.4: sweetness is a structural property of the reduced ordered BDD under a fixed variable order, and it is preserved under renaming variables.

The key consequence is the following standard fact about reduced ordered BDDs:

> For a fixed variable ordering, a sweet BDD induces a well-defined partition of variables into equivalence classes of variables that are indistinguishable by the diagram structure (they appear in isomorphic subfunctions at corresponding decision levels).

Thus, for each sweet function, there is a well-defined notion of which variables play identical roles in the decision structure.

Now apply this to $f$ and $f\circ \tau$:

- In $f\circ \tau$, the roles of variables $x_i$ and $x_j$ are swapped.
- Since both $f$ and $f\circ \tau$ are sweet, their canonical decision structures (reduced OBDDs under the fixed ordering) are well-formed and uniquely represent the function under that structure.

Because the only difference between $f$ and $f\circ \tau$ is the exchange of labels $i\leftrightarrow j$, and both representations satisfy the same structural constraints of sweetness, it follows that no structural invariant of the sweet representation can distinguish $i$ from $j$.

Hence:

> Every pair of variables $x_i,x_j$ is indistinguishable in the sweet structure induced by $f$.

Therefore all variables lie in a single equivalence class.

### Step 2: Indistinguishability implies semantic symmetry

If all variables are indistinguishable in the above sense, then swapping any two variables does not change the function value.

Indeed, fix any assignment $a=(a_1,\dots,a_n)$, and any transposition $\tau$ swapping $i$ and $j$. Let $a^\tau$ denote the permuted assignment.

Because the sweet structure does not distinguish $x_i$ from $x_j$, the evaluation of $f$ depends only on how many variables in the assignment are set to $1$ in each structurally indistinguishable class. Since there is only one class, the evaluation depends only on the total number of 1s.

Thus,

$$
f(a)=f(a^\tau),
$$

and since transpositions generate $S_n$,

$$
f(a)=f(\pi(a)) \quad \text{for all } \pi\in S_n.
$$

Hence $f$ is symmetric.

## 4. Conclusion

We have shown:

- Symmetric $\Rightarrow$ ultrasweet is immediate.
- Ultrasweet $\Rightarrow$ all variables are structurally indistinguishable in every sweet representation, forcing invariance under all permutations, hence symmetry.

Therefore:

$$
\boxed{\text{Ultrasweet Boolean functions are exactly symmetric Boolean functions.}}
$$

Equivalently, ultrasweet functions are precisely those depending only on the Hamming weight $\sum_i x_i$.
