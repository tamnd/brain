## Solution

Let Algorithm B process variables $x_1,\dots,x_n$ sequentially with weights $w_1,\dots,w_n$, and let $m_k$ denote the quantity computed at stage $k$ from the remaining variables $x_k,\dots,x_n$. In the algorithm, each remaining variable contributes either $0$ (if excluded) or $w_i$ (if included), so every attainable continuation from level $k$ has the form

$$
S_k = \sum_{i=k}^n w_i x_i,\quad x_i \in \{0,1\}.
$$

Hence $m_k$ depends only on the set of weights ${w_k,\dots,w_n}$ and not on the Boolean function $f$, since $f$ only restricts which choices are admissible, not the extremal arithmetic values achievable by the linear combination itself.

The largest possible value of $m_k$ is obtained by choosing each $x_i$ to maximize its contribution. For each index $i \ge k$, this means taking $x_i = 1$ when $w_i > 0$ and $x_i = 0$ when $w_i < 0$. The resulting sum is

$$
m_k^{\max} = \sum_{\substack{i=k \\ w_i > 0}}^n w_i.
$$

The smallest possible value of $m_k$ is obtained by choosing each $x_i$ to minimize its contribution. For each index $i \ge k$, this means taking $x_i = 1$ when $w_i < 0$ and $x_i = 0$ when $w_i > 0$. The resulting sum is

$$
m_k^{\min} = \sum_{\substack{i=k \\ w_i < 0}}^n w_i.
$$

Any other assignment of the variables $x_k,\dots,x_n$ replaces at least one maximizing choice in $m_k^{\max}$ by a non-increasing choice, and replaces at least one minimizing choice in $m_k^{\min}$ by a non-decreasing choice, so no assignment can exceed $m_k^{\max}$ or go below $m_k^{\min}$.

Therefore the possible values of $m_k$ lie exactly in the interval determined by these two extremes.

$$
\boxed{m_k^{\min} = \sum_{i=k,\, w_i<0}^n w_i \quad \text{and} \quad m_k^{\max} = \sum_{i=k,\, w_i>0}^n w_i}
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
