**Exercise 156.** [*30*] [30] Theorems J+ and J−suggest that we could save reordering time by only
jumping up when sifting, not bothering to jump down. Then we could eliminate steps
J3, J5, J6, and J7 of Algorithm J. Would that be wise?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 4m18s  

---

We restart from the actual mechanism of Algorithm J (sifting) rather than from a global permutation-reachability model.

## Corrected Solution

Let $x$ be a variable in a BDD with respect to a fixed variable ordering. In Algorithm J, sifting a variable $x$ means moving $x$ through the ordering by adjacent swaps, evaluating the BDD size at each position, and finally placing $x$ at a position where the measured size is minimal. Theorems J$^+$ and J$^-$ justify that these local minima can be found by scanning in one direction and then the other, using up and down moves symmetrically.

The proposed modification deletes all downward movement: once $x$ is moved upward, it is never moved downward during its sift, so steps J3, J5, J6, and J7 (which implement the downward scan and associated reordering) are removed.

The question is whether this can still guarantee finding a locally optimal position for $x$ and, consequently, whether the global sifting heuristic remains effective.

## 1. What is actually being changed

Fix a current ordering. Consider sifting a variable $x$ initially at position $i$.

- In full sifting, $x$ is moved upward step by step to position $1$, recording BDD sizes.
- Then it is moved downward step by step to position $n$, again recording sizes.
- The best position among all visited positions is chosen.

In the modified algorithm:

- $x$ is only moved upward from position $i$ toward position $1$.
- No downward exploration from $i$ is performed.
- Hence the candidate positions for $x$ are restricted to those encountered while moving upward.

This is a restriction on the _search path for the best position of a single variable_, not a restriction on global permutations.

## 2. Key structural fact about BDD sifting

Let $B_k(x)$ denote the BDD size when $x$ is placed at position $k$ (with all other variables fixed).

During full sifting, Algorithm J evaluates $B_k(x)$ for all $k$ between $1$ and $n$, and then selects a global minimum over $k$.

The correctness of sifting relies on the fact that:

$$
\min_{1 \le k \le n} B_k(x)
$$

is achieved somewhere along the full scan.

Theorems J$^+$ and J$^-$ ensure that evaluating in both directions is sufficient to encounter this minimum, even though $B_k(x)$ is not monotone in $k$.

## 3. Why upward-only scanning is insufficient

The crucial point is that $B_k(x)$ is not monotone along the ordering. In particular, there exist BDDs where:

- moving $x$ upward from its initial position strictly increases $B_k(x)$,
- the true minimum occurs at a position below the starting point,
- and that minimum cannot be detected by only exploring upward moves.

This phenomenon is standard in sifting: the cost function behaves like a non-unimodal discrete function over positions, and the optimum may lie on either side of the initial position.

Formally, there exist instances and a variable $x$ such that if $i$ is the initial position of $x$, then

$$
\min_{k < i} B_k(x) \;<\; \min_{k \ge i} B_k(x).
$$

In such a case, any algorithm that only evaluates positions reachable by upward movement from $i$ can never discover the global minimum for $x$.

## 4. Why downward moves are essential even though swaps are symmetric

The objection is not about global permutation reachability but about _local optimal placement of one variable_.

Even though variables can indirectly change positions through swaps of other variables, Algorithm J evaluates configurations only through the controlled trajectory of sifting a single variable while freezing others. The correctness argument for J depends on exhaustive evaluation of that variable’s positional neighborhood.

Removing downward scans breaks the guarantee that the algorithm evaluates all candidate positions for $x$.

Thus the algorithm no longer guarantees:

- that $x$ is placed at the best position relative to the current ordering,
- nor that the global iteration over variables converges to a local minimum of the heuristic.

## 5. Consequence for the global heuristic

Algorithm J repeatedly sifts variables until no improvement occurs. This convergence argument depends on each individual sift performing an exact minimization over the variable’s position.

If upward-only sifting is used, then a single sift may terminate at a position that is not even locally optimal for that variable. As a result:

- the algorithm may accept a strictly suboptimal placement of $x$,
- later sifts of other variables cannot in general repair this loss,
- and the procedure may stabilize at a non-minimal ordering even when improving swaps exist.

## 6. Conclusion

The downward phase in sifting is not redundant. It is essential because the BDD size as a function of a variable’s position is not directionally structured, and its minimum can lie below the starting position.

Therefore, eliminating steps J3, J5, J6, and J7 removes necessary evaluations of candidate positions and breaks the correctness guarantee of Algorithm J.

## Final answer

$$
\boxed{\text{No, it would not be wise. Downward sifting is necessary to ensure each variable is placed at a true local optimum position.}}
$$
