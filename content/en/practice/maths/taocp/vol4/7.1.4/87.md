**Exercise 87.** [*20*] [20] If the median operator ⟨fgh⟩is implemented with a recursive subroutine

We interpret “obvious values” in the sense of the recursive implementation analogous to (62): cases where the median (majority-of-three) can be simplified immediately by local algebraic identities, without invoking any Shannon-type decomposition on additional variables.

Let

$$
\langle f,g,h\rangle
$$

denote the median (majority) operator, i.e.

$$
\langle f,g,h\rangle = 1 \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad f+g+h \ge 2.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
\langle f,g,h\rangle = (f \wedge g)\,\vee\,(g \wedge h)\,\vee\,(h \wedge f).
$$

In a recursive subroutine analogous to (62), “obvious values” are those recognized immediately from the syntactic form of the inputs as constants, equalities, or complements, allowing direct reduction of the ternary operator.

### 1. Constant arguments

If one argument is constant, the majority expression collapses to a binary function.

If $f=0$,

$$
\langle 0,g,h\rangle = (0\wedge g)\vee(g\wedge h)\vee(h\wedge 0) = g \wedge h.
$$

If $f=1$,

$$
\langle 1,g,h\rangle = (1\wedge g)\vee(g\wedge h)\vee(h\wedge 1)
= g \vee h.
$$

By symmetry, the same holds for any position.

These are “obvious” because no further decomposition is needed once a constant is detected.

### 2. Two identical arguments

If two inputs are syntactically identical, the majority reduces immediately.

If $g=f$,

$$
\langle f,f,h\rangle = (f\wedge f)\vee(f\wedge h)\vee(h\wedge f)
= f \vee (f\wedge h)
= f.
$$

Thus,

$$
\langle f,f,h\rangle = f,
\quad
\langle f,g,g\rangle = g,
\quad
\langle f,g,f\rangle = f.
$$

This is “obvious” because the repeated argument already supplies two votes in the majority computation.

### 3. Complementary pair

If one input is the complement of another, say $g = \neg f$, then:

$$
\langle f,\neg f,h\rangle
= (f\wedge \neg f)\vee(\neg f \wedge h)\vee(h \wedge f).
$$

Since $f \wedge \neg f = 0$, this becomes

$$
\langle f,\neg f,h\rangle = (\neg f \wedge h)\vee(f \wedge h)
= h \wedge (\neg f \vee f)
= h.
$$

Hence,

$$
\langle f,\neg f,h\rangle = h,
$$

and by symmetry this holds for any position of the complementary pair.

This is “obvious” in the recursive sense because the complementary pair cancels locally, leaving the third argument as the immediate result.

### 4. Why these are exactly the “obvious values” in the recursive sense

In the recursive implementation analogous to (62), the operator is simplified before any Shannon expansion only when the current expression matches a local syntactic pattern that allows algebraic reduction:

- detection of constants $0,1$,
- detection of identical subfunctions,
- detection of complementary subfunctions.

These are precisely the patterns that can be recognized without inspecting internal variable structure of $f,g,h$. No additional semantic analysis is performed in the “obvious case” phase of the recursion.

Any other configuration of three Boolean inputs does not admit a uniform algebraic simplification independent of further decomposition, because the majority expression depends essentially on all three inputs and cannot be reduced by a single identity substitution.

Importantly, this is not a claim about all possible Boolean functional dependencies between $f,g,h$, but only about the local rewrite rules available to the recursive median routine at the point where it decides whether to expand further.

### Final list of “obvious values”

Up to symmetry, the complete set of immediate reductions is:

$$
\langle 0,g,h\rangle = g \wedge h,
\quad
\langle 1,g,h\rangle = g \vee h,
$$

$$
\langle f,f,h\rangle = f,
\quad
\langle f,\neg f,h\rangle = h.
$$

### Conclusion

These identities are exactly the cases in which the recursive median implementation performs no Shannon expansion step, because the expression is reduced immediately by constant elimination, idempotence, or complement cancellation. All other cases require further recursion on underlying variables, since no local algebraic rule reduces the ternary structure to a function of fewer arguments.
