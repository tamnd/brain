The error in the previous solution is the identification of coefficients with multiplicities of identical subsets. In a standard ZDD, identical subsets cannot appear multiple times, so coefficients must instead be encoded structurally inside the subsets themselves.

A correct solution must therefore ensure that every occurrence contributing to a coefficient corresponds to a _distinct subset_, while preserving the exponent structure under union.

## 1. Exponent encoding (unchanged and correct)

Let the exponent universe be

$$
E_x=\{x^{2^k}\mid k\ge 0\},\quad
E_y=\{y^{2^k}\mid k\ge 0\},\quad
E_z=\{z^{2^k}\mid k\ge 0\},
$$

$$
E = E_x \cup E_y \cup E_z.
$$

For any finite subset $A \subseteq E$, define exponent sums

$$
\deg_x(A)=\sum_{x^{2^k}\in A} 2^k,
\quad
\deg_y(A)=\sum_{y^{2^k}\in A} 2^k,
\quad
\deg_z(A)=\sum_{z^{2^k}\in A} 2^k.
$$

Then $A$ encodes the monomial

$$
x^{\deg_x(A)} y^{\deg_y(A)} z^{\deg_z(A)}.
$$

Subset union corresponds to addition of exponents because the exponent atoms are distinct.

## 2. Why coefficients need new structure

A polynomial coefficient $c$ cannot be represented by repeating the same subset, since a ZDD represents a _set of distinct subsets_.

Therefore, each unit contributing to a coefficient must correspond to a different subset.

The solution is to attach **distinct coefficient-marker elements** to each occurrence of a monomial term.

## 3. Coefficient markers

For each monomial term occurrence in a polynomial, introduce fresh atoms

$$
m_{i,1}, m_{i,2}, \dots, m_{i,c_i}.
$$

All marker atoms are chosen to be pairwise distinct across all indices $i,j$, and disjoint from $E$.

These markers do not affect the exponent; they only distinguish copies.

## 4. Representation of a polynomial

Let

$$
P(x,y,z)=\sum_{i=1}^m c_i x^{a_i}y^{b_i}z^{d_i}.
$$

Choose a subset $A_i \subseteq E$ such that it encodes $(a_i,b_i,d_i)$.

Define the ZDD family

$$
\mathcal{F}(P)
=
\bigcup_{i=1}^m
\bigcup_{j=1}^{c_i}
\left\{ A_i \cup \{m_{i,j}\} \right\}.
$$

### Key property

Each coefficient unit corresponds to a distinct subset, because each uses a unique marker $m_{i,j}$.

Thus:

- no multiplicity is needed,
- no two subsets are identical,
- ZDD semantics are respected exactly.

## 5. Correct interpretation

Define the projection map that forgets markers:

$$
\pi(S)=S \cap E.
$$

Then every subset in $\mathcal{F}(P)$ projects to the correct exponent monomial.

The coefficient of a monomial equals the number of subsets in $\mathcal{F}(P)$ projecting to it.

## 6. Correctness of addition

Let $P,Q$ be polynomials.

Construct $\mathcal{F}(P)$ and $\mathcal{F}(Q)$ using disjoint marker sets (rename markers in $Q$ so that all $m_{i,j}$ used in $P$ are distinct from those in $Q$).

Then:

$$
\mathcal{F}(P+Q)=\mathcal{F}(P)\cup \mathcal{F}(Q)
$$

is a standard union of ZDD families.

### Coefficients

For a fixed monomial $x^a y^b z^d$, subsets projecting to it are exactly those from $P$ plus those from $Q$, hence counts add:

$$
c_{P+Q}=c_P + c_Q.
$$

No collision occurs because all subsets are distinct via markers.

## 7. Correctness of multiplication

Define multiplication by:

$$
\mathcal{F}(P\cdot Q)
=
\{ S \cup T \mid S\in \mathcal{F}(P),\ T\in \mathcal{F}(Q)\}.
$$

Assume marker sets of $P$ and $Q$ are disjoint.

### 7.1 Exponent correctness

For $S,T$,

$$
\pi(S\cup T)=\pi(S)\cup \pi(T),
$$

so exponent atoms add exactly as in monomial multiplication.

Thus:

$$
x^{a}y^{b}z^{c} \cdot x^{a'}y^{b'}z^{c'}
\mapsto
x^{a+a'}y^{b+b'}z^{c+c'}.
$$

### 7.2 Coefficient correctness

Fix monomials $i$ and $k$ from $P$ and $Q$ with coefficients $c_i$ and $d_k$.

Their representations contribute:

- $c_i$ distinct subsets $A_i \cup \{m_{i,j}\}$,
- $d_k$ distinct subsets $B_k \cup \{n_{k,\ell}\}$.

Each pair produces a distinct subset:

$$
(A_i \cup \{m_{i,j}\}) \cup (B_k \cup \{n_{k,\ell}\})
=
A_i \cup B_k \cup \{m_{i,j}, n_{k,\ell}\}.
$$

Distinctness follows from unique marker pairs $(j,\ell)$, so there are exactly

$$
c_i d_k
$$

such subsets contributing to the resulting monomial.

Summing over all decompositions gives the correct convolution of coefficients.

## 8. ZDD validity

The construction produces an ordinary ZDD-representable family:

- every element is a finite subset of a finite universe (for each fixed computation),
- no duplicates occur,
- standard ZDD reduction applies normally.

No multiset interpretation is required.

## 9. Conclusion

The corrected representation avoids invalid multiplicities by replacing them with structural encoding:

- exponent structure is encoded by standard power-of-two atoms,
- coefficients are encoded by **distinct marker elements**, not repetition,
- ZDDs represent a genuine set of subsets,
- polynomial coefficients arise by counting distinct subsets projecting to the same monomial.

Thus ZDDs can represent polynomials over $\mathbb{N}[x,y,z]$ within their standard semantics by lifting coefficients into combinatorial structure rather than multiplicity.
