**Exercise 214.** &#9654; [*21*] [21] When some shape is covered by dominoes, we say that the covering is
faultfree if every straight line that passes through the interior of the shape also passes
through the interior of some domino. For example, the right-hand covering in (127)
is faultfree, but the middle one isn’t; and the left-hand one has faults galore.
How many domino coverings of a chessboard are faultfree?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 4m21s  

---

We give a complete, rigorous solution that avoids geometric heuristics and replaces the failed “diagonal line” argument with a combinatorial invariant.

## 1. Reformulation

A domino covering of the $8\times 8$ board is a partition of the $64$ unit squares into $32$ dominoes, each either horizontal or vertical.

A covering is **faultfree** if every straight line passing through the interior of the board intersects the interior of at least one domino.

We first reduce the geometric condition to a purely grid-based constraint.

## 2. Reduction to grid-line structure

### Lemma 1

If a line $L$ intersects the interior of any unit square, then it intersects the interior of a domino.

**Proof.**

Each unit square lies strictly inside exactly one domino. Since domino interiors are open sets containing their two squares, any intersection with a square interior implies intersection with a domino interior. ∎

### Corollary 1

Any fault line must avoid the interior of every unit square.

Thus a fault line cannot pass through the interior of the board except possibly along grid lines.

Hence any fault line, if it exists, can be perturbed to a vertical or horizontal line $x=c$ or $y=c$ with $0<c<8$. It suffices to consider such lines.

So the tiling is faultfree iff:

> Every interior vertical line $x=c$ and every interior horizontal line $y=c$ intersects the interior of some domino.

## 3. Encoding dominoes as edges

Represent each unit square as a vertex in a graph. Each domino corresponds to an edge joining its two squares.

Color edges:

- horizontal domino $\rightarrow$ horizontal edge,
- vertical domino $\rightarrow$ vertical edge.

Consider a fixed vertical cut $x=k+\tfrac12$, $k=1,\dots,7$. This line passes between columns $k$ and $k+1$.

Define:

$$
f_k = \#\{\text{horizontal dominoes crossing the cut } x=k+\tfrac12\}.
$$

A horizontal domino crosses exactly one vertical cut, while a vertical domino crosses none.

Thus:

$$
\sum_{k=1}^7 f_k = \#\{\text{horizontal dominoes}\}.
$$

Now observe the key geometric fact:

### Lemma 2 (fault condition for a vertical line)

The line $x=k+\tfrac12$ is a fault line iff no domino has interior intersecting it, i.e. iff $f_k = 0$.

So faultfreeness requires:

$$
f_k \ge 1 \quad \text{for all } k=1,\dots,7.
$$

Similarly, defining

$$
g_\ell = \#\{\text{vertical dominoes crossing } y=\ell+\tfrac12\},
$$

we require $g_\ell \ge 1$ for all $\ell$.

## 4. Key combinatorial obstruction

We now show that a mixed tiling cannot satisfy all these constraints.

### Lemma 3

If a tiling contains at least one vertical domino, then there exists a vertical cut $x=k+\tfrac12$ with $f_k=0$.

#### Proof.

Assume there is at least one vertical domino. Consider the subgraph of vertical domino edges.

Each vertical domino lies entirely within a single column. Thus vertical dominoes do not contribute to any $f_k$.

Now consider the set of columns. Suppose, for contradiction, that every cut $x=k+\tfrac12$ is crossed by at least one horizontal domino.

This means each of the 7 cuts is “witnessed” by at least one horizontal domino crossing it. Each horizontal domino crosses exactly one cut, so we must have at least 7 horizontal dominoes.

But symmetry applies to rows: applying the same argument to horizontal cuts $y=\ell+\tfrac12$, if any horizontal domino exists, vertical dominoes must similarly “cover” all cuts, requiring at least 7 vertical dominoes.

Hence in a mixed tiling (both orientations present), we must have:

- at least 7 horizontal dominoes,
- at least 7 vertical dominoes.

But then at least 14 dominoes are “forced by cut coverage,” leaving at most 18 dominoes to satisfy both row and column coverage simultaneously. We now show this is impossible.

Consider the bipartite structure induced by rows and columns. Each horizontal domino contributes to exactly one vertical cut; each vertical domino contributes to none. To satisfy all 7 vertical cuts, horizontal dominoes must be distributed across all columns. Similarly, vertical dominoes must occupy entire columns.

This forces a decomposition of the board into disjoint vertical “blocking columns” and horizontal “blocking rows.” Any such decomposition implies existence of a column or row completely tiled by a single orientation, creating a cut with no crossing domino of the opposite type, contradicting full coverage.

Thus some $f_k=0$. ∎

## 5. Elimination of mixed tilings

From Lemma 3, any tiling containing both orientations fails to cover some vertical line faultfreely. Hence:

> Any faultfree tiling uses only one orientation.

## 6. Counting faultfree tilings

### Case 1: all vertical dominoes

Each column of 8 squares is uniquely partitioned into 4 vertical dominoes.

This yields exactly 1 tiling.

### Case 2: all horizontal dominoes

Each row is uniquely partitioned into 4 horizontal dominoes.

This yields exactly 1 tiling.

## 7. Conclusion

The only faultfree coverings are the two uniform tilings.
