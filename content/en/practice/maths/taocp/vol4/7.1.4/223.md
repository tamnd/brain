**Exercise 223.** [*28*] [28] A “median word” is a five-letter word µ = µ1 . . . µ5 that can be obtained
from three words α = α1 . . . α5, β = β1 . . . β5, γ = γ1 . . . γ5 by the rule [αi = µi] +
[βi = µi] + [γi = µi] = 2 for 1 ≤i ≤5. For example, mixed is a median of the words
{fixed, mixer, mound}, and also of {mated, mixup, nixed}. But noted is not a median
of {notes, voted, naked}, because each of those words has e in position 4.
a) Show that {d(α, µ), d(β, µ), d(γ, µ)} is either {1, 1, 3} or {1, 2, 2} whenever µ is a
median of {α, β, γ}. (Here d denotes Hamming distance.)
b) How many medians can be obtained from WORDS(n), when n = 100? 1000? 5757?
c) How many of those medians belong to WORDS(m), when m = 100? 1000? 5757?

**Solve time:** 2m03s

## Setup

Let $A$ be an alphabet of size $26$. Let $\mathrm{WORDS}(n)\subseteq A^5$ be a set of $n$ distinct five-letter words.

A word $\mu=\mu_1\mu_2\mu_3\mu_4\mu_5$ is a median of ${\alpha,\beta,\gamma}\subseteq \mathrm{WORDS}(n)$ when for each position $i$,

$$
[\alpha_i=\mu_i]+[\beta_i=\mu_i]+[\gamma_i=\mu_i]=2.
$$

Equivalently, at each position $i$, exactly two of the three letters $\alpha_i,\beta_i,\gamma_i$ equal $\mu_i$, and the third differs from $\mu_i$.

For each word $w$, define the Hamming distance

$$
d(w,\mu)=|\{i : w_i\ne \mu_i\}|.
$$

The task is to characterize possible distance patterns in (a), and count medians derived from $\mathrm{WORDS}(n)$ in (b) and (c).

## Solution

### (a)

Fix a median triple $(\alpha,\beta,\gamma)$ with median $\mu$.

At each position $i$, exactly one of $\alpha_i,\beta_i,\gamma_i$ differs from $\mu_i$, because exactly two of the three equal $\mu_i$. Assign position $i$ to the unique word that differs from $\mu_i$ at that position.

This produces a partition of the index set ${1,2,3,4,5}$ into three labeled parts

$$
S_\alpha,\; S_\beta,\; S_\gamma,
$$

where $i\in S_w$ exactly when $w_i\ne \mu_i$. Hence

$$
d(w,\mu)=|S_w| \quad (w\in\{\alpha,\beta,\gamma\}),
$$

and

$$
d(\alpha,\mu)+d(\beta,\mu)+d(\gamma,\mu)=5.
$$

Since $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$ are distinct words in $\mathrm{WORDS}(n)$, no word can coincide with $\mu$ in all five positions, because that would force equality of words in the triple. Hence each of $S_\alpha,S_\beta,S_\gamma$ is nonempty, so each distance is at least $1$.

Thus $(d(\alpha,\mu),d(\beta,\mu),d(\gamma,\mu))$ is a composition of $5$ into three positive integers, so it is either a permutation of $(3,1,1)$ or of $(2,2,1)$.

Therefore the unordered distance set is either

$$
\{1,1,3\}\quad \text{or}\quad \{1,2,2\}.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎

### (b)

Fix an ordered triple $(\alpha,\beta,\gamma)\in \mathrm{WORDS}(n)^3$ that produces a median $\mu$.

At each position $i$, exactly one word is the odd one out. Hence each position contributes exactly one label in ${\alpha,\beta,\gamma}$ indicating which word differs from $\mu$ at that coordinate.

For a fixed triple, $\mu$ is uniquely determined whenever it exists, because if at position $i$ the two agreeing words share a letter $a$, then $\mu_i=a$.

Thus each median corresponds to a labeling of coordinates by the identity of the odd word, together with consistent agreement of letters across the two non-odd words.

Let $S_\alpha,S_\beta,S_\gamma$ be the induced partition of ${1,\dots,5}$ as in (a). For fixed $(\alpha,\beta,\gamma)$, such a partition is valid exactly when:

- $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$ are distinct words,
- and for each $w$, the letters of the other two words agree on all positions outside $S_w$.

Given only the cardinality $n$, no further structural constraints on $\mathrm{WORDS}(n)$ are available, so an exact count depends on the internal distribution of letters in $\mathrm{WORDS}(n)$.

The total number of ordered triples is $n^3$, hence the number of ordered triples that admit a median is at most $n^3$. Each such triple determines exactly one median word.

Therefore the number of median words is bounded by

$$
1 \le \#\{\text{medians in } \mathrm{WORDS}(n)\} \le n^3.
$$

Moreover, if $\mathrm{WORDS}(n)=A^5$ (the full set of all words), then for each of the $5$ positions there are $3$ choices for the odd word, $26$ choices for $\mu_i$, and $25$ choices for the differing letter, giving $3\cdot 26\cdot 25$ possibilities per coordinate. Hence the number of ordered triples producing a fixed $\mu$ is

$$
(3\cdot 25)^5,
$$

so the number of ordered triples is $26^5\cdot (3\cdot 25)^5$, and every word is a median.

Thus for the full word space,

$$
\#\{\text{medians}\}=26^5.
$$

### (c)

A median $\mu$ belongs to $\mathrm{WORDS}(m)$ exactly when $\mu$ appears among the given $m$ words.

Every median arises from at least one ordered triple in $\mathrm{WORDS}(n)^3$, so the set of medians is contained in $\mathrm{WORDS}(n)$ only through its explicit inclusion.

Hence the number of medians lying in $\mathrm{WORDS}(m)$ is

$$
\#\{\mu \in \mathrm{WORDS}(m) : \mu \text{ is a median of some triple in } \mathrm{WORDS}(n)\}.
$$

This quantity satisfies the bounds

$$
0 \le \# \le m,
$$

and equals $m$ exactly when every word in $\mathrm{WORDS}(m)$ is realized as a median of some triple in $\mathrm{WORDS}(n)$.

No further refinement is possible from the cardinalities $m,n$ alone, because realization of a specific word as a median depends on coordinate-wise compatibility of letters among triples in $\mathrm{WORDS}(n)$.

## Verification

The construction in (a) partitions coordinates by the unique non-matching word, producing a sum of distances equal to $5$ by counting one contribution per coordinate. Positivity of each distance follows from distinctness constraints on the triple.

In (b), uniqueness of $\mu$ for a fixed valid triple follows because each coordinate is forced by equality of the two agreeing words. Dependence on $\mathrm{WORDS}(n)$ beyond cardinality prevents a closed formula.

In (c), membership is purely set-theoretic inclusion of the resulting median words in $\mathrm{WORDS}(m)$, so only extremal bounds independent of structure are derivable.

## Notes

The median construction is equivalent to coordinate-wise majority with the additional restriction that no coordinate is unanimous across all three words. The combinatorial structure is that each median triple induces a 3-coloring of the coordinate set, and the Hamming distances are exactly the color class sizes.
