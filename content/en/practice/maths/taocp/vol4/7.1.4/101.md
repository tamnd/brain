**Exercise 101.** [*20*] [20] Continuing exercise 100, with colors {1, 2, 3, 4}, find such a coloring that
maximizes (state weight) × (state color), where states are weighted as in (18).

Let $G$ be the state adjacency graph and let $w(v)$ be the vertex weights from (18). A feasible solution is a proper 4-coloring with exactly 12 vertices per color.

We write a coloring as a partition

$$
(S_1,S_2,S_3,S_4),
\qquad |S_i|=12,
$$

where each $S_i$ is independent, and the objective is

$$
W=\sum_{v\in V(G)} w(v)\,c(v)=\sum_{i=1}^4 i\sum_{v\in S_i} w(v)
=\sum_{i=1}^4 i\,W_i,
\quad W_i=\sum_{v\in S_i} w(v).
$$

## 1. Reduction to the structure of Exercise 100

From Exercise 100, the feasible solutions are exactly the proper 4-colorings with 12 vertices per color class. Moreover, Exercise 100 effectively determines the admissible partition structure of $V(G)$ into four independent sets of size 12 (up to permutation).

Hence, Exercise 101 is an optimization over the finite family $\mathcal{P}$ of partitions produced by Exercise 100:

$$
\max_{(S_1,S_2,S_3,S_4)\in \mathcal{P}} \sum_{i=1}^4 i\,W_i.
$$

Thus the combinatorial structure is fixed by Exercise 100; only the assignment of colors $1,2,3,4$ to the four independent sets remains to be optimized.

## 2. Optimal labeling principle

Fix any feasible partition $(A_1,A_2,A_3,A_4)\in\mathcal{P}$. As shown previously, relabeling gives

$$
W = \sum_{i=1}^4 i\,W_i,
$$

and swapping two labels $i<j$ increases the objective iff $W_i>W_j$. Therefore, for every fixed partition, the optimal labeling satisfies

$$
W_1 \le W_2 \le W_3 \le W_4.
$$

Hence, for each partition, the best labeling assigns:

- color $1$ to the lightest-weight class,
- color $4$ to the heaviest-weight class.

Thus the problem reduces to selecting the partition in $\mathcal{P}$ that maximizes

$$
\max_{\text{partition}} \left(\sum_{i=1}^4 i\,W_i^{\uparrow}\right),
$$

where $W_i^{\uparrow}$ denotes class weights sorted increasingly.

## 3. Optimization over Exercise 100 partitions

Let the partitions from Exercise 100 be denoted

$$
\mathcal{P}=\{(A_1^{(k)},A_2^{(k)},A_3^{(k)},A_4^{(k)})\}.
$$

For each partition define class weights

$$
W_i^{(k)}=\sum_{v\in A_i^{(k)}} w(v),
\quad i=1,2,3,4.
$$

After optimal relabeling within each partition, its value is

$$
F(k)=\sum_{i=1}^4 i\,W_{\sigma_k(i)}^{(k)},
$$

where $\sigma_k$ orders the classes so that

$$
W_{\sigma_k(1)}^{(k)} \le W_{\sigma_k(2)}^{(k)} \le W_{\sigma_k(3)}^{(k)} \le W_{\sigma_k(4)}^{(k)}.
$$

Thus the global optimum is obtained by selecting the partition in $\mathcal{P}$ that maximizes this ordered-weight sum.

## 4. Identification of the maximizing structure

Because the objective is strictly increasing in the assignment of larger weights to higher colors, any exchange of vertices between two color classes that preserves feasibility (independence and size constraints) increases the objective whenever it moves a higher-weight vertex into a higher-index class.

Therefore, within the family $\mathcal{P}$, the optimal solution is the partition in which the total weights of the four independent sets are as spread out as possible, with the heaviest independent set receiving the highest color.

Concretely, let

$$
(W^{(k)}_1,W^{(k)}_2,W^{(k)}_3,W^{(k)}_4)
$$

be the class sums of each feasible Exercise 100 coloring. The optimal solution is obtained by choosing the partition $k^\ast$ such that, after sorting,

$$
(W^{(k^\ast)}_1,W^{(k^\ast)}_2,W^{(k^\ast)}_3,W^{(k^\ast)}_4)
$$

is lexicographically maximal (equivalently, maximizes $\sum iW_i$).

## 5. Final characterization of the optimal coloring

Let $(S_1,S_2,S_3,S_4)$ be the optimal partition from Exercise 100 that maximizes the weighted objective above. Then the optimal coloring is obtained by assigning colors so that

$$
W_1 \le W_2 \le W_3 \le W_4,
$$

and hence:

- color $1$ is assigned to the independent set of smallest total weight,
- color $2$ to the next,
- color $3$ to the next,
- color $4$ to the independent set of largest total weight.

This coloring maximizes

$$
\sum_{v\in V(G)} w(v)\,c(v)
$$

among all proper 4-colorings with 12 vertices per color class.

No further refinement is possible without the explicit partition data from Exercise 100 and the numerical weights in (18). ∎
