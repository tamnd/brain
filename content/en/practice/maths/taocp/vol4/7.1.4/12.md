### (a) Kernels and maximal independent sets in ordinary graphs

Let $G=(V,E)$ be an ordinary (undirected) graph, viewed as a digraph in which

$$
u \to v \;\Longleftrightarrow\; v \to u.
$$

A set $K \subseteq V$ is a kernel if:

1. $v \in K \Rightarrow$ there is no arc $v \to u$ with $u \in K$;
2. $v \notin K \Rightarrow$ there exists $u \in K$ such that $v \to u$.

#### Kernel $\Rightarrow$ maximal independent set

If $u,v \in K$ and $u \neq v$, then by condition (1), neither $u \to v$ nor $v \to u$ holds. Hence $u$ and $v$ are nonadjacent, so $K$ is independent.

Now let $v \notin K$. By condition (2), there exists $u \in K$ with $v \to u$. By symmetry of an undirected graph, this means $u$ is adjacent to $v$. Therefore adding $v$ to $K$ would destroy independence. Hence $K$ is maximal.

#### Maximal independent set $\Rightarrow$ kernel

Let $K$ be a maximal independent set.

If $v \in K$, then no neighbor of $v$ lies in $K$, hence no arc from $v$ enters $K$, so condition (1) holds.

If $v \notin K$, maximality implies $K \cup \{v\}$ is not independent. Hence $v$ is adjacent to some $u \in K$, i.e. $v \to u$. Thus condition (2) holds.

Therefore kernels coincide with maximal independent sets.

### (b) Kernels of the oriented cycle $\vec{C}_n$

Let the vertices be $v_1,\dots,v_n$ with arcs

$$
v_i \to v_{i+1} \quad (1 \le i < n), \qquad v_n \to v_1.
$$

Let $K$ be a kernel.

#### Forced alternation

- If $v_i \in K$, then $v_i \to v_{i+1}$ forbids $v_{i+1} \in K$.
- If $v_i \notin K$, then by kernel condition there must exist $u \in K$ with $v_i \to u$. The only such $u$ is $v_{i+1}$, so $v_{i+1} \in K$.

Hence

$$
v_{i+1} \in K \;\Longleftrightarrow\; v_i \notin K.
$$

#### Even and odd cases

Iterating around the cycle gives:

$$
v_1 \in K \;\Longleftrightarrow\; v_1 \notin K \quad \text{if } n \text{ is odd},
$$

which is impossible. Hence no kernel exists for odd $n$.

If $n$ is even, consistency holds, and the membership alternates around the cycle. There are exactly two kernels:

$$
K_{\text{odd}} = \{v_i : i \text{ odd}\}, \qquad
K_{\text{even}} = \{v_i : i \text{ even}\}.
$$

### (c) Acyclic digraphs have a unique kernel

Let $G=(V,E)$ be an acyclic digraph. Since $G$ is a DAG, there exists a topological ordering $v_1,\dots,v_n$ such that every arc goes from lower to higher index:

$$
v_i \to v_j \;\Rightarrow\; i < j.
$$

We construct a set $K$ by processing vertices in reverse topological order.

#### Step 1: Key equivalence

We prove that a kernel must satisfy, for every vertex $v$,

$$
v \in K \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \text{no successor of } v \text{ lies in } K.
\tag{★}
$$

- If $v \in K$, then by the kernel condition there is no arc $v \to u$ with $u \in K$. Hence no successor of $v$ lies in $K$.
- If no successor of $v$ lies in $K$, then for every $u \notin K$ with $v \to u$ impossible. Thus the only way to satisfy kernel condition (2) would fail unless $v \in K$. More directly: if $v \notin K$, kernel condition (2) requires a successor $u \in K$, contradicting the assumption. Hence $v \in K$.

This proves (★) as an equivalence.

#### Step 2: Well-founded construction

Because all successors of $v_i$ have larger indices, (★) determines membership from already processed vertices.

Define $K$ inductively in reverse topological order:

For $v_n, v_{n-1}, \dots, v_1$, set

$$
v_i \in K \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \text{no arc } v_i \to v_j \text{ with } j>i \text{ and } v_j \in K.
$$

This is well-defined since all $v_j$ with $j>i$ have already been assigned.

#### Step 3: $K$ is a kernel

We verify both kernel conditions.

**(1)** If $v \in K$, then by definition no successor of $v$ lies in $K$, hence there is no arc from $v$ into $K$.

**(2)** If $v \notin K$, then by the defining rule there exists a successor $u$ of $v$ such that $u \in K$. Hence $v \to u$ with $u \in K$.

Thus $K$ is a kernel.

#### Step 4: Uniqueness

Let $K'$ be any kernel. We prove $K'=K$ by reverse topological induction.

Assume $v_{i+1},\dots,v_n$ already agree in both sets. Then membership of $v_i$ is forced by condition (2):

- If some successor of $v_i$ lies in $K'$, then kernel condition (1) forces $v_i \notin K'$.
- If no successor lies in $K'$, then kernel condition (2) forces $v_i \in K'$.

This is exactly the same rule used to define $K$, so $v_i \in K' \iff v_i \in K$.

Hence $K'=K$, proving uniqueness.

### Conclusion

- (a) Kernels in undirected graphs are exactly maximal independent sets.
- (b) The oriented cycle $\vec{C}_n$ has no kernel for odd $n$, and exactly two kernels for even $n$.
- (c) Every acyclic digraph has a unique kernel, constructible by reverse topological order.
