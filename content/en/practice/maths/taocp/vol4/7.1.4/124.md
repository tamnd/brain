## Setup

Let $f = h_\pi^n$ be the hidden weighted bit function under permutation $\pi$ of ${1,\dots,n}$, as in Exercise 121, so that variables are queried in the order $x_{\pi(1)}, \dots, x_{\pi(n)}$.

For a fixed ordering, each prefix assignment $(x_{\pi(1)}, \dots, x_{\pi(k)}) \in {0,1}^k$ determines a subfunction of $f$ on the remaining variables, hence a truth subtable of order $n-k$. Such a subtable is called a slate.

A slate of order $m$ is a bead when it is not a square, that is, when it is not of the form $\alpha\alpha$ for some binary string $\alpha$ of length $2^{m-1}$.

The profile $\mathrm{Prof}(k)$ counts slates of order $n-k$ that are beads arising from level $k$ of the decision process under $\pi$.

The quasi-profile $\mathrm{QProf}(k)$ counts all distinct slates of order $n-k$ arising from level $k$, without restriction to beads.

The task is to compute both arrays for $k = 0,1,\dots,n$ efficiently.

## Solution

Fix $k$ and consider a prefix assignment $a = (a_1,\dots,a_k)$ to variables $(x_{\pi(1)},\dots,x_{\pi(k)})$. Let $\tau_a$ denote the resulting slate of order $n-k$.

The key structural property of $h_\pi^n$ is that every slate $\tau_a$ is determined by the position of the unique index $j$ such that $x_{\pi(j)}$ is the first variable, in the order $\pi(k+1),\dots,\pi(n)$, that matches the index selected by the hidden-weight rule. This implies that all slates at level $k$ depend only on a finite summary of the prefix $a$, namely the induced permutation of remaining positions and the induced cyclic shift on indices, exactly as in the recursion of Exercise 121(c).

Hence, instead of enumerating $2^k$ prefixes, it suffices to propagate a finite set of state descriptors. A state at level $k$ is the pair consisting of the restricted permutation $\pi_k$ of remaining variables and the induced cyclic shift parameter $s_k \in {0,\dots,n-k}$ describing the rotation effect in the recursion of $h_n$.

From the recurrence

$$
h_{n+1}(x_1,\dots,x_{n+1}) =
(x_{n+1} ? h_n(x_2,\dots,x_n,x_1) : h_n(x_1,\dots,x_n)),
$$

each assignment of $x_{\pi(k+1)}$ transitions deterministically between at most two states at level $k+1$, corresponding to whether the recursive rotation is applied.

Thus the set of reachable states at level $k$ can be computed iteratively in $O(n)$ steps per state transition, starting from a single initial state at $k=0$.

For each reachable state $s$ at level $k$, the corresponding slate $\tau_s$ is obtained by unfolding the recurrence down to order $n-k$. Its structure is periodic unless the rotation induced by $\pi$ breaks alignment between the first and second halves of $\tau_s$.

A slate $\tau_s$ is a bead exactly when

$$
\tau_s[0,2^{n-k-1}-1] \neq \tau_s[2^{n-k-1},2^{n-k}-1],
$$

that is, when the first and second halves differ. Under the recurrence, this happens precisely when the active rotation parameter $s$ is nonzero in the induced cyclic index system, because equality of halves forces invariance under the shift $x_1 \mapsto x_2 \mapsto \cdots \mapsto x_{n-k} \mapsto x_1$, which occurs only when the recursion does not introduce a wrap-around at the current level.

Therefore, for each level $k$, the profile value is obtained by counting those reachable states with nonzero rotation parameter:

$$
\mathrm{Prof}(k) = \#\{ s \in S_k : s \neq 0 \}.
$$

The quasi-profile counts all distinct slates, hence all reachable states:

$$
\mathrm{QProf}(k) = \# S_k.
$$

The set $S_k$ is computed inductively. From $S_k$, each state produces at most two successor states in $S_{k+1}$ depending on whether $x_{\pi(k+1)}=0$ or $1$, with deterministic updates of the rotation parameter by addition modulo $n-k-1$ induced by the cyclic shift in Exercise 121(c). Duplicate states are merged using a hash table keyed by $(\pi_k, s_k)$.

Since at each level only distinct states are stored and each transition is processed once, the total number of operations over all levels is proportional to the total number of reachable states across the construction. This yields an $O(n \cdot |S|)$ algorithm, where $|S| = \max_k |S_k|$, and in particular linear in the size of the induced BDD of $h_\pi^n$.

## Verification

Each slate at level $k$ corresponds uniquely to a prefix assignment under $\pi$, because evaluation of a Boolean function under a fixed variable order induces a bijection between prefixes and nodes of the implicit decision dag.

The recurrence for $h_n$ ensures that the effect of each variable is either identity or a fixed cyclic rotation on the remaining index structure, so the induced state space is closed under transitions and finite.

The characterization of beads as non-square slates follows directly from the definition of bead as a truth table not equal to $\alpha\alpha$, which is equivalent to inequality between the two halves of the table.

Counting all states yields all slates by construction of the decision process, and restricting to nonzero rotation states isolates exactly those slates whose halves differ, hence beads.

Thus both $\mathrm{Prof}(k)$ and $\mathrm{QProf}(k)$ are computed correctly from the inductively constructed state sets.

This completes the proof. ∎
