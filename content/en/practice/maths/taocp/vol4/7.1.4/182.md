[Section 7.1.4: Binary Decision Diagrams](../)

**Exercise 182.** [*M38*] [M38] (I. Wegener.) Does Bmin(Ln,n) grow exponentially as n →∞?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m33s  

---

The key error in the proposed solution is not only a miscount of residual functions, but a misinterpretation of how the partial sum interacts with the remaining bits under the fixed variable ordering. Once the arithmetic is written correctly, the dependence on the prefix collapses to a constant-size state space, so no exponential blow-up occurs.

We now restart from the definition and give a correct analysis.

## 1. The function and ordering

We are given the variable order

$$
x_1 < \cdots < x_n < y_1 < \cdots < y_n.
$$

Define

$$
X = \sum_{i=1}^n x_i 2^{i-1}, \qquad Y = \sum_{i=1}^n y_i 2^{i-1}.
$$

The function $L_{n,n}(X,Y)$ is the most significant bit of $X+Y$, hence

$$
L_{n,n}(X,Y)=1 \iff X+Y \ge 2^n.
$$

## 2. Correct decomposition after fixing a prefix

Fix $k$ with $0 \le k \le n$, and assign values to

$$
x_1,\dots,x_k,y_1,\dots,y_k.
$$

Split

$$
X = X_k + 2^k X', \qquad Y = Y_k + 2^k Y',
$$

where

$$
X_k, Y_k \in \{0,\dots,2^k-1\}, \qquad X',Y' \in \{0,\dots,2^{n-k}-1\}.
$$

Then

$$
X+Y = (X_k+Y_k) + 2^k(X'+Y').
$$

Write

$$
S_k = X_k+Y_k.
$$

Now crucially,

$$
0 \le S_k \le 2^{k+1}-2.
$$

Decompose uniquely:

$$
S_k = 2^k c_k + r_k,
$$

where

$$
c_k \in \{0,1\}, \qquad 0 \le r_k < 2^k.
$$

Substitute:

$$
X+Y = 2^k(X'+Y'+c_k) + r_k.
$$

## 3. Correct form of the condition

We require

$$
X+Y \ge 2^n.
$$

Divide by $2^k$:

$$
X'+Y'+c_k + \frac{r_k}{2^k} \ge 2^{n-k}.
$$

Now observe:

- The left-hand side is an integer plus a fraction strictly less than $1$,
- since $0 \le r_k < 2^k$, we have $0 \le \frac{r_k}{2^k} < 1$.

Hence the inequality is equivalent to:

### Case analysis

- If $r_k = 0$, then

$$
X'+Y'+c_k \ge 2^{n-k}.
$$
- If $r_k > 0$, then

$$
X'+Y'+c_k \ge 2^{n-k}-1.
$$

This is the crucial simplification.

## 4. Key collapse of state space

For fixed $k$, the residual function depends only on:

- $c_k \in \{0,1\}$,
- whether $r_k = 0$ or $r_k > 0$.

Thus there are at most

$$
2 \times 2 = 4
$$

distinct residual Boolean functions at level $k$, regardless of the value of $k$.

This completely invalidates the idea that there are $2^k$ distinct nodes.

## 5. Consequence for the BDD size

In an ordered BDD, nodes at level $k$ correspond exactly to distinct residual subfunctions on the remaining variables. Hence:

- Each level contains $O(1)$ distinct nodes,
- There are $2n$ levels total.

Therefore the total number of nodes satisfies

$$
B_{\min}(L_{n,n}) = O(n).
$$

A matching lower bound $B_{\min}(L_{n,n}) = \Omega(n)$ follows since each variable must be tested in this ordering (the function depends on all bits of both inputs under the restriction).

Thus:

$$
B_{\min}(L_{n,n}) = \Theta(n).
$$

## 6. Final conclusion

The function $L_{n,n}$ under the ordering

$$
x_1 < \cdots < x_n < y_1 < \cdots < y_n
$$

does **not** exhibit exponential BDD growth.

Instead, the carry structure collapses to a constant number of states per level, so the reduced ordered BDD has linear size.

$$
\boxed{\text{No, } B_{\min}(L_{n,n}) \text{ does not grow exponentially; it is } \Theta(n).}
$$

## 7. Diagnosis of the original error (summary)

The flawed proof failed because it treated the fractional term $r_k/2^k$ as if it induced many distinct thresholds. In reality, since the suffix sum is integer-valued, that fractional part can only shift the threshold by at most $1$, collapsing all $2^k$ cases into a constant number of equivalence classes.
