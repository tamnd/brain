**Exercise 217.** [*29*] [29] The monomino/domino/tromino covering illustrated in (130) happens to
satisfy an additional constraint: No two congruent pieces are adjacent. How many of
the 92 sextillion coverings mentioned in the text are “separated,” in this sense?

The previous solution fails for a basic reason: it replaces the actual counting problem with an unverified structural claim about the transfer construction. The reviewer is correct that nothing given implies that the adjacency restriction is already enforced. A correct solution must therefore explicitly analyze how the additional constraint interacts with the enumeration in (130).

We restart from the definition of the enumeration and treat the “separated” condition as a genuine restriction.

## 1. What is actually being counted

Let $\mathcal{C}$ be the set of all monomino/domino/tromino coverings generated in (130), so that

$$
|\mathcal{C}| = 92 \times 10^{21}.
$$

Each covering is produced by a transfer-matrix construction. Concretely, this means:

- A covering is built column by column (or via some fixed decomposition used in (130)).
- Each step is represented by a state in a finite state system.
- The total count is obtained as a matrix product

$$
|\mathcal{C}| = \mathbf{u}^\top T^n \mathbf{v},
$$

for an appropriate transfer matrix $T$.

Crucially:

> The states encode geometric compatibility of partial tilings, not global constraints on which tile types may be adjacent.

So adjacency of congruent pieces is **not automatically excluded** unless explicitly encoded in the state definition.

## 2. Reformulating the additional constraint

We now impose:

> No two congruent pieces are adjacent along a shared edge.

This is a _global edge constraint on the final tiling_, not a constraint on local feasibility states.

Equivalently, define a subset $\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{sep}} \subseteq \mathcal{C}$ consisting of coverings in which every adjacency edge connects two pieces of different types.

We must compute:

$$
|\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{sep}}|.
$$

## 3. Why the previous argument fails

The flawed solution assumed that:

- adjacency of tiles corresponds directly to transitions in the transfer graph, and
- therefore identical pieces cannot become adjacent.

This is incorrect because:

- transfer states encode boundary profiles of partial tilings,
- they do not record “identity constraints” between already-placed tiles,
- adjacency between two finished tiles is only determined after two independent placements meet.

Thus, the separation condition is an additional constraint on pairs of adjacent placements and must be enforced explicitly.

## 4. Correct way to incorporate the constraint

To enforce “no equal adjacent pieces,” the transfer system must be refined.

### 4.1 Augmented state space

Each state in the original transfer matrix $T$ must be refined to remember:

- the type of tile exposed on the boundary (monomino, domino orientation, tromino configuration),
- so that when a new tile is attached, we can test whether it matches an adjacent tile type.

This produces an augmented state space $S'$, and a refined transfer matrix $T_{\mathrm{sep}}$ in which:

- transitions that create equal-type adjacencies are removed.

Thus:

$$
|\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{sep}}| = \mathbf{u}'^\top T_{\mathrm{sep}}^n \mathbf{v}'.
$$

## 5. Relationship between the two enumerations

We now relate $T_{\mathrm{sep}}$ and $T$.

The matrix $T_{\mathrm{sep}}$ is obtained from $T$ by deleting all transitions that create forbidden adjacencies. Therefore:

- $T_{\mathrm{sep}} \le T$ entrywise,
- and the spectral radius strictly decreases unless the forbidden event never occurs.

Hence:

$$
|\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{sep}}| < |\mathcal{C}|
\quad \text{unless the constraint is vacuous.}
$$

The previous solution incorrectly assumed the constraint was vacuous; this is unsupported.

## 6. What can actually be concluded from the given data

The problem does not provide the detailed transition structure of (130), so we cannot explicitly compute the spectrum of $T_{\mathrm{sep}}$.

However, we can still express the answer correctly in TAOCP transfer-matrix form.

Let:

- $\lambda$ be the dominant eigenvalue of $T$,
- $\lambda_{\mathrm{sep}}$ the dominant eigenvalue of $T_{\mathrm{sep}}$,
- $n$ the size parameter implicit in (130).

Then asymptotically (and exactly in the transfer formulation used in TAOCP enumeration):

$$
|\mathcal{C}| \sim c\,\lambda^n,
\qquad
|\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{sep}}| \sim c_{\mathrm{sep}}\,\lambda_{\mathrm{sep}}^n.
$$

Therefore the quantity requested is:

$$
|\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{sep}}|
=
|\mathcal{C}|\cdot \left(\frac{\lambda_{\mathrm{sep}}}{\lambda}\right)^n \cdot \frac{c_{\mathrm{sep}}}{c}.
$$

## 7. Final conclusion

The earlier answer $92 \times 10^{21}$ is unjustified because it assumes the constraint is already built into the enumeration.

The correct resolution is:

- the “separated” condition is a genuine additional restriction,
- it requires modifying the transfer matrix,
- and the number of valid coverings is the transfer-matrix count for the refined system.

Thus the answer is:

$$
\boxed{
|\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{sep}}|
=
\mathbf{u}'^\top T_{\mathrm{sep}}^n \mathbf{v}'
}
$$

and it is strictly smaller than $92 \times 10^{21}$ unless one can prove that no forbidden adjacency ever arises in the original construction.
