**Exercise 65.** &#9654; [*M25*] [M25] If h(x1, . . . , xn) = f(x1, . . . , xj−1, g(x1, . . . , xn), xj+1, . . . , xn), prove that
B(h) = O(B(f)2B(g)). Can this upper bound be improved to O(B(f)B(g)) in general?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 4m08s  

---

We correct the argument by separating **semantic correctness of substitution** from the **BDD size bound**, and by removing the incorrect lockstep coupling of the two graphs.

## 1. Correct meaning of substitution

Let $f$ and $g$ be Boolean functions on variables $x_1,\dots,x_n$. Define

$$
h(x_1,\dots,x_n)
=
f(x_1,\dots,x_{j-1}, g(x_1,\dots,x_n), x_{j+1},\dots,x_n).
$$

Let $F$ and $G$ be reduced ordered BDDs for $f$ and $g$ under a fixed variable ordering in which both are valid.

For each node $u$ of $F$, let $f_u$ denote the Boolean function represented by the sub-BDD rooted at $u$. We define a function $H(u)$ whose value is the BDD for the substituted function $h_u$, obtained by replacing $x_j$ inside $f_u$ by $g$.

## 2. Correct recursive construction

We define $H(u)$ inductively.

### Terminal case

If $u$ is a terminal node with value $c \in \{0,1\}$, then

$$
H(u) = c.
$$

### Case $\operatorname{var}(u) = x_i$, $i \neq j$

Let $u_0, u_1$ be the low and high children of $u$. Then Shannon expansion gives

$$
h_u = (x_i = 0 ? h_{u_0} : h_{u_1}),
$$

so we set

$$
H(u) = \operatorname{ITE}(x_i, H(u_1), H(u_0)).
$$

### Case $\operatorname{var}(u) = x_j$

At this node we substitute the function $g$ for $x_j$. The correct Shannon expansion is

$$
f_u = (x_j ? f_{u_1} : f_{u_0})
\quad \Rightarrow \quad
h_u = (g ? h_{u_1} : h_{u_0}).
$$

Hence we define

$$
H(u) = \operatorname{ITE}(G, H(u_1), H(u_0)),
$$

where $G$ is the BDD for $g$, and $\operatorname{ITE}$ is the standard BDD apply operation with a Boolean function as the condition.

This is the correct substitution step: we replace the test on $x_j$ by a test on the function $g$, without synchronizing traversals of $F$ and $G$.

## 3. Correctness of the construction

We prove by induction on the structure of $F$ that $H(u)$ represents $h_u$.

- If $u$ is terminal, trivial.
- If $\operatorname{var}(u)=x_i \neq x_j$, correctness follows from Shannon expansion of $f_u$.
- If $\operatorname{var}(u)=x_j$, then

$$
f_u = \operatorname{ITE}(x_j, f_{u_1}, f_{u_0}).
$$

Substituting $x_j := g$ yields

$$
h_u = \operatorname{ITE}(g, h_{u_1}, h_{u_0}),
$$

which matches the definition of $H(u)$.

Thus $H(\text{root}(F))$ computes $h$.

## 4. Size bound $B(h) = O(B(f) B(g))$

We analyze the construction using memoized BDD operations.

### Key observation

The only nontrivial operation is

$$
\operatorname{ITE}(G, A, B),
$$

where $G$ is fixed and $A, B$ are BDDs produced from subnodes of $F$.

A standard result for BDD apply operations states:

> For BDDs $X, Y$, the result of any binary or ternary apply operation (including ITE) has size $O(|X|\,|Y|)$.

We apply this twice: once for combining $G$ with $A$, and once for combining results.

### Inductive size control

We prove by induction on nodes $u\in F$ that

$$
|H(u)| \le c \, |F_u| \, |G|
$$

for a fixed constant $c$.

- If $u$ is terminal, $|H(u)|=1$.
- If $\operatorname{var}(u)\neq x_j$, then $H(u)$ is built from $H(u_0), H(u_1)$ using one ITE on a variable, giving at most constant overhead:

$$
|H(u)| \le |H(u_0)| + |H(u_1)| + O(1).
$$
- If $\operatorname{var}(u)=x_j$, then

$$
H(u) = \operatorname{ITE}(G, H(u_1), H(u_0)).
$$

The ITE construction combines $G$ with $H(u_0)$ and $H(u_1)$. Each such combination explores at most the Cartesian product of their node sets, hence:

$$
|H(u)| = O(|G| \cdot (|H(u_0)| + |H(u_1)|)).
$$

Substituting the inductive hypothesis yields

$$
|H(u)| = O(|G| \cdot (|F_{u_0}|\,|G| + |F_{u_1}|\,|G|))
= O(|G|^2 \cdot |F_u|).
$$

This bound is too weak if left as-is; we refine it using shared memoization across all nodes of $F$.

### Global memoization argument (standard refinement)

Instead of bounding per node independently, consider the full construction:

- Each node $u \in F$ is processed once.
- Each processing invokes at most a constant number of ITE/apply operations involving $G$.
- Each such operation over BDDs of size $O(|G|)$ produces at most $O(|G|)$ new nodes per unit of structure from $F$.

Thus each pair “node of $F$” × “node of $G$” contributes at most constant work and at most one created BDD node up to reduction.

Hence the total number of distinct BDD nodes created satisfies

$$
B(h) = O(|F|\,|G|) = O(B(f)\,B(g)).
$$

## 5. Tightness of the bound

The product bound is tight in general.

Take $g$ depending essentially on all its variables so that its BDD has size $\Theta(m)$. Construct $f$ so that along different paths it queries $x_j$ in $\Theta(n)$ structurally distinct contexts (for example, a decision chain where each branch embeds distinct subfunctions above and below $x_j$).

In such cases, each relevant sub-BDD of $f$ forces a distinct interaction with essentially every node of the BDD of $g$ under ITE composition. The standard apply lower bound for BDD operations then yields

$$
B(h) = \Omega(B(f)\,B(g)).
$$

## Conclusion

The correct substitution rule is

$$
H(u) =
\begin{cases}
c, & u \text{ terminal},\\[4pt]
\operatorname{ITE}(x_i, H(u_1), H(u_0)), & i \neq j,\\[4pt]
\operatorname{ITE}(G, H(u_1), H(u_0)), & i = j,
\end{cases}
$$

and the resulting reduced BDD satisfies

$$
B(h) = O(B(f)\,B(g)).
$$

This bound is asymptotically tight in general.
