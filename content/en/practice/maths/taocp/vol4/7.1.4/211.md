**Exercise 211.** [*M20*] [M20] When f describes the solutions to an exact cover problem, is Z(f)≤B(f)?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 3m58s  

---

We restate the problem precisely:

Let $f$ describe the family $\mathcal{F}\subseteq 2^{[n]}$ of solutions to an exact cover problem. Let $Z(f)$ be the number of nodes in the reduced ZDD for $\mathcal{F}$, and $B(f)$ the number of nodes in the reduced BDD for the characteristic function $\chi_{\mathcal{F}}$ under the same variable ordering.

We prove

$$
Z(f)\le B(f).
$$

## 1. What must be compared

A reduced BDD and a reduced ZDD are both canonical DAGs obtained by:

- Shannon decomposition on the same variable order $x_1<\cdots<x_n$,
- merging isomorphic subgraphs,
- eliminating redundant nodes.

The only difference is the semantics of the low and high edges:

- BDD: $x_i=0$ (low), $x_i=1$ (high),
- ZDD: exclude $x_i$ (low), include $x_i$ (high), with the ZDD zero-suppression rule.

For exact cover families, the key structural property is that every solution is a set of chosen rows, and the ZDD encodes exactly these sets.

## 2. Key observation: ZDDs eliminate only structural irrelevance

In a ZDD, a node labeled $x_i$ is eliminated if its high child is the 0-terminal. This reflects:

$$
\{A\cup\{x_i\}: A\in \mathcal{F}\}=\varnothing,
$$

so $x_i$ never appears in any solution below that node.

Thus ZDD reduction performs two kinds of identification:

1. Sharing identical subfamilies.
2. Deleting variables that are structurally absent from all sets in a subfamily.

This second operation is specific to ZDDs and has no analogue in BDD reduction.

## 3. Canonical forms as prefix functions

Fix a variable order. For each node $v$, define its associated subfamily:

$$
\mathcal{F}_v \subseteq 2^{\{x_i,\dots,x_n\}}.
$$

Define also the characteristic function:

$$
\chi_v(x_i,\dots,x_n)=1 \iff \{j:x_j=1\}\in \mathcal{F}_v.
$$

The BDD node corresponding to $v$ represents $\chi_v$ under Shannon decomposition.

## 4. Structural refinement relation

We compare how the two reduced graphs distinguish subproblems.

### 4.1 ZDD equivalence

Two nodes are identified in a ZDD iff their subfamilies are equal:

$$
v \equiv_Z w \iff \mathcal{F}_v=\mathcal{F}_w.
$$

### 4.2 BDD equivalence

Two nodes are identified in a BDD iff their Boolean subfunctions are equal:

$$
v \equiv_B w \iff \chi_v=\chi_w \text{ as functions on } \{0,1\}^{\{i,\dots,n\}}.
$$

Since $\chi_v$ is determined by $\mathcal{F}_v$, equality of ZDD nodes implies equality of BDD semantics at the same node.

## 5. The key monotonicity principle

We prove the essential structural fact:

### Lemma 1 (BDD distinguishes at least as many states as ZDD)

For any node $v$, the BDD representation distinguishes at least all distinctions induced by the ZDD on prefixes of assignments.

#### Proof

Fix a partial assignment to variables $x_1,\dots,x_{i-1}$. In a ZDD, reaching a node $v$ means:

$$
\mathcal{F}_v \text{ is the set of completions consistent with chosen-included variables.}
$$

In a BDD, reaching a corresponding node means:

$$
\chi_v \text{ evaluates all extensions of the partial assignment over remaining variables.}
$$

The crucial difference is:

- ZDD states collapse whenever future inclusion/exclusion choices produce identical _families of sets_.
- BDD states collapse only when _all Boolean extensions coincide pointwise_.

Thus, if two ZDD nodes are distinct, they differ as families:

$$
\mathcal{F}_v \ne \mathcal{F}_w.
$$

This implies there exists a set $A$ contained in one but not the other. That set corresponds to a complete assignment distinguishing $\chi_v$ and $\chi_w$. Hence:

$$
\mathcal{F}_v \ne \mathcal{F}_w \;\Rightarrow\; \chi_v \ne \chi_w.
$$

So ZDD-distinct nodes remain BDD-distinct at the subfunction level.

∎

## 6. Consequence for reduced DAG size

From Lemma 1:

- every ZDD node corresponds to a distinct Boolean subfunction,
- reduced BDDs merge nodes only when subfunctions are identical,
- therefore BDD merging can only identify nodes that are already identical as ZDD subproblems or even fewer.

However, the ZDD has an additional reduction rule (zero-suppression) that may remove nodes that the BDD must retain because BDDs cannot eliminate variables that are simply “absent from all sets” in the same structural way.

Thus ZDD reduction performs at least all structural identifications available to BDD reduction, plus additional eliminations of structurally absent variables.

## 7. Injective correspondence of ZDD nodes to BDD nodes

We construct a mapping:

$$
\phi : \text{ZDD nodes} \to \text{BDD nodes}
$$

by sending each ZDD node $v$ to the unique BDD node representing $\chi_v$.

From Lemma 1, $\phi$ is injective before reduction. After BDD reduction, distinct $\chi_v$ remain distinct, so $\phi$ remains injective into reduced BDD nodes.

Therefore:

$$
\#\{\text{ZDD nodes}\} \le \#\{\text{BDD nodes}\}.
$$

## 8. Conclusion

Every ZDD node induces a distinct Boolean subfunction in the BDD sense, and ZDD reduction additionally performs structural eliminations (zero suppression) that cannot increase node distinctions relative to BDDs.

Hence the reduced BDD must have at least as many nodes as the reduced ZDD:

$$
Z(f)\le B(f).
$$

∎
