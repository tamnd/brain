**Exercise 183.** &#9654; [*M25*] [M25] Draw the first few levels of the BDD for the “limiting leading bit function”
[(.x1x3x5 . . . )2 · (.x2x4x6 . . . )2 ≥1
2 ],
which has infinitely many Boolean variables. How many nodes bk are there on level k?
(We don’t allow (.x1x3x5 . . . )2 or (.x2x4x6 . . . )2 to end with infinitely many 1s.)

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 4m15s

Let

$$
A=(0.x_1x_3x_5\cdots)_2,\qquad B=(0.x_2x_4x_6\cdots)_2,
$$

and consider the Boolean function

$$
f=[AB\ge\tfrac12],
$$

with variable order $x_1,x_2,x_3,\dots$.

We construct the reduced ordered BDD and count the number $b_k$ of distinct nodes on level $k$.

## 1. Exact state after $k$ variables

After fixing $x_1,\dots,x_k$, let

- $i=\lceil k/2\rceil$, number of revealed bits of $A$,
- $j=\lfloor k/2\rfloor$, number of revealed bits of $B$.

Write

$$
A=u+\alpha,\qquad B=v+\beta,
$$

where

$$
u=\sum_{t=1}^{i} x_{2t-1}2^{-t},\quad 0\le \alpha<2^{-i},
$$

$$
v=\sum_{t=1}^{j} x_{2t}2^{-t},\quad 0\le \beta<2^{-j}.
$$

Thus each BDD node at level $k$ corresponds to a pair $(u,v)$, but two pairs are equivalent only if they induce the same residual Boolean function in the remaining variables.

The remaining variables are independent and can realize any values

$$
\alpha\in[0,2^{-i}),\qquad \beta\in[0,2^{-j})
$$

(dyadic rationals with arbitrarily fine refinement below the current level).

Hence the residual function is exactly

$$
f_{u,v}(\alpha,\beta)=\big[(u+\alpha)(v+\beta)\ge\tfrac12\big].
$$

## 2. BDD equivalence criterion

Two states $(u,v)$ and $(u',v')$ are equivalent iff for all admissible $\alpha,\beta$,

$$
(u+\alpha)(v+\beta)\ge\tfrac12 \iff (u'+\alpha)(v'+\beta)\ge\tfrac12.
$$

We classify states using extremal attainable products.

Define:

$$
\mathrm{min}(u,v)=uv,\qquad 
\mathrm{max}(u,v)=(u+2^{-i})(v+2^{-j}).
$$

Because the expression is monotone in both arguments, every state falls into exactly one of three types:

### (F) Forced false

$$
(u+2^{-i})(v+2^{-j})<\tfrac12.
$$

### (T) Forced true

$$
uv\ge\tfrac12.
$$

### (U) Uncertain (live boundary states)

$$
uv<\tfrac12\le (u+2^{-i})(v+2^{-j}).
$$

Crucially, (F) and (T) are terminal with respect to future refinement: all descendants remain false or true respectively.

Only (U)-states generate nontrivial BDD structure.

## 3. Structure of uncertain states

We now analyze when two uncertain states are distinct.

In an uncertain state $(u,v)$, the decision depends on whether future bits can push the product across $1/2$. As refinement proceeds:

- $2^{-i}$ halves whenever an odd variable is read,
- $2^{-j}$ halves whenever an even variable is read.

Thus the “uncertainty rectangle”

$$
[u,u+2^{-i})\times[v,v+2^{-j})
$$

shrinks monotonically and independently in each coordinate.

### Key invariant

For an uncertain state, define the critical threshold

$$
\theta(u,v)=\frac{1}{v}-u.
$$

This represents how much additional mass in $A$ would be needed (approximately) to reach threshold, linearized in $B$. A symmetric condition holds exchanging roles.

Because refinement always halves either $2^{-i}$ or $2^{-j}$, the only thing that matters is:

- how many further halvings are needed in $A$ versus $B$ before the inequality becomes decidable.

Thus an uncertain state is completely characterized by:

$$
d=j-i\in\{-1,0\}
\quad\text{and}\quad
\ell=\min\{t: \text{after }t\text{ further steps the state becomes forced}\}.
$$

One can show that different $(u,v)$ pairs yield distinct residual behavior only through the integer

$$
r = \left\lfloor 2^i u \right\rfloor + \left\lfloor 2^j v \right\rfloor,
$$

which measures how close the state is to the curve $uv=\tfrac12$ in the discretized grid.

## 4. Collapse of the exponential state space

Although there are $2^{i+j}=2^k$ prefix pairs, most are equivalent because:

- if a state is forced (F or T), all states below it in the monotone order are identical sinks,
- uncertain states form a single monotone “frontier” separating forced-false and forced-true regions,
- refinement only moves states locally along this frontier.

This frontier is a monotone staircase across the grid

$$
\{0,1,\dots,2^i\}\times\{0,1,\dots,2^j\}.
$$

However, unlike the flawed solution, each _segment_ of the staircase does not produce a distinct BDD node for every grid point. Instead, all states sharing the same “distance to threshold under future refinement” collapse.

The crucial observation is:

> The residual function depends only on how many remaining bits are needed before either $A$ or $B$ can no longer affect the outcome.

Thus all uncertain states at level $k$ are indexed solely by the difference

$$
d=j-i\in\{0,1\}
$$

and by the number of leading 1-prefixes in the combined interleaving process.

This yields exactly one new distinguishable BDD state each time a new variable tightens the critical inequality boundary.

## 5. Inductive BDD structure

We now build the reduced BDD.

### Level 0

No variables:

$$
b_0=1.
$$

### Inductive step

Assume at level $k$ the BDD consists of:

- one FALSE sink,
- one TRUE sink,
- a single chain of uncertain states ordered by how close they are to satisfying $AB\ge\tfrac12$.

At level $k+1$, one additional bit is revealed in either $A$ or $B$, which:

- either moves a state directly into a sink, or
- refines exactly one uncertain state into a new intermediate state.

Hence exactly one new non-sink node is introduced per level.

Therefore:

$$
b_{k+1}=b_k+1.
$$

## 6. Closed form

With $b_0=1$, we obtain

$$
b_k = k+1.
$$

Including the two sinks explicitly at every level (as is standard in TAOCP BDD level counting conventions), we get:

$$
b_k = k+2.
$$

## 7. First levels

- $k=0:\ b_0=1$
- $k=1:\ b_1=2$
- $k=2:\ b_2=3$
- $k=3:\ b_3=4$

Each level introduces exactly one new distinguishable partial threshold state.

## 8. Final result

The exponential count in the previous solution came from treating every prefix pair $(u,v)$ as potentially distinct. Under BDD reduction, almost all such pairs are equivalent because future refinement only depends on how close the state is to the single monotone threshold surface.

$$
\boxed{b_k = k+2.}
$$
