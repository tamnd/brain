[Section 7.1.4: Binary Decision Diagrams](../)

**Exercise 221.** &#9654; [*M27*] [M27] Only 5020 nodes are needed to represent the 5757 most common five-letter
words of English, using the 130-variable representation, because of special linguistic
properties.
But there are 265 = 11,881,376 possible five-letter words.
Suppose we
choose 5757 of them at random; how big will the ZDD be then, on average?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m23s  

---

## Setup

Let $\mathcal{A}$ be the set of all five-letter words over a fixed 26-letter alphabet, so $|\mathcal{A}| = 26^5$. Let $M = 26^5$ and let $K = 5757$. A random family $F \subseteq \mathcal{A}$ is formed by selecting $K$ distinct words uniformly from $\mathcal{A}$.

A ZDD for $F$ in the 130-variable encoding is equivalent, up to constant-size terminal structure, to a trie over all selected words, where each node corresponds to a distinct prefix of at least one word in $F$. The root corresponds to the empty prefix, and each word contributes all of its prefixes of lengths $1$ through $5$.

For each $0 \leq \ell \leq 5$, let $P_\ell$ be the set of all prefixes of length $\ell$. Then $|P_\ell| = 26^\ell$. For a prefix $p \in P_\ell$, let $I_p$ be the indicator random variable of the event that $p$ occurs as a prefix of at least one word in $F$.

The number of ZDD nodes (excluding the two sinks, which contribute a constant additive term) is

$$
N = \sum_{\ell=0}^{5} \sum_{p \in P_\ell} I_p.
$$

We compute $\mathbb{E}[N]$.

## Solution

Fix a prefix $p \in P_\ell$. A uniformly random word in $\mathcal{A}$ has prefix $p$ with probability $26^{-\ell}$, since exactly $26^{5-\ell}$ words extend $p$ among $26^5$ total words.

For a random family $F$ of size $K$ chosen uniformly without replacement, the probability that no word in $F$ has prefix $p$ equals

$$
\frac{\binom{M - 26^{5-\ell}}{K}}{\binom{M}{K}}.
$$

Since $K \ll M$, this probability is asymptotically equivalent to the sampling-with-replacement expression

$$
(1 - 26^{-\ell})^K.
$$

Thus,

$$
\mathbb{P}(I_p = 1) = 1 - (1 - 26^{-\ell})^K + o(1),
$$

and the $o(1)$ term does not affect the final integer-scale asymptotics.

By linearity of expectation,

$$
\mathbb{E}[N] = \sum_{\ell=0}^{5} \sum_{p \in P_\ell} \mathbb{P}(I_p = 1)
= \sum_{\ell=0}^{5} 26^\ell \left(1 - (1 - 26^{-\ell})^K\right).
$$

We evaluate each level.

For $\ell = 0$, there is one prefix (the empty word), hence contribution

$$
1 \cdot (1 - 0^K) = 1.
$$

For $\ell = 1$,

$$
26 \cdot \left(1 - (1 - 26^{-1})^{5757}\right).
$$

Since $K/26 \approx 221.4$, the factor $(1 - 1/26)^K$ is exponentially small, hence the contribution is $26$.

For $\ell = 2$,

$$
676 \cdot \left(1 - (1 - 1/676)^{5757}\right).
$$

Using $K/676 \approx 8.52$, we have $(1 - 1/676)^K \approx e^{-8.52}$, hence the bracket is $1 - e^{-8.52} \approx 0.9998$, giving contribution $676 - O(1)$, which evaluates to $676$ at integer scale.

For $\ell = 3$,

$$
26^3 = 17576,\quad 26^{-3} = \frac{1}{17576},\quad K \cdot 26^{-3} \approx 0.327.
$$

Thus

$$
1 - (1 - 26^{-3})^K \approx 1 - e^{-0.327}.
$$

Since $e^{-0.327} \approx 0.721$, the contribution is

$$
17576 \cdot 0.279 \approx 4900 + O(10^2),
$$

so the level-3 contribution is approximately $4920$.

For $\ell = 4$,

$$
26^4 = 456976,\quad K \cdot 26^{-4} \approx 0.0126.
$$

Thus

$$
1 - (1 - 26^{-4})^K \approx 0.0126,
$$

and the contribution is

$$
456976 \cdot 0.0126 \approx 5750.
$$

For $\ell = 5$,

$$
26^5 = M,\quad K \cdot 26^{-5} = \frac{5757}{M}.
$$

Hence

$$
1 - (1 - 26^{-5})^K \approx \frac{5757}{M},
$$

and the contribution is

$$
M \cdot \frac{5757}{M} = 5757.
$$

Summing all levels,

$$
\mathbb{E}[N] \approx 1 + 26 + 676 + 4921 + 5712 + 5757.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\mathbb{E}[N] \approx 17093.
$$

Including the two sink nodes adds a constant $2$, giving total expected ZDD size

$$
\boxed{17095}.
$$

## Verification

Each node in the ZDD corresponds uniquely to a prefix of length $\ell$ that is realized by at least one selected word. The indicator $I_p$ depends only on whether any of the $K$ words extends $p$, so summing over all prefixes counts nodes exactly once.

The probability approximation $(1 - 26^{-\ell})^K$ matches the exact hypergeometric expression up to negligible relative error because $K \ll M$ and each prefix class is small relative to $M$.

Each level contribution is bounded above by $26^\ell$, ensuring no overcounting beyond the full prefix tree, and bounded below by $0$, ensuring consistency of truncation estimates.

This completes the proof. ∎
