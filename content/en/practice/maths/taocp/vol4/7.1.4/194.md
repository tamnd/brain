**Exercise 194.** [*M25*] [M25] How many f(x1, . . . , xn) have the z-profile (1, . . . , 1)? (See exercise 174.)

The original solution fails because it assumes an artificial linear chain and then performs a local edge count. The correct approach must start from what the z-profile actually guarantees and derive the global structure without imposing a chain.

## 1. What the condition actually means

Let $f(x_1,\ldots,x_n)$ be represented by a reduced ZDD with z-profile $(1,\ldots,1)$.

From Exercise 174, the z-profile condition means:

For each $k\in\{1,\ldots,n\}$, there is exactly one **reachable Z-node** labeled $x_k$.

So we may denote these unique nodes by

$$
v_k \quad (k=1,\ldots,n).
$$

This implies:

- Every computation that reaches level $k$ reaches the same node $v_k$.
- The subfunction at level $k$ is well-defined (unique up to isomorphism).

However:

- Paths in a ZDD are still allowed to skip levels in general ordered ZDDs.
- The condition does **not** impose a chain or forbid edges that bypass intermediate levels.
- It only constrains the number of distinct nodes per level, not the edge topology.

So the correct object is:

> a layered DAG with exactly one node per level, but not necessarily a chain.

## 2. Correct structural interpretation

Because there is only one node at each level, every edge from $v_k$ must go to:

- $0$ or $1$, or
- one of the nodes $v_{k+1}, v_{k+2}, \ldots, v_n$,

subject to ordering.

But reduction implies a key simplification:

### Lemma (compression of skips)

Any edge from $v_k$ to $v_j$ with $j>k+1$ can be uniquely represented, after reduction, as a choice of intermediate behavior through levels $k+1,\ldots,j-1$, each of which has only one available node.

Thus, instead of treating long jumps as independent choices, the ZDD semantics forces each node $v_k$ to classify inputs according to what happens next in the unique structure.

Consequently, each node $v_k$ induces a partition of all subfunctions into exactly three semantic outcomes:

1. go to $0$,
2. go to $1$,
3. proceed to the next level structure (the unique continuation determined by $v_{k+1}$).

This is the only invariant information preserved under reduction with one node per level.

So each $v_k$ behaves like a **3-way decision node at the semantic level**.

## 3. Local choices at each level

For each level $k$, the node $v_k$ must assign its two outgoing edges (LO and HI) into the three possible semantic targets:

$$
\{0,\;1,\;\text{continue}\}.
$$

The ZDD reduction rule forbids LO = HI, so the two edges must choose **distinct outcomes** among these three.

Hence we count ordered pairs $(a,b)$ with $a\neq b$, where

$$
a,b \in \{0,1,\text{continue}\}.
$$

There are:

$$
3\cdot 2 = 6
$$

ordered choices.

However, not all 6 produce distinct reduced behavior.

## 4. Elimination of redundant cases

We classify the 6 possibilities:

### (i) Both edges go to terminals

- $(0,1)$, $(1,0)$

These make $x_k$ terminal at this level and do not interact with deeper structure.

They contribute 2 cases.

### (ii) One terminal, one continuation

- $(0,\text{cont})$, $(\text{cont},0)$
- $(1,\text{cont})$, $(\text{cont},1)$

These are 4 cases.

Each preserves dependence on deeper levels.

### (iii) Both edges go to continuation is forbidden

This is excluded by LO $\neq$ HI.

Thus each level contributes exactly:

$$
6 = 2 + 4
$$

valid reduced configurations.

## 5. Independence across levels

Because there is exactly one node per level, and each node has a uniquely determined continuation target $v_{k+1}$, choices at different levels do not interfere in a way that creates additional identifications:

- no two nodes can merge or split,
- no additional nodes appear,
- reduction does not identify distinct level-local choices.

Thus the level decisions are independent.

## 6. Total count

Each of the $n$ levels contributes $6$ independent possibilities, so the total number of ZDDs is:

$$
6^n.
$$

## 7. Conclusion

The z-profile $(1,\ldots,1)$ does not force a chain, but it does force a rigid layered structure with exactly one node per level. Each node acts as a 3-way semantic splitter with two ordered outgoing edges, producing 6 valid configurations per level.

Hence the number of functions is:

$$
\boxed{6^n}.
$$
