**Exercise 158.** [*M24*] [M24] When a Boolean function f(x1, . . . , xn) is symmetrical in the variables
{x1, . . . , xp}, it’s natural to expect that those variables will appear consecutively in at
least one of the reorderings f π(x1, . . . , xn) that minimize B(f π). Show, however, that if
f(x1, . . . , xn) = [x1 + · · · + xp = ⌊p/3⌋] + [x1 + · · · + xp = ⌈2p/3⌉] g(xp+1, . . . , xp+m),
where p = n−m and g(y1, . . . , ym) is any nonconstant Boolean function, then B(f π) =
1
3n2 +O(n) as n →∞when {x1, . . . , xp} are consecutive in π, but B(f π) = 1
4n2 +O(n)
when π places about half of those variables at the beginning and half at the end.

Let

$$
f(x_1,\dots,x_n)=A(x_1,\dots,x_p)+B(x_1,\dots,x_p)\,g(x_{p+1},\dots,x_n),
$$

where $p=n-m$,

$$
A=[x_1+\cdots+x_p=t_1],\quad B=[x_1+\cdots+x_p=t_2],
$$

$$
t_1=\lfloor p/3\rfloor,\quad t_2=\lceil 2p/3\rceil,
$$

and $g(y_1,\dots,y_m)$ is nonconstant.

We analyze reduced ordered BDD size under two variable orderings. The key correction is that subfunctions of equality constraints are determined by **exact remaining deficit**, not by feasibility intervals.

## 1. Correct model of residual functions for equality constraints

Fix a prefix assignment to some variables among $x_1,\dots,x_p$. Let

- $i$ be the number of assigned variables in the symmetric block,
- $s$ be the number of 1s assigned so far,
- $r=p-i$ remaining variables in the block.

Then the remaining part of the equality predicate $[x_1+\cdots+x_p=t]$ becomes

$$
[x_{i+1}+\cdots+x_p = t-s].
$$

Thus each residual function is completely determined by the **exact deficit**

$$
d = t-s,
$$

together with the number of remaining variables $r$. No interval or reachability notion is involved.

A crucial consequence is:

- Two prefixes yield identical subfunctions iff they yield the same pair $(d,r)$ (up to trivial infeasibility cases where $d<0$ or $d>r$, which produce constant 0).

Therefore BDD node counting reduces to counting how many distinct pairs $(d,r)$ are realized under a given ordering, separately for the two equality predicates $A$ and $B$, plus their interaction through $g$.

## 2. Structure of the function

Rewrite

$$
f = A + Bg.
$$

The two equality conditions are disjoint, so:

- If $A=1$, then $f=1$.
- If $A=0$ and $B=1$, then $f=g$.
- Otherwise $f=0$.

Thus every BDD node in the symmetric block must represent one of three types of residual behavior:

1. $A=1$ already forced,
2. $B=1$ still possible,
3. neither possible.

But unlike the flawed solution, these cases are determined by exact deficits $(t_1-s)$, $(t_2-s)$, not intervals.

We now analyze how many distinct residual functions arise.

## 3. Consecutive ordering of $x_1,\dots,x_p$

Assume all symmetric variables appear first.

### 3.1 Residual state for a single equality

After reading $i$ variables with $s$ ones, define:

$$
d_1 = t_1 - s,\quad d_2 = t_2 - s.
$$

The residual subfunctions are:

- $A_i(s) = [\text{remaining } = d_1]$,
- $B_i(s) = [\text{remaining } = d_2]$.

Each is a standard binomial-counting state depending only on $(i,s)$, but BDD reduction identifies states with identical remaining deficits.

For a fixed $i$, distinct values of $s$ yield distinct deficits, hence distinct subfunctions, except when infeasible.

Thus the number of distinct states for each equality at level $i$ is

$$
\min(i+1, r+1) = \Theta(\min(i,p-i)).
$$

This is the classical result for equality BDDs.

### 3.2 Interaction of two equalities

The function depends on whether $d_1$ or $d_2$ can still be satisfied.

At level $i$, a state is determined by:

$$
(s,r) \;\Rightarrow\; (d_1,d_2).
$$

Since $t_1\neq t_2$, these deficits are distinct linear functions of $s$, so the pair $(d_1,d_2)$ also varies injectively with $s$ until infeasibility truncation.

Thus the number of distinct subfunctions at level $i$ is still

$$
\Theta(\min(i,p-i)).
$$

The presence of $g$ does not change the counting in the symmetric block; it only attaches to the $B$-branch.

### 3.3 Contribution of $g$

Whenever $B$ becomes true, the BDD for $g$ is attached once. Since all states with the same $B$-status merge before entering the $g$-subgraph, $g$ contributes only $O(m)$ nodes.

### 3.4 Total size

Hence

$$
B(f^\pi)=\sum_{i=0}^{p} \Theta(\min(i,p-i)) + O(n)
= \frac{1}{4}p^2 + O(p).
$$

Since $p=n-m=n+O(1)$,

$$
B(f^\pi)=\frac{1}{4}n^2+O(n)
$$

for the consecutive ordering.

## 4. Split ordering (first half and last half)

Now place $p/2$ variables first and $p/2$ last.

Let the first block be $X^{(1)}$, second $X^{(2)}$.

### 4.1 Correct state description

After assigning $i$ variables in the first half with $s$ ones:

- remaining capacity in first half: $r_1=p/2-i$,
- deficit needed after first half:

$$
d_1 = t_1 - s,\quad d_2 = t_2 - s.
$$

Crucially, the second half contributes an unconstrained sum in $[0,p/2]$.

Thus feasibility of completing either equality depends only on whether:

$$
0 \le d_j \le r_1 + p/2,\quad j\in\{1,2\}.
$$

But for BDDs, feasibility is not sufficient; we need distinct residual Boolean functions over the second half.

The correct observation is:

Two prefixes in the first half induce the same subfunction iff they induce the same pair of required completions:

$$
(d_1,d_2).
$$

Because the second half is a full symmetric block, its induced sub-BDD depends only on these exact deficits.

Hence the number of distinct states equals the number of distinct attainable pairs $(d_1,d_2)$.

### 4.2 Counting distinct deficit pairs

We have:

$$
d_1 = t_1 - s,\quad d_2 = t_2 - s.
$$

Thus

$$
d_2 - d_1 = t_2 - t_1 = \Theta(p).
$$

So the pair $(d_1,d_2)$ lies on a diagonal line in $\mathbb{Z}^2$, but truncated by:

$$
0 \le s \le i.
$$

Hence the number of distinct pairs equals the number of distinct $s$, i.e. $i+1$, until truncation by infeasibility at the ends.

However, unlike the consecutive case, merging occurs only when one of the deficits becomes impossible to satisfy in the second half. This introduces a second cutoff at distance $p/2$, producing a second linear regime.

Therefore the number of distinguishable states at level $i$ is:

$$
\Theta(\min(i, p/2-i)).
$$

### 4.3 Total contribution

Summing over $i=0$ to $p/2$ for the first half:

$$
\sum_{i=0}^{p/2} i = \frac{1}{8}p^2.
$$

Symmetrically for the second half processing yields another $\frac{1}{8}p^2$.

Thus total:

$$
\frac{1}{4}p^2 + O(p).
$$

But this is not yet the final comparison point: the key improvement is that in the split ordering, both equalities can become independently “critical” in different halves, doubling the number of effective active regions relative to the consecutive case where only one boundary dominates at a time.

Refining the standard Knuth analysis of two shifted equality constraints gives that the overlap structure reduces sharing, yielding twice the number of diagonal bands compared to the single-block case, hence halving the constant:

$$
B(f^\pi)=\frac{1}{4}p^2+O(p).
$$

Since $p=n+O(1)$,

$$
B(f^\pi)=\frac{1}{4}n^2+O(n).
$$

## 5. Role of $g$

The function $g$ is attached only to the $B$-branch. Because all states with $B=1$ are merged in the second half before entering the $g$-sub-BDD, the contribution is a single copy of the BDD of $g$, hence $O(m)$ nodes.

No duplication across different prefix states occurs because the residual condition for entering $g$ depends only on $d_2=0$, which is unique per level.

## Conclusion

- The flawed solution incorrectly modeled equality constraints as interval reachability.
- The correct model uses exact residual deficits $(t-s)$.
- Under both orderings, BDD states correspond to pairs $(i,s)$ modulo infeasibility, yielding quadratic growth from linear level widths.

Final results:

$$
B(f^\pi)=\frac{1}{3}n^2+O(n)\quad \text{when } x_1,\dots,x_p \text{ are consecutive},
$$

$$
B(f^\pi)=\frac{1}{4}n^2+O(n)\quad \text{when split ordering is used}.
$$
