## Setup

Let $f(x_1,\dots,x_n)$ be a Boolean function and let

$$
f = (\bar{x}_1 \wedge f_0)\ \vee\ (x_1 \wedge f_1),
$$

where $f_0 = f(0,x_2,\dots,x_n)$ and $f_1 = f(1,x_2,\dots,x_n)$.

A cube (subcube) is an implicant of $f$ if it implies $f=1$. A prime implicant is an implicant minimal under deletion of literals. Write $\mathrm{PI}(f)$ for the set of prime implicants of $f$.

Let $e_1$ denote the positive literal $x_1$ and $e'_1$ the negative literal $\bar{x}_1$. Let $;\sqcup;$ denote concatenation of disjoint literals, and set

$$
A = \mathrm{PI}(f_0 \wedge f_1), \quad
B = \mathrm{PI}(f_0)\setminus A, \quad
C = \mathrm{PI}(f_1)\setminus A.
$$

The claim is

$$
\mathrm{PI}(f) = A \ \cup\ (e'_1 \sqcup B)\ \cup\ (e_1 \sqcup C).
$$

## Solution

Let $p \in \mathrm{PI}(f)$. Consider first the case where $p$ contains no literal involving $x_1$. Then $p$ is a cube in variables $x_2,\dots,x_n$. Since $p$ is an implicant of $f$, both substitutions $x_1=0$ and $x_1=1$ satisfy $f=1$ whenever $p$ holds, so $p$ is an implicant of both $f_0$ and $f_1$. Hence $p$ is an implicant of $f_0 \wedge f_1$.

If $p$ were not prime for $f_0 \wedge f_1$, then a proper subcube $q \subset p$ would still imply $f_0 \wedge f_1$, hence would imply $f$, contradicting that $p \in \mathrm{PI}(f)$. Therefore $p \in A$.

Now assume $p$ contains the literal $e'_1$. Write $p = e'_1 \sqcup q$, where $q$ involves only $x_2,\dots,x_n$. Substituting $x_1=0$ in $f$ yields $f_0$, hence $q$ is an implicant of $f_0$.

If $q$ were not a prime implicant of $f_0$, there would exist a proper subcube $q' \subset q$ that is an implicant of $f_0$. Then $e'_1 \sqcup q'$ would be a proper subcube of $p$ that is still an implicant of $f$, contradicting primeness of $p$. Thus $q \in \mathrm{PI}(f_0)$.

If $q \in A = \mathrm{PI}(f_0 \wedge f_1)$, then $q$ is also an implicant of $f_1$. In that case $p = e'_1 \sqcup q$ would remain an implicant after removing the literal $e'_1$, contradicting minimality of $p$. Hence $q \in B$, and $p \in e'_1 \sqcup B$.

The case where $p$ contains $e_1$ is symmetric. Writing $p = e_1 \sqcup q$, the same argument shows $q \in \mathrm{PI}(f_1)$ and $q \notin A$, hence $q \in C$ and $p \in e_1 \sqcup C$.

This proves

$$
\mathrm{PI}(f) \subseteq A \cup (e'_1 \sqcup B) \cup (e_1 \sqcup C).
$$

For the reverse inclusion, let $p \in A$. Then $p$ is an implicant of both $f_0$ and $f_1$, hence it implies $f$ independently of $x_1$. If a proper subcube $q \subset p$ implied $f$, it would imply both $f_0$ and $f_1$, contradicting $p \in \mathrm{PI}(f_0 \wedge f_1)$. Thus $p \in \mathrm{PI}(f)$.

Let $p = e'_1 \sqcup q$ with $q \in B$. Since $q \in \mathrm{PI}(f_0)$, it implies $f_0$, hence $p$ implies $\bar{x}_1 \wedge f_0$, thus implies $f$. If a proper subcube removes $e'_1$, it reduces to $q$, which fails to imply $f_1$ because otherwise $q \in A$; if it removes a literal of $q$, primeness of $q$ in $f_0$ is violated. Hence $p$ is prime for $f$.

The argument for $p = e_1 \sqcup q$ with $q \in C$ is symmetric using $f_1$.

This proves

$$
A \cup (e'_1 \sqcup B) \cup (e_1 \sqcup C) \subseteq \mathrm{PI}(f).
$$

Combining both inclusions yields the identity.

## Verification

Every prime implicant of $f$ either excludes $x_1$, forces $x_1=0$, or forces $x_1=1$, since any cube fixes each variable either by inclusion or omission, and primeness forbids redundant literals.

In the first case, satisfaction under both $x_1=0$ and $x_1=1$ forces membership in $\mathrm{PI}(f_0 \wedge f_1)$ by restriction consistency of implicants.

In the second and third cases, restriction to $x_1=0$ or $x_1=1$ reduces validity exactly to implicants of $f_0$ or $f_1$, and exclusion of $A$ matches the condition that the cube is not already valid in both branches, which is necessary for minimal extension with $e'_1$ or $e_1$.

Each constructed cube on the right-hand side implies $f$ by direct substitution into the Shannon expansion and is minimal because deletion of any literal either breaks minimality inside $f_0$ or $f_1$, or removes the only condition enforcing the correct branch.

## Notes

The decomposition separates prime implicants of $f$ into those stable across both cofactors and those specific to a single branch of the Shannon expansion. The set $A$ corresponds to implicants that are insensitive to $x_1$, while $B$ and $C$ isolate those that become invalid when lifted to the full function without adding the appropriate literal $e'_1$ or $e_1$.
