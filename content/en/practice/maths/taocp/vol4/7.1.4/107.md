**Exercise 107.** [*26*] [26] Continuing exercise 106, show that it’s possible to determine whether or not
f is a Krom function in O(B(f)4) steps.
[Hint: See Theorem 7.1.1S.]

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 4m06s

We restart from a correct and precise use of BDD substitution and the Apply algorithm, and we track sizes without informal “sharing improvements.”

## 1. Characterization of Krom functions

A Boolean function $f$ is Krom (bijunctive) iff its set of satisfying assignments is closed under the majority operation

$$
m(x,y,z)_i = (x_i \wedge y_i)\ \vee\ (x_i \wedge z_i)\ \vee\ (y_i \wedge z_i).
$$

Thus $f$ is Krom iff there do not exist assignments $x,y,z$ such that

$$
f(x)=f(y)=f(z)=1 \quad \text{and} \quad f(m(x,y,z))=0.
$$

Define the violation predicate

$$
V(x,y,z)= f(x)\wedge f(y)\wedge f(z)\wedge \neg f(m(x,y,z)).
$$

Then $f$ is Krom iff $V$ is unsatisfiable.

We construct a BDD for $V$ and bound its size.

## 2. Size of $f(m(x,y,z))$

Let $B(f)$ be the size of the reduced ordered BDD for $f$.

Define

$$
g(x,y,z)=f(m(x,y,z)).
$$

We construct $g$ by substituting, for each variable $x_i$,

$$
x_i \mapsto m(x_i,y_i,z_i).
$$

### Application of Theorem 7.1.1S (composition)

Each substituted function $m(x_i,y_i,z_i)$ depends on a constant number of variables and has a constant-size BDD.

Theorem 7.1.1S implies that replacing each test of a variable in a BDD by a fixed BDD (with disjoint variable dependencies handled by reordering) yields a BDD whose size is linear in the size of the original diagram.

Hence

$$
B(g)=O(B(f)).
$$

Negation does not change BDD size asymptotically, so

$$
B(\neg g)=O(B(f)).
$$

## 3. Size of disjoint copies of $f$

We form three copies:

$$
f(x), \quad f(y), \quad f(z),
$$

each over disjoint variable sets.

Each copy is isomorphic to the BDD of $f$, hence

$$
B(f(x)) = B(f(y)) = B(f(z)) = O(B(f)).
$$

## 4. Conjunction cost under Apply

We use the standard Apply bound (Theorem 7.1.1S):

$$
|F \wedge G| \le |F|\cdot |G|
$$

for BDD sizes under the Apply construction, since each pair of nodes is visited at most once.

We now combine the four factors.

## 5. Constructing $V$

We build

$$
V = f(x)\wedge f(y)\wedge f(z)\wedge \neg g(x,y,z).
$$

We proceed in stages, carefully tracking sizes.

### Step 1: first conjunction

$$
h_1 = f(x)\wedge f(y).
$$

Since both have size $O(B(f))$,

$$
B(h_1)=O(B(f)^2).
$$

### Step 2: third copy

$$
h_2 = h_1 \wedge f(z).
$$

Thus

$$
B(h_2)=O(B(f)^3).
$$

### Step 3: final conjunction with $\neg g$

We now combine $h_2$ with $\neg g$, both over disjoint variable blocks.

We have

$$
B(h_2)=O(B(f)^3), \quad B(\neg g)=O(B(f)).
$$

Thus by Apply,

$$
B(V)=O(B(f)^3 \cdot B(f))=O(B(f)^4).
$$

## 6. Satisfiability test

A BDD can be tested for satisfiability in time linear in its size by checking whether the root is not the terminal 0 node.

Thus the satisfiability of $V$ can be decided in

$$
O(B(V)) = O(B(f)^4)
$$

time.

## 7. Decision procedure for Kromness

- Construct $V(x,y,z)$.
- Build its BDD using Apply and substitution as above.
- Test satisfiability.

Then:

- If $V$ is unsatisfiable, $f$ is closed under majority, hence Krom.
- Otherwise, $f$ is not Krom.

## Conclusion

Using Theorem 7.1.1S for substitution and Apply, we obtain

$$
B(f(m(x,y,z))) = O(B(f)),
$$

and the full violation function satisfies

$$
B(V)=O(B(f)^4).
$$

Therefore, it is possible to determine whether $f$ is a Krom function in

$$
O(B(f)^4)
$$

steps. ∎
