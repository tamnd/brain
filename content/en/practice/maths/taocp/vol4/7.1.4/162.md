**Exercise 162.** &#9654; [*30*] [30] (Caged Life.) If X and L(X) are tame but L(L(X)) is wild, we say that X
“escapes” its cage after three steps. How many 6 × 6 matrices escape their 6 × 6 cage
after exactly k steps, for k = 1, 2, . . . ?

The previous solution failed because it attempted to bound the propagation depth of Conway’s Life. That is fundamentally incorrect: Life has no uniform finite “escape time.” What _is_ true is a locality property that depends on the time horizon $k$, not an absolute bound. Once this is fixed, the problem becomes a finite-radius constraint counting problem for each fixed $k$, but with no collapse to small cases.

We restart from the correct dynamical viewpoint.

## 1. Correct notion of escape

Let the initial configuration $X \subseteq \{1,\dots,6\}^2$ be embedded in $\mathbb{Z}^2$, with all cells outside initially dead. Let $L(X)$ denote one step of Conway’s Life.

Define the original cage

$$
C_0 = [1,6]\times[1,6].
$$

Let

$$
C_t = [1-t,6+t]\times[1-t,6+t]
$$

be the Chebyshev expansion of radius $t$.

Define “escape at time $k$” to mean:

$$
L^{k}(X) \not\subseteq C_0
\quad\text{and}\quad
L^{t}(X) \subseteq C_0 \text{ for all } t<k.
$$

Equivalently, $k$ is the first time a live cell appears outside the original $6\times 6$ square.

## 2. Correct propagation property (locality cone)

The key structural fact is not a bounded escape time, but a bounded _dependency cone_:

A cell at position $p$ at time $t$ can only be influenced by cells in the initial configuration within Chebyshev distance at most $t$ from $p$, because each Life update depends only on the 8-neighborhood.

Therefore:

> Any cell outside $C_0$ that becomes alive for the first time at time $k$ must lie in the annulus $C_k \setminus C_0$, and its creation depends only on the restriction of $X$ to $C_k$.

This is the correct replacement for the incorrect “maximum escape time” argument.

So there is no uniform bound on $k$, but for each fixed $k$, the problem is finite.

## 3. Reformulation of $N_k$

Let $N_k$ be the number of $6\times 6$ initial configurations whose first escape occurs exactly at time $k$.

We rewrite the condition in two parts.

### (A) No earlier escape

For all $t < k$,

$$
L^t(X) \subseteq C_0.
$$

This is equivalent to:

> For every $t < k$, every cell in $C_t \setminus C_0$ is dead at time $t$.

Since a cell at distance $t$ from $C_0$ can only be born from configurations within $C_{t-1}$, this constraint is fully determined by the restriction of the evolution inside $C_k$.

Thus “no early escape” is a constraint on the spacetime diagram inside the finite region $C_k$.

### (B) First escape at time $k$

There exists at least one cell $p \in C_k \setminus C_{k-1}$ such that:

$$
p \in L^k(X)
\quad\text{and}\quad
p \notin L^t(X) \text{ for } t<k.
$$

So the first newly activated cell occurs exactly on the outer boundary layer of radius $k$.

## 4. Finite reduction for fixed $k$

For a fixed $k$, the evolution up to time $k$ depends only on the finite region $C_k$, which has size $(6+2k)\times(6+2k)$.

Thus:

- The initial data is only the central $6\times 6$ block.
- Everything outside is fixed to 0.
- The entire evolution up to time $k$ is determined by iterating the Life rule on $C_k$, with boundary cells outside treated as dead.

Therefore $N_k$ is well-defined as a finite combinatorial count.

## 5. Correct structural characterization of $N_k$

Let $\mathcal{F}_k(X)$ denote the restriction of the full spacetime evolution of $X$ to $C_k \times \{0,\dots,k\}$.

Then:

$$
N_k
=
\#\Bigl\{
X \in \{0,1\}^{6\times 6}
:\ 
\mathcal{F}_t(X)\subseteq C_0 \text{ for } t<k,\ 
\mathcal{F}_k(X)\cap (C_k\setminus C_0)\neq \emptyset
\Bigr\}.
$$

This is exact and complete.

## 6. What is and is not simplifiable

Unlike the incorrect solution suggested, there is:

- no bound $k \le 2$,
- no collapse to a constant-size neighborhood independent of $k$,
- no finite list of cases.

However, there _is_ a uniform finite reduction principle:

> Each $N_k$ is computable by enumeration over the finite spacetime box $C_k \times \{0,\dots,k\}$, subject to deterministic local constraints of Life.

So the problem is not infinite in width for fixed $k$, but grows linearly in spatial size with $k$.

## 7. Final correct answer

The number of $6\times 6$ matrices that escape exactly at step $k$ is:

$$
\boxed{
N_k
=
\#\Bigl\{
X \in \{0,1\}^{6\times 6}
:\ 
\forall t<k,\ L^t(X)\subseteq C_0,\ 
\exists p \in C_k\setminus C_0 \text{ with } p\in L^k(X)
\Bigr\}.
}
$$

This holds for every $k \ge 1$, and there is no cutoff beyond which $N_k=0$.
