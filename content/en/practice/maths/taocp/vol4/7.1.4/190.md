**Exercise 190.** [*20*] [20] Describe all functions f for which (a) Q(f) = B(f); (b) Q(f) = Z(f).

We fix a variable order and use standard OBDD semantics.

Let a _cofactor_ of $f$ be any function obtained by fixing some prefix variables $x_1,\dots,x_k$ according to the order and leaving the rest free. These are exactly the functions labeling nodes in the unreduced ordered decision tree, hence also in $Q(f)$.

# (a) Characterization of $f$ such that $Q(f)=B(f)$

Recall:

- $Q(f)$ is the quasi-reduced OBDD: only nodes with identical children are deleted, but **no merging of identical subfunctions** is performed.
- $B(f)$ is obtained from $Q(f)$ by additionally identifying (merging) nodes that represent the same Boolean function.

Thus $Q(f)=B(f)$ holds exactly when **no two distinct nodes of $Q(f)$ represent the same function**, i.e., no two distinct cofactors of $f$ (under the fixed order) are equal.

### Step 1: Reformulation in terms of cofactors

In $Q(f)$, each node corresponds to a unique cofactor of $f$. Therefore:

$$
Q(f)=B(f)
\quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad
\text{all cofactors of } f \text{ that occur in the OBDD are pairwise distinct.}
$$

Equivalently:

> Whenever two different assignments to some prefix variables produce the same node in the decision tree, this can only happen if the resulting cofactors are identical, and this is forbidden unless the assignments are identical in their effect on the function.

### Step 2: Functional description

Let $x_1<\cdots<x_n$ be the order. For any $k$, define a prefix assignment

$$
\alpha = (x_1=a_1,\dots,x_k=a_k),
$$

and let $f_\alpha$ be the resulting cofactor.

Then $Q(f)=B(f)$ iff:

$$
\forall \alpha \neq \beta \text{ (reachable prefix assignments)}, \quad f_\alpha \neq f_\beta.
$$

### Final characterization

$$
\boxed{
Q(f)=B(f)
\;\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\;
\text{all distinct cofactors of } f \text{ (under the fixed variable order) are distinct as Boolean functions.}
}
$$

Equivalently, the mapping from reachable prefix assignments to cofactors is injective.

No further simplification is possible in general, since this property depends on the full structure of the cofactor system of $f$, not on a local syntactic condition.

# (b) Characterization of $f$ such that $Q(f)=Z(f)$

Recall:

- $Z(f)$ is obtained from $Q(f)$ by the **zero-suppression rule**:

a node labeled $x_i$ with children $(g_0,g_1)$ is removed if

$$
g_1 \equiv 0.
$$

Thus $Q(f)=Z(f)$ holds exactly when this rule never applies in $Q(f)$.

## Step 1: Translate the condition to cofactors

Let $g$ be any cofactor of $f$ appearing in $Q(f)$, and suppose it is labeled by variable $x_i$. Then its children satisfy:

$$
g_0 = g|_{x_i=0}, \quad g_1 = g|_{x_i=1}.
$$

Zero-suppression is triggered exactly when:

$$
g|_{x_i=1} \equiv 0.
$$

Hence $Q(f)=Z(f)$ iff:

$$
\forall \text{ cofactor } g \text{ and tested variable } x_i,\quad g|_{x_i=1} \not\equiv 0.
$$

## Step 2: Functional interpretation

The condition $g|_{x_i=1} \equiv 0$ means:

> every assignment satisfying $g$ forces $x_i=0$ whenever $x_i$ is set to 1.

Equivalently:

$$
g \wedge x_i \equiv 0.
$$

Thus zero-suppression is absent exactly when:

> no cofactor of $f$ becomes identically false after forcing any tested variable to 1.

## Final characterization

$$
\boxed{
Q(f)=Z(f)
\;\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\;
\forall \text{ cofactor } g \text{ of } f,\;
\forall x_i \text{ tested in } g,\;
g \wedge x_i \not\equiv 0.
}
$$

Equivalently:

- No cofactor of $f$ is entirely contained in the hyperplane $x_i=0$ for any variable that appears in its OBDD node.
- Every time a variable is tested in a cofactor, there exists at least one satisfying extension with that variable equal to 1.

# Summary

- **(a)** $Q(f)=B(f)$ exactly when all cofactors of $f$ (under the fixed order) are pairwise distinct, so no two different paths ever represent the same Boolean function.
- **(b)** $Q(f)=Z(f)$ exactly when no cofactor of $f$ has the property that setting a tested variable to $1$ makes the function identically false, so zero-suppression never applies.

These are intrinsic, function-level conditions expressed purely in terms of the cofactor structure of $f$.
