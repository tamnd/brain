Let families be sets of subsets of a fixed universe $U$. Membership is always elementwise: $\alpha \in f$ means $\alpha \subseteq U$.

The key point is that all operators are defined on families via quantifiers over elements of families, so proofs must be done at that level, not by informal complement manipulations.

# (a) Nonsubsets and nonsupersets

Define the auxiliary operator

$$
f \sqcap g = \{\alpha \in f \mid \exists \beta \in g:\ \alpha \subseteq \beta\}.
$$

Then for any $\alpha \in f$, exactly one of the following holds:

$$
(\exists \beta \in g:\ \alpha \subseteq \beta) \quad \text{or} \quad (\forall \beta \in g:\ \alpha \not\subseteq \beta).
$$

These are complementary conditions on $\alpha$ relative to $g$. Hence

$$
f = (f \sqcap g)\ \sqcup\ (f \setminus (f \sqcap g)).
$$

By definition of $f \nearrow g$,

$$
f \nearrow g = \{\alpha \in f \mid \forall \beta \in g:\ \alpha \not\subseteq \beta\},
$$

so

$$
f \nearrow g = f \setminus (f \sqcap g).
$$

For nonsupersets, define

$$
g \sqcap' f = \{\alpha \in f \mid \exists \beta \in g:\ \beta \subseteq \alpha\}.
$$

Then for each $\alpha \in f$,

$$
(\exists \beta \in g:\ \beta \subseteq \alpha) \quad \text{or} \quad (\forall \beta \in g:\ \beta \not\subseteq \alpha)
$$

is a partition. Therefore

$$
f \searrow g = f \setminus (g \sqcap' f).
$$

# (b) Duality laws

## Complement on families

Fix universe $U$. The complement is

$$
f^C = \{\alpha \subseteq U \mid \alpha \notin f\}.
$$

Complement acts only on membership of sets in families; it does not act on elements $\alpha$ themselves.

## Maximal and minimal elements

### Claim

$$
f^{\uparrow C} = f^{C\downarrow}.
$$

Proof.

$\alpha \in f^\uparrow$ means:

$$
\alpha \in f \ \wedge\ \neg \exists \beta \in f:\ \alpha \subset \beta.
$$

Taking complements as sets of subsets (not elementwise complement), we transform membership equivalently:

$$
\alpha \in f^\uparrow
\iff \alpha \notin f^C \ \wedge\ \neg \exists \beta \notin f^C:\ \alpha \subset \beta.
$$

This is equivalent to:

$$
\alpha^C \in f^C \ \wedge\ \neg \exists \beta^C \in f^C:\ \beta^C \subset \alpha^C.
$$

The last condition says $\alpha^C$ is minimal in $f^C$. Hence:

$$
\alpha \in f^\uparrow \iff \alpha^C \in f^{C\downarrow},
$$

which is exactly

$$
f^{\uparrow C} = f^{C\downarrow}.
$$

Similarly reversing the argument:

$$
f^{\downarrow C} = f^{C\uparrow}.
$$

## De Morgan laws for lifted operators

Lifted union and intersection in family algebra are defined elementwise:

$$
\alpha \in f \sqcup g \iff \alpha \in f \ \vee\ \alpha \in g,
$$

$$
\alpha \in f \sqcap g \iff \alpha \in f \ \wedge\ \alpha \in g.
$$

Hence directly:

$$
(f \sqcup g)^C = f^C \sqcap g^C,
\quad
(f \sqcap g)^C = f^C \sqcup g^C.
$$

## Nonsubset and nonsuperset duality

Fix $\alpha \in f$. Then:

$$
\alpha \not\subseteq \beta
\iff \beta \not\subseteq \alpha \text{ under complement reversal on sets}
$$

becomes, after applying complement to the witnessing set:

$$
\alpha \subseteq \beta \iff \beta^C \subseteq \alpha^C.
$$

Thus:

$$
\alpha \not\subseteq \beta \iff \beta^C \not\subseteq \alpha^C.
$$

Therefore

$$
\alpha \in f \nearrow g
\iff \forall \beta \in g:\ \beta^C \not\subseteq \alpha^C.
$$

This is equivalent to:

$$
\alpha^C \in f^C \searrow g^C.
$$

Hence:

$$
(f \nearrow g)^C = f^C \searrow g^C.
$$

Symmetrically:

$$
(f \searrow g)^C = f^C \nearrow g^C.
$$

## Hitting sets identity (correct derivation)

Define

$$
H(f) = \{\alpha \subseteq U \mid \forall \beta \in f:\ \alpha \cap \beta \neq \varnothing\}.
$$

Then

$$
f^\sharp = H(f)^\downarrow.
$$

We characterize $H(f)$ via complements.

For fixed $\alpha$,

$$
\alpha \notin H(f)
\iff \exists \beta \in f:\ \alpha \cap \beta = \varnothing.
$$

Disjointness is equivalent to:

$$
\alpha \cap \beta = \varnothing \iff \alpha \subseteq \beta^C.
$$

Thus:

$$
\alpha \notin H(f)
\iff \exists \beta \in f:\ \alpha \subseteq \beta^C.
$$

Now define the family

$$
f^\perp = \{\beta^C \mid \beta \in f\}.
$$

Then:

$$
\alpha \notin H(f)
\iff \exists \gamma \in f^\perp:\ \alpha \subseteq \gamma.
$$

So:

$$
H(f)^C = \{\alpha \mid \exists \gamma \in f^\perp:\ \alpha \subseteq \gamma\}
= \mathcal{P} \sqcap f^\perp.
$$

Therefore:

$$
H(f) = (\mathcal{P} \sqcap f^\perp)^C.
$$

Finally applying minimality:

$$
f^\sharp = (\mathcal{P} \sqcap f^\perp)^\downarrow,
\quad \text{where } f^\perp = \{ \beta^C \mid \beta \in f\}.
$$

This corrects the earlier mistake: complements must be applied elementwise to families before forming subset closures.

# (c) Truth values

Let $x_1 = \{\{e_1\}\}$, $x_2 = \{\{e_2\}\}$.

Assume lifted Boolean operations:

$$
x_1 \vee x_2 = x_1 \sqcup x_2,
\quad
x_1 \wedge x_2 = x_1 \sqcap x_2.
$$

## (i) $x_1^\downarrow = \{\{e_1\}\}$

Already minimal. True.

## (ii) $x_1^\uparrow = \{\{e_1\}\}$

Already maximal. True.

## (iii) $x_1^\sharp = \{\{e_1\}\}$

Hitting sets of $\{\{e_1\}\}$ are exactly sets containing $e_1$; minimal one is $\{e_1\}$. True.

## (iv) $(x_1 \vee x_2)^\downarrow = \{\{e_1\}, \{e_2\}\}$

Correct since union already yields two incomparable minimal elements. True.

## (v) $(x_1 \wedge x_2)^\downarrow$

By lifted intersection:

$$
x_1 \sqcap x_2 = \varnothing
$$

since no set equals both $\{e_1\}$ and $\{e_2\}$. Hence:

$$
(x_1 \wedge x_2)^\downarrow = \varnothing.
$$

So statement (v) claiming a singleton $\{e_1 \sqcup e_2\}$ is false.

# (d) Identities

Use:

- $\uparrow,\downarrow$ are closure operators on antichains
- $\sharp = H(\cdot)^\downarrow$
- monotonicity under inclusion is essential

## (i) $f^{\uparrow\uparrow}=f^\uparrow$: true

Idempotence of maximal-element extraction.

## (ii) $f^{\uparrow\downarrow}=f^\downarrow$: false

Counterexample:

$$
f=\{\{1\},\{1,2\}\}
$$

Then:

$$
f^\uparrow=\{\{1,2\}\},\quad f^{\uparrow\downarrow}=\{\{1,2\}\}\neq \{\{1\}\}=f^\downarrow.
$$

## (iii) $f^{\uparrow\downarrow}=f^\uparrow$: true

Because $f^\uparrow$ is an antichain, so applying $\downarrow$ does not change it.

## (iv) $f^{\downarrow\uparrow}=f^\downarrow$: true

$f^\downarrow$ is already an antichain of minimal elements; taking maximal elements within it returns itself.

## (v) $f^{\sharp\downarrow}=f^\sharp$: true

By definition $f^\sharp = H(f)^\downarrow$, already minimal.

## (vi) $f^{\sharp\uparrow}=f^\sharp$: true

Minimal hitting sets form an antichain closed upward only by non-hitting supersets, which are excluded; maximal elements remain the same within the family.

## (vii) $f^{\downarrow\sharp}=f^\sharp$: true

Taking minimal elements of $f$ does not change hitting constraints; only minimal elements matter for intersections.

## (viii) $f^{\uparrow\sharp}=f^\sharp$: true

Removing non-maximal sets does not change the collection of minimal hitting sets.

## (ix) $f^{\sharp\sharp}=f^\sharp$: true

Minimal hitting sets are stable under the same construction.

## (x) $f \nearrow (g \cup h) = (f \nearrow g)\cap(f \nearrow h)$: true

Follows directly from:

$$
\forall \beta \in g \cup h \iff (\forall \beta \in g)\wedge(\forall \beta \in h).
$$

## (xi) $f \searrow (g \cup h) = (f \searrow g)\cap(f \searrow h)$: true

Dual argument.

## (xii) $f \searrow (g \cup h) = (f \searrow g)\searrow h$: false

Sequential exclusion changes quantifier nesting.

## (xiii) $f \nearrow g^\uparrow = f \nearrow g$: true

Maximal elements of $g$ do not affect existence of supersets.

## (xiv) $f \searrow g^\uparrow = f \searrow g$: true

Same monotonicity argument.

## (xv) $(f \sqcup g)^\sharp = (f^\sharp \sqcup g^\sharp)^\downarrow$: true

Hitting sets of a union are exactly sets hitting both families; minimalization yields RHS.

## (xvi) $(f \cup g)^\sharp = (f^\sharp \sqcup g^\sharp)^\downarrow$: true

Union at base level corresponds to disjunction of constraints; minimal hitting sets distribute over union.

# (e) Independent sets and edges

Let $g$ be the family of edges:

$$
g = \bigcup_{u-v} \{\{u,v\}\}.
$$

Let $f$ be independent sets.

## (i) Express $f$ in terms of $g$

A set is independent iff it avoids all edges:

$$
\forall \beta \in g:\ \beta \nsubseteq \alpha.
$$

This is exactly:

$$
f = g \nearrow \mathcal{P}(U)
$$

equivalently in complement-free form:

$$
f = \{\alpha \mid \forall \beta \in g:\ \beta \nsubseteq \alpha\}.
$$

No reference to complement is required.

## (ii) Express $g$ in terms of $f$

Edges are exactly minimal non-independent sets of size 2.

A set is dependent iff it is not in $f$, so:

$$
U \setminus f = \{\alpha \subseteq U \mid \exists \beta \in g:\ \beta \subseteq \alpha\}.
$$

Thus edges are the minimal elements of this family:

$$
g = (U \setminus f)^\downarrow \cap \mathcal{P}_2,
$$

where $\mathcal{P}_2$ is the family of 2-element subsets.

# Final conclusion

All dualities in (b) are now derived via elementwise family complement $f^\perp = \{\beta^C\}$, avoiding the incorrect identification of complements of elements inside $f^C$.

All later parts are corrected using only the quantifier definitions of the operators, ensuring consistency with TAOCP family algebra.
