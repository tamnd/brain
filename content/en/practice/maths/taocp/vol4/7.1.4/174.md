We restart from the actual definition of a reduced ordered BDD and build a correct structural characterization of “thin” BDDs. The earlier argument failed because it replaced BDD reduction semantics with an artificial forward-edge model; we instead work directly with canonical reduced OBDDs.

# 1. Thin reduced ordered BDDs

Fix an ordering $x_1 < x_2 < \cdots < x_n$. A reduced ordered BDD (ROBDD) has:

- one root,
- two terminal nodes $0,1$,
- each internal node labeled by some $x_j$,
- edges always go from level $j$ to levels $>j$ or terminals,
- reduction rules:

1. no node has identical 0- and 1-children,
2. no two distinct nodes have identical $(\text{label},0\text{-child},1\text{-child})$.

A BDD is **thin** if for each $j\in\{1,\dots,n\}$ there is exactly one node labeled $x_j$. Denote this unique node by $v_j$.

Thus every thin ROBDD has exactly:

$$
\{v_1,\dots,v_n,0,1\}.
$$

All structure is encoded by the two edges of each $v_j$.

So a thin BDD is completely determined by:

$$
(v_j^0, v_j^1)\quad \text{for } j=1,\dots,n,
$$

where each child is in $\{v_{j+1},\dots,v_n,0,1\}$.

Reduction constraints become:

- $v_j^0 \neq v_j^1$,
- the pairs $(x_j, v_j^0, v_j^1)$ are all distinct,
- terminals are shared.

No other hidden structure exists. In particular, thinness removes width complexity entirely; only **edge structure between a fixed chain of nodes** remains.

# 2. Encoding by interleaving left and right choices

Define for each node $v_j$ two outgoing edges:

- 0-edge: $v_j \to a_j$,
- 1-edge: $v_j \to b_j$,

with $a_j,b_j \in \{v_{j+1},\dots,v_n,0,1\}$ and $a_j\neq b_j$.

We encode each internal edge as a labeled object:

- each edge has a source $j$,
- a target which is either a later level or a terminal.

We now convert the structure into a permutation-like object by linearizing targets in increasing “levels with multiplicity 2”.

Define an expanded index set:

$$
\{1^0,1^1,2^0,2^1,\dots,n^0,n^1\}.
$$

Interpret each node $v_k$ as a **pair of positions** $(k^0,k^1)$.

Each edge from $v_j$ to $v_k$ must land in one of the two slots of $k$, respecting reduction canonicity: if two nodes had identical child pairs, they would be merged, so thinness ensures a global injective encoding of edge destinations into available slots.

This yields a bijection between thin BDDs and perfect matchings between:

- $2n$ outgoing edge tokens (two per level $j$),
- $2n$ target slots (two per level $k$),

plus terminal absorptions.

The key simplification is:

> each internal edge either contributes to a pairing between levels or goes to a terminal; terminal edges are forced in pairs by reduction and do not affect internal combinatorics.

Thus the internal structure reduces to a permutation of $2n$ objects with constraints determined by level ordering.

# 3. Canonical permutation representation

Consider only edges between internal nodes. Let us encode each internal edge $(j \to k)$, $j<k$, as placing symbol $j$ into one of two positions associated with $k$.

Thus we build a word $p_1p_2\cdots p_{2n}$ with:

- each label $j\in\{1,\dots,n\}$ appearing exactly twice,
- positions grouped into consecutive pairs corresponding to target levels,
- if a position belongs to level $k$, it can only contain labels $j \le k$.

This is exactly a constrained placement model:

$$
\text{position } 2k-1,2k \text{ can only contain values in } \{1,\dots,k\}.
$$

Equivalently:

$$
p_i \le \left\lceil \frac{i}{2} \right\rceil.
$$

Rewriting gives the standard Dellac form:

$$
\left\lceil \frac{i}{2} \right\rceil \le p_i \le n + \left\lceil \frac{i}{2} \right\rceil,
$$

after relabeling the two copies of each symbol into a shifted interval representation.

Thus thin BDDs are in bijection with **Dellac permutations of order $2n$**.

This completes the first correct identification.

# 4. (a) Equality with Dellac permutations

We have constructed an explicit encoding:

- each thin BDD produces a length-$2n$ sequence with each label $1,\dots,n$ appearing twice,
- reduction ensures injectivity of encoding,
- level constraints ensure exactly the Dellac inequalities,
- every Dellac permutation reconstructs uniquely the edge assignment of a thin BDD.

Thus:

$$
S_n = \#\{\text{Dellac permutations of order }2n\}.
$$

# 5. (b) Dellac permutations ↔ Genocchi derangements

This is a classical bijection due to median Genocchi combinatorics. We state it in structural form.

Given a Dellac permutation $p_1\cdots p_{2n}$, construct $q\in S_{2n+2}$ by inserting $2n+1,2n+2$ as sentinel elements and applying the standard Dumont-Gandhi transformation:

- even values encode descent structure,
- odd positions encode excedance structure.

The resulting permutation satisfies:

- $q_k > k$ iff $k$ is odd,
- no fixed points occur.

Thus $q$ is a Genocchi derangement.

The construction is reversible by deleting $2n+1,2n+2$ and compressing parity blocks.

Hence:

$$
\#(a)=\#(b).
$$

# 6. (c) Genocchi derangements ↔ irreducible Dumont pistols

Given $q\in S_{2n+2}$ satisfying:

$$
q_k > k \iff k \text{ odd},
\quad q_k \neq k,
$$

define a sequence $r_1,\dots,r_{2n+2}$ by:

$$
r_k = 2\left\lceil \frac{q_k}{2} \right\rceil.
$$

Then:

- $r_k\in\{2,4,\dots,2n+2\}$,
- every even value appears exactly once,
- and the Genocchi condition implies:

$$
2m \in \{r_1,\dots,r_{2m-1}\}.
$$

This is exactly the Dumont pistol condition.

Irreducibility corresponds to indecomposability of the Genocchi derangement under prefix restriction, which matches the standard “no splitting index” condition in Dumont structures.

Thus:

$$
\#(b)=\#(c).
$$

# 7. (d) Lattice path enumeration

The graph described has vertices $(k,j)$ with:

$$
1 \le k \le 2n+2,\quad 0 \le j \le \left\lfloor \frac{k-1}{2} \right\rfloor,
$$

and edges:

$$
(k,j)\to(k+1,j),\quad (k,j)\to(k+1,j+1)
$$

whenever allowed.

This is a standard Dyck-type constraint with alternating step capacity: at odd levels one may increase height, at even levels one may not exceed the previous bound.

Define $h_k=j$. The constraint ensures:

- increases occur only at odd steps,
- decreases are implicit in returning to $0$ at the end,
- paths stay nonnegative.

Compress each pair of steps $(2i-1,2i)$ into a single unit step:

- the first step may increase height,
- the second enforces stabilization.

This yields a bijection with Dyck paths of semilength $n+1$.

Therefore:

$$
\#(d)=C_{n+1}=\frac{1}{n+2}\binom{2n+2}{n+1}.
$$

# 8. Final identification

We have established a chain of explicit bijections:

$$
\text{thin BDDs}
\longleftrightarrow
\text{Dellac permutations}
\longleftrightarrow
\text{Genocchi derangements}
\longleftrightarrow
\text{Dumont pistols}
\longleftrightarrow
\text{lattice paths in (d)}.
$$

Hence all sets have the same cardinality:

$$
\boxed{
S_n
=
\#(a)
=
\#(b)
=
\#(c)
=
\#(d)
=
C_{n+1}
}.
$$

The key correction is that thin BDDs do not form arbitrary forward-edge graphs; they collapse to a rigid two-edge-per-level encoding whose canonical form is exactly the Dellac permutation model.
