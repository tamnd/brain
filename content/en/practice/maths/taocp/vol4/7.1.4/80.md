**Exercise 80.** [*23*] [23] The recursive algorithm (55) computes f ∧g in a depth-first manner, while
Algorithm S does its computation breadth-first. Do both algorithms encounter the same
subproblems f ′ ∧g′ as they proceed (but in a different order), or does one algorithm
consider fewer cases than the other?

## Solution

Let $f$ and $g$ be Boolean functions represented by reduced ordered BDDs. Consider the recursive computation of $h = f \wedge g$ as given by the algorithm in (55), and the breadth-first computation of the same function by Algorithm S.

In both procedures, a subproblem has the form $f' \wedge g'$, where $f'$ and $g'$ are subfunctions obtained by following LO and HI arcs in the respective BDDs. Thus each subproblem corresponds to an ordered pair of BDD nodes $(u,v)$, where $u$ is a node of the BDD for $f$ and $v$ is a node of the BDD for $g$.

Let $\mathcal{P}$ denote the set of all pairs $(u,v)$ such that $u$ is reachable from the root of the BDD of $f$ and $v$ is reachable from the root of the BDD of $g$. For each such pair, Shannon decomposition induces at most one pair of subproblems:

$$
(u_0,v_0), \quad (u_0,v_1), \quad (u_1,v_0), \quad (u_1,v_1),
$$

with the restriction that only the branch corresponding to the smaller variable index is expanded, as required by the ordered structure of BDDs. Hence every recursive call in (55) corresponds to moving along edges in the product graph on $\mathcal{P}$ whose vertices are pairs $(u,v)$ and whose edges are induced by LO and HI transitions in each component.

The recursive algorithm (55) begins at the pair of root nodes and generates subproblems only by applying these transitions. Therefore every subproblem encountered by (55) lies in $\mathcal{P}$ and is reachable from the root pair in this product graph.

Algorithm S performs the same semantic expansion of $f \wedge g$ but schedules exploration in breadth-first order. Its queue contains pairs $(u,v)$, and each extraction expands the same successor pairs determined by LO and HI edges and variable ordering. Hence every pair inserted by Algorithm S is also reachable from the root pair in the same product graph, and no pair outside this set is ever generated.

Since both algorithms generate subproblems exactly by closure under the same transition rules on the same state space $\mathcal{P}$, every subproblem $f' \wedge g'$ that can be produced by one algorithm is reachable by a finite sequence of valid decompositions and is therefore produced by the other algorithm as well. The only difference is that the recursive algorithm explores depth-first order while Algorithm S explores breadth-first order.

Therefore both algorithms encounter exactly the same set of subproblems $f' \wedge g'$, although in different orders. Neither algorithm considers fewer cases than the other.

This completes the proof. ∎
