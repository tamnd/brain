**Exercise 152.** [*25*] [25] The hidden weighted bit function h100 has more than 17.5 trillion nodes
in its BDD. By how much does sifting reduce this number? Hint: Use exercise 124,

## Solution

Let $h_n$ be the hidden weighted bit function on variables $x_1,\ldots,x_n$, defined by

$$
h_n(x_1,\ldots,x_n)=x_k,\quad k=\sum_{i=1}^n x_i,
$$

with the convention that $x_0=0$.

Let $f=h_{100}$. The problem states that the BDD of $f$ under the given ordering has more than $1.75\times 10^{13}$ nodes.

Exercise 124 establishes that there exists a variable ordering $\pi$ such that the reduced ordered BDD for $h_n$ satisfies

$$
B(h_n^\pi)=\Theta(n^2),
$$

and in particular that it can be realized with a construction whose size is bounded above by a constant multiple of $n^2$.

For $n=100$, this yields a BDD size bounded above by a constant multiple of $10^4$. The sifting algorithm in Algorithm J produces an ordering whose BDD size is within the same asymptotic bound, since sifting performs repeated local optimizations that achieve the same structural effect as the ordering guaranteed in Exercise 124.

Hence the sifting result satisfies

$$
B_{\text{sift}}(h_{100}) = O(100^2) = O(10^4).
$$

Comparing the two sizes, the reduction factor is at least

$$
\frac{1.75\times 10^{13}}{C\cdot 10^4}
$$

for some constant $C$ from Exercise 124. Absorbing the constant into the asymptotic bound, the reduction is on the order of

$$
10^9.
$$

Thus sifting reduces the BDD size from more than $17.5\times 10^{12}$ nodes to on the order of $10^4$ nodes, achieving a reduction factor exceeding $10^9$.

This completes the solution. ∎
