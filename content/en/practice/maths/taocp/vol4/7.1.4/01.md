## Solution

Fix the variable ordering $x_1 \prec x_2$. In a reduced ordered BDD, every non-sink node is labeled by one of these variables, and every path queries variables in increasing order.

The sinks are $\bot$ and $\top$. A node $(V,\mathrm{LO},\mathrm{HI})$ contributes one unit to the size $B(f)$ whenever it is reachable from the root.

### Constant functions

The constant function $f(x_1,x_2)=0$ is represented only by the sink $\bot$, since no branching node is reachable. Hence $B(0)=1$.

The constant function $f(x_1,x_2)=1$ is represented only by the sink $\top$, hence $B(1)=1$.

### Functions depending only on one variable

If $f$ depends only on $x_1$, the root is an $x_1$-node whose two edges go directly to sinks.

For $f(x_1,x_2)=x_1$, the LO edge goes to $\bot$ and the HI edge goes to $\top$, producing one $x_1$-node and both sinks, so $B(x_1)=3$.

For $f(x_1,x_2)=\neg x_1$, the LO edge goes to $\top$ and the HI edge goes to $\bot$, giving the same node structure, hence $B(\neg x_1)=3$.

If $f$ depends only on $x_2$, the root is an $x_2$-node. Both sinks must be reachable because both values of $x_2$ occur.

For $f(x_1,x_2)=x_2$, the LO edge goes to $\bot$ and the HI edge goes to $\top$, giving one $x_2$-node and both sinks, hence $B(x_2)=3$.

For $f(x_1,x_2)=\neg x_2$, the LO edge goes to $\top$ and the HI edge goes to $\bot$, again yielding $B(\neg x_2)=3$.

### Two-variable-dependent functions

If both variables are essential, the root must be an $x_1$-node, and its LO and HI subfunctions are Boolean functions of $x_2$. Each such subfunction is either constant, $x_2$, or $\neg x_2$, and identical substructures merge when they match the same triple $(V,\mathrm{LO},\mathrm{HI})$.

#### Conjunction and related monotone functions

For $f(x_1,x_2)=x_1 \wedge x_2$, the restriction at $x_1=0$ is $0$, and at $x_1=1$ is $x_2$. The root $x_1$ therefore has LO pointing to $\bot$ and HI pointing to an $x_2$-node with LO $\bot$ and HI $\top$. The reachable nodes are $\bot$, $\top$, one $x_1$-node, and one $x_2$-node, hence $B(x_1 \wedge x_2)=4$.

For $f(x_1,x_2)=x_1 \vee x_2$, the restriction at $x_1=0$ is $x_2$, and at $x_1=1$ is $1$. The root $x_1$ has LO pointing to an $x_2$-node and HI pointing to $\top$, giving nodes $\bot$, $\top$, $x_1$, $x_2$, hence $B(x_1 \vee x_2)=4$.

For $f(x_1,x_2)=\neg x_1 \wedge x_2$, the restriction at $x_1=0$ is $x_2$, and at $x_1=1$ is $0$. The root has LO pointing to an $x_2$-node and HI to $\bot$, hence again four nodes, so $B(\neg x_1 \wedge x_2)=4$.

For $f(x_1,x_2)=\neg x_1 \vee x_2$ (implication $x_1 \to x_2$), the restriction at $x_1=0$ is $1$, and at $x_1=1$ is $x_2$. The root has LO $\top$ and HI an $x_2$-node, giving four nodes, so $B(x_1 \to x_2)=4$.

For $f(x_1,x_2)=x_2 \to x_1$, the restriction at $x_1=0$ is $\neg x_2$, and at $x_1=1$ is $1$. The root has LO an $x_2$-node with inverted sinks and HI $\top$, giving four nodes, so $B(x_2 \to x_1)=4$.

#### Non-monotone asymmetric cases

For $f(x_1,x_2)=\neg x_1 \wedge \neg x_2$ (NOR), the restriction at $x_1=0$ is $\neg x_2$, and at $x_1=1$ is $0$. The root has LO an $x_2$-node and HI $\bot$, giving four nodes, so $B(\mathrm{NOR})=4$.

For $f(x_1,x_2)=\neg(x_1 \wedge x_2)$ (NAND), the restriction at $x_1=0$ is $1$, and at $x_1=1$ is $\neg x_2$. The root has LO $\top$ and HI an $x_2$-node, hence $B(\mathrm{NAND})=4$.

For $f(x_1,x_2)=1$ if and only if $(x_1,x_2)=(1,0)$, the restriction at $x_1=0$ is $0$, and at $x_1=1$ is $\neg x_2$. The root has LO $\bot$ and HI an $x_2$-node, giving four nodes, so $B(0010)=4$.

For $f(x_1,x_2)=1$ if and only if $(x_1,x_2)=(0,1)$, the restriction at $x_1=0$ is $x_2$, and at $x_1=1$ is $0$. The root has LO an $x_2$-node and HI $\bot$, giving four nodes, so $B(0100)=4$.

#### Symmetric and XOR-type functions

For $f(x_1,x_2)=x_1 \oplus x_2$, the restriction at $x_1=0$ is $x_2$, and at $x_1=1$ is $\neg x_2$. The root has LO an $x_2$-node and HI a second $x_2$-node with swapped sinks. These two $x_2$-nodes are distinct since their triples $(V,\mathrm{LO},\mathrm{HI})$ differ, so both remain in the reduced diagram. Together with the root and sinks, this gives five nodes, hence $B(x_1 \oplus x_2)=5$.

For $f(x_1,x_2)=\neg(x_1 \oplus x_2)$ (XNOR), the same structure occurs with LO and HI exchanged, again producing two distinct $x_2$-nodes, so $B(\mathrm{XNOR})=5$.

#### Remaining mixed cases

For $f(x_1,x_2)=x_1$, the root is an $x_1$-node with LO $\bot$ and HI $\top$, so $B=3$ was already covered; the same applies to its complement $\neg x_1$.

For $f(x_1,x_2)=\neg x_2$, both restrictions at $x_1=0$ and $x_1=1$ equal $\neg x_2$, so the root is an $x_2$-node with both edges determined by $\neg x_2$, and no $x_1$-node is reachable. Hence $B(\neg x_2)=3$.

### Complete classification

Every Boolean function on $(x_1,x_2)$ falls into one of the cases above. The sizes are therefore:

The constant functions $0,1$ have size $1$.

The unary functions $x_1,\neg x_1,x_2,\neg x_2$ have size $3$.

The functions $x_1 \wedge x_2$, $x_1 \vee x_2$, $\neg x_1 \wedge x_2$, $\neg x_1 \vee x_2$, $x_2 \to x_1$, $x_1 \to x_2$, $\mathrm{NOR}$, $\mathrm{NAND}$, and the two single-point functions $0010$ and $0100$ all have size $4$.

The functions $x_1 \oplus x_2$ and $x_1 \equiv x_2$ have size $5$.

This exhausts all $16$ Boolean functions on two variables, completing the classification. ∎
