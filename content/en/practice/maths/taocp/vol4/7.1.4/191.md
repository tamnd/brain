[Section 7.1.4: Binary Decision Diagrams](../)

**Exercise 191.** &#9654; [*HM25*] [HM25] How many functions f(x1, . . . , xn) have no ⊥in their ZDD?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m16s  

---

## Solution

Let $Z(f)$ denote the ZDD of $f(x_1,\dots,x_n)$ in the sense of Section 7.1.4, with terminals $\bot$ and $\top$. The terminal $\bot$ represents the empty family, so any node whose evaluation reaches $\bot$ corresponds to a subfunction that is identically false on the remaining variables.

Assume the ZDD of $f$ contains no $\bot$ node. Then no evaluation path from the root can terminate at $\bot$, since every node of the diagram lies on some path from the root and every path ends at a terminal. Hence every such path must terminate at $\top$.

Let $g$ be any subfunction of $f$ obtained by fixing values of some initial variables $x_1,\dots,x_k$. In the ZDD, $g$ is represented by the node reached after following the corresponding sequence of LO and HI edges. Since the diagram contains no $\bot$ node, the evaluation of $g$ on any assignment of the remaining variables cannot reach $\bot$, and therefore must always terminate at $\top$. This implies that $g$ is identically $1$ on its domain.

In particular, take $k=n$. Then the corresponding subfunction has no remaining variables, so it is a constant function. Since it is identically $1$, the original function satisfies $f(x_1,\dots,x_n)=1$ for every assignment $(x_1,\dots,x_n)$.

Thus $f$ must be the constant-1 function.

Conversely, if $f(x_1,\dots,x_n)\equiv 1$, then its ZDD consists of a single terminal $\top$ and contains no $\bot$ node, so it satisfies the stated condition.

Therefore exactly one Boolean function has a ZDD containing no $\bot$ node, namely the constant-1 function.
