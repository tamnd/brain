[Section 7.1.4: Binary Decision Diagrams](../)

**Exercise 129.** [*M25*] [M25] Define Qm(x1, . . . , xm2) to be 1 if and only if the 0–1 matrix (x(i−1)m+j)
has no all-zero row and no all-zero column. Prove that B(Qπ
m) = Ω(2m/m2) for all π.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 59s

## Solution

Let the variables be arranged as an $m \times m$ matrix,

$$
X=(x_{ij})_{1\le i,j\le m},
$$

where $x_{ij}=x_{(i-1)m+j}$. By definition,

$$
Q_m(X)=1
$$

if and only if every row and every column of $X$ contains at least one $1$.

Fix an arbitrary permutation $\pi$ of the variables. We must prove that

$$
B(Q_m^\pi)=\Omega(2^m/m^2).
$$

The proof is based on counting distinct beads arising from suitable subtables.

Let

$$
R=\{r_1,\ldots,r_m\},\qquad
C=\{c_1,\ldots,c_m\},
$$

denote the row and column constraints. A row constraint $r_i$ is satisfied as soon as some variable in row $i$ has been assigned the value $1$; similarly, a column constraint $c_j$ is satisfied as soon as some variable in column $j$ has been assigned the value $1$.

Process the variables in the order prescribed by $\pi$. After any initial segment of this order, let

$$
S_R\subseteq R,\qquad
S_C\subseteq C
$$

be the sets of row and column constraints already satisfied.

Each variable belongs to exactly one row and one column. Consequently, assigning a single variable can enlarge $S_R$ by at most one element and can enlarge $S_C$ by at most one element.

For every subset $A\subseteq R$, define

$$
t(A)=\min\{t:|S_R|=|A|,\ S_R=A\},
$$

whenever such a stage exists.

Among the $m2^m$ pairs $(A,i)$ with $A\subseteq R$ and $0\le i<m$, two different subsets can have the same value of $t(A)$ only if they have different cardinalities. Hence, by the pigeonhole principle, there exists an integer $k$ with

$$
0\le k\le m
$$

such that at least

$$
\frac{\binom mk}{m+1}
$$

subsets $A$ of cardinality $k$ occur as the satisfied row set after some initial segment.

Since

$$
\max_{0\le k\le m}\binom mk
 \ge
\frac{2^m}{m+1},
$$

there exists such a $k$ for which the number of attainable subsets satisfies

$$
\frac{\binom mk}{m+1}
\ge
\frac{2^m}{(m+1)^2}
=
\Omega\!\left(\frac{2^m}{m^2}\right).
$$

Fix this value of $k$.

Consider two distinct attainable subsets $A\ne A'$ of cardinality $k$. Let $\tau_A$ and $\tau_{A'}$ denote the subtables corresponding to the two partial assignments that first realize these row sets.

The residual function represented by $\tau_A$ depends on the set of rows not yet satisfied. Indeed, every row in $R\setminus A$ must still receive a $1$, while every row in $A$ already satisfies its row requirement permanently. Therefore the collection of remaining legal completions differs whenever $A\ne A'$.

To make this explicit, choose a row

$$
r\in A\triangle A'.
$$

Assume, without loss of generality, that

$$
r\in A\setminus A'.
$$

Complete all remaining variables so that every unsatisfied column receives exactly one $1$, and every row except $r$ satisfies its row requirement. Since row $r$ is already satisfied in state $A$, the resulting completion is accepted by $\tau_A$. Since row $r$ is still unsatisfied in state $A'$, the identical completion is rejected by $\tau_{A'}$.

Thus,

$$
\tau_A\ne\tau_{A'}.
$$

Hence all subtables obtained from these attainable subsets are distinct.

Each such subtable is the truth table of a subfunction that still depends on its first remaining variable. Therefore its root is a bead, by the correspondence between beads and BDD nodes established in Section 7.1.4. Distinct subtables have distinct roots, because every truth table has a unique root.

Consequently the BDD contains at least

$$
\Omega\!\left(\frac{2^m}{m^2}\right)
$$

distinct beads.

Therefore

$$
B(Q_m^\pi)
=
\Omega\!\left(\frac{2^m}{m^2}\right)
$$

for every permutation $\pi$.

This completes the proof.

∎
