[Section 7.1.4: Binary Decision Diagrams](../)

**Exercise 37.** [*M20*] [M20] (R. L. Rivest and J. Vuillemin, 1976.) A Boolean function f(x1, . . . , xn) is
called evasive if every FBDD for f contains a downward path of length n. Let G(z) be
the generating function for f, as in exercise 25. Prove that f is evasive if G(−1) ̸= 0.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m17s  

---

## Solution

Let $f(x_1,\ldots,x_n)$ be given and let

$$
G(z)=\sum_{x\in\{0,1\}^n} f(x)\,z^{x_1+\cdots+x_n}.
$$

Then

$$
G(-1)=\sum_{x\in\{0,1\}^n} f(x)\,(-1)^{x_1+\cdots+x_n}.
$$

Assume $f$ is not evasive. Then there exists an FBDD for $f$ in which every root-to-sink path has length at most $n-1$. Along any such path, at least one variable, say $x_j$, does not appear as a branch variable. Fix a root-to-sink path $P$, and let $S_P\subseteq \{0,1\}^n$ be the set of all inputs that follow $P$. The set $S_P$ is determined by fixing the variables tested along $P$, while all variables not tested along $P$ remain free. In particular, since $x_j$ is not tested on $P$, the set $S_P$ is closed under flipping $x_j$.

Let $f_P$ be the common value of $f(x)$ for all $x\in S_P$, determined by the sink reached by $P$. The contribution of $S_P$ to $G(-1)$ is

$$
f_P \sum_{x\in S_P} (-1)^{x_1+\cdots+x_n}.
$$

Partition $S_P$ into pairs $(x, x^{(j)})$, where $x^{(j)}$ is obtained from $x$ by flipping $x_j$. These pairs are disjoint and exhaust $S_P$, since $x_j$ is unrestricted on $P$. For each pair,

$$
(-1)^{x_1+\cdots+x_n} + (-1)^{x_1+\cdots+(1-x_j)+\cdots+x_n} = 0,
$$

because the two terms differ by a factor of $-1$. Hence

$$
\sum_{x\in S_P} (-1)^{x_1+\cdots+x_n} = 0,
$$

and therefore the contribution of $S_P$ to $G(-1)$ is $0$.

Every input $x\in\{0,1\}^n$ follows exactly one root-to-sink path, so the sets $S_P$ form a partition of $\{0,1\}^n$. Summing over all paths gives

$$
G(-1)=0.
$$

The contrapositive yields that if $G(-1)\neq 0$, then no FBDD for $f$ can have all root-to-sink paths of length at most $n-1$. Hence every FBDD contains a path of length $n$, so $f$ is evasive.

This completes the proof. ∎
