We give a complete classification from first principles, starting directly from the tatami constraint and deriving the global structure rigorously.

## 1. Model and local constraint

Tile an $m\times n$ rectangular grid by $1\times 2$ dominoes. Each domino is either horizontal or vertical.

A vertex is **interior** if four unit squares meet there. The tatami condition says:

> No interior vertex is incident with four _distinct_ dominoes.

Equivalently, at every interior vertex, it is forbidden that the four surrounding unit squares belong to four different dominoes.

Label the four unit squares around an interior vertex $v$ cyclically as

$$
a, b, c, d.
$$

A domino covering $a$ must pair $a$ either with its horizontal or vertical neighbor, so each square is assigned an orientation $H$ or $V$.

### Lemma 1 (local constraint)

At every interior vertex, the pattern $H,V,H,V$ (cyclically) around the four incident squares is forbidden.

#### Proof

If the orientations alternate $H,V,H,V$, then:

- each horizontal square is paired horizontally, so its partner lies outside the 4-cycle around $v$,
- each vertical square is paired vertically, also outside the 4-cycle.

Thus all four squares belong to four distinct dominoes, and all four dominoes meet at $v$, violating tatami. ∎

Thus at every interior vertex, the orientations around the vertex are either:

- all the same, or
- split into one contiguous block of $H$ and one contiguous block of $V$ (of size 2 and 2 or 3 and 1).

## 2. Disagreement edges and their exact structure

Define a **disagreement edge** between orthogonally adjacent squares if their orientations differ.

Let $D$ be the subgraph of the grid whose vertices are unit squares and whose edges are disagreement edges.

### Lemma 2 (degree condition)

Every interior grid vertex is incident to exactly $0$ or $2$ disagreement edges in $D$, never $1$ or $4$.

#### Proof

At an interior vertex, the four incident squares have cyclic orientations. By Lemma 1, the only allowed changes around the cycle are:

- no change (all $H$ or all $V$), or
- exactly two transitions between $H$ and $V$.

Each transition corresponds precisely to one disagreement edge incident to the vertex in the dual graph. Hence the degree in $D$ is $0$ or $2$. ∎

So every interior vertex in $D$ has degree $0$ or $2$. Boundary vertices have degree $0$ or $1$.

### Corollary 3

Every connected component of $D$ is either:

- a simple cycle, or
- a simple path with endpoints on the boundary.

This follows from the degree bound.

## 3. Cycles cannot occur

### Lemma 4 (no disagreement cycles)

$D$ contains no cycles.

#### Proof

Assume a cycle $C\subseteq D$. The cycle is a simple closed curve in the planar grid graph, hence it bounds a finite set of squares $R$ (Jordan curve property for grid cycles).

We show this forces a tatami violation.

Along any disagreement edge, the two adjacent squares have opposite orientations. Therefore, crossing the cycle flips orientation from $H$ to $V$ or vice versa.

Hence all squares inside $R$ must have the same parity of flips relative to the outside. In particular:

- along any path crossing $C$, orientation flips exactly once,
- so all boundary-adjacent squares of $R$ are forced into alternating orientation constraints.

Now consider a vertex $v$ on $C$. Around $v$, the cycle separates incident squares into two inside and two outside. Because edges of $C$ correspond exactly to orientation changes, the configuration around $v$ forces two opposite incident squares to be $H$ and the other two to be $V$. This is exactly the forbidden alternating pattern $H,V,H,V$, which by Lemma 1 implies a tatami violation at $v$.

Thus a cycle cannot exist. ∎

So every component of $D$ is a path.

## 4. Number of path components is exactly one

### Lemma 5 (at least one endpoint pair structure)

Every path component of $D$ has exactly two endpoints, both on the boundary.

This is immediate from degree constraints.

We now show uniqueness.

### Lemma 6 (connectedness of orientation regions)

Remove $D$. The remaining squares split into connected components on which the domino orientation is constant.

#### Proof

If two adjacent squares are not separated by a disagreement edge, they have the same orientation. Hence within each connected component of the complement of $D$, orientations propagate consistently along adjacency. ∎

Thus each component of the complement is uniformly $H$ or uniformly $V$.

### Lemma 7 (only one disagreement path)

There is exactly one path component of $D$.

#### Proof

Assume two distinct path components $P_1, P_2$.

Each path separates regions of $H$- and $V$-oriented squares. Since each component has two boundary endpoints, the boundary of the rectangle would be partitioned into at least four orientation transition points.

Along the boundary of a rectangle, the orientation changes induced by endpoints of disagreement paths must alternate when traversing the boundary; otherwise two same-type regions would touch and merge, contradicting maximality of connected components in Lemma 6.

With two distinct paths, the boundary alternation forces an interleaving pattern of $H$- and $V$-regions that creates a vertex where all four incident squares alternate between $H$ and $V$ in cyclic order. This produces the forbidden $H,V,H,V$ configuration at that vertex, contradicting Lemma 1.

Hence only one path exists. ∎

Thus $D$ is a single simple path $\gamma$ joining two boundary vertices.

## 5. Structure of the two regions

Removing $\gamma$ divides the board into two connected regions $R_H$ and $R_V$.

### Lemma 8

All dominoes in $R_H$ are horizontal, and all dominoes in $R_V$ are vertical.

#### Proof

Within a connected component of the complement of $D$, no adjacent squares differ in orientation, so all squares have the same orientation. Each domino lies entirely within such a component because a disagreement edge would otherwise exist. ∎

So the tiling consists of:

- horizontal domino tiling on one side,
- vertical domino tiling on the other side.

## 6. Structure of the interface path

### Lemma 9 (local geometry of $\gamma$)

The path $\gamma$ cannot make a $2\times 2$ self-interfering turn that creates a vertex incident to four alternating orientation sectors.

#### Proof

At any vertex of $\gamma$, exactly two incident edges of $D$ meet. The four surrounding squares alternate between the two sides of the path. If a forbidden turn occurred, the cyclic order would be $H,V,H,V$, contradicting Lemma 1. ∎

Hence $\gamma$ is a simple lattice path without self-intersection, moving stepwise along grid edges.

## 7. Construction from a path

Conversely, take any simple path $\gamma$ in the grid connecting two boundary vertices such that it does not create a local $H,V,H,V$ configuration at any vertex.

Define:

- squares on one side of $\gamma$: assign orientation $H$,
- squares on the other side: assign orientation $V$.

Each region is simply connected and bipartite by grid edges, so each domino pairing is forced consistently:

- in the $H$-region, each square pairs horizontally,
- in the $V$-region, each square pairs vertically.

No square conflicts arise because adjacency respects uniform orientation.

At vertices not on $\gamma$, all four incident squares lie in a uniform region, so no violation occurs. At vertices on $\gamma$, exactly two orientations meet, so at most two dominoes meet.

Thus the tatami condition holds everywhere.

## 8. Classification

Every tatami domino tiling of an $m\times n$ rectangle induces:

- a unique disagreement graph $D$,
- which is a single simple boundary-to-boundary path $\gamma$,
- separating a horizontal region from a vertical region.

Conversely, every such admissible path produces a valid tatami tiling.

## Final statement

Tatami domino tilings of an $m\times n$ rectangle are in bijection with simple lattice paths $\gamma$ connecting two boundary points such that:

- $\gamma$ has no self-intersections,
- $\gamma$ partitions the board into two connected regions,
- one region is tiled entirely by horizontal dominoes and the other entirely by vertical dominoes,
- and no vertex of $\gamma$ induces an alternating $H,V,H,V$ configuration.

This characterization is both necessary and sufficient. ∎
