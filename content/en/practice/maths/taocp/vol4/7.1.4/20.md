[Section 7.1.4: Binary Decision Diagrams](../)

**Exercise 20.** [*15*] [15] Devise a fast way to compute the Thue–Morse weights (15) for 1 ≤j ≤n.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m12s  

---

Let $w_j$ denote the Thue–Morse weight, so

$$
w_j = \tau(j-1),
$$

where $\tau(k)$ is the parity of the number of 1s in the binary representation of $k$.

The key identity is the standard bit recursion

$$
\tau(k) = \tau\!\left(\left\lfloor \frac{k}{2} \right\rfloor\right) \oplus (k \bmod 2).
$$

This follows because writing $k = 2q + b_0$ with $b_0 \in \{0,1\}$, the binary digits of $k$ are those of $q$ shifted left plus the least significant bit $b_0$, so the parity of bits is the parity of $q$ plus $b_0$ modulo 2.

Substitute $k = j-1$. Then

$$
w_j = \tau(j-1) = \tau\!\left(\left\lfloor \frac{j-1}{2} \right\rfloor\right) \oplus ((j-1) \bmod 2).
$$

Now express this in terms of previously computed $w$-values. Observe that

$$
w_{\lfloor j/2 \rfloor} = \tau\!\left(\left\lfloor \frac{j}{2} \right\rfloor - 1\right).
$$

We distinguish cases.

### Case 1: $j = 2m$ (even)

Then $j-1 = 2m-1$, so

$$
\left\lfloor \frac{j-1}{2} \right\rfloor = m-1,
\quad (j-1)\bmod 2 = 1.
$$

Hence

$$
w_{2m} = \tau(m-1) \oplus 1 = w_m \oplus 1.
$$

### Case 2: $j = 2m+1$ (odd)

Then $j-1 = 2m$, so

$$
\left\lfloor \frac{j-1}{2} \right\rfloor = m,
\quad (j-1)\bmod 2 = 0.
$$

Hence

$$
w_{2m+1} = \tau(m) = w_{m+1}.
$$

We therefore obtain a correct sequentially usable recurrence:

$$
w_1 = 0,
$$

and for $m \ge 1$,

$$
w_{2m} = w_m \oplus 1, \qquad w_{2m+1} = w_{m+1}.
$$

### Algorithm

Compute $w_1, \dots, w_n$ in increasing order:

For $j = 1$:

$$
w_1 = 0.
$$

For $m = 1,2,\dots,\lfloor n/2 \rfloor$:

$$
w_{2m} = w_m \oplus 1,
$$

and if $2m+1 \le n$,

$$
w_{2m+1} = w_{m+1}.
$$

### Correctness

The derivation follows directly from the identity

$$
\tau(k) = \tau(\lfloor k/2 \rfloor) \oplus (k \bmod 2)
$$

applied to $k = j-1$, with the resulting expressions rewritten in terms of already computed $w$-values. The even and odd cases avoid any invalid index shifting and preserve exact equality.

### Complexity

Each value $w_j$ is computed in $O(1)$ time, and each index is processed once, so the total running time is $O(n)$, with $O(n)$ storage.
