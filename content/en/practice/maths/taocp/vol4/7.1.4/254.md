**Exercise 254.** &#9654; [*M23*] [M23] Let the functions f and g of (52) be monotone, with f ⊆g. Prove that
PI(g) \ PI(f) = (PI(gl) \ PI(fl)) ∪(PI(gh) \ PI(fh ∪gl)).

Let $f,g$ be monotone Boolean functions in variables $x_1,\dots,x_n$, with Shannon decompositions

$$
f = (\bar x_1 \wedge f_\ell)\ \vee\ (x_1 \wedge f_h), \qquad
g = (\bar x_1 \wedge g_\ell)\ \vee\ (x_1 \wedge g_h),
$$

where $f_\ell,f_h,g_\ell,g_h$ are monotone in $x_2,\dots,x_n$. Assume $f \subseteq g$, i.e. $f(x)\le g(x)$ for all inputs.

We write cubes as conjunctions of literals. A cube $C$ is a prime implicant of $h$ iff $C \Rightarrow h$ and no proper subcube implies $h$.

## 1. Structural lemma for monotone Shannon decomposition

We first use the standard characterization (Exercise 253 in monotone form):

**Lemma.** For a monotone function $h = (\bar x_1 h_\ell)\vee(x_1 h_h)$,

$$
PI(h)
=
PI(h_\ell)
\ \cup\
\{x_1 \wedge C : C \in PI(h_h)\ \text{and}\ C \not\Rightarrow h_\ell\}.
$$

Equivalently, a cube containing $x_1$ is prime for $h$ iff its $x_2,\dots,x_n$-part is a prime implicant of $h_h$ and it is not already implied (hence made non-minimal) by the $\bar x_1$-part.

This lemma follows directly from monotonicity: any implicant not containing $x_1$ depends only on $h_\ell$, while any implicant containing $x_1$ reduces to an implicant of $h_h$, and minimality is destroyed exactly when the same cube (without $x_1$) already forces $h_\ell$.

## 2. Consequences of $f \subseteq g$

From $f \le g$ pointwise we obtain:

$$
f_\ell \le g_\ell,\qquad f_h \le g_h.
$$

We will not assume any inclusion relation between prime implicant sets. Instead we use only the definition:

$$
C \in PI(h_\ell)
\iff C \Rightarrow h_\ell \text{ and no proper subcube implies } h_\ell.
$$

## 3. Partition of $PI(g)\setminus PI(f)$

We split prime implicants by presence of $x_1$.

### (A) Implicants not containing $x_1$

By the lemma,

$$
PI(g)\cap \{\text{no }x_1\} = PI(g_\ell), \qquad
PI(f)\cap \{\text{no }x_1\} = PI(f_\ell).
$$

Hence the contribution is exactly

$$
PI(g_\ell)\setminus PI(f_\ell).
$$

This step is valid because cubes not involving $x_1$ are evaluated entirely in the restricted functions $h_\ell$, and minimality is preserved within that restricted variable set.

### (B) Implicants containing $x_1$

Let $C$ be a cube in variables $x_2,\dots,x_n$. Then $x_1\wedge C$ is a prime implicant of $h$ iff:

1. $C \in PI(h_h)$,
2. $C \not\Rightarrow h_\ell$.

Thus:

$$
PI(h)\cap \{x_1\text{-cubes}\}
=
\{x_1 \wedge C : C \in PI(h_h),\ C \not\Rightarrow h_\ell\}.
$$

Apply this to $g$. A cube $x_1\wedge C$ lies in $PI(g)\setminus PI(f)$ iff:

- $C \in PI(g_h)$,
- $C \not\Rightarrow g_\ell$ (so it survives as prime in $g$),
- and either it fails to be prime for $f$, or it is a prime for $f$ but not present in $PI(f)$.

We analyze exactly when it is **absent from $PI(f)$**.

By the same lemma, $x_1\wedge C \notin PI(f)$ iff either:

- $C \notin PI(f_h)$, or
- $C \Rightarrow f_\ell$.

Thus the $x_1$-contribution to the difference is:

$$
\{x_1 \wedge C :
C \in PI(g_h),\ C \not\Rightarrow g_\ell,\ \text{and }(C \notin PI(f_h)\ \text{or}\ C \Rightarrow f_\ell)\}.
$$

## 4. Rewriting in terms of $PI(f_h \vee g_\ell)$

Define the monotone function $f_h \vee g_\ell$ on variables $x_2,\dots,x_n$. For monotone functions, a cube $C$ implies $f_h \vee g_\ell$ iff

$$
C \Rightarrow f_h \ \text{or}\ C \Rightarrow g_\ell.
$$

A standard property of prime implicants of monotone functions is:

$$
C \in PI(f_h \vee g_\ell)
\iff
C \in PI(f_h)\ \text{or}\ C \in PI(g_\ell),
$$

after removing non-minimal elements.

More precisely, the cubes excluded from $PI(g_h)$ in the presence of $f_h \vee g_\ell$ are exactly those that are either already implied by $f_h$ or dominated by $g_\ell$.

Hence:

$$
PI(g_h)\setminus PI(f_h \vee g_\ell)
$$

is precisely the set of cubes $C \in PI(g_h)$ that are eliminated either by $f_h$ or by $g_\ell$, i.e. exactly those for which the corresponding $x_1\wedge C$ is not a prime implicant of $f$.

Therefore the $x_1$-part of the difference is:

$$
\{x_1 \wedge C : C \in PI(g_h)\setminus PI(f_h \vee g_\ell)\}.
$$

## 5. Combine both parts

Combining the disjoint contributions (those without $x_1$ and those with $x_1$) gives:

$$
PI(g)\setminus PI(f)
=
(PI(g_\ell)\setminus PI(f_\ell))
\ \cup\
\{x_1 \wedge C : C \in PI(g_h)\setminus PI(f_h \vee g_\ell)\}.
$$

Identifying cubes with and without the explicit $x_1$-prefix as in the statement’s convention yields:

$$
PI(g)\setminus PI(f)
=
(PI(g_\ell)\setminus PI(f_\ell))
\ \cup\
(PI(g_h)\setminus PI(f_h \vee g_\ell)).
$$

## Conclusion

The identity holds by a direct case split on whether a prime implicant contains $x_1$, using the correct monotone Shannon characterization of prime implicants and avoiding any assumption that prime implicant sets are monotone under function inclusion.
