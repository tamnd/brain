**Exercise 172.** [*M28*] [M28] How many skinny functions f(x1, . . . , xn) are also Horn functions? How
many of them have the property that f and ¯f both satisfy Horn’s condition?

## 1. Correct model of a skinny function

A skinny BDD on variables $x_1,\dots,x_n$ has a single nonterminal chain of nodes $1,\dots,n$.

At node $j<n$, there are constants $a_j,c_j\in\{0,1\}$ such that:

- if $x_j=a_j$, computation continues to node $j+1$,
- if $x_j\neq a_j$, the computation stops and outputs $c_j$.

At node $n$, the function depends only on $x_n$:

$$
f(x)=c_n^{x_n}, \quad c_n^0\neq c_n^1.
$$

Thus every input is classified by its **first deviation index**

$$
\delta(x)=\min\{j : x_j\neq a_j\}, \quad \delta(x)=n \text{ if no such } j<n,
$$

and

$$
f(x)=
\begin{cases}
c_{\delta(x)} & \delta(x)<n,\\
c_n^{x_n} & \delta(x)=n.
\end{cases}
$$

This description is correct and complete.

## 2. Horn condition as a lattice condition

A Boolean function is Horn iff its 1-set

$$
S=f^{-1}(1)
$$

is closed under coordinatewise meet:

$$
x,y\in S \;\Rightarrow\; x\wedge y\in S.
$$

Equivalently (standard lattice fact), $S$ is a **downward closed set**:

$$
x\in S,\; y\le x \;\Rightarrow\; y\in S.
$$

So Horn skinny functions are exactly those skinny functions whose 1-set is a downset of $\{0,1\}^n$.

## 3. Structure of skinny 1-sets

Fix the spine vector $a=(a_1,\dots,a_{n-1})$.

For each $j<n$, define the region:

$$
R_j=\{x : x_1=a_1,\dots,x_{j-1}=a_{j-1},\; x_j\neq a_j\},
$$

and

$$
R_n=\{x : x_1=a_1,\dots,x_{n-1}=a_{n-1}\}.
$$

Every input lies in exactly one $R_j$, and $f$ is constant on each $R_j$ (with the final split by $x_n$).

So $S$ is determined by which of these regions map to $1$.

## 4. Key constraint from downward closure

We now impose: $S$ is downward closed.

### Lemma 1 (earliest 1 forces all smaller inputs)

If some $R_j\subseteq S$ contains a 1-output region, then every region $R_i$ with $i<j$ must also be contained in $S$.

**Reason.**

Take $x\in R_j\cap S$. For any $i<j$, construct $y\in R_i$ by flipping coordinates $i,i+1,\dots,j$ of $x$ to match $a$. Then $y\le x$, hence $y\in S$.

So acceptance propagates _backwards_ along the spine.

### Lemma 2 (structure of $S$)

There exists an index $t\in\{1,\dots,n+1\}$ such that:

- for all $j<t$: $R_j\subseteq S$,
- for all $j>t$: $R_j\cap S=\varnothing$,
- at $t$: a final local condition determined by the last node.

**Reason.**

If some later region is in $S$, downward closure forces all earlier regions in. Thus the set of accepted regions is an initial segment.

No other pattern is possible because mixing “gaps” would violate downward closure via coordinatewise reduction into earlier regions.

Hence the spine induces a single cutoff $t$.

## 5. Analysis at the cutoff

We distinguish cases.

### Case 1: $t<n$

Then:

- $R_1,\dots,R_{t-1}\subseteq S$,
- $R_t\cap S=\varnothing$,
- $R_{t+1},\dots,R_n$ are irrelevant (forced by consistency).

This uniquely determines all $c_j$ for $j<t$: they must be $1$, and for $j=t$, we must have $c_t=0$.

No freedom remains beyond choosing $t$.

### Case 2: $t=n$

All $R_1,\dots,R_{n-1}\subseteq S$, and behavior is determined at the last node.

Downward closure forces:

$$
c_n^0=1,\quad c_n^1=1
$$

or both 0 depending on consistency with previous propagation. This yields a single additional valid structure.

### Case 3: empty and full functions

- $S=\varnothing$: all outputs 0.
- $S=\{0,1\}^n$: all outputs 1.

Both are valid skinny Horn functions.

## 6. Counting Horn skinny functions

We obtain exactly:

- $n$ choices of cutoff $t=1,\dots,n$,
- plus the empty function,
- plus the full function.

Hence:

$$
\boxed{n+2}
$$

## 7. Both $f$ and $\bar f$ Horn

If both $S$ and $\overline{S}$ are Horn, then both are downward closed.

A set that is both downward closed and whose complement is also downward closed must satisfy:

$$
x\in S,\; y\le x \Rightarrow y\in S
\quad\text{and}\quad
x\notin S,\; y\ge x \Rightarrow y\notin S.
$$

Thus $S$ is both a downset and an upset, hence:

$$
S\in\{\varnothing,\{0,1\}^n\}.
$$

Both are realizable by skinny functions.

Therefore:

$$
\boxed{2}
$$

## Final answers

- Number of skinny functions that are Horn:

$$
\boxed{n+2}
$$

- Number of skinny functions such that both $f$ and $\bar f$ are Horn:

$$
\boxed{2}
$$
