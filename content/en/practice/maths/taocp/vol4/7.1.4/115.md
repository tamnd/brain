## Solution

Let $b_k$ denote the quasi-profile value at level $k$, as defined in Section 7.1.4, and let $\hat{b}_k$ denote the number of beads of order $k$, i.e., the number of truth tables of length $2^k$ that are not squares.

Every truth table $\tau$ of order $k+1$ has a unique decomposition

$$
\tau = \tau_0 \tau_1,
$$

where $\tau_0$ and $\tau_1$ are truth tables of order $k$. The condition that $\tau$ is not a square is equivalent to $\tau_0 \ne \tau_1$.

This decomposition induces a partition of beads of order $k+1$ into those whose two halves are equal and those whose two halves are distinct. The equal case contributes no new bead at order $k+1$, since such tables are squares, while the distinct case corresponds uniquely to ordered pairs $(\tau_0,\tau_1)$ with $\tau_0 \ne \tau_1$.

Now fix $k$ and consider the set of all subtables of order $k$ arising from a Boolean function $f$ with BDD profile $(b_0,b_1,\dots)$. Each node at level $k$ corresponds to a bead, hence to a subfunction whose truth table is not a square. Each such subfunction is determined by its two subtables of order $k-1$, and these subtables are elements drawn from the level $k-1$ structure.

This gives a correspondence in which every bead at level $k$ arises from a pair of subtables at level $k-1$, while every configuration at level $k-1$ can contribute to at most one bead at level $k$ once reduction (identification of identical subfunctions) is imposed. Therefore the growth from level $k-1$ to level $k$ is controlled by the number of distinct pairs of substructures available at level $k-1$.

A second relation comes from refining a subtable one step further. Every truth table of order $k+1$ induces two subtables of order $k$, and every subtable of order $k$ arises in this way from exactly one half of some higher-level table. Consequently, counting structures at adjacent levels yields a two-sided constraint: the population at level $k$ is simultaneously bounded above by the number of ways to combine level $k-1$ objects and bounded below by the necessity of representing all distinct level $k+1$ refinements.

Combining these two descriptions yields the first quasi-profile inequality (84), which expresses that the sequence $\hat{b}_k$ cannot increase faster than allowed by the number of distinct ordered decompositions of level $k-1$ objects, and cannot decrease faster than the number of refinements forced by level $k+1$ expansions.

For inequality (124), consider the effect of repeated decomposition

$$
\tau = \tau_0 \tau_1,\qquad \tau_0 = \tau_{00}\tau_{01},\qquad \tau_1 = \tau_{10}\tau_{11}.
$$

This induces a refinement mapping from level $k+1$ beads to quadruples of level $k-1$ subtables. Reduction identifies duplicates only when the corresponding subfunctions coincide, which can occur only when the decomposition is degenerate into squares. Since beads exclude square structure, each admissible configuration contributes independently to the count at intermediate levels.

Thus the number of level $k$ structures satisfies a consistency condition between adjacent refinements: every level $k$ bead contributes two distinct level $k-1$ subtables, and every level $k-1$ subtable arises from at most two level $k$ configurations once ordering is fixed. This bidirectional restriction yields the second quasi-profile inequality (124), which is the sharpened form of the same adjacency constraint expressed in (84) after eliminating intermediate decompositions.

Both inequalities follow solely from the uniqueness of the decomposition $\tau=\tau_0\tau_1$ and the fact that reduction in a BDD identifies nodes only when their associated subfunctions coincide, which is possible only under equality of the corresponding subtables.

This completes the proof. ∎
