## Solution

Let the configurations in (104) and (106) describe the ordering of the same set of states before and after a complete sifting pass. Sifting operates by repeatedly swapping adjacent states and recomputing the objective function locally, until the chosen state reaches a position where no adjacent swap improves the value.

Each individual state can therefore move through a sequence of intermediate positions determined by a chain of adjacent interchanges. The representation in (104) records only the initial ordering, while (106) records only the final ordering after all such local exchanges have terminated.

From these two snapshots, only the initial and final positions of each state are observable. The intermediate trajectory of a state depends on which improving adjacent swaps were chosen during sifting, and different valid sequences of adjacent swaps can produce the same final configuration while moving a given state through different intermediate paths.

Consequently, the displacement of a state cannot be recovered from (104) and (106) alone as a single determined quantity. The data do not encode how many intermediate swaps involved that state, nor whether it temporarily moved farther in one direction before settling into its final position.

Therefore, the comparison of (104) with (106) determines only the net change in ordering, not the total distance traveled by each individual state during the sifting process.
