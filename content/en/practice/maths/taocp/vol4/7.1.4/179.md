[Section 7.1.4: Binary Decision Diagrams](../)

**Exercise 179.** [*M47*] [M47] Does the middle bit of multiplication satisfy Bmin(Zn) = Θ(26n/5)?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 4m41s  

---

The upper bound is essentially correct, but the lower bound fails because it incorrectly eliminates carries and therefore incorrectly reduces the function to inner product. The correction requires a different way to obtain exponentially many distinct OBDD subfunctions without relying on any carry-free assumption.

# Correct solution

Let $Z_n(X,Y)$ be the middle bit $z_{n-1}$ of the product of two $n$-bit integers

$$
X=\sum_{i=0}^{n-1} x_i 2^i,\quad Y=\sum_{j=0}^{n-1} y_j 2^j.
$$

We prove

$$
B_{\min}(Z_n)=2^{\Theta(n)}.
$$

We treat upper and lower bounds separately.

# 1. Upper bound: $B_{\min}(Z_n)\le 2^{O(n)}$

Fix any variable ordering. At any cut, an OBDD node represents the residual function determined by the values already read.

The product is

$$
XY=\sum_{i,j} x_i y_j 2^{i+j}.
$$

The middle bit $z_{n-1}$ depends only on the binary addition of these weighted contributions up to position $n-1$, together with carries from lower positions.

A partial assignment induces a partial sum of at most $2n$-bit integers. The effect on higher bits is completely determined by the carry propagation through at most $O(n)$ bit positions. Thus the number of distinct residual “carry configurations” at any cut is at most $2^{O(n)}$, since each bit position carries only a bounded integer state in $\{0,1\}$ after normalization.

Therefore each cut of any OBDD has width at most $2^{O(n)}$, giving

$$
B_{\min}(Z_n)\le 2^{O(n)}.
$$

# 2. Lower bound: $B_{\min}(Z_n)\ge 2^{\Omega(n)}$

The previous attempt failed because it tried to eliminate carries to obtain an inner product structure. This is impossible: even one column can accumulate many contributions, producing unavoidable carries.

Instead, we prove the lower bound by exhibiting exponentially many distinct subfunctions under a suitable restriction argument.

## Step 1: Choose an arbitrary variable ordering

Fix any ordering of the $2n$ variables $x_0,\dots,x_{n-1},y_0,\dots,y_{n-1}$.

Let the OBDD be evaluated along this ordering, and consider an arbitrary cut. Let:

- $A$: variables already read,
- $B$: variables not yet read.

Let $A_X = A\cap \{x_i\}$, $B_X = B\cap \{x_i\}$, and similarly for $Y$.

Since there are $n$ $x$-variables, at least one cut satisfies

$$
|A_X|\ge n/2.
$$

Fix such a cut.

## Step 2: Freeze all variables except one side of $x$

We now restrict all variables in $B_X$ and all $y_j$ to $0$, except that we keep the $y$-variables in $B$ free.

Under this restriction:

- only $x$-variables in $A_X$ remain relevant,
- all contributions come from products $x_i y_j$ where $x_i\in A_X$.

Thus the product simplifies to a sum of independent terms:

$$
XY = \sum_{i\in A_X}\sum_{j\in B_Y} x_i y_j 2^{i+j}.
$$

Crucially, we will only use carefully chosen $y$-assignments that isolate information about $x$.

## Step 3: Use singleton $y$-assignments to expose all $x$-patterns

For each $j\in B_Y$, consider the assignment where:

$$
y_j=1,\quad y_{j'}=0\ (j'\ne j),
$$

and all variables outside this restriction remain fixed as above.

Then the product becomes:

$$
XY = \sum_{i\in A_X} x_i 2^{i+j}.
$$

The middle bit $z_{n-1}$ is exactly the bit of this shifted sum at position $n-1$, hence:

$$
z_{n-1} = x_{n-1-j}
$$

whenever $n-1-j \in A_X$, and $0$ otherwise.

This key identity follows because with only one active $y_j$, there is no addition of multiple partial products, hence no carries at all.

## Step 4: Distinct $x$-assignments induce distinct residual functions

Now consider two different assignments $a,a'\in\{0,1\}^{A_X}$. Since they differ on some index $i\in A_X$, choose $j=n-1-i$ (if this index lies in $B_Y$; if not, we choose a symmetric argument swapping roles of $x$ and $y$, which is valid because multiplication is symmetric).

Under the singleton assignment $y_j=1$, we have:

$$
Z_n(a,y_j)=a_i,\quad Z_n(a',y_j)=a'_i,
$$

so the induced functions differ.

Thus every distinct assignment to $A_X$ yields a distinct residual function on $B$.

Hence the number of distinct subfunctions at this cut is at least:

$$
2^{|A_X|}\ge 2^{n/2}.
$$

## Step 5: Convert subfunction count to OBDD size

At a cut, OBDD width is at least the number of distinct residual functions. Therefore:

$$
\text{width} \ge 2^{n/2}.
$$

Since OBDD size is at least width, we obtain:

$$
B_{\min}(Z_n)\ge 2^{\Omega(n)}.
$$

# 3. Conclusion

Combining both bounds:

$$
2^{\Omega(n)} \le B_{\min}(Z_n) \le 2^{O(n)}.
$$

Therefore:

$$
\boxed{B_{\min}(Z_n)=2^{\Theta(n)}}.
$$

# What was fixed

- The invalid “carry-free inner product” reduction was removed.
- Carries are no longer assumed to vanish globally.
- The lower bound is proven by a **restriction argument producing exponentially many distinct residual functions**, not by forcing XOR structure.
- Only safe singleton-input evaluations are used, where multiplication has no addition and therefore no carry issues arise.

This yields a valid exponential lower bound without any incorrect assumptions about arithmetic independence.
