## Corrected Solution

Let a fixed variable ordering $x_1 \prec x_2 \prec \cdots \prec x_n$ be given throughout.

Equation (137) is the Shannon decomposition with respect to the first variable in the ordering. For every Boolean function $f(x_1,\dots,x_n)$, define its cofactors

$$
f_0(x_2,\dots,x_n) := f(0,x_2,\dots,x_n), \quad
f_1(x_2,\dots,x_n) := f(1,x_2,\dots,x_n).
$$

Then (137) states that the truth table (or equivalently the function) satisfies

$$
f = (x_1 \cdot f_1) \lor (\bar{x}_1 \cdot f_0),
$$

or equivalently that $f$ is uniquely determined by the pair $(f_0,f_1)$.

### 1. Subfunctions determined by partial assignments

For each prefix assignment $(a_1,\dots,a_k) \in \{0,1\}^k$, define the subfunction

$$
f_{a_1,\dots,a_k}(x_{k+1},\dots,x_n) := f(a_1,\dots,a_k,x_{k+1},\dots,x_n).
$$

This defines all cofactors of $f$. In particular, $f_{\emptyset} = f$.

Applying (137) repeatedly shows that every subfunction $f_{a_1,\dots,a_k}$ is uniquely determined by its two cofactors with respect to $x_{k+1}$:

$$
(f_{a_1,\dots,a_k})_0 = f_{a_1,\dots,a_k,0}, \quad
(f_{a_1,\dots,a_k})_1 = f_{a_1,\dots,a_k,1}.
$$

### 2. Construction of the ordered decision graph

We construct a directed acyclic graph whose nodes are subfunctions of $f$, identified up to equality.

Each node corresponding to a subfunction $g(x_{k+1},\dots,x_n)$ is placed at level $k$, meaning it is associated with variable $x_{k+1}$, unless $g$ is constant.

For a nonconstant subfunction $g$, define its two successors using (137):

$$
g_0(x_{k+2},\dots,x_n) := g(0,x_{k+2},\dots,x_n), \quad
g_1(x_{k+2},\dots,x_n) := g(1,x_{k+2},\dots,x_n).
$$

We draw directed edges labeled 0 and 1 from $g$ to $g_0$ and $g_1$, respectively.

Constant functions form terminal nodes $\bot$ (false) and $\top$ (true), with no outgoing edges.

### 3. Ordered structure and acyclicity

The construction respects the fixed ordering $x_1 \prec \cdots \prec x_n$ because a node at level $k$ depends only on variables $x_{k+1},\dots,x_n$, while its children depend only on $x_{k+2},\dots,x_n$. Thus every edge strictly increases the level index.

Define a rank function

$$
\rho(g) = n - k,
$$

where $k$ is the number of fixed variables in the subfunction $g$. Every edge goes from a node of rank $r$ to a node of rank $r-1$. Hence no directed cycle exists, and the graph is acyclic.

### 4. Reduction rules

We now define reduction on the constructed graph:

1. **Sharing rule:** if two nodes represent the same subfunction, they are identified (merged).
2. **Elimination rule:** if a node $g$ has identical successors,

$$
g_0 = g_1,
$$

then the node is removed and replaced by that common successor.

The second rule is essential and corrects the earlier incorrect claim: equality of children is not a contradiction, but a criterion for elimination.

After applying these rules exhaustively, we obtain a reduced ordered decision graph (OBDD).

### 5. Well-definedness of reduction

The elimination rule is consistent with (137): if $g_0 = g_1$, then

$$
g(x_{k+1},\dots,x_n) = g_0(x_{k+2},\dots,x_n),
$$

so $g$ does not depend on $x_{k+1}$. Therefore removing $g$ preserves the computed function.

Merging identical subfunctions is also sound since identical functions have identical cofactors, hence identical outgoing structure.

Thus reduction preserves semantics.

### 6. Uniqueness of the reduced OBDD

We prove that the reduced ordered decision diagram for $f$ is unique up to isomorphism.

We proceed by induction on the number of variables $n$.

#### Base case $n=0$

The function is constant, so the diagram is a single node $\bot$ or $\top$. Uniqueness is immediate.

#### Inductive step

Assume uniqueness holds for functions of $n-1$ variables.

Let $f(x_1,\dots,x_n)$ be given. By (137), it is uniquely determined by its cofactors $f_0$ and $f_1$.

Consider any reduced OBDD $G$ for $f$. Its root node must represent $f$. By construction, its two children must represent $f_0$ and $f_1$, because following the 0- and 1-edges corresponds exactly to substituting $x_1=0$ and $x_1=1$, again by (137).

By the induction hypothesis, the reduced OBDDs for $f_0$ and $f_1$ are unique up to isomorphism. Therefore the subgraphs rooted at the two children of the root are uniquely determined.

Since reduction identifies all isomorphic subgraphs, any two reduced OBDDs for $f$ must have:

- the same root label (representing $f$),
- isomorphic 0-subgraph (for $f_0$),
- isomorphic 1-subgraph (for $f_1$).

Hence the entire structure is uniquely determined up to isomorphism.

### 7. Correspondence with subfunctions

Each node in the reduced diagram corresponds to a distinct subfunction $f_{a_1,\dots,a_k}$. This correspondence is well-defined because:

- every node represents a Boolean function obtained by fixing a prefix,
- reduction merges exactly identical functions,
- no two distinct nodes can represent the same subfunction.

Thus nodes are in bijection with equivalence classes of subfunctions under equality.

### Conclusion

Using the fixed variable ordering and the Shannon decomposition (137), we construct a directed acyclic graph of cofactors, apply sound reduction rules, and obtain a canonical reduced ordered binary decision diagram.

The structure is acyclic, ordered, semantics-preserving, and unique up to isomorphism. This establishes Theorem S. ∎
