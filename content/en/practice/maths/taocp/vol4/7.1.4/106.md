**Exercise 106.** [*25*] [25] Let f $g$h denote the relation “f(x) = g(y) = 1 implies h(x∧y) = 1, for all
x and y.” Show that this relation can be evaluated in at most O(B(f)B(g)B(h)) steps.
[Motivation: Theorem 7.1.1H states that f is a Horn function if and only if f $f $f;
thus we can test Horn-ness in O(B(f)3) steps.]

A fully correct solution must (i) define the meaning of each BDD node precisely, (ii) characterize all terminal cases correctly, (iii) establish a sound inductive invariant for the triple recursion, and (iv) justify synchronization of variable ordering.

## Correct Solution

Let $F, G, H$ be reduced ordered BDDs for Boolean functions $f, g, h$, all over the same variable ordering $x_1 < \cdots < x_n$. For a node $u$ in a BDD, let $f_u$ denote the Boolean function computed by the sub-BDD rooted at $u$. Similarly define $g_v$ and $h_w$.

We are to test whether

$$
\forall x,y,\quad f(x)=1 \land g(y)=1 \;\Rightarrow\; h(x \wedge y)=1.
$$

Equivalently, there is no pair $(x,y)$ such that

$$
f(x)=1,\quad g(y)=1,\quad h(x\wedge y)=0.
$$

Thus we define a recursive predicate $E(u,v,w)$ meaning:

$$
E(u,v,w)=1 \iff \text{no such counterexample exists within } f_u,g_v,h_w.
$$

Formally,

$$
E(u,v,w)=1 \iff \neg \exists x,y \text{ consistent with } u,v \text{ such that }
f_u(x)=1 \land g_v(y)=1 \land h_w(x\wedge y)=0.
$$

## Step 1: Satisfiability information

We use a standard auxiliary predicate:

$$
\mathrm{Sat}(u)=1 \iff \exists x \text{ such that } f_u(x)=1.
$$

This is computed on each BDD by the standard linear-time bottom-up evaluation in $O(B(f))$, $O(B(g))$, $O(B(h))$.

## Step 2: Terminal cases (fully specified)

Let $0$ and $1$ denote terminal BDD nodes.

### Case 1: $w = 1$

Then $h_w(x \wedge y)=1$ for all $x,y$, so no counterexample exists:

$$
E(u,v,1)=1 \quad \text{for all } u,v.
$$

### Case 2: $u = 0$ or $v = 0$

If $f_u(x)=0$ identically or $g_v(y)=0$ identically, then $f_u(x)=1 \land g_v(y)=1$ is impossible, hence the implication is vacuously true:

$$
E(u,v,w)=1 \quad \text{if } u=0 \text{ or } v=0.
$$

### Case 3: $u=1$, $v=1$, $w=0$

Here $f_u(x)=1$ and $g_v(y)=1$ are always true, so we ask whether there exist $x,y$ such that $h_w(x\wedge y)=0$. Since $h_w=0$ identically, any $x,y$ are counterexamples:

$$
E(1,1,0)=0.
$$

### Case 4: $w=0$ and at least one of $u,v$ is not identically 1

If $w=0$ but either $u\neq 1$ or $v\neq 1$, then counterexamples exist iff both $u$ and $v$ are satisfiable:

- We need $f_u(x)=1$ for some $x$,
- and $g_v(y)=1$ for some $y$,
- while $h_w(\cdot)=0$.

Thus:

$$
E(u,v,0)=\neg(\mathrm{Sat}(u)\land \mathrm{Sat}(v)).
$$

This also correctly covers $E(1,1,0)=0$.

## Step 3: Recursive case

Let $i$ be the smallest index among the top variables of $u,v,w$ (treating terminals as $+\infty$).

For BDD node $u$, define children $u_0,u_1$ (or $u$ itself if it does not test $x_i$). Similarly define $v_0,v_1$ and $w_0,w_1$, where for $w$ the variable corresponds to $z_i = x_i \wedge y_i$.

For assignments $(a,b)\in\{0,1\}^2$, we have

$$
z_i = a \wedge b.
$$

Hence the recursion is:

$$
E(u,v,w)
=
\bigwedge_{a,b\in\{0,1\}}
E(u_a, v_b, w_{a\wedge b}).
$$

## Step 4: Correctness of the decomposition

We prove the key invariant:

> **Invariant.**
> 
> For every triple $(u,v,w)$,
> 
> $E(u,v,w)=1$ holds iff no counterexample assignment exists in the subspaces defined by $u,v,w$.

### Proof (induction on the variable ordering)

At node $i$, every assignment to $(x_i,y_i)$ splits the space into four disjoint cases:

$$
(0,0),(0,1),(1,0),(1,1).
$$

For each case:

- $u$ transitions consistently to $u_a$,
- $v$ transitions consistently to $v_b$,
- $h$ depends only on $z_i=a\wedge b$, hence transitions to $w_{a\wedge b}$.

All remaining variables are handled independently in the corresponding sub-BDDs.

Thus every global assignment $(x,y)$ is represented in exactly one of the four recursive subproblems, and vice versa. Therefore, a counterexample exists in $(u,v,w)$ iff it exists in at least one of the subproblems, which yields the conjunction in the recurrence.

The terminal cases are exactly those where no variables remain; the above case analysis exhausts all combinations of constant BDDs and matches the semantic definition of the implication condition.

This establishes correctness.

## Step 5: Complexity analysis

Each triple $(u,v,w)$ is evaluated once via memoization.

- Number of BDD nodes in $F$, $G$, $H$ are $B(f), B(g), B(h)$.
- Hence number of reachable triples is at most $B(f)B(g)B(h)$.

Each evaluation performs:

- $O(1)$ work to compare variable indices,
- constant branching into at most 4 recursive calls,
- memoization lookup/insert in $O(1)$ expected time.

Therefore total time is

$$
O(B(f)B(g)B(h)).
$$

## Conclusion

The algorithm evaluates the relation $f \; g \; h$ by a synchronized product construction of the three BDDs, with fully specified terminal semantics and a correct inductive decomposition over $(x_i,y_i)$. This yields a correct and optimal $O(B(f)B(g)B(h))$ procedure. ∎
