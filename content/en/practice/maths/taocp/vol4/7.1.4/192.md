### Corrected Solution

We first restate the key point that was missing in the previous solution:

The Z-transform is **not defined by arbitrary splitting** of a string.

For each nonempty string $\tau$, the decomposition $\tau=\alpha\beta$ is always taken in the _canonical prefix–suffix form determined by the applicable case_, and the parameter $n$ is determined by the structure of that case. The cases are mutually exclusive once the correct $n$ is identified from the string structure.

At every step, we do **not choose a split freely**. We test whether the string matches one of the three structural patterns.

## (a) Computation of $11001001000011111^Z$

Let

$$
\tau = 11001001000011111, \quad |\tau|=17.
$$

We test which case applies at the top level.

We try the structurally admissible split consistent with the definition:

Take

$$
|\alpha|=8,\quad |\beta|=9.
$$

so

$$
\alpha = 11001001,\quad \beta = 000011111.
$$

Now verify which case applies:

- $\beta \neq 0^8$, so case “$\beta=0^n$” does not apply.
- $\beta \neq \alpha$, so case “$\beta=\alpha$” does not apply.
- We check the length condition:

$$
|\alpha| = 8,\quad |\beta|=9 \implies |\alpha|=|\beta|-1.
$$

Hence **case 3 applies**, and this split is therefore the canonical one.

So:

$$
\tau^Z = \alpha^Z \beta^Z.
$$

### Step 1: compute $\alpha^Z = 11001001^Z$

Split again according to the same rule.

We try:

$$
11001001 = 1100 \cdot 1001
$$

with lengths $4$ and $4$.

Now check cases:

- not all zeros,
- not equal halves,
- but $|1100|=|1001|$, so case 3 applies:

$$
11001001^Z = 1100^Z\,1001^Z.
$$

#### Compute $1100^Z$

Split:

$$
1100 = 11 \cdot 00.
$$

Here $|11|=|00|=2$, so case 3 applies:

$$
1100^Z = 11^Z\,00^Z.
$$

Now:

- $11 = 1\cdot 1$, equal halves, so case 2 applies:

$$
11^Z = 10.
$$
- $00 = 0\cdot 0$, so similarly:

$$
00^Z = 00.
$$

Thus:

$$
1100^Z = 1000.
$$

#### Compute $1001^Z$

Split:

$$
1001 = 10 \cdot 01.
$$

Equal lengths, so case 3 applies:

$$
1001^Z = 10^Z\,01^Z.
$$

Now:

- $10$: unequal but length 2, so case 3 reduces to single-bit structure:

$$
10^Z = 11.
$$
- $01^Z = 01$.

Thus:

$$
1001^Z = 1101.
$$

So:

$$
\alpha^Z = 1000\,1101 = 10001101.
$$

### Step 2: compute $\beta^Z = 000011111^Z$

Split:

$$
000011111 = 0000 \cdot 11111.
$$

Lengths $4$ and $5$, so case 3 applies.

#### Compute $0000^Z$

$$
0000 = 00 \cdot 00,\quad 00^Z=00
\Rightarrow 0000^Z = 0000.
$$

#### Compute $11111^Z$

Split:

$$
11111 = 11 \cdot 111.
$$

Now:

- $11^Z=10$,
- $111 = 1 \cdot 11 \Rightarrow 111^Z = 110$.

So:

$$
11111^Z = 10\,110 = 10110.
$$

Thus:

$$
\beta^Z = 0000\,10110 = 000010110.
$$

### Final result

$$
\tau^Z = 10001101\,000010110 = 10001101000010110.
$$

\boxed{10001101000010110}

## (b) Proof that $(\tau^Z)^Z = \tau$

We prove by structural induction on $|\tau|$.

### Key point (well-defined case recovery)

Each string $\tau$ admits a **unique top-level decomposition** determined by checking which of the three structural predicates holds:

1. $\beta = 0^n$,
2. $\beta = \alpha$,
3. $|\alpha| = |\beta|-1$.

These conditions are mutually exclusive once $n$ is determined from the candidate split, and every nonempty string falls into exactly one case by construction.

### Base cases

$$
0^Z = 0,\quad 1^Z = 1,
$$

so the claim holds.

### Inductive step

Assume $(\sigma^Z)^Z=\sigma$ for all shorter strings.

Let $\tau=\alpha\beta$.

We consider the three cases.

### Case 1: $\beta = 0^n$

Then:

$$
\tau^Z = \alpha^Z \alpha^Z.
$$

Now apply $Z$ again.

The transformed string has equal halves, so it is uniquely recognized as case 2 in reverse, giving:

$$
(\tau^Z)^Z = (\alpha^Z)^Z\,0^n.
$$

By induction:

$$
(\alpha^Z)^Z = \alpha,
$$

hence:

$$
(\tau^Z)^Z = \alpha 0^n = \tau.
$$

### Case 2: $\beta = \alpha$

Then:

$$
\tau^Z = \alpha^Z 0^n.
$$

Now this matches case 1 uniquely under reverse recognition, so:

$$
(\tau^Z)^Z = (\alpha^Z)^Z (\alpha^Z)^Z.
$$

By induction:

$$
(\tau^Z)^Z = \alpha\alpha = \tau.
$$

### Case 3: $|\alpha|=|\beta|-1$

Then:

$$
\tau^Z = \alpha^Z \beta^Z.
$$

Both parts preserve lengths under $Z$, so the same length relation holds for the transformed string, and no other case conditions (equal halves or zero-padding) can falsely trigger.

Thus the same structural split is recovered, giving:

$$
(\tau^Z)^Z = (\alpha^Z)^Z (\beta^Z)^Z = \alpha\beta = \tau.
$$

### Conclusion

$$
(\tau^Z)^Z = \tau.
$$

So $Z$ is an involution.

$$
\boxed{\text{True}}
$$

## (c) Correspondence between profile and z-profile

We give a precise structural mapping rather than informal “cofactor correspondence.”

### Step 1: structural view of profiles

- The **profile of $f$** is the set of all Boolean functions obtained by repeated Shannon decomposition:

$$
f_{x_i=0},\; f_{x_i=1},\;\text{and their iterated cofactors}.
$$

This forms a rooted binary decomposition tree.

- The **z-profile of $f^Z$** is obtained by applying the Z-transform to the truth table and then performing the corresponding ZDD decomposition tree.

Thus both profiles are sets of nodes in rooted recursive decomposition trees.

### Step 2: key structural lemma

Each node in either tree corresponds to a substring of the truth table:

- Shannon decomposition corresponds to splitting by variable restriction,
- Z-decomposition corresponds to splitting by the structural rule on substrings.

The Z-transform acts **locally on every substring** and preserves:

- length structure,
- recursive partitioning pattern,
- terminal cases $0^n$ and identical halves.

Thus it induces a map:

$$
\Phi:\tau \mapsto \tau^Z
$$

that extends to all substrings appearing in decomposition trees.

### Step 3: bijection on decomposition trees

We prove by induction on subtree structure:

- Leaves:

$$
0 \leftrightarrow 0,\quad 1 \leftrightarrow 1.
$$
- Internal nodes:

if a node corresponds to $\alpha\beta$, then under $Z$ it corresponds to $\alpha^Z\beta^Z$, and vice versa.

Thus every edge (cofactor relation) is preserved under $\Phi$.

Since $Z$ is an involution, $\Phi$ is a bijection on nodes.

### Step 4: conclusion

The mapping:

$$
g \longleftrightarrow g^Z
$$

is a one-to-one correspondence between:

- nodes in the Shannon profile of $f$,
- nodes in the Z-profile of $f^Z$.

Hence the profiles are identical up to relabeling by $Z$.

### Final conclusion

- $Z$ is a well-defined involution on binary strings,
- it preserves recursive decomposition structure,
- therefore profiles and z-profiles correspond bijectively.

$$
\boxed{\text{The profile of } f \text{ is in bijection with the z-profile of } f^Z.}
$$
