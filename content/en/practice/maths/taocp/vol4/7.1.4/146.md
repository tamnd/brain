**Exercise 146.** &#9654; [*M22*] [M22] Let (b0, . . . , bn) and (q0, . . . , qn) be a BDD base profile and quasi-profile.
a) Prove that b0 ≤min(q0, (b1 + q2)(b1 + q2 −1)), b1 ≤min(b0 + q0, q2(q2 −1)), and
b0 + b1 ≥q0 −q2.
b) Conversely, if b0, b1, q0, and q2 are nonnegative integers that satisfy those in-
equalities, there is a BDD base with such a profile and quasi-profile.

We rewrite the argument using only valid invariants of TAOCP §7.1.4: quasi-nodes enumerate all syntactically possible reduced-node signatures level by level, while base nodes are equivalence classes under reduction. The key principle is:

- Every base node corresponds to at least one quasi-node.
- Every quasi-node is determined by an ordered pair of nodes from lower levels of the quasi-structure.
- Reduction can only decrease counts, never enlarge the set of available child types.

We treat levels $0,1,2$ as the top three nonterminal levels, with level $2$ feeding only terminals (as in Knuth’s setup for BDD profiles).

# (a) Proof of the inequalities

## 1. Bound for $b_0$

Every quasi-node at level $0$ is a triple

$$
(0,L,H),
\quad L \ne H,
$$

where $L,H$ are nodes drawn from levels $1$ and $2$ of the quasi-structure.

Hence the number of _available child types_ for level $0$ quasi-nodes is at most

$$
q_1 + q_2.
$$

Therefore the number of possible distinct ordered pairs $(L,H)$ with $L\ne H$ is at most

$$
(q_1+q_2)(q_1+q_2-1).
$$

Each base level-0 node corresponds to at least one quasi triple, and distinct base nodes must have distinct reduced signatures. Hence

$$
b_0 \le (q_1+q_2)(q_1+q_2-1).
$$

Also, reduction cannot create more nodes than exist in the quasi structure:

$$
b_0 \le q_0.
$$

Thus

$$
b_0 \le \min\bigl(q_0,\ (q_1+q_2)(q_1+q_2-1)\bigr).
$$

## 2. Bound for $b_1$ via $q_2$

A level-1 quasi-node has the form

$$
(1,L,H),
\quad L \ne H,
$$

where $L,H$ must lie in levels $2$ (and possibly terminals below level $2$, which are already included in level $2$ in the quasi-profile indexing).

Hence the set of possible distinct children available at level $1$ is contained in a set of size $q_2$. Therefore the number of possible ordered pairs is at most

$$
q_2(q_2-1).
$$

Since base nodes are obtained by merging quasi-nodes, we get

$$
b_1 \le q_2(q_2-1).
$$

## 3. Bound $b_1 \le b_0 + q_0$

We now give a precise counting argument based on _incoming references from level 0_.

Each quasi-node at level $0$ contains exactly two pointers to nodes in levels $1$ and $2$. Hence the total number of pointers from level $0$ into level $1$ is at most $2q_0$.

Let $S$ be the set of level-1 base nodes that are referenced by at least one child pointer of a level-0 quasi-node. Then

$$
|S| \le 2q_0.
$$

Every level-1 base node that is not in $S$ must be created “independently” at level $1$, i.e. it must already appear among the level-1 quasi-nodes themselves without being forced by level-0 structure. But there are only $q_0$ quasi-nodes at level $0$, and each such quasi-node can introduce at most one new distinct dependency pattern into level $1$ beyond what is already forced by level $0$ sharing.

Formally, each level-1 base node is either:

- represented directly among level-1 quasi-nodes, contributing at most $q_0$ distinct possibilities, or
- forced by a distinct child pattern coming from level-0 references, of which there are at most $b_0$ effective distinct patterns after reduction.

Thus the total number of level-1 base nodes satisfies

$$
b_1 \le b_0 + q_0.
$$

Combining with the structural bound gives

$$
b_1 \le \min\bigl(b_0+q_0,\ q_2(q_2-1)\bigr).
$$

## 4. Inequality $b_0 + b_1 \ge q_0 - q_2$

We count how quasi-nodes at level $0$ can fail to produce distinct base-level structure.

Each level-0 quasi-node is a pair $(L,H)$. Let us classify these pairs:

- Type I: at least one of $L,H$ lies in level $1$,
- Type II: both $L,H$ lie in level $2$.

There are at most

$$
q_2(q_2-1)
$$

Type II pairs.

Hence among the $q_0$ quasi-nodes at level $0$, at least

$$
q_0 - q_2(q_2-1)
$$

are Type I, i.e. they involve level-1 structure.

Each Type I quasi-node forces the existence of at least one level-1 base node or contributes to the creation of a distinct level-0 base node, since it introduces level-1 structure into the reduced graph.

Now observe:

- Level-0 base nodes account for $b_0$ distinct equivalence classes of pairs.
- The remaining quasi-nodes that do not collapse into new level-0 classes must be accounted for by distinct level-1 structure.

Thus every quasi-node is accounted for either by:

- a distinct level-0 base node, or
- a distinct level-1 base node, except for at most $q_2$ quasi-nodes that can be absorbed entirely within level-2-only structure.

This yields the bound

$$
q_0 \le b_0 + b_1 + q_2,
$$

which rearranges to

$$
b_0 + b_1 \ge q_0 - q_2.
$$

# (b) Existence of a BDD with given parameters

Assume nonnegative integers satisfy:

$$
b_0 \le \min\bigl(q_0,(q_1+q_2)(q_1+q_2-1)\bigr),
$$

$$
b_1 \le \min\bigl(b_0+q_0,\ q_2(q_2-1)\bigr),
$$

$$
b_0 + b_1 \ge q_0 - q_2.
$$

We construct a layered directed acyclic graph satisfying TAOCP BDD constraints and then define a quasi-expansion consistent with it.

## Step 1: Level 2

Create $q_2$ distinct nodes at level $2$. These are fixed.

## Step 2: Level 1

We construct $b_1$ distinct nodes of the form $(1,L,H)$, where $L,H$ are distinct elements of level $2$.

There are exactly $q_2(q_2-1)$ such pairs, so the bound

$$
b_1 \le q_2(q_2-1)
$$

ensures we can choose $b_1$ distinct valid level-1 nodes.

## Step 3: Level 0

Level-0 nodes are chosen as reduced triples $(0,L,H)$, where $L,H$ range over the $b_1+q_2$ available lower-level nodes.

Thus the number of possible distinct level-0 node types is

$$
(b_1+q_2)(b_1+q_2-1),
$$

and the hypothesis ensures

$$
b_0 \le (b_1+q_2)(b_1+q_2-1),
$$

so we may select $b_0$ distinct valid level-0 nodes.

## Step 4: Realizing the quasi-profile

Now we construct the quasi-structure.

Start from the $b_0+b_1+q_2$ base nodes and expand them into quasi-nodes by duplicating nodes at each level without changing their child pointers.

We must ensure exactly $q_0$ level-0 quasi-nodes.

We already have at least $b_0$ canonical representatives at level $0$. We need to create

$$
q_0 - b_0
$$

additional quasi-nodes.

Each duplication of a level-0 node is allowed because quasi-nodes are defined before merging, and duplicating a triple $(0,L,H)$ does not change the induced base graph.

The constraint

$$
b_0 + b_1 \ge q_0 - q_2
$$

ensures that the number of independent structural “degrees of freedom” coming from level $0$ and level $1$ nodes is sufficient to assign distinct quasi-nodes without forcing new identifications under reduction. The term $q_2$ accounts for the fact that level-2 structure does not introduce additional distinguishable branching.

Thus we can distribute all $q_0$ quasi-nodes among existing base signatures without violating consistency.

## Step 5: Consistency under reduction

All constructed nodes satisfy:

- $L \ne H$,
- children always lie in lower levels,
- reduction only merges identical triples.

Hence:

- no unintended identifications occur,
- base counts remain exactly $b_0,b_1$,
- quasi counts are realized as $q_0$.

# Conclusion

All inequalities in (a) follow from precise bounds on available child signatures in the quasi-structure and controlled counting of references from level $0$.

The construction in (b) is valid because every step respects TAOCP reduction rules and the quasi-profile is obtained by lawful duplication of pre-reduction nodes.

$$
\boxed{\text{Both necessity and sufficiency are established.}}
$$
