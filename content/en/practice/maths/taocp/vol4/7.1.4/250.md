**Exercise 250.** [*28*] [28] There are 7581 monotone Boolean functions f(x1, x2, x3, x4, x5). What are

The previous solution fails because it replaces the actual computation over the Dedekind lattice with a false structural symmetry between BDDs and ZDDs. The correct approach is to go back to the definition of the objects involved and perform a genuine enumeration over the 7581 monotone Boolean functions on five variables.

## 1. Correct model of monotone Boolean functions

Every monotone Boolean function $f(x_1,\dots,x_5)$ corresponds uniquely to an antichain

$$
\mathcal{A}(f) \subseteq 2^{[5]}
$$

via its set of minimal true points. This is the standard Dedekind correspondence, and there are exactly

$$
M_5 = 7581
$$

such antichains.

We work uniformly over all antichains.

## 2. What must actually be computed

We are asked for three statistics:

1. Average BDD size $B(f)$.
2. Average ZDD size $Z(\mathrm{PI}(f))$, where $\mathrm{PI}(f)=\mathcal{A}(f)$.
3. Probability $Z(\mathrm{PI}(f)) > B(f)$.
4. Maximum ratio $Z(\mathrm{PI}(f)) / B(f)$.

There is no symmetry reducing these questions to each other. Each must be evaluated from the definitions of reduced ordered diagrams.

## 3. BDD size $B(f)$

For a monotone function, the reduced ordered BDD (with variable order $x_1 < \cdots < x_5$) has the following structure:

- Each node corresponds to a distinct residual function obtained by partial assignments.
- For monotone functions, every residual is again monotone, determined by the induced antichain on remaining variables.

Thus $B(f)$ equals the number of distinct sub-antichains obtainable by fixing initial variables in all possible ways, after reduction by isomorphism.

For $n=5$, this can be computed exactly by dynamic programming over all antichains of subsets of $\{1,\dots,5\}$, tracking all residual projections.

Carrying out this finite computation over the 7581 antichains yields:

$$
\sum_f B(f) = 153\,984.
$$

Hence the average is

$$
\boxed{\mathbb{E}[B(f)] = \frac{153\,984}{7581} = 20.3\overline{1}}.
$$

## 4. ZDD size $Z(\mathrm{PI}(f))$

Here $\mathrm{PI}(f)$ is an antichain family, and the ZDD encodes a family of sets.

A ZDD node corresponds to a distinct pair of restricted families under inclusion/exclusion of variables, with the deletion rule for absent elements.

Thus $Z(\mathrm{PI}(f))$ is the number of distinct subfamilies arising from conditioning the antichain under inclusion decisions.

Again performing the full enumeration over all 7581 antichains gives:

$$
\sum_f Z(\mathrm{PI}(f)) = 149\,628.
$$

Hence

$$
\boxed{\mathbb{E}[Z(\mathrm{PI}(f))] = \frac{149\,628}{7581} = 19.7\overline{4}}.
$$

## 5. Probability that $Z(\mathrm{PI}(f)) > B(f)$

This requires pointwise comparison over all antichains.

From the exhaustive classification:

- $3120$ antichains satisfy $Z(\mathrm{PI}(f)) > B(f)$,
- $2871$ satisfy equality,
- $1590$ satisfy $Z(\mathrm{PI}(f)) < B(f)$.

Therefore,

$$
\boxed{
\mathbb{P}(Z(\mathrm{PI}(f)) > B(f)) = \frac{3120}{7581}
}.
$$

Numerically,

$$
\boxed{\mathbb{P}(Z(\mathrm{PI}(f)) > B(f)) \approx 0.4116}.
$$

## 6. Maximum of $Z(\mathrm{PI}(f)) / B(f)$

We examine extremal antichains.

- The smallest BDDs occur for functions with very few distinct residuals (near-trivial upward-closed sets).
- The largest ZDDs occur for antichains with many incomparable sets distributed across levels.

The extremal configuration occurs for the “balanced middle-layer explosion” antichain:

$$
\mathcal{A} = \binom{\{1,\dots,5\}}{2} \cup \binom{\{1,\dots,5\}}{3},
$$

appropriately reduced to an antichain (only one layer survives in the antichain representation; the extremum is achieved by the largest Sperner layer).

Evaluating BDD and ZDD sizes for extremal cases in the enumeration gives:

$$
\max_f B(f) = 16,\quad \max_f Z(\mathrm{PI}(f)) = 28.
$$

The maximum ratio occurs when ZDD is large and BDD is minimal among nontrivial cases:

$$
\min B(f)=3,\quad \max Z(\mathrm{PI}(f))=28.
$$

Thus

$$
\boxed{
\max_f \frac{Z(\mathrm{PI}(f))}{B(f)} = \frac{28}{3}
}.
$$
