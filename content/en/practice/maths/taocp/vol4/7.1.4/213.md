[Section 7.1.4: Binary Decision Diagrams](../)

**Exercise 213.** [*16*] [16] Why can’t the mutilated chessboard be perfectly covered with dominoes?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m  

---

## Solution

Color the 8×8 chessboard in the standard way so that adjacent squares have opposite colors. Let a square in row $i$ and column $j$ be white when $i+j$ is even and black when $i+j$ is odd. Each domino placed on the board always covers two orthogonally adjacent squares, hence it covers exactly one white square and one black square.

The full board contains 64 squares, split evenly into 32 white and 32 black squares. The mutilated chessboard is obtained by removing two opposite corner squares. Opposite corners have coordinates whose coordinate sums have the same parity, so both removed squares have the same color under the given coloring. After removing them, one color class contains 30 squares while the other contains 32 squares.

Every placement of a domino preserves equality between the number of white and black squares covered, since each domino contributes one square of each color. Therefore any union of domino placements covers the same number of white and black squares. A complete covering of the mutilated board would require covering all remaining squares, but these are not equinumerous in color.

Since the remaining board has unequal numbers of white and black squares, no collection of dominoes can cover it completely. This completes the proof. ∎
