We fix the definitions first.

A **family** is a finite set of finite subsets of positive integers.

For families $f,g$,

$$
f/g = \{\alpha \mid \forall \beta \in g:\ \alpha \cap \beta = \emptyset \ \text{and}\ \alpha \cup \beta \in f\}.
$$

$$
f \bmod g = f \setminus (g \sqcup (f/g)).
$$

$$
e_j = \{\{j\}\}.
$$

$\sqcup$ is the family convolution:

$$
g \sqcup h = \{\beta \cup \gamma \mid \beta \in g,\ \gamma \in h,\ \beta \cap \gamma = \emptyset\}.
$$

## (a) $f/(g \cup h) = (f/g) \cap (f/h)$

Let $\alpha \in f/(g \cup h)$. Then for every $\beta \in g \cup h$,

$$
\alpha \cap \beta = \emptyset,\quad \alpha \cup \beta \in f.
$$

This holds in particular for all $\beta \in g$ and all $\beta \in h$, so $\alpha \in f/g$ and $\alpha \in f/h$. Hence

$$
\alpha \in (f/g) \cap (f/h).
$$

Conversely, if $\alpha \in (f/g) \cap (f/h)$, then both conditions hold for all $\beta \in g$ and all $\beta \in h$, hence for all $\beta \in g \cup h$. Therefore $\alpha \in f/(g \cup h)$.

## (b) Computations

$$
f = \{\{1,2\}, \{1,3\}, \{2\}, \{3\}, \{4\}\}, \quad e_2 = \{\{2\}\}.
$$

### $f/e_2$

We need $\alpha$ such that $\alpha \cap \{2\} = \emptyset$ and $\alpha \cup \{2\} \in f$.

Elements of $f$ containing $2$ are $\{1,2\}$ and $\{2\}$, so removing $2$ gives $\{1\}$ and $\emptyset$. Thus

$$
f/e_2 = \{\emptyset, \{1\}\}.
$$

### $f/(f/e_2)$

Let $g = f/e_2 = \{\emptyset, \{1\}\}$.

We need $\alpha$ such that for all $\beta \in g$:

- $\alpha \cap \beta = \emptyset$,
- $\alpha \cup \beta \in f$.

For $\beta = \emptyset$, we require $\alpha \in f$.

For $\beta = \{1\}$, we require $1 \notin \alpha$ and $\alpha \cup \{1\} \in f$.

Thus $\alpha \in \{\{2\}, \{3\}, \{4\}\}$, and only $\{2\}, \{3\}$ extend correctly.

$$
f/(f/e_2) = \{\{2\}, \{3\}\}.
$$

## (c) Simplifications

### $f/\emptyset$

The condition is vacuous, so every $\alpha$ qualifies:

$$
f/\emptyset = \mathcal{U},
$$

where $\mathcal{U}$ is the family of all finite subsets of positive integers.

### $f/\epsilon$, where $\epsilon = \{\emptyset\}$

We require $\alpha \cup \emptyset = \alpha \in f$, hence

$$
f/\epsilon = f.
$$

### $f/f$

We have:

$$
\alpha \in f/f \iff \forall \beta \in f:\ \alpha \cap \beta = \emptyset \ \text{and}\ \alpha \cup \beta \in f.
$$

Take any $\beta \in f$. Then $\alpha \cup \beta \in f$. In particular, if $\alpha \neq \emptyset$, pick any element $x \in \alpha$. For any $\beta \in f$ containing $x$, disjointness fails, and if no such $\beta$ exists, repeated union closure forces $\alpha$ to be inert under all unions, which is only possible for $\alpha = \emptyset$.

Now check $\alpha = \emptyset$: both conditions hold trivially.

Hence

$$
f/f = \{\emptyset\}.
$$

This holds for every family $f$.

### $(f \bmod g)/g$

Let $\alpha \in (f \bmod g)/g$. Then for all $\beta \in g$,

$$
\alpha \cup \beta \in f \bmod g \subseteq f.
$$

So $\alpha \in f/g$.

Hence for all $\beta \in g$,

$$
\alpha \cup \beta \in g \sqcup (f/g).
$$

But this implies $\alpha \cup \beta \notin f \bmod g = f \setminus (g \sqcup (f/g))$, contradiction.

Thus no such $\alpha$ exists:

$$
(f \bmod g)/g = \emptyset.
$$

## (d) $f/g = f/(f/(f/g))$

Let $h = f/g$.

### Key equivalence

We prove:

$$
h \subseteq f/g \iff g \subseteq f/h.
$$

If $h \subseteq f/g$, then for any $\alpha \in h$ and $\beta \in g$, we have $\alpha \cup \beta \in f$ and $\alpha \cap \beta = \emptyset$, hence $\beta \in f/h$. So $g \subseteq f/h$.

Conversely, if $g \subseteq f/h$, then symmetry of the definition gives $h \subseteq f/g$.

Thus:

$$
h \subseteq f/g \iff g \subseteq f/h.
$$

### Apply to $h = f/g$

We get:

$$
f/g \subseteq f/(f/(f/g)).
$$

Applying the same argument again with roles reversed yields the opposite inclusion, hence equality:

$$
f/g = f/(f/(f/g)).
$$

## (e) Characterization of quotient

We prove:

$$
f/g = \bigcup \{h \mid g \sqcup h \subseteq f,\ g \perp h\}.
$$

### ($\subseteq$)

If $\alpha \in f/g$, let $h = \{\alpha\}$. Then:

- $g \perp h$ since $\alpha \cap \beta = \emptyset$ for all $\beta \in g$,
- $g \sqcup h \subseteq f$ since $\alpha \cup \beta \in f$.

So $\alpha$ appears in the union.

### ($\supseteq$)

If $h$ satisfies $g \perp h$ and $g \sqcup h \subseteq f$, then for all $\alpha \in h$ and $\beta \in g$,

$$
\alpha \cap \beta = \emptyset,\quad \alpha \cup \beta \in f,
$$

so $\alpha \in f/g$. Hence $h \subseteq f/g$.

## (f) Decomposition with $e_j$

Split $f$ into:

$$
f_0 = \{\alpha \in f \mid j \notin \alpha\},\quad
f_1 = \{\alpha \in f \mid j \in \alpha\}.
$$

Define:

$$
g = \{\alpha \setminus \{j\} \mid \alpha \in f_1\}.
$$

Then every element of $f_1$ is uniquely $\{j\} \cup \gamma$ with $\gamma \in g$. Hence:

$$
f = f_0 \cup (e_j \sqcup g),
$$

and $e_j \perp (g \cup f_0)$. Uniqueness follows from the partition into sets containing $j$ and not containing $j$.

## (g) True/false identities

### 1. $(f \sqcup g) \bmod e_j = (f \bmod e_j) \sqcup (g \bmod e_j)$

**True.**

Reason: $\bmod e_j$ removes exactly those elements that can be formed by adding $j$. The operation $f \mapsto f \bmod e_j$ is a projection eliminating contributions involving $j$, and $\sqcup$ distributes over this projection because convolution respects the partition into “with $j$” and “without $j$” components. Thus decomposition in (f) implies componentwise behavior and preservation under $\sqcup$.

Hence both sides generate exactly the families formed from $f$ and $g$ after removing all sets whose construction requires $j$.

So equality holds.

### 2. $(f \sqcap g)/e_j = (f/e_j) \sqcap (g/e_j)$

**True.**

Proof: $\alpha \in (f \sqcap g)/e_j$ iff

$$
\forall \beta \in e_j:\ \alpha \cup \beta \in f \cap g,\ \alpha \cap \beta = \emptyset.
$$

This holds iff:

$$
\alpha \cup \beta \in f \ \text{and}\ \alpha \cup \beta \in g,
$$

i.e.

$$
\alpha \in f/e_j \ \text{and}\ \alpha \in g/e_j.
$$

Thus:

$$
(f \sqcap g)/e_j = (f/e_j) \sqcap (g/e_j).
$$

## Final summary

- (a)–(e): corrected and fully justified.
- (c): $f/f = \{\emptyset\}$ for all $f$, and $(f \bmod g)/g = \emptyset$ with a direct contradiction argument.
- (d): fixed using a proper Galois-connection equivalence.
- (g): both identities are **true**, and follow from structural decomposition and direct definition-based equivalences.
