**Exercise 103.** &#9654; [*20*] [20] Mr. B. C. Dull wanted to evaluate the formula
∃y1 . . . ∃ym((y1 = f1(x1, . . . , xn)) ∧· · · ∧(ym = fm(x1, . . . , xn)) ∧g(y1, . . . , ym)),
for certain functions f1, . . . , fm, and g. But his fellow student, J. H. Quick, found a
much simpler formula for the same problem. What was Quick’s idea?

## Solution

Let $x_1,\ldots,x_n$ be the free variables. The given formula is

$$
\exists y_1 \ldots \exists y_m \Bigl( (y_1 = f_1(x_1,\ldots,x_n)) \land \cdots \land (y_m = f_m(x_1,\ldots,x_n)) \land g(y_1,\ldots,y_m) \Bigr).
$$

Fix an assignment to $x_1,\ldots,x_n$. The conjunction $(y_i = f_i(x_1,\ldots,x_n))$ for $1 \le i \le m$ determines each $y_i$ uniquely as a function of the $x$-variables. Any tuple $(y_1,\ldots,y_m)$ satisfies all equalities if and only if $y_i = f_i(x_1,\ldots,x_n)$ holds for every $i$. Hence, under a fixed assignment to the $x$-variables, the existential quantifiers range over a set containing exactly one admissible tuple, namely

$$
(y_1,\ldots,y_m) = (f_1(x_1,\ldots,x_n),\ldots,f_m(x_1,\ldots,x_n)).
$$

The original formula is true under the given assignment if and only if this tuple satisfies $g(y_1,\ldots,y_m)$. Substituting the forced values yields the equivalent condition

$$
g(f_1(x_1,\ldots,x_n),\ldots,f_m(x_1,\ldots,x_n)).
$$

Thus the entire quantified expression is equivalent to the single formula

$$
g(f_1(x_1,\ldots,x_n),\ldots,f_m(x_1,\ldots,x_n)).
$$

Since both expressions evaluate to the same Boolean value for every assignment of $x_1,\ldots,x_n$, they define the same Boolean function. This completes the proof. ∎
