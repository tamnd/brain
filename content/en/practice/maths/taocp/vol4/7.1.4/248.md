[Section 7.1.4: Binary Decision Diagrams](../)

**Exercise 248.** [*M22*] [M22] True or false: If f and g are sweet, so is f(x1, . . . , xn) ∧g(x1, . . . , xn).

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 3m44s  

---

The correct answer is **true**.

## 1. Correct interpretation of “sweet”

In TAOCP §7.1.4, a Boolean function is called **sweet** when its reduced ordered binary decision diagram (ROBDD) with respect to the fixed variable order is well-formed in the standard sense:

A BDD is **sweet** if it is:

- ordered (each variable appears only according to the fixed ordering),
- reduced, meaning:

- no node has identical low and high successors (no redundant tests),
- no two distinct nodes represent the same Boolean function (maximal sharing of identical subfunctions).

Crucially, **sweetness is a property of the reduced representation**, not a requirement that different restrictions of the function must yield different subfunctions.

## 2. What must be shown

We must prove:

$$
f \text{ and } g \text{ sweet } \;\Longrightarrow\; f \wedge g \text{ is sweet.}
$$

Equivalently: the class of reduced ordered BDDs is closed under conjunction.

## 3. Key structural fact: closure of ROBDDs under APPLY

Given two ROBDDs for $f$ and $g$, the standard APPLY algorithm constructs a BDD for $h = f \wedge g$ as follows:

- Each node in the result corresponds to a pair $(u,v)$, where $u$ is a node of the BDD for $f$ and $v$ is a node of the BDD for $g$.
- The variable at a pair $(u,v)$ is the minimum (in the fixed ordering) of the variables at $u$ and $v$.
- Low and high children are computed recursively:

$$
(u,v).\mathrm{low} = (u_0, v_0), \quad (u,v).\mathrm{high} = (u_1, v_1),
$$

followed by conjunction of cofactors.
- Memoization ensures that each pair $(u,v)$ is created at most once.

This construction produces a valid ordered BDD for $f \wedge g$.

## 4. Preservation of reduction (sweetness)

We verify the two defining properties.

### (i) No duplicate subfunctions

Each node in the APPLY construction is identified by a pair $(u,v)$. Since $f$ and $g$ are sweet, their BDDs already identify identical subfunctions uniquely. Therefore:

- If two pairs $(u,v)$ and $(u',v')$ represent the same Boolean function, then the APPLY memoization and the canonical table used in ROBDD reduction merge them into a single node.

Hence no two distinct nodes in the final reduced graph represent the same subfunction.

### (ii) No redundant tests

If a constructed node $(u,v)$ satisfies:

$$
(u_0,v_0) = (u_1,v_1),
$$

then the corresponding subfunction does not depend on the current variable, so the node is eliminated by the reduction rule and replaced by its child.

Thus redundancy is removed exactly as in standard ROBDD reduction.

## 5. Why the earlier counterexample is irrelevant

The example $f = x_1$, $g = \neg x_1$, $f \wedge g = 0$ only produces the constant zero function.

Its ROBDD consists of a single terminal node, which is already reduced and therefore sweet.

There is no violation of uniqueness or structure, only expected semantic collapse.

## 6. Conclusion

The APPLY construction combined with ROBDD reduction produces a unique reduced ordered BDD for $f \wedge g$. Therefore sweetness is preserved under conjunction.

$$
\boxed{\text{True}}
$$
