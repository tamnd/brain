[Section 7.1.4: Binary Decision Diagrams](../)

**Exercise 5.** [*20*] [20] If you take the BDD for f(x1, . . . , xn) and interchange the LO and HI pointers
of every node, and if you also swap the two sinks ⊥↔⊤, what do you get?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m06s  

---

## Solution

Let $G$ be a reduced ordered BDD for $f(x_1,\ldots,x_n)$. Construct a new directed acyclic graph $G'$ from $G$ by replacing every sink $\bot$ by $\top$ and every sink $\top$ by $\bot$, and by replacing at every branch node the LO pointer by the HI pointer and the HI pointer by the LO pointer.

Let $g$ be the Boolean function represented by $G'$. For each node $v$ of $G$, let $f_v$ denote the function computed by the sub-BDD of $G$ rooted at $v$, and let $g_v$ denote the function computed by the corresponding node in $G'$.

The claim is that for every node $v$, one has

$$
g_v = \neg f_v.
$$

For a sink node $v$, if $v=\bot$ then $f_v=0$ and in $G'$ the corresponding sink is $\top$, so $g_v=1=\neg 0=\neg f_v$. If $v=\top$ then $f_v=1$ and in $G'$ it becomes $\bot$, so $g_v=0=\neg 1=\neg f_v$.

For a branch node $v$ labeled by variable $x_i$ with LO-child $v_0$ and HI-child $v_1$, the function computed by $G$ satisfies

$$
f_v = (\neg x_i \wedge f_{v_0}) \vee (x_i \wedge f_{v_1}).
$$

In the transformed diagram $G'$, the LO-child is $v_1$ and the HI-child is $v_0$, so

$$
g_v = (\neg x_i \wedge g_{v_1}) \vee (x_i \wedge g_{v_0}).
$$

Using the induction hypothesis $g_{v_0}=\neg f_{v_0}$ and $g_{v_1}=\neg f_{v_1}$, this becomes

$$
g_v = (\neg x_i \wedge \neg f_{v_1}) \vee (x_i \wedge \neg f_{v_0}).
$$

Applying De Morgan laws yields

$$
g_v = \neg \bigl((x_i \wedge f_{v_1}) \vee (\neg x_i \wedge f_{v_0})\bigr) = \neg f_v.
$$

Thus the property holds for sinks and is preserved from children to parents, so it holds for the root as well. Therefore the function computed by $G'$ is $\neg f(x_1,\ldots,x_n)$.

Since swapping LO and HI at every branch node exchanges the two cofactors with respect to each variable and swapping $\bot$ and $\top$ complements the terminal values, the resulting BDD represents the Boolean complement of $f$.

This completes the proof. ∎
