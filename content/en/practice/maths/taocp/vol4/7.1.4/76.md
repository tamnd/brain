**Exercise 76.** &#9654; [*M22*] [M22] A “clutter” is a family S of mutually incomparable sets; in other words,
S ̸⊆S′ whenever S and S′ are distinct members of S. Every set S ⊆{0, 1, . . . , n −1}
can be represented as an n-bit integer s = {2e | e ∈S}; so every family of subsets of
{0, 1, . . . , n −1} corresponds to a binary vector x0x1 . . . x2n−1, with xs = 1 if and only
if s represents a set of the family.
Show that the BDD for the function ‘[x0x1 . . . x2n−1 corresponds to a clutter]’ has
a simple relation to the BDD for the monotone-function function µn(x1, . . . , x2n).

Let $U_n=\mathcal P(\{0,1,\dots,n-1\})$. A family is encoded by a Boolean vector $(x_s)_{s\in U_n}$, where $x_s=1$ means that $s$ is included.

A **clutter** (antichain) satisfies

$$
s\subset t \ \Longrightarrow\ \neg(x_s\wedge x_t),
$$

equivalently,

$$
s\subset t \ \Longrightarrow\ x_s x_t = 0.
\tag{1}
$$

We relate this to the monotone-function encoding used in TAOCP: variables $y_s$ indexed by subsets, where a monotone family satisfies

$$
s\subset t \ \Longrightarrow\ y_s \le y_t.
\tag{2}
$$

The key point is that the correct relationship is not via complementing subsets, but via **complementing the chosen family inside each chain constraint**.

## 1. Rewriting the clutter condition correctly

From (1), for every comparable pair $s\subset t$,

$$
x_s=1 \ \Longrightarrow\ x_t=0.
$$

Equivalently, along every chain $s_0 \subset s_1 \subset \cdots \subset s_k$, at most one variable can be 1, and if one is 1 then all larger sets must be 0.

Thus the condition is a **downward-closed exclusion property**, not a closure property.

## 2. Expressing the condition as a monotone violation function

Define new variables

$$
y_s := 1 - x_s.
$$

Then $x_s=1 \iff y_s=0$. Substituting into (1):

If $s\subset t$ and $y_s=0$, then $y_t=1$.

Thus we obtain:

$$
s\subset t \ \Longrightarrow\ y_s \le y_t.
\tag{3}
$$

This is exactly the monotone condition.

However, (3) holds **only on those assignments corresponding to complements of clutters**, not on all assignments. The clutter condition becomes:

> There do not exist $s\subset t$ such that $y_s=0$ and $y_t=0$ is violated in a way that allows two 1’s in the original encoding.

More directly, the clutter condition is equivalent to:

$$
\forall s\subset t,\quad \neg(y_s=0 \wedge y_t=0)
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
y_s=1 \ \text{or}\ y_t=1.
\tag{4}
$$

Thus every comparable pair must contain at least one “selected” (in $y$) node.

This is a **hitting condition on chains**, dual to antichains.

## 3. Connection to the monotone-function BDD $\mu_n$

Let $\mu_n(y)$ be the monotone-function indicator:

$$
\mu_n(y)=1 \Longleftrightarrow (y_s \le y_t \text{ whenever } s\subset t).
$$

Its BDD enforces, locally at each edge $s\subset t$, that the assignment cannot contain a pattern $y_s=1, y_t=0$.

The clutter condition (in $x$-variables) forbids the opposite pattern:

$$
x_s=1, x_t=1 \text{ is forbidden whenever } s\subset t.
$$

Thus, along every edge $s\subset t$, the forbidden pattern is reversed.

This yields a direct local duality:

| relation $s\subset t$ | monotone constraint | clutter constraint |
| --- | --- | --- |
| forbidden pattern | $y_s=1, y_t=0$ | $x_s=1, x_t=1$ |

So the clutter function is obtained from the monotone function by **edgewise De Morgan dualization on constraints**, not by complementing indices.

## 4. BDD transformation

Fix the same variable ordering on all $x_s$ and $y_s$ (indexed by subsets in lexicographic or Knuth order).

The BDD for $\mu_n$ is built by enforcing at each comparable pair $(s,t)$ the constraint:

$$
\neg(y_s \wedge \neg y_t).
$$

For clutters, the constraint is:

$$
\neg(x_s \wedge x_t).
$$

These are related by the local substitution:

$$
y_t := \neg x_t \quad \text{on the upper node of each constraint edge}.
$$

Thus, at each decision node in the BDD for $\mu_n$:

- the 0-edge corresponds to “violate monotonicity upward”,
- the 1-edge corresponds to “satisfy monotonicity upward”,

while for clutters the roles are:

- the 1-edge corresponds to “violate antichain condition downward”,
- the 0-edge corresponds to “satisfy”.

Hence the transformation is:

- swap 0- and 1-terminals,
- swap low/high edges at every node,
- keep the same variable ordering.

This is exactly Boolean dualization of the BDD.

## 5. Correct structural statement

Let $\mathrm{BDD}(f)$ denote the reduced ordered BDD.

Define $f^\ast(x)=\neg f(\neg x)$. Then BDD duality gives:

$$
\mathrm{BDD}(f^\ast)
\cong
\text{dual of } \mathrm{BDD}(f),
$$

where dual means:

- swap terminal 0 and 1,
- swap low and high edges at every node.

The clutter characteristic function $\rho_n$ satisfies:

$$
\rho_n(x)=\neg \mu_n(\neg x),
$$

interpreted on the same indexed variable family $(x_s)$.

Therefore:

$$
\mathrm{BDD}(\rho_n)
\cong
\text{dual}\bigl(\mathrm{BDD}(\mu_n)\bigr).
$$

## 6. Final conclusion

The correct relation is a **Boolean dualization**, not a complement-of-indices transformation.

- The clutter condition forbids simultaneous 1’s on comparable elements.
- The monotone condition forbids 1 above 0 on comparable elements.
- These are De Morgan duals along every comparability constraint.
- At the BDD level this becomes a uniform dualization operation.

Hence the BDD for “$x$ encodes a clutter” is obtained from the BDD for $\mu_n$ by:

- swapping 0 and 1 terminals,
- swapping the low and high branches at every node,
- keeping the same variable ordering on subsets.

This gives a direct structural isomorphism between the two reduced ordered BDDs.
