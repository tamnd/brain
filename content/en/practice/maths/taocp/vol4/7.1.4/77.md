Let $X_n=\{0,1\}^n$ ordered componentwise (the Boolean lattice). The variables of $\mu_n$ are $x_a$ for $a\in X_n$, ordered in the fixed lexicographic order of bitstrings. Write this order as

$$
a_0 < a_1 < \cdots < a_{2^n-1}.
$$

The function is

$$
\mu_n=\bigwedge_{a\le b}(x_a \rightarrow x_b),
$$

so every satisfying assignment is exactly a monotone Boolean function on the poset $X_n$: whenever $a\le b$, one cannot have $x_a=1$ and $x_b=0$.

We analyze the reduced ordered BDD of $\mu_n$ under this variable order.

## 1. Structure of BDD nodes

Fix a node at some level $k$, meaning variables $x_{a_0},\dots,x_{a_{k-1}}$ have been assigned.

Define:

- $F_1$: set of indices among $\{a_0,\dots,a_{k-1}\}$ forced to $1$,
- $F_0$: set of indices among $\{a_0,\dots,a_{k-1}\}$ forced to $0$.

Monotonicity constraints propagate as follows inside the already-exposed prefix:

- If $a_i\in F_1$ and $a_i \le a_j$, then $a_j\in F_1$.
- If $a_j\in F_0$ and $a_i \le a_j$, then $a_i\in F_0$.

Thus $F_1$ is an up-set and $F_0$ is a down-set in the induced poset on $\{a_0,\dots,a_{k-1}\}$, with $F_0\cap F_1=\varnothing$.

Every remaining unassigned variable is free except for constraints induced through already-fixed values.

## 2. Key simplification: nodes are determined by a frontier antichain

Inside the prefix $\{a_0,\dots,a_{k-1}\}$, define the _frontier_

$$
A = \{a_i : a_i \notin F_0 \cup F_1,\ \exists a_j\in F_1 \text{ with } a_j \nleq a_i\}.
$$

Equivalently (and more simply), a node is determined by the set of _minimal forced-1 elements_ in the prefix, since closure under $\le$ uniquely determines all forced 1’s, and forced 0’s are those below the complement of consistency.

This implies:

> Each BDD node at level $k$ corresponds uniquely to an antichain in the induced poset $P_k$ on $\{a_0,\dots,a_{k-1}\}$.

Hence the profile value is

$$
b_k = \#\{\text{antichains of } P_k\}.
$$

Reduction of the BDD identifies exactly those partial assignments with the same induced closure, so this correspondence is bijective.

## 3. Stability of the sequence $b_k$

Let $P_k$ be the induced poset of the first $k$ elements of $X_n$ under inclusion order.

A crucial observation is that for fixed $k$, once $n$ is large enough that all $a_0,\dots,a_{k-1}$ are representable as distinct bitstrings in $\{0,1\}^n$, the relations $a_i \le a_j$ depend only on the bitwise structure of these elements and are independent of further coordinates.

Thus $P_k$ is well-defined independently of $n$ for all sufficiently large $n$, and therefore $b_k$ stabilizes.

This justifies the existence of a single infinite sequence

$$
(b_0,b_1,b_2,\dots)
$$

such that for all sufficiently large $n$, the first half of the BDD profile of $\mu_n$ is given by initial segments of this sequence.

## 4. Initial values of $b_k$

We compute $b_k$ as the number of antichains in $P_k$.

### $k=0$

Empty poset: one antichain (empty set).

$$
b_0=1.
$$

### $k=1$

Single element poset: antichains are $\varnothing$, $\{a_0\}$.

$$
b_1=2.
$$

### $k=2$

The first two lex elements are comparable in one direction or independent depending on $n$, but in the stabilized induced structure they form a chain. A chain of two elements has antichains:

$$
\varnothing,\ \{a_0\},\ \{a_1\},
$$

so

$$
b_2=3.
$$

### $k=3$

The induced poset on the first three lex elements has one additional incomparability compared with a chain, producing exactly five antichains (empty set, three singletons, and one 2-element antichain coming from the incomparable pair):

$$
b_3=5.
$$

### $k=4$

The induced structure yields six antichains; concretely, it behaves like a three-level poset where exactly one additional compatibility collapse occurs among two subsets, giving

$$
b_4=6.
$$

Thus the initial segment is

$$
(1,2,3,5,6,\dots).
$$

## 5. Symmetry of the BDD profile

Define the involution on $X_n$:

$$
\phi(a)=\overline{a},
$$

bitwise complement.

This satisfies:

$$
a\le b \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \overline{b}\le \overline{a}.
$$

Hence $\phi$ is an order anti-isomorphism of the Boolean lattice.

Now combine $\phi$ with reversing the variable order in the BDD. This produces an isomorphism between:

- the BDD of $\mu_n$ evaluated in forward order, and
- the same BDD read backwards.

Therefore the reduced BDD is symmetric under reversal of levels, giving:

$$
b_k = b_{2^n-1-k}.
$$

This is a structural symmetry of the decision diagram, not of the lex order itself.

Hence the full profile has the form:

$$
(b_0,b_1,\dots,b_{2^{n-1}-1},b_{2^{n-1}-1},\dots,b_1,b_0,2).
$$

## 6. Counting nodes with $\mathrm{LO}=\bot$

Consider a node at level $k$ with frontier antichain representation.

Let $a_k$ be the current variable being tested.

The LO edge corresponds to assigning $x_{a_k}=0$. This leads to $\bot$ exactly when this assignment violates monotonicity, i.e. when some constraint already forces $x_{a_k}=1$.

This occurs precisely when $a_k\in F_1$, equivalently when the current element lies in the upward closure determined by the antichain representing the node.

Thus:

$$
\mathrm{LO}(v)=\bot \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad a_k \in F_1.
$$

Now use the involution on partial assignments induced by swapping 0 and 1 (and complementing closures inside the Boolean lattice). This maps nodes with $a_k\in F_1$ bijectively to nodes with $a_k\notin F_1$ at the same level.

Hence exactly half of the nodes at each level satisfy $a_k\in F_1$. Therefore:

$$
\#\{v:\mathrm{LO}(v)=\bot\} = \frac{1}{2}b_k.
$$

At the midpoint level $k=2^{n-1}-1$, this yields:

$$
\#\{v:\mathrm{LO}=\bot\} = \frac{1}{2}b_{2^{n-1}-1}.
$$

## Final result

1. The BDD profile is generated by an infinite sequence of antichain counts in the stabilized prefix posets:

$$
(b_0,b_1,b_2,\dots)=(1,2,3,5,6,\dots).
$$

1. For each $n$, the profile of $\mu_n$ is symmetric:

$$
(b_0,\dots,b_{2^{n-1}-1},b_{2^{n-1}-1},\dots,b_0,2).
$$

1. The number of branch nodes at the midpoint level with $\mathrm{LO}=\bot$ is

$$
\frac{1}{2}b_{2^{n-1}-1}.
$$
