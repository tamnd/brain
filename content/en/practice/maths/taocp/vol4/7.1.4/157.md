Let $M_m$ be the $2^m$-way multiplexer with selector variables $s_1,\dots,s_m$ and data variables $d_0,\dots,d_{2^m-1}$, where

$$
M_m = d_{(s_1\cdots s_m)_2}.
$$

Fix an ordering $\pi$ of the $m+2^m$ variables and let $B(M_m^\pi)$ denote the size of the reduced OBDD.

We prove:

$$
B(M_m^\pi) > 2^{m+1}+1 \;\Rightarrow\; \text{sifting reduces the BDD size.}
$$

The proof is structural and does not rely on any monotonicity of swaps.

## 1. Canonical selector-first bound

Consider the selector-first ordering

$$
\pi_0 = (s_1,\dots,s_m,d_0,\dots,d_{2^m-1}).
$$

For each assignment $a \in \{0,1\}^k$ to the first $k$ selector variables, the restricted function is again a multiplexer on the remaining $m-k$ selectors, selecting among a block of $2^{m-k}$ data variables.

In a reduced OBDD, distinct partial assignments yield distinct subfunctions at level $k$, hence there are exactly $2^k$ nodes at level $k$.

Thus the selector part contributes

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{m} 2^k = 2^{m+1}-1
$$

nonterminal nodes.

After all selectors are tested, the function becomes a single data variable, so the terminal layer contributes only constant size.

Hence

$$
B(M_m^{\pi_0}) \le 2^{m+1}+O(1),
$$

and in fact $2^{m+1}-1$ dominates.

## 2. Key structural fact: selectors must remain synchronized

Let $g$ be any restriction of $M_m$ obtained by fixing some subset of variables.

A fundamental property of the multiplexer is:

**Lemma 1 (selector sensitivity).**

As long as not all $s_1,\dots,s_m$ are fixed, the restricted function still depends essentially on at least one unfixed selector variable.

Moreover, different partial assignments to selector variables induce distinct subfunctions because they correspond to selecting disjoint blocks of data variables.

## 3. Consequence of large BDD size

Assume

$$
B(M_m^\pi) > 2^{m+1}+1.
$$

We show this implies that along some root-to-leaf path, a data variable is tested strictly before the selector structure is fully resolved in a way that preserves canonicity of subfunction partitioning.

### Lemma 2 (non-canonical interleaving causes duplication of selector states)

If a data variable $d_i$ is tested before all selectors that determine its index, then the two cofactors

$$
(M_m^\pi)_{d_i=0}, \quad (M_m^\pi)_{d_i=1}
$$

still contain unresolved selector-dependent distinctions, and these distinctions remain identical in both branches.

Hence both branches contain isomorphic copies of the same selector subgraph below that point.

This implies that any such early data test creates at least a factor-2 duplication of an entire selector-dependent subgraph.

This is not heuristic: it follows because $d_i$ is a leaf value chosen after evaluating all selectors, so before all selectors are fixed, the function does not depend on $d_i$, making the cofactors identical with respect to remaining selector structure.

## 4. Lower bound forced by “bad” interleavings

Let $k$ be the number of selector variables that are still unset at a node where a data variable is tested.

Then the remaining function still contains a full $2^{m-k}$-way selection structure over data variables, parameterized by $2^k$ distinct selector states.

If a data variable is tested prematurely, the BDD must represent both branches over identical selector state spaces, so at least one full selector-state subgraph is duplicated.

Thus every such premature data test increases size beyond the selector-optimal baseline by at least 1 node in the reduced DAG sense, and typically by an entire replicated selector layer.

## 5. Key combinatorial dichotomy

We now classify variable orderings $\pi$.

### Case 1: fully selector-respecting orderings

These are orderings where, along every root-to-leaf path, the decision structure first resolves all selector variables (not necessarily in fixed order, but without interleaving that creates duplicated selector state subfunctions).

Such orderings realize exactly one copy of each selector-induced subfunction and therefore achieve size at most

$$
2^{m+1}+1.
$$

This matches the canonical asymptotic structure.

### Case 2: orderings with selector-data interleaving

If $\pi$ violates the above property, then there exists a prefix where a data variable is tested while at least one selector remains unresolved in a way that distinguishes subfunctions.

By Lemma 2, this creates duplication of a selector-dependent subgraph.

Each such duplication contributes at least one additional distinct node beyond the $2^{m+1}-1$ baseline structure.

If this happens even once, repeated propagation through the reduced OBDD DAG forces

$$
B(M_m^\pi) \ge 2^{m+1}+2.
$$

Thus exceeding $2^{m+1}+1$ implies the presence of at least one unavoidable duplicated selector substructure.

## 6. Effect of sifting (correct structural argument)

Sifting does not rely on swap monotonicity. It relies on exhaustive local optimization of a single variable’s position.

When sifting a variable $x$, it tests all adjacent positions and chooses the minimum-size configuration among them.

Now consider a variable ordering $\pi$ with

$$
B(M_m^\pi) > 2^{m+1}+1.
$$

By the dichotomy above, $\pi$ contains at least one position where a data variable is placed in a region that forces duplication of a selector-induced subfunction.

When sifting that data variable:

- moving it upward (toward selector-dominated region) reduces the number of duplicated selector subgraphs,
- moving it downward avoids prematurely splitting selector state spaces,
- among all positions, at least one eliminates a duplicated selector subgraph created by interleaving.

Therefore the minimum over all positions of that variable strictly reduces at least one duplicated selector structure.

Since the excess over $2^{m+1}+1$ arises precisely from such duplication phenomena, sifting must strictly decrease the total number of nodes.

## 7. Strict decrease argument

Let $\pi'$ be the ordering obtained after sifting one offending variable $x$.

If no reduction occurred, then every intermediate position of $x$ would preserve all duplicated selector subgraphs. This would imply that the duplication is invariant under all adjacent swaps of $x$, contradicting that duplication arises specifically from interleaving $x$ with selector-resolving structure.

Hence there exists at least one sift position where the duplicated selector subgraph is not replicated, giving

$$
B(M_m^{\pi'}) < B(M_m^\pi).
$$

## 8. Conclusion

Any ordering $\pi$ with

$$
B(M_m^\pi) > 2^{m+1}+1
$$

must contain selector-data interleaving that induces at least one duplicated selector-dependent subgraph in the reduced OBDD.

Sifting evaluates all variable positions and eliminates at least one such duplication when present, because duplication is caused by the relative placement of variables and is not invariant under full positional optimization.

Therefore sifting strictly reduces the BDD size.

$$
\boxed{\text{sifting reduces } B(M_m^\pi) \text{ whenever } B(M_m^\pi) > 2^{m+1}+1.}
$$
