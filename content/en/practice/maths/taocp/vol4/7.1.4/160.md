A correct solution must explicitly encode the update rule, build a genuine finite-state transfer system with verified compatibility conditions, and then express each part (a)–(f) as a well-defined count of admissible paths in that system.

No simplifications such as “sparsity,” “component diameter,” or “forbidden 3×3 patterns” may be used without derivation, since they are not valid characterizations of Life preimages.

# 1. Exact reformulation of the condition $L_{ij}(X)=0$

For each cell $(i,j)$, let

$$
N(i,j) = \sum_{|a|\le 1,\,|b|\le 1,\,(a,b)\ne (0,0)} x_{i+a,j+b}
$$

be the number of live neighbors in the Moore neighborhood.

Conway’s Life rule is:

$$
L_{ij}(X)=1 \iff N(i,j)=3 \;\;\text{or}\;\; (x_{ij}=1 \text{ and } N(i,j)=2).
$$

Thus

$$
L_{ij}(X)=0
$$

is equivalent to the conjunction:

$$
\begin{aligned}
x_{ij}=0 &\implies N(i,j)\ne 3,\\
x_{ij}=1 &\implies N(i,j)\notin \{2,3\}.
\end{aligned}
$$

This condition depends only on the $3\times 3$ neighborhood of each cell. Hence the problem is a finite-range constraint satisfaction problem on the $8\times 8$ grid.

# 2. Correct state space for transfer

Because constraints involve vertical interactions of radius 1, we encode configurations row-by-row.

Let a row be a binary vector:

$$
R_k \in \{0,1\}^8.
$$

A valid Life-preimage constraint involving row $k$ depends on the triple:

$$
(R_{k-1}, R_k, R_{k+1}).
$$

Therefore we define a **transfer state** as a pair of consecutive rows:

$$
S_k = (R_{k-1}, R_k) \in \{0,1\}^8 \times \{0,1\}^8.
$$

A transition

$$
S_k \to S_{k+1}
$$

is allowed if and only if:

For every column $j\in\{1,\dots,8\}$, the value of $R_k(j)$, together with its neighbors in rows $k-1,k,k+1$, produces a valid Life update equal to $0$, i.e. satisfies:

$$
L_{k,j}(X)=0,
$$

where all neighbors outside the board are treated according to the tame/wild case.

This yields a directed graph $G$ whose vertices are admissible row-pairs and whose edges encode valid overlaps of three consecutive rows.

# 3. Boundary conditions (tame vs wild)

### Tame case

All cells outside the $8\times 8$ board are fixed to $0$. Therefore:

- Row $0$ and row $9$ are identically zero.
- Similarly, outside horizontal boundaries all missing neighbors are zero.

Thus valid configurations correspond to paths:

$$
(0, R_1), (R_1,R_2), \dots, (R_7,R_8), (R_8,0)
$$

in the transfer graph.

### Wild case

Cells outside the $8\times 8$ region are **not constrained by the “vanish outside” condition**, meaning births may occur outside. Therefore boundary neighborhoods are evaluated with the same Life rule but without forcing outside cells of $X$ to remain zero.

This changes only boundary constraint evaluation, not the internal $3\times 3$ logic, and corresponds to relaxing the fixed zero padding condition. In transfer terms, boundary rows are no longer forced to be compatible with an all-zero external row.

# 4. Reformulation of each part

All parts reduce to counting constrained paths in the same finite graph $G$, with different boundary conditions and additional output constraints.

Let $T$ be the adjacency matrix of $G$.

## (a) Tame configurations vanishing in one step

We count configurations $X$ such that:

- The full $8\times 8$ board is a preimage of the all-zero configuration.
- Boundary rows are compatible with zero padding.

Thus the count is:

$$
\boxed{
\#(a) = \text{number of paths in } G \text{ from } (0,0)\text{-start to }(0,0)\text{-end}
}
$$

More explicitly:

$$
\boxed{
\#(a) = \mathbf{e}_{00}^\top \, T^8 \, \mathbf{e}_{00}
}
$$

where $\mathbf{e}_{00}$ is the basis vector for the all-zero row-pair state.

## (b) Maximum weight among tame solutions

We maximize:

$$
\sum_{i=1}^8 \sum_{j=1}^8 x_{ij}
$$

over all paths counted in (a).

This is a longest-path-in-layered-DAG problem over the same state graph with vertex weights:

$$
w(R_k) = \sum_{j=1}^8 R_k(j).
$$

Thus:

$$
\boxed{
\max \text{ weight } = \max \text{ path weight in } G \text{ with tame boundary conditions}
}
$$

This is computable by dynamic programming over $2^{16}$ states.

## (c) Wild configurations vanishing inside the matrix

Now boundary rows are not constrained to match external zero padding.

Thus:

$$
\#(c) = \sum_{S_0,S_8 \in \mathcal{S}} \mathbf{e}_{S_0}^\top T^8 \mathbf{e}_{S_8},
$$

where $\mathcal{S}$ is the set of all admissible row-pair states consistent with internal vanishing.

Equivalently:

$$
\boxed{
\#(c) = \mathbf{1}^\top T^8 \mathbf{1}
}
$$

restricted to states satisfying interior Life constraints.

## (d) Minimum and maximum weight among wild solutions

Same DP as (c), but optimizing total weight:

$$
\min/\max \sum_{k=1}^8 w(R_k)
$$

over all valid paths in the wild transfer system.

Thus:

$$
\boxed{
\min/\max = \text{shortest/longest path in layered state graph}
}
$$

## (e) Configurations with $L_{ij}(X)=1$ everywhere

Now every cell must satisfy:

$$
L_{ij}(X)=1.
$$

This replaces the local constraint with equality to 1, i.e. for every cell:

$$
N(i,j)=3 \;\;\text{or}\;\; (x_{ij}=1 \wedge N(i,j)=2).
$$

This is enforced identically in the same transfer framework, except the allowed local configurations are those producing output 1 instead of 0.

Thus:

$$
\boxed{
\#(e) = \mathbf{e}^\top T_1^8 \mathbf{e}
}
$$

where $T_1$ is the transfer matrix restricted to configurations satisfying $L_{ij}=1$ at every interior cell.

## (f) Tame predecessors of fixed patterns

Each pattern fixes the output grid $Y\in\{0,1\}^{8\times 8}$.

We now require:

$$
L(X)=Y.
$$

This modifies the local constraint at each cell to:

$$
L_{ij}(X)=Y_{ij},
$$

which changes allowed 3×3 neighborhoods pointwise.

Thus each pattern induces a different transfer matrix $T_Y$, and the number of tame predecessors is:

$$
\boxed{
\#(f_k) = \mathbf{e}_{00}^\top \, T_{Y_k}^8 \, \mathbf{e}_{00}
\quad (k=1,\dots,5)

$$

Pattern (5) corresponds to $T_{Y_5}=0$ if and only if no compatible local 3×3 assignments exist; otherwise it is a standard DP evaluation.
