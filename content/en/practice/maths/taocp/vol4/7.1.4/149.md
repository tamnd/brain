[Section 7.1.4: Binary Decision Diagrams](../)

**Exercise 149.** [*M20*] [M20] (Bollig, Löbbing, and Wegener.) Show that, in addition to Theorem J−,
we also have B(f π
1 , . . . , f π
m) ≤(2k −2)b0 + B(f1, . . . , fm) after a jump-down operation
of k −1 levels, when (b0, . . . , bn) is the profile of {f1, . . . , fm}.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 50s

## Solution

Let $(b_0,\ldots,b_n)$ be the profile of the family ${f_1,\ldots,f_m}$. Thus $b_0$ is the number of branch nodes on level $j$, the level that is moved downward by a jump-down operation of $k-1$ levels.

A jump-down by $k-1$ levels moves every node on level $j$ to level $j+k-1$, preserving the represented functions by inserting new branch nodes on the intervening levels. Theorem J$-$ gives the estimate

$$
B(f_1^\pi,\ldots,f_m^\pi)\le 2^kB(f_1,\ldots,f_m).
$$

The exercise asks for the sharper estimate that isolates the contribution of the nodes on the moved level.

Consider a single branch node $u$ on level $j$. Before the jump, $u$ contributes one node to the BDD. After the jump, the variable ordering requires a chain of $k$ branch nodes, one on each of the levels

$$
j+1,\ldots,j+k-1,j+k,
$$

representing all possible restrictions of the function rooted at $u$ with respect to the skipped variables. Since the chain contains $2^k-1$ internal nodes and replaces the original single node, the net increase contributed by $u$ is at most

$$
(2^k-1)-1=2^k-2.
$$

There are exactly $b_0$ nodes on the moved level. Hence the total increase in the number of branch nodes is at most

$$
(2^k-2)b_0.
$$

Every node on every other level remains present, possibly with changed pointers, but no additional contribution beyond the expansions of these $b_0$ nodes is required. Therefore

$$
B(f_1^\pi,\ldots,f_m^\pi)
   \le
B(f_1,\ldots,f_m)
   +(2^k-2)b_0.
$$

This is precisely the desired inequality,

$$
\boxed{
B(f_1^\pi,\ldots,f_m^\pi)
\le
(2^k-2)b_0+B(f_1,\ldots,f_m).
}
$$

The bound is stronger than Theorem J$-$ because it charges only the nodes on the level that is actually moved. All remaining nodes contribute exactly as before.

This completes the proof.

∎
