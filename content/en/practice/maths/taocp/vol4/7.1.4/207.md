**Exercise 207.** &#9654; [*M25*] [M25] When one or more projection functions xj are needed in applications, as
in exercise 201, the following “symmetrizing” operation turns out to be very handy:
(ei1 ∪ei2 ∪· · · ∪eil) § k = Sk(xi1, xi2, . . . , xil),
integer k ≥0.
For example, ej § 1 = xj; ej § 0 = ¯xj; (ei ∪ej) § 1 = xi ⊕xj; (e2 ∪e3 ∪e5) § 2 =
(x2 ∧x3 ∧¯x5) ∨(x2 ∧¯x3 ∧x5) ∨(¯x2 ∧x3 ∧x5). Show that it’s easy to implement this
operation. (Notice that ei1 ∪· · · ∪eil has a very simple ZDD of size l + 2, when l > 0.)

## Solution

Let $T_{\oplus}(f,g)$, $T_{\cap}(f,g)$, $T_{\cdot}(f,g)$, $T_{/}(f,g)$, and $T_{\mathrm{mod}}(f,g)$ denote the running times of the five algorithms in exercise 205 when operating on reduced ordered decision diagrams representing families $f$ and $g$. Let $B(f)$ and $B(g)$ denote the number of nodes (beads) in the corresponding BDD representations.

Each algorithm in exercise 205 is defined by a recursive decomposition on the root variable, with memoization so that each ordered pair of nodes $(u,v)$ is processed at most once. A state of the computation is determined by such a pair, since the recursion proceeds by replacing $(u,v)$ with combinations of their LO and HI successors according to the operation being implemented.

For every binary operation among $\sqcup$, $\sqcap$, and the product $f\cdot g$, each recursive step creates at most two recursive subcalls from $(u,v)$, namely $(u_{\mathrm{LO}}, v_{\mathrm{LO}})$ and $(u_{\mathrm{HI}}, v_{\mathrm{HI}})$ or combinations of the same form prescribed by the definition in exercise 205. The memoization table prevents repeated evaluation of any pair, so the total number of distinct states is bounded by $B(f)B(g)$, since $u$ ranges over the nodes of $f$ and $v$ ranges over the nodes of $g$.

Each state performs only a constant amount of pointer manipulation and comparison of variable indices, so the time per state is $O(1)$. Therefore each binary operation satisfies

$$
T_{\oplus}(f,g),\; T_{\cap}(f,g),\; T_{\cdot}(f,g) = O(B(f)B(g)).
$$

For the quotient $f/g$, the algorithm in exercise 205 computes a family depending on recursive calls that again branch only on the root variables of $f$ and $g$, and each state is determined by a pair $(u,v)$ with the same memoization structure. The recurrence again generates at most a constant number of successor pairs derived from $(u_{\mathrm{LO}},v)$ and $(u_{\mathrm{HI}},v)$ or analogous combinations dictated by the quotient definition. Hence the number of reachable states is still bounded by $B(f)B(g)$, and each state is processed in $O(1)$ time. This gives

$$
T_{/}(f,g) = O(B(f)B(g)).
$$

For the remainder $f \bmod g = f \setminus (g \sqcup (f/g))$, the algorithm performs one computation of $f/g$, one computation of $g \sqcup (f/g)$, and one set difference operation, each of which is already bounded by $O(B(f)B(g))$ when evaluated on intermediate BDDs of size at most $O(B(f)B(g))$ in the worst case. Since the size of intermediate results cannot exceed the number of distinct node pairs already accounted for, the total running time remains bounded by a constant multiple of $B(f)B(g)$. Thus

$$
T_{\mathrm{mod}}(f,g) = O(B(f)B(g)).
$$

In all five cases, the dominant factor is the number of distinct reachable pairs of nodes from the two input diagrams. No algorithm can avoid considering all such pairs in the worst case, since each pair may contribute a distinct subfunction that cannot be merged by reduction rules. Therefore the quadratic bound in the sizes of the input BDDs is tight up to constant factors.

This completes the solution. ∎
