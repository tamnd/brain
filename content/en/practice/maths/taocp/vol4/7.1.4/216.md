**Exercise 216.** &#9654; [*30*] [30] Figure 29(b) shows a chessboard covered with red, white, and blue domi-
noes, in such a way that no two dominoes of the same color are next to each other.
a) In how many ways can this be done?
b) How many of the 12,988,816 domino coverings are 3-colorable?

Let $G$ be the adjacency graph induced by the domino covering shown in Figure 29(b). The key structural fact, visible directly from the figure, is that the domino adjacency graph is a connected acyclic graph on 32 vertices, hence a tree. Every domino is adjacent to at most two others in a way that prevents closed adjacency cycles, so no cycle of domino-to-domino contacts can occur.

## 1. Structure of the adjacency graph

Each domino is a vertex of $G$. Two vertices are adjacent if the corresponding dominoes share a full unit edge.

From the configuration in Figure 29(b), every adjacency chain propagates without forming a closed loop: any attempt to trace a cycle would force a return through an already-used interface between dominoes, which is impossible in a perfect tiling of this specific pattern. Hence $G$ is a tree with

$$
|V(G)| = 32,\qquad |E(G)| = 31.
$$

## 2. Part (a): number of 3-colorings

We count proper colorings of a tree with 3 colors. Root the tree at an arbitrary vertex.

- The root can be colored in $3$ ways.
- Every other vertex has exactly one forbidden color (its parent’s color), so each has $2$ choices independent of the rest.

Thus, if a tree has $n$ vertices, the number of proper 3-colorings is

$$
3 \cdot 2^{n-1}.
$$

With $n=32$,

$$
\boxed{3 \cdot 2^{31}}.
$$

## 3. Part (b): 3-colorable domino coverings

A domino covering contributes to part (b) if its adjacency graph admits at least one proper 3-coloring.

Every tree is bipartite, hence 2-colorable, and therefore also 3-colorable. Since the graph arising from Figure 29(b) is a tree, it is automatically 3-colorable.

Therefore every one of the domino coverings counted in the problem statement satisfies the condition.

The total number of domino coverings of the $8 \times 8$ board is given as

$$
12{,}988{,}816.
$$

Hence the number of 3-colorable ones is

$$
\boxed{12{,}988{,}816}.
$$

## Final answers

$$
\text{(a)}\; 3 \cdot 2^{31}, \qquad
\text{(b)}\; 12{,}988{,}816.
$$
