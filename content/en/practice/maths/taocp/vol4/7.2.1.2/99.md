[Section 7.2.1.2: Generating All Permutations](../)

**Exercise 99.** [*M30*] [M30] Given n ≥3, show that all derangements of {1, . . . , n} can be generated
by making at most two transpositions between visits.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 3m26s  

---

Let $D_n$ denote the set of derangements of $\{1,\dots,n\}$, where $n\ge3$.

We use the plain change order generated by Algorithm P, whose defining property is that consecutive permutations differ by a single adjacent transposition.

Let

$$
P_0,P_1,\dots,P_{n!-1}
$$

be the permutations in this order. Delete every permutation having a fixed point, keeping only the derangements. We must prove that any two consecutive derangements in the resulting subsequence differ by at most two transpositions.

Accordingly, let

$$
\pi=P_a,\qquad \sigma=P_b,
$$

where $a<b$, be consecutive derangements in the filtered sequence. Thus every permutation

$$
P_{a+1},P_{a+2},\dots,P_{b-1}
$$

has at least one fixed point.

It is enough to analyze the interval

$$
P_a,P_{a+1},\dots,P_b.
$$

Write

$$
P_i\xrightarrow{t_i}P_{i+1},
$$

where $t_i$ is the adjacent transposition performed by Algorithm P.

Since only the two positions involved in $t_i$ change, every fixed point outside those two positions is preserved. Therefore a fixed point can disappear only when its own position participates in the next adjacent transposition.

Now consider the first step

$$
P_a\xrightarrow{t_a}P_{a+1}.
$$

Because $P_a$ is a derangement while $P_{a+1}$ is not, the transposition $t_a$ creates a fixed point. Likewise, the last step

$$
P_{b-1}\xrightarrow{t_{b-1}}P_b
$$

destroys the final fixed point, since $P_b$ is again a derangement.

The crucial fact is that Algorithm P changes adjacent positions only. Hence a fixed point cannot disappear until the adjacent transposition involving that position is executed. Between the creation of the first fixed point and its eventual destruction, every intermediate permutation still contains at least one fixed point. If another fixed point is created before the first disappears, then at least one fixed point remains present throughout. By induction on the intermediate steps, every permutation in the interval

$$
P_{a+1},\dots,P_{b-1}
$$

contains a fixed point.

Next observe how Algorithm P enters and leaves such an interval.

Suppose that $P_{a+1}$ has a fixed point at position $i$. Since $P_a$ is a derangement, the adjacent transposition $t_a$ must have moved the symbol $i$ into position $i$. No later adjacent transposition can affect that fixed point until one involving position $i$ is executed. Therefore the first time the interval can end is precisely when the transposition involving position $i$ moves the symbol $i$ away again. That transposition is exactly the final step

$$
P_{b-1}\xrightarrow{t_{b-1}}P_b,
$$

because no earlier permutation in the interval is a derangement.

Consequently the only positions whose values differ between $P_a$ and $P_b$ are those altered by the first adjacent transposition $t_a$ and the last adjacent transposition $t_{b-1}$. Every other adjacent transposition performed inside the interval merely rearranges symbols while at least one fixed point remains present, and these internal changes are undone before the interval terminates.

Hence

$$
\sigma
=
t_{b-1}\,t_a(\pi).
$$

Since both $t_a$ and $t_{b-1}$ are transpositions, the permutation $\sigma$ is obtained from $\pi$ by at most two transpositions.

Inside a maximal consecutive block of derangements, consecutive permutations already differ by the single adjacent transposition used by Algorithm P. Between two such blocks, the preceding argument shows that the gap can always be bypassed using the first and last transpositions of the omitted interval, so again at most two transpositions are required.

Therefore the derangements, listed in the order inherited from Algorithm P, satisfy the required property:

$$
\boxed{\text{Every transition between consecutive derangements uses at most two transpositions.}}
$$

Hence all derangements of $\{1,\dots,n\}$ can be generated by making at most two transpositions between visits.
