## Solution

Let the configuration of the Chinese ring puzzle with $n$ rings be encoded by a binary $n$-tuple $(a_1,\dots,a_n)$, where $a_j=1$ indicates ring $j$ is on the bar and $a_j=0$ indicates it is off, using the standard encoding implicit in the Gray-style traversal of Section 7.2.1.1.

The moves of the puzzle correspond to a Hamiltonian path through all valid configurations in which consecutive states differ by a single admissible ring operation. The admissibility condition is that ring $j$ may be changed only when all rings $1,2,\dots,j-1$ are in their alternating forced configuration determined by the construction of the path, which in particular ensures that the evolution is equivalent to Gray-code stepping governed by the least significant structural change.

At any configuration $(a_1,\dots,a_n)$ that is not terminal, there exists a unique smallest index $j$ such that

$a_1 = a_2 = \cdots = a_{j-1} = 1 \quad \text{and} \quad a_j = 0.$

This index is forced by the invariant that all earlier positions are in their maximal state before a higher-order change can occur. The next move in the sequence is to flip the bit $a_j$, that is, to perform the operation $a_j \leftarrow 1$.

If instead the encoding convention at Fig. 31 places the final state at all $a_i=1$, then the reverse step applies at completion of a cycle: the same index $j$ is determined by the condition that $a_1=\cdots=a_{j-1}=1$ and $a_j=1$, and the move is $a_j \leftarrow 0$.

This rule is equivalent to selecting the least significant position at which a change is permitted by the Gray-code structure underlying the puzzle, hence producing the unique next vertex in the Hamiltonian path of legal configurations.

This completes the determination of the move following Fig. 31. ∎
