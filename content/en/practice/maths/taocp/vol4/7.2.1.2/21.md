**Exercise 21.** [*M21*] [M21] (E. P. McCravy, 1971.) How many solutions does the alphametic (6) have
in radix b?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 6m05s

## Solution

Let the addition be performed in base $b$ with carries $c_1,c_2,c_3,c_4 \in {0,1}$:

$$
\begin{array}{cccc}
 & S & E & N & D \\
+& M & O & R & E \\
\hline
 & M & O & N & E & Y
\end{array}
$$

Column relations are

$$
\begin{aligned}
D+E &= Y + bc_1, \\
N+R+c_1 &= E + bc_2, \\
E+O+c_2 &= N + bc_3, \\
S+M+c_3 &= O + bc_4, \\
c_4 &= M.
\end{aligned}
$$

Since all carries lie in ${0,1}$, the last equation gives $M \in {0,1}$. The leading digit of a number is nonzero, hence $M \neq 0$, so $M=1$ and $c_4=1$.

The fourth column becomes

$$
S + 1 + c_3 = O + b.
$$

Hence

$$
O = S + 1 + c_3 - b.
$$

Since $0 \le O \le b-1$, one must have $S + 1 + c_3 \ge b$ and $S + 1 + c_3 \le 2b-1$, which forces

$$
S \in \{b-2, b-1\}.
$$

Two cases occur.

### Case 1: $c_3=0$

Then $S+1 \ge b$, hence $S=b-1$, and

$$
O = (b-1)+1-b = 0.
$$

The third column gives

$$
E + O + c_2 = N.
$$

With $O=0$,

$$
N = E + c_2.
$$

If $c_2=0$, then $N=E$, contradicting distinct letters, hence $c_2=1$ and

$$
N = E+1.
$$

The second column becomes

$$
N + R + c_1 = E + b.
$$

Substituting $N=E+1$ gives

$$
E+1 + R + c_1 = E + b,
$$

so

$$
R = b-1-c_1.
$$

If $c_1=0$, then $R=b-1=S$, contradiction, hence $c_1=1$ and

$$
R=b-2.
$$

Distinctness now gives

$$
S=b-1,\quad O=0,\quad M=1,\quad R=b-2,\quad N=E+1.
$$

The only forbidden values for $E$ come from distinctness constraints:

$$
E \notin \{0,1,b-2,b-1,b-3\},
$$

since $E=0$ or $1$ collides with $O$ or $M$, and $E=b-2$ or $b-3$ forces $N$ to collide with $R$ or $S$.

Thus $E$ can take any value in

$$
\{2,3,\dots,b-4\},
$$

which contains $b-5$ elements when $b \ge 5$, and is empty otherwise.

Each such choice determines all letters uniquely.

### Case 2: $c_3=1$

Then $S=b-2$ and $O=0$. The third column becomes

$$
E + c_2 = N + b.
$$

If $c_2=0$, then $E=N+b$, impossible since $E \le b-1$. Hence $c_2=1$, giving

$$
E+1 = N+b.
$$

Thus $E=b-1$ and $N=0$.

This contradicts $O=0$, so this case yields no solutions.

### Final count

Only Case 1 contributes. Therefore the number of solutions is

$$
\boxed{\max(b-5,\,0)}.
$$

This completes the solution. ∎
