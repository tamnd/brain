Let $P$ be a finite poset and let $\mathcal{L}(P)$ be the set of all linear extensions (topological sorts) of $P$. Define a graph $G$ as follows:

- vertices: elements of $\mathcal{L}(P)$,
- edges: two linear extensions are connected by an edge if one can be obtained from the other by a single adjacent transposition of two incomparable elements.

A step of one adjacent swap is exactly traversal of one edge of $G$. A step of at most two adjacent swaps corresponds to a path of length at most $2$ in $G$.

The goal is to produce an ordering of all vertices of $G$ such that consecutive vertices are at distance at most $2$ in $G$. Equivalently, we want a Hamiltonian path in $G^2$, the square of $G$.

## 1. Connectivity of the linear extension graph

### Lemma 1

The graph $G$ is connected.

### Proof

Let $L, L' \in \mathcal{L}(P)$. We show $L$ can be transformed into $L'$ by a sequence of adjacent swaps of incomparable elements.

Consider $L'$ as the target order. We perform a constrained bubble-sort starting from $L$.

At each step, locate the leftmost position $i$ where the current linear extension $L^{(t)}$ differs from $L'$, meaning the element at position $i$ in $L^{(t)}$ is not the same as in $L'$. Let the correct element for position $i$ (according to $L'$) appear at some later position $j>i$ in $L^{(t)}$.

We move this element leftwards toward position $i$ by swapping it with its left neighbor repeatedly.

It remains to justify that each such swap is valid, i.e. the swapped adjacent pair is incomparable in $P$.

If at some stage the element $x$ at position $k$ must move left past $y$ at position $k-1$, then $x$ appears before $y$ in $L'$. If $x \prec y$ in $P$, then $x$ must appear before $y$ in every linear extension, contradicting that $y$ currently lies left of $x$. Hence $x \not\prec y$. Similarly $y \not\prec x$, so $x$ and $y$ are incomparable.

Thus every swap used is a valid edge of $G$, and the process terminates at $L'$. Therefore $G$ is connected. ∎

## 2. Reducing the problem to a tree traversal

Let $T$ be any spanning tree of $G$.

Since $T \subseteq G$, every edge of $T$ is also an edge of $G$. Hence any path in $T$ is also a path in $G$, and in particular has length measured in adjacent swaps.

We will construct a Hamiltonian path in $T^2$, which automatically yields a Hamiltonian path in $G^2$.

## 3. DFS ordering gives a Hamiltonian path in $T^2$

Root the tree $T$ at an arbitrary vertex $r$. Perform a depth-first search (DFS) traversal of $T$, listing vertices in the order they are first visited. Let this order be

$$
v_1, v_2, \dots, v_N.
$$

### Lemma 2

For every $k$, the distance in $T$ between $v_k$ and $v_{k+1}$ is at most $2$.

### Proof

In DFS, each step from $v_k$ to $v_{k+1}$ occurs in one of two ways:

1. **Forward step:** $v_{k+1}$ is a child of $v_k$. Then they are adjacent in $T$, so distance is $1$.
2. **Backtracking step:** DFS finishes exploring a subtree and returns to the nearest ancestor with an unexplored child. In this case, $v_{k+1}$ is the next unvisited child of some ancestor of $v_k$. The transition goes from $v_k$ to its parent, and then to a sibling subtree root. Thus $v_k$ and $v_{k+1}$ are connected by a path of length $2$ in $T$.

No larger jump can occur in DFS, since movement always proceeds along parent links or to a child of an ancestor.

Hence

$$
\operatorname{dist}_T(v_k, v_{k+1}) \le 2.
$$

∎

## 4. Translation back to linear extensions

Because $T \subseteq G$, every edge of $T$ is an adjacent swap of incomparable elements in the poset.

Therefore:

- distance $1$ in $T$ corresponds to one adjacent transposition,
- distance $2$ in $T$ corresponds to two adjacent transpositions,
- and both correspond to valid moves in $G$.

Thus the DFS ordering satisfies:

$$
\forall k,\quad \text{the linear extensions } v_k \text{ and } v_{k+1} \text{ differ by at most two adjacent swaps.}
$$

## 5. Conclusion

The DFS traversal of a spanning tree of the linear extension graph produces an ordering of all linear extensions such that consecutive elements differ by a path of length at most $2$ in the swap graph. Equivalently, each step is achievable by one or two adjacent transpositions of incomparable elements.

Hence all topological sorts of a finite poset can be generated in a sequence where only one or two adjacent transpositions are made at each step. ∎
