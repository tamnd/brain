[Section 7.2.1.2: Generating All Permutations](../)

**Exercise 58.** [*M21*] [M21] True or false: If Algorithm E begins with a0 . . . an−1 = x1 . . . xn then the
final permutation visited begins with a0 = xn.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 6m35s

## Solution

The statement claims that for Algorithm E, if the initial permutation is $a_0 a_1 \dots a_{n-1} = x_1 x_2 \dots x_n$, then the final permutation visited always begins with $a_0 = x_n$.

This is false.

Algorithm E in this section is defined via the coupled recurrences

$$
\alpha_m = \beta_{m-1}^{-1}\tau_{\rho!(m)}\beta_{m-1}\alpha_{m-1}, \qquad
\beta_m = \sigma_{\rho!(m)}\beta_{m-1},
$$

and the text explicitly states that, for suitable choices of the generators $\sigma_k$ and $\tau_k$, Algorithm E specializes to concrete permutation generators such as the adjacent-transposition constructions used earlier in Section 7.2.1.2, including Algorithm P.

Take the specialization described in Exercise 55, where $\sigma_k$ is the flip operation and $\tau_k = (0,k)$. In that case, Algorithm E reduces to the plain-change mechanism underlying Algorithm P, so all structural properties of Algorithm P are inherited as a special case.

For Algorithm P, the generated Hamiltonian cycle on permutations does not end in a permutation whose first entry equals the last element of the initial permutation. In the smallest nontrivial case $n=3$, starting from $123$, the plain-change sequence is

$$
123,\ 132,\ 312,\ 321,\ 231,\ 213.
$$

The final visited permutation before the cycle closes is $213$, whose first entry is $2$, while the last element of the initial permutation is $3$. Hence $a_0 \neq x_3$.

Since this behavior occurs for a valid specialization of Algorithm E, the asserted property cannot hold for Algorithm E in general.

This completes the proof. ∎
