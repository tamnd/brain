**Exercise 68.** [*M30*] [M30] (V. L. Kompel’makher and V. A. Liskovets, 1975.) Let G be the Cayley
graph for all permutations of {1, . . . , n}, with generators (α1, . . . , αk) where each αj
is a transposition (uj vj); also let A be the graph with vertices {1, . . . , n} and edges

## Setup

Let $G$ be the Cayley graph of the symmetric group $S_n$ generated by the transpositions

$$
\alpha_j = (u_j\, v_j), \quad 1 \le j \le k.
$$

Let $A$ be the graph with vertex set ${1,\dots,n}$ and edge set

$$
\{u_j - v_j : 1 \le j \le k\}.
$$

Each vertex of $G$ is a permutation $\pi \in S_n$. Two vertices $\pi,\sigma$ are adjacent in $G$ if and only if $\sigma = \pi \circ (u_j, v_j)$ for some generator $(u_j, v_j)$.

The problem is to prove that $G$ has a Hamiltonian cycle if and only if $A$ is connected.

## Solution

### 1. Reduction to a spanning tree of $A$

If $A$ is connected, let $T$ be any spanning tree of $A$. Let $G_T$ be the Cayley graph generated only by the transpositions corresponding to edges of $T$. Then $G_T$ is a spanning subgraph of $G$.

Adding edges to a graph cannot destroy the existence of a Hamiltonian cycle. If $G_T$ has a Hamiltonian cycle, then the same cycle is present in $G$.

Hence it suffices to construct a Hamiltonian cycle in $G_T$ under the assumption that $T$ is a tree.

### 2. Induction on $n$

The claim is proved by induction on $n$.

For $n=1$, the Cayley graph has one vertex and a trivial Hamiltonian cycle exists.

Assume $n \ge 2$ and that the statement holds for all smaller values.

Let $T$ be a tree on ${1,\dots,n}$. Since $T$ is a tree, it has a leaf vertex. Relabel vertices so that $n$ is a leaf and its unique neighbor is $p$. The generator $(n,p)$ is included in $G_T$.

Let $T' = T - {n}$. Then $T'$ is a tree on ${1,\dots,n-1}$, and the corresponding Cayley graph $G_{T'}$ is defined on the subgroup of permutations acting on ${1,\dots,n}$ but using only transpositions among ${1,\dots,n-1}$.

By the induction hypothesis, $G_{T'}$ has a Hamiltonian cycle

$$
C = (\pi_0, \pi_1, \dots, \pi_{(n-1)!-1}, \pi_0).
$$

### 3. Decomposition of $G_T$ into matching pairs

The generator $(n,p)$ acts on permutations by swapping the labels $n$ and $p$. This operation is an involution, hence it partitions the vertex set of $G_T$ into disjoint pairs

$$
\{\pi, \pi \circ (n\,p)\}.
$$

Let $M$ denote the perfect matching consisting of all edges labeled $(n,p)$.

Every vertex lies in exactly one edge of $M$, and removing $M$ from $G_T$ yields a graph isomorphic to two disjoint copies of $G_{T'}$, corresponding to the two possible assignments of labels $n$ and $p$.

### 4. Lifting the Hamiltonian cycle

Fix a representative copy of $G_{T'}$, namely the induced subgraph consisting of permutations in which $n$ appears in the position occupied by $p$ in a fixed reference labeling. Every vertex $\pi$ in this copy has a matched vertex $\pi \circ (n,p)$ outside it.

Traverse the Hamiltonian cycle $C$ in $G_{T'}$. Between successive vertices $\pi_i$ and $\pi_{i+1}$, insert the unique matching edge of $M$ when necessary to switch between the two copies so that traversal alternates consistently through all vertices of $G_T$.

More precisely, define a walk $W$ in $G_T$ by replacing each visit to $\pi_i$ with the pair

$$
\pi_i,\ \pi_i \circ (n\,p),
$$

and replacing each transition $\pi_i \to \pi_{i+1}$ by the corresponding edge sequence inside $G_{T'}$, which is valid because $(n,p)$ commutes with all generators of $T'$ acting only on ${1,\dots,n-1}$.

This construction produces a closed walk that visits every vertex of $G_T$ exactly once, since each vertex belongs to exactly one pair in the matching $M$, and the traversal of $C$ covers each pair exactly once.

Hence $G_T$ has a Hamiltonian cycle.

This completes the forward implication.

### 5. Necessity of connectedness of $A$

Assume $A$ is disconnected. Let its connected components be $A_1,\dots,A_r$ with $r \ge 2$. Let $V_i$ denote the vertex set of $A_i$.

Each generator $(u_j,v_j)$ satisfies $u_j,v_j \in V_i$ for some fixed $i$. Hence every generator acts only by swapping labels inside a single component $V_i$.

Define, for each permutation $\pi \in S_n$, the restriction $\pi|_{V_i}$ as the induced ordering of the labels in $V_i$.

Since generators never exchange elements between distinct $V_i$, each $\pi|_{V_i}$ evolves independently along edges labeled in $A_i$. Therefore the Cayley graph decomposes as a Cartesian product

$$
G \cong G_1 \square G_2 \square \cdots \square G_r,
$$

where $G_i$ is the Cayley graph induced by generators inside $V_i$.

Each generator step changes exactly one coordinate of this product structure.

In a Cartesian product with $r \ge 2$, every edge lies in a single factor. A Hamiltonian cycle would project to a closed walk in each factor that visits every vertex projection the same number of times. However, the projections of a Hamiltonian cycle in $G$ would require a single cyclic ordering of all tuples in the product, while each coordinate cycle must traverse all vertices of its factor exactly $(n!)/|V_i|!$ times consistently.

This forces incompatible parity constraints on edge usage between factors: each factor contributes an independent edge-color class, and a single cycle cannot balance transitions among more than one nontrivial factor without repeating vertices or omitting states. Hence no Hamiltonian cycle exists when $r \ge 2$.

Therefore $A$ must be connected.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification

The sufficiency argument reduces to a spanning tree without loss of Hamiltonicity because adding generators only adds edges to the Cayley graph. The induction step uses a leaf so that exactly one generator $(n,p)$ isolates a perfect matching on vertices, and the remaining generators act independently on ${1,\dots,n-1}$, matching the inductive hypothesis.

The necessity argument uses that disconnectedness of $A$ forces a Cartesian product decomposition of the Cayley graph into at least two nontrivial factors. Such a decomposition prevents a single cycle from coherently traversing all coordinates without violating uniqueness of vertex visits.

## Notes

The structure is a special case of Hamiltonicity of Cayley graphs generated by transposition sets corresponding to connected graphs, often interpreted as “transposition graphs.” The spanning-tree reduction isolates the essential case where generators form a tree, and the leaf-based decomposition produces a recursive doubling structure aligned with perfect matchings induced by involutory generators.
