**Exercise 64.** [*23*] [23] A “doubly Gray” code for permutations is a Gray cycle with the additional
property that δk+1 = δk ± 1 for all k. Compton and Williamson have proved that such
codes exist for all n ≥3. How many doubly Gray codes exist for n = 5?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 6m54s

## Solution

A Gray cycle for permutations of ${1,2,3,4,5}$ is determined by a delta sequence

$$
\delta_0\delta_1\cdots\delta_{119}, \qquad \delta_k \in \{1,2,3,4\},
$$

satisfying

$$
\pi_{k+1} = (\delta_k\,\,\delta_k+1)\,\pi_k,
$$

together with the closure condition $\pi_{120}=\pi_0$. The condition of the exercise adds

$$
\delta_{k+1}=\delta_k\pm 1 \quad (0 \le k \le 118),
$$

and cyclically $\delta_0=\delta_{119}\pm 1$.

Thus the delta sequence is a closed walk of length $120$ on the path graph $P_4$ with vertices ${1,2,3,4}$, where each step moves to an adjacent vertex, and the final vertex equals the initial one. Every such walk determines a unique sequence of adjacent transpositions, hence a unique Gray cycle once the initial permutation is fixed, for example $\pi_0=12345$. Conversely, every doubly Gray cycle produces such a closed walk, since the delta sequence records exactly the chosen adjacent swap at each step.

The number of such delta sequences equals the number of closed walks of length $120$ on $P_4$. Let $A$ be the adjacency matrix of $P_4$. Then the number of closed walks of length $120$ equals $\mathrm{tr}(A^{120})$.

The eigenvalues of the adjacency matrix of $P_4$ are

$$
2\cos\frac{k\pi}{5}, \qquad k=1,2,3,4,
$$

hence

$$
\lambda_1=\varphi,\quad \lambda_2=\varphi^{-1},\quad \lambda_3=-\varphi^{-1},\quad \lambda_4=-\varphi,
$$

where $\varphi=\frac{1+\sqrt5}{2}$. Therefore

$$
\mathrm{tr}(A^{120})=\varphi^{120}+\varphi^{-120}+(-\varphi^{-1})^{120}+(-\varphi)^{120}.
$$

Since $120$ is even, both signs disappear, giving

$$
\mathrm{tr}(A^{120})=2\left(\varphi^{120}+\varphi^{-120}\right).
$$

Each such closed walk corresponds uniquely to a doubly Gray cycle on ${1,2,3,4,5}$ starting at a fixed initial permutation, and no further identifications occur within the definition of a Gray cycle.

Hence the total number of doubly Gray codes is

$$
\boxed{2\left(\varphi^{120}+\varphi^{-120}\right)}, \qquad \varphi=\frac{1+\sqrt5}{2}.
$$

∎
