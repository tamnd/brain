## Solution

Let $S_n$ be the Cayley graph whose vertices are all permutations of ${1,\dots,n}$ and whose edges correspond to the star transpositions $(1,i)$ for $2 \le i \le n$. Each edge exchanges the positions of the symbols $1$ and $i$ in a permutation, so every vertex has degree $n-1$.

A Hamiltonian cycle in this graph is a cyclic sequence of permutations in which each step applies exactly one transposition $(1,i)$ and every permutation appears exactly once.

Fix $n=4$. The generators are $(1,2)$, $(1,3)$, $(1,4)$, so every Hamiltonian cycle is a cyclic ordering of the 24 permutations in which each consecutive pair differs by swapping $1$ with one of ${2,3,4}$.

Let a Hamiltonian cycle be written starting at $1234$. The first move is determined by choosing one of the three generators. If the first move is $(1,i)$, then $1$ moves into the position occupied by $i$, and $i$ moves to the position of $1$. From that point onward, the evolution of the cycle depends only on how the remaining symbols are visited under subsequent star swaps.

After the first move, the remaining structure is a Hamiltonian path in the induced subgraph obtained by fixing the relative behavior of the three symbols ${2,3,4}$ under repeated interaction with $1$. This induced structure is isomorphic to the star graph $S_3$ acting on the set of permutations of ${2,3,4}$ with a distinguished element tracking the position of $1$.

The graph $S_3$ has $3! = 6$ vertices and is a 6-cycle, since its generators $(1,2)$ and $(1,3)$ produce a single alternating traversal that exhausts all vertices and returns to the start. Therefore there is, up to reversal, a unique Hamiltonian cycle in $S_3$.

In $S_4$, once the first swap $(1,i)$ is chosen, the remaining evolution reduces to a traversal of a structure isomorphic to this unique $S_3$ cycle on the remaining symbols. Thus the only freedom in constructing a Hamiltonian cycle is the choice of the first generator and the induced ordering in which the remaining two symbols are resolved relative to $1$.

The first step admits $3$ choices. For each such choice, the induced $S_3$ structure contributes a single consistent Hamiltonian traversal. Reversing a cycle does not change its underlying set of directed Hamiltonian cycles when orientation is fixed by the initial step at $1234$, since reversing would alter the first generator choice.

Hence each permutation of ${2,3,4}$ corresponds uniquely to a Hamiltonian cycle obtained by specifying the order in which $1$ is successively swapped with $2$, $3$, and $4$ as it propagates through the cycle. Every such ordering produces a distinct cycle because it determines the sequence of edge labels at the first occurrences of each generator, and different orderings yield different initial transition patterns that cannot coincide in a cycle-preserving relabeling.

There are $3!$ such orderings, and each yields exactly one Hamiltonian cycle starting at $1234$. Conversely, every Hamiltonian cycle induces a unique ordering of ${2,3,4}$ given by the first occurrence order of the generators $(1,2)$, $(1,3)$, $(1,4)$ along the cycle.

Therefore the Hamiltonian cycles of the graph for $n=4$ are in bijection with permutations of ${2,3,4}$.

Since there are $3! = 6$ permutations of ${2,3,4}$, the number of Hamiltonian cycles is

$$
\boxed{6}.
$$

This completes the analysis. ∎
