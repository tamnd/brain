**Exercise 2.** [*15*] Prove that $m$ is always greater than $n$ at the beginning of step E1, except possibly the first time this step occurs.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 33s

## Solution

At the beginning of step E1, except possibly on its first occurrence, the variables $m$ and $n$ have just been set in step E3. Step E3 is reached only when step E2 finds that $r \ne 0$.

By step E1, the remainder $r$ is defined by dividing $m$ by $n$, and the parenthetical comment in E1 states that

$$0 \le r < n.$$

Since E3 is executed only when $r \ne 0$, this inequality becomes

$$0 < r < n.$$

Step E3 then performs the replacements

$$m \leftarrow n, \qquad n \leftarrow r.$$

Hence, at the next occurrence of step E1, the current values satisfy

$$m = \text{old } n,
\qquad
n = \text{old } r.$$

Because $0 < \text{old } r < \text{old } n$, we obtain

$$n < m.$$

Therefore $m$ is always greater than $n$ at the beginning of step E1 after the first time that step occurs.

The first occurrence of E1 is exceptional, since the original input may satisfy either $m > n$ or $m < n$. This completes the proof.
