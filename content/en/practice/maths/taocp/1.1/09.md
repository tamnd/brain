**Corrected Solution for Exercise 1.1.9**

Let

$$C_1 = (Q_1, I_1, \Omega_1, f_1), \qquad
C_2 = (Q_2, I_2, \Omega_2, f_2)$$

be computational methods in the sense of ┬¦1.1. The goal is to give a **rigorous set-theoretic definition** of what it means for $C_2$ to be a representation or simulation of $C_1$. Intuitively, $C_2$ may take more steps and retain more state information, but it must faithfully reproduce the computations of $C_1$.

### 1. Representation map

Define a **representation map**

$$\pi: Q_2 \to Q_1$$

where $\pi(q)$ is the abstract state of $C_1$ that the concrete state $q \in Q_2$ represents.

### 2. Simulation relation

Define the **simulation relation** $R \subseteq Q_1 \times Q_2$ by

$$(p,q) \in R \iff \pi(q) = p.$$

The relation $R$ identifies which states of $C_2$ correctly represent states of $C_1$. The definition of simulation will require that $R$ is preserved along all reachable states of $C_2$ starting from $I_2$.

### 3. Conditions for $C_2$ to simulate $C_1$

We say that $C_2$ **simulates** $C_1$ if there exists a map $\pi$ satisfying the following conditions.

#### (a) Initial states

For every initial state $p_0 \in I_1$, there exists at least one initial state $q_0 \in I_2$ such that $(p_0, q_0) \in R$:

$$\forall p_0 \in I_1, \ \exists q_0 \in I_2 : \pi(q_0) = p_0.$$

This ensures that $C_2$ can start computations representing all computations of $C_1$.

#### (b) Step simulation

For all $(p,q) \in R$, if $p$ is nonterminal in $C_1$ (that is, $f_1(p)$ is defined), then there exists a **finite sequence of steps** of $C_2$ taking $q$ to a state $q' \in Q_2$ such that $(f_1(p), q') \in R$. Formally, there exists $k \ge 1$ such that

$$\pi(f_2^k(q)) = f_1(p),$$

and all intermediate states $f_2^j(q)$ for $0 < j < k$ are allowed to contain additional information.

This condition must hold **for all reachable $(p,q) \in R$**, ensuring the simulation invariant is preserved.

#### (c) Terminal states

If $p$ is terminal in $C_1$ (i.e., $f_1(p)$ is undefined), then for any $(p,q) \in R$, the computation of $C_2$ **may continue internally** but must remain in states representing $p$:

$$\pi(f_2^j(q)) = p \quad \text{for all } j \ge 0 \text{ for which } f_2^j(q) \text{ is defined}.$$

This formalizes that $C_2$ cannot simulate a further step of $C_1$ once $p$ has halted, while allowing auxiliary internal computation that does not change the abstract state.

#### (d) Output agreement

For all $(p,q) \in R$, the outputs of $C_2$ must correspond to those of $C_1$ under the representation map. More precisely, let $\Omega_i(p)$ denote the set of outputs observable in state $p$ of $C_i$:

$$\Omega_2(q) = \Omega_1(\pi(q)) = \Omega_1(p).$$

This guarantees that the observable behavior of $C_2$ matches that of $C_1$ whenever it represents an abstract state.

### 4. Reachable computation sequences

Let $p_0, p_1, \dots$ be any computation sequence of $C_1$, with $p_0 \in I_1$ and $p_{i+1} = f_1(p_i)$ for nonterminal $p_i$. Then there exists a sequence $q_0, q_1, \dots$ in $Q_2$ such that:

1. $q_0 \in I_2$ with $(p_0, q_0) \in R$.
2. For each $i \ge 0$, if $p_i$ is nonterminal, there exists $k_i \ge 1$ such that

$$q_{i+1} = f_2^{k_i}(q_i), \quad (p_{i+1}, q_{i+1}) \in R.$$

1. If $p_N$ is terminal, then $\pi(f_2^j(q_N)) = p_N$ for all $j \ge 0$ for which the iterates are defined.

Thus **every computation of $C_1$ is reproduced** by a computation of $C_2**, possibly with extra internal steps and additional state information.

### 5. Summary

A computational method $C_2$ **simulates** or **represents** $C_1$ if there exists a representation map $\pi: Q_2 \to Q_1$ such that:

1. Every initial state of $C_1$ is represented by some initial state of $C_2$.
2. The simulation relation $(p,q) \in R$ is preserved under each $C_1$ step by a finite number of $C_2$ steps.
3. Terminal states of $C_1$ are represented indefinitely by $C_2$.
4. Outputs agree under the representation map for all represented states.

This gives a **set-theoretic, rigorous definition** of the statement ŌĆ£$C_2$ simulates $C_1$ŌĆØ in terms of a representation map and the preservation of a simulation relation along all reachable states.
