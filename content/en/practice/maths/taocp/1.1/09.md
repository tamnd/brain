**Exercise 9.** &#9654; [*M30*] Suppose that $C_1 = (Q_1, I_1, \Omega_1, f_1)$ and $C_2 = (Q_2, I_2, \Omega_2, f_2)$ are computational methods. For example, $C_1$ might stand for Algorithm E as in Eqs. (2), except that $m$ and $n$ are restricted in magnitude, and $C_2$ might stand for a computer program implementation of Algorithm E. (Thus $Q_2$ might be the set of all states of the machine, i.e., all possible configurations of its memory and registers; $f_2$ might be the definition of single machine actions; and $I_2$ might be the set of initial states, each including the program that determines the greatest common divisor as well as the particular values of $m$ and $n$.)

Formulate a set-theoretic definition for the concept “$C_2$ is a representation of $C_1$” or “$C_2$ simulates $C_1$.” This is to mean intuitively that any computation sequence of $C_1$ is mimicked by $C_2$, except that $C_2$ might take more steps in which to do the computation and it might retain more information in its states. (We thereby obtain a rigorous interpretation of the statement, “Program $X$ is an implementation of Algorithm $Y$.”)

## Setup

Let

$$C_1=(Q_1,I_1,\Omega_1,f_1), \qquad
C_2=(Q_2,I_2,\Omega_2,f_2)$$

be computational methods in the sense of Eqs. (3). A computation sequence in $C_i$ is a sequence

$$q_0,q_1,q_2,\ldots$$

such that $q_0\in I_i$ and

$$q_{t+1}=f_i(q_t)$$

for all $t\ge0$, until either termination occurs or the sequence continues indefinitely.

The problem asks for a set-theoretic definition expressing that $C_2$ simulates $C_1$. Intuitively, every computation of $C_1$ is to be reproduced by $C_2$, although several steps of $C_2$ may correspond to one step of $C_1$, and the states of $C_2$ may contain additional information.

A definition must therefore specify:

1. how states of $C_2$ correspond to states of $C_1$;
2. how initial states correspond;
3. how finite stretches of computation in $C_2$ represent single steps of $C_1$;
4. how outputs are preserved.

## Solution

Define a mapping

$$\pi:Q_2\to Q_1$$

called the projection map. The intended meaning is that $\pi(q)$ extracts from a detailed state of $C_2$ the abstract state represented in $C_1$.

We say that $C_2$ is a representation of $C_1$ if there exists such a mapping $\pi$ satisfying the following conditions.

First, every initial state of $C_1$ is represented by at least one initial state of $C_2$:

$$\forall q_1\in I_1,\ \exists q_2\in I_2
\text{ such that }
\pi(q_2)=q_1.
\tag{1}$$

Second, outputs are preserved:

$$\forall q\in Q_2,\qquad
\Omega_2(q)=\Omega_1(\pi(q)).
\tag{2}$$

Third, every step of $C_1$ is simulated by a finite nonempty sequence of steps of $C_2$. Formally, for every $q\in Q_2$, if

$$\pi(q)=p,$$

and if

$$f_1(p)$$

is defined, then there exists an integer $k=k(q)\ge1$ such that

$$\pi\bigl(f_2^{\,k}(q)\bigr)=f_1(p),
\tag{3}$$

and moreover,

$$\pi\bigl(f_2^{\,j}(q)\bigr)=p
\qquad (0\le j<k).
\tag{4}$$

Condition (4) states that the intermediate states of $C_2$ occurring during the simulation of a single step of $C_1$ still represent the original state $p$. Only after the $k$th step does the represented state advance to $f_1(p)$.

Under these conditions, every computation sequence

$$p_0,p_1,p_2,\ldots$$

of $C_1$

is mimicked by a computation sequence

$$q_0,q_1,q_2,\ldots$$

of $C_2$

such that

$$\pi(q_{t_i})=p_i$$

for a strictly increasing sequence of integers

$$0=t_0<t_1<t_2<\cdots.$$

The interval

$$t_{i+1}-t_i$$

is the number of machine steps used by $C_2$ to simulate the single step

$$p_i\mapsto p_{i+1}.$$

This definition formalizes the statement that $C_2$ performs the same computation as $C_1$, possibly with additional internal information and with a greater number of elementary actions.

## Verification

Assume that $p_0,p_1,p_2,\ldots$ is a computation sequence of $C_1$.

By (1), there exists $q_0\in I_2$ such that

$$\pi(q_0)=p_0.$$

Suppose inductively that integers

$$0=t_0<t_1<\cdots<t_i$$

and states

$$q_{t_0},q_{t_1},\ldots,q_{t_i}$$

have been constructed so that

$$\pi(q_{t_i})=p_i.$$

Since

$$p_{i+1}=f_1(p_i),$$

condition (3) yields an integer $k\ge1$ such that

$$\pi\bigl(f_2^{\,k}(q_{t_i})\bigr)=p_{i+1}.$$

Set

$$t_{i+1}=t_i+k,
\qquad
q_{t_{i+1}}=f_2^{\,k}(q_{t_i}).$$

Then

$$\pi(q_{t_{i+1}})=p_{i+1}.$$

By induction, the entire computation of $C_1$ is reproduced inside the computation of $C_2$.

Equation (2) guarantees that corresponding states produce identical outputs:

$$\Omega_2(q_{t_i})
=
\Omega_1(p_i).$$

Hence the simulated computation yields exactly the same observable results.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Notes

The definition above is essentially a homomorphism condition between transition systems, except that a single transition of $C_1$ may correspond to several transitions of $C_2$.

If every simulation length satisfies

$$k(q)=1,$$

then the simulation is step-for-step:

$$\pi(f_2(q))=f_1(\pi(q)).$$

This special case corresponds to a direct implementation with no hidden intermediate states.
