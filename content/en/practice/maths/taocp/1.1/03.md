**Exercise 3.** [*20*] Change Algorithm E (for the sake of efficiency) so that all trivial replacement operations such as “$m \leftarrow n$” are avoided. Write this new algorithm in the style of Algorithm E, and call it Algorithm F.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 37s  

---

The inefficiency in Algorithm E arises from the replacement operations in step E3. At the end of each pass through the algorithm, step E3 performs

$$m \leftarrow n,\qquad n \leftarrow r.$$

The first replacement is frequently trivial. By Exercise 2, at the beginning of every execution of step E1, except possibly the first, we have $m>n$. Hence after the first execution of E1, the quotient is always at least $1$, and therefore the remainder satisfies

$$0 \le r < n < m.$$

Since the next pass requires the pair $(n,r)$, there is no need to retain the old value of $m$. The variable $m$ can therefore be eliminated after the first division, and the current divisor can simply be replaced by the remainder.

The only exceptional case occurs at the beginning, when $m<n$ is possible. In that case the first division produces quotient $0$ and remainder $m$, so the replacements

$$m \leftarrow n,\qquad n \leftarrow r$$

merely interchange $m$ and $n$. This interchange can be handled explicitly once at the beginning.

The revised algorithm is therefore as follows.

**Algorithm F** (_Euclid’s algorithm, avoiding trivial replacements_). Given two positive integers $m$ and $n$, find their greatest common divisor.

**F1.** [Ensure $m \ge n$.] If $m<n$, exchange $m \leftrightarrow n$.

**F2.** [Find remainder.] Divide $m$ by $n$ and let $r$ be the remainder. (We will have $0\le r<n$.)

**F3.** [Is it zero?] If $r=0$, the algorithm terminates; $n$ is the answer.

**F4.** [Reduce.] Set $m \leftarrow r$.

**F5.** [Find remainder.] Divide $n$ by $m$ and let $r$ be the remainder. (We will have $0\le r<m$.)

**F6.** [Is it zero?] If $r=0$, the algorithm terminates; $m$ is the answer.

**F7.** [Reduce.] Set $n \leftarrow r$, and go back to step F2. ▎

This algorithm is equivalent to Algorithm E. After step F1, the inequality $m\ge n$ holds. Steps F2-F4 transform $(m,n)$ into $(r,n)$, where $r<m$. Steps F5-F7 then transform $(r,n)$ into $(r,r')$, where $r'<r$. Thus the same sequence of remainder computations occurs as in Algorithm E, but the trivial replacement operation $m \leftarrow n$ is avoided at every cycle after the initial interchange. The algorithm still terminates with the greatest common divisor, since each division step is identical to the corresponding step of Algorithm E and the divisor at the terminating step is the greatest common divisor by the argument in Section 1.1.
