## Solution

The “Procedure for Reading This Set of Books” presented after the preface fails to satisfy the definition of an algorithm in at least three of the five essential features outlined in Section 1.1. First, an algorithm must be _finite_, consisting of a well-defined sequence of steps that eventually terminates. The reading procedure, however, allows for indefinite behavior: it instructs the reader to “consult whatever sections seem relevant” and to “re-read portions as needed,” which may result in an unbounded number of repetitions. Thus, the procedure violates finiteness.

Second, an algorithm must have _definiteness_: each step must be precisely stated so that it can be performed without ambiguity. The reading procedure contains vague instructions such as “study the material carefully” and “follow the exercises,” leaving the execution dependent on the reader’s judgment. This absence of explicit operations means the procedure does not satisfy definiteness.

Third, an algorithm must be _effective_: all steps must be basic enough to be carried out exactly by a person or machine using only pencil and paper (or other permitted operations). Many steps of the reading procedure, for instance “try to understand the ideas intuitively” or “develop your own examples,” are not mechanical or precisely executable. Hence the procedure is ineffective in the algorithmic sense.

The remaining two features, _input_ and _output_, are partially met. The input could be considered the text itself, and the intended output is the reader’s comprehension. These aspects are only informally specified, and the output is not quantitatively defined, unlike Algorithm E, which produces the greatest common divisor as a single positive integer.

In addition to the deficiencies in essential features, the procedure differs in format from Algorithm E. Algorithm E presents numbered steps with short bracketed summaries, uses symbolic assignments and decisions, and terminates explicitly with a computed result. By contrast, the reading procedure is written in prose without step numbers, contains conditional suggestions rather than explicit test-and-branch operations, and has no clearly defined termination condition. Algorithm E employs the replacement operation $\leftarrow$ to update variables, whereas the reading procedure has no analogous computational structure. These differences illustrate the contrast between a genuine algorithm and informal procedural advice.
