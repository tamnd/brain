**Exercise 5.** &#9654; [*12*] Show that the “Procedure for Reading This Set of Books” that appears after the preface actually fails to be a genuine algorithm on at least three of our five counts! Also mention some differences in format between it and Algorithm E.

## Solution

An algorithm must possess the five properties stated in Section 1.1: finiteness, definiteness, input, output, and effectiveness. The “Procedure for Reading This Set of Books” fails on at least three counts.

The procedure fails the requirement of finiteness. No finite terminating condition is specified. A reader may continue rereading sections, consulting references, or returning to earlier chapters indefinitely. Algorithm E terminates explicitly at step E2 when $r = 0$; the reading procedure contains no corresponding termination rule.

The procedure fails the requirement of definiteness. Several instructions are stated informally and admit multiple interpretations. Directions such as reading “carefully,” studying “until understood,” or selecting portions according to personal interest do not determine uniquely what action is to be performed. In Algorithm E, each action is completely specified. For example, step E3 prescribes the exact replacements

$$m \leftarrow n,\qquad n \leftarrow r.$$

No ambiguity is present there.

The procedure fails the requirement of effectiveness. An effective operation must be sufficiently elementary that it can in principle be carried out exactly and in finite time. Instructions involving judgment, taste, or subjective comprehension are not elementary operations in this sense. An instruction such as “understand the material thoroughly” cannot be reduced to a finite sequence of mechanically executable acts.

The procedure also fails to specify output precisely. Algorithm E terminates with a uniquely defined result, namely the greatest common divisor of $m$ and $n$. The reading procedure does not define a specific final product. Different readers may finish with different levels of comprehension, different selections of material completed, or no identifiable final state at all.

Several differences in format distinguish the reading procedure from Algorithm E. Algorithm E is divided into explicitly numbered steps E1, E2, E3, each with a bracketed descriptive title. The reading procedure does not use systematic step labels of this kind. Algorithm E uses formal replacement notation such as

$$m \leftarrow n,$$

together with explicit conditional tests such as

$$\text{If } r = 0.$$

The reading procedure is written in ordinary expository prose rather than in precise operational notation. Algorithm E also specifies control flow explicitly by commands such as “go back to step E1,” whereas the reading procedure does not present a formally defined sequence of execution.

Therefore the “Procedure for Reading This Set of Books” is not a genuine algorithm according to at least three of the five criteria stated in Section 1.1.

$$\boxed{\text{The procedure fails finiteness, definiteness, and effectiveness; it also lacks a precise output specification.}}$$
