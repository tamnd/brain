**Exercise 7.** [*23*] Formulate and prove by induction a rule for the sums $1^2$, $2^2 - 1^2$, $3^2 - 2^2 + 1^2$, $4^2 - 3^2 + 2^2 - 1^2$, $5^2 - 4^2 + 3^2 - 2^2 + 1^2$, etc.

We consider the sequence of sums

$$
S_1 = 1^2, \quad S_2 = 2^2 - 1^2, \quad S_3 = 3^2 - 2^2 + 1^2, \quad S_4 = 4^2 - 3^2 + 2^2 - 1^2, \quad S_5 = 5^2 - 4^2 + 3^2 - 2^2 + 1^2, \ldots
$$

and aim to formulate a general rule and prove it by induction.

We first examine the pattern of signs. For a positive integer $n$, define

$$
S_n = n^2 - (n-1)^2 + (n-2)^2 - (n-3)^2 + \cdots + (-1)^{n-1} \cdot 1^2.
$$

We separate the cases of $n$ even and $n$ odd.

**Case 1: $n$ is odd.** Let $n = 2k + 1$ for some integer $k \ge 0$. Then

$$
S_{2k+1} = (2k+1)^2 - (2k)^2 + (2k-1)^2 - \cdots + 1^2.
$$

Observe that consecutive pairs can be grouped as

$$
(2k+1)^2 - (2k)^2 = 4k + 1, \quad (2k-1)^2 - (2k-2)^2 = 4k - 3, \quad \dots
$$

continuing down to the last term $1^2$. The sum of these differences yields

$$
S_{2k+1} = (k+1)^2.
$$

**Case 2: $n$ is even.** Let $n = 2k$ for some integer $k \ge 1$. Then

$$
S_{2k} = (2k)^2 - (2k-1)^2 + (2k-2)^2 - \cdots + (-1)^{2k-1} \cdot 1^2.
$$

Similarly, grouping consecutive pairs $(2j)^2 - (2j-1)^2 = 4j - 1$ for $j = k, k-1, \ldots, 1$, we obtain

$$
S_{2k} = k(2k) = k(2k) = k(2k) \text{?}
$$

We compute explicitly:

$$
S_2 = 2^2 - 1^2 = 3, \quad S_4 = 4^2 - 3^2 + 2^2 - 1^2 = 16 - 9 + 4 - 1 = 10.
$$

We notice that $S_2 = 1 \cdot 3$, $S_4 = 2 \cdot 5$. More generally, for $n = 2k$, we claim

$$
S_{2k} = k(2k+1).
$$

We verify with $k = 1$: $1 \cdot 3 = 3$; $k = 2$: $2 \cdot 5 = 10$, consistent with our computations.

Thus the rule can be formulated as

$$
S_n = 
\begin{cases} 
k(2k+1), & n = 2k \text{ even},\\[2mm]
(k+1)^2, & n = 2k+1 \text{ odd}.
\end{cases}
$$

We prove this formula by induction on $n$.

**Base cases.** For $n = 1$, $S_1 = 1^2 = 1$, and $k = 0$ gives $(k+1)^2 = 1^2 = 1$, so the formula holds. For $n = 2$, $S_2 = 2^2 - 1^2 = 3$ and $k = 1$ gives $k(2k+1) = 1 \cdot 3 = 3$, so the formula holds.

**Induction step.** Suppose the formula holds for $S_n$, we prove it for $S_{n+1}$.

**Case A: $n = 2k$ even.** Then

$$
S_n = k(2k+1), \quad S_{n+1} = S_{2k+1} = S_{2k} + (2k+1)^2.
$$

By the induction hypothesis, $S_{2k} = k(2k+1)$. Hence

$$
S_{2k+1} = k(2k+1) + (2k+1)^2 = (2k+1)(k + 2k +1) = (2k+1)(k+1 + k) ?
$$

Compute precisely:

$$
k(2k+1) + (2k+1)^2 = (2k+1)(k + 2k+1) = (2k+1)(3k +1).
$$

This does not match $(k+1)^2$, so we need to check. Perhaps a simpler approach is to consider the alternating sum as a recursion:

$$
S_{n+1} = (n+1)^2 - S_n.
$$

Indeed, the sequence satisfies the recurrence

$$
S_1 = 1, \quad S_{n+1} = (n+1)^2 - S_n.
$$

We prove by induction using this recurrence.

**Base case:** $S_1 = 1$ satisfies the formula.

**Induction hypothesis:** Assume $S_n$ satisfies the formula:

$$
S_n = 
\begin{cases} 
k(2k+1), & n = 2k,\\
(k+1)^2, & n = 2k+1.
\end{cases}
$$

**Inductive step:** Compute $S_{n+1} = (n+1)^2 - S_n$.

- If $n = 2k$ even, then $S_n = k(2k+1)$. Then $n+1 = 2k+1$ odd, and

$$
S_{2k+1} = (2k+1)^2 - k(2k+1) = (2k+1)(2k+1 - k) = (2k+1)(k+1) = (k+1)^2,
$$

since $2k+1 = 1 + 2k$? Wait compute: $(2k+1)^2 - k(2k+1) = (2k+1)(2k+1 - k) = (2k+1)(k+1)$, which matches $(k+1)(2k+1)$, consistent with the odd formula $S_{2k+1} = (k+1)^2$? Not yet, we need $S_{2k+1} = (k+1)^2$, check: $(2k+1)(k+1) = 2k^2 + 3k +1$, and $(k+1)^2 = k^2 + 2k +1$, so discrepancy.

$$
