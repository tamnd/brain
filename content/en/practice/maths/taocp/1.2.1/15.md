**Exercise 15.** &#9654; [*HM28*] (*Generalized induction*.) The text shows how to prove statements $P(n)$ that depend on a single integer $n$, but it does not describe how to prove statements $P(m, n)$ depending on two integers. In these circumstances a proof is often given by some sort of “double induction,” which frequently seems confusing. Actually, there is an important principle more general than simple induction that applies not only to this case but also to situations in which statements are to be proved about uncountable sets, for example, $P(x)$ for all real $x$. This general principle is called *well-ordering*.

Let “$\prec$” be a relation on a set $S$, satisfying the following properties:

i) Given $x$, $y$, and $z$ in $S$, if $x \prec y$ and $y \prec z$, then $x \prec z$.
ii) Given $x$ and $y$ in $S$, exactly one of the following three possibilities is true: $x \prec y$, $x = y$, or $y \prec x$.
iii) If $A$ is any nonempty subset of $S$, there is an element $x$ in $A$ with $x \preceq y$ (that is, $x \prec y$ or $x = y$) for all $y$ in $A$.

This relation is said to be a well-ordering of $S$. For example, it is clear that the positive integers are well-ordered by the ordinary “less than” relation, $<$.

a) Show that the set of *all* integers is not well-ordered by $<$.
b) Define a well-ordering relation on the set of all integers.
c) Is the set of all nonnegative real numbers well-ordered by $<$?
d) (*Lexicographic order*.) Let $S$ be well-ordered by $\prec$, and for $n > 0$ let $T_n$ be the set of all $n$-tuples $(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n)$ of elements $x_j$ in $S$. Define $(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n) \prec (y_1, y_2, \ldots, y_n)$ if there is some $k$, $1 \le k \le n$, such that $x_j = y_j$ for $1 \le j < k$, but $x_k \prec y_k$ in $S$. Is $\prec$ a well-ordering of $T_n$?
e) Continuing part (d), let $T = \bigcup_{n \ge 1} T_n$; define $(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_m) \prec (y_1, y_2, \ldots, y_n)$ if $x_j = y_j$ for $1 \le j < k$ and $x_k \prec y_k$, for some $k \le \min(m, n)$, or if $m < n$ and $x_j = y_j$ for $1 \le j \le m$. Is $\prec$ a well-ordering of $T$?
f) Show that $\prec$ is a well-ordering of $S$ if and only if it satisfies (i) and (ii) above and there is no infinite sequence $x_1, x_2, x_3, \ldots$ with $x_{j+1} \prec x_j$ for all $j \ge 1$.
g) Let $S$ be well-ordered by $\prec$, and let $P(x)$ be a statement about the element $x$ of $S$. Show that if $P(x)$ can be proved under the assumption that $P(y)$ is true for all $y \prec x$, then $P(x)$ is true for *all* $x$ in $S$.

## Setup

Let $\prec$ be a relation on a set $S$ satisfying conditions (i), (ii), and (iii) of the exercise. A relation with these properties is called a well-ordering.

Parts (a)-(g) ask for proofs concerning well-ordered sets and generalized induction.

## Solution

### (a)

The set of all integers is not well-ordered by the ordinary relation $<$.

Consider the subset

$$
A=\{\ldots,-3,-2,-1\}
$$

of the integers. If $A$ had a least element, say $m$, then $m-1$ would also belong to $A$ and satisfy

$$
m-1<m,
$$

contradicting the minimality of $m$. Hence $A$ has no least element, so property (iii) fails. Therefore the integers are not well-ordered by $<$. ∎

### (b)

Define a relation $\prec$ on the integers by

$$
0 \prec 1 \prec -1 \prec 2 \prec -2 \prec 3 \prec -3 \prec \cdots.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
x \prec y
$$

if either

$$
|x|<|y|,
$$

or

$$
|x|=|y| \quad\text{and}\quad x>y.
$$

Properties (i) and (ii) are immediate from the definition. It remains to verify (iii).

Let $A$ be a nonempty set of integers. The set

$$
\{|x|:x\in A\}
$$

is a nonempty set of nonnegative integers, hence possesses a least element $m$. If $m=0$, then $0\in A$, and $0$ is least in $A$ with respect to $\prec$.

If $m>0$, then either $m\in A$ or $-m\in A$. If $m\in A$, then $m\prec -m$, and every other element of $A$ has absolute value at least $m$. Hence $m$ is least in $A$. If $m\notin A$, then necessarily $-m\in A$, and $-m$ is least in $A$.

Thus every nonempty subset has a least element, so $\prec$ is a well-ordering. ∎

### (c)

The set of all nonnegative real numbers is not well-ordered by $<$.

Consider the subset

$$
A=\{x\in\mathbb R:x>0\}.
$$

Suppose $A$ had a least element $a$. Then

$$
\frac a2>0,
$$

so $a/2\in A$, and

$$
\frac a2<a,
$$

contradicting the minimality of $a$. Hence $A$ has no least element. Therefore property (iii) fails. ∎

### (d)

Let $S$ be well-ordered by $\prec$, and let $T_n$ be the set of all $n$-tuples of elements of $S$, ordered lexicographically.

The relation is transitive, since if

$$
(x_1,\ldots,x_n)\prec(y_1,\ldots,y_n)
$$

and

$$
(y_1,\ldots,y_n)\prec(z_1,\ldots,z_n),
$$

then at the first coordinate where the $x$-tuple and $z$-tuple differ, the corresponding inequality in $S$ holds by transitivity in $S$.

Exactly one of the three possibilities

$$
x\prec y,\qquad x=y,\qquad y\prec x
$$

holds for any two tuples, because at the first coordinate where they differ, property (ii) in $S$ determines the comparison.

It remains to prove property (iii). Let $A\subseteq T_n$ be nonempty. Define

$$
A_1=\{x_1:(x_1,\ldots,x_n)\in A\}.
$$

Since $A_1$ is nonempty and $S$ is well-ordered, there exists a least element $a_1$ of $A_1$.

Among all tuples in $A$ whose first coordinate is $a_1$, define

$$
A_2=\{x_2:(a_1,x_2,\ldots,x_n)\in A\}.
$$

Again $A_2$ is nonempty, so it has a least element $a_2$.

Continuing in this way, after $n$ steps we obtain a tuple

$$
(a_1,a_2,\ldots,a_n)\in A.
$$

For every tuple $(x_1,\ldots,x_n)\in A$, either $x_1\ne a_1$, in which case $a_1\prec x_1$, or else $x_1=a_1$ and $x_2\ne a_2$, in which case $a_2\prec x_2$, and so on. Hence

$$
(a_1,\ldots,a_n)\preceq(x_1,\ldots,x_n).
$$

Therefore every nonempty subset of $T_n$ has a least element, and lexicographic order is a well-ordering of $T_n$. ∎

### (e)

Let

$$
T=\bigcup_{n\ge1}T_n,
$$

with the order defined in the statement.

This relation is not a well-ordering.

Let $a\in S$. Consider the infinite sequence

$$
(a)\succ(a,a)\succ(a,a,a)\succ\cdots.
$$

Indeed, according to the definition, if one tuple is a proper initial segment of another, then the shorter tuple precedes the longer. Hence

$$
(a,a,\ldots,a)\prec(a,a,\ldots,a,a).
$$

Now consider the subset

$$
A=\{(a),(a,a),(a,a,a),\ldots\}.
$$

Given any element of $A$, a shorter one also belongs to $A$ and precedes it. Hence $A$ has no least element.

Therefore $\prec$ is not a well-ordering of $T$. ∎

### (f)

Assume first that $\prec$ is a well-ordering. Suppose there existed an infinite sequence

$$
x_1,x_2,x_3,\ldots
$$

such that

$$
x_{j+1}\prec x_j
$$

for all $j\ge1$.

Let

$$
A=\{x_1,x_2,x_3,\ldots\}.
$$

Since $A$ is nonempty, it has a least element $x_k$. But then

$$
x_{k+1}\prec x_k,
$$

contradicting the minimality of $x_k$. Hence no such infinite descending sequence exists.

Conversely, suppose $\prec$ satisfies (i) and (ii), and suppose there is no infinite descending sequence. Assume, for contradiction, that $\prec$ is not a well-ordering. Then some nonempty subset $A\subseteq S$ has no least element.

Choose $x_1\in A$. Since $x_1$ is not least in $A$, there exists $x_2\in A$ such that

$$
x_2\prec x_1.
$$

Since $x_2$ is not least in $A$, there exists $x_3\in A$ such that

$$
x_3\prec x_2.
$$

Continuing inductively, we obtain an infinite descending sequence

$$
x_1\succ x_2\succ x_3\succ\cdots,
$$

contrary to hypothesis.

Therefore every nonempty subset has a least element, and $\prec$ is a well-ordering. ∎

### (g)

Assume that for every $x\in S$, the truth of all statements

$$
P(y),\qquad y\prec x,
$$

implies the truth of $P(x)$.

Suppose, for contradiction, that $P(x)$ is false for some $x\in S$. Let

$$
A=\{x\in S:P(x)\text{ is false}\}.
$$

Then $A$ is nonempty. Since $S$ is well-ordered, $A$ has a least element $a$.

Because $a$ is least in $A$, every element $y\prec a$ satisfies $P(y)$. By hypothesis, this implies that $P(a)$ is true. But $a\in A$ means that $P(a)$ is false. This contradiction shows that $A$ must be empty.

Therefore $P(x)$ is true for all $x\in S$. This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification

In part (d), the construction of the least tuple proceeds coordinate by coordinate. At stage $k$, the first $k-1$ coordinates have already been fixed minimally, and the $k$th coordinate is chosen minimally among tuples compatible with those earlier choices. The resulting tuple precedes every other tuple in the subset at the first coordinate where they differ.

In part (e), the ordering places every proper initial segment before the longer tuple extending it. Consequently,

$$
(a)\prec(a,a)\prec(a,a,a)\prec\cdots,
$$

so the displayed subset has no least element.

In part (g), the contradiction argument uses property (iii) exactly once, to select the least counterexample. The assumption about all predecessors of $a$ then forces $P(a)$ itself.

## Notes

Part (g) is the general form of induction on a well-ordered set. Ordinary mathematical induction corresponds to the well-ordered set of positive integers under $<$. Lexicographic order from part (d) yields induction principles for statements involving several integer variables.
