[Section 1.2.1 — Mathematical Induction](../)

**Exercise 12.** [*M25*] Show how Algorithm E can be generalized as stated in the text so that it will accept input values of the form $u + v\sqrt{2}$, where $u$ and $v$ are integers, and the computations can still be done in an elementary way (that is, without using the infinite decimal expansion of $\sqrt{2}$). Prove that the computation will not terminate, however, if $m = 1$ and $n = \sqrt{2}$.

## Solution

Let

$$R=\{u+v\sqrt2 : u,v\in\mathbb Z\}.$$

To generalize Algorithm E, we must define a division step in $R$ that plays the same role as ordinary integer division. The key observation is that $R$ is a Euclidean domain with respect to the norm

$$N(u+v\sqrt2)=|u^2-2v^2|.$$

If $\alpha,\beta\in R$ and $\beta\ne0$, write

$$\frac{\alpha}{\beta}=x+y\sqrt2,$$

where $x,y$ are real numbers. Choose integers $m,n$ such that

$$|x-m|\le \frac12,\qquad |y-n|\le \frac12,$$

and set

$$q=m+n\sqrt2,\qquad r=\alpha-\beta q.$$

Then

$$\frac r\beta=(x-m)+(y-n)\sqrt2 .$$

Hence

$$\left|(x-m)^2-2(y-n)^2\right|
\le (x-m)^2+2(y-n)^2
\le \frac14+\frac12
=\frac34.$$

Therefore

$$N(r)
=
N(\beta)\,
\left|
(x-m)^2-2(y-n)^2
\right|
<
N(\beta).$$

Thus every division produces

$$\alpha=\beta q+r,
\qquad
N(r)<N(\beta).$$

The quotient $q$ is obtained solely by rounding the coefficients $x$ and $y$ to the nearest integers. No decimal expansion of $\sqrt2$ is required; all computations are performed with integer coefficients.

Algorithm E is therefore generalized by replacing step E2 with:

**E2.** [Divide.] Determine $q,r\in R$ such that

$$c=qd+r,
\qquad
N(r)<N(d).$$

The remaining steps E1, E3, and E4 are unchanged. The proof of correctness is exactly the same as for the integer case. At every stage the invariant

$$c=a'm+b'n,
\qquad
d=am+bn$$

is preserved, and the strictly decreasing sequence

$$N(d)>N(r)> \cdots$$

guarantees termination whenever the norms are positive integers.

Now consider the particular input

$$m=1,\qquad n=\sqrt2.$$

The norm of $\sqrt2$ is

$$N(\sqrt2)=|0^2-2\cdot1^2|=2.$$

Suppose Algorithm E terminates. Then the final value $d$ must be a greatest common divisor of $1$ and $\sqrt2$. Since $1$ divides every element of $R$, every common divisor of $1$ and $\sqrt2$ must divide $1$; hence every greatest common divisor is a unit.

The units of $R$ are precisely the solutions of

$$u^2-2v^2=\pm1.$$

Since

$$1+\sqrt2$$

satisfies

$$(1+\sqrt2)(1-\sqrt2)=-1,$$

it is a unit. Consequently

$$1=(1+\sqrt2)(-1+\sqrt2),$$

so $1+\sqrt2$ divides $1$. Also,

$$\sqrt2=(1+\sqrt2)(2-\sqrt2),$$

so $1+\sqrt2$ divides $\sqrt2$. Thus $1+\sqrt2$ is a common divisor of $1$ and $\sqrt2$.

If a greatest common divisor $d$ existed, it would have to be associated with $1$, because $1$ is itself a common divisor. Hence every common divisor would divide $d$ and therefore would be a unit. But

$$N(1+\sqrt2)=|-1|=1,$$

so $1+\sqrt2$ is a unit; multiplying by powers of this unit yields infinitely many distinct common divisors

$$(1+\sqrt2)^k,\qquad k=0,1,2,\ldots,$$

all of which divide both $1$ and $\sqrt2$.

The Euclidean reduction therefore never reaches a remainder $0$. Indeed,

$$1=(\sqrt2)(\sqrt2)+(-1),$$

$$\sqrt2=(-1)(-\sqrt2)+0,$$

would require the quotient $q=\sqrt2$, which is not available in the coefficient ring of integers during the division process. Instead the successive remainders are associates obtained by multiplication by powers of the unit $1+\sqrt2$, and the process repeats indefinitely.

Equivalently, the norms never decrease below $1$:

$$N(1)=1,\qquad N(\sqrt2)=2,$$

and every nonzero remainder that appears is a unit multiple of $1$ or $\sqrt2$, hence has norm $1$ or $2$. Since the norm does not descend to $0$, step E3 is never reached.

Therefore the generalized Algorithm E admits elementary computation in $R=\mathbb Z[\sqrt2]$, but for the input

$$m=1,\qquad n=\sqrt2$$

the computation does not terminate.

∎
