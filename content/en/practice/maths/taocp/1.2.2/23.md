**Exercise 23.** [*M25*] Give a geometric proof that $\ln xy = \ln x + \ln y$, based on Fig. 6.

## Solution

Consider the geometric interpretation of the natural logarithm as the area under the hyperbola $y = 1/t$ from $t = 1$ to $t = x$, shown in Fig. 6. By definition, for any positive real number $z$,

$\ln z = \int_1^z \frac{dt}{t}.$

Let $x$ and $y$ be positive real numbers. We wish to show geometrically that

$\ln(xy) = \ln x + \ln y.$

Construct a rectangle in the $t$-$u$ plane with vertices $(1,1)$, $(x,1)$, $(1,y)$, and $(x,y)$. Consider the area under the curve $u = 1/t$ from $t = 1$ to $t = xy$. We can decompose this integral using a substitution that corresponds to a geometric transformation.

Set $t = x \cdot s$, so that $s = t/x$. Then $dt = x , ds$, and when $t = 1$, $s = 1/x$, and when $t = xy$, $s = y$. The integral becomes

$$\begin{aligned}
\ln(xy) &= \int_1^{xy} \frac{dt}{t} = \int_1^{xy} \frac{x \, ds}{x s} = \int_{1/x}^{y} \frac{ds}{s} \\
&= \int_{1/x}^{1} \frac{ds}{s} + \int_1^y \frac{ds}{s}.
\end{aligned}$$

The first integral satisfies

$\int_{1/x}^{1} \frac{ds}{s} = -\int_1^{1/x} \frac{ds}{s} = \int_1^x \frac{dt}{t} = \ln x,$

by the substitution $t = 1/s$. The second integral is $\int_1^y ds/s = \ln y$. Therefore,

$\ln(xy) = \ln x + \ln y.$

Geometrically, this corresponds to stretching the area under $y = 1/t$ horizontally by a factor of $x$ to cover the interval $[1, xy]$, which decomposes into the original area under $[1, x]$ plus the scaled area under $[1, y]$. The decomposition of the integral aligns exactly with the addition of the areas representing $\ln x$ and $\ln y$, as illustrated in Fig. 6.

This completes the proof.
