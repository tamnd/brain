**Exercise 9.** [*M23*] Given that $x$ and $y$ are rational, prove the laws of exponents under the assumption that the laws hold when $x$ and $y$ are integers.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 40s  

---

## Solution

Let

$$
x=\frac{p}{q}, \qquad y=\frac{r}{s},
$$

where $p,r$ are integers and $q,s$ are positive integers. By Eq. (6),

$$
b^x=b^{p/q}=\sqrt[q]{b^p},
\qquad
b^y=b^{r/s}=\sqrt[s]{b^r}.
$$

We must prove that the laws of exponents (5),

$$
b^{x+y}=b^xb^y,
\qquad
(b^x)^y=b^{xy},
$$

remain valid for rational $x$ and $y$.

First consider $b^{x+y}=b^xb^y$. Since

$$
x+y=\frac{ps+rq}{qs},
$$

Eq. (6) gives

$$
b^{x+y}
=
b^{(ps+rq)/(qs)}
=
\sqrt[qs]{\,b^{ps+rq}\,}.
$$

Using the integer law of exponents,

$$
b^{ps+rq}=b^{ps}b^{rq}.
$$

Hence

$$
b^{x+y}
=
\sqrt[qs]{\,b^{ps}b^{rq}\,}.
$$

Now

$$
(b^xb^y)^{qs}
=
\left(\sqrt[q]{b^p}\right)^{qs}
\left(\sqrt[s]{b^r}\right)^{qs}.
$$

Since

$$
\left(\sqrt[q]{b^p}\right)^q=b^p,
\qquad
\left(\sqrt[s]{b^r}\right)^s=b^r,
$$

we obtain

$$
\left(\sqrt[q]{b^p}\right)^{qs}
=(b^p)^s=b^{ps},
$$

and similarly

$$
\left(\sqrt[s]{b^r}\right)^{qs}
=(b^r)^q=b^{rq}.
$$

Therefore

$$
(b^xb^y)^{qs}
=
b^{ps}b^{rq}
=
b^{ps+rq}.
$$

Also,

$$
\left(b^{x+y}\right)^{qs}
=
\left(\sqrt[qs]{b^{ps+rq}}\right)^{qs}
=
b^{ps+rq}.
$$

Both $b^{x+y}$ and $b^xb^y$ are positive real numbers whose $qs$th powers are equal. By the uniqueness of positive $qs$th roots proved in Exercise 8,

$$
b^{x+y}=b^xb^y.
$$

Next consider $(b^x)^y=b^{xy}$. Since

$$
y=\frac{r}{s},
$$

Eq. (6) gives

$$
(b^x)^y
=
\sqrt[s]{(b^x)^r}.
$$

Using the result already established,

$$
(b^x)^r
=
\underbrace{b^x b^x \cdots b^x}_{r\ \text{factors}}
=
b^{rx}
$$

when $r\ge0$, and the same identity for negative $r$ follows from the integer exponent laws. Hence

$$
(b^x)^y
=
\sqrt[s]{b^{rx}}.
$$

Since $x=p/q$,

$$
rx=\frac{rp}{q},
$$

so

$$
(b^x)^y
=
\sqrt[s]{\,b^{rp/q}\,}.
$$

Let

$$
A=(b^x)^y,
\qquad
B=b^{xy}=b^{pr/(qs)}.
$$

Then

$$
A^s=b^{rp/q}.
$$

Also,

$$
B^s
=
\left(b^{pr/(qs)}\right)^s.
$$

By Eq. (6),

$$
B=\sqrt[qs]{b^{pr}},
$$

therefore

$$
B^s
=
\sqrt[q]{b^{pr}}
=
b^{pr/q}
=
b^{rp/q}.
$$

Thus

$$
A^s=B^s.
$$

Since $A$ and $B$ are positive, uniqueness of positive $s$th roots yields

$$
A=B.
$$

Therefore

$$
(b^x)^y=b^{xy}.
$$

Both laws of exponents are valid whenever $x$ and $y$ are rational numbers.

This completes the proof.

∎
