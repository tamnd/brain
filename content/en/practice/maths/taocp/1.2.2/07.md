**Exercise 7.** [*M23*] Given that $x$ and $y$ are integers, prove the laws of exponents, starting from the definition given by Eq. (4).

## Solution

We are asked to prove the laws of exponents for integers, starting from the definition in Eq. (4):

$$
b^0 = 1, \qquad b^n = b^{n-1}b \text{ if } n > 0, \qquad b^n = b^{n+1}/b \text{ if } n < 0.
$$

The laws of exponents to be proved are

$$
b^{x+y} = b^x b^y, \qquad (b^x)^y = b^{xy},
$$

for all integers $x$ and $y$.

### Proof of $b^{x+y} = b^x b^y$

**Case 1: $x \ge 0$, $y \ge 0$.**

We proceed by induction on $y$.

**Base case:** $y = 0$. Then $b^{x+0} = b^x = b^x b^0$, since $b^0 = 1$.

**Inductive step:** Assume $b^{x+y} = b^x b^y$ holds for some $y \ge 0$. Then

$$
b^{x+(y+1)} = b^{(x+y)+1} = b^{x+y} b \quad \text{by Eq. (4)}.
$$

By the inductive hypothesis, $b^{x+y} = b^x b^y$, so

$$
b^{x+(y+1)} = (b^x b^y) b = b^x (b^y b) = b^x b^{y+1}.
$$

Thus the law holds for $y+1$. This completes the induction for nonnegative integers $y$.

**Case 2: $x \ge 0$, $y < 0$.**

Let $y = -m$ with $m > 0$. Then

$$
b^{x+y} = b^{x-m} = b^{(x-m)+1}/b \quad \text{if } x-m < 0 \text{ or by repeated application of Eq. (4)}.
$$

Alternatively, we can write

$$
b^x b^y = b^x b^{-m} = b^x \cdot \frac{1}{b^m}.
$$

We can reduce $b^{x-m}$ using Eq. (4) recursively to the same expression $b^x / b^m$. Hence $b^{x+y} = b^x b^y$.

**Case 3: $x < 0$, $y \ge 0$.**

This case is symmetric to Case 2. Let $x = -n$ with $n > 0$. Then

$$
b^{x+y} = b^{-n + y} = b^{y-n}.
$$

If $y \ge n$, this reduces to Case 1; if $y < n$, it reduces to Case 2. Similarly, $b^x b^y = b^{-n} b^y = b^y / b^n$, which coincides with $b^{y-n}$.

**Case 4: $x < 0$, $y < 0$.**

Let $x = -n$, $y = -m$ with $n, m > 0$. Then

$$
b^{x+y} = b^{-n - m} = \frac{1}{b^{n+m}}.
$$

Also

$$
b^x b^y = b^{-n} b^{-m} = \frac{1}{b^n} \cdot \frac{1}{b^m} = \frac{1}{b^{n+m}}.
$$

Hence the law holds.

Thus $b^{x+y} = b^x b^y$ for all integers $x$ and $y$.

### Proof of $(b^x)^y = b^{xy}$

**Case 1: $y \ge 0$.**

Proceed by induction on $y$.

**Base case:** $y = 0$. Then $(b^x)^0 = 1 = b^{x\cdot 0}$.

**Inductive step:** Assume $(b^x)^y = b^{xy}$. Then

$$
(b^x)^{y+1} = (b^x)^y \cdot b^x = b^{xy} \cdot b^x = b^{xy + x} = b^{x(y+1)}.
$$

This proves the statement for $y+1$.

**Case 2: $y < 0$.**

Let $y = -m$ with $m > 0$. Then

$$
(b^x)^y = (b^x)^{-m} = \frac{1}{(b^x)^m}.
$$

By Case 1, $(b^x)^m = b^{xm}$, so

$$
(b^x)^y = \frac{1}{b^{xm}} = b^{-xm} = b^{x(-m)} = b^{xy}.
$$

Hence the law holds for negative $y$.

This completes the proof that $(b^x)^y = b^{xy}$ for all integers $x$ and $y$.

-, Both exponent laws follow directly from the recursive definition of integer powers in Eq. (4) using induction and the handling of negative exponents.

This completes the proof.
