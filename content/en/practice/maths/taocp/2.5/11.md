[Section 2.5: Dynamic Storage Allocation](../)

**Exercise 11.** [**] [M25] Show that the improvement to Algorithm A suggested in the
answer to exercise 6 can also be used to lead to a slight improvement in
Algorithm B, which cuts the average length of search from half the length
of the `AVAIL` list to one-third this length. (Assume that the block
being freed will be inserted into a random place within the sorted
`AVAIL` list.)

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 5m13s  

---

Let the `AVAIL` list contain $m$ free blocks in increasing address order.

The improvement referred to in the answer to Exercise 6 is not a cyclic forward scan. The idea is to remember the position at which the previous search ended and to begin the next search from there. Since the list is maintained in sorted order, the search proceeds toward the required insertion point from the remembered position. If the desired position lies later in the list, the search moves forward; if it lies earlier, the search moves backward. Thus the search distance is the difference between the two insertion positions, not a forward cyclic distance.

Let

$$
I=\text{position of the previous insertion},
\qquad
J=\text{position of the current insertion}.
$$

Number the possible insertion positions by

$$
0,1,\ldots,m-1.
$$

The exercise assumes that the block being freed is inserted into a random place within the sorted `AVAIL` list. Hence $I$ and $J$ may be regarded as independent random variables, each uniformly distributed on

$$
\{0,1,\ldots,m-1\}.
$$

With the remembered-position improvement, the number of links examined is

$$
|I-J|.
$$

Therefore the average search length is

$$
E(|I-J|) =
\frac1{m^{2}}
\sum_{i=0}^{m-1}
\sum_{j=0}^{m-1}
|i-j|.
$$

Using symmetry,

$$
E(|I-J|) =
\frac{2}{m^{2}}
\sum_{0\le i<j<m}(j-i).
$$

Let $d=j-i$. For each $d\in\{1,\ldots,m-1\}$, there are $m-d$ pairs $(i,j)$ having difference $d$. Hence

$$
\sum_{0\le i<j<m}(j-i) =
\sum_{d=1}^{m-1} d(m-d).
$$

Now

$$
\sum_{d=1}^{m-1} d(m-d) =
m\sum_{d=1}^{m-1} d -
\sum_{d=1}^{m-1} d^{2}.
$$

Using

$$
\sum_{d=1}^{m-1} d=\frac{m(m-1)}2,
\qquad
\sum_{d=1}^{m-1} d^{2} =
\frac{m(m-1)(2m-1)}6,
$$

we obtain

$$
\sum_{d=1}^{m-1} d(m-d) =
\frac{m(m-1)(m+1)}6.
$$

Therefore

$$
E(|I-J|) =
\frac{2}{m^{2}}
\cdot
\frac{m(m-1)(m+1)}6 =
\frac{m^{2}-1}{3m}.
$$

Thus

$$
E(|I-J|) =
\frac{m}{3}-\frac{1}{3m}.
$$

For large $m$, the average search length is asymptotic to

$$
\frac{m}{3}.
$$

By contrast, the original Algorithm B always begins its search at the head of the list. If the insertion position is uniformly distributed, its average search length is

$$
\frac{0+1+\cdots+(m-1)}{m} =
\frac{m-1}{2}
\sim
\frac{m}{2}.
$$

Hence the remembered-position improvement reduces the average search length from approximately one-half of the length of the `AVAIL` list to approximately one-third of its length. The factor $1/3$ comes from the expected distance between two independent random insertion positions in the ordered list.
