**Exercise 5.** [*24*] Determine the time required to simulate the `LDA` and `ENTA` operators, compared with the actual time for MIX to execute these operators directly.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 4m41s  

---

## Solution

The simulator assigns execution time $2$ to the instruction `LDA`, as shown by the entry

$$
\texttt{LDA \ \ LOAD(2)}
$$

in `OPTABLE`. This value is stored into `TIME` at line 033, and at the next pass through `CYCLE` it is added to `CLOCK` by lines 007-009. Thus the simulated machine records exactly the same execution time as a real MIX machine, namely $2$ time units.

The question asks for the time required by the simulator itself to execute one simulated `LDA`. Therefore the instructions of the simulator that are executed must be counted.

Assume first that the simulated `LDA` has index field $0$, so that no indexing is performed. The control routine executes lines

$$
007,008,009,010,011,012,013,014,015,016,017,
027,028,029,030,031,032,033,034,035,
$$

before transferring to the `LOAD` routine. This is $20$ MIX instructions.

The `LOAD` routine first calls `GETV`. The call and return mechanism executes

$$
149,150,
$$

then `FCHECK`:

$$
135,136,137,138,139,141,142,143,144,145,
$$

then `MEMORY`:

$$
126,127,128,129,130,131,132,133,134,
$$

and finally the remainder of `GETV`:

$$
152,153,154,155,156,157,158.
$$

The total for `GETV` is therefore

$$
2+10+9+7=28
$$

instructions.

For the ordinary instruction `LDA`, the field specification is $(0{:}5)$. After `GETV` returns, the `LOAD` routine stores the sign and magnitude into the simulated A-register and returns to `CYCLE`. Inspection of the routine shows that this requires $7$ additional instructions.

Hence a simulated `LDA` with no indexing executes

$$
20+28+7=55
$$

MIX instructions.

If the address is indexed, lines 018-026 are executed in addition to the control path above. These contribute

$$
9
$$

more instructions, so an indexed `LDA` requires

$$
55+9=64
$$

instructions.

Since a real MIX machine executes `LDA` in $2$ time units, the simulator requires approximately

$$
\frac{55}{2}=27.5
$$

to

$$
\frac{64}{2}=32
$$

times as much work as direct execution.

Now consider `ENTA`. In `OPTABLE` the instruction belongs to the class

$$
\texttt{INCA \ \ ADDROP(1)},
$$

so the simulated execution time recorded by the simulator is $1$ time unit, exactly matching the MIX specification.

The control routine is identical to that for `LDA`, contributing $20$ instructions when no indexing is used and $29$ when indexing is used.

The `ADDROP` routine obtains the effective address already computed in `M`, transfers it to the appropriate simulated register, performs the required sign processing, and returns to `CYCLE`. Counting the instructions in the routine gives $8$ instructions for the case of `ENTA`.

Therefore

$$
20+8=28
$$

instructions are executed for an unindexed `ENTA`, and

$$
29+8=37
$$

for an indexed `ENTA`.

Since a real MIX machine executes `ENTA` in $1$ time unit, the simulation overhead is approximately

$$
28
$$

to

$$
37
$$

times the work of direct execution.

The required comparison is therefore

$$
\boxed{\text{LDA: }55\text{ to }64\text{ simulator instructions for }2\text{ MIX time units}}
$$

and

$$
\boxed{\text{ENTA: }28\text{ to }37\text{ simulator instructions for }1\text{ MIX time unit}}.
$$

Thus the simulator executes roughly thirty times as many MIX instructions as the machine being simulated for these representative operations. ∎
