[Section 1.2.4: Integer Functions and Elementary Number Theory](../)

**Exercise 22.** &#9654; [*M10*] Give an example to show that Law B is not always true if $a$ is not relatively prime to $m$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 59s  

---

Let $m=4$, $a=2$, $b=2$, $x=0$, $y=2$. Then $a \equiv b \pmod 4$ holds since $2-2=0$ is a multiple of $4$.

Compute $ax=2\cdot 0=0$ and $by=2\cdot 2=4$, so $ax \equiv by \pmod 4$ since $4 \equiv 0 \pmod 4$. However, $x \not\equiv y \pmod 4$, since $0-2=-2$ is not a multiple of $4$. The condition $a \perp m$ fails because $\gcd(2,4)=2 \ne 1$, so cancellation in Law B does not apply. ∎
