**Exercise 29.** [*M22*] A function $f(n)$ of positive integers $n$ is called *multiplicative* if $f(rs)=f(r)f(s)$ whenever $r \perp s$. Show that each of the following functions is multiplicative:

(a) $f(n)=n^c$, where $c$ is any constant;

(b) $f(n)=[n \text{ is not divisible by } k^2 \text{ for any integer } k>1]$;

(c) $f(n)=c^k$, where $k$ is the number of distinct primes that divide $n$;

(d) the product of any two multiplicative functions.

## Solution

Let $f$ be a function on positive integers. The function is multiplicative when $f(rs)=f(r)f(s)$ for all positive integers $r,s$ with $r \perp s$.

### (a) $f(n)=n^c$

Let $r \perp s$ and let $c$ be fixed. Then

$$
f(rs)=(rs)^c.
$$

Since exponentiation distributes over multiplication,

$$
(rs)^c = r^c s^c.
$$

By definition $f(r)=r^c$ and $f(s)=s^c$, hence

$$
f(rs)=f(r)f(s).
$$

This verifies multiplicativity.

### (b) $f(n)=[n \text{ is not divisible by } k^2 \text{ for any integer } k>1]$

Let $f(n)=1$ when $n$ is not divisible by any $k^2$ with $k>1$, and $f(n)=0$ otherwise. Let $r \perp s$.

Assume $f(r)=f(s)=1$. Then neither $r$ nor $s$ is divisible by a square $k^2$ with $k>1$. If a prime $p$ satisfied $p^2 \mid rs$, then $p \mid rs$, so $p \mid r$ or $p \mid s$ since $r \perp s$. If $p \mid r$, then $p^2 \mid r$, contradicting $f(r)=1$, and similarly for $s$. Hence no such $p$ exists and $f(rs)=1$.

Assume at least one of $f(r), f(s)$ equals $0$. If $f(r)=0$, then there exists $k>1$ with $k^2 \mid r$. Since $r \mid rs$, also $k^2 \mid rs$, so $f(rs)=0$. The same holds if $f(s)=0$.

Thus $f(rs)=f(r)f(s)$ in all cases.

### (c) $f(n)=c^k$, where $k$ is the number of distinct primes dividing $n$

Let $r \perp s$. Let $k(r)$ and $k(s)$ denote the number of distinct primes dividing $r$ and $s$. Since $r$ and $s$ are relatively prime, no prime divides both, so the set of prime divisors of $rs$ is the disjoint union of those of $r$ and $s$. Hence

$$
k(rs)=k(r)+k(s).
$$

Therefore,

$$
f(rs)=c^{k(rs)}=c^{k(r)+k(s)}=c^{k(r)}c^{k(s)}=f(r)f(s).
$$

### (d) Product of two multiplicative functions

Let $f$ and $g$ be multiplicative and define $h(n)=f(n)g(n)$. Let $r \perp s$. Then

$$
h(rs)=f(rs)g(rs).
$$

Using multiplicativity of $f$ and $g$,

$$
f(rs)=f(r)f(s), \qquad g(rs)=g(r)g(s).
$$

Hence

$$
h(rs)=f(r)f(s)g(r)g(s).
$$

Rearranging factors,

$$
h(rs)=(f(r)g(r))(f(s)g(s))=h(r)h(s).
$$

This completes the verification that $h$ is multiplicative.

This completes the proof. ∎
