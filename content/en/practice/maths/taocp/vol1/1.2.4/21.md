[Section 1.2.4: Integer Functions and Elementary Number Theory](../)

**Exercise 21.** [*M22*] (*Fundamental theorem of arithmetic.*) Use Law B and exercise 1.2.1–5 to prove that every integer $n>1$ has a *unique* representation as a product of primes (except for the order of the factors). In other words, show that there is exactly one way to write

$$n=p_1p_2\cdots p_k,$$

where each $p_j$ is prime and $p_1 \le p_2 \le \cdots \le p_k$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 57s  

---

## Solution

Let $n>1$. We first prove existence of a prime factorization, then prove uniqueness.

### Existence

Let $S$ be the set of integers $n>1$ that do not admit a representation as a product of primes. Suppose $S$ is nonempty and let $n$ be its least element.

The integer $n$ is not prime, so there exist integers $a,b$ such that $n=ab$ with $1<a<n$ and $1<b<n$. By minimality of $n$, both $a$ and $b$ are not in $S$, hence each can be written as a product of primes,

$$
a=p_1p_2\cdots p_r,\qquad b=q_1q_2\cdots q_s.
$$

Multiplying gives

$$
n=p_1p_2\cdots p_r q_1q_2\cdots q_s,
$$

a product of primes, contradicting $n\in S$. Hence $S$ is empty, and every integer $n>1$ is a product of primes.

### Uniqueness

Assume

$$
n=p_1p_2\cdots p_k = q_1q_2\cdots q_\ell,
$$

where each $p_i$ and $q_j$ is prime and the sequences are nondecreasing.

Since $p_1 \mid q_1q_2\cdots q_\ell$, Exercise 1.2.1–5 (Euclid’s lemma) implies that $p_1 \mid q_j$ for some $j$. Because $q_j$ is prime, this forces $p_1=q_j$.

Reordering the $q$-factors, if necessary, we may assume $q_1=p_1$. Canceling this common factor yields

$$
p_2\cdots p_k = q_2\cdots q_\ell.
$$

The cancellation is valid since equality of integers is preserved under division by a common nonzero factor.

Repeating the same argument, each $p_i$ matches some $q_j$, and after successive cancellations both sides reduce to $1$. This forces $k=\ell$ and equality of the multisets of primes, so the ordered factorizations coincide.

This completes the proof. ∎
