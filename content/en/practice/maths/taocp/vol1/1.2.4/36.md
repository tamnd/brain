**Exercise 36.** [*M23*] Prove that $\sum_{k=1}^n \lfloor k/2 \rfloor = \lfloor n^2/4 \rfloor$; also evaluate $\sum_{k=1}^n \lceil k/2 \rceil$.

## Solution

Let $n \in \mathbb{Z}$, $n \ge 1$.

### 1. Evaluation of $\sum_{k=1}^n \left\lfloor \frac{k}{2} \right\rfloor$

Write $n$ in the form $n=2m$ or $n=2m+1$, where $m \ge 0$.

#### Case $n=2m$

Split the sum into pairs $(2i-1,2i)$ for $i=1,2,\ldots,m$. For each $i$,

$$
\left\lfloor \frac{2i-1}{2} \right\rfloor = i-1,\qquad \left\lfloor \frac{2i}{2} \right\rfloor = i.
$$

Hence each pair contributes

$$
(i-1)+i = 2i-1.
$$

Therefore

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{2m} \left\lfloor \frac{k}{2} \right\rfloor = \sum_{i=1}^{m} (2i-1).
$$

Compute the arithmetic sum:

$$
\sum_{i=1}^{m} (2i-1) = 2\sum_{i=1}^{m} i - \sum_{i=1}^{m} 1 = 2\cdot \frac{m(m+1)}{2} - m = m^2.
$$

Thus,

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{2m} \left\lfloor \frac{k}{2} \right\rfloor = m^2.
$$

Since $n=2m$, this equals $\frac{n^2}{4}$.

#### Case $n=2m+1$

Split again into pairs up to $2m$, and include the last term:

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{2m+1} \left\lfloor \frac{k}{2} \right\rfloor
=
\sum_{k=1}^{2m} \left\lfloor \frac{k}{2} \right\rfloor
+
\left\lfloor \frac{2m+1}{2} \right\rfloor.
$$

From the previous case,

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{2m} \left\lfloor \frac{k}{2} \right\rfloor = m^2,
\qquad
\left\lfloor \frac{2m+1}{2} \right\rfloor = m.
$$

Hence

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{2m+1} \left\lfloor \frac{k}{2} \right\rfloor = m^2 + m.
$$

Now compute

$$
\frac{n^2}{4} = \frac{(2m+1)^2}{4} = m^2 + m + \frac{1}{4},
$$

so

$$
\left\lfloor \frac{n^2}{4} \right\rfloor = m^2 + m.
$$

Thus, in both cases,

$$
\sum_{k=1}^n \left\lfloor \frac{k}{2} \right\rfloor = \left\lfloor \frac{n^2}{4} \right\rfloor.
$$

### 2. Evaluation of $\sum_{k=1}^n \left\lceil \frac{k}{2} \right\rceil$

Again write $n=2m$ or $n=2m+1$.

#### Case $n=2m$

For each $i=1,2,\ldots,m$,

$$
\left\lceil \frac{2i-1}{2} \right\rceil = i,\qquad \left\lceil \frac{2i}{2} \right\rceil = i.
$$

Each pair contributes $2i$, so

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{2m} \left\lceil \frac{k}{2} \right\rceil
=
\sum_{i=1}^{m} 2i
=
2\cdot \frac{m(m+1)}{2}
=
m(m+1).
$$

#### Case $n=2m+1$

Add the last term:

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{2m+1} \left\lceil \frac{k}{2} \right\rceil
=
m(m+1) + \left\lceil \frac{2m+1}{2} \right\rceil.
$$

Since

$$
\left\lceil \frac{2m+1}{2} \right\rceil = m+1,
$$

it follows that

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{2m+1} \left\lceil \frac{k}{2} \right\rceil = (m+1)^2.
$$

Now compare with

$$
\frac{(n+1)^2}{4}.
$$

If $n=2m$, then

$$
\frac{(n+1)^2}{4} = \frac{(2m+1)^2}{4} = m^2 + m + \frac{1}{4},
$$

so

$$
\left\lfloor \frac{(n+1)^2}{4} \right\rfloor = m(m+1).
$$

If $n=2m+1$, then

$$
\frac{(n+1)^2}{4} = \frac{(2m+2)^2}{4} = (m+1)^2.
$$

Hence in both cases,

$$
\sum_{k=1}^n \left\lceil \frac{k}{2} \right\rceil = \left\lfloor \frac{(n+1)^2}{4} \right\rfloor.
$$

### Final results

$$
\sum_{k=1}^n \left\lfloor \frac{k}{2} \right\rfloor = \left\lfloor \frac{n^2}{4} \right\rfloor,
\qquad
\sum_{k=1}^n \left\lceil \frac{k}{2} \right\rceil = \left\lfloor \frac{(n+1)^2}{4} \right\rfloor.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
