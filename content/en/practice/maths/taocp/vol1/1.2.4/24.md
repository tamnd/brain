[Section 1.2.4: Integer Functions and Elementary Number Theory](../)

**Exercise 24.** &#9654; [*M20*] To what extent can Laws A, B, C, and D be generalized to apply to arbitrary real numbers instead of integers?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m03s  

---

Let congruence for real numbers be defined by

$$
x \equiv y \pmod m \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad x-y \in m\mathbb{Z}, \quad m \neq 0,
$$

where $m\mathbb{Z}=\{mk : k\in\mathbb{Z}\}$. This avoids unnecessary use of floor functions and makes all arguments uniform.

We now reconsider Laws A–D from TAOCP under this definition.

## Law A (addition and multiplication)

Assume

$$
a \equiv b \pmod m,\quad x \equiv y \pmod m.
$$

Then there exist integers $k,\ell$ such that

$$
a-b=km,\quad x-y=\ell m.
$$

### Addition and subtraction

$$
(a+x)-(b+y)=(a-b)+(x-y)=(k+\ell)m,
$$

$$
(a-x)-(b-y)=(a-b)-(x-y)=(k-\ell)m.
$$

Since $k\pm \ell\in\mathbb{Z}$, both congruences hold:

$$
a\pm x \equiv b\pm y \pmod m.
$$

### Multiplication

$$
ax-by = a(x-y) + y(a-b) = a(\ell m) + y(km) = m(a\ell + yk).
$$

For this to imply $ax \equiv by \pmod m$, we would need $a\ell + yk \in \mathbb{Z}$, which is not guaranteed when $a,y\in\mathbb{R}$. Hence:

$$
ax \equiv by \pmod m \quad \text{fails in general.}
$$

So only the additive part of Law A extends to arbitrary real numbers.

## Law B (cancellation law)

A typical form is: if

$$
ax \equiv bx \pmod m,
$$

and $x$ is invertible modulo $m$, then $a \equiv b \pmod m$.

For integers, invertibility means $\gcd(x,m)=1$. For real numbers under the relation $x-y\in m\mathbb{Z}$, invertibility would require existence of $u\in\mathbb{R}$ such that

$$
xu \equiv 1 \pmod m,
$$

i.e.

$$
xu - 1 \in m\mathbb{Z}.
$$

This property generally fails for arbitrary real $x$. Without a well-behaved notion of invertibility, cancellation cannot be justified.

Even if one assumes $ax \equiv bx \pmod m$, we only obtain

$$
(a-b)x \in m\mathbb{Z},
$$

which does not imply $a-b \in m\mathbb{Z}$ for real $x$. Hence Law B does not extend in general.

## Law C (scaling)

Let $n\in\mathbb{Z}$, $n\neq 0$, and assume

$$
a \equiv b \pmod m \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad a-b = km.
$$

Then

$$
an - bn = n(a-b) = nkm = k(mn),
$$

so

$$
an \equiv bn \pmod{mn}.
$$

Conversely, if

$$
an \equiv bn \pmod{mn},
$$

then

$$
an - bn = k(mn)
$$

for some integer $k$. Dividing by $n\neq 0$,

$$
a-b = km,
$$

so $a \equiv b \pmod m$.

Thus Law C remains valid for arbitrary real $a,b$.

## Law D (Chinese remainder form)

Let $r,s\in\mathbb{Z}$, $r,s>0$, and $\gcd(r,s)=1$.

### Forward direction

If

$$
a \equiv b \pmod{rs},
$$

then $a-b = krs$, hence $r \mid (a-b)$ and $s \mid (a-b)$. Therefore

$$
a \equiv b \pmod r,\quad a \equiv b \pmod s.
$$

### Converse direction (corrected)

Assume

$$
a \equiv b \pmod r,\quad a \equiv b \pmod s.
$$

Then there exist integers $k,\ell$ such that

$$
a-b = kr,\quad a-b = \ell s.
$$

Thus

$$
kr = \ell s.
$$

Since $\gcd(r,s)=1$, Euclid’s lemma implies $r \mid \ell$. Hence $\ell = rt$ for some integer $t$. Substituting,

$$
a-b = \ell s = (rt)s = rst.
$$

Therefore,

$$
a-b \in rs\mathbb{Z},
$$

so

$$
a \equiv b \pmod{rs}.
$$

This completes the converse direction.

## Conclusion

- **Law A:** only the additive part generalizes; multiplication fails.
- **Law B:** does not generalize due to lack of meaningful modular invertibility for arbitrary real numbers.
- **Law C:** remains valid unchanged.
- **Law D:** remains valid; the converse holds once divisibility is properly justified using Euclid’s lemma.

Thus, among Laws A–D, only Laws C and D extend fully to arbitrary real numbers under the congruence relation $x-y\in m\mathbb{Z}$.
