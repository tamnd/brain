[Section 1.2.4: Integer Functions and Elementary Number Theory](../)

**Exercise 17.** [*M15*] Prove Law A directly from the definition of congruence, and also prove half of Law D: If $a \equiv b$ (modulo $rs$), then $a \equiv b$ (modulo $r$) and $a \equiv b$ (modulo $s$). (Here $r$ and $s$ are arbitrary integers.)

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m  

---

## Solution

Let $m$ be a fixed integer.

Assume $a \equiv b \pmod m$ and $x \equiv y \pmod m$. By definition of congruence, there exist integers $k$ and $\ell$ such that

$a - b = km,\qquad x - y = \ell m.$

For addition, compute

$(a + x) - (b + y) = (a - b) + (x - y) = km + \ell m = (k + \ell)m.$

Hence $(a + x) - (b + y)$ is a multiple of $m$, so $a + x \equiv b + y \pmod m$.

For subtraction, compute

$(a - x) - (b - y) = (a - b) - (x - y) = km - \ell m = (k - \ell)m.$

Hence $(a - x) - (b - y)$ is a multiple of $m$, so $a - x \equiv b - y \pmod m$.

For multiplication, expand

$ax - by = ax - ay + ay - by = a(x - y) + y(a - b).$

Substituting $x - y = \ell m$ and $a - b = km$ gives

$ax - by = a(\ell m) + y(km) = m(a\ell + yk).$

Thus $ax - by$ is a multiple of $m$, so $ax \equiv by \pmod m$.

This proves Law A.

For the first half of Law D, assume $a \equiv b \pmod{rs}$. By definition, there exists an integer $t$ such that

$a - b = t(rs).$

Rewrite this as

$a - b = r(ts).$

Hence $a - b$ is a multiple of $r$, so $a \equiv b \pmod r$.

Also rewrite

$a - b = s(tr).$

Hence $a - b$ is a multiple of $s$, so $a \equiv b \pmod s$.

This completes the proof. ∎
