[Section 1.2.4: Integer Functions and Elementary Number Theory](../)

**Exercise 45.** &#9654; [*M28*] The result of exercise 37 is somewhat surprising, since it implies that

$$
\sum_{0 \le k < n} \left\lfloor \frac{mk+x}{n} \right\rfloor
=
\sum_{0 \le k < m} \left\lfloor \frac{nk+x}{m} \right\rfloor
$$

when $m$ and $n$ are positive integers and $x$ is arbitrary. This “reciprocity relationship” is one of many similar formulas (see Section 3.3.3). Show that in general we have

$$
\sum_{0 \le j < n} f\left(\left\lfloor \frac{mj}{n} \right\rfloor\right)
=
\sum_{0 \le r < m} \left\lceil \frac{rn}{m} \right\rceil \bigl(f(r-1)-f(r)\bigr) + n f(m-1)
$$

for any function $f$ and all integers $m,n>0$. In particular, prove that

$$
\sum_{0 \le j < n} \binom{\lfloor mj/n \rfloor + 1}{k}
+
\sum_{0 \le j < m} \left\lceil \frac{jn}{m} \right\rceil \binom{j}{k-1}
=
n\binom{m}{k}.
$$

[Hint: Consider the change of variable $r=\lfloor mj/n \rfloor$. Binomial coefficients $\binom{m}{k}$ are discussed in Section 1.2.6.]

Let $m,n>0$ and let $f$ be defined on $\{0,1,\dots,m-1\}$. Set

$$
r(j)=\left\lfloor \frac{mj}{n}\right\rfloor,\qquad 0\le j<n.
$$

### 1. Partition by level sets

For each $r\in\{0,1,\dots,m-1\}$, define

$$
J_r=\{j:0\le j<n,\ r(j)=r\},\qquad N_r=|J_r|.
$$

Then

$$
\{0,1,\dots,n-1\}=\bigsqcup_{r=0}^{m-1}J_r,
$$

so

$$
\sum_{0\le j<n} f\!\left(\left\lfloor \frac{mj}{n}\right\rfloor\right)
=\sum_{r=0}^{m-1} N_r f(r).
$$

### 2. Compute the multiplicities

The condition $r(j)=r$ is equivalent to

$$
r \le \frac{mj}{n} < r+1
\iff \frac{rn}{m} \le j < \frac{(r+1)n}{m}.
$$

Hence $J_r$ is an interval of integers, and if we define

$$
j_r=\min J_r \quad (0\le r<m), \qquad j_m=n,
$$

then

$$
J_r=\{j_r,j_r+1,\dots,j_{r+1}-1\},\qquad N_r=j_{r+1}-j_r.
$$

Moreover,

$$
j_r=\left\lceil \frac{rn}{m}\right\rceil.
$$

### 3. Discrete summation by parts

We have

$$
\sum_{r=0}^{m-1} N_r f(r)
=\sum_{r=0}^{m-1} (j_{r+1}-j_r)f(r).
$$

Expand:

$$
=\sum_{r=0}^{m-1} j_{r+1}f(r)-\sum_{r=0}^{m-1} j_r f(r).
$$

Shift index in the first sum:

$$
\sum_{r=0}^{m-1} j_{r+1}f(r)=\sum_{r=1}^{m} j_r f(r-1).
$$

Therefore,

$$
\sum_{0\le j<n} f(r(j))
=\sum_{r=1}^{m} j_r f(r-1)-\sum_{r=0}^{m-1} j_r f(r).
$$

Now separate boundary terms and combine overlapping ranges:

$$
=\; j_m f(m-1)\;+\;\sum_{r=1}^{m-1} j_r f(r-1)\;-\;\sum_{r=1}^{m-1} j_r f(r),
$$

since $j_0=0$. Using $j_m=n$, this becomes

$$
= n f(m-1)+\sum_{r=1}^{m-1} j_r\bigl(f(r-1)-f(r)\bigr).
$$

Substitute $j_r=\left\lceil \frac{rn}{m}\right\rceil$:

$$
\sum_{0\le j<n} f\!\left(\left\lfloor \frac{mj}{n}\right\rfloor\right)
=
n f(m-1)+\sum_{r=1}^{m-1}\left\lceil \frac{rn}{m}\right\rceil \bigl(f(r-1)-f(r)\bigr).
$$

### 4. Removal of the boundary issue

The term $r=0$ does not appear because the derivation produces a sum over $r=1,\dots,m-1$. If one rewrites the formula with $0\le r<m$, the additional $r=0$ term would be

$$
\left\lceil 0\right\rceil (f(-1)-f(0)),
$$

which is not well-formed since $f(-1)$ is undefined. However, this term has coefficient $0$, so the correct statement is the $r=1$ to $m-1$ form above, which is fully well-defined and equivalent to the intended identity.

### 5. Binomial specialization

Let $f(r)=\binom{r+1}{k}$. Then for $r\ge 1$,

$$
f(r-1)-f(r)=\binom{r}{k}-\binom{r+1}{k}=-\binom{r}{k-1}.
$$

Substitute into the identity:

$$
\sum_{0\le j<n} \binom{\lfloor mj/n\rfloor+1}{k}
=
n\binom{m}{k}
-
\sum_{r=1}^{m-1}\left\lceil \frac{rn}{m}\right\rceil \binom{r}{k-1}.
$$

Move the second term to the left:

$$
\sum_{0\le j<n} \binom{\lfloor mj/n\rfloor+1}{k}
+
\sum_{r=1}^{m-1}\left\lceil \frac{rn}{m}\right\rceil \binom{r}{k-1}
=
n\binom{m}{k}.
$$

Since the $r=0$ term equals $0\cdot \binom{0}{k-1}=0$, the sum may be extended to $0\le r<m$, giving

$$
\sum_{0 \le j < n} \binom{\lfloor mj/n \rfloor + 1}{k}
+
\sum_{0 \le j < m} \left\lceil \frac{jn}{m} \right\rceil \binom{j}{k-1}
=
n\binom{m}{k}.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
