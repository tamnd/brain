[Section 1.2.4: Integer Functions and Elementary Number Theory](../)

**Exercise 31.** [*M22*] Prove that if $f(n)$ is multiplicative, so is

$$g(n)=\sum_{d \mid n} f(d).$$

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m03s  

---

## Solution

Let $r \perp s$. Write

$$
g(rs)=\sum_{d \mid rs} f(d).
$$

Every positive divisor $d$ of $rs$ can be expressed uniquely in the form $d=ab$, where $a \mid r$ and $b \mid s$.

To see existence, let $d \mid rs$. Every prime factor of $d$ divides $rs$, hence divides either $r$ or $s$. Define $a$ as the product of those prime powers in the factorization of $d$ whose primes divide $r$, and define $b$ as the product of those prime powers whose primes divide $s$. Then $d=ab$, $a \mid r$, and $b \mid s$.

For uniqueness, if $d=ab=a'b'$ with $a,a' \mid r$ and $b,b' \mid s$, then $a/a' = b'/b$ is a rational number whose prime factors divide both $r$ and $s$. Since $r \perp s$, no prime divides both $r$ and $s$, hence $a=a'$ and $b=b'$.

The factorization also implies $a \perp b$, since any prime dividing $a$ divides $r$ and cannot divide $s$, while any prime dividing $b$ divides $s$ and cannot divide $r$.

Therefore,

$$
g(rs)=\sum_{a \mid r}\sum_{b \mid s} f(ab).
$$

Since $f$ is multiplicative and $a \perp b$, Law A in its multiplicative form gives

$$
f(ab)=f(a)f(b).
$$

Substitution yields

$$
g(rs)=\sum_{a \mid r}\sum_{b \mid s} f(a)f(b).
$$

Rearranging the finite sum,

$$
g(rs)=\left(\sum_{a \mid r} f(a)\right)\left(\sum_{b \mid s} f(b)\right).
$$

By definition of $g$,

$$
g(rs)=g(r)g(s).
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
