[Section 1.2.4: Integer Functions and Elementary Number Theory](../)

**Exercise 19.** &#9654; [*M10*] (*Law of inverses.*) If $n \perp m$, there is an integer $n'$ such that $nn' \equiv 1$ (modulo $m$). Prove this, using the extension of Euclid’s algorithm (Algorithm 1.2.1E).

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m01s  

---

Since $n \perp m$, the greatest common divisor of $n$ and $m$ is $1$. By the extension of Euclid’s algorithm (Algorithm 1.2.1E), there exist integers $x$ and $y$ such that

$nx + my = 1.$

Reducing this identity modulo $m$ gives $my \equiv 0 \pmod m$, hence

$nx \equiv 1 \pmod m.$

Let $n' = x$. Then $nn' \equiv 1 \pmod m$, which establishes the existence of a multiplicative inverse of $n$ modulo $m$.

This completes the proof. ∎
