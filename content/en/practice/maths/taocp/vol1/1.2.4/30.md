**Exercise 30.** [*M30*] Prove that the function $\varphi(n)$ of exercise 27 is multiplicative. Using this fact, evaluate $\varphi(1000000)$, and give a method for evaluating $\varphi(n)$ in a simple way once $n$ has been factored into primes.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m22s

## Setup

Let $\varphi(n)$ denote the number of integers $a$ with $1 \le a \le n$ such that $a \perp n$. The goal is to prove that $\varphi$ is multiplicative: if $r \perp s$, then

$\varphi(rs)=\varphi(r)\varphi(s).$

Then evaluate $\varphi(1000000)$ and give a general evaluation method once $n$ is factored into primes.

## Solution

Assume $r \perp s$. Consider the mapping

$a \mapsto (a \bmod r,\; a \bmod s)$

from the integers modulo $rs$ to pairs of residues modulo $r$ and $s$.

By the Chinese remainder property for relatively prime moduli, each pair $(x,y)$ with $0 \le x < r$ and $0 \le y < s$ corresponds to a unique residue class modulo $rs$. Hence this mapping is a bijection between residues modulo $rs$ and ordered pairs of residues modulo $r$ and $s$.

An integer $a$ satisfies $a \perp rs$ if and only if $a \perp r$ and $a \perp s$. Indeed, if a prime divides $r$ or $s$, it divides $rs$, and divisibility relations transfer through common factors of $a$ with $rs$.

Under the bijection, the condition $a \perp r$ depends only on $a \bmod r$, and $a \perp s$ depends only on $a \bmod s$. Therefore the set of residues modulo $rs$ that are relatively prime to $rs$ corresponds exactly to the Cartesian product of the set of residues modulo $r$ relatively prime to $r$ with the set of residues modulo $s$ relatively prime to $s$.

The number of admissible residues modulo $r$ is $\varphi(r)$ and the number of admissible residues modulo $s$ is $\varphi(s)$. The bijection preserves this product structure, giving

$\varphi(rs)=\varphi(r)\varphi(s).$

This proves that $\varphi$ is multiplicative.

Now factor

$1000000 = 10^6 = 2^6 \cdot 5^6.$

Since $2^6 \perp 5^6$, multiplicativity gives

$\varphi(1000000)=\varphi(2^6)\varphi(5^6).$

For a prime power $p^e$, the integers not relatively prime to $p^e$ are exactly the multiples of $p$, namely $p,2p,\ldots,p^e$, which number $p^{e-1}$. Hence

$\varphi(p^e)=p^e-p^{e-1}.$

Applying this formula,

$\varphi(2^6)=2^6-2^5=64-32=32,$

$\varphi(5^6)=5^6-5^5=15625-3125=12500.$

Therefore

$\varphi(1000000)=32 \cdot 12500=400000.$

A general evaluation method follows from unique prime factorization. If

$n=\prod_{i=1}^k p_i^{e_i},$

then repeated use of multiplicativity yields

$\varphi(n)=\prod_{i=1}^k \varphi(p_i^{e_i})=\prod_{i=1}^k (p_i^{e_i}-p_i^{e_i-1})=n\prod_{i=1}^k \left(1-\frac{1}{p_i}\right).$

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification

The bijection between residues modulo $rs$ and pairs of residues modulo $r$ and $s$ uses that $r \perp s$, which ensures existence and uniqueness of simultaneous congruence classes.

The characterization $a \perp rs \iff (a \perp r \ \text{and}\ a \perp s)$ follows from prime factorization of divisibility: a common divisor of $a$ and $rs$ must divide either $r$ or $s$, and conversely any common divisor of $a$ with $r$ or $s$ divides $rs$.

For the computation,

$2^6-2^5=32,$

$5^6=15625,\quad 5^5=3125,\quad 15625-3125=12500,$

and

$32 \cdot 12500=400000.$

All arithmetic steps are consistent with the multiplicative formula and prime power evaluation.

## Notes

The identity

$\varphi(n)=n\prod_{p \mid n}\left(1-\frac{1}{p}\right)$

depends only on the distinct prime divisors of $n$, not their exponents, beyond contributing to the factor $n$. This structure parallels multiplicative behavior of Dirichlet convolutions and appears in the derivation of Euler’s theorem in the preceding exercise.
