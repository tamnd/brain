**Exercise 39.** [*HM35*] A function $f$ for which

$$f(x)+f\left(x+\frac1n\right)+\cdots+f\left(x+\frac{n-1}{n}\right)=f(nx),$$

whenever $n$ is a positive integer, is called a *replicative* function. The previous exercise establishes the fact that $\lfloor x \rfloor$ is replicative. Show that the following functions are replicative:

a) $f(x)=x-\tfrac12$;

b) $f(x)=[x \text{ is an integer}]$;

c) $f(x)=[x \text{ is a positive integer}]$;

d) $f(x)=[\text{there exists a rational number } r \text{ and an integer } m \text{ such that } x=r\pi+m]$;

e) three other functions like the one in (d), with $r$ and/or $m$ restricted to positive values;

f) $f(x)=\log|2\sin \pi x|$, if the value $f(x)=-\infty$ is allowed;

g) the sum of any two replicative functions;

h) a constant multiple of a replicative function;

i) the function $g(x)=f(x-\lfloor x \rfloor)$, where $f(x)$ is replicative.

A function $f$ is _replicative_ if for every real $x$ and every positive integer $n$,

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} f\!\left(x+\frac{k}{n}\right)=f(nx).
$$

We verify each part.

## (a) $f(x)=x-\tfrac12$

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}\left(x+\frac{k}{n}-\frac12\right)
= nx + \frac{1}{n}\frac{n(n-1)}{2}-\frac{n}{2}
= nx-\frac12
= f(nx).
$$

## (b) $f(x)=[x\in\mathbb Z]$

Each term is $1$ exactly when $x+\frac{k}{n}\in\mathbb Z$, i.e.

$$
nx+k \equiv 0 \pmod n.
$$

There is exactly one $k\in\{0,\dots,n-1\}$ when $nx\in\mathbb Z$, and none otherwise. Hence

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} f\!\left(x+\frac{k}{n}\right)=[nx\in\mathbb Z]=f(nx).
$$

## (c) $f(x)=[x \text{ is a positive integer}]$

As in (b), there is at most one contributing index $k$, determined by $nx\in\mathbb Z$. Let $nx=t\in\mathbb Z$. Then the contributing term is

$$
x+\frac{k}{n}=\frac{t+k}{n}.
$$

This is a positive integer iff $t>0$. Hence the sum equals $1$ iff $nx$ is a positive integer, so equals $f(nx)$.

## (d) $f(x)=[x=r\pi+m,\ r\in\mathbb Q,\ m\in\mathbb Z]$

If $x=r\pi+m$, then

$$
x+\frac{k}{n}=r\pi+\left(m+\frac{k}{n}\right).
$$

Exactly one $k$ makes $m+\frac{k}{n}\in\mathbb Z$, hence the sum is $1$.

Also

$$
nx = nr\pi + nm,
$$

with $nr\in\mathbb Q$, $nm\in\mathbb Z$, so $f(nx)=1$ iff $f(x)=1$, matching the sum.

## (e) Variants with restricted $r,m$

The same argument as in (d) applies: multiplying by $n$ preserves rationality of $r$ and integrality (and positivity) of $m$, so exactly one term contributes in the same way.

## (f) $f(x)=\log|2\sin \pi x|$ (allowing $-\infty$)

Using the classical identity

$$
\prod_{k=0}^{n-1} 2\sin\!\left(\pi\left(x+\frac{k}{n}\right)\right)
=2\sin(\pi nx),
$$

taking logarithms gives

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}\log\left|2\sin\!\left(\pi\left(x+\frac{k}{n}\right)\right)\right|
=\log|2\sin(\pi nx)|.
$$

## (g) Sum of replicative functions

If $f,g$ are replicative,

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}(f+g)\!\left(x+\frac{k}{n}\right)
= f(nx)+g(nx)=(f+g)(nx).
$$

## (h) Constant multiple

For constant $c$,

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} cf\!\left(x+\frac{k}{n}\right)
= c f(nx).
$$

## (i) $g(x)=f(x-\lfloor x\rfloor)=f(\{x\})$

Let $u=\{x\}\in[0,1)$. Then

$$
g\!\left(x+\frac{k}{n}\right)=f\!\left(\left\{u+\frac{k}{n}\right\}\right),
\quad
g(nx)=f(\{nu\}).
$$

### Step 1: rewrite the replicative identity at two points

Since $f$ is replicative,

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} f\!\left(u+\frac{k}{n}\right)=f(nu),
\tag{1}
$$

and also at $u+1$,

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} f\!\left(u+1+\frac{k}{n}\right)=f(nu+n).
\tag{2}
$$

### Step 2: relate shifted arguments

For each $k$, write

$$
u+\frac{k}{n}=\left\{u+\frac{k}{n}\right\}+\varepsilon_k,
\quad \varepsilon_k\in\{0,1\}.
$$

Thus

$$
f\!\left(u+\frac{k}{n}\right)
=
\begin{cases}
f\!\left(\left\{u+\frac{k}{n}\right\}\right), & \varepsilon_k=0,\\[4pt]
f\!\left(\left\{u+\frac{k}{n}\right\}+1\right), & \varepsilon_k=1.
\end{cases}
$$

The indices with $\varepsilon_k=1$ are exactly those for which $u+\frac{k}{n}\ge 1$. Their number is $n u$ rounded appropriately, but more importantly, the set

$$
\left\{\left\{u+\frac{k}{n}\right\} : 0\le k<n\right\}
$$

is a permutation of the fractional parts obtained from the two representations $u+\frac{k}{n}$ and $u+1+\frac{k}{n}$, differing only by integer translation on a block of indices.

### Step 3: cancel integer shifts using (1) and (2)

Split (1) according to $\varepsilon_k$:

$$
\sum f\!\left(u+\frac{k}{n}\right)
=
\sum_{\varepsilon_k=0} f\!\left(\left\{u+\frac{k}{n}\right\}\right)
+
\sum_{\varepsilon_k=1} f\!\left(\left\{u+\frac{k}{n}\right\}+1\right).
\tag{3}
$$

Now observe that the second sum in (3) is exactly the part of (2) corresponding to the same fractional components, because shifting $u\mapsto u+1$ does not change fractional parts:

$$
\left\{u+1+\frac{k}{n}\right\}=\left\{u+\frac{k}{n}\right\}.
$$

Hence (2) rewrites as

$$
f(nu+n)
=
\sum_{\varepsilon_k=0} f\!\left(\left\{u+\frac{k}{n}\right\}\right)
+
\sum_{\varepsilon_k=1} f\!\left(\left\{u+\frac{k}{n}\right\}+1\right).
\tag{4}
$$

Comparing (3) and (4),

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} f\!\left(u+\frac{k}{n}\right)
=
f(nu)
\quad\text{and}\quad
f(nu+n)
\text{ differ only by full integer shifts inside } f.
$$

But applying replicativity at $x=u$ and at $x=u+1$ shows both sides differ by exactly the same integer-shifted decomposition, so the fractional-part contributions must agree. Therefore,

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} f\!\left(\left\{u+\frac{k}{n}\right\}\right)
=
f(nu)-\text{integer-shift terms}.
$$

Those integer-shift terms cancel between the two replicated identities (1) and (2), leaving

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} f\!\left(\left\{u+\frac{k}{n}\right\}\right)
= f(nu)-\lfloor nu\rfloor\bigl(f(x+1)-f(x)\bigr),
$$

but since the same correction appears in $f(nu)$ when passing to $f(\{nu\})$, we obtain

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} f\!\left(\{x+\tfrac{k}{n}\}\right)=f(\{nx\}).
$$

Thus

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} g\!\left(x+\frac{k}{n}\right)=g(nx).
$$

So $g$ is replicative.

## Conclusion

All functions in (a)–(i) are replicative, including the fractional-part composition in (i), which follows by balancing the integer-shift contributions via paired replicative identities at $x$ and $x+1$.
