[Section 1.2.4: Integer Functions and Elementary Number Theory](../)

**Exercise 23.** [*M10*] Give an example to show that Law D is not always true if $r$ is not relatively prime to $s$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m07s  

---

Take $r=s=2$, so $rs=4$, and $a=2$, $b=0$. Then $a-b=2$, hence $2 \mid (a-b)$, so $a \equiv b \pmod 2$ and also $a \equiv b \pmod 2$ for the second modulus. Thus both congruences modulo $r$ and modulo $s$ hold.

However $4 \nmid (a-b)$ since $2 \bmod 4 = 2$, so $a \not\equiv b \pmod 4$. Therefore $a \equiv b \pmod r$ and $a \equiv b \pmod s$ does not imply $a \equiv b \pmod{rs}$ when $r$ and $s$ are not relatively prime. ∎
