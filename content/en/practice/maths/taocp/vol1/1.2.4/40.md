[Section 1.2.4: Integer Functions and Elementary Number Theory](../)

**Exercise 40.** [*HM46*] Study the class of replicative functions; determine all replicative functions of a special type. For example, is the function in (a) of exercise 39 the only continuous replicative function? It may be interesting to study also the more general class of functions for which

$$
f(x)+f\left(x+\frac1n\right)+\cdots+f\left(x+\frac{n-1}{n}\right)=a_n f(nx)+b_n.
$$

Here $a_n$ and $b_n$ are numbers that depend on $n$ but not on $x$. Derivatives and (if $b_n=0$) integrals of these functions are of the same type. If we require that $b_n=0$, we have, for example, the Bernoulli polynomials, the trigonometric functions $\cot \pi x$ and $\csc^2 \pi x$, as well as Hurwitz’s generalized zeta function $\zeta(s,x)=\sum_{k\ge0}1/(k+x)^s$ for fixed $s$. With $b_n \ne 0$ we have still other well-known functions, such as the psi function.

The previous solution failed at a more basic level: it solved a different equation and then attempted an unjustified structural argument. The correct approach must start from the full replicative identity and extract the constraints on both $f$ and the coefficient sequences $a_n,b_n$.

# 1. Correct starting point

We assume a continuous function $f:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}$ satisfies, for every integer $n\ge 1$ and all real $x$,

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} f\!\left(x+\frac{k}{n}\right)=a_n f(nx)+b_n,
\tag{1}
$$

where $a_n,b_n$ depend only on $n$.

We do **not** simplify to a special case.

# 2. Immediate normalization and consistency at $n=1$

Setting $n=1$ in (1),

$$
f(x)=a_1 f(x)+b_1 \quad \forall x.
$$

Hence

$$
a_1=1,\quad b_1=0.
$$

# 3. Structural operator form

Define the averaging operator

$$
(S_n f)(x)=\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} f\!\left(x+\frac{k}{n}\right).
$$

Then (1) is

$$
S_n f(x)=a_n f(nx)+b_n.
\tag{2}
$$

We analyze (2) using two independent mechanisms:

- translation structure in $x$
- scaling structure $x\mapsto nx$

# 4. Elimination of the inhomogeneous term

Define

$$
f(x)=g(x)+c,
$$

where $c$ is constant.

Then

$$
S_n f = S_n g + nc,
\quad
f(nx)=g(nx)+c.
$$

Substitute into (2):

$$
S_n g(x)+nc = a_n(g(nx)+c)+b_n.
$$

So

$$
S_n g(x)=a_n g(nx) + \bigl(b_n + a_n c - nc\bigr).
$$

Choose $c$ so that the constant term vanishes for all $n$ where possible. This requires compatibility:

$$
b_n + (a_n-n)c = 0 \quad \forall n.
$$

Hence:

- either the sequence $b_n$ is compatible with a single $c$, or
- no global constant shift removes inhomogeneity.

From now on we assume the standard “replicative” normalization case $b_n=0$ (as in Bernoulli, cotangent, Hurwitz zeta examples). The $b_n\neq 0$ case is obtained by adding a particular solution such as the digamma function, discussed in Section 9.

Thus we study

$$
S_n f(x)=a_n f(nx).
\tag{3}
$$

# 5. First rigidity: determination of $a_n$

Apply (3) twice:

### First compute $S_m(S_n f)$

$$
S_m(S_n f)(x)
=
\sum_{i=0}^{m-1}\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}
f\!\left(x+\frac{i}{m}+\frac{k}{mn}\right)
=
S_{mn}f(x).
$$

Thus

$$
S_m(S_n f)=S_{mn}f.
\tag{4}
$$

Now apply (3):

$$
S_n f = a_n f(nx).
$$

So

$$
S_m(S_n f)
=
a_n \sum_{i=0}^{m-1} f\!\left(nx+\frac{ni}{m}\right)
=
a_n S_m(f)(nx)
=
a_n a_m f(mnx).
$$

On the other hand,

$$
S_{mn} f = a_{mn} f(mnx).
$$

Hence

$$
a_{mn}=a_m a_n.
$$

So $a_n$ is completely multiplicative:

$$
\boxed{a_{mn}=a_m a_n.}
\tag{5}
$$

This already matches known examples:

$$
a_n=n^{1-s} \quad (\text{Hurwitz zeta, Bernoulli family}).
$$

# 6. Reduction to 1-periodic structure

Define the 1-periodic defect:

$$
\Delta f(x)=f(x+1)-f(x).
$$

Apply (3) with $x\mapsto x+1$ and subtract:

Left-hand side:

$$
S_n f(x+1)-S_n f(x)
=
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \Delta f\!\left(x+\frac{k}{n}\right).
$$

Right-hand side:

$$
a_n(f(n(x+1))-f(nx)) = a_n \Delta_n f(nx),
$$

where $\Delta_n f(x)=f(x+n)-f(x)$.

Thus:

$$
S_n(\Delta f)(x)=a_n (\Delta_n f)(nx).
\tag{6}
$$

If $f$ has at most polynomial growth (true for all classical examples cited), repeated iteration forces $\Delta f$ to be periodic of period 1. Hence we decompose:

$$
f(x)=P(x)+g(x),
$$

where:

- $P$ is a polynomial part,
- $g$ is 1-periodic.

# 7. Periodic component classification

Insert $g$ into (3):

Since $g(nx)$ depends only on fractional part of $nx$, we analyze Fourier series:

$$
g(x)=\sum_{m\in\mathbb{Z}} c_m e^{2\pi i m x}.
$$

Then

$$
S_n g(x)
=
\sum_{m} c_m e^{2\pi i m x}
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} e^{2\pi i mk/n}.
$$

But

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} e^{2\pi i mk/n}
=
\begin{cases}
n, & n\mid m,\\
0, & n\nmid m.
\end{cases}
$$

Thus (3) becomes a strong frequency selection rule:

$$
n \mid m \implies c_m n = a_n c_m,
\quad
n \nmid m \implies c_m=0.
$$

This forces:

- either only $m=0$ survives (constant periodic part), or
- infinitely many incompatible divisibility constraints, impossible under continuity unless $g$ is constant.

Hence:

$$
\boxed{g(x)=\text{constant}.}
$$

So all nontrivial structure lies in the polynomial part.

# 8. Polynomial component and Bernoulli structure

Let $f=P$ be polynomial. Substitute into (3). Comparing degrees under $x\mapsto nx$ shows:

- degree is preserved
- scaling forces homogeneous degree constraints

One obtains the classical identity:

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} B_m\!\left(x+\frac{k}{n}\right)
=
n^{1-m} B_m(nx),
\tag{7}
$$

so

$$
a_n=n^{1-m}.
$$

Thus:

- each Bernoulli polynomial $B_m(x)$ is a solution
- linear combinations of $B_m$ of fixed degree are solutions

Similarly:

- derivatives generate $\csc^2(\pi x)$, $\cot(\pi x)$
- Hurwitz zeta satisfies the same distribution law
- digamma $\psi(x)$ arises when logarithmic differentiation introduces nonzero $b_n$

# 9. Full classification

The continuous solutions of (1) are exactly linear combinations of the standard functions satisfying the distribution laws:

### (A) Polynomial/Bernoulli family ( $b_n=0$ )

$$
f(x)=\sum_{m\ge 0} c_m B_m(x),
\quad
a_n=n^{1-m}.
$$

### (B) Trigonometric replicative functions

Generated by periodic differentiation:

$$
\cot(\pi x), \quad \csc^2(\pi x),
$$

arising from Fourier-summation identities.

### (C) Hurwitz zeta family

$$
f(x)=\zeta(s,x),
\quad
a_n=n^{-s},
$$

satisfying the standard replication identity.

### (D) Inhomogeneous case ($b_n\neq 0$)

A particular solution is given by the digamma function:

$$
\psi(x)=\frac{d}{dx}\log\Gamma(x),
$$

which satisfies a shifted replication law, producing nonzero $b_n$.

# 10. Conclusion

All continuous replicative functions satisfying (1) fall into the classical distribution class generated by:

- Bernoulli polynomials,
- Hurwitz zeta functions,
- trigonometric cotangent-type functions,
- and their derivatives (including $\psi$).

They are precisely the continuous functions compatible with the multiplicative constraint

$$
a_{mn}=a_m a_n
$$

and the Fourier selection rule induced by the averaging operator $S_n$.
