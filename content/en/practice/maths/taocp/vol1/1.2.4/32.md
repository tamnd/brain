[Section 1.2.4: Integer Functions and Elementary Number Theory](../)

**Exercise 32.** [*M18*] Prove the double-summation identity

$$\sum_{d \mid n}\sum_{c \mid d} f(c,d)=\sum_{c \mid n}\sum_{d \mid (n/c)} f(c,cd),$$

for any function $f(x,y)$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m21s  

---

## Solution

All sums are finite since the set of positive divisors of $n$ is finite.

Consider the left-hand side

$$
\sum_{d \mid n}\sum_{c \mid d} f(c,d).
$$

The indexing set consists of all ordered pairs $(c,d)$ such that $d \mid n$ and $c \mid d$. These conditions are equivalent to the existence of an integer $k \ge 1$ with

$$
d = c k, \quad k \in \mathbb{Z}, \quad k \mid \frac{n}{c}.
$$

Indeed, from $c \mid d$ we write $d = c k$ for some integer $k \ge 1$. From $d \mid n$ it follows that $c k \mid n$, hence $k \mid n/c$, which implies $c \mid n$.

Thus every admissible pair $(c,d)$ determines a unique pair $(c,k)$ such that $c \mid n$ and $k \mid (n/c)$, with $d = ck$.

Conversely, if $c \mid n$ and $k \mid (n/c)$, then $d = ck$ satisfies $d \mid n$, since

$$
\frac{n}{d} = \frac{n}{ck} = \frac{1}{k}\cdot \frac{n}{c}
$$

is an integer, and also $c \mid d$ holds because $d = ck$. Hence every pair $(c,k)$ with $c \mid n$ and $k \mid (n/c)$ corresponds to exactly one pair $(c,d)$ with $d = ck$, $d \mid n$, and $c \mid d$.

Substituting $d = ck$ in the summand yields

$$
f(c,d) = f(c,ck).
$$

Therefore the left-hand side can be reindexed over the pairs $(c,k)$ as

$$
\sum_{c \mid n}\sum_{k \mid (n/c)} f(c,ck).
$$

Renaming the inner variable $k$ as $d$ gives

$$
\sum_{c \mid n}\sum_{d \mid (n/c)} f(c,cd),
$$

which is the right-hand side. This completes the proof. ∎
