[Section 2.3.5: Lists and Garbage Collection](../)

**Exercise 1.** [**] [M21] In Section 2.3.4 we saw that trees are special cases of the
"classical" mathematical concept of a directed graph. Can Lists be described in
graph-theoretic terminology?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 1m58s  

---

A List can be described as a finite directed graph whose vertices correspond to memory nodes and whose directed edges correspond to pointer fields such as `RLINK` and `DLINK`. Each vertex carries a type label as in Section 2.3.5, so vertices are partitioned into atoms, list heads, and sublist nodes, and the outgoing edges are determined by this type. A node of list type has a distinguished ordered outgoing structure representing a linear successor link and possibly a sublist link, while an atom has only the structural pointer used to reach its stored data representation.

Unlike trees, the underlying directed graph of a List is not required to be acyclic and is not required to have indegree constraints, since sharing of sublists is permitted and multiple pointers may refer to the same node. Cycles may also occur through recursive references such as those illustrated in (4)–(5). Therefore a List is precisely a finite, typed, directed graph with labeled edges corresponding to list structure, where no restriction is imposed to enforce acyclicity or uniqueness of parent pointers. This characterizes Lists as general pointer graphs rather than tree-like structures. This completes the proof. ∎
