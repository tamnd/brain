**Exercise 17.** [*M27*] Let $f(z)$ and $g(z)$ be generating functions that represent probability distributions.

a) Show that $h(z)=g(f(z))$ is also a generating function representing a probability distribution.

b) Interpret the significance of $h(z)$ in terms of $f(z)$ and $g(z)$.

c) Give formulas for the mean and variance of $h$ in terms of those for $f$ and $g$.

## Setup

Let

$$
f(z)=\sum_{k\ge 0} a_k z^k,\qquad g(z)=\sum_{n\ge 0} b_n z^n
$$

be probability generating functions. Hence $a_k\ge 0$, $b_n\ge 0$, and

$$
f(1)=\sum_k a_k=1,\qquad g(1)=\sum_n b_n=1.
$$

Define

$$
h(z)=g(f(z)).
$$

Let $X$ be the random variable with generating function $f$, so

$$
\mathbb{P}(X=k)=a_k,
$$

and let $N$ be the random variable with generating function $g$, so

$$
\mathbb{P}(N=n)=b_n.
$$

The required tasks are:

a) Show that $h(z)$ is a probability generating function.

b) Interpret $h(z)$ probabilistically in terms of $f$ and $g$.

c) Express the mean and variance of $h$ in terms of those of $f$ and $g$.

## Solution

### (a) $h(z)$ is a probability generating function

Since $f(1)=1$, substitution gives

$$
h(1)=g(f(1))=g(1)=1.
$$

Write

$$
g(f(z))=\sum_{n\ge 0} b_n (f(z))^n.
$$

Each power $(f(z))^n$ expands as a Cauchy product of $n$ copies of $\sum_{k\ge 0} a_k z^k$, so it is a formal power series in $z$ whose coefficients are finite sums of products of the form $a_{k_1}\cdots a_{k_n}$, hence nonnegative.

Multiplying by $b_n\ge 0$ preserves nonnegativity of coefficients, and summing over $n$ preserves nonnegativity termwise. Therefore every coefficient of $h(z)$ is nonnegative.

Together with $h(1)=1$, this shows that $h(z)$ is a probability generating function.

### (b) Probabilistic interpretation of $h(z)$

Let $X_1,X_2,\ldots$ be independent random variables each distributed as $X$, with generating function $f(z)$, and let $N$ be independent of all $X_i$ with generating function $g(z)$.

Define the random sum

$$
S=\sum_{i=1}^{N} X_i,
$$

with the convention that $S=0$ when $N=0$.

Conditioning on $N=n$, the generating function of $S$ becomes

$$
\mathbb{E}(z^S \mid N=n) = (f(z))^n,
$$

since independence makes the generating function of a sum equal to the product of the generating functions.

Taking expectation over $N$ gives

$$
\mathbb{E}(z^S)=\sum_{n\ge 0} b_n (f(z))^n=g(f(z))=h(z).
$$

Thus $h(z)$ is the generating function of the compound sum $S$, where $N$ selects the number of independent $X$-summands.

### (c) Mean and variance of $h$

Let

$$
\mu_f = f'(1), \qquad \sigma_f^2 = f''(1)+f'(1)-f'(1)^2,
$$

and similarly

$$
\mu_g = g'(1), \qquad \sigma_g^2 = g''(1)+g'(1)-g'(1)^2.
$$

#### Mean

Differentiate $h(z)=g(f(z))$:

$$
h'(z)=g'(f(z))f'(z).
$$

Evaluating at $z=1$ gives

$$
h'(1)=g'(f(1))f'(1)=g'(1)f'(1).
$$

Thus

$$
\operatorname{mean}(h)=\mu_g\,\mu_f.
$$

#### Variance

Differentiate again:

$$
h''(z)=g''(f(z))(f'(z))^2+g'(f(z))f''(z).
$$

Evaluating at $z=1$ gives

$$
h''(1)=g''(1)(f'(1))^2+g'(1)f''(1).
$$

Now apply the variance identity

$$
\operatorname{var}(h)=h''(1)+h'(1)-h'(1)^2.
$$

Substituting the computed terms yields

$$
\operatorname{var}(h)=g''(1)(f'(1))^2+g'(1)f''(1)+g'(1)f'(1)-g'(1)^2 f'(1)^2.
$$

Group terms using $\mu_f=f'(1)$ and $\mu_g=g'(1)$:

$$
\operatorname{var}(h)=g'(1)\bigl(f''(1)+f'(1)-f'(1)^2\bigr)+\bigl(g''(1)+g'(1)-g'(1)^2\bigr)(f'(1))^2.
$$

Recognizing the variance expressions gives

$$
\operatorname{var}(h)=\mu_g\,\sigma_f^2+\sigma_g^2\,\mu_f^2.
$$

## Verification

The coefficient conditions for a probability generating function hold because composition of formal power series with nonnegative coefficients preserves nonnegativity term by term, and normalization follows from $h(1)=g(f(1))=1$.

The interpretation of $h$ matches the conditioning identity

$$
\mathbb{E}(z^S)=\mathbb{E}\bigl(\mathbb{E}(z^S\mid N)\bigr),
$$

together with multiplicativity of generating functions for independent sums.

The mean formula follows directly from the chain rule applied at $z=1$ and the normalization $f(1)=g(1)=1$. The variance formula follows from direct differentiation combined with the definition in Eq. (13), and algebraic identification of mean and variance terms.

## Notes

The result identifies $h(z)=g(f(z))$ as the probability generating function of a compound distribution. The mean and variance decomposition

$$
\operatorname{var}(h)=\mathbb{E}(N)\operatorname{var}(X)+\operatorname{var}(N)\mathbb{E}(X)^2
$$

is the standard law of total variance expressed in generating-function form.
