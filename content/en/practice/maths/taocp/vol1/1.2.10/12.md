**Exercise 12.** [*HM21*] When $G(z)=p_0+p_1z+p_2z^2+\cdots$ represents a probability distribution, the quantities

$$
M_n = \sum_k k^n p_k
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
m_n = \sum_k (k-M_1)^n p_k
$$

are called the "$n$th moment" and "$n$th central moment," respectively. Show that

$$
G(e^t) = 1 + M_1t + M_2t^2/2! + \cdots;
$$

then use Arbogast's formula (exercise 1.2.5-21) to show that

$$
\kappa_n
= \sum_{k_1,k_2,\ldots,k_n \ge 0}
\frac{(-1)^{k_1+k_2+\cdots+k_n-1}n!(k_1+\cdots+k_n-1)!}
{k_1!1!^{k_1}k_2!2!^{k_2}\cdots k_n!n!^{k_n}}
M_1^{k_1}M_2^{k_2}\cdots M_n^{k_n},
$$

where $k_1+2k_2+\cdots=n$. In particular,

$$
\kappa_1 = M_1,\quad
\kappa_2 = M_2-M_1^2,\quad
\kappa_3 = M_3-3M_1M_2+2M_1^3,
$$

$$
\kappa_4 = M_4 - 4M_1M_3 + 12M_1^2M_2 - 3M_2^2 - 6M_1^4.
$$

What are the analogous expressions for $\kappa_n$ in terms of the central moments $m_2,m_3,\ldots$, when $n \ge 2$?

Let

$$
F(t)=G(e^t)=\sum_{k\ge 0} p_k e^{kt}.
$$

Then, by expanding $e^{kt}$,

$$
F(t)=\sum_{k\ge 0} p_k \sum_{n\ge 0}\frac{k^n t^n}{n!}
=\sum_{n\ge 0}\frac{t^n}{n!}\sum_{k\ge 0}k^n p_k
=\sum_{n\ge 0}\frac{M_n t^n}{n!},
$$

which proves

$$
G(e^t)=1+M_1 t+\frac{M_2 t^2}{2!}+\cdots.
$$

Define the cumulant generating function

$$
K(t)=\log G(e^t).
$$

Then

$$
K(t)=\log\Bigl(1+M_1 t+\frac{M_2 t^2}{2!}+\cdots\Bigr),
\qquad
\kappa_n=K^{(n)}(0).
$$

## 1. Translation invariance of cumulants for $n\ge 2$

Let $X$ be the random variable with $G(e^t)=\mathbb{E}(e^{tX})$. For a constant $a$, define $X'=X-a$. Then

$$
\mathbb{E}(e^{tX'})=\mathbb{E}(e^{t(X-a)})=e^{-at}\mathbb{E}(e^{tX})=e^{-at}G(e^t).
$$

Hence the cumulant generating function becomes

$$
K_{X'}(t)=\log\mathbb{E}(e^{tX'})=\log G(e^t)-at=K_X(t)-at.
$$

Differentiating,

$$
\kappa_1(X')=\kappa_1(X)-a,\qquad
\kappa_n(X')=\kappa_n(X)\quad(n\ge 2),
$$

since the term $-at$ vanishes after one derivative.

Taking $a=M_1$, we obtain a centered variable $Y=X-M_1$ with

$$
\kappa_n(X)=\kappa_n(Y)\quad(n\ge 2),\qquad \mathbb{E}Y=0.
$$

## 2. Moments of the centered variable

For $Y=X-M_1$, define its moments

$$
\mathbb{E}(Y^n)=m_n.
$$

Thus the raw moments of $Y$ are

$$
M^{(Y)}_n = m_n,\qquad M^{(Y)}_1=0.
$$

## 3. Apply Arbogast’s formula to $Y$

From Exercise 1.2.5-21 (Arbogast’s formula), the cumulants in terms of raw moments are

$$
\kappa_n
= \sum_{\substack{k_1,\ldots,k_n\ge 0\\ k_1+2k_2+\cdots+n k_n=n}}
\frac{(-1)^{k_1+\cdots+k_n-1}n!(k_1+\cdots+k_n-1)!}
{k_1!1!^{k_1}k_2!2!^{k_2}\cdots k_n!n!^{k_n}}
\prod_{j=1}^n (M_j)^{k_j}.
$$

Applying this to $Y$, we substitute

$$
M^{(Y)}_1=0,\qquad M^{(Y)}_j=m_j\ (j\ge 2).
$$

Hence every term with $k_1\ge 1$ vanishes, and the constraint reduces to

$$
2k_2+3k_3+\cdots+n k_n=n.
$$

Therefore, for $n\ge 2$,

$$
\boxed{
\kappa_n
=
\sum_{\substack{k_2,\ldots,k_n\ge 0\\ 2k_2+3k_3+\cdots+n k_n=n}}
\frac{(-1)^{k_2+\cdots+k_n-1}n!(k_2+\cdots+k_n-1)!}
{k_2!2!^{k_2}k_3!3!^{k_3}\cdots k_n!n!^{k_n}}
\prod_{j=2}^n m_j^{k_j}.
}
$$

This is the desired expression in central moments.

## 4. Low-order cases

Substituting directly:

- $n=2$:

$$
\kappa_2=m_2.
$$

- $n=3$:

$$
\kappa_3=m_3.
$$

- $n=4$:

$$
\kappa_4=m_4-3m_2^2.
$$

- $n=5$:

$$
\kappa_5=m_5-10m_3m_2.
$$

- $n=6$:

$$
\kappa_6=m_6-15m_4m_2-10m_3^2+30m_2^3.
$$

## Conclusion

For $n\ge 2$, cumulants are obtained by applying Arbogast’s formula to the centered random variable, whose first moment vanishes and whose higher raw moments are exactly the central moments $m_n$. This yields a cumulant expansion involving only $m_2,m_3,\ldots,m_n$, with all $M_1$-terms eliminated rigorously via the exponential generating function shift property.
