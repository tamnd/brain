**Exercise 15.** [*HM23*] When the probability that some quantity has the value $k$ is $e^{-\mu}(\mu^k/k!)$, it is said to have the Poisson distribution with mean $\mu$.

a) What is the generating function for this set of probabilities?

b) What are the values of the semi-invariants?

c) Show that as $n\to\infty$ the Poisson distribution with mean $np$ approaches the normal distribution in the sense of exercise 13.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m24s  

---

## Solution

### a) Generating function

The probability that the quantity has value $k$ is $e^{-\mu}\frac{\mu^k}{k!}$. The generating function is therefore

$$
G(z)=\sum_{k\ge 0} e^{-\mu}\frac{\mu^k}{k!}z^k
= e^{-\mu}\sum_{k\ge 0}\frac{(\mu z)^k}{k!}
= e^{-\mu}e^{\mu z}.
$$

Hence

$$
G(z)=e^{\mu(z-1)}.
$$

Thus

$$
\boxed{G(z)=e^{\mu(z-1)}}.
$$

### b) Semi-invariants

Let $\kappa_n$ denote the semi-invariants defined in Exercise 12, so that they are the coefficients in the expansion of $\log G(e^t)$ in powers of $t$.

From part (a),

$$
G(z)=e^{\mu(z-1)}.
$$

Substituting $z=e^t$ gives

$$
G(e^t)=e^{\mu(e^t-1)}.
$$

Taking logarithms,

$$
\log G(e^t)=\mu(e^t-1).
$$

Expanding $e^t$,

$$
\log G(e^t)=\mu\left(t+\frac{t^2}{2!}+\frac{t^3}{3!}+\cdots\right).
$$

Comparing with the defining expansion of semi-invariants,

$$
\log G(e^t)=\sum_{n\ge 1}\kappa_n \frac{t^n}{n!},
$$

gives

$$
\kappa_n=\mu \quad (n\ge 1).
$$

Thus

$$
\boxed{\kappa_n=\mu \text{ for all } n\ge 1}.
$$

### c) Convergence to the normal distribution

Let the Poisson distribution have mean $\mu=np$. Its generating function is

$$
G(z)=e^{np(z-1)}.
$$

Let

$$
\mu_n=np,\qquad \sigma_n=\sqrt{np}.
$$

The quantity to be studied is

$$
e^{-it\mu_n/\sigma_n}G(e^{it/\sigma_n}).
$$

Substituting,

$$
e^{-it\mu_n/\sigma_n}G(e^{it/\sigma_n})
=
\exp\!\left(-it\frac{np}{\sqrt{np}}\right)
\exp\!\left(np\left(e^{it/\sqrt{np}}-1\right)\right).
$$

Combine exponents:

$$
=\exp\!\left(np\left(e^{it/\sqrt{np}}-1-\frac{it}{\sqrt{np}}\right)\right).
$$

Let $x=\frac{t}{\sqrt{np}}$. Then $x\to 0$ as $n\to\infty$, and

$$
e^{ix}=1+ix-\frac{x^2}{2}+O(x^3).
$$

Hence

$$
e^{ix}-1-ix=-\frac{x^2}{2}+O(x^3).
$$

Substituting back,

$$
np\left(e^{it/\sqrt{np}}-1-\frac{it}{\sqrt{np}}\right)
=
np\left(-\frac{t^2}{2np}+O\!\left(\frac{1}{(np)^{3/2}}\right)\right).
$$

This simplifies to

$$
-\frac{t^2}{2}+o(1).
$$

Therefore,

$$
e^{-it\mu_n/\sigma_n}G(e^{it/\sigma_n})
=
\exp\!\left(-\frac{t^2}{2}+o(1)\right).
$$

Taking the limit as $n\to\infty$ yields

$$
\lim_{n\to\infty} e^{-it\mu_n/\sigma_n}G(e^{it/\sigma_n}) = e^{-t^2/2}.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
