**Exercise 2.** [*HM16*] Derive Eq. (13) from Eq. (10).

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m13s  

---

## Solution

Let

$$
G(z)=\sum_{k} p_k z^k,
\qquad G(1)=\sum_k p_k=1.
$$

From Eq. (10), the variance is defined by

$$
\operatorname{var}(G)=\sum_k k^2 p_k - \left(\sum_k k p_k\right)^2.
$$

From Eq. (12), the mean is

$$
\operatorname{mean}(G)=\sum_k k p_k = G'(1).
$$

The remaining task is to express $\sum_k k^2 p_k$ in terms of derivatives of $G(z)$ at $z=1$.

Differentiating termwise,

$$
G'(z)=\sum_k k p_k z^{k-1},
$$

and differentiating again,

$$
G''(z)=\sum_k k(k-1)p_k z^{k-2}.
$$

Evaluating at $z=1$ gives

$$
G''(1)=\sum_k k(k-1)p_k.
$$

Each term $k^2 p_k$ decomposes as $k^2 = k(k-1)+k$, hence

$$
\sum_k k^2 p_k
= \sum_k k(k-1)p_k + \sum_k k p_k
= G''(1) + G'(1).
$$

Substituting into the definition of variance,

$$
\operatorname{var}(G)
= \left(G''(1)+G'(1)\right) - \left(G'(1)\right)^2.
$$

Thus,

$$
\operatorname{var}(G)=G''(1)+G'(1)-G'(1)^2.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
