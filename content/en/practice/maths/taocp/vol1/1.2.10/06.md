**Exercise 6.** [*HM27*] We've computed the mean and the variance of the important probability distributions (8), (18), (20). What is the third semi-invariant, $\kappa_3$, in each of those cases?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m27s  

---

## Setup

Let $G(z)=\sum_k p_k z^k$ be a probability generating function with $G(1)=1$. The $r$-th semi-invariant $\kappa_r$ is the $r$-th cumulant of the distribution determined by $G$. For the present exercise, $\kappa_1$ and $\kappa_2$ have already been expressed in terms of $G'(1)$ and $G''(1)$.

The task is to compute $\kappa_3$ for the three distributions:

1. $G_n(z)=\dfrac{1}{n!}(z+n-1)(z+n-2)\cdots(z+1)$,
2. $G_n(z)=(q+pz)^n$ with $q=1-p$,
3. $G(z)=\dfrac{1}{n}(z+z^2+\cdots+z^n)$.

## Solution

### 1. Distribution (8)

From Eq. (8),

$$
G_n(z)=\prod_{k=1}^{n-1}\left(\frac{z+k}{k+1}\right).
$$

Each factor has the form

$$
Q_k(z)=1-\frac{1}{k}+\frac{1}{k}z,
$$

so the associated random variable is a sum of independent Bernoulli variables $B_k$ with

$$
\Pr(B_k=1)=\frac{1}{k}, \qquad \Pr(B_k=0)=1-\frac{1}{k}.
$$

Thus $A=\sum_{k=2}^n B_k$, and cumulants add:

$$
\kappa_3(A)=\sum_{k=2}^n \kappa_3(B_k).
$$

For a Bernoulli variable with parameter $p$,

$$
\kappa_3 = p(1-p)(1-2p).
$$

With $p=\frac{1}{k}$,

$$
\kappa_3(B_k)=\frac{1}{k}\left(1-\frac{1}{k}\right)\left(1-\frac{2}{k}\right)
=\frac{(k-1)(k-2)}{k^3}.
$$

Hence

$$
\kappa_3(A)=\sum_{k=1}^n \frac{(k-1)(k-2)}{k^3}.
$$

Expanding,

$$
\frac{(k-1)(k-2)}{k^3}=\frac{k^2-3k+2}{k^3}
=\frac{1}{k}-\frac{3}{k^2}+\frac{2}{k^3}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\kappa_3
= \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{1}{k}
-3\sum_{k=1}^n \frac{1}{k^2}
+2\sum_{k=1}^n \frac{1}{k^3}.
$$

Using harmonic notation,

$$
\kappa_3 = H_n - 3H_n^{(2)} + 2H_n^{(3)}.
$$

### 2. Distribution (18)

From Eq. (18),

$$
G_n(z)=(q+pz)^n.
$$

This is the binomial distribution for the number of successes in $n$ independent trials with success probability $p$. The third cumulant of a sum of independent identically distributed Bernoulli variables adds linearly.

For a single Bernoulli variable,

$$
\kappa_3 = p(1-p)(1-2p)=pqn(1-2p)\ \text{(per trial)},
$$

so for $n$ trials,

$$
\kappa_3 = n p q (1-2p).
$$

### 3. Distribution (20)

From Eq. (20), the distribution is uniform on ${1,2,\ldots,n}$. Its probability generating function is symmetric with respect to reflection about $(n+1)/2$.

For a distribution symmetric about its mean, all odd central moments of order at least $3$ vanish, hence the third cumulant vanishes as well.

Thus,

$$
\kappa_3 = 0.
$$

## Verification

For case (8), each component Bernoulli variable contributes $\frac{(k-1)(k-2)}{k^3}$, and summation over $k=1,\ldots,n$ yields a polynomial combination of harmonic sums $H_n$, $H_n^{(2)}$, and $H_n^{(3)}$, matching the expansion.

For case (18), the binomial distribution is a sum of $n$ independent Bernoulli variables, so the third cumulant scales linearly with $n$, giving $npq(1-2p)$.

For case (20), symmetry about $(n+1)/2$ forces vanishing of all odd cumulants beyond the first.

## Final answers

$$
\boxed{\kappa_3^{(8)} = H_n - 3H_n^{(2)} + 2H_n^{(3)}}
$$

$$
\boxed{\kappa_3^{(18)} = npq(1-2p)}
$$

$$
\boxed{\kappa_3^{(20)} = 0}
$$

∎
