**Exercise 13.** [*HM38*] A sequence of probability generating functions $G_n(z)$ with means $\mu_n$ and deviations $\sigma_n$ is said to approach a normal distribution if

$$
\lim_{n\to\infty} e^{-it\mu_n/\sigma_n}G_n(e^{it/\sigma_n}) = e^{-t^2/2}
$$

for all real values of $t$. Using $G_n(z)$ as given by Eq. (8), show that $G_n(z)$ approaches a normal distribution.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m26s  

---

## Setup

Let

$$
G_n(z)=\prod_{k=2}^{n}\frac{z+k-1}{k}
$$

be the probability generating function in Eq. (8). Write

$$
G_n(e^{it})=\prod_{k=2}^{n}\frac{e^{it}+k-1}{k}.
$$

Let

$$
\mu_n = H_n - 1, \qquad \sigma_n^2 = H_n - H_n^{(2)}
$$

be the mean and variance of the distribution defined by $G_n(z)$, as given earlier in Eq. (14) and Eq. (16).

The required limit is

$$
\lim_{n\to\infty} e^{-it\mu_n/\sigma_n}G_n(e^{it/\sigma_n}) = e^{-t^2/2}
$$

for every real $t$.

It suffices to analyze

$$
\log\left(e^{-it\mu_n/\sigma_n}G_n(e^{it/\sigma_n})\right)
= -\frac{it\mu_n}{\sigma_n} + \sum_{k=2}^{n}\log\left(1+\frac{e^{it/\sigma_n}-1}{k}\right).
$$

## Solution

Set

$$
u_n = \frac{it}{\sigma_n}, \qquad x_{k,n} = \frac{e^{\nu_n}-1}{k}.
$$

Then

$$
\log\left(e^{-it\mu_n/\sigma_n}G_n(e^{it/\sigma_n})\right)
= -\frac{it\mu_n}{\sigma_n} + \sum_{k=2}^{n}\log(1+x_{k,n}).
$$

For fixed $t$ and large $n$, one has $\nu_n \to 0$, hence

$$
e^{\nu_n}-1 = \nu_n + \frac{\nu_n^2}{2} + O(\nu_n^3).
$$

This yields

$$
x_{k,n} = \frac{\nu_n}{k} + \frac{\nu_n^2}{2k} + O\!\left(\frac{|\nu_n|^3}{k}\right).
$$

Since $|x_{k,n}| \le C/(k\sigma_n)$ for sufficiently large $n$, and $\sigma_n \to \infty$, the bound $|x_{k,n}| \to 0$ uniformly in $k$.

Use the expansion

$$
\log(1+x)=x-\frac{x^2}{2}+O(x^3),
$$

applied termwise:

$$
\sum_{k=2}^{n}\log(1+x_{k,n})
= \sum_{k=2}^{n}x_{k,n} - \frac12\sum_{k=2}^{n}x_{k,n}^2 + \sum_{k=2}^{n}O(x_{k,n}^3).
$$

### First-order term

Substitute $x_{k,n}$:

$$
\sum_{k=2}^{n}x_{k,n}
= (e^{\nu_n}-1)\sum_{k=2}^{n}\frac{1}{k}.
$$

Since $\sum_{k=2}^{n} \frac{1}{k}=\mu_n$, this becomes

$$
\sum_{k=2}^{n}x_{k,n} = (e^{\nu_n}-1)\mu_n.
$$

Expanding $e^{\nu_n}-1$,

$$
(e^{\nu_n}-1)\mu_n
= \left(\nu_n + \frac{\nu_n^2}{2} + O(\nu_n^3)\right)\mu_n.
$$

Thus

$$
\sum_{k=2}^{n}x_{k,n}
= \frac{it\mu_n}{\sigma_n} - \frac{t^2\mu_n}{2\sigma_n^2} + O\!\left(\frac{\mu_n}{\sigma_n^3}\right).
$$

### Cancellation of linear term

Insert into the full logarithm:

$$
-\frac{it\mu_n}{\sigma_n} + \sum_{k=2}^{n}x_{k,n}
= -\frac{t^2\mu_n}{2\sigma_n^2} + O\!\left(\frac{\mu_n}{\sigma_n^3}\right).
$$

### Second-order term

Now

$$
\sum_{k=2}^{n}x_{k,n}^2
= (e^{\nu_n}-1)^2 \sum_{k=2}^{n}\frac{1}{k^2}.
$$

Since $\sum_{k=2}^{n}1/k^2 \to H_n^{(2)}-1$ and is bounded, while $(e^{\nu_n}-1)^2 = O(1/\sigma_n^2)$, we obtain

$$
\sum_{k=2}^{n}x_{k,n}^2 = O\!\left(\frac{1}{\sigma_n^2}\right).
$$

Hence

$$
-\frac12\sum_{k=2}^{n}x_{k,n}^2 = O\!\left(\frac{1}{\sigma_n^2}\right).
$$

### Remainder term

Since $|x_{k,n}| \le C/(k\sigma_n)$,

$$
\sum_{k=2}^{n} |x_{k,n}|^3 \le \frac{C^3}{\sigma_n^3}\sum_{k=2}^{n}\frac{1}{k^3},
$$

and the series $\sum 1/k^3$ converges, hence this term is

$$
O\!\left(\frac{1}{\sigma_n^3}\right).
$$

### Assembly

Collecting terms,

$$
\log\left(e^{-it\mu_n/\sigma_n}G_n(e^{it/\sigma_n})\right)
= -\frac{t^2\mu_n}{2\sigma_n^2} + o(1).
$$

Since

$$
\sigma_n^2 = H_n - H_n^{(2)} = H_n - \frac{\pi^2}{6} + o(1),
$$

and

$$
\mu_n = H_n - 1,
$$

it follows that

$$
\frac{\mu_n}{\sigma_n^2} \to 1.
$$

Therefore

$$
\log\left(e^{-it\mu_n/\sigma_n}G_n(e^{it/\sigma_n})\right)
\to -\frac{t^2}{2}.
$$

Exponentiating yields

$$
\lim_{n\to\infty} e^{-it\mu_n/\sigma_n}G_n(e^{it/\sigma_n})
= e^{-t^2/2}.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification

The expansion is valid since $\max_k |x_{k,n}| \le C/\sigma_n \to 0$, which guarantees uniform control of $\log(1+x)$ remainders.

The linear term cancels exactly with $e^{-it\mu_n/\sigma_n}$ because $\sum_{k=2}^{n}1/k=\mu_n$.

The quadratic contribution matches the normalization since

$$
\sum_{k=2}^{n}\frac{1}{k^2} = H_n^{(2)} + O(1),
$$

and $\sigma_n^2 = H_n - H_n^{(2)}$ grows without bound while retaining the same leading harmonic growth as $\mu_n$.

All higher-order contributions vanish after scaling by $\sigma_n$.

## Notes

The structure is that of a triangular array of independent contributions encoded in the product representation $G_n(z)=\prod Q_k(z)$. Each factor contributes mean $1/k$ and variance $1/k - 1/k^2$, and the normalization by $\sigma_n$ enforces convergence of the log generating function to the quadratic exponent $-t^2/2$, which is the characteristic function of the standard normal distribution.
