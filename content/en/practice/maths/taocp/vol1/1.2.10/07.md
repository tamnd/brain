**Exercise 7.** [*M27*] In our analysis of Algorithm M, we assumed that all the $X[k]$ were distinct. Suppose, instead, that we make only the weaker assumption that $X[1],X[2],\ldots,X[n]$ contain precisely $m$ distinct values; the values are otherwise random, subject to this constraint. What is the probability distribution of $A$ in this case?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m44s  

---

## Setup

Let $X[1],X[2],\ldots,X[n]$ be a random sequence containing exactly $m$ distinct values. Assume the values are totally ordered and that all sequences consistent with this constraint are equally likely.

Algorithm M produces a quantity $A$, the number of times the current maximum is changed during the scan of the sequence. Equivalently, $A$ is the number of indices $i \ge 2$ such that

$$
X[i] > \max_{1 \le j < i} X[j].
$$

We seek the probability distribution

$$
p^{(m)}_{nk} = \mathbb{P}(A = k).
$$

## Solution

Consider the sequence $X[1],\ldots,X[n]$ and compress it by deleting all occurrences of a value after its first appearance. Let $Y[1],\ldots,Y[m]$ be the resulting sequence of the $m$ distinct values in the order of their first appearance.

Each time a new distinct value appears in $X$, its first occurrence appears in $Y$, and no later occurrences affect whether it is a new maximum. Hence the value of $A$ depends only on the relative order of first occurrences of the $m$ distinct values, not on their multiplicities or on their positions after first appearance.

The sequence $Y[1],\ldots,Y[m]$ is a permutation of the $m$ distinct values. To see that this permutation is uniform, observe that every assignment of positions to the $m$ labels that yields exactly one first occurrence per label contributes equally under the assumed symmetry of the model, and relabeling the $m$ values preserves the set of admissible sequences. Therefore each of the $m!$ possible orders of first appearances occurs with equal probability.

Now Algorithm M applied to the reduced sequence $Y[1],\ldots,Y[m]$ is exactly the same as Algorithm M applied to a random permutation of $m$ distinct elements. Consequently the distribution of $A$ is identical to the distribution already derived in Section 1.2.10 for permutations of size $m$.

From equation (9) in Section 1.2.10, the probability that $A=k$ for a random permutation of $m$ elements is

$$
p^{(m)}_{mk} = \frac{\left[{m \atop k+1}\right]}{m!}.
$$

Since $A$ depends only on the induced permutation of the $m distinct values, the same expression holds for the original length-$n$ sequence:

$$
p^{(m)}_{nk} = \frac{\left[{m \atop k+1}\right]}{m!}, \quad 0 \le k \le m-1,
$$

and $p^{(m)}_{nk} = 0$ otherwise.

Thus the distribution of $A$ depends only on $m$ and coincides with the distribution for Algorithm M on a random permutation of $m$ elements.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification

The reduction step preserves $A$ because Algorithm M increments the current maximum exactly at first occurrences of record-high values, and duplicate occurrences never exceed a previously seen maximum.

The induced order of first appearances is uniform over all $m!$ permutations by symmetry under relabeling of the $m$ distinct values, so no ordering bias is introduced.

The mapping from sequences to permutations of first occurrences is many-to-one but class-preserving with respect to $A$, ensuring that aggregation over all preimages yields the same distribution as the uniform permutation model of size $m$.

## Notes

The result shows that multiplicities of repeated values do not affect the record structure of Algorithm M. Only the induced order of first appearances of distinct values matters, which collapses the problem to the classical permutation case of size $m$.
