**Exercise 11.** [*M15*] What happens to the semi-invariants of a distribution if we change $G(z)$ to $F(z)=z^nG(z)$?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m46s  

---

## Solution

Let $G(z)=\sum_k p_k z^k$ be the probability generating function of a distribution for a random variable $A$. The transformation

$$
F(z)=z^n G(z)
$$

defines a new generating function

$$
F(z)=\sum_k p_k z^{k+n}.
$$

Hence the new distribution assigns probability $p_k$ to the value $k+n$, so the associated random variable is

$$
A' = A + n.
$$

Let the semi-invariants of a distribution be defined in terms of the cumulant generating function

$$
K(t)=\log M(t), \qquad M(t)=G(e^t),
$$

so that the $r$th semi-invariant is $K^{(r)}(0)$.

For the transformed distribution,

$$
M_F(t)=F(e^t)=e^{nt}G(e^t)=e^{nt}M(t).
$$

Taking logarithms,

$$
K_F(t)=\log M_F(t)=\log(e^{nt}M(t))=nt+\log M(t)=nt+K(t).
$$

Differentiating, the first derivative satisfies

$$
K_F'(t)=n+K'(t),
$$

so at $t=0$,

$$
K_F'(0)=n+K'(0).
$$

For $r\ge 2$, differentiation yields

$$
K_F^{(r)}(t)=K^{(r)}(t),
$$

since the term $nt$ contributes only to the first derivative and vanishes under higher derivatives. Hence, at $t=0$,

$$
K_F^{(r)}(0)=K^{(r)}(0), \qquad r\ge 2.
$$

Thus only the first semi-invariant changes, increasing by $n$, while all higher semi-invariants remain unchanged.

This completes the proof. ∎
