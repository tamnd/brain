**Exercise 10.** [*M20*] Combine the results of the preceding three exercises to obtain a formula for the probability that $A=k$ under the assumption that each $X$ is selected at random from a set of $M$ objects.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m  

---

Let $X[1],\ldots,X[n]$ be independent uniform draws from a set of $M$ objects. Let $A$ be the number of executions of step M4 in Algorithm M.

We condition on the number $m$ of distinct values occurring among $X[1],\ldots,X[n]$.

## 1. Conditioning on $m$ distinct values

Fix $m$. First choose the set $S$ of distinct values. Every $m$-subset $S \subseteq [M]$ is equally likely, so we condition on a fixed set $S$ with $|S|=m$.

Under this condition, all sequences $(x_1,\ldots,x_n)\in S^n$ in which every element of $S$ appears at least once are equally likely.

## 2. Induced permutation structure

For any such sequence, define the _first-occurrence permutation_ $\pi$ of $S$ as follows: list the elements of $S$ in the order in which they first appear in the sequence.

This produces a bijection:

- each valid sequence induces a unique permutation $\pi \in S_m$,
- conversely, fixing $\pi$, we may consider all sequences whose first appearances occur in that order.

We now show that, conditioned on “all $m$ symbols appear at least once”, the induced permutation $\pi$ is uniform over the $m!$ permutations.

Fix a permutation $\pi = (a_1,\ldots,a_m)$ of $S$. Count sequences in $S^n$ for which the first appearance order is exactly $\pi$. In such sequences:

- there exist indices $1 \le t_1 < \cdots < t_m \le n$ such that $X[t_i] = a_i$,
- no $a_j$ appears before $t_j$,
- all other positions are arbitrary elements of $S$, subject only to these first-occurrence constraints.

Now observe the key symmetry: if we apply any permutation of $S$ to a sequence, the set of valid sequences is invariant, and the property “first-occurrence order equals $\pi$” is carried bijectively to “first-occurrence order equals $\sigma(\pi)$”. Hence every permutation $\pi$ is realized by the same number of sequences.

Therefore, conditional on the event that all $m$ symbols appear, the first-occurrence permutation $\pi$ is uniform on $S_m$.

## 3. Reduction to the permutation model

Algorithm M depends only on the comparison structure of the distinct elements, hence only on their relative order as revealed by first occurrences. Consequently, once we condition on $m$ distinct values, the induced randomness relevant to $A$ is exactly that of a uniform random permutation of $m$ distinct objects.

Therefore,

$$
\mathbb{P}(A=k \mid m \text{ distinct}) = p_{mk}.
$$

From Eq. (9) in the text,

$$
p_{mk} = \frac{\left[{m \atop k+1}\right]}{m!}.
$$

## 4. Distribution of $m$

The probability that exactly $m$ distinct values occur is obtained by standard occupancy counting:

$$
\mathbb{P}(m \text{ distinct})
= \frac{\binom{M}{m} m!\, S(n,m)}{M^n},
$$

where $S(n,m)$ is a Stirling number of the second kind.

## 5. Combine by total probability

By conditioning on $m$,

$$
\mathbb{P}(A=k)
= \sum_{m=1}^{\min(M,n)} \mathbb{P}(A=k \mid m)\,\mathbb{P}(m \text{ distinct}).
$$

Substitute the expressions:

$$
\mathbb{P}(A=k)
= \sum_{m=1}^{\min(M,n)}
\frac{\left[{m \atop k+1}\right]}{m!}
\cdot
\frac{\binom{M}{m} m!\, S(n,m)}{M^n}.
$$

Cancel $m!$:

$$
\mathbb{P}(A=k)
= \frac{1}{M^n}
\sum_{m=1}^{\min(M,n)}
\binom{M}{m} S(n,m)\left[{m \atop k+1}\right].
$$

## Final result

$$
\boxed{
\mathbb{P}(A=k)
= \frac{1}{M^n}
\sum_{m=1}^{\min(M,n)}
\binom{M}{m}\, S(n,m)\,\left[{m \atop k+1}\right]
}.
$$

This completes the corrected derivation.
