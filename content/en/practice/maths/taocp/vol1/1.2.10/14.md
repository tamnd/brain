**Exercise 14.** [*HM30*] (A. de Moivre.) Using the conventions of the previous exercise, show that the binomial distribution $G_n(z)$ given by Eq. (18) approaches the normal distribution.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m40s  

---

## Setup

Let $G_n(z) = (q + pz)^n$ be the generating function of the binomial distribution, where $p+q=1$, $q=1-p$, $0<p<1$.

From the previous analysis, the mean and variance are

$$
\mu_n = pn, \qquad \sigma_n^2 = pqn, \qquad \sigma_n = \sqrt{pqn}.
$$

We must show that

$$
\lim_{n\to\infty} e^{-it\mu_n/\sigma_n} G_n(e^{it/\sigma_n}) = e^{-t^2/2}
$$

for every real $t$.

Substituting $G_n(z)$ gives the equivalent form

$$
e^{-it pn/\sigma_n}\,(q + p e^{it/\sigma_n})^n.
$$

The goal is to evaluate the limit of this expression.

## Solution

Let

$$
z_n = e^{it/\sigma_n}.
$$

Then

$$
(q + pz_n)^n = \exp\!\bigl(n \log(q + pz_n)\bigr).
$$

The quantity to study becomes

$$
\exp\!\left(n \log(q + p e^{it/\sigma_n}) - \frac{itpn}{\sigma_n}\right).
$$

Write

$$
e^{it/\sigma_n} = 1 + \frac{it}{\sigma_n} - \frac{t^2}{2\sigma_n^2} + O(\sigma_n^{-3}).
$$

Multiplying by $p$ and adding $q=1-p$ gives

$$
q + p e^{it/\sigma_n}
= 1 + \frac{pit}{\sigma_n} - \frac{pt^2}{2\sigma_n^2} + O(\sigma_n^{-3}).
$$

Let

$$
u_n = \frac{pit}{\sigma_n} - \frac{pt^2}{2\sigma_n^2} + O(\sigma_n^{-3}),
$$

so that

$$
q + p e^{it/\sigma_n} = 1 + u_n.
$$

Using the expansion $\log(1+u)=u-\frac{u^2}{2}+O(u^3)$ gives

$$
\log(1+u_n)
= u_n - \frac{u_n^2}{2} + O(\sigma_n^{-3}).
$$

Since $u_n = O(\sigma_n^{-1})$, the square satisfies

$$
u_n^2 = \frac{p^2 i^2 t^2}{\sigma_n^2} + O(\sigma_n^{-3})
= -\frac{p^2 t^2}{\sigma_n^2} + O(\sigma_n^{-3}).
$$

Substituting into the logarithm,

$$
\log(1+u_n)
= \frac{pit}{\sigma_n}
- \frac{pt^2}{2\sigma_n^2}
+ \frac{p^2 t^2}{2\sigma_n^2}
+ O(\sigma_n^{-3}).
$$

Combine the quadratic terms:

$$
- \frac{pt^2}{2\sigma_n^2} + \frac{p^2 t^2}{2\sigma_n^2}
= -\frac{pq\,t^2}{2\sigma_n^2}.
$$

Hence

$$
\log(q + p e^{it/\sigma_n})
= \frac{pit}{\sigma_n} - \frac{pq\,t^2}{2\sigma_n^2} + O(\sigma_n^{-3}).
$$

Multiply by $n$:

$$
n \log(q + p e^{it/\sigma_n})
= \frac{pitn}{\sigma_n} - \frac{pq\,t^2 n}{2\sigma_n^2} + O(n\sigma_n^{-3}).
$$

Now $\sigma_n^2=pqn$, so

$$
\frac{n}{\sigma_n^2} = \frac{1}{pq}, \qquad n\sigma_n^{-3} = O(n^{-1/2}).
$$

Thus

$$
n \log(q + p e^{it/\sigma_n})
= \frac{pitn}{\sigma_n} - \frac{t^2}{2} + o(1).
$$

Subtract the centering term:

$$
\frac{pitn}{\sigma_n} - \frac{itpn}{\sigma_n} = 0.
$$

Therefore the exponent becomes

$$
-\frac{t^2}{2} + o(1).
$$

Taking exponentials yields

$$
e^{-it pn/\sigma_n}(q + p e^{it/\sigma_n})^n
= \exp\!\left(-\frac{t^2}{2} + o(1)\right).
$$

Passing to the limit gives

$$
\lim_{n\to\infty} e^{-it\mu_n/\sigma_n} G_n(e^{it/\sigma_n})
= e^{-t^2/2}.
$$

This completes the proof. âˆŽ

## Verification

The cancellation of the linear term follows from matching

$$
\mu_n = pn, \qquad \sigma_n = \sqrt{pqn},
$$

so the shift factor exactly removes the first-order contribution in the logarithmic expansion.

The quadratic term is determined by the second-order expansion of $\log(1+u)$ together with $\sigma_n^2=pqn$, which converts the coefficient into $-t^2/2$ independently of $p$ and $q$.

All remaining terms are of order $O(n^{-1/2})$ in the exponent and vanish in the limit.

## Notes

The same method applies to any distribution whose generating function has a quadratic logarithmic expansion near $z=1$. The binomial case is distinguished by the exact identity $G_n(z)=(q+pz)^n$, which makes the central limit behavior explicit without additional combinatorial arguments.
