**Exercise 6.** [*HM30*] Show that Stirling's approximation holds for noninteger $n$ as well:

$$
\Gamma(x+1)
= \sqrt{2\pi x}\left(\frac{x}{e}\right)^x
\left(1+O\!\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)\right),
\qquad x \ge a > 0.
$$

We restart the argument using a representation of $\Gamma$ that avoids any delicate extraction from the digamma kernel. The clean TAOCP-style approach is to work directly with Euler’s integral and perform a Laplace-type analysis.

# Corrected Solution

We prove that for $x \ge a > 0$,

$$
\Gamma(x+1)
= \sqrt{2\pi x}\left(\frac{x}{e}\right)^x
\left(1+O\!\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)\right).
$$

We use the integral representation

$$
\Gamma(x+1) = \int_0^\infty t^x e^{-t}\,dt.
$$

## 1. Scaling and normalization

Let $t = x(1+u)$, so $dt = x\,du$. Then

$$
\Gamma(x+1)
= \int_0^\infty (x(1+u))^x e^{-x(1+u)} x\,du.
$$

Factor out the main growth:

$$
(x(1+u))^x e^{-x(1+u)}
= x^x e^{-x} (1+u)^x e^{-xu}.
$$

Hence

$$
\Gamma(x+1)
= x^{x+1} e^{-x} \int_{-1}^{\infty} (1+u)^x e^{-xu}\,du.
$$

Now set

$$
\Gamma(x+1)
= x^x e^{-x} x \int_{-1}^{\infty} \exp\!\big(x\log(1+u) - xu\big)\,du.
$$

Define the phase function

$$
\phi(u) = \log(1+u) - u.
$$

Then

$$
\Gamma(x+1)
= x^x e^{-x} x \int_{-1}^{\infty} e^{x\phi(u)}\,du.
$$

## 2. Localization near the maximum

We analyze $\phi(u)$. We compute:

$$
\phi(0) = 0,\quad \phi'(u) = \frac{1}{1+u} - 1,\quad \phi'(0)=0,
$$

$$
\phi''(u) = -\frac{1}{(1+u)^2},\quad \phi''(0) = -1.
$$

Thus $u=0$ is a unique maximum and

$$
\phi(u) = -\frac{u^2}{2} + O(u^3)
\quad (u \to 0).
$$

Split the integral into

$$
\int_{-1}^{\infty} = \int_{|u|\le x^{-2/3}} + \int_{|u|> x^{-2/3}}.
$$

## 3. Negligible tail

For $|u| \ge x^{-2/3}$, Taylor’s inequality gives

$$
\phi(u) \le -c u^2
$$

for some $c>0$ in a neighborhood of 0, and outside that neighborhood $\phi(u)\le -c_0<0$.

Hence

$$
\int_{|u|>x^{-2/3}} e^{x\phi(u)}\,du
\le e^{-c x^{1/3}},
$$

so the tail contributes

$$
O(e^{-c x^{1/3}}),
$$

which is negligible compared to any power of $1/x$.

## 4. Main Gaussian contribution

On $|u|\le x^{-2/3}$, expand:

$$
\phi(u) = -\frac{u^2}{2} + O(u^3).
$$

Thus

$$
x\phi(u) = -\frac{x u^2}{2} + O(xu^3).
$$

Since $u = O(x^{-2/3})$,

$$
xu^3 = O(x \cdot x^{-2}) = O(x^{-1}),
$$

so

$$
e^{x\phi(u)} = e^{-xu^2/2}\left(1 + O\!\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)\right).
$$

Therefore

$$
\int_{|u|\le x^{-2/3}} e^{x\phi(u)}\,du
= \left(1+O\!\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)\right)
\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-xu^2/2}\,du.
$$

(The extension of limits introduces only exponentially small error.)

Compute the Gaussian integral:

$$
\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-xu^2/2}\,du
= \sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{x}}.
$$

Hence

$$
\int_{-1}^{\infty} e^{x\phi(u)}\,du
= \sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{x}}\left(1+O\!\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)\right).
$$

## 5. Assemble the asymptotic form

Substitute back:

$$
\Gamma(x+1)
= x^x e^{-x} x \cdot \sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{x}}\left(1+O\!\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)\right).
$$

Simplify:

$$
x \sqrt{\frac{1}{x}} = \sqrt{x}.
$$

Thus

$$
\Gamma(x+1)
= \sqrt{2\pi x}\, x^x e^{-x}\left(1+O\!\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)\right).
$$

Rewriting $x^x e^{-x} = \left(\frac{x}{e}\right)^x$,

$$
\Gamma(x+1)
= \sqrt{2\pi x}\left(\frac{x}{e}\right)^x
\left(1+O\!\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)\right).
$$

## 6. Uniformity for $x \ge a > 0$

All estimates used depend only on local Taylor bounds for $\phi(u)$ near $0$ and global negativity away from $0$. These bounds are uniform on any interval $x \ge a > 0$. The error term $O(1/x)$ is therefore uniform in that range.

## Conclusion

By a direct Laplace method applied to Euler’s integral for $\Gamma(x+1)$, we obtain

$$
\Gamma(x+1)
= \sqrt{2\pi x}\left(\frac{x}{e}\right)^x
\left(1+O\!\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)\right),
\quad x \ge a > 0,
$$

which extends Stirling’s approximation from integers to all real (and hence noninteger) arguments.
