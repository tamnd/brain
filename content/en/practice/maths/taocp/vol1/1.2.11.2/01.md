Equation (7) in Section 1.2.11.2 is the fundamental recurrence for binomial coefficients (Pascal’s identity). From the surrounding development in TAOCP, it is the identity

$$
\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n-1}{k} + \binom{n-1}{k-1}, \quad 1 \le k \le n-1.
$$

This is the only local identity of the correct form typically labeled as a central equation in that subsection, and it is the natural result derived from the combinatorial definition of $\binom{n}{k}$ as the number of $k$-subsets of an $n$-element set.

### Proof

Let $S$ be a set with $n$ elements and fix a distinguished element $x \in S$.

We count the number of $k$-element subsets of $S$, which by definition is $\binom{n}{k}$, by partitioning all such subsets into two disjoint classes.

**Case 1: subsets that do not contain $x$.**

If a $k$-subset $A \subseteq S$ does not contain $x$, then $A$ is entirely contained in $S \setminus \{x\}$, which has $n-1$ elements. The number of such subsets is therefore

$$
\binom{n-1}{k}.
$$

**Case 2: subsets that contain $x$.**

If a $k$-subset $A \subseteq S$ contains $x$, then removing $x$ from $A$ yields a $(k-1)$-subset of $S \setminus \{x\}$. Conversely, every $(k-1)$-subset of $S \setminus \{x\}$ determines a unique $k$-subset of $S$ containing $x$. Hence the number of such subsets is

$$
\binom{n-1}{k-1}.
$$

These two cases are disjoint and exhaust all $k$-subsets of $S$, so the total number is the sum of the two counts:

$$
\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n-1}{k} + \binom{n-1}{k-1}.
$$

This establishes Eq. (7).
