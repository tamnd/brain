**Exercise 9.** [*M25*] Find the asymptotic value of $\binom{2n}{n}$ with a relative error of $O(n^{-3})$, in two ways.

*1.2.11.3. Some asymptotic calculations.* In this subsection we shall investigate the following three intriguing sums, in order to deduce their approximate values:

$$
P(n) = 1 + \frac{n-1}{n} + \frac{(n-2)^2}{n(n-1)} + \cdots
= \sum_{k=0}^{n} \frac{(n-k)^k (n-k)!}{n!}, \tag{1}
$$

$$
Q(n) = 1 + \frac{n-1}{n} + \frac{n-2}{n}\frac{n-1}{n} + \cdots
= \sum_{k=1}^{n} \frac{n!}{(n-k)!n^k}, \tag{2}
$$

$$
R(n) = 1 + \frac{n}{n+1} + \frac{n}{n+1}\frac{n}{n+2} + \cdots
= \sum_{k\ge 0} \frac{n!n^k}{(n+k)!}. \tag{3}
$$

Both $P(n)$ and $Q(n)$ are finite sums, while $R(n)$ is an infinite sum. It seems that when $n$ is large, all three sums will be nearly equal, although it is not obvious what the approximate value of any of them will be.

First, we observe an important connection between $Q(n)$ and $R(n)$:

$$
Q(n)+R(n)
= \frac{n!e^n}{n^n}. \tag{4}
$$

Stirling's formula tells us that $n!e^n/n^n$ is approximately $\sqrt{2\pi n}$, so we can guess that $Q(n)$ and $R(n)$ will each turn out to be roughly equal to $\sqrt{\pi n/2}$.

## Solution

Let

$$
\binom{2n}{n}=\frac{(2n)!}{(n!)^2}.
$$

### First method: direct use of Stirling expansions

Use Stirling’s expansion in the form

$$
n! = \sqrt{2\pi n}\left(\frac{n}{e}\right)^n \left(1+\frac{1}{12n}+\frac{1}{288n^2}+O(n^{-3})\right).
$$

Substituting $2n$ gives

$$
(2n)! = \sqrt{4\pi n}\left(\frac{2n}{e}\right)^{2n}
\left(1+\frac{1}{24n}+\frac{1}{1152n^2}+O(n^{-3})\right).
$$

For $(n!)^2$, first square the expansion of $n!$:

$$
(1+\tfrac{1}{12n}+\tfrac{1}{288n^2})^2
=1+\frac{1}{6n}+\frac{1}{72n^2}+O(n^{-3}),
$$

since the coefficient of $n^{-2}$ is

$$
2\cdot \frac{1}{288}+\left(\frac{1}{12}\right)^2=\frac{1}{144}+\frac{1}{144}=\frac{1}{72}.
$$

Hence

$$
(n!)^2 = 2\pi n \left(\frac{n}{e}\right)^{2n}
\left(1+\frac{1}{6n}+\frac{1}{72n^2}+O(n^{-3})\right).
$$

Form the quotient:

$$
\binom{2n}{n}
=
\frac{\sqrt{4\pi n}}{2\pi n}\cdot \frac{(2n)^{2n}}{n^{2n}}
\cdot
\frac{1+\frac{1}{24n}+\frac{1}{1152n^2}+O(n^{-3})}{1+\frac{1}{6n}+\frac{1}{72n^2}+O(n^{-3})}.
$$

The prefactor simplifies to

$$
\frac{\sqrt{4\pi n}}{2\pi n}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi n}},
\qquad
\frac{(2n)^{2n}}{n^{2n}}=4^n,
$$

so

$$
\binom{2n}{n}= \frac{4^n}{\sqrt{\pi n}} \cdot S(n),
$$

where $S(n)$ is the quotient of the two parenthesized series.

To expand $S(n)$, first invert the denominator:

$$
\frac{1}{1+\frac{1}{6n}+\frac{1}{72n^2}}
=
1-\frac{1}{6n}+\frac{1}{72n^2}+O(n^{-3}),
$$

since the coefficient of $n^{-2}$ is

$$
\left(\frac{1}{6}\right)^2-\frac{1}{72}=\frac{1}{36}-\frac{1}{72}=\frac{1}{72}.
$$

Multiply with the numerator:

$$
\left(1+\frac{1}{24n}+\frac{1}{1152n^2}\right)
\left(1-\frac{1}{6n}+\frac{1}{72n^2}\right).
$$

The coefficient of $n^{-1}$ is

$$
\frac{1}{24}-\frac{1}{6}=-\frac{1}{8}.
$$

The coefficient of $n^{-2}$ is

$$
\frac{1}{1152}-\frac{1}{144}+\frac{1}{72}
=
\frac{1-8+16}{1152}
=
\frac{9}{1152}
=
\frac{1}{128}.
$$

Thus

$$
S(n)=1-\frac{1}{8n}+\frac{1}{128n^2}+O(n^{-3}).
$$

Therefore

$$
\binom{2n}{n}
=
\frac{4^n}{\sqrt{\pi n}}
\left(1-\frac{1}{8n}+\frac{1}{128n^2}+O(n^{-3})\right).
$$

### Second method: logarithmic expansion

Start from

$$
\ln \binom{2n}{n}=\ln(2n)!-2\ln(n!).
$$

Use Stirling’s logarithmic form derived from Euler’s summation formula:

$$
\ln(n!) = n\ln n - n + \frac{1}{2}\ln(2\pi n) + \frac{1}{12n}+O(n^{-3}).
$$

Apply this to $2n$:

$$
\ln(2n)! = 2n\ln(2n)-2n+\frac{1}{2}\ln(4\pi n)+\frac{1}{24n}+O(n^{-3}).
$$

Subtracting twice the expression for $\ln(n!)$ gives

$$
\ln \binom{2n}{n}
=
2n\ln 2
-\frac{1}{2}\ln(\pi n)
-\frac{1}{8n}
+O(n^{-3}).
$$

Exponentiating,

$$
\binom{2n}{n}
=
\exp(2n\ln 2)\cdot \exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{2}\ln(\pi n)\right)\cdot \exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{8n}+O(n^{-3})\right).
$$

Each factor simplifies:

$$
\exp(2n\ln 2)=4^n,\qquad
\exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{2}\ln(\pi n)\right)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi n}}.
$$

Expanding the remaining exponential,

$$
\exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{8n}+O(n^{-3})\right)
=
1-\frac{1}{8n}+\frac{1}{128n^2}+O(n^{-3}),
$$

since the square of $-1/(8n)$ contributes $1/(128n^2)$ and no term of order $n^{-2}$ appears in the exponent itself.

Combining terms yields

$$
\binom{2n}{n}
=
\frac{4^n}{\sqrt{\pi n}}
\left(1-\frac{1}{8n}+\frac{1}{128n^2}+O(n^{-3})\right).
$$

This completes the derivation in both methods. ∎
