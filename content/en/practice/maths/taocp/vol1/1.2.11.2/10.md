[Section 1.2.11.2: Euler's Summation Formula](../)

**Exercise 10.** [*HM22*] Make a statement similar to that in exercise 9 about $\ln(1+O(z^m))$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m10s  

---

## Solution

Let $u(z)=O(z^m)$ as $z\to 0$. By definition, there exist constants $C>0$ and $z_0>0$ such that $|u(z)|\le C|z|^m$ for all $|z|\le z_0$. In particular, $u(z)\to 0$ as $z\to 0$.

For $u\to 0$, Euler’s expansion for the logarithm gives

$$
\ln(1+u)=u+O(u^2),
$$

since the Taylor expansion of $\ln(1+u)$ at $u=0$ has first term $u$ and remainder bounded by a constant multiple of $u^2$ in a neighborhood of $0$.

Substituting $u(z)$ yields

$$
\ln(1+u(z)) = u(z) + O(u(z)^2).
$$

Because $u(z)=O(z^m)$, there exists $C_1>0$ such that $|u(z)|\le C_1|z|^m$ for sufficiently small $z$. Hence

$$
u(z)^2 = O(z^{2m}).
$$

Therefore,

$$
\ln(1+O(z^m)) = O(z^m) + O(z^{2m}).
$$

Since $z^{2m}=o(z^m)$ as $z\to 0$, the combined order reduces to

$$
\ln(1+O(z^m)) = O(z^m).
$$

$$
\boxed{\ln(1+O(z^m)) = O(z^m)}
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
