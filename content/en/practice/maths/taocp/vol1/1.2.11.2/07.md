**Exercise 7.** [*HM32*] What is the approximate value of $1^1 2^2 3^3 \cdots n^n$?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m53s  

---

## Setup

Let

$$
P_n = 1^1 2^2 3^3 \cdots n^n.
$$

Taking logarithms,

$$
\ln P_n = \sum_{k=1}^n k \ln k.
$$

Define

$$
f(x) = x \ln x,
$$

so that

$$
\ln P_n = \sum_{k=1}^n f(k).
$$

The task is to determine an asymptotic expression for $P_n$ as $n \to \infty$.

## Solution

Eulerâ€™s summation formula in its first terms gives, for a sufficiently smooth $f$,

$$
\sum_{k=1}^n f(k)
= \int_1^n f(x)\,dx + \frac{f(n)+f(1)}{2}
+ \frac{1}{12}\bigl(f'(n)-f'(1)\bigr)
+ O\!\left(\int_1^n |f''(x)|\,dx\right).
$$

For $f(x)=x\ln x$, we compute

$$
f'(x)=\ln x + 1, \qquad f''(x)=\frac{1}{x}.
$$

### Integral term

$$
\int x \ln x \, dx = \frac{x^2}{2}\ln x - \frac{x^2}{4}.
$$

Hence

$$
\int_1^n x\ln x\,dx
= \frac{n^2}{2}\ln n - \frac{n^2}{4} + O(1).
$$

### Endpoint term

$$
\frac{f(n)+f(1)}{2}
= \frac{n\ln n}{2} + O(1).
$$

### Derivative correction

$$
\frac{1}{12}(f'(n)-f'(1))
= \frac{1}{12}(\ln n + 1 - 1)
= \frac{1}{12}\ln n.
$$

### Remainder term

Since $f''(x)=1/x$,

$$
\int_1^n |f''(x)|\,dx = \ln n,
$$

so the remainder contributes $O(\ln n)$.

### Combination

Summing all contributions,

$$
\ln P_n
= \frac{n^2}{2}\ln n - \frac{n^2}{4}
+ \frac{n\ln n}{2}
+ O(\ln n).
$$

Exponentiating,

$$
P_n
= \exp\!\left(\frac{n^2}{2}\ln n - \frac{n^2}{4} + \frac{n\ln n}{2} + O(\ln n)\right).
$$

Separating dominant factors,

$$
P_n
= n^{n^2/2}\, e^{-n^2/4}\, n^{n/2}\, e^{O(\ln n)}.
$$

Since $e^{O(\ln n)} = n^{O(1)}$, this gives the compact asymptotic form

$$
P_n
= n^{\frac{n^2+n}{2}} \, e^{-n^2/4} \, n^{O(1)}.
$$

A standard condensed expression is

$$
P_n
= \exp\!\left(\frac{n^2}{2}\ln n - \frac{n^2}{4} + \frac{n\ln n}{2} + O(\ln n)\right).
$$

Thus an approximate value is

$$
\boxed{
1^1 2^2 \cdots n^n
\sim \exp\!\left(\frac{n^2}{2}\ln n - \frac{n^2}{4} + \frac{n\ln n}{2}\right)
\text{ up to a factor } e^{O(\ln n)}
}.
$$

## Verification

The dominant term arises from $\int_1^n x\ln x,dx$, which grows like $\frac{1}{2}n^2\ln n$. The next-order corrections from endpoint and derivative terms are of lower order $n\ln n$ and $\ln n$, consistent with the structure of Eulerâ€™s summation formula applied to a twice differentiable function with $f''(x)=1/x$. All remaining contributions are bounded by a constant multiple of $\ln n$, which is negligible compared to $n\ln n$ and $n^2\ln n$. âˆŽ
