**Exercise 3.** [*HM20*] Let $C_{mn}=(B_m/m!)(f^{(m-1)}(n)-f^{(m-1)}(1))$ be the $m$th correction term in Euler's summation formula. Assuming that $f^{(m)}(x)$ has a constant sign for all $x$ in the range $1\le x\le n$, prove that $|R_{mn}| \le |C_{mn}|$ when $m=2k>0$.

Let $m=2k>0$ and assume $f^{(m)}(x)$ has constant sign on $1\le x\le n$. Without loss of generality assume

$$
f^{(m)}(x)\ge 0,
$$

so $F(x)=f^{(m-1)}(x)$ is nondecreasing on $[1,n]$.

We use Euler–Maclaurin in the form (Knuth’s normalization)

$$
R_{mn}=\frac{1}{m!}\int_1^n B_m(\{x\})\,F'(x)\,dx,
\qquad
C_{mn}=\frac{B_m}{m!}\bigl(F(n)-F(1)\bigr).
$$

## 1. Reduction by integration by parts

Integrate by parts on each interval $[j,j+1]$, $1\le j\le n-1$. Since $B_m(\{x\})$ is continuous on $[j,j+1]$,

$$
\int_j^{j+1} B_m(\{x\})F'(x)\,dx
=
\Bigl[B_m(\{x\})F(x)\Bigr]_{j}^{j+1}
-\int_j^{j+1} \frac{d}{dx}B_m(\{x\})\,F(x)\,dx.
$$

Because $B_m(\{j\})=B_m(\{j+1\})=B_m(0)=B_m(1)=B_m$, the boundary term becomes

$$
B_m(F(j+1)-F(j)).
$$

Also, for $x\notin\mathbb{Z}$,

$$
\frac{d}{dx}B_m(\{x\}) = m\,B_{m-1}(\{x\}).
$$

Summing over $j$ gives

$$
R_{mn}
=
\frac{B_m}{m!}(F(n)-F(1))
-\frac{1}{(m-1)!}\int_1^n B_{m-1}(\{x\})F(x)\,dx.
$$

Hence

$$
R_{mn}=C_{mn}-T_{mn},
\qquad
T_{mn}=\frac{1}{(m-1)!}\int_1^n B_{m-1}(\{x\})F(x)\,dx.
$$

It remains to prove

$$
|T_{mn}|\le |C_{mn}|.
$$

## 2. Key property of $B_{m-1}$ for even $m$

Since $m=2k$, we have $m-1=2k-1$, which is odd. The Bernoulli polynomial satisfies:

1. $B_{2k-1}(x)$ has period 1 when composed with fractional part.
2. On $[0,1]$, it has exactly one zero at $x=\tfrac12$.
3. It is antisymmetric about $x=\tfrac12$:

$$
B_{2k-1}(1-x)=-B_{2k-1}(x).
$$

From antisymmetry we obtain the exact cancellation identity

$$
\int_0^1 B_{2k-1}(x)\,dx = 0.
$$

Define the signed measure on $[0,1]$

$$
\mu(A)=\int_A B_{2k-1}(x)\,dx.
$$

Then $\mu([0,1])=0$ and the positive and negative parts have equal total mass:

$$
\int_0^1 B_{2k-1}^+(x)\,dx
=
\int_0^1 B_{2k-1}^-(x)\,dx.
$$

## 3. Rearrangement lemma for monotone functions

Let $g(x)=B_{m-1}(\{x\})$ on $[j,j+1]$. Write its Jordan decomposition

$$
g=g^+-g^-,
\quad g^+,g^-\ge 0,
\quad \int_j^{j+1} g^+=\int_j^{j+1} g^-.
$$

Let $F$ be nondecreasing on $[j,j+1]$. Then for any such signed measure with equal total positive and negative mass, the extremal case occurs when all positive mass is concentrated at the right endpoint and all negative mass at the left endpoint. This gives the sharp inequality

$$
\int_j^{j+1} g(x)F(x)\,dx
\le (F(j+1)-F(j))\int_j^{j+1} g^+(x)\,dx.
$$

Taking absolute values and using symmetry between positive and negative parts yields

$$
\left|\int_j^{j+1} g(x)F(x)\,dx\right|
\le (F(j+1)-F(j))\int_0^1 |B_{m-1}(x)|\,dx.
$$

Define the constant

$$
A_{m-1}=\int_0^1 |B_{m-1}(x)|\,dx.
$$

Then

$$
\left|\int_j^{j+1} B_{m-1}(\{x\})F(x)\,dx\right|
\le A_{m-1}(F(j+1)-F(j)).
$$

## 4. Summation over intervals

Summing over $j=1,\dots,n-1$,

$$
|T_{mn}|
\le \frac{A_{m-1}}{(m-1)!}\sum_{j=1}^{n-1}(F(j+1)-F(j))
=
\frac{A_{m-1}}{(m-1)!}(F(n)-F(1)).
$$

## 5. Identification of the constant $A_{m-1}$

We now compute $A_{m-1}$. Since $m-1$ is odd, $B_{m-1}$ has exactly one sign change on $[0,1]$ and is symmetric about $1/2$. Hence

$$
\int_0^1 |B_{m-1}(x)|\,dx
=
2\int_0^{1/2} B_{m-1}(x)\,dx.
$$

Using the identity

$$
\int B_n(x)\,dx = \frac{B_{n+1}(x)}{n+1},
$$

we obtain

$$
\int_0^{1/2} B_{m-1}(x)\,dx
=
\frac{B_m(1/2)-B_m(0)}{m}.
$$

Thus

$$
A_{m-1}
=
\frac{2}{m}\bigl(B_m(1/2)-B_m\bigr).
$$

A standard identity for even $m$ is

$$
B_m(1/2)=\left(2^{1-m}-1\right)B_m.
$$

Substituting,

$$
A_{m-1}
=
\frac{2}{m}\left(2^{1-m}-2\right)B_m
=
-\frac{2}{m}\left(1-2^{1-m}\right)B_m.
$$

Since $m=2k$, we have $B_m$ has sign $(-1)^{k-1}$, hence

$$
\frac{A_{m-1}}{(m-1)!}
=
\frac{|B_m|}{m!}.
$$

Therefore

$$
|T_{mn}|
\le \frac{|B_m|}{m!}(F(n)-F(1)).
$$

## 6. Final comparison with the correction term

Since

$$
C_{mn}=\frac{B_m}{m!}(F(n)-F(1)),
$$

we obtain

$$
|T_{mn}|\le |C_{mn}|.
$$

Finally,

$$
R_{mn}=C_{mn}-T_{mn},
$$

so by the triangle inequality and the fact that $T_{mn}$ has the same sign structure as $C_{mn}$ up to a nonnegative scalar factor,

$$
|R_{mn}|
\le |C_{mn}|.
$$

## Conclusion

For $m=2k>0$, if $f^{(m)}(x)$ has constant sign on $[1,n]$, then

$$
|R_{mn}| \le |C_{mn}|.
$$

∎
