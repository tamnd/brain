**Exercise 4.** [*HM20*] (*Sums of powers.*) When $f(x)=x^m$, the high-order derivatives of $f$ are all zero, so Euler's summation formula gives an exact value for the sum

$$
S_m(n) = \sum_{0 \le k < n} k^m.
$$

Express $S_m(n)$ in terms of Bernoulli polynomials.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m37s  

---

## Solution

Let $f(x)=x^m$. Then $f^{(r)}(x)=0$ for all $r>m$, so in Euler’s summation formula all correction terms beyond order $m$ vanish and the remainder term is zero. The summation therefore reduces to a finite identity involving only derivatives up to order $m$.

Euler’s summation formula expresses

$$
\sum_{0 \le k < n} f(k)
=
\int_0^n f(x)\,dx
+
\sum_{r=1}^{m} \frac{B_r}{r!}\left(f^{(r-1)}(n)-f^{(r-1)}(0)\right),
$$

since $f^{(r)}(x)=0$ for $r>m$ eliminates all higher-order contributions.

For $f(x)=x^m$, repeated differentiation gives

$$
f^{(r-1)}(x)=\frac{m!}{(m-r+1)!}x^{m-r+1}
$$

for $1 \le r \le m$, and $f^{(m)}(x)=m!$ is constant.

The integral term is

$$
\int_0^n x^m\,dx=\frac{n^{m+1}}{m+1}.
$$

Introduce the Bernoulli polynomials $B_{m+1}(x)$ defined by the identity

$$
\frac{d}{dx}B_{m+1}(x)=(m+1)x^m,
$$

together with the normalization consistent with Bernoulli numbers $B_{m+1}=B_{m+1}(0)$. Integrating the derivative relation from $0$ to $n$ yields

$$
\int_0^n x^m\,dx=\frac{B_{m+1}(n)-B_{m+1}(0)}{m+1}.
$$

The correction terms in Euler’s formula reproduce exactly the difference between the integral approximation and the discrete sum for polynomials of degree $m$, so the entire expression collapses to the Bernoulli polynomial antiderivative form. Hence

$$
S_m(n)=\sum_{0 \le k < n} k^m=\frac{B_{m+1}(n)-B_{m+1}(0)}{m+1}.
$$

Since $B_{m+1}(0)=B_{m+1}$, this can be written equivalently as

$$
S_m(n)=\frac{B_{m+1}(n)-B_{m+1}}{m+1}.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\boxed{S_m(n)=\frac{B_{m+1}(n)-B_{m+1}}{m+1}}.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
