[Section 2.5: Dynamic Storage Allocation](../)

**Exercise 11.** [**] [M25] Show that the improvement to Algorithm A suggested in the
answer to exercise 6 can also be used to lead to a slight improvement in
Algorithm B, which cuts the average length of search from half the length
of the `AVAIL` list to one-third this length. (Assume that the block
being freed will be inserted into a random place within the sorted
`AVAIL` list.)

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 5m16s  

---

Use the same idea as the improvement to Algorithm A: retain a pointer to the place in the sorted `AVAIL` list where the most recent insertion occurred, and begin the search for the next insertion from that point instead of always starting at the head of the list. Since Algorithm B inserts the newly freed block into its proper position in the address-ordered list, the desired position is equally likely to lie anywhere in the list by hypothesis.

Let the `AVAIL` list contain $m$ links. If every search begins at the head, the insertion point is equally likely to occur after any of the $m$ links, so the average search examines

$$
\frac{0+1+\cdots+(m-1)}{m}
=\frac{m-1}{2}
$$

links, which is approximately half the length of the list. With the remembered pointer, the previous insertion point divides the circularized list into two arcs whose expected lengths are each $m/2$. The next insertion point is uniformly distributed over the list, so the expected distance from the remembered position to the correct insertion point is

$$
\frac{0+1+\cdots+(m/2-1)}{m/2}
=\frac{m/2-1}{2}
\approx \frac{m}{4}.
$$

Only half of the searches start in the correct half of the list; the other half require wrapping around the end. Averaging over both cases gives an expected search length of approximately

$$
\frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{m}{4}
+\frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{m}{2}
=\frac{m}{3},
$$

hence about one-third of the length of the `AVAIL` list. Therefore the same remembered-position improvement used for Algorithm A yields a corresponding improvement for Algorithm B, reducing the average search from about $m/2$ links to about $m/3$ links.
