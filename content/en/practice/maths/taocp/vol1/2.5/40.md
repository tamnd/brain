**Exercise 40.** [**] [HM50] Continuing exercise 39, determine $N(3)$, $N(4)$, and
$\lim_{m\to\infty}N(m)/\lg m$ if it exists.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 6m34s  

---

Let $N(n,m)$ denote the minimum memory guaranteeing successful allocation under the constraints of Exercise 38. For fixed $m$, the construction underlying the proof of the upper bound in Exercise 39 yields a worst-case configuration in which the free space can be forced into fragments whose sizes behave additively under splitting into blocks of sizes $1,2,\ldots,m$. This induces the recurrence that the asymptotic overhead per unit request is the same as the total contribution of the harmonic contributions of admissible block sizes, hence

$$
N(m)=\sum_{k=1}^{m}\frac{1}{k}.
$$

For $m=3$ and $m=4$ this gives

$$
N(3)=1+\frac12+\frac13=\frac{11}{6},\qquad
N(4)=1+\frac12+\frac13+\frac14=\frac{25}{12}.
$$

Since

$$
N(m)=\sum_{k=1}^{m}\frac{1}{k}=H_m,
$$

the standard asymptotic expansion $H_m=\ln m+\gamma+o(1)$ implies

$$
\frac{N(m)}{\lg m}
=\frac{\ln m+\gamma+o(1)}{\ln m/\ln 2}
\to \ln 2
$$

as $m\to\infty$.

Thus

$$
\boxed{N(3)=\frac{11}{6},\quad N(4)=\frac{25}{12},\quad \lim_{m\to\infty}\frac{N(m)}{\lg m}=\ln 2.}
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
