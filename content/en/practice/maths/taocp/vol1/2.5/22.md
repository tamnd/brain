[Section 2.5: Dynamic Storage Allocation](../)

**Exercise 22.** [**] [21] The text repeatedly states that the buddy system allows only
blocks of size $2^k$ to be used, and exercise 21 shows this can lead to a
substantial increase in the storage required. But if an 11-word block is
needed in connection with the buddy system, why couldn't we find a
16-word block and divide it into an 11-word piece together with two free
blocks of sizes 4 and 1?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 4m22s  

---

The buddy system depends on the invariant that every free block has size $2^k$ and begins at an address aligned for a block of that size. Two free buddies of size $2^k$ can then be recognized immediately and merged into a block of size $2^{k+1}$. The entire allocation and coalescing mechanism relies on this structure.

If a $16$-word block were split into blocks of sizes $11$, $4$, and $1$, the $11$-word piece would not have size $2^k$. When that block later became free, there would be no uniquely defined buddy with which it could be combined. The free space would therefore no longer fit into the buddy-system classification by powers of two, and the simple buddy-merging procedure would break down. The price paid for the efficiency of the buddy system is precisely that requests must be rounded up to the next power of two, so an $11$-word request occupies a $16$-word block.
