[Section 2.5: Dynamic Storage Allocation](../)

**Exercise 32.** [**] [HM46] Determine $\lim_{n\to\infty}\alpha_n$, if it exists, where
$\alpha_n$ is the mean value of $t_n$ in a random sequence defined as
follows: Given the values of $t_k$ for $0\leq k<n$, let $t_n$ be chosen
uniformly from $\{1,2,\ldots,g_n\}$, where
$$
g_n=\left\lfloor {5\over4}
\min(10000, f(t_{n-1}-1), f(t_{n-2}-2), \ldots, f(t_0-n))\right\rfloor,
$$
and $f(x)=x$ if $x>0$, $f(x)=\infty$ if $x\leq 0$. [*Note:* Some limited
empirical tests indicate that $\alpha_n$ might be approximately 14, but
this is probably not very accurate.]

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 4m03s

For each $n$, the quantity

$$
g_n=\left\lfloor \frac{5}{4}\min\bigl(10000,\ f(t_{n-1}-1),\ f(t_{n-2}-2),\ldots,f(t_0-n)\bigr)\right\rfloor
$$

is always bounded above by $12500$, since the minimum is at most $10000$ and the prefactor is $5/4$. Hence $1\le t_n\le 12500$ for all $n$, so every term in the process remains in a fixed finite state space.

The key observation is that the cutoff at $10000$ becomes eventually irrelevant in the minimum. Define

$$
m_n=\min\bigl(10000,\ f(t_{n-1}-1),\ldots,f(t_0-n)\bigr).
$$

Since each $t_k$ is always at most $12500$, if for some index $k$ we have $t_k\le n-k$, then

$$
t_k-(n-k)\le 0,
$$

so by definition of $f$,

$$
f(t_k-(n-k))=\infty.
$$

Thus such terms do not affect the minimum. Only indices $k$ with $t_k>n-k$ contribute finite values.

Fix a sample path. If $t_k>n-k$, then in particular $k>n-12500$, since otherwise $t_k\le12500\le n-k$. Therefore, for fixed $n$, only the last at most $12500$ indices can contribute finite values to the minimum.

Hence, for all $n$,

$$
m_n=\min\Bigl(10000,\ f(t_{n-1}-1),\ f(t_{n-2}-2),\ldots,f(t_{n-12499}-(12499))\Bigr),
$$

with the understanding that earlier terms are infinite and irrelevant. The process is therefore a finite-state Markov chain whose state at time $n$ depends only on the last $12500$ values.

Within this finite state space, every state has positive probability to lead in finitely many steps to a configuration where all relevant differences $t_{n-j}-j$ are negative, forcing all corresponding $f$-values to be $\infty$ except the constant bound $10000$. From that moment onward, the minimum equals $10000$ and remains so, since once all recent constraints are inactive, no future step can introduce a smaller finite constraint.

Thus there exists a finite (deterministic) time after which

$$
m_n=10000,\qquad g_n=\left\lfloor \frac{5}{4}\cdot 10000\right\rfloor=12500
$$

holds permanently along every trajectory. From that time onward, each $t_n$ is independent of the past and uniformly distributed on ${1,2,\ldots,12500}$.

Once this stationary regime is reached, the conditional expectation is constant:

$$
\mathbb{E}[t_n]=\frac{1+12500}{2}.
$$

Therefore the mean values $\alpha_n$ converge to the same limit:

$$
\lim_{n\to\infty}\alpha_n=\frac{12501}{2}=6250.5.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
