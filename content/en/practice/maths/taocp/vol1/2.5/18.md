**Exercise 18.** [**] [20] Figures 42 and 43 were obtained using the same data, and
essentially the same algorithms (Algorithms A and B), except that Fig. 43
was prepared by modifying Algorithm A to choose best-fit instead of
first-fit. Why did this cause Fig. 42 to have a large available area in
the higher locations of memory, while in Fig. 43 there is a large
available area in the lower locations?

**Solve time:** 3m27s

Memory is treated as an ordered sequence of variable-size free blocks. Each allocation selects a block, removes a prefix of it for the request, and leaves any remainder in the same position in memory. Thus the spatial distribution of free space is determined by which blocks are selected and where the unused remainders are left.

In Algorithm A (first-fit), the free list is scanned from low addresses upward until the first block large enough to satisfy the request is found. This search rule implies that low-address blocks are tested first for almost every request. Whenever such a block is large enough, it is split, and the allocated portion is removed while the remainder stays in place at a higher address than the allocated segment. Hence every successful use of a low-address block tends to reduce usable low memory and replace it by smaller fragments or by no block at all, while leaving any unused remainder in a higher position than the allocated portion. Blocks in higher memory are reached only when no earlier block suffices, so they are selected less frequently and therefore remain largely intact. Over time, repeated removal and splitting near the beginning of memory eliminates large contiguous free regions there, while large blocks persist only in higher addresses. This produces a visible concentration of available contiguous memory in the upper part of the address space.

In Algorithm B (best-fit), each request inspects all available free blocks and chooses one of minimum size among those that satisfy the request. This global selection rule implies that large blocks are avoided whenever a smaller sufficient block exists, independent of position. Small blocks are therefore preferentially consumed wherever they exist, since they are most likely to be the best fit. When a large block is eventually used, it is split into an allocated portion and a relatively small remainder, and that remainder remains in the same memory position as the original block. Because best-fit repeatedly removes small blocks throughout the list, the remaining structure becomes dominated by a few larger blocks that are not at the bottom of the address space but instead arise from earlier splits of large high-address regions. Meanwhile, the lower-address region is less frequently chosen once it has been reduced to blocks that are too large to be best fits for typical requests, since smaller blocks elsewhere will be preferred. This leaves the lower part of memory comparatively undisturbed and allows it to retain or accumulate a larger contiguous free region.

The contrast follows directly from selection locality. First-fit acts locally in address order, so it continuously erodes the lowest available region and pushes surviving large blocks upward. Best-fit acts globally in size, so it continuously removes small fragments wherever they occur and leaves positional structure less biased by address order, which results in the largest remaining contiguous region tending to persist in lower memory.
