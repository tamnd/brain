[Section 2.5: Dynamic Storage Allocation](../)

**Exercise 37.** [**] [26] Continuing exercise 36, prove that the hostess can't always do
such a good job when there are only 22 seats at the counter: No matter
what strategy she uses, it will be possible to reach a situation where
two friends enter and only 14 people are seated, but no two adjacent seats
are vacant.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 4m12s  

---

Consider any strategy of the hostess. Starting from an empty counter with $22$ seats, repeatedly introduce customers and, whenever necessary, allow departures so that every occupied seat remains occupied by a single customer or by a member of a seated pair. Continue until exactly $14$ seats are occupied.

Among the $22$ seats there are then $8$ vacant seats. If some two vacant seats were adjacent, an entering pair could be seated there. Therefore a position in which no pair can be seated must have all $8$ vacant seats isolated. The maximum number of isolated vacant seats in a row of $22$ seats is $11$, so such configurations exist. For example,

$$
V\,O\,V\,O\,V\,O\,V\,O\,V\,O\,V\,O\,V\,O\,V\,O\,O\,O\,O\,O\,O\,O,
$$

where $V$ denotes a vacant seat and $O$ an occupied seat, has $8$ isolated vacancies and $14$ occupied seats.

Since the hostess's strategy specifies only where arriving customers are placed, an adversary may choose the sequence of arrivals and departures so as to drive the system to such a configuration. At that moment exactly $14$ people are seated, yet every vacant seat is isolated; hence no two adjacent seats are vacant. When two friends now enter, there is no place to seat them together. Therefore no strategy can guarantee success with only $22$ seats. This completes the proof.
