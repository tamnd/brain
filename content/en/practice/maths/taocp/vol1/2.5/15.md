**Exercise 15.** [**] [24] Show how to speed up Algorithm C at the expense of a slightly
longer program, by not changing any more links than absolutely necessary
in each of four cases depending on whether `TAG(P0 - 1)`,
`TAG(P0 + SIZE(P0))` are plus or minus.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m49s  

---

Algorithm C maintains a circular doubly linked free list with head pointer $AVAIL$. Each free block $P$ has two links, $LLINK(P)$ and $RLINK(P)$, and insertion or deletion changes these links only when a block is actually removed or inserted. Coalescing at a newly freed block $P_0$ depends on the tags of its immediate neighbors and determines which blocks are physically removed from the free list and which are merged without list operations.

Let $L$ be the block at address $P_0 - \text{SIZE}(L)$ when `TAG(P_0 - 1) = plus`, and let $R$ be the block at address $P_0 + \text{SIZE}(P_0)$ when `TAG(P_0 + \text{SIZE}(P_0)) = plus`. A link change means a modification of a predecessor or successor pointer in the free list.

### Case 1: `TAG(P_0 - 1) = minus`, `TAG(P_0 + SIZE(P_0)) = minus`

Neither neighbor is free, so no block is removed from the free list. The only operation is insertion of the new free block representing $P_0$. This requires setting

$$
LLINK(P_0) \leftarrow AVAIL,\quad RLINK(P_0) \leftarrow RLINK(AVAIL),
$$

followed by updating

$$
LLINK(RLINK(AVAIL)) \leftarrow P_0,\quad RLINK(AVAIL) \leftarrow P_0.
$$

No other link changes occur, since no coalescing is performed. These four pointer updates are necessary because the block must be inserted between two existing free-list nodes.

### Case 2: `TAG(P_0 - 1) = minus`, `TAG(P_0 + SIZE(P_0)) = plus`

The right neighbor $R$ is in the free list and must be removed. The left neighbor is not in the list. The optimal strategy is to delete $R$ and extend $P_0$ to cover both blocks.

Deletion of $R$ requires exactly two link changes:

$$
RLINK(LLINK(R)) \leftarrow RLINK(R),\quad LLINK(RLINK(R)) \leftarrow LLINK(R).
$$

After removal, no insertion of $P_0$ is performed as a separate node in the free list, since $P_0$ inherits the position and links of $R$. The merged block replaces $R$ in place, so no additional pointer updates are required.

Thus only the unlinking of $R$ is performed, and no extra insertion operations occur.

### Case 3: `TAG(P_0 - 1) = plus`, `TAG(P_0 + SIZE(P_0)) = minus`

This is symmetric to Case 2. The left neighbor $L$ is in the free list and must be removed, while the right neighbor is not.

Deletion of $L$ requires exactly two link changes:

$$
RLINK(LLINK(L)) \leftarrow RLINK(L),\quad LLINK(RLINK(L)) \leftarrow LLINK(L).
$$

After $L$ is removed, the merged block consisting of $L$ and $P_0$ occupies the same position in memory, so no separate insertion of $P_0$ is performed. The free list structure remains consistent with only the two updates above.

### Case 4: `TAG(P_0 - 1) = plus`, `TAG(P_0 + SIZE(P_0)) = plus`

Both neighbors $L$ and $R$ are in the free list, so both must be removed and merged with $P_0$.

Unlinking $L$ contributes two link changes:

$$
RLINK(LLINK(L)) \leftarrow RLINK(L),\quad LLINK(RLINK(L)) \leftarrow LLINK(L),
$$

and unlinking $R$ contributes two link changes:

$$
RLINK(LLINK(R)) \leftarrow RLINK(R),\quad LLINK(RLINK(R)) \leftarrow LLINK(R).
$$

After both deletions, no insertion of $P_0$ is performed, since the merged block replaces both neighbors and remains in place of the larger combined block. Any attempt to reinsert $P_0$ separately would introduce at least two additional link updates, violating minimality because it would duplicate a node already represented by the surviving free-list structure.

### Minimality argument

Each free-list deletion requires exactly two pointer updates, since both the predecessor’s forward link and successor’s backward link must be repaired to maintain list consistency. No smaller number of link changes suffices because breaking either direction would leave a dangling pointer in a doubly linked structure. Insertions require at least two pointer updates for the same reason.

In Cases 2 and 3, avoiding insertion of $P_0$ is necessary because $P_0$ is absorbed into an existing list node, so any additional insertion would strictly increase link modifications without changing the structure. In Case 4, avoiding insertion of $P_0$ is necessary for the same reason, since the final free block is represented entirely by the remaining adjusted list links after removing $L$ and $R$.

Thus the program is optimized by performing only those deletions required by actual free neighbors and performing insertion only in Case 1, where no coalescing occurs. This yields the minimum number of link changes consistent with correctness of the free list structure. ∎
