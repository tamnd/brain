[Section 2.5: Dynamic Storage Allocation](../)

**Exercise 14.** [**] [21] What difference would it make to Algorithm C and the algorithm
of exercise 12, (a) if the `SIZE` field were not present in the last word
of a free block? or (b) if the `SIZE` field were not present in the first
word of a reserved block?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m25s  

---

The boundary-tag representation in (7)–(9) relies on the fact that every block can be navigated and coalesced using only local information at its endpoints, in particular that the size of a block is available when one inspects either boundary word. The free-list operations in both Algorithm C and the modified Algorithm A of Exercise 12 depend on this symmetry for constant-time adjacency detection.

If the `SIZE` field is omitted from the last word of a free block, then a free block no longer provides its length at the point where its right boundary is examined. Consequently, when a block is freed and its right neighbor is tested for coalescing, the algorithm cannot determine the address of that neighbor’s right endpoint without first knowing the neighbor’s length. This breaks the ability to locate and merge adjacent free blocks in $O(1)$ time. In Algorithm C and in Exercise 12, coalescing would then require either maintaining an external auxiliary structure that stores block sizes indexed by address or performing a linear scan forward to reconstruct block boundaries, both of which destroy the intended constant-time boundary-tag advantage.

If the `SIZE` field is omitted from the first word of a reserved block, then allocated blocks cannot be used to recover their length when encountered from the left during traversal or coalescing. Although allocated blocks are not stored in `AVAIL`, the boundary-tag scheme requires that every block boundary be interpretable regardless of whether the block is free or allocated, since coalescing depends on detecting the exact start and end of neighboring blocks in memory. Without a size field at the left boundary of reserved blocks, the algorithm cannot reliably locate the boundary of the previous block when scanning backward or verifying adjacency, and it loses the ability to compute the correct starting address of a merged block in constant time. This again forces either additional bookkeeping outside the boundary tags or a scan of memory to recover structure, invalidating the constant-time local-update property of both Algorithm C and the modified Algorithm A of Exercise 12.

Thus, in both cases, removing either boundary tag destroys the symmetry that allows both forward and backward navigation between blocks using only endpoint information, and the algorithms lose their constant-time coalescing behavior.
