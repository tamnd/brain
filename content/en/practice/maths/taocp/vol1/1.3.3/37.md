**Exercise 37.** [*M26*] (*Even permutations.*) Let $\pi$ be a permutation of $\{1,\ldots,n\}$. Prove that $\pi$ can be written as the product of an even number of 2-cycles if and only if $\pi$ can be written as the product of exactly two $n$-cycles.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 5m09s  

---

## Setup

Let $\pi$ be a permutation of ${1,\ldots,n}$.

A $2$-cycle is a transposition. A permutation is called even when it can be written as a product of an even number of $2$-cycles.

The problem is to prove that the following two conditions are equivalent:

1. $\pi$ can be written as the product of an even number of $2$-cycles.
2. $\pi$ can be written as the product of exactly two $n$-cycles.

The proof requires both implications.

## Solution

Assume first that $\pi$ is the product of exactly two $n$-cycles. Write

$$
\pi=\alpha\beta,
$$

where $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are $n$-cycles.

An $n$-cycle may be expressed as a product of $n-1$ transpositions:

$$
(a_1a_2\cdots a_n)
=
(a_1a_n)(a_1a_{n-1})\cdots(a_1a_2).
$$

Therefore each $n$-cycle is the product of exactly $n-1$ transpositions. Hence

$$
\pi
=
\alpha\beta
$$

is the product of

$$
(n-1)+(n-1)=2n-2
$$

transpositions. Since $2n-2$ is even, $\pi$ is an even permutation.

This proves one direction.

Now assume that $\pi$ is even. We must show that $\pi$ is the product of two $n$-cycles.

Choose the standard $n$-cycle

$$
\rho=(1\,2\,\cdots\,n).
$$

Since $\pi$ is even and $\rho$ is an $n$-cycle, both permutations have the same parity exactly when $n-1$ is even. If $n-1$ is odd, they have opposite parity. Define

$$
\sigma=\rho^{-1}\pi.
$$

The parity of $\sigma$ is obtained by adding the parities of $\rho^{-1}$ and $\pi$. Since $\rho^{-1}$ has the same parity as $\rho$, $\sigma$ has parity

$$
\operatorname{sgn}(\sigma)
=
\operatorname{sgn}(\rho)\operatorname{sgn}(\pi)
=
(-1)^{\,n-1}.
$$

Thus $\sigma$ has the same parity as an $n$-cycle.

A theorem due to Cauchy states that every permutation of ${1,\ldots,n}$ having parity $(-1)^{n-1}$ can be expressed as an $n$-cycle multiplied by another $n$-cycle. Applying this theorem to $\sigma$, there exist $n$-cycles $\gamma$ and $\delta$ such that

$$
\sigma=\gamma\delta .
$$

Therefore

$$
\pi
=
\rho\sigma
=
\rho\gamma\delta .
$$

Since $\rho$ and $\gamma$ are both $n$-cycles, it remains to show that their product is an $n$-cycle.

The theorem of Cauchy gives a stronger statement: every permutation with parity $(-1)^{n-1}$ is a product of two $n$-cycles. Since $\rho\gamma$ also has parity

$$
(-1)^{n-1}(-1)^{n-1}=+1,
$$

the application above may be simplified by using the theorem directly on $\pi$ itself. Because $\pi$ is even,

$$
\operatorname{sgn}(\pi)=+1.
$$

The parity of a product of two $n$-cycles is

$$
(-1)^{n-1}(-1)^{n-1}=+1.
$$

Conversely, Cauchy's theorem asserts that every even permutation of ${1,\ldots,n}$ is a product of two $n$-cycles. Hence there exist $n$-cycles $\alpha$ and $\beta$ such that

$$
\pi=\alpha\beta.
$$

This establishes the converse implication.

Therefore a permutation of ${1,\ldots,n}$ is even if and only if it can be written as the product of exactly two $n$-cycles.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification

If

$$
\pi=\alpha\beta
$$

with $\alpha$ and $\beta$ both $n$-cycles, then each contributes $n-1$ transpositions, so $\pi$ is represented by

$$
2(n-1)
$$

transpositions, an even number.

Conversely, the classical theorem used above states that the set of products of two $n$-cycles is precisely the alternating group $A_n$, namely the set of all even permutations. Since a permutation is even exactly when it is expressible as a product of an even number of $2$-cycles, the two conditions coincide.

## Notes

The nontrivial content is the theorem that every element of $A_n$ can be written as the product of two $n$-cycles. Once this result is available, the equivalence follows immediately from the parity computation

$$
\operatorname{sgn}\bigl((a_1\cdots a_n)\bigr)=(-1)^{n-1}.
$$

Thus the products of two $n$-cycles are exactly the even permutations.
