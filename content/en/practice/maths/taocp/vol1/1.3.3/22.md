**Exercise 22.** [*HM34*] (The following approach, due to L. Shepp and S. P. Lloyd, gives a convenient and powerful method for solving problems related to the cycle structure of random permutations.) Instead of regarding the number, $n$, of objects as fixed, and the permutation variable, let us assume instead that we independently choose the quantities $\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \alpha_3, \ldots$ appearing in exercises 20 and 21 according to some probability distribution. Let $w$ be any real number between 0 and 1.

a) Suppose that we choose the random variables $\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \alpha_3, \ldots$ according to the rule that "the probability that $\alpha_m = k$ is $f(w, m, k)$," for some function $f(w, m, k)$. Determine the value of $f(w, m, k)$ so that the following two conditions hold:

1. $\sum_{k \ge 0} f(w, m, k) = 1$, for $0 < w < 1$ and $m \ge 1$;
2. the probability that $\alpha_1 + 2\alpha_2 + 3\alpha_3 + \cdots = n$ and that $\alpha_1 = k_1$, $\alpha_2 = k_2$, $\alpha_3 = k_3$, ... equals $(1 - w)w^n P(n; k_1, k_2, k_3, \ldots)$, where $P(n; k_1, k_2, k_3, \ldots)$ is defined in exercise 21.

b) A permutation whose cycle structure is $\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \alpha_3, \ldots$ clearly permutes exactly $\alpha_1 + 2\alpha_2 + 3\alpha_3 + \cdots$ objects. Show that if the $\alpha$'s are randomly chosen according to the probability distribution in part (a), the probability that $\alpha_1 + 2\alpha_2 + 3\alpha_3 + \cdots = n$ is $(1 - w)w^n$; the probability that $\alpha_1 + 2\alpha_2 + 3\alpha_3 + \cdots$ is infinite is zero.

c) Let $\phi(\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \ldots)$ be any function of the infinitely many numbers $\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \ldots$. Show that if the $\alpha$'s are chosen according to the probability distribution in (a), the average value of $\phi$ is $(1 - w)\sum_{n \ge 0} w^n \phi_n$; here $\phi_n$ denotes the average value of $\phi$ taken over all permutations of $n$ objects, where the variable $\alpha_j$ represents the number of $j$-cycles of a permutation. [For example, if $\phi(\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \ldots) = \alpha_1$, the value of $\phi_n$ is the average number of singleton cycles in a random permutation of $n$ objects; we showed in (28) that $\phi_n = 1$ for all $n$.]

d) Use this method to find the average number of cycles of *even* length in a random permutation of $n$ objects.

e) Use this method to solve exercise 18.

We correct the argument by deriving the distribution in part (a) directly from the two stated conditions, without assuming Poisson structure or independence in advance.

## (a) Determination of $f(w,m,k)$

From Exercise 21, the probability that a uniformly random permutation of $n$ objects has cycle counts $(\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\ldots)$ with $\sum m\alpha_m=n$ is

$$
P(n;k_1,k_2,\ldots)=\frac{1}{\prod_{m\ge 1} m^{k_m} k_m!}.
$$

Hence condition (2) can be written explicitly as

$$
\mathbb{P}(\alpha_1=k_1,\alpha_2=k_2,\ldots)
=(1-w)w^{\sum m k_m}\prod_{m\ge 1}\frac{1}{m^{k_m}k_m!}.
\tag{1}
$$

Rewrite (1) by separating the $m$-th coordinate:

$$
\mathbb{P}(\alpha_1=k_1,\alpha_2=k_2,\ldots)
=(1-w)\left(\frac{w^m}{m}\right)^{k_m}\frac{1}{k_m!}
\prod_{j\ne m}\left(\frac{w^j}{j}\right)^{k_j}\frac{1}{k_j!}.
$$

Now fix $m$ and $k$, and sum over all $(k_j)_{j\ne m}$. This gives the marginal law

$$
f(w,m,k)=\mathbb{P}(\alpha_m=k)
=(1-w)\frac{(w^m/m)^k}{k!}
\prod_{j\ne m}\sum_{k_j\ge 0}\frac{(w^j/j)^{k_j}}{k_j!}.
$$

Each inner sum is an exponential series:

$$
\sum_{k_j\ge 0}\frac{(w^j/j)^{k_j}}{k_j!}=\exp(w^j/j).
$$

Therefore

$$
f(w,m,k)
=(1-w)\frac{(w^m/m)^k}{k!}\exp\!\left(\sum_{j\ne m}\frac{w^j}{j}\right).
$$

Using

$$
\sum_{j\ge 1}\frac{w^j}{j}=-\log(1-w),
$$

we get

$$
\sum_{j\ne m}\frac{w^j}{j}=-\log(1-w)-\frac{w^m}{m}.
$$

Hence

$$
\exp\!\left(\sum_{j\ne m}\frac{w^j}{j}\right)
=(1-w)^{-1}e^{-w^m/m}.
$$

Substituting,

$$
f(w,m,k)=e^{-w^m/m}\frac{(w^m/m)^k}{k!}.
$$

Thus each $\alpha_m$ is Poisson with mean $w^m/m$, derived rather than assumed.

## (b) Distribution of $S=\sum m\alpha_m$

From (1),

$$
\mathbb{P}(\{\alpha_m\})=(1-w)\prod_{m\ge 1}\frac{(w^m/m)^{\alpha_m}}{\alpha_m!}.
$$

Summing over all configurations with fixed $S=n$,

$$
\mathbb{P}(S=n)
=(1-w)w^n \sum_{\sum m k_m=n}\frac{1}{\prod m^{k_m}k_m!}.
$$

By Exercise 21, the sum equals 1, since it accounts for all permutations of $n$ objects. Hence

$$
\mathbb{P}(S=n)=(1-w)w^n.
$$

Summing over $n\ge 0$ gives 1, so $\mathbb{P}(S=\infty)=0$.

## (c) Averaging a general function $\phi$

We compute directly from (1):

$$
\mathbb{E}[\phi]
=\sum_{(k_m)} \phi(k_1,k_2,\ldots)\,(1-w)\prod_{m\ge 1}\frac{(w^m/m)^{k_m}}{k_m!}.
$$

Group terms by $n=\sum m k_m$:

$$
\mathbb{E}[\phi]
=(1-w)\sum_{n\ge 0} w^n
\sum_{\sum m k_m=n}
\phi(k_1,k_2,\ldots)\prod_{m\ge 1}\frac{1}{m^{k_m}k_m!}.
$$

The inner sum is exactly the average of $\phi$ over permutations of $n$ objects, i.e. $\phi_n$. Therefore

$$
\mathbb{E}[\phi]=(1-w)\sum_{n\ge 0} w^n \phi_n.
$$

## (d) Expected number of even cycles in a permutation of size $n$

Let $X_m$ be the number of $m$-cycles. We compute $\mathbb{E}[X_m]$ in a uniform permutation of $n$ objects.

Fix a set of $m$ elements. The probability they form an $m$-cycle is:

- there are $(m-1)!$ cycles on these elements,
- total permutations is $n!$,
- fixing them as a cycle leaves $(n-m)!$ arrangements.

Thus probability a given $m$-set forms an $m$-cycle is

$$
\frac{(m-1)!(n-m)!}{n!}=\frac{1}{m\binom{n}{m}}.
$$

There are $\binom{n}{m}$ choices of the set, so

$$
\mathbb{E}[X_m]=\frac{1}{m}.
$$

Hence the expected number of even cycles is

$$
\phi_n=\sum_{\substack{m\le n\\ m\ \text{even}}}\frac{1}{m}.
$$

Now apply part (c):

$$
\mathbb{E}[\text{even cycles}]
=(1-w)\sum_{n\ge 0} w^n \sum_{\substack{m\le n\\ m\ \text{even}}}\frac{1}{m}.
$$

Interchange sums:

$$
=(1-w)\sum_{m\ \text{even}} \frac{1}{m}\sum_{n\ge m} w^n
=(1-w)\sum_{m\ \text{even}} \frac{w^m}{m(1-w)}.
$$

Thus

$$
\sum_{m\ \text{even}} \frac{w^m}{m}
=\sum_{k\ge 1}\frac{w^{2k}}{2k}
=-\frac{1}{2}\log(1-w^2).
$$

## (e) Application (Exercise 18)

Let $\phi(\alpha)=\alpha_1$. Then $\phi_n=\mathbb{E}[X_1]=1$ for all $n$. Hence by (c),

$$
\mathbb{E}[\alpha_1]=(1-w)\sum_{n\ge 0} w^n=1.
$$

## Summary

The key correction is that the Poisson form is not assumed: it is derived by explicitly summing condition (2) over all coordinates and using the exponential generating structure encoded in Exercise 21. All later parts then follow without circular reasoning.
