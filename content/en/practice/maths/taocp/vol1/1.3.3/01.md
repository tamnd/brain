**Exercise 1.** [*02*] Consider the transformation of $\{0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}$ that replaces $x$ by $2x \bmod 7$. Show that this transformation is a permutation, and write it in cycle form.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m35s  

---

Consider the mapping $f(x)=2x \bmod 7$ on ${0,1,2,3,4,5,6}$. Since $\gcd(2,7)=1$, there exists an inverse multiplier $4$ with $2\cdot 4 \equiv 1 \pmod 7$, so each residue class has a unique preimage under $f$. Hence $f$ is a bijection of the set onto itself and therefore a permutation.

Compute images:

$$
0\mapsto 0,\quad 1\mapsto 2,\quad 2\mapsto 4,\quad 4\mapsto 1,
$$

so $(1,2,4)$ is a cycle. Also,

$$
3\mapsto 6,\quad 6\mapsto 5,\quad 5\mapsto 3,
$$

so $(3,6,5)$ is a cycle. The element $0$ is fixed and contributes a singleton cycle $(0)$, which is omitted in standard cycle notation. Therefore the permutation is

$$
\boxed{(1\,2\,4)(3\,6\,5)}.
$$
