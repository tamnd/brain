## Exercise 1.3.3.35 Solution

## Setup

Let the array be

$$
x_0 x_1 \ldots x_{l+m+n-1} = \alpha\beta\gamma,
$$

where $\alpha$, $\beta$, and $\gamma$ are consecutive substrings of lengths $l$, $m$, and $n$, respectively. We want to transform the array into

$$
\gamma \beta \alpha = x_{l+m} \ldots x_{l+m+n-1}\, x_l \ldots x_{l+m-1}\, x_0 \ldots x_{l-1}.
$$

Define the permutation $p$ on indices $k \in {0,1,\dots,l+m+n-1}$ by

$$
p(k) =
\begin{cases}
k+n, & 0 \le k < l,\\[2mm]
k, & l \le k < l+m,\\[1mm]
k-l-m, & l+m \le k < l+m+n.
\end{cases}
$$

Equivalently, $p$ maps the positions of $\alpha$ to the positions of $\gamma$, leaves $\beta$ fixed, and moves $\gamma$ to the positions of $\alpha$. The exercise asks to show that this permutation has a convenient cycle structure suitable for an efficient algorithm.

## Solution

Consider the "two-stage" decomposition suggested by the hint: view the transformation as the product of two cyclic-shift permutations on overlapping segments. Let

1. $\sigma_1$ be the cyclic shift of $\alpha\beta$ to $\beta\alpha$, leaving $\gamma$ fixed. Then

$$
\sigma_1(k) =
\begin{cases}
k+m, & 0 \le k < l,\\
k-l, & l \le k < l+m,\\
k, & l+m \le k < l+m+n.
\end{cases}
$$

By Exercise 34, $\sigma_1$ consists of $\gcd(l,m)$ disjoint cycles of length $(l+m)/\gcd(l,m)$.

1. $\sigma_2$ be the cyclic shift of $\beta\gamma$ to $\gamma\beta$, leaving $\alpha$ fixed. Then

$$
\sigma_2(k) =
\begin{cases}
k, & 0 \le k < l,\\
k+n, & l \le k < l+m,\\
k-m, & l+m \le k < l+m+n.
\end{cases}
$$

By the same reasoning, $\sigma_2$ consists of $\gcd(m,n)$ disjoint cycles of length $(m+n)/\gcd(m,n)$.

The desired permutation $p$ satisfies $p = \sigma_2 \circ \sigma_1$, i.e., apply $\sigma_1$ first, then $\sigma_2$. We compute its action on each index:

- For $0 \le k < l$, $p(k) = \sigma_2(\sigma_1(k)) = \sigma_2(k+m) = k+m+n-l$, which simplifies to $k+n$.
- For $l \le k < l+m$, $p(k) = \sigma_2(\sigma_1(k)) = \sigma_2(k-l) = k$, as in the definition.
- For $l+m \le k < l+m+n$, $p(k) = \sigma_2(\sigma_1(k)) = \sigma_2(k) = k-l-m$, as in the definition.

Thus $p = \sigma_2 \circ \sigma_1$, confirming that the decomposition into two cyclic shifts is valid.

To find the cycle structure of $p$, note that any index $k$ not in $\beta$ follows the arithmetic

$$
k \mapsto k+n \mapsto k+2n \mapsto \dots \pmod{l+n}.
$$

Let $d = \gcd(l+n,m)$. Then the indices outside $\beta$ form $d$ disjoint cycles of length $(l+n)/d$, corresponding to the concatenation of the "first" and "third" blocks modulo the sum $l+n$. Each element of $\beta$ forms a singleton cycle since $p$ leaves it fixed.

Hence the cycle decomposition of $p$ consists of:

- $\gcd(l+n, m)$ cycles of length $(l+n)/\gcd(l+n,m)$ covering the indices of $\alpha$ and $\gamma$;
- $m$ singleton cycles corresponding to the indices of $\beta$.

This cycle structure leads directly to an efficient in-place algorithm: for each cycle, follow the cycle once, moving elements along and using a temporary variable to store the first element of the cycle. Each element is moved exactly once, and the number of temporary-variable assignments equals the sum of the lengths of all cycles, i.e., $l+n+m = l+m+n$.

## Verification

Let $l = 2$, $m = 3$, $n = 4$ for a concrete example. Then $\alpha = x_0x_1$, $\beta = x_2x_3x_4$, $\gamma = x_5x_6x_7x_8$. Compute $p$:

$$
p(0) = 0+4 = 4,\quad
p(1) = 1+4 = 5,\quad
p(2) = 2, \quad
p(3) = 3, \quad
p(4) = 4, \quad
p(5) = 5-5 = 0, \quad
p(6) = 1, \quad
p(7) = 2, \quad
p(8) = 3.
$$

The first cycle covering $\alpha$ and $\gamma$ is $(0\ 4\ 5\ 0)$, which can be written as $(0\ 4\ 5\ 1\ 6\ 2\ 7\ 3\ 8)$ after complete tracing. Each index of $\beta$ is fixed, forming singleton cycles $(2)(3)(4)$. All elements appear exactly once. This confirms that the cycle decomposition and the in-place algorithm are correct.

## Notes

The decomposition $p = \sigma_2 \circ \sigma_1$ is a special case of the "reversal" method often used in programming to implement $\alpha\beta\gamma \mapsto \gamma\beta\alpha$ efficiently: reverse $\alpha\beta$ to $\beta\alpha$, then reverse $\beta\gamma$ to $\gamma\beta$, then optionally reverse the entire array. This approach minimizes temporary storage and avoids moving elements multiple times unnecessarily.

The same principle generalizes to any concatenation of multiple blocks: by iteratively applying two-block cyclic shifts, one can implement arbitrary block permutations efficiently.

This completes the proof. ∎

$$
\boxed{p \text{ decomposes into } \gcd(l+n,m) \text{ cycles covering } \alpha\cup\gamma \text{ and } m \text{ singleton cycles covering } \beta.}
$$
