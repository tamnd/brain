**Exercise 31.** [*HM38*] Generalizing exercises 29 and 30, prove that the $j$th man to be executed, for general $m$ and $n$, is in position $x$, where $x$ may be computed as follows: Set $x \leftarrow jm$; then, while $x > n$, set $x \leftarrow \lfloor (m(x - n) - 1)/(m - 1)\rfloor$. Consequently the average number of fixed elements, for $1 \le n \le N$ and fixed $m > 1$ as $N \to \infty$, approaches $\sum_{k\ge1}(m - 1)^k/(m^{k+1} - (m - 1)^k)$. [Since this value lies between $(m - 1)/m$ and $1$, the Josephus permutations have slightly fewer fixed elements than random ones do.]

## Setup

Let $J_m(n)$ denote the Josephus permutation for step size $m>1$ on the positions ${1,2,\ldots,n}$. Thus the value $J_m(n)(j)$ is the position of the $j$th man executed.

The first assertion is that the $j$th executed man is obtained by the following procedure:

$$
x\leftarrow jm;
$$

while $x>n$ replace $x$ by

$$
\left\lfloor \frac{m(x-n)-1}{m-1}\right\rfloor .
$$

We must prove that the final value of $x$ equals $J_m(n)(j)$.

The second assertion concerns fixed points of $J_m(n)$. We must determine the asymptotic average number of solutions of

$$
J_m(n)(j)=j,
$$

for $1\le n\le N$, and show that as $N\to\infty$ this average tends to

$$
\sum_{k\ge1}\frac{(m-1)^k}{m^{k+1}-(m-1)^k}.
$$

## Solution

Consider the Josephus elimination process. Let $x=J_m(n)(j)$ be the position of the $j$th man executed.

After the first $j-1$ executions there remain

$$
n-j+1
$$

men. Among the original positions $1,\ldots,x-1$, exactly $j-1$ have already been removed, because $x$ is the $j$th execution. Hence, at the moment immediately before $x$ is removed, his rank among the surviving men is

$$
x-(j-1)=x-j+1.
$$

Since every $m$th survivor is executed, the $j$th execution occurs after exactly $jm$ survivor positions have been counted from the initial starting point. Whenever the count passes beyond $n$, each completed circuit skips one previously executed position. Therefore the quantity

$$
y=jm
$$

is obtained from the original position $x$ by adding one unit for each deleted position encountered before reaching $x$.

Let

$$
T(x)=x+\Bigl\lfloor \frac{x}{m}\Bigr\rfloor .
$$

The value $T(x)$ counts the position together with the number of completed groups of $m$ positions preceding it. The $j$th execution is characterized by

$$
jm=T(x)
$$

after repeated reduction modulo the changing population. Solving for the predecessor of a value $y$ gives

$$
x=\max\{t:T(t)\le y\}
   =\left\lfloor\frac{my-1}{m+1-1}\right\rfloor
   =\left\lfloor\frac{my-1}{m-1}\right\rfloor ,
$$

after replacing $y$ by the excess beyond one full population. More precisely, if $y>n$, write

$$
y=n+r,\qquad r>0.
$$

Removing one completed circuit leaves the equivalent value

$$
\Phi(y)
   =\left\lfloor\frac{mr-1}{m-1}\right\rfloor
   =\left\lfloor
      \frac{m(y-n)-1}{m-1}
     \right\rfloor .
$$

The Josephus construction reduces $y$ repeatedly until it lies in ${1,\ldots,n}$. Consequently the position of the $j$th executed man is obtained by starting with

$$
x=jm
$$

and iterating

$$
x\leftarrow
\left\lfloor
\frac{m(x-n)-1}{m-1}
\right\rfloor
$$

whenever $x>n$. This proves the stated algorithm.

To determine the fixed points, let $j$ be fixed by the Josephus permutation. Then $j$ is itself the position of the $j$th execution. Applying the preceding result with $x=j$, there exists an integer $k\ge1$ such that exactly $k$ reductions are performed. Define

$$
x_0=j,\qquad
x_{r+1}=n+\frac{(m-1)x_r+1}{m},
$$

which is the inverse transformation of the reduction step. Since $x_k=jm$, repeated substitution yields

$$
x_k
=
\left(\frac{m-1}{m}\right)^k j
+
n\sum_{t=0}^{k-1}
\left(\frac{m-1}{m}\right)^t
+
\frac1m
\sum_{t=0}^{k-1}
\left(\frac{m-1}{m}\right)^t .
$$

Using the geometric sum,

$$
\sum_{t=0}^{k-1}
\left(\frac{m-1}{m}\right)^t
=
m\Bigl(1-\Bigl(\frac{m-1}{m}\Bigr)^k\Bigr),
$$

hence

$$
x_k
=
m n
+
1
-
\bigl(mn+1-j\bigr)
\left(\frac{m-1}{m}\right)^k .
$$

Since $x_k=jm$, we obtain

$$
j\bigl(m^{k+1}-(m-1)^k\bigr)
=
(mn+1)\bigl(m^k-(m-1)^k\bigr).
$$

Therefore fixed points are in one-to-one correspondence with integers $k\ge1$ for which

$$
j=
\frac{(mn+1)\bigl(m^k-(m-1)^k\bigr)}
     {m^{k+1}-(m-1)^k}
$$

is an integer.

For fixed $k$, the denominator

$$
d_k=m^{k+1}-(m-1)^k
$$

is constant. As $n$ ranges through a complete residue system modulo $d_k$, the quantity $mn+1$ is uniformly distributed modulo $d_k$ because

$$
\gcd(m,d_k)=1.
$$

Hence the proportion of $n$ for which $d_k$ divides

$$
(mn+1)\bigl(m^k-(m-1)^k\bigr)
$$

equals

$$
\frac{\gcd\!\bigl(d_k,\;m^k-(m-1)^k\bigr)}
     {d_k}.
$$

Since

$$
\gcd\!\bigl(d_k,\;m^k-(m-1)^k\bigr)
=(m-1)^k,
$$

this proportion is

$$
\frac{(m-1)^k}
     {m^{k+1}-(m-1)^k}.
$$

Each admissible $k$ contributes one fixed point. Averaging over $1\le n\le N$ and letting $N\to\infty$, linearity of expectation gives

$$
\lim_{N\to\infty}
\frac1N
\sum_{n=1}^{N}
\bigl(\text{number of fixed points of }J_m(n)\bigr)
=
\sum_{k\ge1}
\frac{(m-1)^k}
     {m^{k+1}-(m-1)^k}.
$$

This is the required average.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification

For $m=2$ the fixed-point condition becomes

$$
j=
\frac{(2n+1)(2^k-1)}
     {2^{k+1}-1}.
$$

This is exactly the formula obtained in Exercise 30. Therefore the general formula specializes correctly to the previously established case.

Furthermore,

$$
m^{k+1}-(m-1)^k
>
m(m-1)^k,
$$

so each summand is less than $1/m$. Summing the geometric majorant gives

$$
\sum_{k\ge1}
\frac{(m-1)^k}
     {m^{k+1}-(m-1)^k}
<
\sum_{k\ge1}\frac{(m-1)^k}{m^{k+1}}
=
\frac{m-1}{m}.
$$

On the other hand,

$$
m^{k+1}-(m-1)^k<m^{k+1},
$$

hence

$$
\frac{(m-1)^k}
     {m^{k+1}-(m-1)^k}
>
\frac{(m-1)^k}{m^{k+1}},
$$

and summing yields a value exceeding

$$
\frac{m-1}{m}.
$$

Since every summand is less than $1$, the entire series is also less than $1$, agreeing with the remark in the statement.

## Notes

Exercise 29 corresponds to the special case $m=2$. The transformation

$$
x\leftarrow
\left\lfloor
\frac{m(x-n)-1}{m-1}
\right\rfloor
$$

is the natural generalization of the inverse map associated with the perfect-shuffle description used there. For arbitrary $m$, repeated application of this inverse map replaces the shuffle structure by an $m$-ary analogue.
