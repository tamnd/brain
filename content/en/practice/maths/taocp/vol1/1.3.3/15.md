[Section 1.3.3: Applications to Permutations](../)

**Exercise 15.** [*M12*] Is there a permutation that represents exactly the same transformation both in the canonical cycle form without parentheses and in the linear form?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m16s  

---

Let $\pi$ be a permutation of $\{1,2,\dots,n\}$.

Write the **one-line form** as

$$
(\pi(1)\ \pi(2)\ \cdots\ \pi(n)).
$$

Write the **canonical cycle form** with cycles ordered by increasing smallest element, each cycle starting with its smallest element:

$$
(x_{11}\ x_{12}\ \cdots\ x_{1k_1})(x_{21}\ x_{22}\ \cdots\ x_{2k_2})\cdots(x_{r1}\ x_{r2}\ \cdots\ x_{rk_r}),
$$

where $x_{11} < x_{21} < \cdots < x_{r1}$, and each cycle is written so that $x_{i1}$ is its smallest element.

Removing parentheses yields the sequence

$$
S = (x_{11}, x_{12}, \dots, x_{1k_1}, x_{21}, \dots, x_{r k_r}).
$$

We are asked whether it is possible that

$$
(\pi(1)\ \pi(2)\ \cdots\ \pi(n)) = (x_{11}, x_{12}, \dots, x_{r k_r}),
$$

as sequences.

## Step 1: The first entry forces $1$ to be fixed

In canonical cycle form, the first cycle begins with the smallest element of the entire permutation, hence $x_{11} = 1$. Therefore the concatenated sequence satisfies

$$
S[1] = 1.
$$

Equality of the two forms implies

$$
\pi(1) = 1.
$$

Thus $1$ is a fixed point and forms a singleton cycle $(1)$.

## Step 2: Remove the fixed point safely

Define a new permutation $\pi'$ on $\{2,3,\dots,n\}$ obtained by restricting $\pi$ to this set.

Since $1$ is a singleton cycle, removing it from the canonical cycle decomposition of $\pi$ produces exactly the canonical cycle decomposition of $\pi'$, with the same ordering of cycles (all remaining cycles are unaffected and their smallest elements remain unchanged relative to each other).

Similarly, removing the entry $1$ from the one-line form of $\pi$ produces the one-line form of $\pi'$, namely

$$
(\pi(2)\ \pi(3)\ \cdots\ \pi(n)).
$$

Because the original one-line form and concatenated cycle sequence are identical, deleting the same value $1$ from both sequences preserves equality. Hence $\pi'$ also satisfies the same property: its one-line form equals the concatenation of its canonical cycle form.

## Step 3: Induction

We have shown:

- $\pi(1)=1$,
- the restriction $\pi'$ on $\{2,\dots,n\}$ satisfies the same condition.

By induction on $n$, the only permutation on $\{2,\dots,n\}$ with this property is the identity permutation. Therefore,

$$
\pi(i)=i \quad \text{for all } i \ge 2.
$$

Combining with $\pi(1)=1$, we obtain

$$
\pi(i)=i \quad \text{for all } i.
$$

## Step 4: Verification

For the identity permutation, the canonical cycle form is

$$
(1)(2)\cdots(n),
$$

and removing parentheses yields

$$
(1\ 2\ \cdots\ n),
$$

which matches the one-line form.

## Conclusion

The only permutation whose canonical cycle form (with parentheses removed) coincides with its one-line notation is the identity permutation:

$$
\boxed{\pi = \mathrm{id}}.
$$
