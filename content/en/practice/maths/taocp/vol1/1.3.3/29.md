**Exercise 29.** [*M25*] Prove that the cycle form of the Josephus permutation when $m = 2$ can be obtained by first expressing the "perfect shuffle" permutation of $\{1, 2, \ldots, 2n\}$, which takes $(1, 2, \ldots, 2n)$ into $(2, 4, \ldots, 2n, 1, 3, \ldots, 2n - 1)$, in cycle form, then reversing left and right and erasing all the numbers greater than $n$. For example, when $n = 11$ the perfect shuffle is $(1\,2\,4\,8\,16\,9\,18\,13\,3\,6\,12)(5\,10\,20\,17\,11\,22\,21\,19\,15\,7\,14)$ and the Josephus permutation is $(7\,11\,10\,5)(6\,3\,9\,8\,4\,2\,1)$.

## Solution

Let $S$ denote the perfect shuffle permutation on ${1,2,\ldots,2n}$. By definition,

$$
S(i)=
\begin{cases}
2i, & 1\le i\le n,\\[2mm]
2(i-n)-1, & n<i\le 2n.
\end{cases}
$$

Equivalently,

$$
S(i)=
\begin{cases}
2i, & 2i\le 2n,\\[2mm]
2i-(2n+1), & 2i>2n.
\end{cases}
$$

Hence, for every $i$,

$$
S(i)\equiv 2i \pmod{2n+1}.
\tag{1}
$$

The Josephus permutation for $m=2$ is obtained by repeatedly removing every second survivor. Let $J$ denote this permutation. We shall show that if a cycle of $S$ is written in reverse order and all entries exceeding $n$ are deleted, the resulting cycles are precisely the cycles of $J$.

Consider the Josephus process. Suppose that after some eliminations there remain $k$ persons. Number the survivors cyclically beginning with the next person to be counted. The next eliminated person is then the second survivor in this cyclic ordering.

A standard description of the case $m=2$ is obtained by recording the eliminations in reverse order. Let

$$
x_0,x_1,x_2,\ldots
$$

be successive elements of a cycle of the perfect shuffle. By (1),

$$
x_{r+1}\equiv 2x_r \pmod{2n+1}.
\tag{2}
$$

Therefore

$$
x_r\equiv 2^r x_0 \pmod{2n+1}.
\tag{3}
$$

Now reverse the cycle. Consecutive elements in the reversed cycle satisfy

$$
x_r \equiv 2x_{r-1}\pmod{2n+1},
$$

or equivalently,

$$
x_{r-1}\equiv \frac{x_r}{2}\pmod{2n+1}.
\tag{4}
$$

Since $2n+1$ is odd, division by $2$ modulo $2n+1$ is well defined. Relation (4) is exactly the recurrence governing the Josephus problem with step size $2$: tracing the eliminations backward replaces a label by its predecessor under halving modulo $2n+1$.

The numbers greater than $n$ never occur as labels of persons in the Josephus problem. They arise only because the perfect shuffle acts on the larger set ${1,\ldots,2n}$. When a reversed shuffle cycle is traversed, every transition from one number $\le n$ to the next number $\le n$ is obtained by passing through some intermediate numbers $>n$. Deleting all entries greater than $n$ therefore compresses the reversed shuffle cycle to the orbit of the corresponding Josephus permutation on ${1,\ldots,n}$.

It remains to prove that every Josephus orbit is obtained in this way. The cycles of $S$ partition ${1,\ldots,2n}$. Reversing them still yields a partition of ${1,\ldots,2n}$. Every number $1,\ldots,n$ appears in exactly one reversed cycle, and after deleting all numbers exceeding $n$ it still appears exactly once. Hence the resulting cycles partition ${1,\ldots,n}$. By the preceding argument, the successor relation within each such cycle is exactly the successor relation of the Josephus permutation $J$. Therefore the resulting cycle decomposition is the cycle form of $J$.

For the example $n=11$,

$$
(1\,2\,4\,8\,16\,9\,18\,13\,3\,6\,12)
(5\,10\,20\,17\,11\,22\,21\,19\,15\,7\,14)
$$

is the cycle form of the perfect shuffle. Reversing the cycles gives

$$
(12\,6\,3\,13\,18\,9\,16\,8\,4\,2\,1)
(14\,7\,15\,19\,21\,22\,11\,17\,20\,10\,5).
$$

Erasing all numbers greater than $11$ yields

$$
(6\,3\,9\,8\,4\,2\,1)(7\,11\,10\,5),
$$

which is the Josephus permutation stated in the exercise.

This completes the proof.

∎
