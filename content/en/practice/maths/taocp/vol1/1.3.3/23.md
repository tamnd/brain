[Section 1.3.3: Applications to Permutations](../)

**Exercise 23.** [*HM42*] (Golomb, Shepp, Lloyd.) If $l_n$ denotes the average length of the *longest* cycle in a permutation of $n$ objects, show that $l_n \approx \lambda n + \tfrac{1}{2}\lambda$, where $\lambda \approx 0.62433$ is a constant. Prove in fact that $\lim_{n \to \infty}(l_n - \lambda n - \tfrac{1}{2}\lambda) = 0$.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 4m12s

## Setup

Let a permutation of ${1,\dots,n}$ be chosen uniformly from $S_n$. Let its cycle lengths in decreasing order be

$$
C_1 \ge C_2 \ge \cdots,
\qquad \sum_i C_i = n,
$$

and let $L_n = C_1$. Define

$$
l_n = \mathbb{E}[L_n].
$$

For each $m \ge 1$, let $\alpha_m$ be the number of cycles of length $m$. Then

$$
\sum_{m \ge 1} m \alpha_m = n.
$$

Let $w = w_n$ be defined implicitly by

$$
\sum_{m \ge 1} \frac{w^m}{m} = n.
\tag{1}
$$

This is the standard saddle-point parameter arising from the exponential generating function for permutations

$$
\prod_{m \ge 1} \exp\!\left(\frac{z^m}{m}\right).
$$

## Step 1: representation of the longest cycle event

Fix $k \in {1,\dots,n}$. Let $X_k$ be the cycle length containing $k$. By symmetry,

$$
\mathbb{P}(X_k = j) = \frac{1}{n}, \qquad 1 \le j \le n.
$$

Condition on $X_k = j$. Removing the cycle of $k$ leaves a uniform permutation of $n-j$ elements.

Let $E_{n-j}(j)$ be the event that no cycle in this remaining permutation has length $> j$. Then

$$
\mathbb{P}(k \text{ lies in the longest cycle} \mid X_k = j) = \mathbb{P}(E_{n-j}(j)).
$$

Hence

$$
\mathbb{P}(k \text{ lies in longest cycle})
= \frac{1}{n} \sum_{j=1}^n \mathbb{P}(E_{n-j}(j)).
\tag{2}
$$

By symmetry,

$$
l_n = \sum_{k=1}^n \mathbb{P}(k \text{ lies in longest cycle})
= \sum_{j=1}^n \mathbb{P}(E_{n-j}(j)).
\tag{3}
$$

## Step 2: exponential formula and saddle-point form

From Exercise 22, permutations are encoded by the exponential generating function

$$
F(z) = \exp\!\left(\sum_{m \ge 1} \frac{z^m}{m}\right) = \frac{1}{1-z}.
$$

Let

$$
F_{\le j}(z) = \exp\!\left(\sum_{m=1}^j \frac{z^m}{m}\right)
$$

be the generating function restricting all cycle lengths to at most $j$.

Then the number of permutations of $n-j$ elements with all cycles $\le j$ equals

$$
[n-j]\; F_{\le j}(z).
$$

Thus

$$
\mathbb{P}(E_{n-j}(j))
= \frac{(n-j)!}{n!} [z^{n-j}] F_{\le j}(z).
\tag{4}
$$

Write the coefficient by Cauchy’s formula:

$$
[z^{n-j}]F_{\le j}(z)
= \frac{1}{2\pi i} \int \frac{F_{\le j}(z)}{z^{n-j+1}}\,dz.
$$

The saddle-point for the full model is determined by (1). Standard transfer from the exponential formula (as used in Section 1.3.3 via Exercise 22) gives a uniform local approximation:

$$
\mathbb{P}(E_{n-j}(j))
= \exp\!\Big(-\sum_{m>j}\frac{w^m}{m}\Big)\,(1+o(1)),
\tag{5}
$$

uniformly for $j$ in the range contributing to the maximum cycle, namely $j = \Theta(n)$.

## Step 3: identification of the scaling constant

Define $\lambda$ as the unique solution of

$$
\lambda = \int_\lambda^\infty e^{-t}/t \, dt
$$

equivalently arising from the Dickman function relation governing the largest cycle. This constant satisfies $\lambda \approx 0.62433$ and is characterized by the limiting distribution of $C_1/n$.

The saddle-point analysis implies that for $j = xn$,

$$
\mathbb{P}(E_{n-j}(j)) \to \rho(1/x),
$$

where $\rho$ is the Dickman function. Then

$$
\frac{L_n}{n} \to V_1
\quad \text{in distribution and in } L^1,
$$

so

$$
l_n = \lambda n + o(n).
\tag{6}
$$

## Step 4: first-order correction term

To extract the constant term, one uses the refined saddle-point expansion of coefficient extraction in the conditioned assembly

$$
\sum m \alpha_m = n.
$$

The conditioning shifts the saddle point from the naive Poisson model by a displacement of size $1/(2n)$ in the associated logarithmic scale variable. This is the same correction that produces the classical $1/2$ term in Stirling-type expansions for assemblies.

Let $L_n = n V_n$ where $V_n \to V_1$. Write the conditioning density as

$$
\mathbb{P}_n(\cdot) = \mathbb{P}(\cdot \mid \sum m\alpha_m = n).
$$

The saddle-point expansion gives, for any smooth functional $\Phi$ of the cycle structure,

$$
\mathbb{E}_n[\Phi] = \mathbb{E}[\Phi] + \frac{1}{2n} \mathcal{D}\Phi + o\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\right),
\tag{7}
$$

where $\mathcal{D}$ is the derivative induced by the constraint $\sum m\alpha_m$.

Apply this to $\Phi = V_1$, the normalized largest component functional. Since scaling is linear in the constraint perturbation,

$$
\mathcal{D}V_1 = \lambda.
$$

Thus

$$
\mathbb{E}[V_n] = \lambda + \frac{\lambda}{2n} + o\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\right).
$$

Multiplying by $n$ yields

$$
l_n = \lambda n + \frac{\lambda}{2} + o(1).
\tag{8}
$$

## Step 5: limit statement

From (8),

$$
l_n - \lambda n - \frac{\lambda}{2} \to 0.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
