**Exercise 21.** [*M22*] What is the probability $P(n;\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\ldots)$ that a permutation of $n$ objects has exactly $\alpha_1$ one-cycles, $\alpha_2$ two-cycles, etc.?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 5m40s  

---

## Solution

Let

$$
\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\ldots
$$

denote the numbers of cycles of lengths

$$
1,2,\ldots,
$$

respectively. A permutation of $n$ objects can have this cycle structure only if

$$
\sum_{m\ge1} m\alpha_m=n.
\tag{1}
$$

We first count the permutations having the prescribed cycle structure.

Choose the elements belonging to the various cycles. Since the $n$ elements are distinct, the number of ways to partition them into labeled groups consisting of $\alpha_m$ groups of size $m$ for each $m$ is

$$
\frac{n!}{\prod_{m\ge1}(m!)^{\alpha_m}}.
\tag{2}
$$

Within a group of $m$ specified elements, the number of distinct $m$-cycles is

$$
\frac{m!}{m}=(m-1)!,
$$

because the $m$ cyclic rotations represent the same cycle. Hence the groups of size $m$ can be arranged into cycles in

$$
((m-1)!)^{\alpha_m}
$$

ways.

The $\alpha_m$ cycles of length $m$ are not distinguished from one another. Dividing by $\alpha_m!$ for each $m$, the total number of permutations with cycle structure

$$
(\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\ldots)
$$

is

$$
N(n;\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\ldots)
=
\frac{n!}{\prod_{m\ge1}(m!)^{\alpha_m}}
\prod_{m\ge1}
\frac{((m-1)!)^{\alpha_m}}{\alpha_m!}.
$$

Since

$$
\frac{(m-1)!}{m!}=\frac1m,
$$

this simplifies to

$$
N(n;\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\ldots)
=
\frac{n!}
{\prod_{m\ge1} m^{\alpha_m}\alpha_m!}.
\tag{3}
$$

There are $n!$ permutations altogether. Therefore the required probability is

$$
P(n;\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\ldots)
=
\frac{N(n;\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\ldots)}{n!}
=
\frac{1}
{\prod_{m\ge1} m^{\alpha_m}\alpha_m!},
$$

subject to the condition (1). If (1) is not satisfied, no such permutation exists and the probability is $0$.

Thus

$$
\boxed{
P(n;\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\ldots)=
\begin{cases}
\displaystyle
\frac{1}{\prod_{m\ge1} m^{\alpha_m}\alpha_m!},
&
\sum_{m\ge1}m\alpha_m=n,
\\[2ex]
0,
&
\sum_{m\ge1}m\alpha_m\ne n.
\end{cases}
}
$$

This completes the proof.

∎
