**Exercise 17.** [*M24*] (a) The text demonstrates that there are $n!H_n$ cycles altogether, among all the permutations on $n$ elements. If these cycles (including singleton cycles) are individually written on $n!H_n$ slips of paper, and if one of these slips of paper is chosen at random, what is the average length of the cycle that is thereby picked? (b) If we write the $n!$ permutations on $n!$ slips of paper, and if we choose a number $k$ at random and also choose one of the slips of paper, what is the probability that the cycle containing $k$ on that slip is an $m$-cycle? What is the average length of the cycle containing $k$?

## Solution

### (a)

Let all cycles occurring in all permutations of $n$ elements be listed, including singleton cycles. Each permutation decomposes uniquely into disjoint cycles whose total length is $n$, so summing the lengths of all cycles in a fixed permutation gives $n$.

Summing over all $n!$ permutations, the total sum of lengths of all cycles equals $n \cdot n!$.

The number of cycles occurring across all permutations is given as $n! H_n$. Selecting one cycle uniformly from this collection assigns equal weight to each cycle occurrence. The average cycle length is therefore the ratio of total cycle length to total number of cycles:

$$
\frac{n \cdot n!}{n! H_n} = \frac{n}{H_n}.
$$

Hence the average length is

$$
\boxed{\frac{n}{H_n}}.
$$

### (b)

Fix an element $k \in {1,\dots,n}$. A permutation is chosen uniformly from the $n!$ permutations, and $k$ is fixed as the reference element. The cycle containing $k$ has length $m$ if and only if $k$ lies in a cycle of size $m$ in the permutation.

Count permutations in which the cycle of $k$ has length $m$. First choose the remaining $m-1$ elements of the cycle from the other $n-1$ elements, which can be done in $\binom{n-1}{m-1}$ ways. Arrange these $m$ elements into a cycle containing $k$, which can be done in $(m-1)!$ ways since fixing $k$ removes rotational symmetry. The remaining $n-m$ elements may be permuted arbitrarily, contributing $(n-m)!$ possibilities.

The total number of such permutations is therefore

$$
\binom{n-1}{m-1}(m-1)!(n-m)!.
$$

This expression simplifies as

$$
\binom{n-1}{m-1}(m-1)!(n-m)! = \frac{(n-1)!}{(m-1)!(n-m)!}(m-1)!(n-m)! = (n-1)!,
$$

which is independent of $m$.

Dividing by the total number of permutations $n!$, the probability that the cycle containing $k$ has length $m$ is

$$
\frac{(n-1)!}{n!} = \frac{1}{n}.
$$

Thus

$$
\boxed{\mathbb{P}(\text{cycle of }k\text{ has length }m) = \frac{1}{n}}.
$$

The expected length of the cycle containing $k$ is

$$
\sum_{m=1}^n m \cdot \frac{1}{n} = \frac{1}{n} \cdot \frac{n(n+1)}{2} = \frac{n+1}{2}.
$$

Hence

$$
\boxed{\frac{n+1}{2}}.
$$

This completes the solution. ∎
