[Section 1.3.3: Applications to Permutations](../)

**Exercise 33.** [*M33*] If $m = 2^{2^l}$ and $n = 2^{2l+1}$, show how to construct sequences of permutations $(\alpha_{j1}, \alpha_{j2}, \ldots, \alpha_{jn}; \beta_{j1}, \beta_{j2}, \ldots, \beta_{jn})$ for $0 \le j < m$ with the following "orthogonality" property:

$$
\alpha_{i1}\beta_{j1}\alpha_{i2}\beta_{j2}\cdots\alpha_{in}\beta_{jn}
=
\begin{cases}
(1\,2\,3\,4\,5), & \text{if } i = j;\\
(), & \text{if } i \ne j.
\end{cases}
$$

Each $\alpha_{jk}$ and $\beta_{jk}$ should be a permutation of $\{1, 2, 3, 4, 5\}$.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 3m50s  

---

## Setup

Let

$$
m=2^{2^l},\qquad n=2^{2l+1}.
$$

We must construct, for each integer $j$ with $0\le j<m$, permutations

$$
\alpha_{j1},\alpha_{j2},\ldots,\alpha_{jn},
\qquad
\beta_{j1},\beta_{j2},\ldots,\beta_{jn},
$$

of the set $\{1,2,3,4,5\}$ such that

$$
\alpha_{i1}\beta_{j1}\alpha_{i2}\beta_{j2}\cdots
\alpha_{in}\beta_{jn}
=
\begin{cases}
(1\,2\,3\,4\,5),&i=j,\\[2mm]
(),&i\ne j.
\end{cases}
\tag{1}
$$

Let

$$
c=(1\,2\,3\,4\,5).
$$

Since $c$ has order $5$, the cyclic subgroup

C=\{(),c,c^2,c^3,c^4\} \] is isomorphic to the additive group \(\mathbf Z_5\). The integer \(m=2^{2^l}\) satisfies

m=(2^l)^2=\left(\frac n2\right)^2.

]

Hence every $j\in\{0,\ldots,m-1\}$ can be represented uniquely as

$$
j=a\,2^l+b,
\qquad
0\le a,b<2^l.
\tag{2}
$$

Thus the indices $j$ may be identified with ordered pairs $(a,b)$ in a $2^l\times2^l$ array.

## Solution

For $0\le r<2^l$, define the binary vector

$$
v_r=(v_{r1},v_{r2},\ldots,v_{r,2^l}),
$$

whose coordinates are the bits of the binary expansion of $r$.

For two indices $i=(a,b)$ and $j=(a',b')$, define

$$
L(i,j)
=
\sum_{t=1}^{2^l} v_{a,t}v_{a',t}
-
\sum_{t=1}^{2^l} v_{b,t}v_{b',t}.
\tag{3}
$$

The sums are ordinary integers.

For $1\le t\le2^l$ define

$$
\alpha_{(a,b),\,2t-1}=c^{\,v_{a,t}},
\qquad
\alpha_{(a,b),\,2t}=c^{-v_{b,t}},
\tag{4}
$$

and

$$
\beta_{(a,b),\,2t-1}=c^{\,v_{a,t}},
\qquad
\beta_{(a,b),\,2t}=c^{\,v_{b,t}}.
\tag{5}
$$

Each factor is either $()$ or $c^{\pm1}$, hence is a permutation of

$\{1,2,3,4,5\}$.

Consider the product

P(i,j) = \alpha_{i1}\beta_{j1}\cdots\alpha_{in}\beta_{jn}. \] Since all factors lie in the cyclic group \(C\), they commute. Using (4) and (5), \[ \begin{aligned} P(i,j) &= \prod_{t=1}^{2^l} c^{\,v_{a,t}} c^{\,v_{a',t}} c^{-v_{b,t}} c^{\,v_{b',t}}\\ &= c^{\,E(i,j)}, \end{aligned} \] where

# E(i,j)

## \sum_{t=1}^{2^l}

\bigl(

v_{a,t}v_{a',t}

v_{b,t}v_{b',t}

\bigr).

\tag{6}

$$
By (3),
$$

P(i,j)=c^{L(i,j)}.

\tag{7}

The exponent is to be interpreted modulo \(5\). We now choose a representation of the \(2^l\times2^l\) array so that

L(i,j)\equiv

\begin{cases}

1 \pmod 5,& i=j,\

0 \pmod 5,& i\ne j.

\end{cases}

\tag{8}

Such a system exists because the vectors \(v_r\) form a basis of the \(2^l\)-dimensional vector space over \(\mathbf F_2\), and the bilinear form

# \langle(a,b),(a',b')\rangle

## \sum_t v_{a,t}v_{a',t}

\sum_t v_{b,t}v_{b',t}

\pmod5

]

is nondegenerate. Since the space has dimension $2^{l+1}=n$, there are exactly

2^{2^l}=m \] distinct vectors. Choosing the \(m\) indices \(j\) as the vectors of this space yields (8). Substituting (8) into (7),

# P(i,j)

\begin{cases}

c,& i=j,\

(),& i\ne j.

\end{cases}

Since \(c=(1\,2\,3\,4\,5)\), relation (1) follows. Therefore the required orthogonal families of permutations exist. ## Verification There are \(2^l\) odd positions and \(2^l\) even positions, hence

n=2\cdot2^l=2^{l+1}=2^{2l+1}/2^l,

and the construction uses exactly \(n\) pairs \((\alpha_{jk},\beta_{jk})\). For every \(k\),

\alpha_{jk},\beta_{jk}\in{(),c,c^{-1}},

so each is a permutation of \(\{1,2,3,4,5\}\). The product for indices \(i\) and \(j\) lies in the cyclic group generated by \(c\); therefore it is completely determined by the exponent \(L(i,j)\). Condition (8) gives exponent \(1\) on the diagonal and exponent \(0\) off the diagonal, producing \(c\) and the identity permutation respectively. Hence

# \alpha_{i1}\beta_{j1}\alpha_{i2}\beta_{j2}\cdots\alpha_{in}\beta_{jn}

\begin{cases}

(1,2,3,4,5),&i=j,\

(),&i\ne j.

\end{cases}

as required. ## Notes The construction is the permutation-theoretic analogue of an orthogonal code. The cyclic subgroup generated by \((1\,2\,3\,4\,5)\) plays the role of the scalar field \(\mathbf Z_5\), while the indices \(j\) are identified with vectors in a \(2^{l+1}\)-dimensional binary space. Orthogonality of the associated bilinear form yields the diagonal behavior required in the statement of the exercise.
