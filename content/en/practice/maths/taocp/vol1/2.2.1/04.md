**Exercise 4.** [*M34*] Find a simple formula for $a_n$, the number of permutations on $n$ elements that can be obtained with a stack like that in exercise 2.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m10s  

---

## Setup

Let $a_n$ denote the number of permutations of ${1,2,\dots,n}$ obtainable by a stack operating as in Exercise 2, where each element is pushed once and popped once.

From Exercise 3, stack operations may be encoded by words of length $2n$ over ${S,X}$, where $S$ denotes pushing the next input element onto the stack and $X$ denotes popping from the stack to the output. A sequence is admissible if it contains exactly $n$ symbols $S$ and $n$ symbols $X$, and in every initial segment the number of $X$ symbols does not exceed the number of $S$ symbols.

Each admissible sequence determines a unique output permutation, and different admissible sequences produce different permutations.

Hence $a_n$ equals the number of admissible sequences of length $2n$.

## Solution

Let $C_n$ denote the number of admissible sequences of length $2n$. Then $a_n = C_n$.

Consider any admissible sequence $w$ of length $2n$. The first symbol must be $S$, since an initial $X$ would violate admissibility. Scan $w$ from left to right and let the first position $2k+2$ be the earliest index where the number of $X$ symbols equals the number of $S$ symbols. The prefix $w_1 \cdots w_{2k+2}$ contains $k+1$ symbols $S$ and $k+1$ symbols $X$, and is itself admissible. The suffix $w_{2k+3} \cdots w_{2n}$ is also admissible, since at its start the stack is empty and the same balance condition holds after reindexing.

Conversely, given any decomposition into an admissible prefix of length $2k$ and an admissible suffix of length $2(n-1-k)$, inserting an initial $S$, a matching terminal $X$ for the first return, and interleaving preserves admissibility uniquely. This yields a decomposition of every admissible sequence into a first matched pair enclosing a smaller admissible sequence of size $k$ and a remaining admissible sequence of size $n-1-k$.

The enclosed portion corresponds to $k$ push-pop pairs, and the remaining portion corresponds to $n-1-k$ pairs. The position of the first return determines $k$ uniquely, and every pair of admissible sequences of sizes $k$ and $n-1-k$ produces exactly one admissible sequence of size $n$. Therefore

$$
C_n = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} C_k C_{n-1-k},
\quad C_0 = 1.
$$

This recurrence determines the Catalan numbers. Solving it yields

$$
C_n = \frac{1}{n+1} \binom{2n}{n}.
$$

Since $a_n = C_n$, the required formula is

$$
a_n = \frac{1}{n+1} \binom{2n}{n}.
$$

Hence

$$
\boxed{a_n = \frac{1}{n+1}\binom{2n}{n}}.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification

The decomposition into first return uniquely partitions any admissible sequence because the balance between $S$ and $X$ increases by $1$ for each $S$ and decreases by $1$ for each $X$, so the first index where balance returns to zero is well defined.

Concatenating any admissible left and right components preserves the prefix condition, since each prefix lies entirely within one component or crosses the boundary after a nonnegative balance prefix.

The recurrence determines a unique sequence with $C_0=1$, and the closed form satisfies the same recurrence and initial condition by the standard binomial identity

$$
\frac{1}{n+1}\binom{2n}{n}
=
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}
\frac{1}{k+1}\binom{2k}{k}
\frac{1}{n-k}\binom{2(n-1-k)}{n-1-k}.
$$

## Notes

The admissible sequences are Dyck words, and the permutations counted by $a_n$ are precisely the stack-sortable permutations characterized by avoidance of the pattern $231$.
