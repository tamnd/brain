**Exercise 7.** [*25*] Consider the problem of exercise 2, with a deque substituted for a stack.
(a) Find a permutation of $1234$ that can be obtained with an input-restricted deque, but it cannot be obtained with an output-restricted deque.
(b) Find a permutation of $1234$ that can be obtained with an output-restricted deque but not with an input-restricted deque.
[As a consequence of (a) and (b), there is definitely a difference between input-restricted and output-restricted deques.]
(c) Find a permutation of $1234$ that cannot be obtained with either an input-restricted or an output-restricted deque.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m41s

## Solution

A deque supports insertions and deletions at its ends. Let the ends be called left and right. The input sequence is $1,2,3,4$ in this order.

For the input-restricted deque (IRD), insertions occur only at the right end, while deletions may occur at either end. The configuration is therefore determined by inserting $1,2,3,4$ sequentially at the right, interleaving deletions from left or right.

After inserting $1,2,3,4$ with no deletions, the deque is $[1,2,3,4]$. Any obtainable permutation arises by successively removing either the leftmost or rightmost remaining element.

Starting from $[1,2,3,4]$, if the first deletion removes $1$, the remaining configurations evolve from $[2,3,4]$. From $[2,3,4]$, removing $2$ yields $[3,4]$, from which the outputs $1234$ and $1243$ are obtained depending on the final choice of end deletions. Removing $4$ instead yields $[2,3]$, producing $1423$ and $1432$. Hence all outputs beginning with $1$ are $1234,1243,1423,1432$.

If the first deletion removes $4$, the remaining deque is $[1,2,3]$. Removing $1$ next yields $[2,3]$, producing $4123$ and $4132$. Removing $3$ instead yields $[1,2]$, producing $4312$ and $4321$. Hence all IRD permutations are

$$
1234,1243,1423,1432,4123,4132,4312,4321.
$$

For the output-restricted deque (ORD), insertions may occur at both ends, while deletions occur only at the left end. The output is therefore determined by the left-to-right order of the deque after all insertions, since deletions remove elements in that order.

Constructing all possible configurations by sequential insertion of $1,2,3,4$, each element may be placed at the left or right end.

After inserting $1$, the deque is $[1]$. After inserting $2$, the possibilities are $[2,1]$ or $[1,2]$. After inserting $3$, the possible states are $[3,2,1], [2,1,3], [3,1,2], [1,2,3]$. After inserting $4$, the possible final deques are

$$
4321,3214,4213,2134,4312,3124,4123,1234.
$$

These are exactly the ORD permutations.

### (a)

The permutation $1243$ appears in the IRD set and does not appear in the ORD set. It is obtainable in IRD by inserting $1,2,3,4$ into the deque in order and deleting $1$, then $2$, then $4$, then $3$, each time choosing the appropriate end as described in the IRD construction. Since $1243$ is absent from the ORD set, no sequence of end insertions under output restriction yields it.

Thus

$$
\boxed{1243}.
$$

### (b)

The permutation $2134$ appears in the ORD set and does not appear in the IRD set. It is obtained in ORD by inserting $1$ right, $2$ left, $3$ right, $4$ right, producing the final deque $[2,1,3,4]$, whose left-to-right deletion yields $2134$. Since $2134$ is absent from the IRD set, no IRD deletion sequence produces it.

Thus

$$
\boxed{2134}.
$$

### (c)

The permutation $2413$ does not appear in either the IRD set or the ORD set. In IRD it cannot occur because every IRD permutation must arise from successive deletions of endpoints of an interval $[i,j]$ containing consecutive integers, which forces the first element to be $1$ or $4$ at the first step, while $2413$ begins with $2$. In ORD it cannot occur because every ORD permutation is one of the explicitly determined final deque orders listed above, and $2413$ is not among them.

Thus

$$
\boxed{2413}.
$$

This completes the solution. ∎
