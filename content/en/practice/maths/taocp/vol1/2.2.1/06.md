[Section 2.2.1: Stacks, Queues, and Deques](../)

**Exercise 6.** [*00*] Consider the problem of exercise 2, with a queue substituted for a stack. What permutations of $12\ldots n$ can be obtained with use of a queue?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m06s  

---

Every insertion into the queue places the new element at the rear, and every deletion removes the element at the front. After the elements $1,2,\ldots,n$ are inserted in this order, element $i$ enters the queue before element $j$ whenever $i<j$, so $i$ is positioned ahead of $j$ in the queue at all times thereafter.

No operation moves an element ahead of another element that arrived earlier, since insertions never occur at the front and deletions never occur at the rear. Thus, whenever a deletion $x \Leftarrow A$ is performed, the removed element is always the earliest element among those not yet deleted, so it must be $1$ first, then $2$, and so on in strict order.

Every admissible sequence of queue operations therefore produces the output $12\ldots n$, and no other permutation can occur, since any deviation would require an element that arrived later to be removed before an earlier one.

This completes the proof. ∎
