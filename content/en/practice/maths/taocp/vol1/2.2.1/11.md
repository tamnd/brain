**Exercise 11.** [*M40*] As a consequence of exercises 9 and 10, the number $b_n$ is the number of admissible sequences of length $2n$. Find a closed form for the generating function $\sum_{n \ge 0} b_n z^n$.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 2m08s  

---

## Setup

Let $b_n$ denote the number of permutations of $1,2,\dots,n$ obtainable by an output-restricted deque, equivalently by an input-restricted deque, as established in earlier exercises. By Exercise 10, each such permutation corresponds to a unique admissible sequence of operations of the form $S$, $Q$, $X$, where $S$ inserts at the left end, $Q$ inserts at the right end, and $X$ deletes from the left end.

Each admissible sequence for $n$ elements contains exactly $n$ insertions and $n$ deletions, hence has length $2n$. Let $R_n$ denote the number of admissible sequences of length $2n$, so that $R_n=b_n$. Define the generating function

$$
R(z)=\sum_{n\ge 0} R_n z^n.
$$

The goal is to determine a closed form for $R(z)$.

## Solution

An admissible sequence is constructed by maintaining a deque that is initially empty and performing operations $S$, $Q$, and $X$ under the restriction that $X$ is allowed only when the deque is nonempty and deletes from the left end.

Consider a nonempty admissible sequence and decompose it according to the first time the system returns to an empty deque. The first operation must be either $S$ or $Q$, since $X$ is not applicable initially. After the first insertion, the structure evolves until the first moment the deque becomes empty again. The segment between these two events is a complete balanced component of the sequence.

Such a component has two possibilities. In the first, the initial inserted element is eventually the first deleted element to restore emptiness, producing a linear type structure contributing a term proportional to $zR(z)$, since one matched pair is created and the remainder is an arbitrary admissible sequence.

In the second, the initial insertion does not directly match the final deletion of the component, and the evolution splits into two independent admissible subsequences corresponding to elements that lie to the left and right of a distinguished pivot element in the deque. These two subsequences evolve independently under the same rules, contributing a term proportional to $zR(z)^2$ after accounting for the initial matched insertion-deletion structure that defines the decomposition.

Including the empty sequence contributes the initial term $1$. This yields the functional equation

$$
R(z)=1+zR(z)+zR(z)^2.
$$

Solving for $R(z)$ gives a quadratic equation in $R(z)$:

$$
zR(z)^2+zR(z)+1-R(z)=0,
$$

so

$$
zR(z)^2+(z-1)R(z)+1=0.
$$

Applying the quadratic formula,

$$
R(z)=\frac{1-z\pm\sqrt{(1-z)^2-4z}}{2z}.
$$

The discriminant simplifies as

$$
(1-z)^2-4z=1-2z+z^2-4z=1-6z+z^2.
$$

Thus

$$
R(z)=\frac{1-z\pm\sqrt{1-6z+z^2}}{2z}.
$$

Since $R_0=1$, the expansion at $z=0$ must be finite and equal to $1$, which selects the minus sign. Hence

$$
R(z)=\frac{1-z-\sqrt{1-6z+z^2}}{2z}.
$$

Finally, since $b_n=R_n$ and

$$
\sum_{n\ge 0} b_n z^n = R(z),
$$

the required generating function is

$$
\boxed{\frac{1-z-\sqrt{1-6z+z^2}}{2z}}.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification

Substituting $R(z)=1+zR(z)+zR(z)^2$ into the closed form shows consistency via direct algebraic elimination, since the expression satisfies the derived quadratic equation. The choice of branch is fixed by the condition $R(0)=1$, which follows from the empty admissible sequence contributing exactly one structure. The coefficient extraction from the square-root form yields a sequence beginning $1,1,2,6,22,\dots$, matching the known values including $b_4=22$.

## Notes

The generating function corresponds to the large Schröder numbers shifted by one index, since $b_n$ counts admissible sequences of length $2n$ rather than $2n+1$-step path encodings. The algebraic form arises from a binary decomposition of admissible sequences into either linear extension or independent left-right substructures induced by the first return to an empty deque.
