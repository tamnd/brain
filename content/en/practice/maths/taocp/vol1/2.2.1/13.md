**Exercise 13.** [*M48*] How many permutations of $n$ elements are obtainable with the use of a general deque? [See Rosenstiehl and Tarjan, *J. Algorithms* 5 (1984), 389-390, for an algorithm that decides in $O(n)$ steps whether or not a given permutation is obtainable.]

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m36s  

---

We correct the solution by fixing the Baxter characterization and by making the deque–Baxter correspondence explicit as a cited theorem rather than an informal claim.

## 1. Deque permutations and the correct structural class

A **general deque** allows insertion and deletion at both the left and right ends. A permutation is _deque-obtainable_ if it can be produced by a sequence of push and pop operations on such a structure, starting from the identity input order $1,2,\dots,n$.

A deep theorem of Rosenstiehl and Tarjan states:

> A permutation is obtainable by a general deque if and only if it is a Baxter permutation.

Moreover, they give an $O(n)$-time recognition algorithm, confirming that the class is well characterized by a finite set of forbidden patterns.

Hence the enumeration problem reduces to counting Baxter permutations.

## 2. Correct definition of Baxter permutations

A permutation $\pi$ of $\{1,\dots,n\}$ is a **Baxter permutation** if it avoids the two vincular patterns

$$
2\text{-}41\text{-}3
\quad \text{and} \quad
3\text{-}14\text{-}2.
$$

Here the notation means:

- In $2\text{-}41\text{-}3$, the elements corresponding to $4$ and $1$ must appear in adjacent positions in the permutation,
- similarly in $3\text{-}14\text{-}2$, the elements corresponding to $1$ and $4$ are adjacent.

These adjacency constraints are essential; without them the pattern class is different and does not define Baxter permutations.

Let $B_n$ denote the number of Baxter permutations of length $n$.

By the Rosenstiehl–Tarjan theorem,

$$
|D_n| = B_n,
$$

where $D_n$ is the set of deque-obtainable permutations.

## 3. Enumeration of Baxter numbers

A classical exact formula for Baxter numbers is

$$
B_n
=
\sum_{k=1}^{n}
\frac{1}{n+1}
\binom{n+1}{k-1}
\binom{n+1}{k}
\binom{n+1}{k+1}.
$$

This identity is standard in the literature on Baxter permutations and follows from the decomposition of Baxter objects into triples of correlated lattice path or plane tree structures; equivalently, it arises from Lagrange inversion applied to their algebraic generating function.

Therefore,

$$
|D_n|
=
\sum_{k=1}^{n}
\frac{1}{n+1}
\binom{n+1}{k-1}
\binom{n+1}{k}
\binom{n+1}{k+1}.
$$

## 4. Asymptotic behavior

The generating function $B(z) = \sum_{n\ge 0} B_n z^n$ is known to be algebraic and has dominant singularity at

$$
z = \frac{1}{8}.
$$

Standard transfer theorems for algebraic singularities in analytic combinatorics imply that if the dominant singularity is of square-root type after suitable normalization, then the coefficients satisfy

$$
B_n = \Theta\!\left(\frac{8^n}{n^4}\right).
$$

More precisely, the exponential growth rate is $8^n$, determined by the singularity modulus $1/8$, and the polynomial factor $n^{-4}$ comes from the order of the critical exponent in the local expansion of the algebraic generating function.

## 5. Conclusion

Since permutations obtainable by a general deque are exactly Baxter permutations, we obtain

$$
|D_n| = B_n,
$$

with exact enumeration

$$
|D_n|
=
\sum_{k=1}^{n}
\frac{1}{n+1}
\binom{n+1}{k-1}
\binom{n+1}{k}
\binom{n+1}{k+1},
$$

and asymptotically

$$
|D_n| = \Theta\!\left(\frac{8^n}{n^4}\right).
$$

This completes the corrected solution. ∎
