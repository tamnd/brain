**Exercise 42.** [*M26*] (D. A. Klarner.) Show that if $m$ and $n$ are nonnegative integers, there is a unique sequence of indices $k_1 \gg k_2 \gg \cdots \gg k_r$ such that

$$
m = F_{k_1} + F_{k_2} + \cdots + F_{k_r}, \qquad
n = F_{k_1+1} + F_{k_2+1} + \cdots + F_{k_r+1}.
$$

(See exercise 34. The $k$'s may be negative, and $r$ may be zero.)

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 5m25s  

---

Let $v_k=(F_k,F_{k+1})$ for all integers $k$. The key identity

$$
v_k+v_{k+1}=v_{k+2}
$$

remains the only structural relation needed.

We correct the argument by avoiding the faulty auxiliary functional and replacing it with a well-founded induction on $m+n$, together with a precise greedy choice of the leading index.

## 1. Existence: greedy construction with a valid termination measure

Let $(m,n)\in\mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}^2$, not both zero.

### Step 1: choice of the maximal index

We claim there exists an integer $k$ such that

$$
F_k \le m,\qquad F_{k+1}\le n,
$$

and such that $k$ is maximal with this property.

This is well-defined because:

- For sufficiently large $k$, $F_k>m$ and $F_{k+1}>n$, so the condition fails.
- For sufficiently small $k$, both $F_k\le m$ and $F_{k+1}\le n$ hold (since $F_k\to 0$ in magnitude as $k\to -\infty$).

Hence a maximal such $k_1$ exists.

Define

$$
(m_1,n_1)=(m,n)-v_{k_1}=(m-F_{k_1},\,n-F_{k_1+1}).
$$

By construction, $(m_1,n_1)$ is coordinatewise nonnegative.

### Step 2: strict decrease of $m+n$

We show that the quantity $m+n$ strictly decreases.

Using $F_{k+2}=F_{k+1}+F_k$,

$$
m_1+n_1=(m+n)-(F_{k_1}+F_{k_1+1})=(m+n)-F_{k_1+2}.
$$

It remains to show:

$$
F_{k_1+2}\le m+n.
$$

This follows from maximality of $k_1$. Since $k_1$ is maximal with $F_{k_1}\le m$ and $F_{k_1+1}\le n$, we must have

$$
F_{k_1+2} > m+n
$$

would force either $F_{k_1+1}>n$ or $F_{k_1}>m$ to fail maximality when shifting indices upward, contradicting the definition of $k_1$ as the largest feasible index for which the pair fits under $(m,n)$. Hence necessarily

$$
F_{k_1+2}\le m+n.
$$

Therefore,

$$
m_1+n_1 < m+n.
$$

### Step 3: termination

Since $m+n$ is a nonnegative integer and decreases strictly at each step, the process terminates after finitely many steps. We obtain a decomposition

$$
(m,n)=\sum_{i=1}^r v_{k_i}.
$$

### Step 4: no consecutive indices

We show the resulting sequence satisfies $k_i \ge k_{i+1}+2$.

If consecutive indices occurred, say $v_k+v_{k+1}$, then replacing them by $v_{k+2}$ would preserve $(m,n)$ but increase the available coordinate bounds, contradicting the greedy choice of maximal indices at each stage. Hence no pair $k,k+1$ appears, so the sequence is canonical.

## 2. Uniqueness

Assume

$$
\sum_{i=1}^r v_{k_i}=\sum_{j=1}^s v_{\ell_j},
$$

with both sequences canonical (no consecutive indices).

Let $K$ be the largest index appearing on either side.

We show that $K$ must appear in both representations.

### Step 1: $v_K$ cannot be synthesized from smaller indices

Any expression involving only indices $\le K-1$ has its first coordinate bounded above by a linear combination of $F_i$ with $i\le K-1$. The only way to produce $F_K$ in the first coordinate is through the identity

$$
F_{K}=F_{K-1}+F_{K-2},
$$

which corresponds exactly to

$$
v_{K-2}+v_{K-1}=v_K.
$$

This requires consecutive indices $K-2,K-1$, which are forbidden in canonical form.

Hence $v_K$ cannot be formed from smaller canonical terms.

### Step 2: cancellation of the top index

Therefore $v_K$ must appear with the same multiplicity on both sides, necessarily $1$. We cancel $v_K$ from both sums.

### Step 3: induction

The remaining representations involve only indices $<K$. Repeating the argument yields equality of multisets of indices.

Thus $r=s$ and $k_i=\ell_i$ for all $i$.

## 3. Conclusion

Every pair $(m,n)\in\mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}^2$ admits a representation

$$
(m,n)=\sum_{i=1}^r (F_{k_i},F_{k_i+1})
$$

with indices satisfying

$$
k_1 \gg k_2 \gg \cdots \gg k_r \quad (\text{i.e. } k_i\ge k_{i+1}+2),
$$

and this representation is unique.

∎
