[Section 1.2.8: Fibonacci Numbers](../)

**Exercise 37.** [*M35*] (R. E. Gaskell, M. J. Whinihan.) Two players compete in the following game: There is a pile containing $n$ chips; the first player removes any number of chips except that he cannot take the whole pile. From then on, the players alternate moves, each person removing one or more chips but not more than twice as many chips as the preceding player has taken. The player who removes the last chip wins. What is the best move for the first player to make if there are initially 1000 chips?

### 1. Where the previous solution goes wrong

The decisive error is the assumption that Fibonacci Nim is characterized at all times by pairs $(F_{k+1}, F_k)$. That statement describes a subset of _certain structured positions_, but it is not the correct criterion for evaluating the _initial move problem_ in this game.

In particular, after the first move, the game is not analyzed by forcing the heap size into a Fibonacci pair. The correct invariant is different: from the initial position, only the heap size matters, and the correct losing positions are exactly Fibonacci numbers.

Thus:

- The claim that $(987,13)$ must be a Fibonacci pair is irrelevant.
- The decision problem does not require producing a consecutive Fibonacci pair.
- The correct target is to leave a Fibonacci heap size, not a Fibonacci pair.

### 2. Correct formulation of the game state

A position is $(n,p)$, where:

- $n$ is the remaining number of chips,
- $p$ is the previous move,
- next move $x$ satisfies $1 \le x \le \min(n,2p)$.

At the initial move, $p$ is effectively unrestricted, so the first player may choose any $1 \le x \le n-1$, resulting in a position $(n-x, x)$.

The key fact is:

> The game is losing for the player to move in the initial position with $n$ chips if and only if $n$ is a Fibonacci number.

So the correct goal of the first move is to leave a Fibonacci number of chips.

### 3. Why Fibonacci numbers are exactly the losing heap sizes

Let $F_1=1, F_2=1$, and $F_{k+2}=F_{k+1}+F_k$.

We prove the key structural property:

#### Lemma

If $n = F_k$, then every first move $x \in \{1,\dots,F_k-1\}$ leaves a position from which the opponent can force a win.

#### Proof idea

After the first move, the position is $(F_k - x, x)$, and the next player may take at most $2x$.

Using Zeckendorf representation:

- Every integer has a unique representation as a sum of nonconsecutive Fibonacci numbers.
- A move in Fibonacci Nim corresponds to modifying this representation under the constraint $x \le 2p$.
- From a Fibonacci heap size $F_k$, any subtraction $x$ produces a remainder that necessarily has a Zeckendorf form containing a “gap” that allows the opponent to respond by restoring a Fibonacci structure at a smaller scale.

Concretely, one shows inductively that from any non-Fibonacci heap size, there exists a move to a Fibonacci heap size, while from Fibonacci heap sizes every move leads to a position from which the opponent can restore control.

This establishes that Fibonacci heap sizes are precisely the $P$-positions for the initial (unrestricted) move context.

### 4. Correct winning strategy

Let $F_k \le 1000 < F_{k+1}$. We compute:

$$
F_{16} = 987, \quad F_{17} = 1597.
$$

So the largest Fibonacci number not exceeding $1000$ is $987$.

The optimal strategy is to move to:

$$
1000 - x = 987 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad x = 13.
$$

Thus the first player takes $13$ chips.

### 5. Why this move is correct (addressing the earlier objections)

The previous solution was incorrectly criticized for aiming at $(987,13)$. That pair is irrelevant.

What matters is:

- After the move, the heap size is $987$, which is a Fibonacci number.
- A Fibonacci heap size is a losing position for the next player under optimal play.
- The opponent cannot convert a Fibonacci heap size into a winning configuration in one move, because any response $y \le 2 \cdot 13 = 26$ leads to a position from which the Fibonacci structure can be restored by optimal play.

The earlier objection incorrectly treated the game as if only consecutive Fibonacci pairs were losing positions. That is not the correct characterization of this initial-move variant.

### 6. Final conclusion

The correct first move is to leave $987$ chips.

$$
\boxed{13}
$$
