[Section 1.2.8: Fibonacci Numbers](../)

**Exercise 16.** [*M20*] Fibonacci numbers appear implicitly in Pascal's triangle if it is viewed from the right angle. Show that the following sum of binomial coefficients is a Fibonacci number:

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n-k}{k}.
$$

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m21s  

---

## Solution

Let

$$
S_n=\sum_{k=0}^{n}\binom{n-k}{k}.
$$

The summand vanishes when $k>n-k$, since then $\binom{n-k}{k}=0$. Hence the sum is finite and well defined.

We shall show that $\langle S_n\rangle$ satisfies the Fibonacci recurrence with the same initial values as $\langle F_{n+1}\rangle$.

For $n=0$,

$$
S_0=\binom00=1=F_1.
$$

For $n=1$,

$$
S_1=\binom10+\binom01=1+0=1=F_2.
$$

Now let $n\ge0$. Using Pascal's identity,

$$
\binom{m}{r}=\binom{m-1}{r}+\binom{m-1}{r-1},
$$

we obtain

$$
\begin{aligned}
S_{n+2}
&=\sum_{k=0}^{n+2}\binom{n+2-k}{k}\\
&=\sum_{k=0}^{n+2}\left(\binom{n+1-k}{k}
+\binom{n+1-k}{k-1}\right).
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
S_{n+2}
&=\sum_{k=0}^{n+2}\binom{n+1-k}{k}
+\sum_{k=0}^{n+2}\binom{n+1-k}{k-1}.
\end{aligned}
$$

In the second sum, put $j=k-1$. Then

$$
\begin{aligned}
\sum_{k=0}^{n+2}\binom{n+1-k}{k-1}
&=\sum_{j=-1}^{n+1}\binom{n-j}{j}.
\end{aligned}
$$

The terms with $j=-1$ and $j=n+1$ are zero, hence

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n+2}\binom{n+1-k}{k-1}
=\sum_{j=0}^{n}\binom{n-j}{j}
=S_n.
$$

Similarly,

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n+2}\binom{n+1-k}{k}
=S_{n+1},
$$

because the terms with $k=n+1$ and $k=n+2$ are zero. Consequently,

$$
S_{n+2}=S_{n+1}+S_n.
$$

Thus $\langle S_n\rangle$ satisfies the same recurrence as the Fibonacci numbers, and the initial values are

$$
S_0=1=F_1,\qquad S_1=1=F_2.
$$

By induction on $n$,

$$
S_n=F_{n+1}.
$$

Hence

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n}\binom{n-k}{k}=F_{n+1}.
$$

Therefore the required sum is a Fibonacci number, namely

$$
\boxed{\sum_{k=0}^{n}\binom{n-k}{k}=F_{n+1}.}
$$

This completes the proof.

∎

## Notes

If Pascal's triangle is viewed from the right angle, the entries lying on a diagonal parallel to the hypotenuse are

$$
\binom{n}{0},\ \binom{n-1}{1},\ \binom{n-2}{2},\ \ldots,
$$

and their sum is $F_{n+1}$. These diagonal sums therefore generate the sequence

$$
1,1,2,3,5,8,13,\ldots.
$$
