**Exercise 27.** [*M20*] Using the previous exercise, show that if $p$ is a prime different from $5$, then either $F_{p-1}$ or $F_{p+1}$ (not both) is a multiple of $p$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m28s  

---

## Solution

From Exercise 26, for any odd prime $p \ne 5$,

$$
F_p \equiv 5^{(p-1)/2} \pmod p.
$$

By Euler’s criterion,

$$
5^{(p-1)/2} \equiv \left(\frac{5}{p}\right) \pmod p,
$$

and the Legendre symbol $\left(\frac{5}{p}\right)$ equals $1$ or $-1$. Hence

$$
F_p \equiv \pm 1 \pmod p,
$$

which implies

$$
F_p^2 \equiv 1 \pmod p.
$$

Using Cassini’s identity (4),

$$
F_{n+1}F_{n-1} - F_n^2 = (-1)^n,
$$

and substituting $n=p$ gives

$$
F_{p+1}F_{p-1} - F_p^2 = (-1)^p = -1.
$$

Therefore,

$$
F_{p+1}F_{p-1} = F_p^2 - 1.
$$

Reducing modulo $p$ and using $F_p^2 \equiv 1 \pmod p$ yields

$$
F_{p+1}F_{p-1} \equiv 0 \pmod p.
$$

Hence $p$ divides the product $F_{p+1}F_{p-1}$, so $p$ divides at least one of $F_{p-1}$ or $F_{p+1}$.

Assume $p$ divides both $F_{p-1}$ and $F_{p+1}$. By identity (7),

$$
\gcd(F_{p-1},F_{p+1}) = F_{\gcd(p-1,p+1)}.
$$

Since $\gcd(p-1,p+1)=2$, this becomes

$$
\gcd(F_{p-1},F_{p+1}) = F_2 = 1.
$$

Thus no prime $p$ can divide both $F_{p-1}$ and $F_{p+1}$ simultaneously.

Therefore, exactly one of $F_{p-1}$ or $F_{p+1}$ is a multiple of $p$, and not both.

This completes the proof. ∎
