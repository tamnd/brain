**Exercise 34.** [*M24*] (*The Fibonacci number system.*) Let the notation $k \gg m$ mean that $k \ge m+2$. Show that every positive integer $n$ has a unique representation

$$
n = F_{k_1} + F_{k_2} + \cdots + F_{k_r},
$$

where $k_1 \gg k_2 \gg \cdots \gg k_r \gg 0$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m09s  

---

Assume the Fibonacci numbers are defined by

$$
F_1=1,\quad F_2=1,\quad F_{n+2}=F_{n+1}+F_n \quad (n\ge 1).
$$

The relation $k \gg m$ means $k \ge m+2$, so indices differ by at least $2$. Thus no two consecutive Fibonacci indices may appear.

We prove that every positive integer $n$ has a unique representation

$$
n = F_{k_1}+F_{k_2}+\cdots+F_{k_r}, \quad k_1 \gg k_2 \gg \cdots \gg k_r \gg 0.
$$

## Existence (greedy construction)

Let $n\ge 1$. Choose $k_1$ maximal such that $F_{k_1} \le n$. Then

$$
F_{k_1} \le n < F_{k_1+1}.
$$

Define $n_1 = n - F_{k_1}$. Then $0 \le n_1 < F_{k_1+1}-F_{k_1} = F_{k_1-1}$.

Now choose $k_2$ maximal such that $F_{k_2} \le n_1$. Since $n_1 < F_{k_1-1}$, we must have $k_2 \le k_1-2$. Hence the gap condition begins.

Iterate: given $n_j$, choose $k_{j+1}$ maximal with $F_{k_{j+1}} \le n_j$, and set $n_{j+1} = n_j - F_{k_{j+1}}$. Each step strictly decreases the remainder, so the process terminates.

At each step, the inequality $n_j < F_{k_j-1}$ ensures the next chosen index satisfies $k_{j+1} \le k_j-2$. Therefore the constructed representation satisfies

$$
k_1 \gg k_2 \gg \cdots \gg k_r.
$$

This proves existence.

## Key Lemma (maximal admissible sums)

Let $S(m)$ be the maximum possible value of a sum of distinct Fibonacci numbers with indices $\le m$, subject to the condition that no two chosen indices are consecutive.

**Lemma.**

$$
S(m) \le F_{m+2}-1 \quad \text{for all } m \ge 1.
$$

### Proof of Lemma

We proceed by induction on $m$.

**Base case $m=1$.**

Only choice is $F_1=1$. Since $F_3=2$,

$$
S(1)=1 \le 2-1 = F_3-1.
$$

**Base case $m=2$.**

Only allowed sum is $F_2=1$, so

$$
S(2)=1 \le F_4-1 = 3-1=2.
$$

**Inductive step.**

Assume $S(j) \le F_{j+2}-1$ for all $j<m$. Consider an optimal sum with largest allowed index $m$.

There are two cases.

**Case 1: index $m$ is not used.**

Then all indices are $\le m-1$, so the sum is at most $S(m-1)$. Hence

$$
S(m) \le S(m-1) \le F_{m+1}-1 \le F_{m+2}-1.
$$

**Case 2: index $m$ is used.**

Then index $m-1$ cannot be used. The remaining indices are $\le m-2$, so the sum is

$$
F_m + (\text{sum using indices } \le m-2).
$$

Hence

$$
S(m) \le F_m + S(m-2).
$$

By the induction hypothesis,

$$
S(m-2) \le F_m - 1,
$$

since $F_{(m-2)+2} = F_m$. Therefore,

$$
S(m) \le F_m + (F_m - 1) = 2F_m - 1.
$$

Using the Fibonacci identity $F_{m+2} = F_{m+1} + F_m > 2F_m$ for $m\ge 2$, we obtain

$$
2F_m - 1 < F_{m+2}.
$$

Thus

$$
S(m) \le F_{m+2}-1.
$$

This completes the induction.

## Uniqueness

Let $n$ be given, and let $k$ be maximal such that $F_k \le n$. Then

$$
F_k \le n < F_{k+1}.
$$

### Step 1: Forced inclusion of $F_k$

Assume a valid representation of $n$ does not include $F_k$. Then all indices are $\le k-2$, so the total sum is at most $S(k-2)$. By the lemma,

$$
S(k-2) \le F_k - 1 < F_k \le n,
$$

contradiction. Hence every representation must include $F_k$.

### Step 2: Reduction

Write

$$
n = F_k + n', \quad n' = n - F_k.
$$

From $n < F_{k+1} = F_k + F_{k-1}$, we get

$$
n' < F_{k-1}.
$$

Thus any representation of $n'$ uses only indices $\le k-2$.

### Step 3: Induction on $n$

The same argument applies to $n'$: the largest possible index is uniquely determined at each step, since omission of the greedy choice would violate the bound from the lemma. By induction on $n$, the representation of $n'$ is unique, hence the representation of $n$ is unique.

## Conclusion

Every positive integer $n$ admits a representation as a sum of Fibonacci numbers with indices differing by at least $2$, constructed by a greedy algorithm, and this representation is unique.
