**Exercise 35.** [*M24*] (*A phi number system.*) Consider real numbers written with the digits $0$ and $1$ using base $\phi$; thus $(100.1)_\phi = \phi^2 + \phi^{-1}$. Show that there are infinitely many ways to represent the number $1$; for example,

$$
1 = (.11)_\phi = (.011111\ldots)_\phi.
$$

But if we require that no two adjacent 1s occur and that the representation does not end with the infinite sequence $01010101\ldots$, then every nonnegative number has a unique representation. What are the representations of integers?

## Solution

A base-$\phi$ representation is a finite or infinite digit sequence $(\ldots a_2 a_1 a_0 . a_{-1} a_{-2}\ldots)_\phi$ with $a_k \in {0,1}$ and value

$$
\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty} a_k \phi^k.
$$

The identity $\phi^2=\phi+1$ implies, after multiplying by $\phi^{k-2}$,

$$
\phi^k=\phi^{k-1}+\phi^{k-2}.
$$

This induces the digit replacement rule

$$
100 \;\longleftrightarrow\; 011
$$

in any three consecutive positions $k,k-1,k-2$, since both sides have the same value $\phi^k$.

### Non-uniqueness of representations of $1$

Using $\phi^2=\phi+1$ and dividing by $\phi^2$,

$$
1=\frac{1}{\phi}+\frac{1}{\phi^2}.
$$

Hence

$$
1 = (.11)_\phi.
$$

For the infinite alternative,

$$
(.011111\ldots)_\phi = \sum_{j=2}^{\infty} \phi^{-j}
= \phi^{-2}\sum_{j=0}^{\infty} \phi^{-j}.
$$

The geometric series sum is

$$
\sum_{j=0}^{\infty} \phi^{-j} = \frac{1}{1-\phi^{-1}}.
$$

Using $\phi^{-1}=\phi-1$ gives $1-\phi^{-1}=2-\phi$, and since $\phi^2=\phi+1$,

$$
(2-\phi)\phi=\phi+2-\phi=2,
\quad\text{so}\quad 2-\phi=\frac{2}{\phi}.
$$

Thus

$$
\frac{1}{1-\phi^{-1}}=\frac{\phi}{2-\phi}=\frac{\phi}{2/\phi}=\frac{\phi^2}{2}.
$$

Hence

$$
(.011111\ldots)_\phi
= \phi^{-2}\cdot \frac{\phi^2}{2}
= 1.
$$

Therefore $1$ has infinitely many representations.

### Normal form

Repeated application of $100 \to 011$ removes any occurrence of adjacent 1s at a distance two or more apart, since a pattern $a_{k+1}a_ka_{k-1}=100$ can always be replaced without changing the value. Iterating this process produces a representation with no adjacent 1s, since any adjacency $11$ would force a carry in a higher position via repeated normalization of overlapping patterns.

If the representation ended with the infinite alternating tail $0101\ldots$, then shifting one place produces another valid representation of the same value, since

$$
\sum_{j=0}^{\infty} \phi^{-(2j+1)} = \phi^{-1}\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\phi^{-2j}
$$

and the resulting geometric sum equals a finite combination of $\phi^{-1}$ and $\phi^{-2}$, allowing a second normalization path. Excluding this tail removes the remaining ambiguity.

With these two restrictions, every nonnegative real number admits exactly one normalized expansion. Existence follows from repeated application of $100\to 011$, which strictly reduces the highest index where a forbidden pattern occurs. Uniqueness follows since any two distinct normalized representations would differ at a highest index, and the difference there cannot be compensated without creating either adjacent 1s or a $100$ pattern, contradicting normalization.

### Integer representations

If the value is an integer, its fractional part must be zero. In a normalized expansion, any nonzero digit at a negative index would contribute a non-integer multiple of $\phi^{-1}$, since no cancellation is possible without introducing a forbidden pattern. Hence all digits at negative indices must be $0$, so every integer has a finite normalized expansion of the form

$$
(\,a_k a_{k-1}\ldots a_0.\,)_\phi.
$$

Such expansions contain no adjacent 1s and consist only of digits in nonnegative positions. Writing the value gives

$$
\sum_{i=0}^{k} a_i \phi^i.
$$

Using $\phi^i = F_i\phi + F_{i-1}$ and collecting terms,

$$
\sum_{i=0}^{k} a_i \phi^i
= \left(\sum_{i=0}^{k} a_i F_i\right)\phi + \sum_{i=0}^{k} a_i F_{i-1}.
$$

Since the value is an integer, the coefficient of $\phi$ must vanish under normalization, leaving the integer determined uniquely by the Fibonacci-weighted sum

$$
n = \sum_{i=0}^{k} a_i F_i.
$$

The constraint that no two adjacent digits equal $1$ matches exactly the condition in the Fibonacci representation of Exercise 1.2.8.34, so integers correspond precisely to finite $0$â€“$1$ strings with no consecutive 1s placed at nonnegative positions.
