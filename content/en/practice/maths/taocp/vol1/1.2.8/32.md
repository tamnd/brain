**Exercise 32.** [*M24*] The remainder of one Fibonacci number divided by another is $\pm$ a Fibonacci number: Show that, modulo $F_n$,

$$
F_{mn+r} \equiv
\begin{cases}
F_r, & \text{if } m \bmod 4 = 0; \\
(-1)^{r+1}F_{n-r}, & \text{if } m \bmod 4 = 1; \\
(-1)^nF_r, & \text{if } m \bmod 4 = 2; \\
(-1)^{r+1+n}F_{n-r}, & \text{if } m \bmod 4 = 3.
\end{cases}
$$

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m30s

The original argument fails for two independent reasons: a sign error in reducing Cassini’s identity and an invalid cancellation using a false coprimality claim. The matrix approach is still sound, but it must be completed using identities that do not require divisibility assumptions.

Start again from the Fibonacci matrix

$$
M=\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}, \qquad
M^n=\begin{pmatrix}F_{n+1}&F_n\\F_n&F_{n-1}\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Working modulo $F_n$, we have $F_n \equiv 0$, hence

$$
M^n \equiv 
\begin{pmatrix}
F_{n+1} & 0\\
0 & F_{n-1}
\end{pmatrix}
\pmod{F_n}.
$$

Raising to the $m$-th power preserves this diagonal form in the quotient ring:

$$
(M^n)^m \equiv 
\begin{pmatrix}
F_{n+1}^m & 0\\
0 & F_{n-1}^m
\end{pmatrix}
\pmod{F_n}.
$$

Multiplying by $M^r$, the $(1,2)$-entry gives

$$
F_{mn+r} \equiv F_{n+1}^m F_r \pmod{F_n}.
$$

So the problem reduces to understanding $F_{n+1}^m \bmod F_n$ and rewriting the factor $F_{n+1}F_r$.

### 1. Correct evaluation of $F_{n+1}^m \bmod F_n$

Cassini’s identity states

$$
F_{n+2}F_n - F_{n+1}^2 = (-1)^{n+1}.
$$

Reducing modulo $F_n$, the term $F_{n+2}F_n$ vanishes, so

$$
- F_{n+1}^2 \equiv (-1)^{n+1} \pmod{F_n},
$$

hence

$$
F_{n+1}^2 \equiv (-1)^n \pmod{F_n}.
$$

Let $a \equiv F_{n+1} \pmod{F_n}$. Then

$$
a^2 \equiv (-1)^n,\qquad a^4 \equiv 1 \pmod{F_n}.
$$

Therefore powers of $a$ depend only on $m \bmod 4$:

$$
F_{n+1}^m \equiv
\begin{cases}
1, & m \equiv 0 \pmod 4,\\
F_{n+1}, & m \equiv 1 \pmod 4,\\
(-1)^n, & m \equiv 2 \pmod 4,\\
(-1)^n F_{n+1}, & m \equiv 3 \pmod 4.
\end{cases}
$$

Substituting into $F_{mn+r} \equiv F_{n+1}^m F_r$, we obtain four cases:

$$
F_{mn+r} \equiv
\begin{cases}
F_r, & m \equiv 0 \pmod 4,\\
F_{n+1}F_r, & m \equiv 1 \pmod 4,\\
(-1)^n F_r, & m \equiv 2 \pmod 4,\\
(-1)^n F_{n+1}F_r, & m \equiv 3 \pmod 4.
\end{cases}
$$

### 2. Eliminating $F_{n+1}F_r$ without any gcd cancellation

The incorrect step in the original solution attempted to cancel $F_r$. This is unnecessary. Instead we use the standard Fibonacci identity valid for all integers $n \ge r$:

$$
F_n F_{r+1} - F_{n+1}F_r = (-1)^r F_{n-r}.
$$

This identity can be proved directly from the matrix formula for $M^k$ or by induction on $n$. It does not require any coprimality assumptions.

Reducing modulo $F_n$, the term $F_n F_{r+1}$ vanishes, giving

$$
- F_{n+1}F_r \equiv (-1)^r F_{n-r} \pmod{F_n},
$$

so

$$
F_{n+1}F_r \equiv (-1)^{r+1} F_{n-r} \pmod{F_n}.
$$

### 3. Final assembly

Now substitute this reduction into the cases above.

For $m \equiv 1 \pmod 4$,

$$
F_{mn+r} \equiv F_{n+1}F_r \equiv (-1)^{r+1}F_{n-r}.
$$

For $m \equiv 3 \pmod 4$,

$$
F_{mn+r} \equiv (-1)^n F_{n+1}F_r \equiv (-1)^{n+r+1}F_{n-r}.
$$

For $m \equiv 0 \pmod 4$ and $m \equiv 2 \pmod 4$, we already have

$$
F_{mn+r} \equiv F_r,\qquad F_{mn+r} \equiv (-1)^n F_r.
$$

Combining all cases yields

$$
F_{mn+r} \equiv
\begin{cases}
F_r, & m \bmod 4 = 0; \\
(-1)^{r+1}F_{n-r}, & m \bmod 4 = 1; \\
(-1)^nF_r, & m \bmod 4 = 2; \\
(-1)^{r+1+n}F_{n-r}, & m \bmod 4 = 3.
\end{cases}
$$
