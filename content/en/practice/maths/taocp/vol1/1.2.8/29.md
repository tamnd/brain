**Exercise 29.** [*M23*] (*Fibonomial coefficients.*) Edouard Lucas defined the quantities

$$
\binom{n}{k}_{\mathcal{F}}
= \frac{F_nF_{n-1}\cdots F_{n-k+1}}{F_kF_{k-1}\cdots F_1}
= \prod_{j=1}^{k}\left(\frac{F_{n-k+j}}{F_j}\right)
$$

in a manner analogous to binomial coefficients. (a) Make a table of $\binom{n}{k}_{\mathcal{F}}$ for $0 \le k \le n \le 6$. (b) Show that $\binom{n}{k}_{\mathcal{F}}$ is always an integer because we have

$$
\binom{n}{k}_{\mathcal{F}}
= F_{k-1}\binom{n-1}{k}_{\mathcal{F}}
+ F_{n-k+1}\binom{n-1}{k-1}_{\mathcal{F}}.
$$

## (a) Table of $\binom{n}{k}_{\mathcal{F}}$ for $0 \le k \le n \le 6$

Let $F_1=1, F_2=1$, $F_3=2, F_4=3, F_5=5, F_6=8, F_7=13$. The definition is

$$
\binom{n}{k}_{\mathcal{F}}=\prod_{j=1}^{k}\frac{F_{n-k+j}}{F_j}.
$$

Computing directly:

- $n=0$: $1$
- $n=1$: $1,1$
- $n=2$: $1,1,1$
- $n=3$: $1,2,2,1$
- $n=4$: $1,3,6,3,1$
- $n=5$: $1,5,15,15,5,1$
- $n=6$: $1,8,40,60,40,8,1$

Hence the full table is

$$
\begin{array}{c|ccccccc}
n \backslash k & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 & 5 & 6 \\
\hline
0 & 1 \\
1 & 1 & 1 \\
2 & 1 & 1 & 1 \\
3 & 1 & 2 & 2 & 1 \\
4 & 1 & 3 & 6 & 3 & 1 \\
5 & 1 & 5 & 15 & 15 & 5 & 1 \\
6 & 1 & 8 & 40 & 60 & 40 & 8 & 1
\end{array}
$$

## (b) Recurrence and integrality

Define

$$
C(n,k)=\binom{n}{k}_{\mathcal{F}}=\frac{N(n,k)}{D(k)},
\quad
N(n,k)=\prod_{j=1}^{k}F_{n-k+j}, 
\quad
D(k)=\prod_{j=1}^{k}F_j.
$$

We prove the identity

$$
C(n,k)=F_{k-1}C(n-1,k)+F_{n-k+1}C(n-1,k-1)
$$

for $1 \le k \le n$.

### Step 1: Fibonacci decomposition

Use the standard identity

$$
F_{a+b}=F_a F_{b+1}+F_{a-1}F_b.
$$

Set $a=k$, $b=n-k$. Then

$$
F_n = F_k F_{n-k+1} + F_{k-1}F_{n-k}.
$$

Multiply both sides by $\prod_{j=1}^{k-1}F_{n-k+j}$:

$$
F_n \prod_{j=1}^{k-1}F_{n-k+j}
=
F_k F_{n-k+1}\prod_{j=1}^{k-1}F_{n-k+j}
+
F_{k-1}F_{n-k}\prod_{j=1}^{k-1}F_{n-k+j}.
$$

The left-hand side is $N(n,k)$, since

$$
N(n,k)=F_n \prod_{j=1}^{k-1}F_{n-k+j}.
$$

Thus

$$
N(n,k)
=
F_k F_{n-k+1}\prod_{j=1}^{k-1}F_{n-k+j}
+
F_{k-1}F_{n-k}\prod_{j=1}^{k-1}F_{n-k+j}.
$$

### Step 2: Rewrite each term

First term:

$$
F_k F_{n-k+1}\prod_{j=1}^{k-1}F_{n-k+j}
=
F_{n-k+1}F_k \prod_{j=1}^{k-1}F_{n-k+j}.
$$

Second term: note that

$$
\prod_{j=1}^{k}F_{n-k-1+j}
=
F_{n-k}\prod_{j=1}^{k-1}F_{n-k+j}.
$$

Hence

$$
F_{k-1}F_{n-k}\prod_{j=1}^{k-1}F_{n-k+j}
=
F_{k-1}\prod_{j=1}^{k}F_{n-k-1+j}.
$$

So we obtain

$$
N(n,k)
=
F_{k-1} \prod_{j=1}^{k}F_{n-k-1+j}
+
F_{n-k+1}F_k \prod_{j=1}^{k-1}F_{n-k+j}.
$$

### Step 3: Divide by $D(k)$

Divide both sides by $D(k)=\prod_{j=1}^{k}F_j$:

$$
C(n,k)
=
F_{k-1}\frac{\prod_{j=1}^{k}F_{n-k-1+j}}{\prod_{j=1}^{k}F_j}
+
F_{n-k+1}\frac{F_k\prod_{j=1}^{k-1}F_{n-k+j}}{\prod_{j=1}^{k}F_j}.
$$

Recognize the fibonomial coefficients:

$$
\frac{\prod_{j=1}^{k}F_{n-k-1+j}}{\prod_{j=1}^{k}F_j}
=
C(n-1,k),
$$

$$
\frac{\prod_{j=1}^{k-1}F_{n-k+j}}{\prod_{j=1}^{k-1}F_j}
=
C(n-1,k-1).
$$

Thus

$$
C(n,k)=F_{k-1}C(n-1,k)+F_{n-k+1}C(n-1,k-1).
$$

## Step 4: Integrality

We prove by induction on $n$.

- Base cases:

$C(n,0)=1$ and $C(n,n)=1$, hence integers.
- Inductive step: assume all $C(n-1,\cdot)$ are integers.

Since Fibonacci numbers are integers, the recurrence expresses $C(n,k)$ as a sum of products of integers:

$$
C(n,k)=F_{k-1}C(n-1,k)+F_{n-k+1}C(n-1,k-1).
$$

Hence $C(n,k)$ is an integer.

Therefore all fibonomial coefficients are integers.

$\square$
