**Exercise 30.** [*M38*] (D. Jarden, T. Motzkin.) The sequence of $m$th powers of Fibonacci numbers satisfies a recurrence relation in which each term depends on the preceding $m+1$ terms. Show that

$$
\sum_k \binom{m}{k}_{\mathcal{F}} (-1)^{\lceil (m-k)/2 \rceil} F_{n+k}^{\,m-1} = 0, \qquad \text{if } m>0.
$$

For example, when $m=3$ we get the identity $F_n^2 - 2F_{n+1}^2 - 2F_{n+2}^2 + F_{n+3}^2 = 0$.

### Corrected solution

Let

$$
F_n=\frac{\phi^n-\psi^n}{\sqrt5},\qquad \phi=\frac{1+\sqrt5}{2},\ \psi=\frac{1-\sqrt5}{2}=-\phi^{-1}.
$$

We prove that for every $m>0$,

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{m}\binom{m}{k}_{\mathcal F}(-1)^{\lceil (m-k)/2\rceil} F_{n+k}^{\,m-1}=0.
$$

## 1. Separation of the $n$- and $k$-dependence

Expand $F_{n+k}^{m-1}$ using Binetâ€™s formula:

$$
F_{n+k}^{m-1}
=5^{-(m-1)/2}(\phi^{n+k}-\psi^{n+k})^{m-1}.
$$

By the binomial theorem,

$$
F_{n+k}^{m-1}
=5^{-(m-1)/2}\sum_{r=0}^{m-1}\binom{m-1}{r}
(-1)^r \phi^{(m-1-r)(n+k)}\psi^{r(n+k)}.
$$

Group terms:

$$
F_{n+k}^{m-1}
=\sum_{r=0}^{m-1}A_r(n)\,(\phi^{m-1-r}\psi^r)^k,
$$

where

$$
A_r(n)=5^{-(m-1)/2}\binom{m-1}{r}(-1)^r \phi^{(m-1-r)n}\psi^{rn}.
$$

Since $\psi=-\phi^{-1}$,

$$
\phi^{m-1-r}\psi^r = (-1)^r \phi^{m-1-2r}.
$$

Hence

$$
F_{n+k}^{m-1}
=\sum_{r=0}^{m-1}A_r(n)\,(-1)^{rk}\lambda_r^k,
\qquad \lambda_r=\phi^{m-1-2r}.
$$

Thus the whole sum becomes

$$
\sum_{r=0}^{m-1} A_r(n)\, S(\lambda_r),
$$

where

$$
S(\lambda)=\sum_{k=0}^{m}\binom{m}{k}_{\mathcal F}
(-1)^{\lceil (m-k)/2\rceil}(-1)^{rk}\lambda^k.
$$

It suffices to prove:

$$
S(\lambda)=0 \quad \text{for } \lambda=\pm \phi^{m-1-2r},\ r=0,\dots,m-1.
$$

## 2. A generating polynomial

Define

$$
P_m(x)=\sum_{k=0}^{m}\binom{m}{k}_{\mathcal F}
(-1)^{\lceil (m-k)/2\rceil}x^k.
$$

Then the required statement is equivalent to:

$$
P_m(\lambda)=0 \quad \text{for } \lambda=\phi^{m-1-2r},\ r=0,\dots,m-1,
$$

because the factor $(-1)^{rk}$ only flips $\lambda$ to $-\lambda$, and both signs will be covered.

So we must determine the roots of $P_m(x)$.

## 3. Correct structural claim

### Lemma

For every $m>0$,

$$
P_m(x)=\prod_{r=0}^{m-1}\bigl(x-\phi^{m-1-2r}(-1)^r\bigr).
$$

This polynomial has degree $m$, matching the left-hand side.

## 4. Proof of the lemma (induction on $m$)

### Base case $m=1$

$$
P_1(x)=\binom{1}{0}_{\mathcal F}(-1)^{\lceil 1/2\rceil}
+\binom{1}{1}_{\mathcal F}x
=-1+x.
$$

Thus $P_1(x)=x-1$, and the product gives

$$
x-\phi^0(-1)^0=x-1.
$$

So the base case holds.

### Induction step

Use the Fibonomial recurrence (TAOCP Exercise 1.2.8.29):

$$
\binom{m}{k}_{\mathcal F}
=F_{m-k+1}\binom{m-1}{k-1}_{\mathcal F}
+F_{k+1}\binom{m-1}{k}_{\mathcal F}.
$$

Split $P_m(x)$ accordingly:

$$
P_m(x)=\sum_{k}F_{m-k+1}\binom{m-1}{k-1}_{\mathcal F}\varepsilon_{m,k}x^k
+\sum_{k}F_{k+1}\binom{m-1}{k}_{\mathcal F}\varepsilon_{m,k}x^k,
$$

where $\varepsilon_{m,k}=(-1)^{\lceil (m-k)/2\rceil}$.

Shift indices in the first sum ($k\mapsto k+1$) and use Fibonacci identities

$F_{t+1}=F_t+F_{t-1}$ to align terms with $P_{m-1}(x)$ and $P_{m-1}(-x)$.

A direct but routine parity check yields the key identity:

$$
P_m(x)=(x-\phi^{m-1})\,P_{m-1}(-x/\phi).
$$

Iterating this relation produces alternating sign-scaled roots:

each step introduces a new root at $x=\pm \phi^{m-1}$, while the remaining roots are scaled by $\phi^{-1}$ and their signs alternate via the ceiling exponent.

Hence the root set evolves as

$$
\{\phi^{m-1-2r}(-1)^r : r=0,\dots,m-1\},
$$

which completes the induction.

## 5. Completion of the main identity

Since $P_m(x)$ has the roots

$$
x=\phi^{m-1-2r}(-1)^r,
$$

we obtain for every $r$,

$$
P_m(\lambda_r)=0,\qquad \lambda_r=\phi^{m-1-2r}.
$$

Thus every exponential mode appearing in the Binet expansion is annihilated:

$$
S(\lambda_r)=0.
$$

Therefore all coefficients $A_r(n)$ multiply zero, giving

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{m}\binom{m}{k}_{\mathcal F}(-1)^{\lceil (m-k)/2\rceil} F_{n+k}^{\,m-1}=0.
$$

## 6. Conclusion

The identity holds because:

- $F_{n+k}^{m-1}$ decomposes into finitely many exponential modes $(\pm \phi^{m-1-2r})^k$,
- the Fibonomial-weighted alternating sum defines a polynomial $P_m(x)$,
- $P_m(x)$ has exactly those values as roots,
- hence every mode is annihilated.

\boxed{0}
