**Exercise 9.** [*M20*] Using the conventions of exercise 8, determine whether Eqs. (4), (6), (14), and (15) still hold when the subscripts are allowed to be any integers.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m49s  

---

## Solution

The Fibonacci sequence is extended to all integers $n$ by the recurrence

$$
F_{n+2}=F_{n+1}+F_n
$$

for all integers $n$, together with the initial values $F_0=0$ and $F_1=1$.

From the recurrence with $n=-1$,

$$
F_1=F_0+F_{-1},
$$

so $1=0+F_{-1}$, hence $F_{-1}=1$.

With $n=-2$,

$$
F_0=F_{-1}+F_{-2},
$$

so $0=1+F_{-2}$, hence $F_{-2}=-1$.

Continuing inductively yields the identity

$$
F_{-n}=(-1)^{n+1}F_n
$$

for all integers $n$. This relation is preserved under the recurrence since both sequences satisfy the same second-order linear recurrence and agree at $n=0$ and $n=1$.

### Verification of Eq. (4)

Equation (4) states

$$
F_{n+1}F_{n-1}-F_n^2 = (-1)^n.
$$

Replacing $n$ by $-n$ gives

$$
F_{-n+1}F_{-n-1}-F_{-n}^2.
$$

Using $F_{-k}=(-1)^{k+1}F_k$,

$$
F_{-n+1}=(-1)^n F_{n-1}, \quad F_{-n-1}=(-1)^{n+2}F_{n+1}, \quad F_{-n}=(-1)^{n+1}F_n.
$$

Substitution yields

$$
F_{-n+1}F_{-n-1}-F_{-n}^2
= (-1)^n(-1)^{n+2}F_{n-1}F_{n+1} - (-1)^{2n+2}F_n^2.
$$

Since $(-1)^n(-1)^{n+2}=(-1)^{2n+2}=1$, this becomes

$$
F_{n-1}F_{n+1}-F_n^2.
$$

By Eq. (4) for positive indices this equals $(-1)^n$. Hence Eq. (4) holds for all integers $n$.

### Verification of Eq. (6)

Equation (6) states, for positive $m$,

$$
F_{n+m} = F_m F_{n+1} + F_{m-1}F_n.
$$

Fix $n$ and define

$$
A_m = F_{n+m}, \quad B_m = F_m F_{n+1} + F_{m-1}F_n.
$$

Both sequences satisfy the recurrence $X_{m+2}=X_{m+1}+X_m$ in the variable $m$, since each term is a linear combination of Fibonacci numbers in $m$.

They agree at $m=0$:

$$
A_0 = F_n, \quad B_0 = F_0F_{n+1}+F_{-1}F_n = 0\cdot F_{n+1}+1\cdot F_n = F_n.
$$

They agree at $m=1$:

$$
A_1 = F_{n+1}, \quad B_1 = F_1F_{n+1}+F_0F_n = 1\cdot F_{n+1}+0 = F_{n+1}.
$$

Since a second-order linear recurrence is determined by two initial values, $A_m=B_m$ for all integers $m$, including negative $m$ as well. Hence Eq. (6) holds for all integers $m,n$.

### Verification of Eq. (14)

Equation (14) is

$$
F_n = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}(\phi^n-\hat{\phi}^n).
$$

Both $\phi^n$ and $\hat{\phi}^n$ are defined for all integers $n$ using $x^{-n}=(x^n)^{-1}$ for $x\neq 0$. Since $\phi\hat{\phi}=-1$, the identity remains algebraically valid under negative exponents.

Define

$$
B_n = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}(\phi^n-\hat{\phi}^n).
$$

Then $B_n$ satisfies $B_{n+2}=B_{n+1}+B_n$ for all integers $n$, since $\phi$ and $\hat{\phi}$ are roots of $x^2=x+1$. Also,

$$
B_0=\frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}(1-1)=0, \quad B_1=\frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}(\phi-\hat{\phi})=1.
$$

Thus $B_n$ satisfies the same recurrence and initial conditions as $F_n$ extended to all integers, hence $F_n=B_n$ for all integers $n$. Therefore Eq. (14) holds for all integer subscripts.

### Verification of Eq. (15)

Equation (15) states

$$
F_n = \phi^n/\sqrt{5} \text{ rounded to the nearest integer.}
$$

From Eq. (14),

$$
F_n - \frac{\phi^n}{\sqrt{5}} = -\frac{\hat{\phi}^n}{\sqrt{5}}.
$$

For $n\ge 0$, $|\hat{\phi}|<1$, hence $|\hat{\phi}^n/\sqrt{5}|<1/2$ for sufficiently large $n$, and direct computation shows it is always within $1/2$ for all $n\ge 0$. Thus rounding is valid for nonnegative integers.

For negative $n$, write $n=-k$ with $k>0$. Then

$$
\hat{\phi}^n = \hat{\phi}^{-k} = \frac{1}{\hat{\phi}^k}.
$$

Since $|\hat{\phi}|<1$, it follows that $|\hat{\phi}^{-k}|>1$ and grows without bound as $k$ increases. The difference

$$
\left|F_n-\frac{\phi^n}{\sqrt{5}}\right|=\frac{|\hat{\phi}^n|}{\sqrt{5}}
$$

is therefore not bounded by $1/2$ for all negative $n$, so the nearest-integer statement fails in general.

Hence Eq. (15) holds for $n\ge 0$ but does not hold for all integers $n$.
