[Section 1.2.8: Fibonacci Numbers](../)

**Exercise 19.** [*M27*] What is $\cos 36^\circ$?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m32s  

---

## Setup

Let $\phi = \frac{1}{2}(1+\sqrt{5})$ as in equation (3). The goal is to determine the exact value of $\cos 36^\circ$.

Consider a regular pentagon inscribed in a circle of radius $R$. Let $s$ denote the side length of the pentagon and $d$ the length of a diagonal.

The central angle subtending one side is $72^\circ$, so a side is a chord of angle $72^\circ$. A diagonal spans two edges, hence it subtends $144^\circ$.

Thus

$$
s = 2R \sin 36^\circ, \qquad d = 2R \sin 72^\circ.
$$

The well-known pentagon relation gives

$$
\frac{d}{s} = \phi.
$$

Hence

$$
\frac{\sin 72^\circ}{\sin 36^\circ} = \phi.
$$

## Solution

Use the double-angle identity

$$
\sin 72^\circ = \sin(2 \cdot 36^\circ) = 2 \sin 36^\circ \cos 36^\circ.
$$

Substituting into the ratio,

$$
\frac{2 \sin 36^\circ \cos 36^\circ}{\sin 36^\circ} = \phi.
$$

Canceling $\sin 36^\circ$ (nonzero since $36^\circ \neq 0^\circ$),

$$
2 \cos 36^\circ = \phi.
$$

Thus

$$
\cos 36^\circ = \frac{\phi}{2}.
$$

Using $\phi = \frac{1}{2}(1+\sqrt{5})$,

$$
\cos 36^\circ = \frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{4}.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\boxed{\cos 36^\circ = \frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{4}}.
$$

## Verification

The identity $\sin 72^\circ = 2 \sin 36^\circ \cos 36^\circ$ follows directly from $\sin(2x) = 2\sin x \cos x$ with $x = 36^\circ$.

The ratio $\frac{d}{s} = \phi$ holds in a regular pentagon because the diagonal-to-side ratio is invariant under similarity and equals the golden ratio by the standard pentagon similarity construction.

Substitution into the chord expressions yields a consistent cancellation of $2R$, leaving only trigonometric quantities, so no dependence on the radius remains.

## Notes

The same computation shows $\sin 18^\circ = \frac{\sqrt{5}-1}{4}$ by applying the half-angle identity to $\cos 36^\circ$.
