**Exercise 7.** [*23*] Formulate and prove by induction a rule for the sums $1^2$, $2^2 - 1^2$, $3^2 - 2^2 + 1^2$, $4^2 - 3^2 + 2^2 - 1^2$, $5^2 - 4^2 + 3^2 - 2^2 + 1^2$, etc.

## Solution

Let

$S_n = n^2 - (n-1)^2 + (n-2)^2 - \cdots + (-1)^{n-1} 1^2$

for positive integers $n$.

The first values are

$S_1 = 1,$

$S_2 = 4 - 1 = 3,$

$S_3 = 9 - 4 + 1 = 6,$

$S_4 = 16 - 9 + 4 - 1 = 10,$

$S_5 = 25 - 16 + 9 - 4 + 1 = 15.$

These values satisfy $1 = 1\cdot 2/2$, $3 = 2\cdot 3/2$, $6 = 3\cdot 4/2$, $10 = 4\cdot 5/2$, $15 = 5\cdot 6/2$, so the rule

$S_n = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}$

is formulated for all positive integers $n$.

The proof proceeds by induction on $n$.

For $n=1$, $S_1 = 1$ and $\frac{1\cdot 2}{2} = 1$, so the formula holds for $n=1$.

Assume that for some positive integer $n$,

$S_n = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}.$

The definition of $S_{n+1}$ gives

$S_{n+1} = (n+1)^2 - n^2 + (n-1)^2 - \cdots + (-1)^n 1^2.$

Grouping the last $n$ terms yields

$S_{n+1} = (n+1)^2 - \bigl(n^2 - (n-1)^2 + \cdots + (-1)^{n-1}1^2\bigr),$

so

$S_{n+1} = (n+1)^2 - S_n.$

Substituting the inductive hypothesis,

$S_{n+1} = (n+1)^2 - \frac{n(n+1)}{2}.$

Factoring $(n+1)$,

$S_{n+1} = (n+1)\left((n+1) - \frac{n}{2}\right) = (n+1)\left(\frac{2n+2-n}{2}\right).$

This simplifies to

$S_{n+1} = \frac{(n+1)(n+2)}{2}.$

This matches the claimed formula with $n$ replaced by $n+1$, so the induction step is established.

This completes the proof. ∎
