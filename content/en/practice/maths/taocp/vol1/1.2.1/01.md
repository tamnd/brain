[Section 1.2.1: Mathematical Induction](../)

**Exercise 1.** [*05*] Explain how to modify the idea of proof by mathematical induction, in case we want to prove some statement $P(n)$ for all *nonnegative* integers, that is, for $n = 0$, $1$, $2$, $\ldots$ instead of for $n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots\,$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m21s  

---

To extend induction from positive integers to nonnegative integers, the starting point is shifted from $1$ to $0$. One first establishes the base case $P(0)$ directly, giving a proof that the statement holds at $n = 0$.

The inductive step is formulated for all integers $n \ge 0$: assume $P(0), P(1), \ldots, P(n)$ are true (or, in the simple form, assume only $P(n)$ is true), and prove that $P(n+1)$ is true. In Algorithm I this corresponds to replacing the initialization step by setting $k \leftarrow 0$ and outputting a proof of $P(0)$, while the remainder of the procedure is unchanged, so that the iteration produces proofs up through $P(n)$ for any given nonnegative integer $n$. ∎
