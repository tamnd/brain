**Exercise 9.** [*20*] Prove by induction that if $0 < a < 1$, then $(1 - a)^n \ge 1 - na$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m18s  

---

## Solution

Let $P(n)$ be the statement $(1 - a)^n \ge 1 - na$ for a fixed real number $a$ satisfying $0 < a < 1$, and for all positive integers $n$.

For $n = 1$, we have $(1 - a)^1 = 1 - a = 1 - 1 \cdot a$, so $P(1)$ holds.

Assume $P(n)$ holds for some positive integer $n$, so

$$
(1 - a)^n \ge 1 - na.
$$

Since $0 < a < 1$, we have $1 - a > 0$. Multiplying both sides of the inequality by $1 - a$ preserves the inequality direction, giving

$$
(1 - a)^{n+1} \ge (1 - na)(1 - a).
$$

Expanding the right-hand side,

$$
(1 - na)(1 - a) = 1 - a - na + na^2 = 1 - (n+1)a + na^2.
$$

Since $a^2 \ge 0$ and $n \ge 1$, we have $na^2 \ge 0$, hence

$$
1 - (n+1)a + na^2 \ge 1 - (n+1)a.
$$

Combining the inequalities yields

$$
(1 - a)^{n+1} \ge 1 - (n+1)a,
$$

so $P(n+1)$ holds.

By mathematical induction, $P(n)$ holds for all positive integers $n$.

This completes the proof. ∎
