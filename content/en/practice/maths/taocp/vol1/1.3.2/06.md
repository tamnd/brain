**Exercise 6.** [*HM20*] (a) Show that if `n` is not prime, `n` has a divisor `d` with `1 < d \le \sqrt{n}`. (b) Use this fact to show that the test in step `P7` of Algorithm `P` proves that `N` is prime.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m22s  

---

## Solution

### (a)

Assume $n$ is not prime. Then there exist integers $a,b$ such that

$$
n = ab,\quad 1 < a \le b < n.
$$

From $a \le b$ it follows that $a^2 \le ab = n$, hence $a \le \sqrt{n}$.

Setting $d = a$ yields a divisor $d$ of $n$ with $1 < d \le \sqrt{n}$.

This completes part (a). ∎

### (b)

In Algorithm $P$, step $P6$ performs the division of $N$ by $\text{PRIME}[K]$ and produces quotient $Q$ and remainder $R$ such that

$$
N = \text{PRIME}[K]\cdot Q + R,\quad 0 \le R < \text{PRIME}[K].
$$

If $R = 0$, then $\text{PRIME}[K]$ divides $N$, and step $P4$ is executed, so $N$ is not declared prime.

Assume $R \ne 0$. Then $\text{PRIME}[K]$ does not divide $N$, and step $P7$ tests whether

$$
Q \le \text{PRIME}[K].
$$

Suppose this condition holds. From $N = \text{PRIME}[K]\cdot Q + R$ and $R \ge 1$, it follows that

$$
N > \text{PRIME}[K]\cdot Q \ge Q^2,
$$

since $Q \le \text{PRIME}[K]$. Hence

$$
Q < \sqrt{N}.
$$

Also,

$$
\text{PRIME}[K] \ge Q.
$$

Thus $\text{PRIME}[K] \ge \sqrt{N}$ is not required; what is required is that every prime divisor of $N$ less than or equal to $\sqrt{N}$ would already appear among $\text{PRIME}[1], \ldots, \text{PRIME}[K-1]$.

Assume $N$ is not prime. By part (a), there exists a divisor $d$ of $N$ such that $1 < d \le \sqrt{N}$. Let $p$ be the smallest prime divisor of $d$. Then $p \mid N$ and $p \le d \le \sqrt{N}$.

Since the algorithm generates $\text{PRIME}[1], \text{PRIME}[2], \ldots$ in increasing order and tests each of them in step $P6$ before advancing $K$, there exists an index $k$ such that $\text{PRIME}[k] = p$ and $k < K$.

At iteration $k$, division of $N$ by $p$ would produce remainder $0$, causing step $P4$ to execute. This contradicts the assumption that the algorithm has reached step $P7$ at index $K$ without earlier termination.

Therefore no such divisor $p$ exists, so $N$ has no divisor in $1 < p \le \sqrt{N}$, and by part (a) $N$ must be prime.

This completes the proof. ∎
