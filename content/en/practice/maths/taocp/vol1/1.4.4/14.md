**Exercise 14.** [*20*] Suppose the computational program does not alternate between ASSIGN and RELEASE, but instead gives the sequence of actions `... ASSIGN ... ASSIGN ... RELEASE ... RELEASE`. What effect does this have on the algorithms described in the text? Is it possibly useful?

## Solution

In the multiple-buffering scheme described in the text, the normal discipline is

$$
\cdots\ \text{ASSIGN}\ \cdots\ \text{RELEASE}\ \cdots\ \text{ASSIGN}\ \cdots\ \text{RELEASE}\ \cdots
$$

so that the computational program holds at most one buffer at a time. After a buffer has been assigned, computation is performed on its contents; the buffer is then released and becomes available for further input or output operations.

If the computational program instead performs

$$
\cdots\ \text{ASSIGN}\ \cdots\ \text{ASSIGN}\ \cdots\ \text{RELEASE}\ \cdots\ \text{RELEASE}\ \cdots,
$$

it acquires two buffers before returning either of them. Consequently, two buffers are removed simultaneously from the pool of free buffers.

Assume that the circle contains $N$ buffers. Under the original discipline, at most one buffer is unavailable because it is assigned to the computational program. Under the modified discipline, at most two buffers are unavailable for this reason. Therefore the effective number of buffers available for overlap between computation and I/O is reduced from $N-1$ to $N-2$.

The algorithms themselves require no essential change. The ASSIGN operation merely advances to the next full buffer, and RELEASE returns a previously assigned buffer to the free list. If ASSIGN is executed twice before any RELEASE, the second ASSIGN simply obtains the next available full buffer. The bookkeeping must retain the identities of both assigned buffers until the corresponding RELEASE operations occur, but the fundamental circular-buffer mechanism remains unchanged.

This behavior is useful when the computation requires simultaneous access to two records or blocks. For example, a merging operation may need to compare data from two input buffers at the same time. By assigning two buffers before releasing either one, both blocks remain available throughout the comparison. Similarly, certain matching, correlation, or look-ahead algorithms may require information from consecutive blocks simultaneously.

The cost of this flexibility is a reduction in buffering capacity. Since two buffers are tied up by the computational program, fewer buffers remain available to absorb differences between computation time and I/O time. If the number of buffers is small, this reduction may increase the probability that an I/O process must wait for a free buffer or that the computational process must wait for a filled buffer.

Thus the effect is equivalent to decreasing the number of available buffers by one. The method is useful whenever the computation must retain two assigned buffers simultaneously; otherwise the alternating ASSIGN-RELEASE discipline is preferable because it leaves more buffers available for overlapping computation with input/output.

$$
\boxed{\text{Two buffers are held simultaneously, reducing the effective buffering capacity from }N-1\text{ to }N-2,\text{ but permitting computations that require concurrent access to two buffers.}}
$$
