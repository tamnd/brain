**Exercise 4.** [*M20*] Show that if a program refers to a single I/O device, we might be able to cut the running time in half by buffering the I/O, in favorable circumstances; but we can never decrease the running time by more than a factor of two, with respect to the time taken by unbuffered I/O.

## Solution

Let a program process $n$ blocks of data using a single I/O device. Let

$$
T=\text{I/O time required per block},
$$

and let

$$
C=\text{computation time performed between successive I/O requests}.
$$

Ignoring start-up and termination effects, the total running time is obtained by summing the time required for each block.

With unbuffered I/O, the computation cannot begin until the current I/O operation has finished. By the discussion leading to equation (1), each block therefore requires

$$
T+C
$$

units of time. Hence the total running time is

$$
U=n(T+C).
$$

With buffering, the I/O for one block can proceed while the computation associated with another block is being performed. Since the program refers to only one I/O device, at any instant there is at most one computation and at most one I/O operation that can be executed simultaneously. Therefore, during each block interval, the elapsed time cannot be less than the larger of the two requirements, $T$ and $C$. Buffer swapping achieves this bound, as shown in the discussion following subroutine (4), so the buffered running time is essentially

$$
B=n\max(T,C).
$$

The improvement factor is therefore

$$
\frac{U}{B}
=
\frac{T+C}{\max(T,C)}.
$$

Assume first that $T\ge C$. Then

$$
\frac{U}{B}
=
\frac{T+C}{T}
=
1+\frac{C}{T}
\le 2.
$$

Assume next that $C\ge T$. Then

$$
\frac{U}{B}
=
\frac{T+C}{C}
=
1+\frac{T}{C}
\le 2.
$$

Thus in all cases

$$
\frac{U}{B}\le 2.
$$

Consequently, buffering can never reduce the running time by more than a factor of two relative to unbuffered I/O.

To show that a factor of two can actually be attained, consider the favorable circumstance

$$
T=C.
$$

Then

$$
U=n(T+C)=2nT,
$$

while

$$
B=n\max(T,C)=nT.
$$

Hence

$$
\frac{U}{B}
=
\frac{2nT}{nT}
=
2.
$$

In this case the I/O activity and the computation overlap completely, so buffering cuts the running time exactly in half.

Therefore the maximum possible improvement factor is $2$, and this factor is attained when the computation time per block equals the I/O time per block.

$$
\boxed{\text{Buffering may reduce the running time by a factor of }2,\text{ but by no larger factor.}}
$$

This completes the proof.
