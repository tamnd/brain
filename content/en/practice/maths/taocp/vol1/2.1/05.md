**Exercise 5.** [*21*] Give an algorithm that essentially undoes the effect of exercise 4: Assuming that the pile is not empty and that its bottom card is face down, your algorithm should

remove the bottom card and make NEWCARD link to it. (This algorithm is sometimes called "cheating" in solitaire games.)

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m13s  

---

## Solution

Let the pile be represented as a linked structure of cards. For each card $V$, let $\mathrm{LINK}(V)$ be the address of the next card in the pile, and let $0$ denote the null link. Let $\mathrm{LOC}(P)$ denote the address of the top card of the pile $P$. The bottom card is the unique card $B$ such that $\mathrm{LINK}(B)=0$.

The required algorithm must remove this bottom card and return it through $\mathrm{NEWCARD}$ while restoring the remaining pile structure.

Define $V$ as a running pointer and $U$ as its predecessor.

**Algorithm.** (_Remove bottom card of a pile_)

**F1.** Set $V \leftarrow \mathrm{LOC}(P)$, $U \leftarrow 0$.

**F2.** If $\mathrm{LINK}(V)=0$, go to F4.

**F3.** Set $U \leftarrow V$, $V \leftarrow \mathrm{LINK}(V)$, and return to F2.

**F4.** Set $\mathrm{NEWCARD} \leftarrow V$.

**F5.** If $U=0$, set $\mathrm{LOC}(P) \leftarrow 0$ and terminate.

**F6.** Set $\mathrm{LINK}(U) \leftarrow 0$, and terminate.

The loop in steps F2–F3 advances $V$ along the unique chain of links starting at $\mathrm{LOC}(P)$. Since the pile is finite and every step moves strictly along a link, the process must eventually reach the unique terminal card $B$ satisfying $\mathrm{LINK}(B)=0$, at which point step F2 transfers control to F4.

During the traversal, $U$ always holds the predecessor of $V$ except when $V$ is the first card, in which case $U=0$. When termination occurs, $V=B$ holds the bottom card, so setting $\mathrm{NEWCARD} \leftarrow V$ correctly outputs the removed card.

If $U=0$, then the pile contained exactly one card, since no predecessor exists for $V$. In this case step F5 sets the pile to empty by assigning $\mathrm{LOC}(P)\leftarrow 0$, which correctly reflects removal of its only element. If $U\neq 0$, then $U$ is the unique predecessor of $B$, so setting $\mathrm{LINK}(U)\leftarrow 0$ removes $B$ from the end of the chain while preserving all preceding links unchanged.

The resulting structure is exactly the original pile with its bottom card removed, and $\mathrm{NEWCARD}$ points to that removed card. This completes the construction of an algorithm that undoes the effect of placing a card at the bottom of the pile. ∎

## Notes

The correctness depends on the invariant that after $k$ executions of step F3, $V$ is the $(k+1)$-st card from the top of the pile and $U$ is its predecessor, except when $k=0$, where $U=0$. This invariant follows directly from the definition of $\mathrm{LINK}$ and the initialization in F1.
