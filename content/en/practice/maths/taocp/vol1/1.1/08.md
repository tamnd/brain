**Exercise 8.** [*M25*] Give an “effective” formal algorithm for computing the greatest common divisor of positive integers $m$ and $n$, by specifying $\theta_j$, $\phi_j$, $a_j$, $b_j$ as in Eqs. [*Hint:* Use Algorithm E, but instead of division in step E1, set $r \leftarrow |m - n|$, $n \leftarrow \min(m, n)$.]

The flaw in the previous solution is not merely a mistake in the proof, but an incorrect encoding of Euclid’s subtraction step. A correct unary formalization must preserve information about both components during subtraction; otherwise the transformation cannot realize

$$
(m,n)\mapsto (|m-n|,\min(m,n)).
$$

A standard fix is to introduce an auxiliary symbol to store the cancelled pairs.

## Correct formal algorithm (Knuth-style unary gcd)

The input is a string $x \in \{a,b\}^*$ of the form $a^m b^n$, $m,n>0$. We introduce a third symbol $c$. The working alphabet is $\{a,b,c\}$. The intended invariant is:

- $a^r b^s c^t$ represents the pair $(r+t,\, s+t)$, hence the encoded gcd is unchanged by trading $(a,b)$ pairs for $c$.

All steps are given in the sense of Knuth’s formal system (3), with deterministic choices such as “leftmost occurrence”.

We set $a_j=b_j=1$ for all $j$.

## Step 1 (cancellation phase)

$$
\theta_1(x): \text{the string contains at least one } a \text{ and at least one } b.
$$

$$
\phi_1:
\begin{cases}
\text{delete one } a \text{ and one } b,\\
\text{append one } c.
\end{cases}
$$

Thus each application transforms:

$$
a^m b^n c^t \;\to\; a^{m-1} b^{n-1} c^{t+1}.
$$

This step is repeated while $\theta_1$ holds.

### Effect of Step 1

After exactly $\min(m,n)$ applications, Step 1 terminates and the string is:

- If $m \ge n$:

$$
a^{m-n} c^n
$$

- If $n \ge m$:

$$
b^{n-m} c^m
$$

No further $a,b$ pairs exist, so cancellation stops.

## Step 2 (relabeling to restore canonical form)

### Case distinction encoded syntactically

$$
\theta_2(x): \text{the string contains no } b.
$$

$$
\phi_2:
\begin{cases}
c \mapsto b
\end{cases}
$$

(No other changes.)

This applies exactly when the remaining majority symbol is $a$, i.e. $m \ge n$. Then:

$$
a^{m-n} c^n \;\to\; a^{m-n} b^n
$$

## Step 3 (symmetric relabeling case)

$$
\theta_3(x): \text{the string contains no } a.
$$

$$
\phi_3:
\begin{cases}
b \mapsto a,\\
c \mapsto b.
\end{cases}
$$

Then:

$$
b^{n-m} c^m \;\to\; a^{n-m} b^m
$$

## Step 4 (iteration structure)

After Step 2 or Step 3, return to Step 1 unless termination occurs.

## Step 5 (halting condition)

$$
\theta_H(x): \text{the string contains no } b.
$$

$$
\phi_H(x): \text{halt and output the number of } a\text{’s}.
$$

## Correctness

### 1. Preservation of gcd

Each cancellation replaces one $a$ and one $b$ by one $c$. In terms of the represented pair:

- $a$ contributes $+1$ to the first component,
- $b$ contributes $+1$ to the second,
- $c$ contributes equally to both components after interpretation.

Thus the transformation

$$
(m,n) \to (m-1,n-1)
$$

is compensated by storing one unit in $c$, preserving the invariant pair

$$
(m,n) \equiv (m-1+t,\, n-1+t).
$$

After full cancellation,

$$
(m,n) \to (m-n,n) \quad \text{or} \quad (m,n-m,m),
$$

which is exactly the Euclidean step:

$$
\gcd(m,n)=\gcd(|m-n|,\min(m,n)).
$$

Relabeling does not change numeric values, only symbol names, hence preserves gcd.

### 2. Correct implementation of Euclid’s algorithm

Step 1 performs repeated subtraction of the smaller number from the larger until one component is exhausted, exactly realizing:

$$
(m,n)\mapsto (|m-n|,\min(m,n)).
$$

Steps 2 and 3 restore canonical encoding so the process can repeat, matching Algorithm E.

### 3. Termination

Each full cycle of Step 1 reduces $m+n$ by $\min(m,n)$, hence strictly decreases $m+n$ while preserving positivity. Therefore only finitely many cycles occur.

When one component becomes zero, Step 1 is disabled and Step 5 triggers halting.

### 4. Output correctness

At termination, the string contains only $a^{\gcd(m,n)}$, because repeated Euclidean reduction leaves exactly the greatest common divisor as the surviving count in the canonical $a$-block.

Thus the output is $\gcd(m,n)$.

## Conclusion

The corrected algorithm fixes the central defect of the previous solution: it avoids destroying information during subtraction by introducing a marker symbol $c$, ensuring that the Euclidean transformation

$$
(m,n)\mapsto (|m-n|,\min(m,n))
$$

is faithfully realized at the string level.

All $\theta_j$ and $\phi_j$ are explicit, deterministic, and effective, so the construction satisfies Knuth’s definition of a formal algorithm.
