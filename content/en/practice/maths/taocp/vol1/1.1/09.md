**Exercise 9.** &#9654; [*M30*] Suppose that $C_1 = (Q_1, I_1, \Omega_1, f_1)$ and $C_2 = (Q_2, I_2, \Omega_2, f_2)$ are computational methods. For example, $C_1$ might stand for Algorithm E as in Eqs. (2), except that $m$ and $n$ are restricted in magnitude, and $C_2$ might stand for a computer program implementation of Algorithm E. (Thus $Q_2$ might be the set of all states of the machine, i.e., all possible configurations of its memory and registers; $f_2$ might be the definition of single machine actions; and $I_2$ might be the set of initial states, each including the program that determines the greatest common divisor as well as the particular values of $m$ and $n$.)

Formulate a set-theoretic definition for the concept “$C_2$ is a representation of $C_1$” or “$C_2$ simulates $C_1$.” This is to mean intuitively that any computation sequence of $C_1$ is mimicked by $C_2$, except that $C_2$ might take more steps in which to do the computation and it might retain more information in its states. (We thereby obtain a rigorous interpretation of the statement, “Program $X$ is an implementation of Algorithm $Y$.”)

Let $C_1=(Q_1,I_1,\Omega_1,f_1)$ and $C_2=(Q_2,I_2,\Omega_2,f_2)$. Write $q\to_i q'$ when $q'=f_i(q)$ is defined, and let $q\Rightarrow_i q'$ denote the reflexive transitive closure.

A computation of $C_i$ is a maximal sequence $q^{(0)},q^{(1)},\dots$ with $q^{(t)}\to_i q^{(t+1)}$ whenever defined, terminating when no successor exists.

## Definition (representation / simulation)

$C_2$ **represents** (or **simulates**) $C_1$ if there exists a relation

$$
R \subseteq Q_2 \times Q_1
$$

and a partial observation map

$$
\rho:\Omega_2 \to \Omega_1
$$

such that the following conditions hold.

## 1. Initial-state correspondence (both soundness and completeness)

Every concrete initial state represents some abstract initial state, and every abstract initial state has a representation:

$$
\forall q_2 \in I_2,\ \exists q_1 \in I_1 \text{ such that } (q_2,q_1)\in R,
$$

$$
\forall q_1 \in I_1,\ \exists q_2 \in I_2 \text{ such that } (q_2,q_1)\in R.
$$

## 2. Step correspondence (forward completeness)

If a related pair performs an abstract step, the concrete system can match it by a finite nonempty computation segment:

$$
(q_2,q_1)\in R \ \wedge\ q_1\to_1 q_1'
\ \Longrightarrow\
\exists q_2' \ (q_2 \Rightarrow_2 q_2' \ \wedge\ (q_2',q_1')\in R).
$$

This ensures every abstract transition can be implemented by $C_2$.

## 3. Concrete-step soundness (no spurious behavior)

Every concrete step must correspond either to stuttering at the abstract level or to a valid abstract step:

If

$$
(q_2,q_1)\in R \ \wedge\ q_2\to_2 q_2',
$$

then either

**(stutter)**

$$
(q_2',q_1)\in R,
$$

or there exists $q_1'\in Q_1$ such that

$$
q_1\to_1 q_1' \quad \text{and} \quad (q_2',q_1')\in R.
$$

This is the missing constraint ensuring that every computation of $C_2$ projects to a valid computation of $C_1$.

## 4. Computation correspondence (induced abstraction of runs)

Let

$$
q_2^{(0)},q_2^{(1)},q_2^{(2)},\dots
$$

be any computation of $C_2$, and choose $q_1^{(0)}$ such that $(q_2^{(0)},q_1^{(0)})\in R$ (possible by initial correspondence).

By repeated use of Step Correspondence and Concrete-step Soundness, one constructs a sequence

$$
q_1^{(0)},q_1^{(1)},q_1^{(2)},\dots
$$

such that:

- either $q_2$-steps that are stuttering leave $q_1$ unchanged,
- or each non-stuttering $C_2$-step matches a valid $C_1$-step,
- and $(q_2^{(t)},q_1^{(t)})\in R$ for all $t$.

Hence every concrete computation projects to an abstract computation (possibly with stuttering).

## 5. Termination correspondence

If a related pair reaches a terminal concrete state, then the abstract state is also terminal, and conversely:

- If $q_2\in Q_2$ is terminal and $(q_2,q_1)\in R$, then $q_1$ is terminal.
- If $q_1$ is terminal and $(q_2,q_1)\in R$, then every concrete path from $q_2$ reaches a terminal state representing $q_1$ after finitely many stuttering steps.

Thus halting behavior is preserved both ways under representation.

## 6. Output consistency

Assume outputs are produced at terminal states (or are part of terminal configurations). Then for any related terminal pair,

$$
(q_2,q_1)\in R,\quad q_2\in \Omega_2,\quad q_1\in \Omega_1,
$$

we require

$$
\rho(q_2)=q_1.
$$

Equivalently, concrete outputs, when interpreted by $\rho$, agree exactly with abstract outputs.

## Conclusion

The relation $R$ together with $\rho$ satisfies:

- every abstract initial state is implemented by some concrete initial state and vice versa,
- every abstract step is implementable by a finite concrete computation,
- every concrete step is behaviorally consistent with the abstract system (stutter or valid transition),
- every concrete computation projects to a valid abstract computation,
- termination and outputs are preserved.

Therefore $C_2$ is a faithful set-theoretic representation (simulation) of $C_1$ in the sense of “implementation correctness,” ruling out both missing behaviors and spurious behaviors.
