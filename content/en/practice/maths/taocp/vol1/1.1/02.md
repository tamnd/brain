**Exercise 2.** [*15*] Prove that $m$ is always greater than $n$ at the beginning of step E1, except possibly the first time this step occurs.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m16s  

---

## Solution

Let the variables $m$ and $n$ at the beginning of a general execution of step E1 be those currently held by Algorithm E. The statement concerns all entries into step E1 after the first execution.

Consider one complete pass through steps E1–E3 that does not terminate the algorithm. At step E1, $r$ is defined as the remainder when $m$ is divided by $n$, so there exists an integer $q$ such that

$$
m = qn + r,\quad 0 \le r < n.
$$

At step E2, the condition $r = 0$ causes termination, so if the algorithm proceeds beyond E2, then $r \ne 0$, hence

$$
0 < r < n.
$$

At step E3, the assignments $m \leftarrow n$, $n \leftarrow r$ are performed. Let $m'$ and $n'$ denote the values after step E3. Then

$$
m' = n,\quad n' = r.
$$

Since $0 < r < n$, it follows that

$$
m' = n > r = n'.
$$

Thus, whenever the algorithm returns to step E1 after executing step E3 without terminating, the inequality $m > n$ holds at the beginning of step E1.

The only situation not covered is the first entry into step E1, when $m$ and $n$ are the original inputs and no prior execution of step E3 has occurred. No relation between the initial values is imposed by the algorithm, so the inequality need not hold in that case.

This completes the proof. ∎
