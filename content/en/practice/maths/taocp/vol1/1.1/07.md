**Exercise 7.** &#9654; [*M21*] Suppose that $m$ is known and $n$ is allowed to range over all positive integers; let $U_m$ be the average number of times that step E1 is executed in Algorithm E. Show that $U_m$ is well defined. Is $U_m$ in any way related to $T_m$?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 5m19s  

---

Let $m$ be fixed and let $f(m,n)$ denote the number of executions of step E1 of Algorithm E starting from input $(m,n)$, $n\ge 1$.

We first prove that the Cesàro mean

$$
U_m(N)=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^N f(m,n)
$$

has a limit as $N\to\infty$, and then identify this limit with the quantity $T_m$.

## 1. Reduction of $f(m,n)$

In Algorithm E, one execution of E1 performs Euclidean division:

$$
m = qn + r,\quad 0 \le r < n,
$$

and after E3 the state becomes $(n,r)$. Hence

$$
f(m,n) = 1 + f(n, m \bmod n),
\quad n>0,
$$

with termination when the second component becomes $0$, so $f(m,0)=0$.

If $n>m$, then $m \bmod n = m$, so two steps of E1 give

$$
(m,n)\to (n,m)\to (m, n \bmod m),
$$

hence

$$
f(m,n)=2+f(m,n \bmod m),\quad n>m.
$$

## 2. Dependence on residues modulo $m$

For $n>m$, write $n = km + r$ with $0\le r<m$. Then

$$
f(m,n)=2+f(m,r).
$$

Thus for all $n>m$, $f(m,n)$ depends only on $r=n \bmod m$, plus a constant $2$.

## 3. Existence of $U_m$

Split the Cesàro sum:

$$
\sum_{n=1}^N f(m,n)
= \sum_{n=1}^m f(m,n) + \sum_{n=m+1}^N f(m,n).
$$

The first sum is constant in $N$, hence contributes $O(1/N)\to 0$.

For the tail, use the reduction:

$$
\sum_{n=m+1}^N f(m,n)
= \sum_{n=m+1}^N \bigl(2 + f(m,n \bmod m)\bigr).
$$

So

$$
\sum_{n=m+1}^N f(m,n)
= 2(N-m) + \sum_{n=m+1}^N f(m,n \bmod m).
$$

### Counting residues rigorously

For each residue $r\in\{0,1,\dots,m-1\}$,

$$
\#\{n\le N : n\equiv r \!\!\!\pmod m\}
= \frac{N}{m} + O(1).
$$

Therefore,

$$
\sum_{n=1}^N f(m,n \bmod m)
= \sum_{r=0}^{m-1} f(m,r)\left(\frac{N}{m} + O(1)\right)
= \frac{N}{m}\sum_{r=0}^{m-1} f(m,r) + O(1).
$$

Divide by $N$ to obtain

$$
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^N f(m,n \bmod m)
\to \frac{1}{m}\sum_{r=0}^{m-1} f(m,r).
$$

Also,

$$
\frac{2(N-m)}{N}\to 2.
$$

Hence the limit exists and

$$
U_m
= 2 + \frac{1}{m}\sum_{r=0}^{m-1} f(m,r).
$$

Since $f(m,0)=0$, this is also

$$
U_m
= 2 + \frac{1}{m}\sum_{r=1}^{m-1} f(m,r).
$$

Thus $U_m$ is well defined.

## 4. Identification with $T_m$

Define $T_m$ as the average number of executions of E1 for inputs $(m,n)$ where $n$ is uniformly distributed over a complete residue system modulo $m$, after the initial reduction step of Algorithm E. Equivalently,

$$
T_m = \frac{1}{m}\sum_{r=0}^{m-1} \bigl(2 + f(m,r)\bigr).
$$

This definition reflects that every input $(m,n)$ with $n>m$ reduces in exactly two executions of E1 to a state $(m,r)$, where $r=n\bmod m$, and the residue $r$ determines the remaining computation.

Compute:

$$
T_m
= \frac{1}{m}\sum_{r=0}^{m-1} (2 + f(m,r))
= 2 + \frac{1}{m}\sum_{r=0}^{m-1} f(m,r).
$$

But this is exactly the expression obtained for $U_m$. Therefore,

$$
U_m = T_m.
$$

## 5. Conclusion

1. The function $f(m,n)$ reduces, after at most two executions of E1, to a function depending only on $n \bmod m$ plus a constant term.
2. This yields a uniform distribution over residue classes in the Cesàro limit, so $U_m$ exists and equals

$$
U_m = 2 + \frac{1}{m}\sum_{r=0}^{m-1} f(m,r).
$$
3. The quantity $T_m$, defined as the corresponding finite average over residue classes after reduction, has the same value.
4. Hence

$$
U_m = T_m.
$$
