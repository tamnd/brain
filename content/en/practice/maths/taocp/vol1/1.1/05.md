**Exercise 5.** &#9654; [*12*] Show that the “Procedure for Reading This Set of Books” that appears after the preface actually fails to be a genuine algorithm on at least three of our five counts! Also mention some differences in format between it and Algorithm E.

## Solution

An algorithm in the sense of Section 1.1 is required to satisfy five properties: finiteness, definiteness, input, output, and effectiveness.

The “Procedure for Reading This Set of Books” is not finite. It prescribes actions such as continuing to read forward, consulting other parts of the book when confusion arises, and returning to earlier material as needed. No bound is given on the number of such transitions, and the process admits the possibility of never reaching a terminal state. Therefore the requirement of finiteness fails.

The procedure is not definite. Several directives are formulated in terms of the reader’s judgment, such as deciding when material is not understood or when additional background should be consulted. These conditions are not specified by a unique mechanical test on symbolic data. Since different readers may make different choices under identical circumstances, the next step of the procedure is not uniquely determined, violating definiteness.

The procedure is not effective. An algorithm requires that each step consist of a basic operation that can be carried out exactly in finite time by a mechanical agent. Instructions such as “study this section until it becomes clear” or “review earlier material as necessary” are not primitive operations in this sense, since they require unbounded interpretation and reasoning without a specified finite rule set.

The input requirement is only partially met. Although the procedure implicitly assumes a reader beginning at a designated starting point, it does not formally specify input data in the sense used for Algorithm E, where explicit numerical values of $m$ and $n$ are given and transformed. The reading procedure operates without a well-defined symbolic input state.

The output requirement is also not well defined. No precise final state is specified beyond a general goal of understanding. In contrast, Algorithm E produces a specific integer, namely the greatest common divisor, determined uniquely from the input values.

Thus the procedure fails at least finiteness, definiteness, and effectiveness among the five required properties.

The format also differs fundamentally from Algorithm E. Algorithm E is expressed as a sequence of numbered steps (E1, E2, E3), each beginning with a bracketed summary phrase followed by a precise operational instruction using replacement notation $\leftarrow$ and explicit conditional control flow. The reading procedure is written in informal prose, without numbered steps, without explicit state variables, and without a formal separation between condition and action. It also lacks explicit termination markers and does not use the replacement operation or structured control flow such as “go back to step E1.”

This completes the solution. ∎
