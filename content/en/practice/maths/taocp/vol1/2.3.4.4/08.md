**Exercise 8.** [**] [20] According to Eq. (9), there are six free trees with six vertices. Draw them, and indicate their centroids.

Equation (9) gives

$$
F(z)=z+z^2+z^3+2z^4+3z^5+6z^6+\cdots,
$$

so the coefficient of $z^6$ is $6$. Hence there are exactly six free trees with six vertices. It remains to list the six isomorphism classes and determine their centroids using the definition preceding Eq. (6): for a vertex $X$, remove $X$, let the resulting subtree sizes be $s_1,\ldots,s_k$, and define $\operatorname{weight}(X)=\max(s_1,\ldots,s_k)$.

For $n=6$, every tree has five edges, so the sum of degrees is $10$. The six unlabeled trees on six vertices are uniquely determined by standard structural decomposition into paths and branchings, and they are listed below explicitly by adjacency structure.

The first tree is the path $P_6$ with vertices

$$
1-2-3-4-5-6.
$$

Removing vertex $3$ leaves components of sizes $2$ and $3$, so $\operatorname{weight}(3)=3$. Removing vertex $4$ also yields component sizes $3$ and $2$, so $\operatorname{weight}(4)=3$. Removing $2$ or $5$ produces a component of size $4$, so their weight is $4$. Leaves have weight $5$. Hence the centroids are $3$ and $4$.

The second tree is the star $K_{1,5}$ with center $c$ and five leaves. Removing $c$ produces five components of size $1$, so $\operatorname{weight}(c)=1$. Removing any leaf produces a component of size $5$, so each leaf has weight $5$. Hence the unique centroid is $c$.

The third tree has degree sequence $(4,1,1,1,1,1)$, namely a star $K_{1,5}$ subdivided once on one edge:

$$
c-a-b,\quad c \text{ also adjacent to } d,e,f.
$$

Removing $c$ produces components of sizes $2,1,1,1,1$, so $\operatorname{weight}(c)=2$. Removing $a$ gives components of sizes $5$, so $\operatorname{weight}(a)=5$. Removing $b$ gives components of sizes $1$ and $4$, so $\operatorname{weight}(b)=4$. Leaves adjacent to $c$ have weight $5$. Hence the centroid is $c$.

The fourth tree is the “fork” with degree sequence $(3,2,1,1,1,1)$:

$$
v \text{ adjacent to } a,b,c,\quad a \text{ adjacent to } d,\quad b,c \text{ leaves}.
$$

For $v$, removing $v$ yields components of sizes $2,1,1,1$, so $\operatorname{weight}(v)=2$. For $a$, removing $a$ yields components of sizes $4$ and $1$, so $\operatorname{weight}(a)=4$. Leaves have weight $5$. Hence the centroid is $v$.

The fifth tree has degree sequence $(3,2,2,1,1,1)$:

$$
v \text{ adjacent to } a,b,c,\quad a \text{ adjacent to } d,\quad b \text{ adjacent to } e,\quad c \text{ leaf}.
$$

For $v$, removing $v$ yields components of sizes $2,2,1$, so $\operatorname{weight}(v)=2$. For $a$, removing $a$ yields components of sizes $4$ and $1$, so $\operatorname{weight}(a)=4$. Similarly for $b$, $\operatorname{weight}(b)=4$. Leaves have weight $5$. Hence the centroid is $v$.

The sixth tree has degree sequence $(3,3,1,1,1,1)$, consisting of two adjacent vertices $u,v$, each of degree $3$, each attached to two leaves:

$$
u \sim v,\quad u \text{ has leaves } a,b,\quad v \text{ has leaves } c,d.
$$

For $u$, removing $u$ yields components of sizes $3,1,1$, so $\operatorname{weight}(u)=3$. For $v$, the same computation gives $\operatorname{weight}(v)=3$. Any leaf has weight $5$. Hence the centroid set consists of $u$ and $v$.

These six structures are pairwise non-isomorphic since they have distinct degree patterns and distinct branching configurations, and every free tree on six vertices must fall into one of these configurations by the standard classification of trees on six vertices (paths, stars, and single-branch extensions of smaller trees), which yields exactly six isomorphism classes. This exhausts all cases, and the centroid of each tree is determined by direct application of the weight definition. ∎
