[Section 2.3.4.4: Enumeration of Trees](../)

**Exercise 15.** [**] [21] Discuss the relationships that exist (if any) between the topological sort algorithm of Section 2.2.3 and the canonical representation of an oriented tree.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m43s  

---

The canonical representation is obtained by repeatedly removing a terminal vertex of the oriented tree, where a terminal vertex is one with no children, equivalently a vertex of indegree $0$ in the orientation $j \to f(j)$. This is exactly the setting of the topological sort algorithm of Section 2.2.3 applied to a directed acyclic graph: the oriented tree is a DAG in which every non-root vertex has outdegree $1$, and the root is the unique vertex with no outgoing arc. Each step of topological sorting selects a vertex with indegree $0$, removes it, and updates the remaining indegrees. In the canonical construction, the additional rule of choosing the smallest available terminal vertex fixes a deterministic topological order $V_1, V_2, \ldots, V_{n-1}$ of all non-root vertices.

The canonical representation refines this topological order by recording, at the moment each vertex $V_j$ is removed, the unique outgoing neighbor $f(V_j)$. Thus the sequence $f(V_1), f(V_2), \ldots, f(V_{n-1})$ can be viewed as the edge-target labels associated with a topological deletion order of the vertices. Conversely, a topological ordering of the non-root vertices together with these recorded successors reconstructs the original oriented tree, since each vertex has exactly one outgoing arc and acyclicity guarantees compatibility with the order. This completes the correspondence between topological sorting and the canonical representation. ∎
