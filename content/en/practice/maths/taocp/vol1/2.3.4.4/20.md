**Exercise 20.** [**] [M24] (J. Riordan.) How many different labeled, oriented trees are there having $n$ vertices, $k_0$ of which have in-degree 0, $k_1$ have in-degree 1, $k_2$ have in-degree 2, ...? (Note that necessarily $k_0+k_1+k_2+\cdots=n$, and $k_1+2k_2+3k_3+\cdots=n-1$.)

Let the vertices be ${1,2,\ldots,n}$ and let the oriented tree be directed toward its root $r$. Then every vertex $v\neq r$ has exactly one outgoing arc, so its in-degree equals the number of its children, while the root has in-degree equal to its total degree. If the in-degree of vertex $v$ is $d(v)$, then the corresponding undirected degree is $d(r)$ for the root and $d(v)+1$ for $v\neq r$. Hence

$$
\sum_{v=1}^n \bigl(d(v)+\mathbf{1}_{v\neq r}\bigr)=2(n-1),
$$

so the degree sequence satisfies the standard tree constraints used in Cayley’s formula.

By Cayley’s formula for labeled trees with prescribed degrees, the number of labeled trees with undirected degrees $\delta(v)$ is

$$
\frac{(n-2)!}{\prod_v (\delta(v)-1)!}.
$$

Substituting $\delta(r)=d(r)$ and $\delta(v)=d(v)+1$ for $v\neq r$, the number of oriented trees with fixed root $r$ and fixed in-degree function $d$ becomes

$$
\frac{(n-2)!}{(d(r)-1)!\prod_{v\neq r} d(v)!}.
$$

Now fix the in-degree profile $k_0,k_1,k_2,\ldots$, where exactly $k_i$ vertices have in-degree $i$, with $\sum_i k_i=n$ and $\sum_i i k_i=n-1$. The root must have in-degree $t\ge 1$, so it can be chosen in $k_t$ ways. For such a choice, there remain $k_t-1$ vertices of in-degree $t$ and $k_i$ vertices of in-degree $i$ for $i\neq t$, giving

$$
\frac{(n-2)!}{(t-1)!(t!)^{k_t-1}\prod_{i\neq t}(i!)^{k_i}}
$$

trees for each fixed root of in-degree $t$. Summing over all admissible root degrees yields

$$
\boxed{\sum_{t\ge 1} k_t \cdot \frac{(n-2)!}{(t-1)!(t!)^{k_t-1}\prod_{i\neq t}(i!)^{k_i}} }.
$$

This accounts for all labeled oriented trees with the prescribed in-degree distribution, since each such tree determines its root uniquely and is counted exactly once by the corresponding term in the sum. ∎
