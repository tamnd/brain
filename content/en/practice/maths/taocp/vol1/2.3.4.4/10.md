[Section 2.3.4.4: Enumeration of Trees](../)

**Exercise 10.** [**] [M22] Prove that a free tree with $n$ vertices and two centroids consists of two free trees with $n/2$ vertices, joined by an edge. Conversely, if two free trees with $m$ vertices are joined by an edge, we obtain a free tree with $2m$ vertices and two centroids.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 5m  

---

Let the two centroids be $C_1$ and $C_2$. By exercise 9 they are adjacent. Remove the edge $C_1C_2$; the tree splits into two components having $s$ and $n-s$ vertices. Since $C_1$ is a centroid, one of the subtrees at $C_1$ has size $n-s$, hence $\operatorname{weight}(C_1)\ge n-s$. Since $C_2$ is also a centroid, one of the subtrees at $C_2$ has size $s$, hence $\operatorname{weight}(C_2)\ge s$. The two centroids have the same minimum weight, and by the discussion preceding this exercise a bicentroidal tree has weight $n/2$. Therefore

$$
s\le \frac n2,\qquad n-s\le \frac n2.
$$

Adding these inequalities gives equality throughout, hence

$$
s=n-s=\frac n2.
$$

Thus the original tree consists of two free trees with $n/2$ vertices, joined by the edge $C_1C_2$.

Conversely, join two free trees of $m$ vertices by an edge connecting vertices $C_1$ and $C_2$. In the resulting free tree with $2m$ vertices, removing the joining edge leaves two components of $m$ vertices each. At $C_1$ the subtree containing $C_2$ has size $m$, while every other subtree has size at most $m-1$; therefore $\operatorname{weight}(C_1)=m$. Similarly, $\operatorname{weight}(C_2)=m$. Any other vertex lies in one of the two $m$-vertex components. When such a vertex is taken as root, one of its subtrees contains the other component together with at least one additional vertex of its own component, so its weight exceeds $m$. Hence no other vertex has weight $m$, and $C_1$ and $C_2$ are the two centroids. This completes the proof.

∎
