[Section 2.3.4.4: Enumeration of Trees](../)

**Exercise 2.** [**] [HM24] (R. Otter.) Show that the numbers $a_n$ satisfy the following condition:

$$
na_{n+1}=a_1s_{n1}+2a_2s_{n2}+\cdots+na_ns_{nn},
$$

where

$$
s_{nk}=\sum_{1\le j\le n/k}a_{n+1-jk}.
$$

(These formulas are useful for the calculation of the $a_n$, since $s_{nk}=s_{(n-k)k}+a_{n+1-k}$.)

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 4m21s  

---

We have $A(z)=\sum_{n\ge1} a_n z^n$ and we seek a recurrence for $a_{n+1}$ in terms of previous $a_j$. Consider an oriented tree with $n+1$ vertices and a distinguished vertex that will become the root. Let $k$ be the size of the subtree attached to a given child of the root. There are $a_k$ ways to form that subtree, and the remaining $n+1-k$ vertices contribute $s_{nk}$ possibilities for the rest of the tree, where

$$
s_{nk}=\sum_{1\le j\le n/k} a_{n+1-jk}.
$$

The factor $k$ in $ka_k s_{nk}$ accounts for the $k$ choices of which vertex in the $k$-vertex subtree attaches directly to the root. Summing over all possible $k$ from $1$ to $n$ gives

$$
na_{n+1}=a_1 s_{n1} + 2 a_2 s_{n2} + \cdots + n a_n s_{nn}.
$$

The relation $s_{nk}=s_{(n-k)k}+a_{n+1-k}$ follows from splitting the sum in $s_{nk}$ into the term $j=1$ and the remaining terms $j>1$, giving a convenient recursive formula for efficient computation of $a_{n+1}$. This completes the derivation. ∎
