[Section 2.3.4.4: Enumeration of Trees](../)

**Exercise 9.** [**] [M20] From the fact that at most one subtree of a vertex in a free tree can contain a centroid, prove that there are at most two centroids in a free tree; furthermore if there are two, then they must be adjacent.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 4m  

---

Let $T$ be a free tree with $n$ vertices and let $\operatorname{weight}(X)$ denote the centroid weight defined in Section 2.3.4.4.

Fix a centroid $X$ of $T$ and root $T$ at $X$. Let the subtrees at $X$ have sizes $s_1,\dots,s_k$, so $s_1+\cdots+s_k=n-1$ and

$$
\operatorname{weight}(X)=\max(s_1,\dots,s_k).
$$

Let $Y$ be a second centroid. Let $P$ be the unique path from $X$ to $Y$, and let $XU$ be the first edge on this path, where $U$ lies in the subtree of $X$ of size $s_j$.

### 1. Lower bound on the opposite component

For any vertex $V$ in the $j$th subtree of $X$, the subtree of $V$ containing $X$ has size $n-s_j$. Hence, by the definition of weight,

$$
\operatorname{weight}(V)\ge n-s_j.
\tag{1}
$$

In particular,

$$
\operatorname{weight}(Y)\ge n-s_j.
\tag{2}
$$

Since $X$ is a centroid,

$$
\operatorname{weight}(X)\le \operatorname{weight}(Y),
$$

so combining with (2) gives

$$
\operatorname{weight}(X)\ge n-s_j.
\tag{3}
$$

### 2. Symmetric bound

Apply the same argument at $Y$. Let the edge $YX$ separate the tree into subtrees at $Y$ with sizes $t_1,\dots,t_\ell$, and let $X$ lie in the subtree of size $t_i$. Then for every vertex $V$ in that subtree,

$$
\operatorname{weight}(V)\ge n-t_i,
$$

so in particular

$$
\operatorname{weight}(X)\ge n-t_i.
\tag{4}
$$

Since $Y$ is also a centroid,

$$
\operatorname{weight}(Y)\le \operatorname{weight}(X),
$$

and combining with (4) yields

$$
\operatorname{weight}(Y)\ge n-t_i.
\tag{5}
$$

### 3. Identification of the two complementary components

The edge $XU$ separates $T$ into two components. Let $A$ be the component containing $X$, and $B$ the component containing $Y$. Then

$$
|B|=n-|A|=n-s_j.
$$

From (3), $\operatorname{weight}(X)\ge |B|$, and from the definition of centroid,

$$
\operatorname{weight}(X)\le |B|,
$$

since $B$ is one of the subtrees contributing to the maximum at $X$. Hence

$$
\operatorname{weight}(X)=|B|=n-s_j.
\tag{6}
$$

Similarly, from the symmetric argument at $Y$, the component containing $X$ must also realize the maximum defining $\operatorname{weight}(Y)$, giving

$$
\operatorname{weight}(Y)=|A|.
\tag{7}
$$

Since $X$ is a centroid,

$$
|B|\le |A|,
$$

and since $Y$ is a centroid,

$$
|A|\le |B|.
$$

Thus

$$
|A|=|B|=\frac{n}{2},
$$

so $n$ is even and $X$ and $Y$ are adjacent.

### 4. Uniqueness of centroids

Let $Z$ be any centroid. Every centroid must lie in a subtree of size at least $n/2$ at any centroidal vertex, since otherwise a larger subtree would determine a strictly smaller weight. Hence $Z$ must lie in one of the two components $A$ or $B$.

Assume $Z\in A$. At vertex $X$, all subtrees other than $B$ lie inside $A$, so the largest subtree at $Z$ contains at least all vertices of $B$ together with $X$, hence has size at least $n/2+1$. Thus

$$
\operatorname{weight}(Z)\ge \frac{n}{2}+1>\frac{n}{2}=\operatorname{weight}(X),
$$

contradicting that $Z$ is a centroid. The same argument holds if $Z\in B$.

Hence no third centroid exists.

Therefore a free tree has at most two centroids, and if two exist they are adjacent. ∎
