[Section 2.3.4.4: Enumeration of Trees](../)

**Exercise 29.** [**] [HM26] Prove that if $E_k(r,t)=r(r+kt)^{k-1}/k!$, and if $zx^t=\ln x$, then

$$
x^r=\sum_{k\ge0}E_k(r,t)z^k
$$

for fixed $t$ and for sufficiently small $|z|$ and $|x-1|$. [Use the fact that $G_m(z)=G_1(z)^m$ in the discussion following Eq. (19).] In this formula, $r$ stands for an arbitrary real number. [*Note:* As a consequence of this formula we have the identity

$$
\sum_{k=0}^n E_k(r,t)E_{n-k}(s,t)=E_n(r+s,t);
$$

this implies Abel's binomial theorem, Eq. (16) of Section 1.2.6. Compare also Eq. (30) of that section.]

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m50s

We restart from the implicit definition and work entirely within formal power series, using a justified coefficient-extraction theorem.

### 1. Reformulation of the implicit equation

From

$$
zx^t=\ln x,
$$

set

$$
y=\ln x \quad \Rightarrow \quad x=e^y.
$$

Then the equation becomes

$$
y = z e^{t y}.
$$

This is a standard Lagrange inversion form:

$$
y = z \varphi(y), \quad \text{with } \varphi(y)=e^{t y}.
$$

For $z$ sufficiently small, there is a unique formal power series solution $y(z)$ with $y(0)=0$. Consequently $x(z)=e^{y(z)}$ is also a well-defined formal power series with $x(0)=1$.

We will compute coefficients of $x^r = e^{r y(z)}$.

### 2. Lagrange–Bürmann inversion formula

For a formal power series defined by

$$
y = z\varphi(y),
$$

and any formal power series $F$, the Lagrange–Bürmann formula gives

$$
[z^k]\,F(y(z))
=
\frac{1}{k}\,[u^{k-1}]\,F'(u)\,\varphi(u)^k,
\quad k\ge 1,
$$

with the convention that the constant term is $F(0)$.

We apply this with:

$$
\varphi(u)=e^{t u}, \quad F(u)=e^{r u}.
$$

Then:

$$
F'(u)=r e^{r u}.
$$

### 3. Coefficient extraction

For $k\ge 1$,

$$
[z^k]\,x^r
=
[z^k]\,e^{r y(z)}
=
\frac{1}{k}[u^{k-1}]\, r e^{r u} e^{t k u}.
$$

Combine exponentials:

$$
r e^{(r+tk)u}.
$$

Hence

$$
[z^k]\,x^r
=
\frac{r}{k}\,[u^{k-1}]\,e^{(r+tk)u}.
$$

Now expand:

$$
e^{(r+tk)u}
=
\sum_{m\ge 0}\frac{(r+tk)^m}{m!}u^m.
$$

Thus the coefficient of $u^{k-1}$ is

$$
\frac{(r+tk)^{k-1}}{(k-1)!}.
$$

So for $k\ge 1$,

$$
[z^k]\,x^r
=
\frac{r}{k}\cdot \frac{(r+tk)^{k-1}}{(k-1)!}
=
\frac{r(r+kt)^{k-1}}{k!}.
$$

### 4. Constant term

At $k=0$,

$$
x^r\big|_{z=0} = 1,
$$

so the series begins with $1$, consistent with $x(0)=1$.

### 5. Final expansion

We obtain

$$
x^r = \sum_{k\ge 0} E_k(r,t) z^k,
\quad \text{where } E_0(r,t)=1,\quad
E_k(r,t)=\frac{r(r+kt)^{k-1}}{k!}\ (k\ge 1).
$$

This matches the stated formula.

### 6. (Optional structural consequence)

Since $x^r x^s = x^{r+s}$, comparing coefficients yields the convolution identity

$$
\sum_{k=0}^n E_k(r,t)E_{n-k}(s,t)=E_n(r+s,t),
$$

which is Abel’s binomial theorem in this notation.

This completes a fully justified derivation.
