[Section 1.2.9: Generating Functions](../)

**Exercise 24.** [*M22*] Prove that, if $G(z)$ is any generating function, we have

$$
\sum_k \binom{m}{k}[z^{\,n-k}]G(z)^k
= [z^n](1+zG(z))^m.
$$

Evaluate both sides of this identity when $G(z)$ is (a) $1/(1-z)$; (b) $(e^z-1)/z$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m11s  

---

## Solution

Expand the right-hand side using the binomial theorem and the definition of generating functions:

$$
(1+zG(z))^m=\sum_{k=0}^m \binom{m}{k}(zG(z))^k
=\sum_{k=0}^m \binom{m}{k} z^k G(z)^k.
$$

For a fixed $k$, the product $z^k G(z)^k$ shifts coefficients by $k$ according to (3), so

$$
[z^n]\bigl(z^k G(z)^k\bigr) = [z^{n-k}]G(z)^k.
$$

Taking coefficients of $z^n$ on both sides of the expansion gives

$$
[z^n](1+zG(z))^m
= \sum_{k=0}^m \binom{m}{k}[z^n](z^k G(z)^k)
= \sum_{k=0}^m \binom{m}{k}[z^{n-k}]G(z)^k.
$$

This matches the required identity.

$$
\sum_k \binom{m}{k}[z^{\,n-k}]G(z)^k
= [z^n](1+zG(z))^m.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎

Now evaluate both sides.

### (a) $G(z)=\frac{1}{1-z}$

Then

$$
1+zG(z)=1+\frac{z}{1-z}=\frac{1}{1-z}.
$$

Hence

$$
(1+zG(z))^m = (1-z)^{-m}.
$$

By the binomial series (20),

$$
(1-z)^{-m}=\sum_{n\ge 0} \binom{m+n-1}{n}z^n.
$$

Thus

$$
[z^n](1+zG(z))^m=\binom{m+n-1}{n}.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\sum_k \binom{m}{k}[z^{\,n-k}]\left(\frac{1}{1-z}\right)^k
= \binom{m+n-1}{n}.
$$

### (b) $G(z)=\frac{e^z-1}{z}$

Then

$$
1+zG(z)=1+(e^z-1)=e^z.
$$

Hence

$$
(1+zG(z))^m=e^{mz}.
$$

Using the exponential series (22),

$$
e^{mz}=\sum_{n\ge 0}\frac{m^n}{n!}z^n,
$$

so

$$
[z^n](1+zG(z))^m=\frac{m^n}{n!}.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\sum_k \binom{m}{k}[z^{\,n-k}]\left(\frac{e^z-1}{z}\right)^k
=\frac{m^n}{n!}.
$$
