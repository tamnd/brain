[Section 1.2.9: Generating Functions](../)

**Exercise 26.** [*M40*] Explore a generalization of the notation (31) according to which we might write, for example, $[z^2 - 2z^5]G(z)=a_2-2a_5$ when $G(z)$ is given by (1).

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m11s  

---

## Setup

Let $G(z)=\sum_{n\ge 0} a_n z^n$ be a generating function in the sense of (1). The coefficient extraction operator satisfies $[z^n]G(z)=a_n$.

We extend the notation by interpreting expressions such as $[z^2-2z^5]G(z)$ as a linear combination of coefficient extractions.

The task is to define this extension rigorously and justify its consistency, in particular that

$$
[z^2-2z^5]G(z)=a_2-2a_5.
$$

## Solution

Let $\varphi(z)=\sum_{k=0}^m \alpha_k z^k$ be any finite polynomial in $z$. Define an operator acting on generating functions by

$$
[\varphi(z)]G(z)=\sum_{k=0}^m \alpha_k [z^k]G(z).
$$

Since $[z^k]G(z)=a_k$, substitution into the definition gives

$$
[\varphi(z)]G(z)=\sum_{k=0}^m \alpha_k a_k.
$$

For $\varphi(z)=z^2-2z^5$, the coefficients are $\alpha_2=1$, $\alpha_5=-2$, and all others zero. Hence

$$
[z^2-2z^5]G(z)=a_2-2a_5.
$$

An equivalent formulation uses the coefficient operator on products. Write

$$
\varphi\!\left(\frac{1}{z}\right)=\sum_{k=0}^m \alpha_k z^{-k}.
$$

Then

$$
\varphi\!\left(\frac{1}{z}\right)G(z)
=\left(\sum_{k\ge 0} a_k z^k\right)\left(\sum_{j=0}^m \alpha_j z^{-j}\right)
=\sum_{k\ge 0}\sum_{j=0}^m \alpha_j a_k z^{k-j}.
$$

The coefficient of $z^0$ occurs when $k=j$, so

$$
[z^0]\left(\varphi\!\left(\frac{1}{z}\right)G(z)\right)=\sum_{k=0}^m \alpha_k a_k.
$$

Therefore,

$$
[\varphi(z)]G(z)=[z^0]\left(\varphi\!\left(\frac{1}{z}\right)G(z)\right).
$$

Both definitions determine the same linear functional on generating functions.

## Verification

Linearity follows directly from the definition:

$$
[\alpha \varphi(z)+\beta \psi(z)]G(z)
=\sum_k (\alpha \varphi_k+\beta \psi_k)a_k
=\alpha[\varphi(z)]G(z)+\beta[\psi(z)]G(z).
$$

For monomials, $\varphi(z)=z^n$ yields

$$
[z^n]G(z)=a_n,
$$

which matches the original coefficient extraction operator, so the extension is consistent with (1).

The constant-term formulation produces the same value since only terms with matching exponents $z^{k-k}$ contribute, and all other exponents are nonzero.

This completes the proof. ∎
