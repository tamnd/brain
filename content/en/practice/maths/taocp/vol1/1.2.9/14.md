[Section 1.2.9: Generating Functions](../)

**Exercise 14.** [*HM21*] Prove Eq. (13).

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m08s  

---

## Solution

Let

$$
G(z)=\sum_{n\ge 0} a_n z^n,\qquad \omega=e^{2\pi i/m},\qquad \omega^m=1,\ \omega^k\ne 1\ (1\le k<m).
$$

Start from the right-hand side of Eq. (13):

$$
\frac{1}{m}\sum_{k=0}^{m-1}\omega^{-kr}G(\omega^k z).
$$

Substituting the defining series (1) gives

$$
\frac{1}{m}\sum_{k=0}^{m-1}\omega^{-kr}\sum_{n\ge 0} a_n (\omega^k z)^n.
$$

Reordering summations yields

$$
\sum_{n\ge 0} a_n z^n \left(\frac{1}{m}\sum_{k=0}^{m-1}\omega^{kn}\omega^{-kr}\right)
=
\sum_{n\ge 0} a_n z^n \left(\frac{1}{m}\sum_{k=0}^{m-1}\omega^{k(n-r)}\right).
$$

Define

$$
S_{n,r}=\frac{1}{m}\sum_{k=0}^{m-1}\omega^{k(n-r)}.
$$

The inner sum is a finite geometric series with ratio $\omega^{n-r}$. Two cases occur.

If $n-r$ is divisible by $m$, then $\omega^{n-r}=1$, hence every term equals $1$, and

$$
S_{n,r}=\frac{1}{m}\cdot m=1.
$$

If $n-r$ is not divisible by $m$, then $\omega^{n-r}\ne 1$, and

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{m-1}\omega^{k(n-r)}
=\frac{1-\omega^{m(n-r)}}{1-\omega^{n-r}}.
$$

Since $\omega^m=1$, the numerator equals $1-1=0$, so the sum is $0$, hence $S_{n,r}=0$.

Therefore

$$
S_{n,r}=
\begin{cases}
1, & n\equiv r \pmod m,\\
0, & n\not\equiv r \pmod m.
\end{cases}
$$

Substituting this into the series gives

$$
\sum_{\substack{n\ge 0\\ n\equiv r\ (\mathrm{mod}\ m)}} a_n z^n,
$$

which matches the left-hand side of Eq. (13).

This completes the proof. ∎
