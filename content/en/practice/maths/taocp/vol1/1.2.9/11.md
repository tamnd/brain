[Section 1.2.9: Generating Functions](../)

**Exercise 11.** [*M25*] Equation (39) can also be used to express the $S$'s in terms of the $h$'s: We find $S_1=h_1$, $S_2=2h_2-h_1^2$, $S_3=3h_3-3h_1h_2+h_1^3$, etc. What is the coefficient of $h_1^{k_1}h_2^{k_2}\cdots h_m^{k_m}$ in this representation of $S_m$, when $k_1+2k_2+\cdots+mk_m=m$?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m46s  

---

## Solution

Let

$$
H(z)=\sum_{m\ge 0} h_m z^m,\qquad h_0=1,
$$

and let

$$
S_j=\sum_i x_i^j.
$$

From Eq. (39) in the text,

$$
H(z)=\prod_i \frac{1}{1-x_i z}.
$$

Taking logarithms gives

$$
\ln H(z)= -\sum_i \ln(1-x_i z).
$$

Using Eq. (24),

$$
-\ln(1-x_i z)=\sum_{j\ge 1}\frac{x_i^j}{j}z^j.
$$

Summing over $i$ yields

$$
\ln H(z)=\sum_{j\ge 1}\frac{S_j}{j}z^j,
$$

hence

$$
S_m = m [z^m]\ln H(z).
$$

Write

$$
H(z)=1+U(z),\qquad U(z)=\sum_{r\ge 1} h_r z^r.
$$

Using Eq. (24),

$$
\ln H(z)=\ln(1+U(z))=\sum_{k\ge 1}\frac{(-1)^{k+1}}{k}U(z)^k.
$$

Fix $k\ge 1$. Expanding $U(z)^k$,

$$
U(z)^k=\left(\sum_{r\ge 1} h_r z^r\right)^k.
$$

A monomial $h_1^{k_1}h_2^{k_2}\cdots h_m^{k_m}$ appears only when

$$
k_1+k_2+\cdots+k_m=k,\qquad 1k_1+2k_2+\cdots+mk_m=m.
$$

For such a choice, the multinomial theorem gives coefficient

$$
\frac{k!}{k_1!k_2!\cdots k_m!}z^m.
$$

Therefore the coefficient of $h_1^{k_1}\cdots h_m^{k_m}z^m$ in $\ln H(z)$ equals

$$
\frac{(-1)^{k+1}}{k}\cdot \frac{k!}{k_1!k_2!\cdots k_m!}
= (-1)^{k+1}\frac{(k-1)!}{k_1!k_2!\cdots k_m!}.
$$

Multiplying by $m$ to obtain $S_m$ gives the final coefficient.

Hence, when

$$
k_1+2k_2+\cdots+mk_m=m,
$$

the coefficient of $h_1^{k_1}h_2^{k_2}\cdots h_m^{k_m}$ in $S_m$ is

$$
\boxed{(-1)^{k+1}\, m\, \frac{(k-1)!}{k_1!k_2!\cdots k_m!}},
$$

where $k=k_1+k_2+\cdots+k_m$.

This completes the proof. ∎
