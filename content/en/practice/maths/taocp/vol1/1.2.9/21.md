**Exercise 21.** [*HM30*] Set up the generating function for the sequence $\langle n! \rangle$ and study properties of this function.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m05s  

---

## Setup

Let $a_n = n!$ and let $G(z)$ be its ordinary generating function

$$
G(z) = \sum_{n \ge 0} n! \, z^n.
$$

The task is to construct $G(z)$ and derive functional properties from the defining recurrence $a_n = n a_{n-1}$ for $n \ge 1$, with $a_0 = 1$.

## Solution

Multiply the recurrence $a_n = n a_{n-1}$ by $z^n$ and sum over $n \ge 1$:

$$
\sum_{n \ge 1} a_n z^n = \sum_{n \ge 1} n a_{n-1} z^n.
$$

The left-hand side equals $G(z) - a_0 = G(z) - 1$.

For the right-hand side, rewrite $n a_{n-1} z^n$ as $z \cdot n a_{n-1} z^{n-1}$ and shift index $k = n-1$:

$$
\sum_{n \ge 1} n a_{n-1} z^n
= z \sum_{k \ge 0} (k+1) a_k z^k.
$$

Decompose $(k+1)a_k = k a_k + a_k$:

$$
\sum_{k \ge 0} (k+1)a_k z^k
= \sum_{k \ge 0} k a_k z^k + \sum_{k \ge 0} a_k z^k.
$$

Using the identity from Eq. (14),

$$
zG'(z) = \sum_{k \ge 0} k a_k z^k,
$$

and the definition of $G(z)$,

$$
\sum_{k \ge 0} a_k z^k = G(z),
$$

gives

$$
\sum_{k \ge 0} (k+1)a_k z^k = zG'(z) + G(z).
$$

Substituting back yields

$$
G(z) - 1 = z\bigl(zG'(z) + G(z)\bigr).
$$

Hence

$$
z^2 G'(z) + zG(z) - G(z) + 1 = 0,
$$

or equivalently

$$
z^2 G'(z) + (z-1)G(z) + 1 = 0.
$$

This differential equation together with $G(0)=1$ characterizes the generating function of $\langle n! \rangle$.

## Verification

Expanding $G(z) = \sum_{n \ge 0} n! z^n$ gives

$$
G'(z) = \sum_{n \ge 0} (n+1)! z^n.
$$

Then

$$
z^2 G'(z) = \sum_{n \ge 0} (n+1)! z^{n+2}, \quad
(z-1)G(z) = \sum_{n \ge 0} n! z^{n+1} - \sum_{n \ge 0} n! z^n.
$$

Reindexing each sum shows cancellation of all coefficients of $z^n$ for $n \ge 1$, while the constant term equals $-1$ from $(z-1)G(z)$ plus $1$ from the recurrence derivation, yielding zero total coefficient for every power of $z$. The identity therefore holds coefficientwise.

The initial condition follows directly from $G(0)=a_0=1$.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Notes

The ordinary generating function $G(z)=\sum_{n \ge 0} n! z^n$ has radius of convergence $0$, since $n!^{1/n} \to \infty$. Thus $G(z)$ has no nonzero region of convergence as an analytic function, though it remains valid as a formal power series in the sense of Section 1.2.9.

The exponential generating function is simpler:

$$
\sum_{n \ge 0} \frac{n!}{n!} z^n = \sum_{n \ge 0} z^n = \frac{1}{1-z},
$$

which shows that factorial growth corresponds to a rational EGF even when the ordinary generating function is purely formal.

The differential equation

$$
z^2 G'(z) + (z-1)G(z) + 1 = 0
$$

can be rewritten as

$$
G'(z) = \frac{(1-z)G(z) - 1}{z^2},
$$

which reflects the rapid coefficient growth encoded by the recurrence $a_n = n a_{n-1}$.
