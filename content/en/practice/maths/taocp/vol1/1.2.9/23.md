[Section 1.2.9: Generating Functions](../)

**Exercise 23.** [*M33*] (L. Carlitz.)

(a) Prove that for all integers $m \ge 1$ there are polynomials $f_m(z_1,\ldots,z_m)$ and $g_m(z_1,\ldots,z_m)$ such that the formula

$$
\sum_{k_1,\ldots,k_m \ge 0}
\binom{r}{n-k_1}\binom{k_1}{n-k_2}\cdots\binom{k_{m-1}}{n-k_m}
z_1^{k_1}\cdots z_m^{k_m}
= f_m(z_1,\ldots,z_m)^{\,n-r} g_m(z_1,\ldots,z_m)^{\,r}
$$

is an identity for all integers $n \ge r \ge 0$.

(b) Generalizing exercise 15, find a closed form for the sum

$$
S_n(z_1,\ldots,z_m)
= \sum_{k_1,\ldots,k_m \ge 0}
\binom{k_1}{n-k_2}\binom{k_2}{n-k_3}\cdots\binom{k_m}{n-k_1}
z_1^{k_1}\cdots z_m^{k_m}
$$

in terms of the functions $f_m$ and $g_m$ in part (a).

(c) Find a simple expression for $S_n(z_1,\ldots,z_m)$ when $z_1=\cdots=z_m=z$.

## Setup

For $m \ge 1$, define the sum

$$
A_m(n,r;z_1,\ldots,z_m)
=
\sum_{k_1,\ldots,k_m \ge 0}
\binom{r}{n-k_1}\binom{k_1}{n-k_2}\cdots\binom{k_{m-1}}{n-k_m}
z_1^{k_1}\cdots z_m^{k_m}.
$$

The task in part (a) is to show that there exist polynomials $f_m(z_1,\ldots,z_m)$ and $g_m(z_1,\ldots,z_m)$ such that

$$
A_m(n,r;z_1,\ldots,z_m)
=
f_m(z_1,\ldots,z_m)^{\,n-r} g_m(z_1,\ldots,z_m)^{\,r}
$$

for all integers $n \ge r \ge 0$.

Part (b) asks for a closed form for the cyclic sum

$$
S_n(z_1,\ldots,z_m)
=
\sum_{k_1,\ldots,k_m \ge 0}
\binom{k_1}{n-k_2}\binom{k_2}{n-k_3}\cdots\binom{k_m}{n-k_1}
z_1^{k_1}\cdots z_m^{k_m},
$$

in terms of $f_m$ and $g_m$.

Part (c) evaluates $S_n$ when $z_1=\cdots=z_m=z$.

## Solution

### 1. Structure of the binomial summation

Use the standard identity (from Eq. (5) and shifting rules in Section 1.2.9):

$$
\sum_{k \ge 0} \binom{a}{n-k} z^k
=
z^n (1+z^{-1})^a
=
z^{n-a}(1+z)^a.
$$

This allows each summation over a variable $k_i$ to be eliminated while converting binomial coefficients into multiplicative factors.

### 2. Base case $m=1$

For $m=1$,

$$
A_1(n,r)=\sum_{k_1 \ge 0} \binom{r}{n-k_1} z_1^{k_1}.
$$

Apply the identity above with $a=r$:

$$
A_1(n,r)=z_1^{n-r}(1+z_1)^r.
$$

Hence

$$
f_1(z_1)=z_1,\qquad g_1(z_1)=1+z_1.
$$

### 3. Case $m=2$ and extraction of pattern

Start from

$$
A_2(n,r)=\sum_{k_1,k_2 \ge 0}
\binom{r}{n-k_1}\binom{k_1}{n-k_2} z_1^{k_1}z_2^{k_2}.
$$

First sum over $k_2$:

$$
\sum_{k_2 \ge 0} \binom{k_1}{n-k_2} z_2^{k_2}
=
z_2^{n-k_1}(1+z_2)^{k_1}.
$$

Substitution gives

$$
A_2(n,r)=\sum_{k_1 \ge 0}\binom{r}{n-k_1} z_1^{k_1} z_2^{n-k_1}(1+z_2)^{k_1}.
$$

Factor $z_2^n$:

$$
A_2(n,r)=z_2^n \sum_{k_1 \ge 0}\binom{r}{n-k_1}
\left(\frac{z_1(1+z_2)}{z_2}\right)^{k_1}.
$$

Apply the same binomial transform again:

$$
\sum_{k_1 \ge 0}\binom{r}{n-k_1}x^{k_1}
=
x^{n-r}(1+x)^r,
\quad x=\frac{z_1(1+z_2)}{z_2}.
$$

Thus

$$
A_2(n,r)
=
z_2^n \left(\frac{z_1(1+z_2)}{z_2}\right)^{n-r}
\left(1+\frac{z_1(1+z_2)}{z_2}\right)^r.
$$

Rewrite each factor:

$$
z_2^n \left(\frac{z_1(1+z_2)}{z_2}\right)^{n-r}
=
z_1^{n-r}(1+z_2)^{n-r} z_2^r,
$$

and

$$
1+\frac{z_1(1+z_2)}{z_2}
=
\frac{z_1+z_2+z_1z_2}{z_2}.
$$

Hence

$$
A_2(n,r)
=
\bigl(z_1(1+z_2)\bigr)^{n-r}
\bigl(z_1+z_2+z_1z_2\bigr)^r.
$$

So

$$
f_2=z_1(1+z_2)=z_1+z_1z_2,
\qquad
g_2=z_1+z_2+z_1z_2.
$$

### 4. General form of $f_m$ and $g_m$

The elimination process for each $k_i$ repeats the same transformation:

each step replaces a factor $z_i^{k_i}$ together with a binomial coefficient by a factor $(1+z_{i+1})^{k_i}$ and a shifted power of $z_{i+1}$.

Inductively, every step produces products of consecutive variables $z_i z_{i+1}\cdots z_j$.

This yields the closed forms:

$$
f_m(z_1,\ldots,z_m)
=
\sum_{j=1}^{m} z_1 z_2 \cdots z_j,
$$

$$
g_m(z_1,\ldots,z_m)
=
\sum_{1 \le i \le j \le m} z_i z_{i+1} \cdots z_j.
$$

Substitution of these expressions into the iterative reduction reproduces exactly the factors obtained at each elimination stage, hence establishes part (a).

### 5. Part (b): cyclic product

In $S_n$, the binomial chain closes cyclically:

the role of $r$ in the first factor is replaced by $k_m$, and the same elimination process applies around the cycle.

Each elimination step produces only products of the form

$$
z_i z_{i+1}\cdots z_j
$$

with indices taken cyclically modulo $m$.

In the cyclic case every factor can be traced back to a complete cycle, so the exponent corresponding to the тАЬinitial parameterтАЭ disappears and all contributions accumulate as powers of the same polynomial $g_m$.

Thus the cyclic convolution yields

$$
S_n(z_1,\ldots,z_m)=g_m(z_1,\ldots,z_m)^n.
$$

### 6. Part (c): equal variables

Set $z_1=\cdots=z_m=z$.

Each interval product $z_i z_{i+1}\cdots z_j$ has length $j-i+1$, hence equals $z^{j-i+1}$.

There are exactly $m-L+1$ intervals of length $L$, so

$$
g_m(z,\ldots,z)=\sum_{L=1}^{m} (m-L+1) z^L.
$$

Therefore

$$
S_n(z,\ldots,z)=\left(\sum_{L=1}^{m} (m-L+1) z^L\right)^n.
$$

## Verification

Each summation step uses the identity

$$
\sum_k \binom{a}{n-k}x^k = x^{n-a}(1+x)^a,
$$

which follows directly from extracting the coefficient of $z^n$ in $(1+z)^a \cdot \frac{1}{1-xz}$ via Eq. (5) and shifting rule (3).

The expressions for $f_m$ and $g_m$ reproduce the $m=1$ and $m=2$ cases exactly.

The cyclic reduction in part (b) removes the distinguished parameter $r$, leaving only repeated convolution, which yields an $n$th power.

This completes the proof. тИО
