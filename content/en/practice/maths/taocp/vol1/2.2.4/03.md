[Section 2.2.4: Circular Lists](../)

**Exercise 3.** [*20*] What does operation (3) do if `PTR₁` and `PTR₂` are both pointing to nodes in the *same* circular list?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m15s  

---

Let the circular list be a directed cycle in which each node has exactly one outgoing link. Let `PTR₁` point to node $p$ and `PTR₂` point to node $q$, with $p \neq q$. Write

$$
\alpha = \text{LINK}(p), \qquad \beta = \text{LINK}(q).
$$

Since the list is a single circular list, there exist unique directed paths along the cycle from $p$ to $q$ and from $q$ to $p$. Thus the cycle can be decomposed into two directed segments:

$$
p \to \cdots \to q \to \beta \to \cdots \to p,
$$

where $\alpha$ is the successor of $p$ on the path toward $q$, and $\beta$ is the successor of $q$ on the path toward $p$.

Operation (3) performs the exchange

$$
\text{LINK}(p) \leftrightarrow \text{LINK}(q),
$$

so after execution,

$$
\text{LINK}(p) = \beta, \qquad \text{LINK}(q) = \alpha.
$$

All other links remain unchanged.

### Structure after the operation

Consider the effect of the new links.

Starting at $p$, we follow:

$$
p \to \beta.
$$

From $\beta$, we continue along the original cycle until we eventually reach $p$. This is because the segment from $q$ back to $p$ in the original cycle is preserved, and $\beta$ lies on that segment. Hence the nodes visited from $p$ form a closed directed cycle containing $p$, $\beta$, and all nodes originally on the path from $q$ to $p$.

Thus one cycle is:

$$
p \to \beta \to \cdots \to p.
$$

Now start at $q$:

$$
q \to \alpha.
$$

From $\alpha$, we follow the original links (unchanged) until we return to $q$, because the segment from $p$ back to $q$ in the original cycle is preserved, and $\alpha$ lies on that segment. Hence the nodes visited from $q$ form a second closed directed cycle:

$$
q \to \alpha \to \cdots \to q.
$$

### Conclusion

After operation (3) with $p$ and $q$ in the same circular list, the original single cycle is cut at $p$ and $q$ and reconnected crosswise. The result is not one cycle but two disjoint circular lists:

- One circular list containing $p$ and all nodes originally between $q$ and $p$, with link structure starting $p \to \beta$.
- A second circular list containing $q$ and all nodes originally between $p$ and $q$, with link structure starting $q \to \alpha$.

Thus the operation splits a single circular list into two circular lists, except in degenerate adjacency cases where one of the resulting cycles may collapse to a single node (for example when $q = \text{LINK}(p)$ or $p = \text{LINK}(q)$).
