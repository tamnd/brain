[Section 2.2.4: Circular Lists](../)

**Exercise 7.** [*10*] Why is it useful to assume that the `ABC` fields of a polynomial list appear in decreasing order?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m15s  

---

The decreasing order of the `ABC` fields makes it possible to compare the current terms of two polynomials and determine immediately whether the exponents are equal, or whether one polynomial contains a term that is absent from the other. In Algorithm $A$, step `A2` uses the relations $ABC(P)<ABC(Q)$, $ABC(P)=ABC(Q)$, and $ABC(P)>ABC(Q)$ to decide whether to advance through `polynomial(Q)`, add coefficients, or insert a new term. Since both lists are ordered in the same way, each pointer moves only forward around its list, and no searching from the beginning is necessary.

The same ordering is advantageous for multiplication and for maintaining a unique representation of a polynomial. Terms having the same exponents occur together when they are created, hence they can be combined immediately, and the resulting list remains arranged in decreasing order. Thus the ordered arrangement permits efficient sequential processing of polynomial terms. $\boxed{\text{The decreasing order allows corresponding terms to be found by a single forward scan and preserves a unique ordered representation.}}$
