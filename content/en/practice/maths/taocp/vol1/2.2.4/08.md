[Section 2.2.4: Circular Lists](../)

**Exercise 8.** [*10*] Why is it useful to have `Q1` trailing one step behind `Q` in Algorithm `A`?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m10s  

---

The list representation used in Algorithm $A$ is singly linked, so the link field of a node gives access only to its successor. When the algorithm moves the pointer $Q$ forward through the polynomial, the previous node is no longer directly accessible unless it is remembered. The variable $Q1$ serves exactly this purpose: it always satisfies $Q = \mathrm{LINK}(Q1)$, so $Q1$ points to the node immediately preceding $Q$ in the traversal order.

This trailing relationship is required whenever the structure is modified. In step $A4$, deletion of the node at $Q$ is performed by changing $\mathrm{LINK}(Q1)$, and in step $A5$ a new node $Q2$ is inserted between $Q1$ and $Q$. Both operations depend on having direct access to the predecessor of $Q$ at the moment of modification. Without $Q1$, each insertion or deletion would require retracing the list from the current starting point to locate the node preceding $Q$, destroying the linear-time behavior of the algorithm. This completes the answer. ∎
