**Exercise 12.** [*M28*] The advantage of having two tables coexist in memory by growing towards each other, rather than by having them kept in separate independently bounded areas, may be quantitatively estimated (to a certain extent) as follows. Use the model of exercise 9 with $n = 2$: for each of the $2^m$ equally probable sequences $a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_m$, let there be $k_1$ 1s and $k_2$ 2s. (Here $k_1$ and $k_2$ are the respective sizes of the two tables after the memory is full. We are able to run the algorithm with $m = k_1 + k_2$ locations when the tables are adjacent, instead of $2\max(k_1, k_2)$ locations to get the same effect with separate tables.)

What is the average value of $\max(k_1, k_2)$?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 2m18s

## Setup

Let $a_1,\ldots,a_m$ be independent choices with $\Pr{a_j=1}=\Pr{a_j=2}=1/2$. Let $k_1$ be the number of indices $j$ with $a_j=1$, and $k_2=m-k_1$ the number with value $2$. Define

$$
K := k_1,
$$

so $K \sim \mathrm{Bin}(m,1/2)$ and $k_2 = m-K$.

The quantity to be determined is

$$
\mathbb{E}[\max(k_1,k_2)] = \mathbb{E}[\max(K, m-K)].
$$

For each integer $k$ with $0 \le k \le m$,

$$
\max(k, m-k) =
\begin{cases}
m-k, & k \le m/2,\\
k, & k \ge m/2.
\end{cases}
$$

This can be rewritten as

$$
\max(k, m-k) = \frac{m}{2} + \left|k - \frac{m}{2}\right|.
$$

## Solution

Taking expectation over $K$,

$$
\mathbb{E}[\max(K,m-K)]
= \frac{m}{2} + \mathbb{E}\left|K - \frac{m}{2}\right|.
$$

Using the distribution of $K$,

$$
\mathbb{E}\left|K - \frac{m}{2}\right|
= \sum_{k=0}^{m} \left|k - \frac{m}{2}\right| \frac{\binom{m}{k}}{2^m}.
$$

Hence,

$$
\mathbb{E}[\max(k_1,k_2)]
= \frac{m}{2} + \frac{1}{2^m}\sum_{k=0}^{m} \left|k - \frac{m}{2}\right|\binom{m}{k}.
$$

By symmetry of the binomial coefficients, terms pair as $k$ and $m-k$ with equal weight, and for $k \le m/2$,

$$
\left|k - \frac{m}{2}\right| = \frac{m}{2} - k.
$$

Thus the sum may also be written over the upper half:

$$
\mathbb{E}\left|K - \frac{m}{2}\right|
= \frac{2}{2^m}\sum_{k > m/2}\left(k - \frac{m}{2}\right)\binom{m}{k}.
$$

Substituting this expression yields

$$
\mathbb{E}[\max(k_1,k_2)]
= \frac{m}{2} + \frac{2}{2^m}\sum_{k > m/2}\left(k - \frac{m}{2}\right)\binom{m}{k}.
$$

This expression depends only on binomial coefficients and the midpoint separation from $m/2$.

## Verification

For each outcome with $K=k$, the identity

$$
\max(k,m-k) = \frac{m}{2} + \left|k - \frac{m}{2}\right|
$$

holds by direct case distinction on whether $k \le m/2$ or $k \ge m/2$, since $m-k - m/2 = m/2 - k$.

The expectation expansion uses linearity of summation over the finite probability space ${0,\ldots,m}$ with weights $\binom{m}{k}/2^m$, which sum to $1$ by the binomial theorem.

Symmetry follows from $\binom{m}{k}=\binom{m}{m-k}$, ensuring paired contributions of $k$ and $m-k$ are equal in probability and contribute identical absolute deviations.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Result

$$
\boxed{
\mathbb{E}[\max(k_1,k_2)]
=
\frac{m}{2}
+
\frac{1}{2^m}\sum_{k=0}^{m}\left|k-\frac{m}{2}\right|\binom{m}{k}
}
$$
