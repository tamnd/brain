**Exercise 9.** [*M27*] Using the mathematical model described near the end of the text, prove that Eq. (14) is the expected number of moves. (Note that the sequence 1, 1, 4, 2, 3, 1, 2, 4, 2, 1 specifies $0 + 0 + 0 + 1 + 1 + 3 + 2 + 0 + 3 + 6 = 16$ moves.)

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m55s  

---

Let the model in the text generate a random sequence

$$
a_1,a_2,\dots,a_n,
$$

where each $a_i$ is chosen independently and uniformly from $\{1,2,\dots,m\}$. In the sequential allocation model used near Eq. (14), a “move” occurs exactly when an element is shifted past a previously stored element of larger label. Hence each unit move corresponds to an inversion of the form

$$
(i,j)\quad \text{with } i<j \text{ and } a_i>a_j.
$$

Thus the total number of moves $M$ is exactly the number of inversions:

$$
M=\sum_{1\le i<j\le n} X_{ij},
\qquad
X_{ij}=
\begin{cases}
1,& a_i>a_j,\\
0,& \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
$$

This corrects the earlier flawed viewpoint: moves are not tied to locations or repacking events, but to ordered pairs whose relative order triggers a shift.

## 1. Linearity of expectation

Taking expectations and using linearity,

$$
\mathbb{E}[M]
=\sum_{1\le i<j\le n}\mathbb{E}[X_{ij}]
=\sum_{1\le i<j\le n}\Pr(a_i>a_j).
$$

No independence between different $X_{ij}$ is needed.

## 2. Computing $\Pr(a_i>a_j)$

Since $a_i$ and $a_j$ are independent and uniformly distributed on $\{1,\dots,m\}$,

$$
\Pr(a_i>a_j)
=\sum_{x=1}^m\sum_{y=1}^m \Pr(a_i=x,a_j=y)\,[x>y].
$$

Because all pairs $(x,y)$ are equally likely,

$$
\Pr(a_i=x,a_j=y)=\frac{1}{m^2}.
$$

For fixed $x$, the number of $y<x$ is $x-1$, so

$$
\Pr(a_i>a_j)
=\frac{1}{m^2}\sum_{x=1}^m (x-1)
=\frac{1}{m^2}\cdot \frac{m(m-1)}{2}
=\frac{m-1}{2m}.
$$

## 3. Substituting into the expectation

There are $\binom{n}{2}$ pairs $(i,j)$ with $i<j$, hence

$$
\mathbb{E}[M]
=\binom{n}{2}\cdot \frac{m-1}{2m}
=\frac{n(n-1)}{2}\cdot \frac{m-1}{2m}.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\mathbb{E}[M]=\frac{n(n-1)(m-1)}{4m}.
$$

This is exactly Eq. (14) in the text.

## 4. Connection with the given example

For the sequence

$$
1,1,4,2,3,1,2,4,2,1,
$$

each term contributes the number of earlier terms that are larger:

- $1\mapsto 0$
- $1\mapsto 0$
- $4\mapsto 0$
- $2\mapsto 1$
- $3\mapsto 1$
- $1\mapsto 3$
- $2\mapsto 2$
- $4\mapsto 0$
- $2\mapsto 3$
- $1\mapsto 6$

Summing gives $16$, consistent with counting inversions.

## 5. Conclusion

The random variable counting moves is the inversion count

$$
M=\sum_{i<j} [a_i>a_j].
$$

Its expectation is obtained by direct evaluation of pairwise probabilities:

$$
\mathbb{E}[M]
=\sum_{i<j}\Pr(a_i>a_j)
=\binom{n}{2}\frac{m-1}{2m}.
$$

This matches Eq. (14), completing the proof. ∎
