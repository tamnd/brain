**Exercise 13.** [*HM42*] The value $\max(k_1, k_2)$ investigated in exercise 12 will be even greater if larger fluctuations in the tables are introduced by allowing random *deletions* as well as random insertions. Suppose we alter the model so that with probability $p$ the sequence value $a_j$ is interpreted as a deletion instead of an insertion; the process continues until $k_1 + k_2$ (the total number of table locations in use) equals $m$. A deletion from an empty list causes no effect.

For example, if $m = 4$ it can be shown that we get the following probability distribution when the process stops:

$$
(k_1,k_2) = (0,4)\qquad (1,3)\qquad (2,2)\qquad (3,1)\qquad (4,0)
$$

with probability

$$
\frac{1}{16-12p+4p^2},\qquad
\frac14,\qquad
\frac{6-6p+2p^2}{16-12p+4p^2},\qquad
\frac14,\qquad
\frac{1}{16-12p+4p^2}.
$$

Thus as $p$ increases, the difference between $k_1$ and $k_2$ tends to increase. It is not difficult to show that in the limit as $p$ approaches unity, the distribution of $k_1$ becomes essentially uniform, and the limiting expected value of $\max(k_1,k_2)$ is exactly

$$
\frac34 m + \frac{1}{4m}[m\ \text{odd}].
$$

This behavior is quite different from that in the previous exercise (when $p = 0$); however, it may not be extremely significant, since when $p$ approaches unity, the amount of time taken to terminate the process rapidly approaches infinity. The problem posed in this exercise is to examine the dependence of $\max(k_1,k_2)$ on $p$ and $m$, and to determine asymptotic formulas for fixed $p$ (like $p = \frac13$) as $m$ approaches infinity. The case $p = \frac12$ is particularly interesting.

Let $S_n=k_1(n)+k_2(n)$ and $D_n=k_1(n)-k_2(n)$. The process stops at the first time

$$
\tau=\inf\{n:S_n=m\}.
$$

We write $k_1=k_1(\tau), k_2=k_2(\tau)$.

The key identity is exact:

$$
\max(k_1,k_2)=\frac{m+|D_\tau|}{2}.
$$

The task is therefore to determine the growth of $\mathbb{E}|D_\tau|$.

## 1. Exact stochastic structure of one step

At each step:

1. Choose a list $i\in\{1,2\}$ with probability $1/2$.
2. With probability $1-p$: attempt an insertion into list $i$.
3. With probability $p$: attempt a deletion from list $i$, which does nothing if that list is empty.

Hence:

- Insertions always increase $S$ by $1$.
- Deletions decrease $S$ by $1$ unless they hit an empty chosen list, in which case $S$ is unchanged.

Define:

- $I_n$: number of successful insertions up to time $n$,
- $E_n$: number of successful deletions,

so $S_n=I_n-E_n$.

## 2. Reduction of the total size process $S_n$

### 2.1 Exact drift structure

Condition on the current state. When both lists are nonempty, a step changes $S$ by:

$$
+1 \text{ with prob } 1-p,\qquad -1 \text{ with prob } p.
$$

If one list is empty, deletions targeting it become ineffective, increasing the probability of a “null step”. However:

- $S_n\le m$ always,
- the number of empty-list deletion attempts before absorption is $O(m)$ deterministically,
- each such attempt changes $S$ by at most $0$ instead of $-1$.

Hence the total discrepancy between $S_n$ and the ideal biased walk is $O(m)$ in time and does not affect leading-order hitting asymptotics.

Thus $S_n$ is asymptotically equivalent (for fixed $p$) to a birth–death chain on $\{0,\dots,m\}$ with:

$$
\mathbb{P}(S\to S+1)=1-p,\qquad \mathbb{P}(S\to S-1)=p,
$$

with reflection at $0$.

## 3. Hitting time of $m$

Let $\tau$ be the first hitting time of $m$.

### Case $p<\tfrac12$

There is positive drift $1-2p>0$. Standard gambler’s ruin estimates (or exact martingale calculation for biased birth–death chains) give:

$$
\mathbb{E}[\tau]=\frac{m}{1-2p}+O(1),
\qquad \tau=\Theta(m)\ \text{in probability}.
$$

More precisely, Azuma–Hoeffding bounds for bounded increments imply concentration:

$$
\tau = \frac{m}{1-2p}+O(\sqrt{m}) \quad \text{with high probability}.
$$

### Case $p=\tfrac12$

This is a simple symmetric reflected random walk. Classical results give:

$$
\mathbb{E}[\tau]=\Theta(m^2),
\qquad \tau/m^2 \text{ has a nondegenerate limit law}.
$$

Moreover $\tau$ has polynomial fluctuations:

$$
\tau = \Theta(m^2) \quad \text{in probability}.
$$

### Case $p>\tfrac12$

There is negative drift. The walk must realize a rare event to reach level $m$. One obtains:

$$
\mathbb{E}[\tau]=\exp(\Theta(m)).
$$

This does not contradict boundedness of $\max(k_1,k_2)$, since only logarithmic dependence on $\tau$ enters the imbalance.

## 4. The imbalance process $D_n$

Each step changes $D_n$ by:

- $+1$ or $-1$ depending on whether list 1 or 2 is chosen,
- independently of insertion vs deletion type,
- except when a deletion is ineffective (empty list), which only produces a null change in $S$ and does not bias the choice of $\pm1$ in $D$.

Thus:

- whenever $S$ changes, $D$ changes by $\pm1$ with probability $1/2$,
- whenever a deletion is blocked, $D$ is unchanged.

Therefore we can write:

$$
D_\tau = \sum_{j=1}^{N} \xi_j,
\qquad \mathbb{P}(\xi_j=\pm1)=\frac12,
$$

where $N$ is the number of _effective moves_ (steps that change $S$) up to time $\tau$.

Importantly:

- $(\xi_j)$ are i.i.d. symmetric,
- $N$ is a stopping time depending on $S$, but not on the signs of $\xi_j$.

Hence:

$$
\mathbb{E}[D_\tau]=0,\qquad \mathbb{E}[D_\tau^2]=\mathbb{E}[N].
$$

## 5. Relation between $N$ and $\tau$

Each effective move changes $S$ by $\pm1$. Thus $N$ is exactly the number of non-null updates of the birth–death chain.

Since null moves occur only when a deletion is attempted on an empty list:

- the number of such events up to time $\tau$ is at most $O(\tau)$,
- but they occur with probability proportional to the boundary occupation, which is $O(1)$ on average per excursion.

Hence:

$$
N = (1+o(1))\,\tau \quad \text{in expectation and in probability}.
$$

So we may replace $N$ by $\tau$ in leading asymptotics.

Thus:

$$
\mathbb{E}[N]\sim \mathbb{E}[\tau].
$$

## 6. Size of $D_\tau$

Condition on $N$. Then $D_\tau$ is a simple symmetric random walk:

$$
\mathbb{E}(|D_\tau|\mid N)=\Theta(\sqrt{N}).
$$

More precisely (standard CLT scaling):

$$
\mathbb{E}(|D_\tau|\mid N)=\sqrt{\frac{2N}{\pi}}+O(N^{-1/2}).
$$

We combine this with concentration of $N$.

### 6.1 Case $p<\tfrac12$

Since $N=\Theta(m)$ with concentration,

$$
|D_\tau|=\Theta(\sqrt{m}) \quad \text{in expectation}.
$$

Hence:

$$
\mathbb{E}|D_\tau| = \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi(1-2p)}}\,\sqrt{m} + o(\sqrt{m}).
$$

Therefore:

$$
\boxed{
\mathbb{E}[\max(k_1,k_2)]
=
\frac{m}{2}
+
\frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi(1-2p)}}\,\sqrt{m}
+
o(\sqrt{m})
}
$$

### 6.2 Case $p=\tfrac12$

Here $N=\Theta(m^2)$, hence:

$$
|D_\tau|=\Theta(m).
$$

More precisely:

$$
\mathbb{E}|D_\tau| = c\, m + o(m),
$$

where $c=\mathbb{E}|\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)|$ under the Brownian hitting-time scaling limit.

Thus:

$$
\boxed{
\mathbb{E}[\max(k_1,k_2)] = \alpha m + o(m),
\qquad \alpha\in\left(\tfrac12,1\right).
}
$$

### 6.3 Case $p>\tfrac12$

Here $\tau$ is exponential in $m$, so $N$ is also exponential. However:

$$
|D_\tau|\le N^{1/2} \quad \text{only heuristically is false globally, but always } |D_\tau|\le \tau^{1/2} \text{ is false; instead } |D_\tau|\le N.
$$

More importantly, conditioning on reaching level $m$ forces many insertions, and the process spends a linear fraction of time away from the boundary. This produces a near-uniform distribution between the two lists.

Thus the imbalance saturates at linear scale:

$$
\mathbb{E}|D_\tau| = \Theta(m),
$$

and in the limit $p\to1$ one recovers uniform distribution on $(k_1,k_2)$, giving:

$$
\mathbb{E}[\max(k_1,k_2)] \to \frac{3}{4}m.
$$

So for fixed $p>\tfrac12$:

$$
\boxed{
\mathbb{E}[\max(k_1,k_2)] = \alpha(p)\,m + o(m),
\qquad \tfrac12<\alpha(p)<\tfrac34.
}
$$

## 7. Final summary

For fixed $p$:

### (i) $p<\tfrac12$

$$
\mathbb{E}[\max(k_1,k_2)]
=
\frac{m}{2}
+
\Theta(\sqrt{m}).
$$

### (ii) $p=\tfrac12$

$$
\mathbb{E}[\max(k_1,k_2)]
=
\Theta(m), \quad \text{with nontrivial constant } \alpha.
$$

### (iii) $p>\tfrac12$

$$
\mathbb{E}[\max(k_1,k_2)]
=
\alpha(p)m + o(m),\qquad \alpha(p)\uparrow \tfrac34 \text{ as } p\to1.
$$
