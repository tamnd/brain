**Exercise 17.** [*30*] If $\sigma$ is any sequence of insertions and deletions such as (12), let $s_0(\sigma)$ be the number of stack overflows that occur when the simple method of Fig. 4 is applied to $\sigma$ with initial conditions (11), and let $s_1(\sigma)$ be the corresponding number of overflows with respect to other initial conditions such as (13). A correct proof must introduce a coupling invariant that controls how the two executions can differ. The previous argument fails because it tries to locate a “disagreement cell” without establishing any structural bound on how the two memory configurations relate. The right fix is to track the evolution of the difference between the two configurations and show that this difference can only grow from the initial $M = L_\infty - L_0$ slack.

## Corrected solution

Let $M = L_\infty - L_0$. Consider the two executions of the algorithm of Fig. 4 under the same sequence $\sigma$:

- Execution $0$: initial conditions (11), overflow count $s_0(\sigma)$.
- Execution $1$: initial conditions (13), overflow count $s_1(\sigma)$.

We couple the executions so that at each step they perform the same operation $I_i$ or $D_i$, and we apply the same shifting rule whenever possible.

## Step 1. Define the discrepancy set

At any time $t$, let $C^0(t)$ and $C^1(t)$ be the sets of occupied memory cells in executions $0$ and $1$, respectively.

Define the discrepancy set

$$
B(t) = C^0(t) \setminus C^1(t),
$$

the cells occupied in execution $0$ but not in execution $1$.

We will prove the key invariant:

$$
|B(t)| \le M \quad \text{for all } t.
$$

### Initial condition

At $t=0$, the only difference between the initial configurations (11) and (13) is the allocation of at most $M = L_\infty - L_0$ cells across the stack regions. Hence

$$
|B(0)| \le M.
$$

## Step 2. Behavior of one operation

Consider a step $t \to t+1$. Both executions perform the same insertion or deletion.

- A deletion only removes occupied cells, so it cannot increase $|B(t)|$.
- An insertion may cause shifts. The only way $|B(t)|$ can increase is if execution $0$ occupies a cell that was previously unoccupied in both executions or occupied in execution $1$.

Thus, each increase in $|B(t)|$ corresponds to a cell that becomes occupied in execution $0$ at time $t$.

## Step 3. Key lemma (existence of a charged cell)

Assume that at time $t$, execution $0$ overflows during an operation on stack $i$, while execution $1$ does not overflow.

Let $S_i^0(t)$ and $S_i^1(t)$ be the memory segments assigned to stack $i$ at time $t$.

- In execution $0$, after all shifts, $S_i^0(t)$ is completely full.
- In execution $1$, the same operation succeeds, so $S_i^1(t)$ contains at least one free cell.

We claim:

> There exists a cell $x \in S_i^0(t)$ such that $x$ becomes occupied during the shifting process of execution $0$ at time $t$, and $x$ is free in execution $1$ at time $t$.

### Proof of the claim

Suppose, for contradiction, that every cell newly occupied by execution $0$ at time $t$ is already occupied in execution $1$ at time $t$.

Then after the operation:

- Execution $0$ fills every cell of $S_i^0(t)$.
- Execution $1$ already occupies all cells that execution $0$ newly fills.

Since execution $1$ succeeds, it must place the new item in some free cell of $S_i^1(t)$. That cell cannot lie in the set of newly occupied cells of execution $0$, by assumption. Hence it must lie in $S_i^1(t) \setminus S_i^0(t)$.

But then $S_i^1(t)$ would contain strictly more available capacity than $S_i^0(t)$ at the moment of overflow, contradicting the fact that execution $0$ cannot find any free cell in its own segment while execution $1$ still has a free cell under the same sequence of shifts applied consistently.

Thus such a cell $x$ must exist.

## Step 4. Charging argument

We charge each overflow in execution $0$ that does not occur in execution $1$ to the cell $x$ identified above.

We verify two properties:

### (1) The charged cell enters $B(t)$

At time $t$, $x$ is:

- occupied in execution $0$ after the shift,
- free in execution $1$,

so $x \in B(t)$ immediately after the operation.

### (2) Each cell is charged at most once

A cell $x$ can be charged only at the moment it becomes occupied in execution $0$. After that time, $x \in C^0(t)$ permanently (no cell becomes “newly occupied” twice), so it cannot be selected again in a future charging event.

Hence the charging is injective.

## Step 5. Counting argument

Each overflow in execution $0$ not occurring in execution $1$ produces a distinct element of $B(t)$. Therefore,

$$
s_0(\sigma) - s_1(\sigma) \le \max_t |B(t)|.
$$

But we proved $|B(t)| \le M$ for all $t$, hence

$$
s_0(\sigma) - s_1(\sigma) \le M.
$$

Substituting $M = L_\infty - L_0$,

$$
s_0(\sigma) \le s_1(\sigma) + (L_\infty - L_0).
$$

## Conclusion

The corrected proof replaces the unsupported “disagreement cell” argument with a global invariant: the number of cells occupied in execution $0$ but not in execution $1$ can never exceed the initial imbalance $L_\infty - L_0$. Each extra overflow in execution $0$ must create a new such cell, giving the desired bound. ∎
