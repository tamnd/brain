**Exercise 10.** [*M21*] Given that "$\subset$" is a relation that satisfies properties (i) and (ii) of a partial ordering, prove that the relation "$\preceq$", defined by the rule "$x \preceq y$ if and only if $x = y$ or $x \subset y$," satisfies all three properties of a partial ordering.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m07s  

---

## Solution

Define the relation $\preceq$ by

$$
x \preceq y \iff (x = y \ \text{or}\ x \subset y).
$$

Assume the relation $\subset$ satisfies properties (i) and (ii) of a strict partial ordering, namely, for all $x,y,z$,

$$
x \subset y \ \text{and}\ y \subset z \Rightarrow x \subset z,
$$

and

$$
x \subset x \ \text{never holds}.
$$

### Reflexivity

For any $x$, the definition gives $x = x$, hence $x \preceq x$. Thus $\preceq$ is reflexive.

### Antisymmetry

Assume $x \preceq y$ and $y \preceq x$. Then each relation is either equality or a strict $\subset$ relation.

If $x = y$, the conclusion holds.

If $x \subset y$, then $y \preceq x$ forces either $y = x$ or $y \subset x$. The case $y = x$ contradicts $x \subset y$. If $y \subset x$, then by transitivity of $\subset$ applied to $x \subset y$ and $y \subset x$, we obtain $x \subset x$, contradicting property (ii). Hence $x = y$.

Thus $x \preceq y$ and $y \preceq x$ imply $x = y$.

### Transitivity

Assume $x \preceq y$ and $y \preceq z$. Each relation is either equality or $\subset$.

If $x = y$ and $y = z$, then $x = z$, hence $x \preceq z$.

If $x = y$ and $y \subset z$, then $x \subset z$, hence $x \preceq z$.

If $x \subset y$ and $y = z$, then $x \subset z$, hence $x \preceq z$.

If $x \subset y$ and $y \subset z$, then by property (i) of $\subset$,

$$
x \subset z,
$$

hence $x \preceq z$.

All cases yield $x \preceq z$, so $\preceq$ is transitive.

Since $\preceq$ is reflexive, antisymmetric, and transitive, it is a partial ordering.

This completes the proof. ∎
