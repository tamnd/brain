**Exercise 9.** [*20*] Which of the following relations is a partial ordering on the specified set $S$? [*Note:* If the relation "$x \prec y$" is defined below, the intent is to define the relation "$x \preceq y \equiv (x \prec y \text{ or } x = y)$," and then to determine whether $\preceq$ is a partial ordering.]

(a) $S =$ all rational numbers, $x \prec y$ means $x > y$.

(b) $S =$ all people, $x \prec y$ means $x$ is an ancestor of $y$.

(c) $S =$ all integers, $x \preceq y$ means $x$ is a multiple of $y$ (that is, $x \bmod y = 0$).

(d) $S =$ all the mathematical results proved in this book, $x \prec y$ means the proof of $y$ depends upon the truth of $x$.

(e) $S =$ all positive integers, $x \preceq y$ means $x + y$ is even.

(f) $S =$ a set of subroutines, $x \prec y$ means "$x$ calls $y$," that is, $y$ may be in operation while $x$ is in operation, with recursion not allowed.

## Solution

A relation $\preceq$ is a partial ordering on $S$ if it is reflexive, antisymmetric, and transitive.

By definition in the problem, $x \preceq y$ means $(x \prec y \text{ or } x=y)$.

### (a) $S =$ all rational numbers, $x \prec y$ means $x > y$

Then $x \preceq y$ means $(x>y \text{ or } x=y)$, hence $x \preceq y \equiv x \ge y$.

Reflexivity holds since $x \ge x$ for all $x \in \mathbb{Q}$.

If $x \ge y$ and $y \ge x$, then $x \ge y$ implies $x-y \ge 0$ and $y-x \ge 0$, so $x-y=0$, hence $x=y$, giving antisymmetry.

If $x \ge y$ and $y \ge z$, then $x-y \ge 0$ and $y-z \ge 0$, so $x-z=(x-y)+(y-z) \ge 0$, hence $x \ge z$, giving transitivity.

Thus this is a partial ordering.

### (b) $S =$ all people, $x \prec y$ means $x$ is an ancestor of $y$

Then $x \preceq y$ means $x$ is an ancestor of $y$ or $x=y$, so it is the ancestor-or-equal relation.

Reflexivity holds by the equality case.

If $x$ is an ancestor of $y$ and $y$ is an ancestor of $x$, then $x=y$ since ancestry does not permit cycles; otherwise a person would be their own proper ancestor through a finite chain, contradicting the intended structure of ancestry. Hence antisymmetry holds.

If $x$ is an ancestor of $y$ and $y$ is an ancestor of $z$, then concatenating the two ancestry chains shows $x$ is an ancestor of $z$, giving transitivity.

Thus this is a partial ordering.

### (c) $S =$ all integers, $x \preceq y$ means $x$ is a multiple of $y$

The condition means $y \mid x$, so $x \preceq y \equiv (y \mid x)$.

Reflexivity holds since $x = 1 \cdot x$, so $x \mid x$.

Antisymmetry fails. Take $x=1$, $y=-1$. Then $-1 \mid 1$ since $1 = (-1)(-1)$, and $1 \mid -1$ since $-1 = 1 \cdot (-1)$. Both $x \preceq y$ and $y \preceq x$ hold while $x \ne y$.

Since antisymmetry fails, this is not a partial ordering.

### (d) $S =$ all mathematical results proved in this book, $x \prec y$ means the proof of $y$ depends on $x$

Then $x \preceq y$ means $x$ is used in the proof of $y$, or $x=y$.

Reflexivity holds by equality.

If $x$ is used in the proof of $y$ and $y$ is used in the proof of $z$, then every dependency used to establish $y$ is also required for $z$, so $x$ is used in the proof of $z$. This gives transitivity.

If $x \preceq y$ and $y \preceq x$ with $x \ne y$, then each depends on the other, producing a circular dependency in proofs, which is excluded in a well-founded system of mathematical results. Hence antisymmetry holds.

Thus this is a partial ordering.

### (e) $S =$ positive integers, $x \preceq y$ means $x+y$ is even

The condition $x+y$ even holds exactly when $x$ and $y$ have the same parity, so every element is related to all elements of the same parity class.

Reflexivity holds since $x+x=2x$ is even.

Antisymmetry fails since $1 \preceq 3$ and $3 \preceq 1$ hold while $1 \ne 3$.

Since antisymmetry fails, this is not a partial ordering.

### (f) $S =$ set of subroutines, $x \prec y$ means $x$ calls $y$, recursion not allowed

Then $x \preceq y$ means $x$ calls $y$ or $x=y$.

Reflexivity holds by equality.

If $x$ calls $y$ and $y$ calls $z$, then execution of $x$ may activate $y$, and execution of $y$ may activate $z$, so $x$ calls $z$; hence transitivity holds.

If $x \preceq y$ and $y \preceq x$ with $x \ne y$, then $x$ calls $y$ and $y$ calls $x$, forming a cycle, contradicting the assumption that recursion is not allowed. Hence antisymmetry holds.

Thus this is a partial ordering.

## Answer

(a) partial ordering

(b) partial ordering

(c) not a partial ordering

(d) partial ordering

(e) not a partial ordering

(f) partial ordering ∎
