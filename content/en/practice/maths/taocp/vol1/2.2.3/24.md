## Corrected Solution

We work with Program $T$, the topological sorting algorithm of Section 2.2.3, including the extensions from Exercise 23, which provide the necessary representation of adjacency and allow efficient access to incoming or outgoing neighbors in the remaining subgraph.

At the moment step $T8$ is reached, the set $R$ of remaining vertices is nonempty and has the property:

$$
\forall v \in R,\quad \operatorname{indeg}_R(v) \ge 1.
$$

Here $\operatorname{indeg}_R(v)$ denotes indegree within the subgraph induced by $R$. Consequently, every vertex in $R$ has at least one incoming edge from another vertex of $R$.

## Key construction: predecessor selection inside $R$

We define a function $\operatorname{PRED}$ directly from the stored adjacency structure of Program $T$.

For each vertex $v \in R$, scan the adjacency lists (as maintained in Program $T$ with the extensions of Exercise 23) to find a vertex $u \in R$ such that

$$
u \to v
$$

is an edge of the original graph that has not been removed.

Define:

$$
\operatorname{PRED}(v) = \text{any such } u.
$$

This is well-defined because $\operatorname{indeg}_R(v) \ge 1$ for every $v \in R$. Hence $\operatorname{PRED}$ is a **total function on $R$**.

Moreover, every chosen edge $v \mapsto \operatorname{PRED}(v)$ is an actual edge of the original graph restricted to $R$.

## Structural consequence

The mapping $\operatorname{PRED}: R \to R$ defines a directed graph in which every vertex has outdegree exactly $1$. Such a directed graph is a functional digraph on a finite set.

A standard property of finite functional digraphs implies:

> Every connected component contains exactly one directed cycle, and every vertex lies on a directed path leading into that cycle.

We now extract such a cycle.

## Cycle extraction procedure

1. Choose any vertex $v_0 \in R$.
2. Form the sequence:

$$
v_0,\ v_1,\ v_2,\ \dots
\quad \text{where } v_{i+1} = \operatorname{PRED}(v_i).
$$
3. Since $R$ is finite, there exist indices $i < j$ such that $v_i = v_j$.

Then the sequence

$$
v_i,\ v_{i+1},\ \dots,\ v_{j-1}
$$

forms a directed cycle, because each step follows a directed edge $x \to \operatorname{PRED}(x)$, and the sequence returns to its starting vertex.

## Output modification of Program $T$

4. Output the cycle formed between the first repetition and its predecessor occurrence.

## Correctness justification

### 1. Well-defined predecessor assignment

For each $v \in R$, the condition $\operatorname{indeg}_R(v) \ge 1$ guarantees existence of at least one vertex $u \in R$ with $u \to v$. Hence $\operatorname{PRED}(v)$ is well-defined for all $v \in R$.

Thus $\operatorname{PRED}$ is a total function on $R$.

### 2. Formation of a finite functional digraph

The mapping $\operatorname{PRED}: R \to R$ assigns exactly one outgoing edge from each vertex in $R$. Therefore the edges

$$
v \to \operatorname{PRED}(v)
$$

form a directed graph in which every vertex has outdegree $1$.

### 3. Existence of a directed cycle

Since $R$ is finite, any infinite sequence

$$
v_0, v_1, v_2, \dots \quad (v_{i+1} = \operatorname{PRED}(v_i))
$$

must repeat a vertex. Let $v_i = v_j$ with $i < j$ be the first repetition. Then the segment from $v_i$ to $v_{j-1}$ forms a directed cycle, because each transition follows a valid edge in the original graph.

### 4. The cycle lies in the original graph

Each edge $v \to \operatorname{PRED}(v)$ was chosen from the original adjacency structure restricted to $R$. No artificial edges are introduced. Hence every edge in the output cycle is an edge of the original input graph.

### 5. Proper incorporation into Program $T$

The construction uses only:

- the remaining vertex set $R$,

and introduces no auxiliary traversal mechanism external to the algorithm’s state.
