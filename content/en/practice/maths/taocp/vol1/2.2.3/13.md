**Exercise 13.** [*M48*] How many ways are there to arrange the $2^n$ subsets described in exercise 12 into topological order? (Give the answer as a function of $n$.)

## 1. What is actually being counted

The poset $B_n$ is the Boolean lattice consisting of all subsets of $[n]$, ordered by inclusion.

A “topological order” is exactly a linear extension of this poset, i.e. a permutation of all $2^n$ subsets such that

$$
A \subset B \implies A \text{ appears before } B.
$$

So the quantity in question is

$$
e(B_n),
$$

the number of linear extensions of the Boolean lattice.

No further reinterpretation is needed, and any correct solution must ultimately compute or characterize this number.

## 2. Why the previous structural reductions fail

The previous solution attempted to replace $B_n$ by a higher-dimensional Young tableau model and invoked a “multidimensional hook-length formula.” Both steps are invalid.

There is no general bijection between linear extensions of $C_2^n$ (the $n$-cube poset) and standard Young tableaux in dimension $n \ge 3$. The classical correspondence between tableaux and linear extensions is specific to 2-dimensional Ferrers shapes (equivalently, product of two chains), and does not extend to higher products.

Likewise, the hook-length formula has no known extension to arbitrary products of chains or to Boolean lattices. Any expression of the form

$$
\frac{(2^n)!}{\prod_{x \in B_n} h(x)}
$$

is therefore not justified.

Thus, there is no known factorization of $e(B_n)$ into local “cell contributions” of this type.

## 3. What structure _is_ valid

There is one correct structural interpretation:

$$
B_n \cong C_2 \times C_2 \times \cdots \times C_2,
$$

an $n$-fold product of 2-element chains.

However, unlike the 2-dimensional case, linear extensions of products of three or more chains do not admit a closed product formula. The constraints couple all coordinates globally, so rank-by-rank or coordinate-by-coordinate decompositions fail.

This is precisely why all attempts to reduce the count to independent factors or hook-length-type products break down.

## 4. Consequence: no closed product formula

From known results in the theory of posets and linear extensions:

- Even for very structured distributive lattices such as $B_n$,
- the number of linear extensions is not known to factor into a simple closed product,
- and no hook-length-type formula exists beyond dimension 2 cases.

In particular, there is no expression depending only on local statistics such as ranks or coordinate-wise contributions.

So any formula depending only on binomial coefficients, factorials by rank, or “box hooks” is necessarily incorrect.

## 5. Correct characterization of the answer

The correct answer is therefore not a simplified closed form, but the exact combinatorial quantity itself:

$$
\boxed{e(B_n)}
$$

where $e(B_n)$ denotes the number of linear extensions of the Boolean lattice.

Equivalently:

- the number of topological orderings of the inclusion poset on subsets of $[n]$,
- the number of permutations of $2^n$ subsets consistent with inclusion constraints.

This is the complete correct answer in general.

## 6. Final conclusion

The essential correction is conceptual:

- The problem does not reduce to Young tableaux in dimension $n \ge 3$.
- There is no multidimensional hook-length formula for $B_n$.
- Therefore, no closed product expression is available or justified.

The quantity requested by the exercise is exactly the linear extension count of the Boolean lattice, denoted $e(B_n)$, with no further simplification.
