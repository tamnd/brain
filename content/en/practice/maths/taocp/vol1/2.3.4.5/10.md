[Section 2.3.4.5: Path Length](../)

**Exercise 10.** [**] [M26] (D. Huffman.) Show how to construct a $t$-ary tree with minimum
weighted path length, given nonnegative weights $w_1,\ldots,w_m$. Construct the
optimal ternary tree corresponding to exercise 2.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m07s  

---

The binary case extends directly. In any optimal extended $t$-ary tree, the $t$ external nodes of greatest depth must carry the $t$ smallest weights, since interchanging a smaller weight with a larger weight at greater depth cannot increase the weighted path length. These $t$ leaves must have a common parent; replacing that parent and its $t$ children by a single external node of weight equal to their sum decreases every one of those $t$ path lengths by $1$, hence decreases the total weighted path length by exactly $w_1+\cdots+w_t$. Therefore a tree for $w_1,\ldots,w_m$ is optimal if and only if the contracted tree is optimal for the weights $w_1+\cdots+w_t,w_{t+1},\ldots,w_m$. This gives the inductive algorithm: repeatedly combine the $t$ smallest weights into their sum, solve the reduced problem, then expand the combined weight into a parent with those $t$ leaves as children. If $(m-1)\not\equiv0\pmod{t-1}$, first adjoin enough zero weights to make the congruence hold, since zero-weight leaves do not affect the weighted path length.

For the ternary case of exercise 2, the weights are $2,3,4,11$. Since $(4-1)\not\equiv0\pmod2$, adjoin one zero weight. The successive combinations are

$$
0+2+3=5,\qquad 4+5+11=20.
$$

Hence the optimal ternary tree has the root with children of weights $4$, $11$, and an internal node; that internal node has three terminal children of weights $0$, $2$, and $3$. The weighted path length is

$$
4\cdot1+11\cdot1+2\cdot2+3\cdot2=25,
$$

which is minimal by the inductive argument. Therefore the optimum weighted path length is

$$
\boxed{25}.
$$
