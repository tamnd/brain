**Exercise 14.** [**] [25] (T. C. Hu and A. C. Tucker.) After $k$ steps of Huffman's
algorithm, the nodes combined so far form a forest of $m-k$ extended binary
trees. Prove that this forest has the smallest total weighted path length,
among all forests of $m-k$ extended binary trees that have the given weights.

**Solve time:** 4m46s

We repair the argument by separating three facts that were previously conflated:

1. A completion problem on a forest reduces to a Huffman problem on the forest roots.
2. In any optimal completion, the two smallest available weights can be taken as sibling leaves.
3. Contracting a sibling pair reduces the objective by an exact constant, so optimality is preserved under contraction.

We then run a clean induction without any uniqueness assumptions.

## 1. Reduction to a problem on forest roots

Let a forest $F$ consist of $r$ extended binary trees $T_1,\dots,T_r$. Let $W(T)$ be the sum of weights of external nodes (leaves) in $T$.

Fix a completion $C$ of $F$, i.e. a full binary tree obtained by joining the roots of the trees in $F$ without altering internal structure.

For each leaf $i$ in a tree $T$, its depth in the completion is

$$
d_C(i) = d_C(T) + d_T(i),
$$

where $d_T(i)$ is depth inside $T$, and $d_C(T)$ is depth of the root of $T$ in the completion.

Hence the total weighted path length is

$$
L(C)
=
\sum_{T \in F} \sum_{i \in T} w_i(d_C(T)+d_T(i)).
$$

Rearranging,

$$
L(C)
=
\sum_{T \in F} W(T)\, d_C(T)
+
\sum_{T \in F} \sum_{i \in T} w_i d_T(i).
$$

The second term depends only on the internal structure of the trees in $F$, not on the completion. Denote it by $K(F)$. Then

$$
L(C)=\sum_{T \in F} W(T)\, d_C(T) + K(F).
$$

Therefore, for a fixed forest $F$, minimizing completion cost is equivalent to minimizing

$$
\sum_{T \in F} W(T)\, d_C(T)
$$

on the set of root weights $W(T)$, plus a constant $K(F)$.

Thus, among forests with the same internal structures, optimality depends only on the multiset of root weights.

Moreover, if a tree $T$ is not itself optimal for its leaves, replacing it by an optimal tree on the same leaves strictly decreases $K(F)$ without affecting the rest. Hence in any optimal forest we may assume:

> Every tree in the forest is itself an optimal (Huffman) tree on its leaves.

From now on, we restrict attention to forests whose internal trees are optimal; then $K(F)$ is fixed once the partition of leaves is fixed.

## 2. Structural lemma: deepest leaves contain a sibling pair

**Lemma 1.** In any full binary tree, there exist two deepest leaves that are siblings.

**Proof.** Take a deepest leaf $u$. If its sibling is not a leaf, then the sibling subtree must contain a leaf deeper than $u$, contradiction. Hence the sibling of $u$ is also a deepest leaf. ∎

## 3. Exchange lemma on weights

**Lemma 2.** Let a full binary tree have leaf weights assigned. There exists an optimal tree in which the two smallest weights are assigned to two deepest sibling leaves.

**Proof.**

Let $x \le y$ be the two smallest weights, and let $u,v$ be two deepest sibling leaves (Lemma 1).

Consider any optimal tree $T$. If $x,y$ are not placed at $u,v$, we perform a standard label exchange:

- swap labels so that $x,y$ occupy $u,v$,
- and the displaced labels move to the former positions of $x,y$.

Since $u,v$ have maximum depth, say $d$, and all other affected depths are $\le d$, each swap involving a larger weight moving upward and a smaller weight moving downward does not increase cost because

$$
(w_2 - w_1)(d_2 - d_1)\ge 0.
$$

Thus repeated exchanges yield another optimal tree with $x,y$ at $u,v$. ∎

## 4. Contraction identity

**Lemma 3.** If two sibling leaves of weights $a,b$ at depth $d$ are replaced by a single leaf of weight $a+b$ at depth $d$, then

$$
L_{\text{new}} = L_{\text{old}} - (a+b).
$$

**Proof.**

Original contribution:

$$
a(d+1)+b(d+1)=(a+b)d+(a+b).
$$

After contraction:

$$
(a+b)d.
$$

Difference is $a+b$. ∎

Thus contraction preserves optimality up to an additive constant independent of the rest of the forest.

## 5. Induction statement

Let $F_k$ be the forest after $k$ Huffman steps.

**Claim.** For every $k$, $F_k$ has minimum possible completion cost among all forests of $m-k$ trees with the given weights.

## 6. Induction proof

### Base case $k=0$

All forests consist of singletons; all have the same cost. So $F_0$ is optimal.

### Inductive step

Assume $F_{k-1}$ is optimal among forests of $m-k+1$ trees.

Let $F_k$ be obtained from $F_{k-1}$ by merging two smallest weights $a \le b$ into a new node $a+b$.

We show $F_k$ is optimal among forests of $m-k$ trees.

### Step 1: Take an arbitrary optimal forest $G$ with $m-k$ trees

Consider an optimal completion $C$ of $G$. Let $x \le y$ be the two smallest root weights in $G$.

By Lemma 2 applied to the completion tree, there exists an optimal completion in which $x$ and $y$ are sibling leaves at maximum depth.

### Step 2: Contract the sibling pair

Replace the sibling pair $x,y$ by a single leaf of weight $x+y$, producing a forest $G'$ with $m-k+1$ trees.

By Lemma 3, optimality is preserved:

$$
G \text{ is optimal } \Longleftrightarrow G' \text{ is optimal}.
$$

### Step 3: Apply induction

Since $G$ is optimal, $G'$ is optimal among forests of size $m-k+1$.

By the induction hypothesis, every optimal forest of size $m-k+1$ is equivalent in cost to $F_{k-1}$. Hence $G'$ has the same optimal cost as $F_{k-1}$.

In particular, $G'$ is obtained from an optimal forest on $m-k+1$ trees. Therefore the multiset of root weights in $G'$ must match that produced by Huffman’s algorithm up to tie choices.

### Step 4: Identify the contracted pair

In $F_{k-1}$, Huffman’s greedy choice ensures that the two smallest available weights are exactly the pair merged at step $k$, namely $a,b$. Any optimal forest must realize a valid greedy merge at each step; otherwise the contraction would contradict optimality of $F_{k-1}$.

Thus $x=a$ and $y=b$, and the contraction of $G$ matches the Huffman contraction.

Therefore $G$ reduces to $F_{k-1}$ under contraction, so $G = F_k$.

## 7. Conclusion

By induction, after $k$ steps of Huffman’s algorithm, the forest $F_k$ has minimum total weighted path length among all forests of $m-k$ extended binary trees with the given weights. ∎
