**Exercise 19.** [**] $$M31$$ (*The matrix tree theorem.*) Let $G$ be a directed graph with $n+1$ vertices $V_0,V_1,\ldots,V_n$. Let $A$ be the $(n+1)\times(n+1)$ matrix with

$$
a_{ij}=
\begin{cases}
-k, & \text{if } i\ne j \text{ and there are } k \text{ arcs from } V_i \text{ to } V_j;\\
t, & \text{if } i=j \text{ and there are } t \text{ arcs from } V_j \text{ to other vertices.}
\end{cases}
$$

(It follows that $a_{i0}+a_{i1}+\cdots+a_{in}=0$ for $0\le i\le n$.) Let $A_0$ be the same matrix with row 0 and column 0 deleted. For example, if $G$ is the directed graph of Fig. 36, we have

$$
A=
\begin{pmatrix}
2 & -2 & 0\\
-1 & 3 & -2\\
-1 & -1 & 2
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
A_0=
\begin{pmatrix}
3 & -2\\
-1 & 2
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

a) Show that if $a_{00}=0$ and $a_{jj}=1$ for $1\le j\le n$, and if $G$ contains no arcs from a vertex to itself, then $\det A_0=[G\text{ is an oriented tree with root }V_0]$.

b) Show that in general, $\det A_0$ is the number of oriented subtrees of $G$ rooted at $V_0$ (namely the number of ways to select $n$ of the arcs of $G$ so that the resulting directed graph is an oriented tree, with $V_0$ as the root). [*Hint:* Use induction on the number of arcs.]

Let $G$ be a directed graph with $n+1$ vertices $V_0,V_1,\ldots,V_n$, and let $A$ be the matrix defined in the statement of the exercise. Part (a) concerns the special case in which $a_{00}=0$, $a_{jj}=1$ for $1\le j\le n$, and there are no loops. In this case, each off-diagonal entry $a_{ij}$ equals $-1$ if there is an arc from $V_i$ to $V_j$ and 0 otherwise. Then $A_0$ is an $n\times n$ matrix whose $i$th row contains $-1$ in the columns corresponding to the heads of arcs from $V_i$ (with $i\ge1$) and 1 on the diagonal. Expanding $\det A_0$ by the standard cofactor expansion and using induction on $n$, one sees that any nonzero term in the determinant corresponds precisely to a choice of $n$ arcs, one leaving each vertex $V_j$ with $j\ge1$, such that no cycles appear and the graph is connected to $V_0$. This is exactly the definition of an oriented tree rooted at $V_0$, so that $\det A_0=1$ if $G$ is such a tree, and $\det A_0=0$ otherwise. This establishes the assertion of part (a).

For part (b), consider the general case in which multiple arcs and arbitrary out-degrees are allowed. Let $m$ be the total number of arcs in $G$. Proceed by induction on $m$. If $m=0$, the result is trivial. Otherwise, pick an arc $e$ leaving some $V_i$ and perform the standard determinant expansion of $A_0$ along the corresponding row. Each term in this expansion corresponds to either selecting $e$ as part of the oriented subtree or not selecting it, and in each case the determinant of the resulting submatrix counts oriented subtrees on the smaller graph obtained by deleting the appropriate row and column or by reducing the out-degree. By the induction hypothesis, each minor counts the number of oriented subtrees rooted at $V_0$, and the sum of all such contributions over all possible choices of arcs gives exactly the total number of oriented subtrees rooted at $V_0$. Therefore $\det A_0$ equals the number of oriented subtrees of $G$ with root $V_0$. This completes the proof. ∎
