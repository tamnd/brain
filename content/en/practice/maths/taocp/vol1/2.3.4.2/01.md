[Section 2.3.4.2: Oriented Trees](../)

**Exercise 1.** [**] $$M20$$ Prove that if $V$ and $V'$ are vertices of a directed graph and if there is an oriented walk from $V$ to $V'$, then there is a simple oriented path from $V$ to $V'$.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 6m04s  

---

Let

$$
P=(e_1,e_2,\ldots,e_n)
$$

be an oriented walk from $V$ to $V'$. Among all oriented walks from $V$ to $V'$, choose one of minimum length. Suppose that this walk is not simple. Then either two of the vertices

$$
\operatorname{init}(e_1),\ldots,\operatorname{init}(e_n),\operatorname{fin}(e_n)
$$

coincide, or two of the vertices

$$
\operatorname{fin}(e_1),\ldots,\operatorname{fin}(e_n)
$$

coincide. In either case there exist indices $i<j$ such that the same vertex occurs at the end of $e_i$ and at the end of $e_j$, or at the beginning of $e_{i+1}$ and $e_{j+1}$. The segment

$$
(e_{i+1},e_{i+2},\ldots,e_j)
$$

therefore forms an oriented cycle.

Deleting this cycle yields

$$
(e_1,\ldots,e_i,e_{j+1},\ldots,e_n),
$$

which is still an oriented walk from $V$ to $V'$ because the vertex where the deleted segment begins is the same as the vertex where it ends. Its length is smaller than $n$, contradicting the choice of $P$ as a shortest oriented walk from $V$ to $V'$.

Hence a shortest oriented walk from $V$ to $V'$ contains no repeated vertex. By the definition in Section 2.3.4.2, such a walk is a simple oriented path from $V$ to $V'$. This completes the proof.

∎
