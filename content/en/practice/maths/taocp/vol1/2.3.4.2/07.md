[Section 2.3.4.2: Oriented Trees](../)

**Exercise 7.** [**] $$M22$$ True or false: A directed graph satisfying properties (a) and (b) of the definition of oriented tree, and having no oriented cycles, is an oriented tree.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m40s  

---

The statement is **true**.

Assume a finite directed graph satisfies properties (a) and (b): each vertex $V \ne R$ is the initial vertex of exactly one arc $e[V]$, and $R$ is the initial vertex of no arc. Assume also that the graph contains no oriented cycles.

From any vertex $V \ne R$, repeatedly follow the unique outgoing arc:

$$
V, \operatorname{fin}(e[V]), \operatorname{fin}(e[\operatorname{fin}(e[V])]), \ldots
$$

This process is well-defined at every step because property (a) assigns exactly one outgoing arc to each vertex except $R$. Since the graph has finitely many vertices, this sequence cannot pass through infinitely many distinct vertices.

If some vertex were repeated in the sequence before reaching $R$, then the segment between two occurrences would form a nonempty closed directed walk in which every vertex has exactly one outgoing arc along the walk, hence would contain an oriented cycle. This contradicts the assumption that the graph has no oriented cycles. Therefore no vertex can repeat before termination.

The sequence must therefore terminate at a vertex with no outgoing arc. By property (b), the only such vertex is $R$. Hence from every $V \ne R$ there is an oriented path from $V$ to $R$.

Thus property (c) holds, and the graph satisfies all defining properties of an oriented tree with root $R$. This completes the proof. ∎
