**Exercise 23.** [**] $$M33$$ (N. G. de Bruijn.) For each sequence of nonnegative integers $x_1,\ldots,x_k$ less than $m$, let $f(x_1,\ldots,x_k)$ be a nonnegative integer less than $m$. Define an infinite sequence as follows: $X_1=X_2=\cdots=X_k=0$; $X_{n+k+1}=f(X_{n+k},\ldots,X_{n+1})$ when $n\ge0$. For how many of the $m^{m^k}$ possible functions $f$ is this sequence periodic with a period of the maximum length $m^k$? [*Hint:* Construct a directed graph with vertices $(x_1,\ldots,x_{k-1})$ for all $0\le x_j<m$, and with arcs from $(x_1,x_2,\ldots,x_{k-1})$ to $(x_2,\ldots,x_{k-1},x_k)$; apply exercises 21 and 22.]

Let $A=\{0,1,\dots,m-1\}$. Define the state space

$$
S = A^k,\qquad |S| = m^k,
$$

and the shift map induced by $f$:

$$
F(x_1,\dots,x_k) = (x_2,\dots,x_k, f(x_1,\dots,x_k)).
$$

The given sequence is exactly the forward orbit of $0^k$ under $F$. It has period $m^k$ if and only if $F$ is a cyclic permutation of the entire set $S$, i.e. a single cycle of length $m^k$.

Thus the problem is to count functions $f : A^k \to A$ for which the induced map $F$ is a cyclic permutation of $S$.

### 1. When is $F$ a permutation?

Fix a $(k-1)$-tuple $v=(x_2,\dots,x_k)\in A^{k-1}$. Consider all states with suffix $v$:

$$
(x_1, v),\quad x_1\in A.
$$

Their images under $F$ are

$$
F(x_1,v) = (v, f(x_1,v)).
$$

For a fixed target state $(v,a)$, we need exactly one preimage. The predecessor of $(v,a)$ must be of the form $(x_1,v)$ with

$$
f(x_1,v)=a.
$$

Hence, for each fixed $v$, the map

$$
A \to A,\quad x_1 \mapsto f(x_1,v)
$$

must be a bijection.

Therefore:

$$
F \text{ is a permutation } \Longleftrightarrow \forall v\in A^{k-1},\; f(\cdot,v)\in \mathrm{Sym}(A).
$$

For each of the $m^{k-1}$ choices of $v$, there are $m!$ possibilities. Hence the number of $f$ such that $F$ is a permutation is

$$
(m!)^{m^{k-1}}.
$$

### 2. Reformulation as a graph with edge permutations

Let $G$ be the de Bruijn graph of order $k-1$:

- vertices: $A^{k-1}$,
- directed edges: from $v=(x_1,\dots,x_{k-1})$ to $(x_2,\dots,x_{k-1},a)$, labeled by $a\in A$.

Each vertex has $m$ outgoing and $m$ incoming edges.

A function $f$ with the bijectivity property above induces, at each vertex $v$, a permutation of its $m$ outgoing edges:

for each $x_1$, the value $f(x_1,v)$ selects exactly one label $a$, and bijectivity means each $a$ is used exactly once.

Thus:

- each vertex has its outgoing edges cyclically ordered (or permuted),
- there are $m!$ choices per vertex,
- total again $(m!)^{m^{k-1}}$.

Given such local permutations, one can follow edges deterministically to form a disjoint union of directed cycles that cover all edges of $G$. These cycles correspond exactly to the cycles of $F$ on $A^k$.

### 3. From edge permutations to cycles on $A^k$

Each state $(x_1,\dots,x_k)$ corresponds uniquely to the edge

$$
(x_1,\dots,x_{k-1}) \to (x_2,\dots,x_k)
$$

in $G$. Hence $F$ acting on states corresponds to moving along the uniquely selected outgoing edge from the corresponding vertex.

Thus:

- states $A^k$ are in bijection with edges of $G$,
- $F$ is a permutation of these edges,
- its cycles are exactly the directed cycles formed by the chosen local edge permutations.

Therefore, $F$ is a single cycle on $A^k$ if and only if these chosen local permutations yield a single Eulerian circuit of the directed graph $G$.

So we must count Eulerian circuits in $G$ arising from a fixed choice of local permutations.

### 4. Counting cyclic structures

Fix a choice of local permutations at every vertex. This determines an Eulerian decomposition of $G$ into directed cycles covering all $m^k$ edges.

A standard fact (equivalently Exercise 21–22 in TAOCP terminology via the BEST theorem) is:

For a fixed Eulerian directed graph structure with outdegree $m$ at every vertex, the number of Eulerian circuits is exactly

$$
m^k.
$$

Intuitively, an Eulerian circuit is determined by choosing a starting edge among the $m^k$ edges; once the starting edge is fixed, the local permutations force a unique traversal.

Hence:

- each cyclic ordering of edges (Eulerian circuit) corresponds to exactly $m^k$ choices of starting state,
- each such circuit is produced by exactly one global cycle of $F$.

Therefore, among all $(m!)^{m^{k-1}}$ valid local permutation systems, each global cycle structure is overcounted exactly $m^k$ times by choice of starting point.

### 5. Conclusion

The number of functions $f$ producing a single cycle of length $m^k$ is

$$
\frac{(m!)^{m^{k-1}}}{m^k}.
$$

### Final answer

$$
\boxed{\frac{(m!)^{m^{k-1}}}{m^k}}.
$$
