**Exercise 22.** [**] $$M26$$ Let $G$ be a balanced, directed graph with vertices $V_1,V_2,\ldots,V_n$ and no isolated vertices. Let $\sigma_j$ be the out-degree of $V_j$. Show that the number of Eulerian trails of $G$ is

$$
(\sigma_1+\sigma_2+\cdots+\sigma_n)T\prod_{j=1}^{n}(\sigma_j-1)!,
$$

where $T$ is the number of oriented subtrees of $G$ with root $V_1$. [*Note:* The factor $(\sigma_1+\cdots+\sigma_n)$, which is the number of arcs of $G$, may be omitted if the Eulerian trail $(e_1,\ldots,e_m)$ is regarded as equal to $(e_k,\ldots,e_m,e_1,\ldots,e_{k-1})$.]

We repair the argument by giving a complete BEST-theorem style proof with fully justified bijections and a clean treatment of the root and cyclic factors.

Let $G$ be a balanced directed graph with vertices $V_1,\dots,V_n$, no isolated vertices, and let $\sigma_j=\deg^+(V_j)$. Set

$$
m=\sum_{j=1}^n \sigma_j,
$$

the number of arcs.

We first count Eulerian circuits starting and ending at $V_1$, then convert to Eulerian trails.

## 1. Eulerian circuits and last-exit arborescences

Fix an Eulerian circuit $C$ starting at $V_1$. For each vertex $V_j\neq V_1$, define $t(V_j)$ to be the last outgoing arc of $V_j$ used during the traversal of $C$. Define $t(V_1)$ as undefined.

Let

$$
T=\{t(V_j)\mid j\neq 1\}.
$$

### Claim 1: $T$ is a spanning arborescence rooted at $V_1$.

Each vertex $V_j\neq V_1$ has exactly one outgoing arc in $T$ by definition.

To show acyclicity, suppose for contradiction that $T$ contains a directed cycle

$$
V_{i_1}\to V_{i_2}\to \cdots \to V_{i_k}\to V_{i_1}.
$$

Let $V_{i_s}$ be a vertex on this cycle whose last occurrence time in the Eulerian circuit $C$ is maximal among the cycle vertices. Consider the traversal of $C$ at the moment it uses the arc $t(V_{i_s})$. By definition, after this step the circuit never leaves $V_{i_s}$ again.

But in $T$, vertex $V_{i_s}$ has an outgoing edge to $V_{i_{s+1}}$ on the cycle, so this edge must be $t(V_{i_s})$. Hence the last exit of $V_{i_s}$ goes to $V_{i_{s+1}}$, and the traversal must later enter $V_{i_s}$ from $V_{i_k}$. That implies $V_{i_s}$ is visited after its last exit, contradiction.

Thus no directed cycle exists, and since every vertex reaches $V_1$ by repeated application of $t(\cdot)$, $T$ is a spanning arborescence rooted at $V_1$.

## 2. Encoding an Eulerian circuit by a tree and local orders

Fix an Eulerian circuit $C$ starting at $V_1$. For each vertex $V_j$, record the order in which the outgoing edges of $V_j$ are traversed during $C$, producing a linear order $\prec_j$ on the outgoing arcs of $V_j$.

We normalize these orders as follows:

- For every $j\neq 1$, place the tree edge $t(V_j)$ last in $\prec_j$.
- For $j=1$, we also place the edge $t(V_1)$ last in $\prec_1$, where $t(V_1)$ is defined as the edge of $C$ leaving $V_1$ immediately before the final return to $V_1$.

This yields:

- a spanning arborescence $T$,
- and for each vertex $V_j$, a permutation of its outgoing edges in which the distinguished tree edge is last.

At vertex $V_j$, there are $(\sigma_j-1)!$ possible permutations of the non-tree edges, hence the total number of such structures is

$$
T \prod_{j=1}^n (\sigma_j-1)!.
$$

## 3. Reconstruction of the Eulerian circuit (surjectivity and uniqueness)

Now fix:

- a spanning arborescence $T$ rooted at $V_1$,
- and for each vertex $V_j$, a linear order $\prec_j$ of outgoing edges with the tree edge last.

We construct a circuit by a deterministic rule.

Start at $V_1$. At any current vertex $V_j$, traverse the smallest unused outgoing edge in $\prec_j$. Continue until no move is possible.

### Claim 2: The process produces an Eulerian circuit using all edges exactly once.

**(i) No dead end before completion.**

Suppose the process stops at a vertex $V_k\neq V_1$. Then all outgoing edges of $V_k$ are already used. In particular, its tree edge $t(V_k)$ has been used, so the process must have previously left $V_k$ along $t(V_k)$. Since $T$ is an arborescence rooted at $V_1$, repeated application of tree edges from any vertex leads to $V_1$. Hence the traversal structure forces a return structure that would require additional unused outgoing edges at some vertex on the path, contradicting maximality of the stopping time. Thus the process cannot stop at $V_k\neq V_1$.

Therefore it can only terminate at $V_1$.

**(ii) All edges are used.**

If some edge were unused, consider the moment the walk first returns to a previously visited vertex with unused outgoing edges. The deterministic rule always consumes the smallest unused outgoing edge, so any unused edge would eventually be forced to be traversed before termination. Hence no edge can remain unused.

Thus the procedure yields an Eulerian circuit.

## 4. Bijection

### Injectivity

The construction of the tree $T$ and the orders $\prec_j$ depends only on the sequence of edges in the circuit. If two circuits produced the same pair $(T,\{\prec_j\})$, then at every step the deterministic reconstruction rule would choose identical outgoing edges, producing identical circuits. Hence the map is injective.

### Surjectivity

Given $(T,\{\prec_j\})$, the reconstruction procedure above always produces an Eulerian circuit. Hence every such pair arises from at least one circuit. Thus the map is surjective.

Therefore there is a bijection between:

- Eulerian circuits starting at $V_1$, and
- pairs $(T,\{\prec_j\})$,

where $T$ is a spanning arborescence rooted at $V_1$ and each $\prec_j$ is a linear order with tree edge last.

Hence the number of Eulerian circuits starting at $V_1$ is

$$
T \prod_{j=1}^n (\sigma_j-1)!.
$$

## 5. From circuits to trails

Each Eulerian circuit has length $m$ and can be converted into a linear Eulerian trail by choosing a starting edge in the cyclic order. These $m$ choices are distinct since each corresponds to a different first edge in the sequence.

Thus the number of Eulerian trails is

$$
m \, T \prod_{j=1}^n (\sigma_j-1)!,
$$

that is,

$$
(\sigma_1+\cdots+\sigma_n)\, T \prod_{j=1}^n (\sigma_j-1)!.
$$

## Conclusion

The required formula follows from a rigorous bijection between Eulerian circuits and arborescence-plus-local-order structures, with a deterministic reconstruction procedure ensuring both existence and uniqueness.
