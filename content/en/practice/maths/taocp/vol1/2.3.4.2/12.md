[Section 2.3.4.2: Oriented Trees](../)

**Exercise 12.** [**] $$M10$$ In the correspondence between oriented trees as defined here and oriented trees as defined at the beginning of Section 2.3, is the *degree* of a tree node equal to the *in-degree* or the *out-degree* of the corresponding vertex?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m35s  

---

In the oriented tree defined in Section 2.3.4.2, each vertex $V \ne R$ is the initial vertex of exactly one arc $e[V]$ directed from $V$ to its parent, and $R$ is the initial vertex of no arc. Hence every non-root vertex has out-degree $1$, while the root has out-degree $0$. The in-degree of a vertex $V$ is the number of arcs whose final vertex is $V$, which is exactly the number of children of $V$ in the underlying rooted tree structure.

In the correspondence with the oriented trees defined at the beginning of Section 2.3, the degree of a node is the number of its children. Since this number coincides with the number of incoming arcs in the oriented representation, the degree of a tree node equals the in-degree of the corresponding vertex. $\boxed{\text{in-degree}}$
