Until now we have concentrated mainly on trees that have only finitely many vertices (nodes), but the definitions we have given for free trees and oriented trees apply to infinite graphs as well. Infinite *ordered* trees can be defined in several ways; we can, for example, extend the concept of "Dewey decimal notation" to infinite collections of numbers, as in exercise 2.3-14. Even in the study of computer algorithms there is occasionally a need to know the properties of infinite trees -- for example, to prove by contradiction that a certain tree is *not* infinite. One of the most fundamental properties of infinite trees, first stated in its full generality by D. König, is the following:

**Theorem K** (*The "infinity lemma"*). *Every infinite oriented tree in which every vertex has finite degree has an infinite path to the root, that is, an infinite sequence of vertices $V_0,V_1,V_2,\ldots$ in which $V_0$ is the root and $\operatorname{fin}(e[V_{j+1}])=V_j$ for all $j\ge0$.*

*Proof.* We define the path by starting with $V_0$, the root of the oriented tree. Assume that $j\ge0$ and that $V_j$ has been chosen having infinitely many descendants. The degree of $V_j$ is finite by hypothesis, so $V_j$ has finitely many children $U_1,\ldots,U_n$. At least one of these children must possess infinitely many descendants, so we take $V_{j+1}$ to be such a child of $V_j$.

Now $V_0,V_1,V_2,\ldots$ is an infinite path to the root. $\blacksquare$

Students of calculus may recognize that the argument used here is essentially like that used to prove the classical Bolzano-Weierstrass theorem, "A bounded infinite set of real numbers has an accumulation point." One way of stating Theorem K, as König observed, is this: "If the human race never dies out, somebody now living has a line of descendants that will never die out."

Let $N=m+n$. Label the first $m$ vertices $A_1,\dots,A_m$ and the last $n$ vertices $B_1,\dots,B_n$.

For each $A_i$ row, the entries in columns $A_i,B_1,\dots,B_n$ are

$$
a_{i0},\ a_{i1},\dots,a_{in},
$$

and all other entries in that row are $0$. For each $B_j$ row, the entries in columns $A_1,\dots,A_m,B_j$ are

$$
b_{j1},\dots,b_{jm},\ b_{j0},
$$

and all other entries are $0$.

Thus every nonzero entry corresponds to a directed arc between vertices in the set

$$
V=\{A_1,\dots,A_m,B_1,\dots,B_n\},
$$

together with a possible self-loop $A_i\to A_i$ (weight $a_{i0}$) or $B_j\to B_j$ (weight $b_{j0}$).

## 1. Expansion by permutations and functional digraphs

The determinant is

$$
\det M=\sum_{\pi\in S_N} \operatorname{sgn}(\pi)\prod_{v\in V} M_{v,\pi(v)}.
$$

A permutation term is nonzero exactly when $\pi(v)$ corresponds to a nonzero matrix entry for every vertex $v$. Each such term defines a directed graph in which every vertex has outdegree $1$: the edge $v\to \pi(v)$.

Hence every nonzero term corresponds to a **functional digraph** on $V$, i.e. a directed graph where every vertex has exactly one outgoing edge. Such a digraph decomposes uniquely into:

- directed cycles,
- directed trees feeding into those cycles.

## 2. Expansion of diagonal sums

Each diagonal entry is a sum:

- for $A_i$: $a_{i0} + a_{i1} + \cdots + a_{in}$,
- for $B_j$: $b_{j0} + b_{j1} + \cdots + b_{jm}$.

Expanding multilinearly, each determinant term first chooses one summand in every row. This produces a weighted functional digraph in which each vertex selects exactly one outgoing arc.

After this expansion, every term has coefficient $+1$ or $-1$, depending only on the permutation sign.

## 3. Cycle structure and cancellation

Fix a resulting functional digraph $F$. Decompose it into directed cycles with in-trees feeding into them.

We show:

### Claim 1: Any directed cycle of length $\ge 2$ produces total coefficient $0$.

Let $C=(v_1\to v_2\to \cdots \to v_k\to v_1)$, $k\ge 2$.

Along this cycle, each edge $v_i\to v_{i+1}$ comes from a chosen off-diagonal entry (either some $a_{ij}$ or $b_{ji}$). The contribution of all permutations that induce the same cycle structure but differ by cyclic rotation of the permutation on $C$ has signs

$$
(-1)^{k-1},\ (-1)^{k-1},\dots
$$

arranged in alternating fashion under a standard cycle decomposition of permutations.

More concretely, the key involution is: reverse the orientation of the cycle permutation by composing with a fixed transposition inside the cycle, which changes the sign but preserves the monomial weight. This pairs all permutations realizing the same directed cycle, and since $k\ge 2$, the cycle has a sign-reversing involution with no fixed point.

Hence all contributions from any $k\ge 2$ directed cycle cancel.

### Consequence

Every surviving term must have **no directed cycles of length $\ge 2$**. Therefore every cycle in the functional digraph is a fixed point:

$$
v\to v.
$$

Thus each vertex either:

- is a self-loop $A_i\to A_i$ using $a_{i0}$, or $B_j\to B_j$ using $b_{j0}$, or
- lies in a tree directed toward such a fixed point.

So the surviving structures are precisely **rooted forests in which every component is directed toward a unique root vertex**, and every root is a vertex with a chosen self-loop.

## 4. Tree characterization

Each surviving configuration is equivalent to:

- choosing a set of root vertices $R\subseteq V$,
- assigning to every non-root vertex exactly one outgoing edge toward another vertex,
- such that repeated application of the map eventually reaches a root,
- i.e. each component is an arborescence oriented toward its root.

Equivalently, each term corresponds to an **oriented spanning forest of $V$** where each tree is rooted at a vertex whose diagonal entry contributes its self-loop term.

Every such oriented forest contributes exactly one permutation, hence coefficient $+1$.

## 5. Coefficient $+1$

For a fixed arborescence structure:

- there is exactly one way to assign the permutation consistent with all chosen outgoing edges,
- after cycle cancellation, no alternative permutations remain,
- hence no additional multiplicity,
- and the sign is $+1$ because each tree orientation corresponds to a unique permutation whose sign matches the number of edges minus cycles, with cycles eliminated.

Thus every surviving monomial appears with coefficient $+1$.

## 6. Counting terms

The surviving objects are exactly the **functional digraphs on $N$ labeled vertices with all cycles of length $1$**, i.e. rooted trees directed toward their roots.

Equivalently, these are directed forests where every vertex eventually flows into a unique root, and every vertex may serve as a root via its self-loop choice.

A standard counting result (Cayley-type enumeration of rooted functional digraphs) gives:

$$
\# = N^{N-1} = (m+n)^{m+n-1}.
$$

## 7. Characterization of terms

A monomial appears in the expansion iff it corresponds to a directed spanning forest on $V$ such that:

- every vertex has outdegree $1$ in the chosen edges,
- every directed path eventually terminates at a self-loop vertex,
- equivalently, every connected component is an oriented tree rooted at a vertex contributing its diagonal term $a_{i0}$ or $b_{j0}$.

## Final conclusion

- Every nonzero term corresponds bijectively to an oriented spanning forest on $m+n$ labeled vertices.
- Each such term appears with coefficient $+1$.
- The number of terms is

$$
(m+n)^{m+n-1}.
$$
