[Section 2.3.4.2: Oriented Trees](../)

**Exercise 5.** [**] $$M16$$ Let $G$ be a directed graph that contains an oriented walk $(e_1,\ldots,e_n)$ with $\operatorname{fin}(e_n)=\operatorname{init}(e_1)$. Give a proof that $G$ is not an oriented tree, using the terminology defined in this section.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 4m50s  

---

Let $v_0=\operatorname{init}(e_1)$ and for $1\le k\le n$ let $v_k=\operatorname{fin}(e_k)$, so $v_k=\operatorname{init}(e_{k+1})$ for $1\le k<n$ and $v_n=v_0$. Since the vertex sequence is finite and $v_n=v_0$, there exist indices $0\le i<j\le n$ such that $v_i=v_j$ and $j-i$ is minimal with this property. Then $(e_{i+1},e_{i+2},\ldots,e_j)$ is an oriented walk from $v_i$ to $v_j=v_i$ in which all intermediate initial vertices are distinct and all intermediate final vertices are distinct; otherwise a shorter repetition would occur, contradicting the minimality of $j-i$. Hence this subwalk is an oriented cycle.

An oriented tree admits no oriented cycles by the consequence of property (c) in Section 2.3.4.2. Since $G$ contains an oriented cycle, it cannot satisfy the defining properties of an oriented tree for any choice of root $R$. This completes the proof. ∎
