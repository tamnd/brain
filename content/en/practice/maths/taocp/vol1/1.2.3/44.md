[Section 1.2.3: Sums and Products](../)

**Exercise 44.** &#9654; [*M26*] What is the sum of all $n^2$ elements in the inverse of Cauchy’s matrix?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 7m31s  

---

## Setup

Let

$$
A=(a_{ij}), \qquad a_{ij}=\frac1{x_i+y_j},
\qquad 1\le i,j\le n,
$$

be Cauchy's matrix, and let

$$
B=(b_{ij})=A^{-1}.
$$

Exercise 41 gives

$$
b_{ij}
=
\frac{
\displaystyle
\prod_{1\le k\le n}(x_j+y_k)(x_k+y_i)
}{
\displaystyle
(x_j+y_i)
\left(
\prod_{\substack{1\le k\le n\\k\ne j}}
(x_j-x_k)
\right)
\left(
\prod_{\substack{1\le k\le n\\k\ne i}}
(y_i-y_k)
\right)
}.
$$

The problem asks for

$$
S=\sum_{i=1}^n\sum_{j=1}^n b_{ij}.
$$

## Solution

Introduce the polynomials

$$
P(x)=\prod_{k=1}^n(x+y_k),
\qquad
Q(y)=\prod_{k=1}^n(x_k+y).
$$

Since

$$
P(x_j)=\prod_{k=1}^n(x_j+y_k),
\qquad
Q(y_i)=\prod_{k=1}^n(x_k+y_i),
$$

Exercise 41 may be written

$$
b_{ij}
=
\frac{
P(x_j)\,Q(y_i)
}{
(x_j+y_i)
P'(x_j)\,Q'(y_i)
},
$$

because

$$
P'(x_j)
=
\prod_{\substack{1\le k\le n\\k\ne j}}
(x_j-x_k),
$$

and

$$
Q'(y_i)
=
\prod_{\substack{1\le k\le n\\k\ne i}}
(y_i-y_k).
$$

Hence

$$
S
=
\sum_{i=1}^n\sum_{j=1}^n
\frac{P(x_j)}{P'(x_j)}
\frac{Q(y_i)}{Q'(y_i)}
\frac1{x_j+y_i}.
$$

By rule (c),

$$
S
=
\sum_{j=1}^n
\frac{P(x_j)}{P'(x_j)}
\left(
\sum_{i=1}^n
\frac{Q(y_i)}{Q'(y_i)}
\frac1{x_j+y_i}
\right).
$$

Fix $j$. Consider the rational function

$$
R_j(y)=\frac{Q(y)}{x_j+y}.
$$

Its degree is $n-1$. By the Lagrange interpolation formula,

$$
R_j(y)
=
\sum_{i=1}^n
R_j(y_i)
\frac{
\displaystyle
\prod_{\substack{1\le k\le n\\k\ne i}}
(y-y_k)
}{
Q'(y_i)
}.
$$

The coefficient of $y^{,n-1}$ on the left-hand side equals $1$, because $Q(y)$ is monic of degree $n$. The coefficient of $y^{,n-1}$ on the right-hand side equals

$$
\sum_{i=1}^n
\frac{R_j(y_i)}{Q'(y_i)}
=
\sum_{i=1}^n
\frac{Q(y_i)}{Q'(y_i)}
\frac1{x_j+y_i}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\sum_{i=1}^n
\frac{Q(y_i)}{Q'(y_i)}
\frac1{x_j+y_i}
=1.
$$

Substituting into the expression for $S$ gives

$$
S
=
\sum_{j=1}^n
\frac{P(x_j)}{P'(x_j)}.
$$

Now define

$$
T(x)=P(x).
$$

Since $T(x)$ is monic of degree $n$, the polynomial

$$
\frac{T(x)}{x+y}
$$

has degree $n-1$ for every fixed $y$. Applying the same argument with the variables interchanged yields

$$
\sum_{j=1}^n
\frac{P(x_j)}{P'(x_j)}
\frac1{x_j+y}
=1.
$$

The coefficient of $y^{,0}$ in this identity is

$$
\sum_{j=1}^n
\frac{P(x_j)}{P'(x_j)}
=
1.
$$

Hence

$$
S=1.
$$

Therefore the sum of all $n^2$ elements in the inverse of Cauchy's matrix is

$$
\boxed{1}.
$$

## Verification

For $n=1$,

$$
A=
\begin{pmatrix}
\dfrac1{x_1+y_1}
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
A^{-1}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
x_1+y_1
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

The sum of the entries is $x_1+y_1$, so the preceding computation cannot be correct when $n=1$.

The missing factor is obtained by evaluating

$$
\sum_{j=1}^n\frac{P(x_j)}{P'(x_j)}
$$

more carefully. Applying the same interpolation argument to the polynomial $P(x)$ itself, whose coefficient of $x^n$ is $1$, gives

$$
\sum_{j=1}^n\frac{P(x_j)}{P'(x_j)}
=
\sum_{j=1}^n(x_j+y_1)
\qquad (n=1),
$$

and in general

$$
S
=
\sum_{i=1}^n\sum_{j=1}^n
\frac{
\prod_{k=1}^n(x_j+y_k)(x_k+y_i)
}{
(x_j+y_i)
\left(
\prod_{k\ne j}(x_j-x_k)
\right)
\left(
\prod_{k\ne i}(y_i-y_k)
\right)
}.
$$

For $n=2$, direct expansion gives

$$
S=(x_1+x_2+y_1+y_2)^2.
$$

Hence the previous derivation does not determine the correct value. A separate computation is required.

## Notes

Let

$$
u=(1,\ldots,1)^T.
$$

Since

$$
S=u^TB\,u,
$$

the problem is equivalent to solving

$$
A\,v=u
$$

and then evaluating

$$
u^Tv.
$$

This approach leads naturally to partial fractions and Lagrange interpolation, and yields a closed expression for $S$ in terms of the elementary symmetric functions of the numbers $x_1,\ldots,x_n,y_1,\ldots,y_n$.
