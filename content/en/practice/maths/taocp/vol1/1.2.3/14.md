[Section 1.2.3: Sums and Products](../)

**Exercise 14.** [*11*] Using the result of the previous exercise, evaluate $\sum_{j=m}^n \sum_{k=r}^s jk$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m11s  

---

## Solution

Let

$S=\sum_{j=m}^n \sum_{k=r}^s jk.$

For fixed $j$, the factor $j$ does not depend on $k$, hence

$\sum_{k=r}^s jk = j \sum_{k=r}^s k.$

Substituting into $S$ gives

$S=\sum_{j=m}^n \left(j \sum_{k=r}^s k\right).$

The factor $\sum_{k=r}^s k$ does not depend on $j$, so by rule (a) (distributive law for products of sums),

$S=\left(\sum_{k=r}^s k\right)\left(\sum_{j=m}^n j\right).$

By Exercise 1.2.3.13, using Eq. (15),

$\sum_{j=m}^n j = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}-\frac{(m-1)m}{2},$

and similarly,

$\sum_{k=r}^s k = \frac{s(s+1)}{2}-\frac{(r-1)r}{2}.$

Substituting these expressions yields

$S=\left(\frac{n(n+1)}{2}-\frac{(m-1)m}{2}\right)\left(\frac{s(s+1)}{2}-\frac{(r-1)r}{2}\right).$

Therefore,

$\boxed{S=\frac{(n(n+1)-(m-1)m)(s(s+1)-(r-1)r)}{4}}.$
