[Section 1.2.3: Sums and Products](../)

**Exercise 16.** [*M22*] Prove that

$$\sum_{j=0}^n jx^j = \frac{nx^{n+2} - (n+1)x^{n+1} + x}{(x-1)^2},$$

if $x \ne 1$, without using mathematical induction.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m20s  

---

## Solution

Let

$$
S=\sum_{j=0}^n jx^j.
$$

Multiply both sides by $x$:

$$
xS=\sum_{j=0}^n jx^{j+1}.
$$

Change index by writing the sum in powers of $x^j$:

$$
xS=\sum_{j=1}^{n+1} (j-1)x^j.
$$

The term corresponding to $j=0$ is absent since it would be $(-1)x^0$ and does not occur in the shifted form; the term $j=n+1$ contributes $n x^{n+1}$.

Now write both sums with aligned ranges:

$$
S=\sum_{j=1}^n jx^j,
\qquad
xS=\sum_{j=1}^n (j-1)x^j + n x^{n+1}.
$$

Subtracting gives

$$
xS-S=\sum_{j=1}^n (j-1-j)x^j + n x^{n+1}.
$$

Each coefficient simplifies to $(j-1-j)=-1$, hence

$$
xS-S=-\sum_{j=1}^n x^j + n x^{n+1}.
$$

Factor the left-hand side:

$$
(x-1)S=n x^{n+1}-\sum_{j=1}^n x^j.
$$

The remaining sum is a geometric progression:

$$
\sum_{j=1}^n x^j = x\sum_{j=0}^{n-1} x^j = x\frac{1-x^n}{1-x}=\frac{x(x^n-1)}{x-1}.
$$

Substituting into the previous equation yields

$$
(x-1)S = n x^{n+1} - \frac{x(x^n-1)}{x-1}.
$$

Dividing by $x-1$ gives

$$
S=\frac{n x^{n+1}}{x-1}-\frac{x(x^n-1)}{(x-1)^2}.
$$

Putting both terms over the common denominator $(x-1)^2$ produces

$$
S=\frac{n x^{n+1}(x-1)-x(x^n-1)}{(x-1)^2}.
$$

Expanding the numerator,

$$
n x^{n+1}(x-1)-x(x^n-1)=n x^{n+2}-n x^{n+1}-x^{n+1}+x.
$$

Combining like terms yields

$$
S=\frac{n x^{n+2}-(n+1)x^{n+1}+x}{(x-1)^2}.
$$

Hence,

$$
\boxed{\sum_{j=0}^n jx^j = \frac{n x^{n+2}-(n+1)x^{n+1}+x}{(x-1)^2}}, \quad x\ne 1.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
