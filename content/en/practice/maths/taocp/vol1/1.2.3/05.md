**Exercise 5.** &#9654; [*HM20*] Prove that rule (a) is valid for arbitrary infinite series, provided that the series converge.

## Corrected Solution

Let

$$
A_n=\sum_{i=1}^n a_i,\qquad B_m=\sum_{j=1}^m b_j,
$$

and assume both series $\sum_i a_i$ and $\sum_j b_j$ converge, so

$$
\lim_{n\to\infty} A_n = A,\qquad \lim_{m\to\infty} B_m = B.
$$

### Step 1: Work only with finite sums

For each $n,m$, consider the finite double sum

$$
S_{n,m}=\sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{j=1}^m a_i b_j.
$$

Since all sums are finite, distributivity is valid without any convergence issues:

$$
S_{n,m}
= \left(\sum_{i=1}^n a_i\right)\left(\sum_{j=1}^m b_j\right)
= A_n B_m.
$$

This identity is purely algebraic and requires no limit arguments.

### Step 2: Passage to the limit of rectangular partial sums

Because $A_n \to A$ and $B_m \to B$, we have

$$
A_n B_m \to AB \quad \text{as } n,m \to \infty.
$$

Indeed, given $\varepsilon>0$, choose $N$ such that $|A_n-A|<\varepsilon$ for $n\ge N$, and $M$ such that $|B_m-B|<\varepsilon$ for $m\ge M$. Then for $n\ge N, m\ge M$,

$$
|A_n B_m - AB|
\le |A_n||B_m-B| + |B||A_n-A|,
$$

which tends to $0$ since $A_n$ is bounded and $B_m \to B$. Hence $S_{n,m}\to AB$.

We therefore define the double series by rectangular partial sums:

$$
\sum_i \sum_j a_i b_j := \lim_{n,m\to\infty} S_{n,m}.
$$

This limit exists and equals $AB$.

### Step 3: Iterated sums

Fix $n$. Then

$$
\sum_{j=1}^m a_i b_j = a_i B_m,
$$

so

$$
\sum_{j=1}^m \sum_{i=1}^n a_i b_j
= A_n B_m.
$$

Taking $m \to \infty$,

$$
\lim_{m\to\infty} \sum_{j=1}^m \sum_{i=1}^n a_i b_j
= A_n B.
$$

Now take $n \to \infty$:

$$
\lim_{n\to\infty} A_n B = AB.
$$

Thus the iterated sum also equals $AB$. The same argument works with the order of summation reversed.

### Conclusion

The double series defined by rectangular partial sums satisfies

$$
\sum_i \sum_j a_i b_j = AB = \left(\sum_i a_i\right)\left(\sum_j b_j\right),
$$

and both iterated sums converge to the same value.

Hence rule (a) is valid for arbitrary convergent infinite series. ∎
