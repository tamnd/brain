**Exercise 7.** [*HM23*] Given that $c$ is an integer, show that $\sum_{R(j)} a_j = \sum_{R(c-j)} a_{c-j}$, even if both series are infinite.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m17s  

---

## Solution

Define the transformation $p(j)=c-j$. For each integer $j$, there is a unique integer $k=c-j$, and conversely $j=c-k$, so $p$ is a permutation of the integers.

By rule (b), equation (5), valid for transformations of the form $c-j$, one may replace the index variable $j$ by $p(j)$ without changing the value of a sum whenever the correspondence between indices is bijective.

Starting from the left-hand side,

$\sum_{R(j)} a_j,$

apply the substitution $j=c-k$. Then $a_j=a_{c-k}$, and the condition $R(j)$ becomes $R(c-k)$. The range of summation is preserved because the mapping $j \leftrightarrow k=c-j$ is one-to-one on the integers. Hence

$\sum_{R(j)} a_j = \sum_{R(c-k)} a_{c-k}.$

Renaming the dummy index $k$ as $j$ yields

$\sum_{R(j)} a_j = \sum_{R(c-j)} a_{c-j}.$

The transformation is of the form permitted in rule (5), so the equality remains valid even when the sums are infinite.

This completes the proof. ∎
