[Section 1.2.3: Sums and Products](../)

**Exercise 27.** [*M20*] Generalize the result of exercise 1.2.1–9 by proving that

$$\prod_{j=1}^n (1 - a_j) \ge 1 - \sum_{j=1}^n a_j,$$

assuming that $0 < a_j < 1$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m04s  

---

## Solution

Let $P_n = \prod_{j=1}^n (1 - a_j)$ and $S_n = \sum_{j=1}^n a_j$, where $0 < a_j < 1$ for each $j$.

For $n = 1$, the identity reduces to

$P_1 = 1 - a_1 = 1 - S_1,$

so the claimed inequality holds with equality.

Assume for some $n \ge 2$ that

$P_{n-1} \ge 1 - S_{n-1}.$

Then

$P_n = \prod_{j=1}^{n} (1 - a_j) = (1 - a_n)\prod_{j=1}^{n-1} (1 - a_j) = (1 - a_n)P_{n-1}.$

Using the inductive hypothesis,

$P_n \ge (1 - a_n)(1 - S_{n-1}).$

Expanding the right-hand side,

$$
(1 - a_n)(1 - S_{n-1}) = 1 - S_{n-1} - a_n + a_n S_{n-1}.
$$

Rewriting the linear terms in $a_j$,

$$
1 - S_{n-1} - a_n = 1 - S_n,
$$

so

$$
(1 - a_n)(1 - S_{n-1}) = 1 - S_n + a_n S_{n-1}.
$$

Since $a_n > 0$ and $S_{n-1} > 0$, the product $a_n S_{n-1}$ is nonnegative, hence

$$
1 - S_n + a_n S_{n-1} \ge 1 - S_n.
$$

Therefore,

$P_n \ge 1 - S_n.$

By induction on $n$, the inequality holds for all $n \ge 1$:

$\prod_{j=1}^n (1 - a_j) \ge 1 - \sum_{j=1}^n a_j.$

This completes the proof. ∎
