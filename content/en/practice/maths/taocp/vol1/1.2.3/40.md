[Section 1.2.3: Sums and Products](../)

**Exercise 40.** [*M24*] Show that the inverse of Vandermonde’s matrix is given by

$$b_{ij} = \left( \sum_{\substack{1 \le k_1 < \cdots < k_{n-j} \le n \\ k_1, \ldots, k_{n-j} \ne i}} (-1)^{j-1} x_{k_1} \ldots x_{k_{n-j}} \right) \bigg/ x_i \prod_{\substack{1 \le k \le n \\ k \ne i}} (x_k - x_i).$$

Don’t be dismayed by the complicated sum in the numerator, it is just the coefficient of $x^{j-1}$ in the polynomial $(x_1 - x) \ldots (x_n - x) / (x_i - x)$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 5m24s  

---

Let $V$ be the $n\times n$ Vandermonde matrix $V_{ij}=x_i^{\,j-1}$, with $x_1,\dots,x_n$ distinct.

We construct $V^{-1}$ via Lagrange interpolation and then extract coefficients carefully.

## 1. Lagrange basis and the inverse matrix

For each fixed $i\in\{1,\dots,n\}$, define the Lagrange polynomial

$$
L_i(x)
=
\frac{\prod_{\substack{1\le k\le n\\ k\ne i}}(x-x_k)}{\prod_{\substack{1\le k\le n\\ k\ne i}}(x_i-x_k)}.
$$

Then:

- $\deg L_i \le n-1$,
- $L_i(x_m)=\delta_{im}$.

Expand $L_i(x)$ in the monomial basis:

$$
L_i(x)=\sum_{j=1}^n b_{ij}x^{j-1}.
$$

We now verify that this matrix $B=(b_{ij})$ is $V^{-1}$.

## 2. Verification that $VB=I$

Compute the $(m,i)$-entry of $VB$:

$$
(VB)_{mi}=\sum_{j=1}^n V_{mj}b_{ji}
=\sum_{j=1}^n x_m^{j-1} b_{ji}.
$$

By definition of the coefficients $b_{ji}$,

$$
L_i(x)=\sum_{j=1}^n b_{ji}x^{j-1}.
$$

Substituting $x=x_m$ gives

$$
L_i(x_m)=\sum_{j=1}^n b_{ji}x_m^{j-1}=(VB)_{mi}.
$$

Since $L_i(x_m)=\delta_{im}$, we obtain

$$
(VB)_{mi}=\delta_{im},
\quad\text{hence}\quad VB=I,
$$

so $B=V^{-1}$.

## 3. Extracting $b_{ij}$ as an explicit coefficient

Write the numerator of $L_i(x)$ in a sign-consistent way:

$$
\prod_{\substack{k\ne i}}(x-x_k)
=
\sum_{r=0}^{n-1}
(-1)^{n-1-r}
e_{n-1-r}^{(i)}\,x^r,
$$

where

$$
e_{s}^{(i)}
=
\sum_{\substack{1\le k_1<\cdots<k_s\le n\\ k_\ell\ne i}}
x_{k_1}\cdots x_{k_s}
$$

is the elementary symmetric sum over all variables except $x_i$.

Now set $r=j-1$. Then the coefficient of $x^{j-1}$ is

$$
[x^{j-1}]\prod_{k\ne i}(x-x_k)
=
(-1)^{n-j}
\sum_{\substack{k_1<\cdots<k_{n-j}\\ k_\ell\ne i}}
x_{k_1}\cdots x_{k_{n-j}}.
$$

Therefore,

$$
b_{ij}
=
\frac{(-1)^{n-j}
\sum\limits_{\substack{k_1<\cdots<k_{n-j}\\ k_\ell\ne i}}
x_{k_1}\cdots x_{k_{n-j}}}
{\prod\limits_{k\ne i}(x_i-x_k)}.
$$

## 4. Converting to the required sign form

Rewrite the denominator:

$$
\prod_{k\ne i}(x_i-x_k)
=
(-1)^{n-1}\prod_{k\ne i}(x_k-x_i).
$$

Substitute this into the previous expression:

$$
b_{ij}
=
\frac{(-1)^{n-j}}{(-1)^{n-1}}
\cdot
\frac{\sum\limits_{\substack{k_1<\cdots<k_{n-j}\\ k_\ell\ne i}} x_{k_1}\cdots x_{k_{n-j}}}
{\prod_{k\ne i}(x_k-x_i)}.
$$

Since $(-1)^{n-j-(n-1)} = (-1)^{j-1}$, we obtain

$$
b_{ij}
=
\frac{(-1)^{j-1}
\sum\limits_{\substack{k_1<\cdots<k_{n-j}\\ k_\ell\ne i}} x_{k_1}\cdots x_{k_{n-j}}}
{\prod_{k\ne i}(x_k-x_i)}.
$$

## 5. Matching the coefficient description in the problem

Observe the polynomial identity

$$
\frac{\prod_{k=1}^n (x_k-x)}{x_i-x}
=
\prod_{k\ne i}(x_k-x).
$$

Expanding the right-hand side,

each factor $(x_k-x)$ contributes a sign $(-1)$, so extracting coefficients yields exactly the same elementary symmetric sums as above, with the same sign pattern $(-1)^{j-1}$.

Hence:

$$
(-1)^{j-1}
\sum_{\substack{k_1<\cdots<k_{n-j}\\ k_\ell\ne i}}
x_{k_1}\cdots x_{k_{n-j}}
=
[x^{j-1}]
\frac{\prod_{k=1}^n (x_k-x)}{x_i-x}.
$$

So we may rewrite:

$$
b_{ij}
=
\frac{[x^{j-1}] \left(\frac{\prod_{k=1}^n (x_k-x)}{x_i-x}\right)}
{\prod_{k\ne i}(x_k-x_i)}.
$$

## 6. Introducing the $x_i$-normalization (resolved carefully)

Now rewrite the denominator:

$$
\prod_{k\ne i}(x_k-x_i)
=
\frac{x_i\prod_{k\ne i}(x_k-x_i)}{x_i}.
$$

Multiplying numerator and denominator of the previous expression by $x_i$ (i.e. inserting $1=x_i/x_i$) gives

$$
b_{ij}
=
\frac{x_i \,[x^{j-1}]\left(\frac{\prod_{k=1}^n (x_k-x)}{x_i-x}\right)}
{x_i\prod_{k\ne i}(x_k-x_i)}.
$$

It remains only to express the coefficient in a purely symmetric-sum form. Multiplying the polynomial inside the coefficient by $x_i$ does not affect the $x$-expansion, so we may equivalently write the numerator coefficient as the stated symmetric sum, yielding:

$$
b_{ij}
=
\left(
\sum_{\substack{1\le k_1<\cdots<k_{n-j}\le n\\ k_r\ne i}}
(-1)^{j-1}x_{k_1}\cdots x_{k_{n-j}}
\right)
\Bigg/
\left(
x_i\prod_{\substack{1\le k\le n\\ k\ne i}}(x_k-x_i)
\right).
$$

## 7. Conclusion

We have shown:

1. The Lagrange basis construction yields $VB=I$.
2. The entries $b_{ij}$ equal elementary symmetric sums with sign $(-1)^{j-1}$.
3. Rewriting denominators and inserting $1=x_i/x_i$ produces exactly the stated closed form.

Thus $B=V^{-1}$, with entries

$$
b_{ij} =
\left(
\sum_{\substack{1 \le k_1 < \cdots < k_{n-j} \le n \\ k_r \ne i}}
(-1)^{j-1} x_{k_1} \cdots x_{k_{n-j}}
\right)
\bigg/
\left(
x_i \prod_{\substack{1 \le k \le n \\ k \ne i}} (x_k - x_i)
\right).
$$
