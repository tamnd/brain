**Exercise 18.** [*M20*] Show how to interchange the order of summation as in Eq. (9) given that $R(i)$ is the relation “$n$ is a multiple of $i$” and $S(i, j)$ is the relation “$1 \le j < i$.”

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m13s  

---

## Solution

Let $R(i)$ be the relation “$i$ is a positive integer such that $n$ is a multiple of $i$,” equivalently $i \mid n$, and let $S(i,j)$ be the relation $1 \le j < i$.

The given sum is

$\sum_{R(i)} \sum_{S(i,j)} a_{ij} = \sum_{i \mid n} \sum_{1 \le j < i} a_{ij}.$

To interchange the order, Eq. (9) requires determining $S'(j)$ and $R'(i,j)$.

The relation $R'(i,j)$ is defined as the conjunction of $R(i)$ and $S(i,j)$, hence

$R'(i,j):\ i \mid n \ \text{and}\ 1 \le j < i.$

The relation $S'(j)$ asserts the existence of an integer $i$ such that both $i \mid n$ and $1 \le j < i$ hold. Thus $S'(j)$ holds exactly when there exists a divisor $i$ of $n$ satisfying $j < i$. Since $n \mid n$, the choice $i = n$ is always available, and the inequality $j < n$ is necessary and sufficient for such an $i$ to exist. Hence

$S'(j):\ 1 \le j \le n-1.$

Substituting these relations into Eq. (9) yields

$\sum_{i \mid n} \sum_{1 \le j < i} a_{ij} = \sum_{j=1}^{n-1} \sum_{\substack{i \mid n \\ j < i}} a_{ij}.$

This expresses the same set of index pairs $(i,j)$, since each pair satisfies $i \mid n$ and $1 \le j < i$, and every such pair appears exactly once under either order of summation.

This completes the proof. ∎
