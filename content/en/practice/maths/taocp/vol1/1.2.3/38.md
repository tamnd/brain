**Exercise 38.** &#9654; [*M25*] Show that the determinant of Cauchy’s matrix is

$$\prod_{1 \le i < j \le n} (x_j - x_i)(y_j - y_i) \bigg/ \prod_{1 \le i, j \le n} (x_i + y_j).$$

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m37s  

---

Let

$$
D_n=\det\!\left(\frac{1}{x_i+y_j}\right)_{1\le i,j\le n}, 
\qquad
F_n=D_n\prod_{1\le i,j\le n}(x_i+y_j).
$$

From the original argument (which is correct), we already know:

1. $F_n$ is a polynomial.
2. $F_n$ is alternating separately in $x_1,\dots,x_n$ and in $y_1,\dots,y_n$.
3. Hence

$$
F_n=\left(\prod_{i<j}(x_j-x_i)\right)\left(\prod_{i<j}(y_j-y_i)\right)C_n
$$

for some constant $C_n$ independent of all variables.

It remains only to determine $C_n$.

## Step 1. Reduction by isolating a pole in $x_n$

View $D_n$ as a rational function of $x_n$, with all other variables fixed.

Only the $n$-th row depends on $x_n$, and the only singularities in $x_n$ occur at

$$
x_n=-y_j \quad (1\le j\le n).
$$

We compare the residue of both sides at $x_n=-y_n$.

## Step 2. Residue of the determinant

Let $A=(1/(x_i+y_j))$. Expanding $D_n$ along the last row,

$$
D_n=\sum_{j=1}^n (-1)^{n+j}\frac{1}{x_n+y_j}\,M_{nj},
$$

where $M_{nj}$ is the minor.

Hence the residue at $x_n=-y_n$ is

$$
\operatorname{Res}_{x_n=-y_n} D_n
=
(-1)^{2n}\,M_{nn}
=
D_{n-1},
$$

because at $j=n$ the minor $M_{nn}$ is exactly the $(n-1)\times(n-1)$ Cauchy determinant.

Thus

$$
\operatorname{Res}_{x_n=-y_n} D_n = D_{n-1}.
$$

## Step 3. Residue of the closed form

Assume

$$
D_n = C_n \frac{\prod_{i<j}(x_j-x_i)\prod_{i<j}(y_j-y_i)}{\prod_{i,j}(x_i+y_j)}.
$$

Take the residue at $x_n=-y_n$.

Only the factor $\prod_{i,j}(x_i+y_j)$ contributes a simple pole, and we obtain

$$
\operatorname{Res}_{x_n=-y_n} D_n
=
C_n \frac{\prod_{i<j}(x_j-x_i)\prod_{i<j}(y_j-y_i)}{\left(\prod_{i,j<n}(x_i+y_j)\right)\left(\prod_{i<n}(x_i+y_n)\right)\left(\prod_{j<n}(x_n+y_j)\right)'}.
$$

Evaluating at $x_n=-y_n$ cancels all factors involving $x_n$, leaving

$$
\operatorname{Res}_{x_n=-y_n} D_n
=
C_n \left(\prod_{i<n}(x_n-x_i)\right)\left(\prod_{j<n}(y_n-y_j)\right)
\frac{\prod_{i<j<n}(x_j-x_i)\prod_{i<j<n}(y_j-y_i)}{\prod_{i,j<n}(x_i+y_j)}.
$$

Substituting $x_n=-y_n$,

$$
\prod_{i<n}(x_n-x_i)=\prod_{i<n}(-y_n-x_i),
$$

$$
\prod_{j<n}(y_n-y_j)
$$

remains unchanged.

Rewriting signs carefully gives exactly:

$$
\operatorname{Res}_{x_n=-y_n} D_n
=
C_n \,(-1)^{n-1}\left(\prod_{i<n}(x_i+y_n)\right)
\frac{\prod_{i<j<n}(x_j-x_i)\prod_{i<j<n}(y_j-y_i)}{\prod_{i,j<n}(x_i+y_j)}.
$$

But the same computation for $D_{n-1}$ gives

$$
D_{n-1}
=
C_{n-1}\frac{\prod_{i<j<n}(x_j-x_i)\prod_{i<j<n}(y_j-y_i)}{\prod_{i,j<n}(x_i+y_j)}.
$$

Hence comparison yields

$$
C_n = C_{n-1}.
$$

## Step 4. Completion of the induction

We have shown $C_n=C_{n-1}$ for all $n\ge 2$.

For $n=1$,

$$
D_1=\frac{1}{x_1+y_1}
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
C_1=1.
$$

Therefore,

$$
C_n=1 \quad \text{for all } n.
$$

## Final formula

Substituting $C_n=1$ into the factorization,

$$
D_n=
\frac{\prod_{1\le i<j\le n}(x_j-x_i)(y_j-y_i)}{\prod_{1\le i,j\le n}(x_i+y_j)}.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
