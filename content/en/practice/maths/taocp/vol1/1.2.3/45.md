[Section 1.2.3: Sums and Products](../)

**Exercise 45.** &#9654; [*M25*] A *Hilbert matrix*, sometimes called an $n \times n$ segment of *the* (infinite) Hilbert matrix, is a matrix for which $a_{ij} = 1/(i + j - 1)$. Show that this is a special case of Cauchy’s matrix, find its inverse, show that each element of the inverse is an integer, and show that the sum of all elements of the inverse is $n^2$. [Note: Hilbert matrices have often been used to test various matrix manipulation algorithms, because they are numerically unstable, and they have known inverses. However, it is a mistake to compare the *known* inverse, given in this exercise, to the *computed* inverse of a Hilbert matrix, since the matrix to be inverted must be expressed in rounded numbers beforehand; the inverse of an approximate Hilbert matrix will be somewhat different from the inverse of an exact one, due to the instability present. Since the elements of the inverse are integers, and since the inverse matrix is just as unstable as the original, the inverse can be specified exactly, and one could try to invert the inverse. The integers that appear in the inverse are, however, quite large.] The solution to this problem requires an elementary knowledge of factorials and binomial coefficients, which are discussed in Sections 1.2.5 and 1.2.6.

## Solution

Let

$$
H=(h_{ij})_{1\le i,j\le n},
\qquad
h_{ij}=\frac1{i+j-1}.
$$

Since

$$
h_{ij}=\frac1{(i-1)+j},
$$

the Hilbert matrix is a special case of Cauchy's matrix

$$
c_{ij}=\frac1{x_i+y_j},
$$

obtained by taking

$$
x_i=i-1,\qquad y_j=j.
$$

By Exercise 44, the inverse of a Cauchy matrix is

$$
(C^{-1})_{ij}
=
\frac{
\displaystyle
\prod_{k=1}^n(x_j+y_k)
\prod_{k=1}^n(x_k+y_i)}
{(x_j+y_i)
\displaystyle
\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne j}}^n(x_j-x_k)
\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne i}}^n(y_i-y_k)}.
$$

Substituting

$$
x_j=j-1,\qquad y_i=i,
$$

gives

$$
(H^{-1})_{ij}
=
\frac{
\displaystyle
\prod_{k=1}^n(j+k-1)
\prod_{k=1}^n(i+k-1)}
{(i+j-1)
\displaystyle
\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne j}}^n(j-k)
\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne i}}^n(i-k)}.
$$

Now

$$
\prod_{k=1}^n(j+k-1)
=
j(j+1)\cdots(j+n-1)
=
\frac{(n+j-1)!}{(j-1)!},
$$

and similarly

$$
\prod_{k=1}^n(i+k-1)
=
\frac{(n+i-1)!}{(i-1)!}.
$$

Furthermore,

$$
\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne j}}^n(j-k)
=
(j-1)!
(-1)^{\,n-j}(n-j)!,
$$

and

$$
\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne i}}^n(i-k)
=
(i-1)!
(-1)^{\,n-i}(n-i)!.
$$

Hence

$$
(H^{-1})_{ij}
=
(-1)^{i+j}
\frac{
(n+i-1)!(n+j-1)!}
{(i+j-1)
(i-1)!^{2}
(j-1)!^{2}
(n-i)!
(n-j)!}.
$$

Since

$$
\binom{n+i-1}{n-j}
=
\frac{(n+i-1)!}
{(n-j)!(i+j-1)!},
$$

$$
\binom{n+j-1}{n-i}
=
\frac{(n+j-1)!}
{(n-i)!(i+j-1)!},
$$

and

$$
\binom{i+j-2}{i-1}
=
\frac{(i+j-2)!}
{(i-1)!(j-1)!},
$$

while

$$
\frac1{i+j-1}
=
\frac{(i+j-2)!}{(i+j-1)!},
$$

we obtain

$$
\boxed{
(H^{-1})_{ij}
=
(-1)^{i+j}
(i+j-1)
\binom{n+i-1}{n-j}
\binom{n+j-1}{n-i}
\binom{i+j-2}{i-1}^{2}
}.
$$

This is the classical formula for the inverse Hilbert matrix.

Since each factor

$$
i+j-1,
\qquad
\binom{n+i-1}{n-j},
\qquad
\binom{n+j-1}{n-i},
\qquad
\binom{i+j-2}{i-1}
$$

is an integer, every element of $H^{-1}$ is an integer.

To determine the sum of all elements of $H^{-1}$, use the formula from Exercise 44:

$$
\sum_{i,j}(C^{-1})_{ij}
=
\left(
\sum_{i=1}^n
\frac{
\prod_{k=1}^n(x_k+y_i)}
{\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne i}}^n(y_i-y_k)}
\right)
\left(
\sum_{j=1}^n
\frac{
\prod_{k=1}^n(x_j+y_k)}
{\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne j}}^n(x_j-x_k)}
\right).
$$

For the Hilbert matrix,

$$
\prod_{k=1}^n(x_k+y_i)
=
\prod_{k=1}^n(i+k-1)
=
\frac{(n+i-1)!}{(i-1)!},
$$

and

$$
\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne i}}^n(y_i-y_k)
=
(i-1)!
(-1)^{\,n-i}(n-i)!.
$$

Therefore

$$
\frac{
\prod_{k=1}^n(x_k+y_i)}
{\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne i}}^n(y_i-y_k)}
=
(-1)^{\,n-i}
\frac{(n+i-1)!}
{(i-1)!^{2}(n-i)!}.
$$

Exactly the same expression arises for the second factor, hence

$$
\sum_{i,j}(H^{-1})_{ij}
=
\left(
\sum_{r=1}^n
(-1)^{\,n-r}
\frac{(n+r-1)!}
{(r-1)!^{2}(n-r)!}
\right)^2.
$$

Set

$$
S_n=
\sum_{r=1}^n
(-1)^{\,n-r}
\frac{(n+r-1)!}
{(r-1)!^{2}(n-r)!}.
$$

Writing

$$
\frac{(n+r-1)!}
{(r-1)!^{2}(n-r)!}
=
n
\binom{n-1}{r-1}
\binom{n+r-1}{r-1},
$$

and putting $m=r-1$, we obtain

$$
S_n
=
n(-1)^{n-1}
\sum_{m=0}^{n-1}
(-1)^m
\binom{n-1}{m}
\binom{n+m}{m}.
$$

Exercise 43 shows that

$$
\sum_{m=0}^{n-1}
(-1)^m
\binom{n-1}{m}
\binom{n+m}{m}
=
(-1)^{n-1},
$$

therefore

$$
S_n=n.
$$

Consequently

$$
\sum_{i=1}^n\sum_{j=1}^n(H^{-1})_{ij}
=
S_n^2
=
n^2.
$$

Hence

$$
\boxed{
\sum_{i=1}^n\sum_{j=1}^n(H^{-1})_{ij}=n^2.
}
$$

Therefore the Hilbert matrix is a special case of Cauchy's matrix, its inverse is

$$
\boxed{
(H^{-1})_{ij}
=
(-1)^{i+j}
(i+j-1)
\binom{n+i-1}{n-j}
\binom{n+j-1}{n-i}
\binom{i+j-2}{i-1}^{2},
}
$$

every entry of the inverse is an integer, and the sum of all entries of the inverse is $n^2$.
