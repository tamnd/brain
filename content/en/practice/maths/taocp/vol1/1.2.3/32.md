[Section 1.2.3: Sums and Products](../)

**Exercise 32.** [*M20*] Prove that

$$\prod_{j=1}^n \sum_{i=1}^m a_{ij} = \sum_{1 \le i_1, \ldots, i_n \le m} a_{i_1 1} \ldots a_{i_n n}.$$

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m06s  

---

## Solution

For each integer $j$ with $1 \le j \le n$, define

$$
S_j = \sum_{i=1}^m a_{ij}.
$$

The left-hand side is

$$
\prod_{j=1}^n S_j = S_1 S_2 \cdots S_n.
$$

The proof proceeds by repeated use of the distributive law (4).

For $n=1$,

$$
\prod_{j=1}^1 \sum_{i=1}^m a_{ij} = \sum_{i_1=1}^m a_{i_1 1},
$$

which matches the right-hand side since the condition $1 \le i_1 \le m$ is exactly the summation range.

Assume for $k \ge 1$ that

$$
\prod_{j=1}^k \sum_{i=1}^m a_{ij}
=
\sum_{1 \le i_1,\ldots,i_k \le m} a_{i_1 1} \cdots a_{i_k k}.
$$

Consider the case $k+1$:

$$
\prod_{j=1}^{k+1} \sum_{i=1}^m a_{ij}
=
\left(\prod_{j=1}^k \sum_{i=1}^m a_{ij}\right)\left(\sum_{i_{k+1}=1}^m a_{i_{k+1},k+1}\right).
$$

Substituting the induction hypothesis gives

$$
\left(\sum_{1 \le i_1,\ldots,i_k \le m} a_{i_1 1} \cdots a_{i_k k}\right)
\left(\sum_{i_{k+1}=1}^m a_{i_{k+1},k+1}\right).
$$

Applying the distributive law (4) yields

$$
\sum_{1 \le i_1,\ldots,i_k \le m}
\sum_{i_{k+1}=1}^m
\left(a_{i_1 1} \cdots a_{i_k k}\right)a_{i_{k+1},k+1}.
$$

This is

$$
\sum_{1 \le i_1,\ldots,i_{k+1} \le m}
a_{i_1 1} \cdots a_{i_{k+1},k+1},
$$

which matches the required form for $k+1$.

By induction on $n$, the identity holds for all $n \ge 1$.

This completes the proof.
