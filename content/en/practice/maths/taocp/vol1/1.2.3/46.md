We restart from the correct expansion and repair only the faulty combinatorial step by eliminating the incorrect bijection claim and replacing it with a valid classification of _injective_ maps.

## Starting point

From the Leibniz expansion and expansion of matrix products, we already have the correct identity

$$
\det(AB)
=
\sum_{f:\{1,\dots,m\}\to\{1,\dots,n\}}
\left(\prod_{i=1}^m a_{i,f(i)}\right)\det(B^f),
\tag{1}
$$

where $B^f$ is the $m\times m$ matrix whose $i$-th row is the $f(i)$-th row of $B$.

## Step 1: Vanishing for non-injective $f$

If $f(i)=f(k)$ for some $i\neq k$, then $B^f$ has two identical rows, hence

$$
\det(B^f)=0.
$$

Therefore only injective maps contribute in (1), so

$$
\det(AB)
=
\sum_{f\ \text{injective}}
\left(\prod_{i=1}^m a_{i,f(i)}\right)\det(B^f).
\tag{2}
$$

## Step 2: Encode injective maps by subsets and permutations

Let $f$ be injective. Define its image set

$$
J=\{f(1),\dots,f(m)\}\subseteq \{1,\dots,n\}, \quad |J|=m.
$$

Write $J$ in increasing order:

$$
J=\{j_1<j_2<\cdots<j_m\}.
$$

Because $f$ is a bijection from $\{1,\dots,m\}$ to $J$, there exists a **unique permutation** $\sigma\in S_m$ such that

$$
f(i)=j_{\sigma(i)}.
\tag{3}
$$

This uniqueness is valid because the ordering $j_1<\cdots<j_m$ is fixed, so each value $f(i)$ identifies exactly one index in $\{1,\dots,m\}$.

Thus we have a true bijection:

$$
f \longleftrightarrow (J,\sigma),
$$

where $J\subseteq \{1,\dots,n\}, |J|=m$, and $\sigma\in S_m$.

## Step 3: Rewrite the determinant factor

Let $B^J$ denote the $m\times m$ matrix consisting of rows $j_1,\dots,j_m$ in that order.

From $f(i)=j_{\sigma(i)}$, the matrix $B^f$ is obtained from $B^J$ by permuting rows according to $\sigma$. Hence

$$
\det(B^f)=\operatorname{sgn}(\sigma)\det(B^J).
\tag{4}
$$

## Step 4: Rewrite the product over $a_{i,f(i)}$

Using $f(i)=j_{\sigma(i)}$,

$$
\prod_{i=1}^m a_{i,f(i)}
=
\prod_{i=1}^m a_{i,j_{\sigma(i)}}.
\tag{5}
$$

## Step 5: Substitute into the sum

Substituting (4) and (5) into (2), and summing over all injective $f$, equivalently over all pairs $(J,\sigma)$, gives

$$
\det(AB)
=
\sum_{J}
\sum_{\sigma\in S_m}
\left(\prod_{i=1}^m a_{i,j_{\sigma(i)}}\right)
\operatorname{sgn}(\sigma)\det(B^J).
\tag{6}
$$

## Step 6: Recognize the determinant of $A_J$

The matrix $A_J$ is the $m\times m$ matrix formed from columns $j_1,\dots,j_m$. By the Leibniz formula,

$$
\det(A_J)
=
\sum_{\sigma\in S_m}
\operatorname{sgn}(\sigma)\prod_{i=1}^m a_{i,j_{\sigma(i)}}.
\tag{7}
$$

Substituting (7) into (6),

$$
\det(AB)
=
\sum_J \det(A_J)\det(B^J).
$$

## Conclusion

The key correction is that we do **not** sum over all functions $f$, nor do we attempt a non-existent bijection from $(J,\sigma)$ to arbitrary $f$. Instead:

- Only injective $f$ contribute,
- Each injective $f$ corresponds uniquely to a pair $(J,\sigma)$,
- This yields a valid reindexing with no multiplicity issues.

Hence the Cauchy–Binet identity is proved:

$$
\boxed{
\det(AB)=\sum_{1\le j_1<\cdots<j_m\le n}\det(A_{j_1\cdots j_m})\det(B^{j_1\cdots j_m})
}.
$$
