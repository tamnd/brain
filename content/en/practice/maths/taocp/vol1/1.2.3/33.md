[Section 1.2.3: Sums and Products](../)

**Exercise 33.** &#9654; [*M30*] One evening Dr. Matrix discovered some formulas that might even be classed as more remarkable than those of exercise 20:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{1}{(a-b)(a-c)} + \frac{1}{(b-a)(b-c)} + \frac{1}{(c-a)(c-b)} &= 0, \\[5pt]
\frac{a}{(a-b)(a-c)} + \frac{b}{(b-a)(b-c)} + \frac{c}{(c-a)(c-b)} &= 0, \\[5pt]
\frac{a^2}{(a-b)(a-c)} + \frac{b^2}{(b-a)(b-c)} + \frac{c^2}{(c-a)(c-b)} &= 1, \\[5pt]
\frac{a^3}{(a-b)(a-c)} + \frac{b^3}{(b-a)(b-c)} + \frac{c^3}{(c-a)(c-b)} &= a+b+c.
\end{aligned}
$$

Prove that these formulas are a special case of a general law; let $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n$ be distinct numbers, and show that

$$
\sum_{j=1}^n \left( x_j^r \bigg/ \prod_{\substack{1 \le k \le n \\ k \ne j}} (x_j - x_k) \right) = \begin{cases} 0, & \text{if } 0 \le r < n - 1; \\ 1, & \text{if } r = n - 1; \\ \sum_{j=1}^n x_j, & \text{if } r = n. \end{cases}
$$

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m28s  

---

## Setup

Let $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n$ be distinct numbers and define

$P(x)=\prod_{k=1}^n (x-x_k).$

Then for each $j$,

$P'(x_j)=\prod_{\substack{1\le k\le n\\ k\ne j}}(x_j-x_k).$

For an integer $r\ge 0$, define

$S_r=\sum_{j=1}^n \frac{x_j^r}{\prod_{k\ne j}(x_j-x_k)}=\sum_{j=1}^n \frac{x_j^r}{P'(x_j)}.$

The task is to determine $S_r$ for $0\le r\le n$.

## Solution

Consider the rational function

$F(x)=\frac{x^r}{P(x)}.$

### Partial fraction decomposition

The function $F(x)$ has simple poles at $x=x_j$. Its partial fraction decomposition has the form

$F(x)=\sum_{j=1}^n \frac{A_j}{x-x_j}$

for constants $A_j$. Multiplying by $(x-x_j)$ and taking the limit $x\to x_j$ yields

$A_j=\frac{x_j^r}{P'(x_j)}.$

Hence

\frac{x^r}{P(x)}=\sum_{j=1}^n \frac{x_j^r}{P'(x_j)(x-x_j)}. \tag{1}

### Extraction of the coefficient of $1/x$

Expand each term of (1) for large $x$:

$\frac{1}{x-x_j}=\frac{1}{x}\cdot \frac{1}{1-x_j/x}=\frac{1}{x}\left(1+\frac{x_j}{x}+O\!\left(\frac{1}{x^2}\right)\right).$

Substituting into (1),

$\frac{x^r}{P(x)}=\frac{1}{x}\sum_{j=1}^n \frac{x_j^r}{P'(x_j)}+\frac{1}{x^2}\sum_{j=1}^n \frac{x_j^{r+1}}{P'(x_j)}+O\!\left(\frac{1}{x^3}\right).$

Thus the coefficient of $1/x$ in the Laurent expansion of $x^r/P(x)$ equals $S_r$.

### Direct asymptotic expansion

Write

$P(x)=x^n\prod_{k=1}^n \left(1-\frac{x_k}{x}\right).$

Then

$\frac{1}{P(x)}=x^{-n}\prod_{k=1}^n \left(1-\frac{x_k}{x}\right)^{-1}.$

Each factor satisfies

$\left(1-\frac{x_k}{x}\right)^{-1}=1+\frac{x_k}{x}+O\!\left(\frac{1}{x^2}\right).$

Multiplying these expansions,

$\prod_{k=1}^n \left(1-\frac{x_k}{x}\right)^{-1}=1+\frac{\sum_{k=1}^n x_k}{x}+O\!\left(\frac{1}{x^2}\right).$

Therefore

$\frac{x^r}{P(x)}=x^{r-n}\left(1+\frac{\sum_{k=1}^n x_k}{x}+O\!\left(\frac{1}{x^2}\right)\right),$

so

$\frac{x^r}{P(x)}=x^{r-n}+\left(\sum_{k=1}^n x_k\right)x^{r-n-1}+O\!\left(x^{r-n-2}\right).$

The coefficient of $1/x$ is determined as follows.

If $r<n-1$, then $r-n\le -2$, so no term of order $x^{-1}$ appears and the coefficient is $0$.

If $r=n-1$, then $x^{r-n}=x^{-1}$ contributes coefficient $1$ and no other term contributes $x^{-1}$, so the coefficient is $1$.

If $r=n$, then $x^{r-n}=x^0$ and the term producing $x^{-1}$ is

$\left(\sum_{k=1}^n x_k\right)x^{-1},$

so the coefficient is $\sum_{k=1}^n x_k$.

Since this coefficient also equals $S_r$, the stated values follow.

Hence

$$
\sum_{j=1}^n \left( \frac{x_j^r}{\prod_{k\ne j}(x_j-x_k)} \right)
=
\begin{cases}
0, & 0\le r<n-1,\\
1, & r=n-1,\\
\sum_{j=1}^n x_j, & r=n.
\end{cases}
$$

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification

The identity (1) is justified because both sides are rational functions with the same simple poles at $x=x_j$ and identical residues $x_j^r/P'(x_j)$, while their difference has no poles and vanishes at infinity, hence it is identically zero.

The Laurent expansion of $P(x)$ follows from factoring $x^n$ and expanding each factor $(1-x_k/x)$; collecting terms up to order $1/x$ suffices since only the coefficient of $x^{-1}$ is required for all cases $0\le r\le n$.

The case analysis on $r$ depends only on the exponent $r-n$ and the first-order correction term, which fully determines whether an $x^{-1}$ term appears.

## Notes

The expression is the coefficient extraction form of Lagrange interpolation. The weights

$\frac{1}{\prod_{k\ne j}(x_j-x_k)}$

are the barycentric interpolation denominators, and the identity corresponds to evaluating moments of the interpolating basis functions applied to $x^r$.
