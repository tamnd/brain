**Exercise 22.** &#9654; [*20*] State the appropriate analogs of Eqs. (5), (7), (8), and (11) for *products* instead of sums.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m40s

## Solution

The product notation is defined in Eq. (20) as

$$
\prod_{R(j)} a_j,
$$

the product of all $a_j$ such that the integer $j$ satisfies $R(j)$, with value $1$ when no such $j$ exists. The required analogs are obtained by replacing additive structure by multiplicative structure while preserving the role of indexing relations.

For Eq. (5), change of variable, the same permutation argument applies because a permutation of indices merely relabels the factors without changing their multiplicative order. Hence

$$
\prod_{R(i)} a_i = \prod_{R(j)} a_j = \prod_{R(p(j))} a_{p(j)}.
$$

For Eq. (7), interchange of order of summation becomes interchange of order of products over independent index relations. Since all factors commute under multiplication, the grouping induced by nested products can be rearranged without affecting the total product. Therefore

$$
\prod_{R(i)} \prod_{S(j)} a_{ij} = \prod_{S(j)} \prod_{R(i)} a_{ij}.
$$

For Eq. (8), distributivity of addition over a sum corresponds in multiplicative form to the decomposition of a product of pointwise products into separate products. Each factor $b_i c_i$ contributes independently to the product, so the total factorization separates into two independent products over the same index set. Thus

$$
\prod_{R(i)} (b_i c_i) = \left(\prod_{R(i)} b_i\right)\left(\prod_{R(i)} c_i\right).
$$

For Eq. (11), domain decomposition corresponds to splitting a product over a union of index sets into contributions from each region, correcting for the double counting of the intersection. Each index satisfying only $R(j)$ or only $S(j)$ contributes once on the left-hand side, while indices satisfying both relations contribute twice. This matches the factorization

$$
\left(\prod_{R(j)} a_j\right)\left(\prod_{S(j)} a_j\right)
=
\left(\prod_{R(j)\ \text{or}\ S(j)} a_j\right)
\left(\prod_{R(j)\ \text{and}\ S(j)} a_j\right).
$$

This completes the set of product analogs of Eqs. (5), (7), (8), and (11). ∎
