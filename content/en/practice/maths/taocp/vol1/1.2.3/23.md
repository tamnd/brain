**Exercise 23.** [*10*] Explain why it is a good idea to define $\sum_{R(j)} a_j$ and $\prod_{R(j)} a_j$ as zero and one, respectively, when no integers satisfy $R(j)$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m07s  

---

When no integer satisfies $R(j)$, the expression $\sum_{R(j)} a_j$ must behave as an additive identity so that identities such as Eq. (11) remain valid without additional exceptional cases. In particular, if $R(j)$ and $S(j)$ have disjoint solution sets, then Eq. (11) reduces to $\sum_{R(j)} a_j + \sum_{S(j)} a_j = \sum_{R(j)\text{ or }S(j)} a_j$, and this remains correct only when the contribution from an empty condition contributes nothing to the total. Defining the empty sum as $0$ preserves this additivity and ensures that repeated decomposition of a summation domain never introduces spurious terms.

For products, the corresponding requirement is that $\prod_{R(j)} a_j$ act as a multiplicative identity when no factors are present. This choice makes laws analogous to Eqs. (5), (11), and the distributive behavior of products consistent with boundary cases of finite products, where removing all factors must leave a neutral element. Defining the empty product as $1$ preserves identities obtained from repeated factorization and ensures compatibility with product rules and reductions of index sets without needing special exceptions. ∎
