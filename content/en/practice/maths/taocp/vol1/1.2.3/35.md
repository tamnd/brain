**Exercise 35.** [*HM20*] The notation $\sup_{R(j)} a_j$ is used to denote the least upper bound of the elements $a_j$, in a manner exactly analogous to the $\sum$- and $\prod$-notations. (When $R(j)$ is satisfied for only finitely many $j$, the notation $\max_{R(j)} a_j$ is often used to denote the same quantity.) Show how rules (a), (b), (c), and (d) can be adapted for manipulation of *this* notation. In particular discuss the following analog of rule (a):

$$(\sup\nolimits_{R(i)} a_i) + (\sup\nolimits_{S(j)} b_j) = \sup\nolimits_{R(i)} (\sup\nolimits_{S(j)} (a_i + b_j)),$$

and give a suitable definition for the notation when $R(j)$ is satisfied for *no* $j$.

## Exercises

*Determinants and matrices*. The following interesting problems are for the reader who has experienced at least an introduction to determinants and elementary matrix theory. A determinant may be evaluated by astutely combining the operations of: (a) factoring a quantity out of a row or column; (b) adding a multiple of one row (or column) to another row (or column); (c) expanding by cofactors. The simplest and most often used version of operation (c) is to simply delete the entire first row and column, provided that the element in the upper left corner is $+1$ and the remaining elements in either the entire first row or the entire first column are zero; then evaluate the resulting smaller determinant. In general, the cofactor of an element $a_{ij}$ in an $n \times n$ determinant is $(-1)^{i+j}$ times the $(n-1) \times (n-1)$ determinant obtained by deleting the row and column in which $a_{ij}$ appeared. The value of a determinant is equal to $\sum a_{ij} \cdot \text{cofactor}(a_{ij})$ summed with either $i$ or $j$ held constant and with the other subscript varying from 1 to $n$.

If $(b_{ij})$ is the *inverse* of matrix $(a_{ij})$, then $b_{ij}$ equals the cofactor of $a_{ji}$ (not $a_{ij}$), divided by the determinant of the whole matrix.

The following types of matrices are of special importance:

*Vandermonde’s matrix*,

$a_{ij} = x_j^i$
$$
\begin{pmatrix}
x_1 & x_2 & \ldots & x_n \\
x_1^2 & x_2^2 & \ldots & x_n^2 \\
\vdots & & & \vdots \\
x_1^n & x_2^n & \ldots & x_n^n
\end{pmatrix}
$$

Combinatorial matrix,
$a_{ij} = y + \delta_{ij}x$
$$
\begin{pmatrix}
x+y & y & \ldots & y \\
y & x+y & \ldots & y \\
\vdots & & & \vdots \\
y & y & \ldots & x+y
\end{pmatrix}
$$

Cauchy’s matrix,
$a_{ij} = 1/(x_i + y_j)$
$$
\begin{pmatrix}
1/(x_1 + y_1) & 1/(x_1 + y_2) & \ldots & 1/(x_1 + y_n) \\
1/(x_2 + y_1) & 1/(x_2 + y_2) & \ldots & 1/(x_2 + y_n) \\
\vdots & & & \vdots \\
1/(x_n + y_1) & 1/(x_n + y_2) & \ldots & 1/(x_n + y_n)
\end{pmatrix}
$$

## Solution

Let

$$
M=\sup_{R(i)} a_i,\qquad N=\sup_{S(j)} b_j.
$$

The rules for $\sup$ are obtained by replacing addition of terms by formation of least upper bounds.

For a nonempty set of indices, $\sup_{R(j)}a_j$ denotes the least upper bound of the numbers $a_j$ satisfying $R(j)$. When no integer $j$ satisfies $R(j)$, the set of values is empty. In order that the analog of rule (a) remain valid, it is convenient to adjoin the value $-\infty$ and define

$$
\sup_{R(j)}a_j=-\infty
$$

whenever no $j$ satisfies $R(j)$. Since

$$
-\infty+x=-\infty,
$$

the formulas below continue to hold without exception.

Rule (b), change of variable, is unchanged. If $p(j)$ is a permutation of the relevant values,

$$
\sup_{R(i)}a_i
=
\sup_{R(j)}a_j
=
\sup_{R(p(j))}a_{p(j)}.
$$

Indeed, a permutation merely rearranges the same collection of numbers, and the least upper bound of a set is unaffected by reordering.

Rule (c), interchanging order, becomes

$$
\sup_{R(i)}\sup_{S(j)}a_{ij}
=
\sup_{S(j)}\sup_{R(i)}a_{ij}.
$$

Both sides are the least upper bound of the entire collection of numbers $a_{ij}$ satisfying both conditions. More generally,

$$
\sup_{R(i)}\sup_{S(i,j)}a_{ij}
=
\sup_{S'(j)}\sup_{R'(i,j)}a_{ij},
$$

with $S'(j)$ and $R'(i,j)$ defined exactly as in equation (9).

Rule (d), manipulation of the domain, takes the form

$$
\sup_{R(j)\text{ or }S(j)}a_j
=
\max\left(
\sup_{R(j)}a_j,\,
\sup_{S(j)}a_j
\right).
$$

The least upper bound of the union of two sets is the larger of their two least upper bounds. Equivalently,

$$
\max\left(
\sup_{R(j)}a_j,\,
\sup_{S(j)}a_j
\right)
=
\sup_{R(j)\text{ or }S(j)}a_j.
$$

The analog of rule (a) is

$$
(\sup_{R(i)}a_i)+(\sup_{S(j)}b_j)
=
\sup_{R(i)}
\left(
\sup_{S(j)}(a_i+b_j)
\right).
$$

To prove this formula, let

$$
M=\sup_{R(i)}a_i,\qquad N=\sup_{S(j)}b_j.
$$

For every admissible $i$ and $j$,

$$
a_i\le M,\qquad b_j\le N,
$$

hence

$$
a_i+b_j\le M+N.
$$

Therefore

$$
\sup_{R(i)}
\sup_{S(j)}(a_i+b_j)
\le M+N.
$$

Conversely, let $\epsilon>0$. Since $M$ and $N$ are least upper bounds, there exist indices $i_0$ and $j_0$ satisfying

$$
a_{i_0}>M-\frac{\epsilon}{2},
\qquad
b_{j_0}>N-\frac{\epsilon}{2}.
$$

Hence

$$
a_{i_0}+b_{j_0}
>
M+N-\epsilon.
$$

Consequently,

$$
\sup_{R(i)}
\sup_{S(j)}(a_i+b_j)
\ge
M+N-\epsilon.
$$

Since this inequality holds for every $\epsilon>0$,

$$
\sup_{R(i)}
\sup_{S(j)}(a_i+b_j)
\ge
M+N.
$$

Combining the two inequalities gives

$$
\sup_{R(i)}
\sup_{S(j)}(a_i+b_j)
=
M+N,
$$

which is

$$
(\sup\nolimits_{R(i)}a_i)+(\sup\nolimits_{S(j)}b_j)
=
\sup\nolimits_{R(i)}
\left(
\sup\nolimits_{S(j)}(a_i+b_j)
\right).
$$

Thus rules (a), (b), (c), and (d) for $\sup$ are

$$
(\sup\nolimits_{R(i)}a_i)+(\sup\nolimits_{S(j)}b_j)
=
\sup\nolimits_{R(i)}
\sup\nolimits_{S(j)}
(a_i+b_j),
$$

$$
\sup\nolimits_{R(i)}a_i
=
\sup\nolimits_{R(p(j))}a_{p(j)},
$$

$$
\sup\nolimits_{R(i)}
\sup\nolimits_{S(j)}a_{ij}
=
\sup\nolimits_{S(j)}
\sup\nolimits_{R(i)}a_{ij},
$$

and

$$
\max\left(
\sup\nolimits_{R(j)}a_j,\,
\sup\nolimits_{S(j)}a_j
\right)
=
\sup\nolimits_{R(j)\text{ or }S(j)}a_j.
$$

This completes the proof.

∎

## Notes

The choice

$$
\sup\varnothing=-\infty
$$

plays the same role for $\sup$ that the convention

$$
\prod\varnothing=1
$$

plays for products. It makes the algebraic laws valid even when one of the index sets is empty.
