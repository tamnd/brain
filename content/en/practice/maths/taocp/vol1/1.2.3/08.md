[Section 1.2.3: Sums and Products](../)

**Exercise 8.** [*HM25*] Find an example of infinite series in which Eq. (7) is false.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m14s  

---

## Solution

Let $R(i)$ and $S(j)$ both be the relation $i \ge 0$ and $j \ge 0$. Define $a_{ij}$ for integers $i,j \ge 0$ by

$$
a_{ij} =
\begin{cases}
1, & j = i, \\
-1, & j = i - 1, \\
0, & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
$$

For each fixed $i \ge 0$, the inner sum $\sum_{S(j)} a_{ij} = \sum_{j=0}^{\infty} a_{ij}$ has only finitely many nonzero terms.

When $i = 0$, the only nonzero term is $a_{00} = 1$, hence

$$
\sum_{j=0}^{\infty} a_{0j} = 1.
$$

When $i \ge 1$, the nonzero terms are $a_{ii} = 1$ and $a_{i,i-1} = -1$, hence

$$
\sum_{j=0}^{\infty} a_{ij} = 1 + (-1) = 0.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\sum_{R(i)} \sum_{S(j)} a_{ij}
= \sum_{i=0}^{\infty} \sum_{j=0}^{\infty} a_{ij}
= 1 + \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} 0
= 1.
$$

Now reverse the order of summation. For each fixed $j \ge 0$, the inner sum $\sum_{R(i)} a_{ij} = \sum_{i=0}^{\infty} a_{ij}$ has nonzero terms only at $i = j$ and $i = j + 1$. These contribute $a_{jj} = 1$ and $a_{j+1,j} = -1$, hence

$$
\sum_{i=0}^{\infty} a_{ij} = 1 + (-1) = 0.
$$

Thus,

$$
\sum_{S(j)} \sum_{R(i)} a_{ij}
= \sum_{j=0}^{\infty} \sum_{i=0}^{\infty} a_{ij}
= \sum_{j=0}^{\infty} 0
= 0.
$$

Since

$$
\sum_{R(i)} \sum_{S(j)} a_{ij} = 1 \quad \text{and} \quad \sum_{S(j)} \sum_{R(i)} a_{ij} = 0,
$$

Eq. (7) fails for this double series.

This completes the proof. ∎
