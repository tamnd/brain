**Exercise 21.** &#9654; [*M25*] Derive rule (d) from (8) and (17).

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m11s  

---

## Solution

From (17), each restricted sum may be written as a full sum using Iverson’s convention:

$$
\sum_{R(j)} a_j = \sum_j a_j [R(j)], \qquad \sum_{S(j)} a_j = \sum_j a_j [S(j)].
$$

Hence

$$
\sum_{R(j)} a_j + \sum_{S(j)} a_j
= \sum_j a_j [R(j)] + \sum_j a_j [S(j)].
$$

Using (8) in the form that a sum over $j$ is linear in the summand,

$$
\sum_j a_j [R(j)] + \sum_j a_j [S(j)]
= \sum_j a_j \bigl([R(j)] + [S(j)]\bigr).
$$

The identity between indicator expressions

$$
[R(j)] + [S(j)] = [R(j)\ \text{or}\ S(j)] + [R(j)\ \text{and}\ S(j)]
$$

holds by exhaustion of the four possible truth values of $R(j)$ and $S(j)$.

Substituting this identity gives

$$
\sum_j a_j \bigl([R(j)] + [S(j)]\bigr)
= \sum_j a_j [R(j)\ \text{or}\ S(j)] + \sum_j a_j [R(j)\ \text{and}\ S(j)].
$$

Applying (17) in reverse to each term yields

$$
\sum_j a_j [R(j)\ \text{or}\ S(j)] = \sum_{R(j)\ \text{or}\ S(j)} a_j,
\qquad
\sum_j a_j [R(j)\ \text{and}\ S(j)] = \sum_{R(j)\ \text{and}\ S(j)} a_j.
$$

Therefore

$$
\sum_{R(j)} a_j + \sum_{S(j)} a_j
= \sum_{R(j)\ \text{or}\ S(j)} a_j + \sum_{R(j)\ \text{and}\ S(j)} a_j,
$$

which is rule (d). ∎
