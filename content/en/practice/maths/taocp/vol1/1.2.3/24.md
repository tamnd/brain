**Exercise 24.** [*20*] Suppose that $R(j)$ is true for only finitely many $j$. By induction on the number of integers satisfying $R(j)$, prove that $\log_b \prod_{R(j)} a_j = \sum_{R(j)} (\log_b a_j)$, assuming that all $a_j > 0$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m19s  

---

## Solution

Let $J$ be the finite set of all integers $j$ for which $R(j)$ is true, and let $|J| = n$. Since $R(j)$ holds for only finitely many $j$, such an enumeration exists. Write $J = {j_1, j_2, \dots, j_n}$.

The statement becomes

$$
\log_b \prod_{k=1}^n a_{j_k} = \sum_{k=1}^n \log_b a_{j_k}.
$$

The proof proceeds by induction on $n$.

For $n = 0$, the product $\prod_{k=1}^0 a_{j_k}$ is an empty product and equals $1$. Hence

$$
\log_b \prod_{k=1}^0 a_{j_k} = \log_b 1 = 0.
$$

The corresponding sum $\sum_{k=1}^0 \log_b a_{j_k}$ is an empty sum and equals $0$. Both sides are equal.

Assume the statement holds for a set of size $n-1$, where $n \ge 1$. Let $J = {j_1, \dots, j_n}$. Separate one index, say $j_n$, from the product:

$$
\prod_{k=1}^n a_{j_k} = a_{j_n} \prod_{k=1}^{n-1} a_{j_k}.
$$

Apply the logarithm:

$$
\log_b \prod_{k=1}^n a_{j_k}
= \log_b a_{j_n} + \log_b \prod_{k=1}^{n-1} a_{j_k}.
$$

By the induction hypothesis,

$$
\log_b \prod_{k=1}^{n-1} a_{j_k} = \sum_{k=1}^{n-1} \log_b a_{j_k}.
$$

Substituting gives

$$
\log_b \prod_{k=1}^n a_{j_k}
= \log_b a_{j_n} + \sum_{k=1}^{n-1} \log_b a_{j_k}
= \sum_{k=1}^n \log_b a_{j_k}.
$$

Thus the statement holds for $n$, completing the induction.

This completes the proof. ∎
