[Section 1.2.5: Permutations and Factorials](../)

**Exercise 17.** [**] [HM20] Prove that the infinite product

$$
\prod_{n \ge 1}\frac{(n+\alpha_1)\cdots(n+\alpha_k)}{(n+\beta_1)\cdots(n+\beta_k)}
$$

equals

$$
\frac{\Gamma(1+\beta_1)\cdots\Gamma(1+\beta_k)}{\Gamma(1+\alpha_1)\cdots\Gamma(1+\alpha_k)},
$$

if $\alpha_1+\cdots+\alpha_k=\beta_1+\cdots+\beta_k$ and if none of the $\beta$'s is a negative integer.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 9m10s  

---

Let

$$
P_N=\prod_{n=1}^{N}\frac{(n+\alpha_1)\cdots(n+\alpha_k)}
{(n+\beta_1)\cdots(n+\beta_k)}.
$$

Using Euler's formula (13) with $n=\alpha_i$ and $n=\beta_i$,

$$
\Gamma(1+\alpha_i)
=\lim_{N\to\infty}
\frac{N^{\alpha_i}N!}
{(\alpha_i+1)(\alpha_i+2)\cdots(\alpha_i+N)},
$$

hence

$$
(\alpha_i+1)\cdots(\alpha_i+N)
\sim
\frac{N^{\alpha_i}N!}{\Gamma(1+\alpha_i)},
$$

and similarly

$$
(\beta_i+1)\cdots(\beta_i+N)
\sim
\frac{N^{\beta_i}N!}{\Gamma(1+\beta_i)}.
$$

Therefore

$$
P_N
=
\prod_{i=1}^{k}
\frac{(\alpha_i+1)\cdots(\alpha_i+N)}
     {(\beta_i+1)\cdots(\beta_i+N)}
\sim
N^{\sum\alpha_i-\sum\beta_i}
\prod_{i=1}^{k}
\frac{\Gamma(1+\beta_i)}
     {\Gamma(1+\alpha_i)}.
$$

Since

$$
\alpha_1+\cdots+\alpha_k
=
\beta_1+\cdots+\beta_k,
$$

the power of $N$ is $N^0=1$. Consequently the limit of $P_N$ exists and equals

$$
\prod_{n\ge1}
\frac{(n+\alpha_1)\cdots(n+\alpha_k)}
     {(n+\beta_1)\cdots(n+\beta_k)}
=
\frac{\Gamma(1+\beta_1)\cdots\Gamma(1+\beta_k)}
     {\Gamma(1+\alpha_1)\cdots\Gamma(1+\alpha_k)}.
$$

The assumption that no $\beta_i$ is a negative integer guarantees that the gamma factors in the denominator of Euler's formula are finite and that none of the factors $n+\beta_i$ vanishes. Thus the stated identity holds. ∎
