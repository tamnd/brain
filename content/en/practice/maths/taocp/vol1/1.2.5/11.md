**Exercise 11.** [**] [M15] Let the representation of $n$ in the binary system be $n = 2^{e_1}+2^{e_2}+\cdots+2^{e_r}$, where $e_1 > e_2 > \cdots > e_r \ge 0$. Show that $n!$ is divisible by $2^{n-r}$ but not by $2^{n-r+1}$.

x 12. [M22] (A. Legendre, 1808.) Generalizing the result of the previous exercise, let $p$ be a prime number, and let the representation of $n$ in the $p$-ary number system be

$$
n = a_k p^k + a_{k-1}p^{k-1} + \cdots + a_1p + a_0.
$$

Express the number $\mu$ of Eq. (8) in a simple formula involving $n$, $p$, and the $a$'s.

Let $\mu=\sum_{k>0}\left\lfloor \frac{n}{2^k}\right\rfloor$ be the exponent of $2$ in $n!$ by equation (8). It suffices to show $\mu=n-r$, where $r$ is the number of nonzero digits in the binary representation $n=2^{e_1}+2^{e_2}+\cdots+2^{e_r}$ with $e_1>\cdots>e_r\ge 0$.

Define $F(n)=n-r$ for this representation. It is enough to verify that $F(n!)$ satisfies the same recursive characterization as $\mu$. From $n!= (n-1)!,n$ it follows that the exponent of $2$ in $n!$ satisfies $\mu(n)=\mu(n-1)+v_2(n)$, where $v_2(n)$ is the largest $t$ such that $2^t\mid n$.

Write $n-1$ in binary. If $n-1$ ends with exactly $t$ trailing $1$-bits, then adding $1$ produces $n$ by changing those $t$ trailing $1$-bits to $0$ and changing the next $0$-bit to $1$. Hence

$v_2(n)=t,\qquad r(n)=r(n-1)+1-t,$

since $r(n-1)$ loses $t$ ones and gains one new one.

Therefore

$F(n)-F(n-1)=(n-r(n))-(n-1-r(n-1))=1-(r(n)-r(n-1))=1-(1-t)=t=v_2(n).$

Thus $F(n)$ satisfies $F(n)=F(n-1)+v_2(n)$ with $F(1)=0$, matching the defining recurrence for $\mu(n)$ with the same initial value. Hence $\mu=F(n)=n-r$.

Therefore $2^{n-r}\mid n!$ and $2^{n-r+1}\nmid n!$, since $\mu=n-r$ is the exact exponent of $2$ in $n!$.

This completes the proof. ∎
