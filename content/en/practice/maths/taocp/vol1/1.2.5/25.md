[Section 1.2.5: Permutations and Factorials](../)

**Exercise 25.** [**] [M20] Do factorial powers satisfy a law analogous to the ordinary law of exponents, $x^{m+n}=x^m x^n$?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 8m04s  

---

Factorial powers, defined by

$x^{\overline{n}} = x(x+1)\cdots(x+n-1), \qquad x^{\underline{n}} = x(x-1)\cdots(x-n+1),$

do not satisfy a simple law analogous to ordinary exponentiation, $x^{m+n} = x^m x^n$, for general integers $m,n>0$. Indeed,

$x^{\overline{m+n}} = x(x+1)\cdots(x+m+n-1)$

cannot in general be factored as $x^{\overline{m}} , x^{\overline{n}}$, because $x^{\overline{n}}$ would start at $x$ rather than at $x+m$. The correct analogue is

$x^{\overline{m+n}} = x^{\overline{m}} \, (x+m)^{\overline{n}}, \qquad x^{\underline{m+n}} = x^{\underline{m}} \, (x-m)^{\underline{n}},$

where the second factor is a factorial power starting at the shifted base. These identities reduce to ordinary exponent laws only in the trivial case $n=0$ or $m=0$, but otherwise the shift prevents the simple factorization. This completes the proof. ∎
