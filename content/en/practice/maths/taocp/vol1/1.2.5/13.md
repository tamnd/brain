[Section 1.2.5: Permutations and Factorials](../)

**Exercise 13.** [**] [M23] (Wilson's theorem, actually due to Leibniz, 1682.) If $p$ is prime, then $(p-1)! \bmod p = p-1$. Prove this, by pairing off numbers among $\{1,2,\ldots,p-1\}$ whose product modulo $p$ is $1$.

x 14. [M28] (L. Stickelberger, 1890.) In the notation of exercise 12, we can determine $n! \bmod p$ in terms of the $p$-ary representation, for any positive integer $n$, thus generalizing Wilson's theorem. In fact, prove that

$$
\frac{n!}{p^\mu} \equiv (-1)^\mu a_0!a_1!\cdots a_k! \pmod p.
$$

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 14m48s

For each $a\in{1,2,\ldots,p-1}$ there is a unique inverse $a^{-1}$ modulo $p$, since $p$ is prime. If $a\not\equiv a^{-1}\pmod p$, the pair $a,a^{-1}$ contributes

$aa^{-1}\equiv 1 \pmod p.$

The elements satisfying $a\equiv a^{-1}\pmod p$ are the solutions of

$a^2\equiv 1 \pmod p,$

hence

$(a-1)(a+1)\equiv 0 \pmod p.$

Since $p$ is prime, this implies

$a\equiv 1 \pmod p \qquad\text{or}\qquad a\equiv -1 \pmod p,$

that is, $a=1$ or $a=p-1$ among the numbers $1,2,\ldots,p-1$.

Therefore all factors in $(p-1)!$ can be paired with their inverses except for $1$ and $p-1$. Multiplying all pairs gives

$$
(p-1)!
\equiv 1\cdot(p-1)\cdot
\prod aa^{-1}
\equiv (p-1)\cdot 1
\equiv p-1
\pmod p.
$$

Hence

$\boxed{(p-1)! \bmod p = p-1}.$

This completes the proof.
