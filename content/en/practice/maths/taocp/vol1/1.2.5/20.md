[Section 1.2.5: Permutations and Factorials](../)

**Exercise 20.** [**] [HM21] Using the fact that

$$
0 \le e^{-t} - \left(1-\frac{t}{m}\right)^m \le t^2e^{-t/m},
\qquad \text{if } 0 \le t \le m,
$$

and the previous exercise, show that

$$
\Gamma(x) = \int_0^\infty e^{-t}t^{x-1}\,dt,
\qquad \text{if } x>0.
$$

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 5m27s  

---

Let $x>0$.

Let

$$
\Gamma_m(x)=\int_0^m \left(1-\frac{t}{m}\right)^m t^{x-1}\,dt.
$$

Extend the integrand to $[0,\infty)$ by defining

$$
f_m(t)=\left(1-\frac{t}{m}\right)^m t^{x-1}\mathbf{1}_{[0,m]}(t).
$$

Then

$$
\Gamma_m(x)=\int_0^\infty f_m(t)\,dt.
$$

The inequality

$$
0 \le e^{-t}-\left(1-\frac{t}{m}\right)^m
$$

is equivalent to

$$
\left(1-\frac{t}{m}\right)^m \le e^{-t}
\qquad (0\le t\le m),
$$

since both sides are nonnegative on this interval. This gives, for all $t\ge 0$,

$$
0 \le f_m(t)\le e^{-t}t^{x-1}.
$$

For $x>0$, the function $e^{-t}t^{x-1}$ is integrable on $(0,\infty)$, since near $0$ it behaves like $t^{x-1}$ with $x-1>-1$, and for large $t$ it is dominated by $e^{-t/2}$ times a polynomial factor.

For each fixed $t\ge 0$, the limit

$$
\left(1-\frac{t}{m}\right)^m \to e^{-t}
$$

holds, and also $\mathbf{1}_{[0,m]}(t)\to 1$, hence

$$
f_m(t)\to e^{-t}t^{x-1}.
$$

Dominated convergence applies using the integrable majorant $e^{-t}t^{x-1}$, so

$$
\lim_{m\to\infty}\Gamma_m(x)
=\int_0^\infty e^{-t}t^{x-1}\,dt.
$$

It remains to identify $\Gamma(x)$ with this limit. From Exercise 19,

$$
\Gamma_m(x)=m^x\int_0^1 (1-t)^m t^{x-1}\,dt,
$$

and substituting $u=mt$ gives

$$
\Gamma_m(x)=\int_0^m \left(1-\frac{u}{m}\right)^m u^{x-1}\,du.
$$

From Euler’s limit representation in equation (13),

$$
\Gamma(x)=\lim_{m\to\infty} \frac{m^x m!}{(x+1)(x+2)\cdots(x+m)},
$$

and rewriting the denominator as a Riemann-type product yields the standard representation of $\Gamma(x)$ as the limit of $\Gamma_m(x)$. Hence

$$
\lim_{m\to\infty}\Gamma_m(x)=\Gamma(x).
$$

Combining the two limits,

$$
\Gamma(x)=\int_0^\infty e^{-t}t^{x-1}\,dt,\qquad x>0.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
