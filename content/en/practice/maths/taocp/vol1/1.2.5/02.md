[Section 1.2.5: Permutations and Factorials](../)

**Exercise 2.** [**] [10] In the notation of Eq. (2), show that $p_n^{(n-1)} = p_n^{(n)}$, and explain why this happens.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m54s  

---

From equation (2),

$$
p_{nk} = n(n-1)\cdots(n-k+1).
$$

Setting $k=n$ gives

$$
p_n^{(n)} = p_{nn} = n(n-1)\cdots 2\cdot 1.
$$

Setting $k=n-1$ gives

$$
p_n^{(n-1)} = p_{n,n-1} = n(n-1)\cdots 3\cdot 2.
$$

The second product differs from the first only by the absence of the final factor $1$. Since multiplication by $1$ does not change a product,

$$
p_n^{(n)} = p_{n,n-1}\cdot 1 = p_{n,n-1}.
$$

Thus both quantities count the same set of objects. Combinatorially, $p_{n,n}$ counts permutations of all $n$ objects, while $p_{n,n-1}$ counts ordered selections of $n-1$ objects from $n$; after the first $n-1$ positions are filled, exactly one object remains and can be placed in the last position in only one way, so both constructions yield $n!$. This completes the proof. ∎
