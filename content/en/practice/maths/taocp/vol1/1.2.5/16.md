[Section 1.2.5: Permutations and Factorials](../)

**Exercise 16.** [**] [HM15] Show that the infinite sum in Eq. (11) does not converge unless $n$ is a nonnegative integer.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 4m46s  

---

Let

$$
c_k=1-\frac1{1!}+\frac1{2!}-\cdots+(-1)^k\frac1{k!}.
$$

Then Eq. (11) has the form

$$
\sum_{k\ge0} c_k\,n(n-1)\cdots(n-k+1).
$$

The coefficients $c_k$ are the partial sums of the alternating series for $e^{-1}$, hence

$$
\lim_{k\to\infty} c_k=e^{-1}\neq0.
$$

If $n$ is not a nonnegative integer, none of the factors $n-j$ is zero, so

$$
a_k=c_k\,n(n-1)\cdots(n-k+1)
$$

is the general term of the series. A necessary condition for convergence is $a_k\to0$. Since $c_k\to e^{-1}$, it suffices to examine the product. For sufficiently large $k$,

$$
|n-j|\ge \frac j2 \qquad (j\ge J),
$$

for some fixed $J$. Therefore

$$
|n(n-1)\cdots(n-k+1)|
\ge C\prod_{j=J}^{k-1}\frac j2
= C\,2^{-(k-J)}\frac{(k-1)!}{(J-1)!},
$$

where $C>0$ is constant. The right-hand side tends to $\infty$ as $k\to\infty$, so $|a_k|\to\infty$. Consequently $a_k$ does not tend to $0$, and the series cannot converge.

If $n$ is a nonnegative integer, the factor $n-n$ occurs when $k=n+1$, so

$$
n(n-1)\cdots(n-k+1)=0 \qquad (k>n).
$$

All subsequent terms vanish, and the series reduces to a finite sum. Hence the infinite sum in Eq. (11) converges only when $n$ is a nonnegative integer. This completes the proof.

∎
