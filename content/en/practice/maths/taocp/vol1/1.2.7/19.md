[Section 1.2.7: Harmonic Numbers](../)

**Exercise 19.** [*M30*] List all nonnegative integers $n$ for which $H_n$ is an integer.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 1m08s  

---

## Setup

For $n \ge 0$, the harmonic number is defined by

$$
H_n = \sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac{1}{k},
$$

with $H_0 = 0$ by the empty sum convention.

The task is to determine all nonnegative integers $n$ for which $H_n$ is an integer.

It is required to show:

1. $H_n \in \mathbb{Z}$ for $n=0$ and $n=1$.
2. $H_n \notin \mathbb{Z}$ for all $n \ge 2$.

## Solution

For $n=0$, the definition gives $H_0=0$, which is an integer.

For $n=1$, the sum reduces to

$$
H_1 = 1,
$$

which is an integer.

Let $n \ge 2$. By BertrandŌĆÖs postulate, there exists a prime $p$ such that

$$
\frac{n}{2} < p \le n.
$$

In the sum

$$
H_n = \sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac{1}{k},
$$

the term $\frac{1}{p}$ occurs exactly once. For every integer $k \ne p$, the denominator of $\frac{1}{k}$ in lowest terms is not divisible by $p$, since $p$ is prime and $p \nmid k$ for $k < p$.

Let $L = \mathrm{lcm}(1,2,\dots,n)$. Writing $H_n$ with common denominator $L$ gives

$$
H_n = \frac{1}{L}\sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac{L}{k}.
$$

The numerator is

$$
N = \sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac{L}{k}.
$$

Since $p \mid L$ and $p \nmid \frac{L}{p}$, the term $\frac{L}{p}$ is divisible by $p$ exactly once in its factorization, while for $k \ne p$ the integer $\frac{L}{k}$ is divisible by $p$. Indeed, for $k \ne p$, the factor $p$ remains in $L/k$ because $p \le n$ and $p \nmid k$ implies $k$ does not remove the factor $p$ from $L$.

Hence every term in $N$ except $\frac{L}{p}$ is divisible by $p$, while $\frac{L}{p}$ is not divisible by $p$. Therefore

$$
N \not\equiv 0 \pmod p,
$$

so $p \nmid N$.

Since $p \mid L$ and $p \nmid N$, the fraction $\frac{N}{L}$ is not an integer. Therefore $H_n \notin \mathbb{Z}$ for all $n \ge 2$.

Thus the only nonnegative integers $n$ for which $H_n$ is an integer are $n=0$ and $n=1$.

This completes the proof. ŌłÄ

## Verification

For $n=0$, the definition yields $H_0=0$ directly from an empty sum.

For $n=1$, the sum contains only one term, giving $H_1=1$.

For $n \ge 2$, BertrandŌĆÖs postulate guarantees a prime $p$ with $n/2 < p \le n$, ensuring $p$ appears exactly once among denominators $1,2,\dots,n$.

Construction of $L=\mathrm{lcm}(1,\dots,n)$ ensures $p \mid L$ and isolates the contribution of the term $k=p$ as the unique term in the numerator not divisible by $p$.

This establishes $p \nmid N$ while $p \mid L$, which prevents cancellation to an integer.

## Notes

The argument isolates a prime in the interval $(n/2,n]$ to control divisibility in a common denominator representation. This technique applies to many sums of reciprocals where uniqueness of a prime divisor forces non-integrality.
