[Section 1.2.7: Harmonic Numbers](../)

**Exercise 20.** [*HM22*] There is an analytic way to approach summation problems such as the one leading to Theorem A in this section: If $f(x)=\sum_{k \ge 0} a_k x^k$, and this series converges for $x=x_0$, prove that

$$
\sum_{k \ge 0} a_k x_0^k H_k
= \int_0^1 \frac{f(x_0)-f(x_0y)}{1-y}\,dy.
$$

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 1m03s

## Solution

Let $f(x)=\sum_{k\ge 0} a_k x^k$ converge at $x=x_0$. For $0\le y<1$, substitution into the series gives

$$
f(x_0)=\sum_{k\ge 0} a_k x_0^k,
\qquad
f(x_0y)=\sum_{k\ge 0} a_k x_0^k y^k.
$$

Hence

$$
f(x_0)-f(x_0y)=\sum_{k\ge 0} a_k x_0^k(1-y^k).
$$

For $k\ge 0$ and $y\ne 1$, the algebraic identity

$$
\frac{1-y^k}{1-y}=\sum_{j=0}^{k-1} y^j
$$

holds, with the right-hand side equal to $0$ when $k=0$. Therefore

$$
\frac{f(x_0)-f(x_0y)}{1-y}
=\sum_{k\ge 1} a_k x_0^k \sum_{j=0}^{k-1} y^j.
$$

For each fixed $y\in[0,1)$, the series in $k$ converges absolutely, since it is dominated by the power series defining $f(x_0y)$. The partial sums therefore admit termwise integration over $[0,1]$, and the resulting identity is obtained by passing to the limit over partial sums.

Integrating term by term yields

$$
\int_0^1 \frac{f(x_0)-f(x_0y)}{1-y}\,dy
= \sum_{k\ge 1} a_k x_0^k \sum_{j=0}^{k-1} \int_0^1 y^j\,dy.
$$

For each integer $j\ge 0$,

$$
\int_0^1 y^j\,dy=\frac{1}{j+1}.
$$

Substitution gives

$$
\int_0^1 \frac{f(x_0)-f(x_0y)}{1-y}\,dy
= \sum_{k\ge 1} a_k x_0^k \sum_{j=0}^{k-1} \frac{1}{j+1}.
$$

The inner sum equals $H_k$, since

$$
H_k=\sum_{m=1}^k \frac{1}{m}=\sum_{j=0}^{k-1}\frac{1}{j+1}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\int_0^1 \frac{f(x_0)-f(x_0y)}{1-y}\,dy
= \sum_{k\ge 1} a_k x_0^k H_k.
$$

Adding the $k=0$ term, which is $a_0 x_0^0 H_0=0$, yields

$$
\sum_{k\ge 0} a_k x_0^k H_k
= \int_0^1 \frac{f(x_0)-f(x_0y)}{1-y}\,dy.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
