[Section 1.2.7: Harmonic Numbers](../)

**Exercise 9.** [*M18*] Theorem A applies only when $x>0$; what is the value of the sum considered when $x=-1$?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m15s  

---

## Solution

Let

$$
S_n=\sum_{k=1}^{n}\binom{n}{k}(-1)^k H_k.
$$

Using $H_k=\sum_{j=1}^{k}\frac{1}{j}$,

$$
S_n=\sum_{k=1}^{n}\binom{n}{k}(-1)^k \sum_{j=1}^{k}\frac{1}{j}
=\sum_{j=1}^{n}\frac{1}{j}\sum_{k=j}^{n}(-1)^k\binom{n}{k}.
$$

The inner sum is evaluated by splitting the full binomial sum:

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n}(-1)^k\binom{n}{k}=(1-1)^n=0,
$$

so

$$
\sum_{k=j}^{n}(-1)^k\binom{n}{k}=-\sum_{k=0}^{j-1}(-1)^k\binom{n}{k}.
$$

The partial alternating binomial identity

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{m}(-1)^k\binom{n}{k}=(-1)^m\binom{n-1}{m}
$$

applied with $m=j-1$ yields

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{j-1}(-1)^k\binom{n}{k}=(-1)^{j-1}\binom{n-1}{j-1}.
$$

Substitution gives

$$
\sum_{k=j}^{n}(-1)^k\binom{n}{k}=(-1)^j\binom{n-1}{j-1}.
$$

Hence

$$
S_n=\sum_{j=1}^{n}\frac{1}{j}(-1)^j\binom{n-1}{j-1}.
$$

With $i=j-1$,

$$
S_n=-\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}\frac{(-1)^i}{i+1}\binom{n-1}{i}.
$$

The integral identity

$$
\int_{0}^{1}(1-x)^{n-1}\,dx=\frac{1}{n}
$$

combined with the expansion

$$
(1-x)^{n-1}=\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}(-1)^i\binom{n-1}{i}x^i
$$

yields

$$
\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}\frac{(-1)^i}{i+1}\binom{n-1}{i}=\frac{1}{n}.
$$

Therefore

$$
S_n=-\frac{1}{n}.
$$

Thus, for $x=-1$,

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{n}\binom{n}{k}(-1)^k H_k = -\frac{1}{n}.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎

## Answer

$$
\boxed{-\frac{1}{n}}
$$
