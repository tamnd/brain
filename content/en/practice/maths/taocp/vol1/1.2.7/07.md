**Exercise 7.** [*M21*] Let $T(m,n)=H_m+H_n-H_{mn}$. Show that when $m$ or $n$ increases, $T(m,n)$ never increases (assuming that $m$ and $n$ are positive). Compute the minimum and maximum values of $T(m,n)$ for $m,n>0$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m46s  

---

Define

$$
T(m,n)=H_m+H_n-H_{mn}, \qquad m,n\in \mathbb{Z}_{>0}.
$$

## 1. Monotonicity in each variable

Fix $n$. Using $H_{m+1}=H_m+\frac{1}{m+1}$,

$$
T(m+1,n)-T(m,n)=\frac{1}{m+1}-\bigl(H_{(m+1)n}-H_{mn}\bigr).
$$

Expand the harmonic difference:

$$
H_{(m+1)n}-H_{mn}=\sum_{k=mn+1}^{(m+1)n}\frac{1}{k}.
$$

For each term, $k \le (m+1)n$, hence $\frac{1}{k}\ge \frac{1}{(m+1)n}$. There are exactly $n$ terms, so

$$
H_{(m+1)n}-H_{mn}\ge \frac{n}{(m+1)n}=\frac{1}{m+1}.
$$

Thus

$$
T(m+1,n)-T(m,n)\le 0.
$$

So $T(m,n)$ is nonincreasing in $m$. By symmetry $T(m,n)=T(n,m)$, it is also nonincreasing in $n$.

## 2. Maximum value

Since $T(m,n)$ is nonincreasing in each variable, its maximum occurs at the smallest positive integers:

$$
T(1,1)=H_1+H_1-H_1=1.
$$

Hence

$$
\max_{m,n\ge 1} T(m,n)=1.
$$

## 3. A precise form for $T(m,n)$

Write

$$
H_k=\ln k+\gamma+\varepsilon_k,
$$

where

$$
\varepsilon_k := H_k-\ln k-\gamma.
$$

A standard result on harmonic numbers gives:

1. $\varepsilon_k>0$ for all $k\ge 1$,
2. $\varepsilon_k$ is strictly decreasing in $k$.

Now substitute into $T(m,n)$:

$$
T(m,n)
= (\ln m+\gamma+\varepsilon_m)+(\ln n+\gamma+\varepsilon_n)-(\ln(mn)+\gamma+\varepsilon_{mn}).
$$

The logarithmic terms cancel:

$$
\ln m+\ln n-\ln(mn)=0,
$$

and one $\gamma$ remains, so

$$
T(m,n)=\gamma+\varepsilon_m+\varepsilon_n-\varepsilon_{mn}.
$$

Hence

$$
T(m,n)-\gamma=\varepsilon_m+\varepsilon_n-\varepsilon_{mn}.
$$

## 4. Lower bound and infimum

Since $\varepsilon_k$ is decreasing and positive, we have

$$
\varepsilon_{mn}\le \varepsilon_m,\qquad \varepsilon_{mn}\le \varepsilon_n.
$$

Thus

$$
\varepsilon_m+\varepsilon_n-\varepsilon_{mn}\ge \varepsilon_n>0,
$$

and similarly it is also $\ge \varepsilon_m>0$. Therefore

$$
T(m,n)>\gamma \quad \text{for all } m,n\ge 1.
$$

Next, let $m,n\to\infty$. Since $\varepsilon_k\to 0$,

$$
T(m,n)\to \gamma.
$$

Thus $\gamma$ is a lower bound approached by the function.

So

$$
\inf_{m,n\ge 1} T(m,n)=\gamma,
$$

and this value is not attained for any finite $m,n$.

## 5. Conclusion

- $T(m,n)$ is nonincreasing in each argument.
- The maximum value is

$$
\boxed{T(1,1)=1.}
$$

- The function satisfies

$$
T(m,n)>\gamma \quad \text{for all } m,n\ge 1,
$$

and

$$
\lim_{m,n\to\infty} T(m,n)=\gamma.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\boxed{\inf_{m,n\ge 1} T(m,n)=\gamma, \text{ not attained}.}
$$
