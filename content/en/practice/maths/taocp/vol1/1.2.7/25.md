[Section 1.2.7: Harmonic Numbers](../)

**Exercise 25.** [*M21*] Let

$$
H_n^{(u,v)} = \sum_{1 \le j \le k \le n} \frac{1}{j^u k^v}.
$$

What are $H_n^{(0,v)}$ and $H_n^{(u,0)}$? Prove the general identity

$$
H_n^{(u,v)} + H_n^{(v,u)} = H_n^{(u)}H_n^{(v)} + H_n^{(u+v)}.
$$

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m33s  

---

## Solution

Let $H_n^{(u)}=\sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac{1}{k^u}$. Then

$$
H_n^{(u,v)}=\sum_{1\le j\le k\le n}\frac{1}{j^u k^v}.
$$

### Evaluation of $H_n^{(0,v)}$

Since $j^0=1$,

$$
H_n^{(0,v)}=\sum_{1\le j\le k\le n}\frac{1}{k^v}.
$$

For fixed $k$, the index $j$ ranges from $1$ to $k$, contributing $k$ identical terms. Hence

$$
H_n^{(0,v)}=\sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac{k}{k^v}=\sum_{k=1}^{n}k^{1-v}.
$$

### Evaluation of $H_n^{(u,0)}$

Since $k^0=1$,

$$
H_n^{(u,0)}=\sum_{1\le j\le k\le n}\frac{1}{j^u}.
$$

For fixed $k$, the inner sum runs over $j=1,\dots,k$, so

$$
H_n^{(u,0)}=\sum_{k=1}^{n}\sum_{j=1}^{k}\frac{1}{j^u}
=\sum_{k=1}^{n}H_k^{(u)}.
$$

### Proof of the identity

Consider the product

$$
H_n^{(u)}H_n^{(v)}=\left(\sum_{j=1}^{n}\frac{1}{j^u}\right)\left(\sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac{1}{k^v}\right)
=\sum_{j=1}^{n}\sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac{1}{j^u k^v}.
$$

Split the double sum into the regions $j\le k$ and $j>k$:

$$
H_n^{(u)}H_n^{(v)}
=\sum_{1\le j\le k\le n}\frac{1}{j^u k^v}
+\sum_{1\le k<j\le n}\frac{1}{j^u k^v}.
$$

The first term equals $H_n^{(u,v)}$.

For the second term, rename indices by swapping $j$ and $k$. Then

$$
\sum_{1\le k<j\le n}\frac{1}{j^u k^v}
=\sum_{1\le j<k\le n}\frac{1}{k^u j^v}.
$$

The right-hand side is the strictly off-diagonal part of $H_n^{(v,u)}$, hence

$$
\sum_{1\le j<k\le n}\frac{1}{k^u j^v}
=H_n^{(v,u)}-\sum_{j=1}^{n}\frac{1}{j^{u+v}}
=H_n^{(v,u)}-H_n^{(u+v)}.
$$

Substituting into the product decomposition yields

$$
H_n^{(u)}H_n^{(v)}=H_n^{(u,v)}+H_n^{(v,u)}-H_n^{(u+v)}.
$$

Rearranging gives

$$
H_n^{(u,v)}+H_n^{(v,u)}=H_n^{(u)}H_n^{(v)}+H_n^{(u+v)}.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
