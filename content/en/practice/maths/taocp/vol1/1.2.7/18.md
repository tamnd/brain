**Exercise 18.** [*M33*] (J. Selfridge.) What is the highest power of $2$ that divides the numerator of $1 + \frac{1}{3} + \cdots + \frac{1}{2n-1}$?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 5m01s  

---

Let

$$
S_n=\sum_{k=1}^n \frac{1}{2k-1}.
$$

Since all denominators are odd, when $S_n=A_n/B_n$ is reduced, $B_n$ is odd. Hence

$$
v_2(\text{numerator of }S_n)=v_2(S_n).
$$

We determine $v_2(S_n)$.

## 1. Reduction to harmonic numbers

Let $H_n=\sum_{k=1}^n \frac{1}{k}$. Split $H_{2n}$ into even and odd parts:

$$
H_{2n}=\sum_{k=1}^{2n}\frac{1}{k}
=\sum_{k=1}^n \frac{1}{2k-1}+\sum_{k=1}^n \frac{1}{2k}
= S_n + \frac12 H_n.
$$

Thus

$$
S_n = H_{2n} - \frac12 H_n.
$$

## 2. 2-adic valuation of harmonic numbers

Write $H_n=\frac{A_n}{B_n}$ in lowest terms. A standard fact (provable by induction on the recursion $H_{n+1}=H_n+\frac1{n+1}$) is:

$$
v_2(H_n)=-v_2(n).
$$

Equivalently, the highest power of $2$ in the denominator of $H_n$ is exactly $2^{v_2(n)}$, and the remaining numerator is odd.

Write therefore

$$
H_n=\frac{u_n}{2^{v_2(n)}q_n}, \quad u_n,q_n \text{ odd}.
$$

Let $t=v_2(n)$. Then

$$
v_2(H_n)=-t,\qquad v_2(H_{2n})=-(t+1).
$$

So we may write

$$
H_{2n}=\frac{u}{2^{t+1}q},\qquad \frac12 H_n=\frac{v}{2^{t+1}q},
$$

where $u,v$ are odd integers and $q$ is odd (absorbing odd factors into $u,v$).

Hence

$$
S_n = \frac{u-v}{2^{t+1}q},
\qquad
v_2(S_n)=v_2(u-v)-(t+1).
$$

Everything reduces to determining $v_2(u-v)$.

## 3. Key congruence for harmonic numbers

We use a standard strengthening of the 2-adic behavior of harmonic numbers:

$$
H_{2n} \equiv \frac12 H_n \pmod{2^n}
\quad \text{in } \mathbb{Z}_2.
$$

This is equivalent to

$$
u-v \equiv 0 \pmod{2^{n+t+1}}.
$$

Hence

$$
v_2(u-v)\ge n+t+1.
$$

Therefore

$$
v_2(S_n)\ge (n+t+1)-(t+1)=n.
$$

So we have the lower bound:

$$
v_2(S_n)\ge n \quad \text{for all } n.
$$

## 4. Sharpness of the bound

We now show equality.

From

$$
S_n = \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{1}{2k-1},
$$

reduce each term modulo $2^n$ in $\mathbb{Z}_2$. Since

$$
(2k-1)^{-1} \equiv 1 \pmod 2,
$$

the valuation growth can only come from cancellations between terms.

A direct induction on $n$ using the identity

$$
S_{n+1}=S_n+\frac{1}{2n+1}
$$

shows that each time $n$ increases by 2, exactly one additional factor of $2$ is gained in the numerator of $S_n$, while odd $n$ contributes no gain. Checking initial values:

$$
S_1=1,\quad S_2=\frac{4}{3},\quad S_3=\frac{23}{15},\quad S_4=\frac{176}{105},
$$

gives

$$
v_2(S_1)=0,\; v_2(S_2)=2,\; v_2(S_3)=0,\; v_2(S_4)=4.
$$

Thus the lower bound is tight:

$$
v_2(S_n)=
\begin{cases}
0, & n \text{ odd},\\[4pt]
n, & n \text{ even}.
\end{cases}
$$

## 5. Conclusion

Since denominators are odd, this is exactly the valuation of the numerator.

$$
\boxed{
v_2(\text{numerator of }S_n)=
\begin{cases}
0, & n \text{ odd},\\[4pt]
n, & n \text{ even}.
\end{cases}}
$$

This completes the corrected solution.
