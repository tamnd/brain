[Section 1.2.7: Harmonic Numbers](../)

**Exercise 13.** [*M22*] Prove the identity

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac{x^k}{k}
= H_n + \sum_{k=1}^{n}\binom{n}{k}\frac{(x-1)^k}{k}.
$$

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m05s  

---

## Solution

Start with the binomial expansion

$$
x^k = (1+(x-1))^k = \sum_{j=0}^{k}\binom{k}{j}(x-1)^j.
$$

Divide by $k$ and sum over $1 \le k \le n$:

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac{x^k}{k}
= \sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac{1}{k}\sum_{j=0}^{k}\binom{k}{j}(x-1)^j.
$$

Interchanging the order of summation yields

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac{x^k}{k}
= \sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac{1}{k}
+ \sum_{k=1}^{n}\sum_{j=1}^{k}\frac{1}{k}\binom{k}{j}(x-1)^j.
$$

The first term equals $H_n$ by definition of harmonic numbers. Hence

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac{x^k}{k}
= H_n + \sum_{j=1}^{n}(x-1)^j \sum_{k=j}^{n}\frac{1}{k}\binom{k}{j}.
$$

For the inner sum, use the identity

$$
\binom{k}{j} = \frac{k}{j}\binom{k-1}{j-1},
$$

which implies

$$
\frac{1}{k}\binom{k}{j} = \frac{1}{j}\binom{k-1}{j-1}.
$$

Thus

$$
\sum_{k=j}^{n}\frac{1}{k}\binom{k}{j}
= \frac{1}{j}\sum_{k=j}^{n}\binom{k-1}{j-1}.
$$

Let $m=k-1$. Then $m$ runs from $j-1$ to $n-1$, so

$$
\sum_{k=j}^{n}\binom{k-1}{j-1}
= \sum_{m=j-1}^{n-1}\binom{m}{j-1}.
$$

By the hockey-stick identity (Eq. 1.2.6–(11)),

$$
\sum_{m=j-1}^{n-1}\binom{m}{j-1} = \binom{n}{j}.
$$

Hence

$$
\sum_{k=j}^{n}\frac{1}{k}\binom{k}{j} = \frac{1}{j}\binom{n}{j}.
$$

Substituting back gives

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac{x^k}{k}
= H_n + \sum_{j=1}^{n}\binom{n}{j}\frac{(x-1)^j}{j}.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
