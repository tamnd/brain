[Section 1.2.7: Harmonic Numbers](../)

**Exercise 24.** [*HM21*] Show that

$$
xe^{\gamma x} \prod_{k \ge 1} \left(\left(1+\frac{x}{k}\right)e^{-x/k}\right) = \frac{1}{\Gamma(x)}.
$$

(Consider the partial products of this infinite product.)

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m56s

## Solution

Define the partial products

$$
P_n(x)=xe^{\gamma x}\prod_{k=1}^n\left(\left(1+\frac{x}{k}\right)e^{-x/k}\right).
$$

We shall prove that

$$
\lim_{n\to\infty}P_n(x)=\frac1{\Gamma(x)}.
$$

First write

$$
P_n(x)
=
xe^{\gamma x}
e^{-x\sum_{k=1}^n1/k}
\prod_{k=1}^n\left(1+\frac{x}{k}\right).
$$

If

$$
H_n=\sum_{k=1}^n\frac1k,
$$

then

$$
P_n(x)
=
xe^{x(\gamma-H_n)}
\prod_{k=1}^n\frac{k+x}{k}
=
xe^{x(\gamma-H_n)}
\frac{(x+1)(x+2)\cdots(x+n)}{n!}.
$$

Assume first that $x$ is not a nonpositive integer, so that $\Gamma(x)$ is finite. By repeated use of the functional equation,

$$
\Gamma(x+n+1)
=
(x+n)(x+n-1)\cdots(x+1)\Gamma(x+1),
$$

hence

$$
(x+1)(x+2)\cdots(x+n)
=
\frac{\Gamma(x+n+1)}{\Gamma(x+1)}.
$$

Since

$$
\Gamma(x+1)=x\Gamma(x),
$$

we obtain

$$
x(x+1)(x+2)\cdots(x+n)
=
\frac{\Gamma(x+n+1)}{\Gamma(x)}.
$$

Therefore

$$
P_n(x)
=
e^{x(\gamma-H_n)}
\frac{\Gamma(x+n+1)}
{n!\,\Gamma(x)},
$$

or equivalently,

$$
\Gamma(x)P_n(x)
=
e^{x(\gamma-H_n)}
\frac{\Gamma(x+n+1)}{\Gamma(n+1)}.
\tag{1}
$$

It remains to evaluate the limit of the right-hand side.

By Stirling's formula,

$$
\Gamma(n+x+1)
\sim
\sqrt{2\pi}\,(n+x)^{\,n+x+\frac12}e^{-(n+x)},
$$

and

$$
\Gamma(n+1)
\sim
\sqrt{2\pi}\,n^{\,n+\frac12}e^{-n}.
$$

Hence

$$
\frac{\Gamma(n+x+1)}{\Gamma(n+1)}
\sim
e^{-x}
\left(1+\frac{x}{n}\right)^{n+\frac12}
n^x.
$$

Since

$$
\left(1+\frac{x}{n}\right)^n\to e^x
$$

and

$$
\left(1+\frac{x}{n}\right)^{1/2}\to1,
$$

it follows that

$$
\frac{\Gamma(n+x+1)}{\Gamma(n+1)}
\sim n^x.
\tag{2}
$$

Also,

$$
H_n=\log n+\gamma+o(1),
$$

so

$$
x(\gamma-H_n)
=
-x\log n+o(1),
$$

and therefore

$$
e^{x(\gamma-H_n)}
=
n^{-x}(1+o(1)).
\tag{3}
$$

Combining (1), (2), and (3), we obtain

$$
\Gamma(x)P_n(x)
=
\left(n^{-x}(1+o(1))\right)
\left(n^x(1+o(1))\right)
\to1.
$$

Hence

$$
P_n(x)\to\frac1{\Gamma(x)},
$$

that is,

$$
xe^{\gamma x}
\prod_{k\ge1}
\left(\left(1+\frac{x}{k}\right)e^{-x/k}\right)
=
\frac1{\Gamma(x)}
$$

for every $x$ that is not a nonpositive integer.

If $x=0,-1,-2,\ldots$, one factor in the product vanishes, namely

$$
1+\frac{x}{|x|}=0
$$

when $x<0$, and the leading factor $x$ vanishes when $x=0$. Thus the left-hand side equals $0$. Since $1/\Gamma(x)$ also vanishes at the poles of $\Gamma$, the identity remains valid at these points.

Therefore

$$
\boxed{
xe^{\gamma x}
\prod_{k\ge1}
\left(\left(1+\frac{x}{k}\right)e^{-x/k}\right)
=
\frac1{\Gamma(x)}
}.
$$

$\square$
