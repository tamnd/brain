[Section 1.2.7: Harmonic Numbers](../)

**Exercise 14.** [*M22*] Show that

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac{H_k}{k}
= \frac{1}{2}(H_n^2 + H_n^{(2)}),
$$

and evaluate $\sum_{k=1}^{n} H_k/(k+1)$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m38s  

---

## Solution

Let

$$
S=\sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac{H_k}{k}.
$$

Using $H_k=\sum_{j=1}^{k}\frac{1}{j}$,

$$
S=\sum_{k=1}^{n}\sum_{j=1}^{k}\frac{1}{jk}.
$$

The index set is $1\le j\le k\le n$, so

$$
S=\sum_{1\le j\le k\le n}\frac{1}{jk}.
$$

Split the full square sum

$$
\sum_{j=1}^{n}\sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac{1}{jk}
$$

into regions $j\le k$ and $j>k$. The full sum equals $H_n^2$, and symmetry gives

$$
H_n^2 = \sum_{j\le k}\frac{1}{jk} + \sum_{j>k}\frac{1}{jk} = S + T,
$$

where

$$
T=\sum_{1\le k<j\le n}\frac{1}{jk}.
$$

On the diagonal $j=k$,

$$
\sum_{j=1}^{n}\frac{1}{j^2}=H_n^{(2)}.
$$

The difference between the symmetric decomposition yields

$$
S-T = H_n^{(2)}.
$$

Solving the system

$$
S+T=H_n^2,\quad S-T=H_n^{(2)}
$$

gives

$$
S=\frac{1}{2}\left(H_n^2+H_n^{(2)}\right).
$$

This proves the first identity.

Now consider

$$
U=\sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac{H_k}{k+1}.
$$

Let $m=k+1$, so $m$ runs from $2$ to $n+1$:

$$
U=\sum_{m=2}^{n+1}\frac{H_{m-1}}{m}.
$$

Using $H_{m-1}=H_m-\frac{1}{m}$,

$$
U=\sum_{m=2}^{n+1}\frac{H_m}{m}-\sum_{m=2}^{n+1}\frac{1}{m^2}.
$$

For the first sum,

$$
\sum_{m=2}^{n+1}\frac{H_m}{m}
=\sum_{m=1}^{n+1}\frac{H_m}{m}-\frac{H_1}{1}.
$$

Since $H_1=1$ and from the first result,

$$
\sum_{m=1}^{n+1}\frac{H_m}{m}=\frac{1}{2}\left(H_{n+1}^2+H_{n+1}^{(2)}\right),
$$

this becomes

$$
\sum_{m=2}^{n+1}\frac{H_m}{m}
=\frac{1}{2}\left(H_{n+1}^2+H_{n+1}^{(2)}\right)-1.
$$

For the second sum,

$$
\sum_{m=2}^{n+1}\frac{1}{m^2}=H_{n+1}^{(2)}-1.
$$

Substituting,

$$
U=\frac{1}{2}\left(H_{n+1}^2+H_{n+1}^{(2)}\right)-1-\left(H_{n+1}^{(2)}-1\right).
$$

Simplification yields

$$
U=\frac{1}{2}H_{n+1}^2-\frac{1}{2}H_{n+1}^{(2)}.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac{H_k}{k+1}
=\frac{1}{2}\left(H_{n+1}^2-H_{n+1}^{(2)}\right).
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
