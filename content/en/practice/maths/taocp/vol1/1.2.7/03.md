**Exercise 3.** [*M21*] Generalize the argument used in the previous exercise to show that, for $r>1$, the sum $H_n^{(r)}$ remains bounded for all $n$. Find an upper bound.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m09s  

---

## Solution

Let

$$
H_n^{(r)} = \sum_{k=1}^{n} \frac{1}{k^r}, \qquad r>1.
$$

For each integer $j \ge 0$, consider the block of indices

$$
2^j \le k \le 2^{j+1}-1.
$$

For every $k$ in this range,

$$
k \ge 2^j \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{1}{k^r} \le \frac{1}{(2^j)^r}.
$$

The block contains exactly $2^j$ terms, so the contribution of this block satisfies

$$
\sum_{k=2^j}^{2^{j+1}-1} \frac{1}{k^r}
\le 2^j \cdot \frac{1}{(2^j)^r}
= 2^{j(1-r)}.
$$

Let $m$ be the unique integer such that $2^m \le n < 2^{m+1}$. Splitting the sum into full blocks and a final partial block gives

$$
H_n^{(r)} \le \sum_{j=0}^{m-1} 2^{j(1-r)} + \sum_{k=2^m}^{n} \frac{1}{k^r}.
$$

For the last partial block, the same bound applies:

$$
\sum_{k=2^m}^{n} \frac{1}{k^r} \le 2^{m(1-r)}.
$$

Hence

$$
H_n^{(r)} \le \sum_{j=0}^{m} 2^{j(1-r)}.
$$

Since $r>1$, the ratio $2^{1-r}$ satisfies $0<2^{1-r}<1$, so the geometric series extends to

$$
\sum_{j=0}^{m} 2^{j(1-r)} < \sum_{j=0}^{\infty} 2^{j(1-r)} = \frac{1}{1-2^{1-r}}.
$$

Therefore,

$$
H_n^{(r)} < \frac{1}{1-2^{1-r}}.
$$

Since the right-hand side is independent of $n$, the sequence $H_n^{(r)}$ remains bounded for all $n$ when $r>1$.

This completes the proof. ∎
