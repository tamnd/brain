[Section 2.3: Trees](../)

**Exercise 3.** [**] [M20] Prove rigorously from the definitions that for every node `X` in a tree there is a unique path up to the root, namely a unique sequence of $k \ge 1$ nodes `X_1`, `X_2`, ..., `X_k` such that `X_1` is the root of the tree, `X_k = X`, and `X_j` is the parent of `X_{j+1}` for $1 \le j < k$. (This proof will be typical of the proofs of nearly all the elementary facts about tree structures.) *Hint:* Use induction on the number of nodes in the tree.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 40s  

---

Proceed by induction on the number of nodes in the tree.

If the tree has one node, that node is the root. For $X$ equal to the root, the sequence consisting of the single node $X_1=X$ satisfies the required conditions. Since there are no other nodes, the path is unique.

Assume that the theorem holds for all trees with fewer than $n$ nodes, where $n>1$, and consider a tree $T$ with $n$ nodes. Let $R$ be its root. If $X=R$, the unique path is the one-term sequence $R$. If $X\ne R$, remove the root $R$ and all edges incident with it. By the definition of a tree, the remaining nodes are partitioned into disjoint subtrees rooted at the children of $R$. The node $X$ lies in exactly one of these subtrees, say $T'$. Since $T'$ has fewer than $n$ nodes, the induction hypothesis gives a unique sequence

$$
X_1,\ldots,X_k
$$

in $T'$ from the root of $T'$ to $X$, each node being the parent of the next. Let $C=X_1$ be the root of $T'$. Since $C$ is a child of $R$, the sequence

$$
R,C,X_2,\ldots,X_k
$$

is a path from the root of $T$ to $X$.

For uniqueness, let

$$
R,Y_2,\ldots,Y_m=X
$$

be any path from the root of $T$ to $X$. Since $Y_2$ is a child of $R$ and $X$ lies in the subtree $T'$, necessarily $Y_2=C$. Deleting the initial node $R$ leaves a path in $T'$ from $C$ to $X$. By the induction hypothesis, this path is uniquely determined, hence

$$
Y_2,\ldots,Y_m=C,X_2,\ldots,X_k.
$$

Therefore the entire path from $R$ to $X$ is unique.

By induction, every node $X$ in a tree has a unique path up to the root. This completes the proof.

∎
