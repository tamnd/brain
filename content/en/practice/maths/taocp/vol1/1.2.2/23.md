**Exercise 23.** [*M25*] Give a geometric proof that $\ln xy = \ln x + \ln y$, based on Fig. 6.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m30s  

---

## Solution

Let $\ln x$ be interpreted as the area described in Fig. 6, namely the area under the curve $y = 1/t$ from $t = 1$ to $t = x$. Thus

$$
\ln x = \int_{1}^{x} \frac{1}{t}\,dt,
$$

in geometric terms as an area bounded by the coordinate axes, the curve $y = 1/t$, and the vertical lines $t = 1$ and $t = x$.

For $x > 0$ and $y > 0$, consider the area corresponding to $\ln(xy)$:

$$
\ln(xy) = \text{area under } y = \frac{1}{t} \text{ from } 1 \text{ to } xy.
$$

This area is decomposed into two adjacent regions, split at $t = x$:

$$
\ln(xy) = \int_{1}^{x} \frac{1}{t}\,dt + \int_{x}^{xy} \frac{1}{t}\,dt.
$$

The first term is $\ln x$ by definition. For the second term, make the geometric substitution $t = xu$, which corresponds to a horizontal scaling by factor $x$. Then $dt = x,du$, and when $t = x$, $u = 1$, while when $t = xy$, $u = y$. Hence

$$
\int_{x}^{xy} \frac{1}{t}\,dt
= \int_{1}^{y} \frac{1}{xu} \cdot x\,du
= \int_{1}^{y} \frac{1}{u}\,du
= \ln y.
$$

Substituting both parts gives

$$
\ln(xy) = \ln x + \ln y.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
