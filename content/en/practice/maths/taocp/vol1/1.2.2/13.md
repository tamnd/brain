**Exercise 13.** &#9654; [*M23*] (a) Given that $x$ is a positive real number and $n$ is a positive integer, prove the inequality $\sqrt[n]{1 + x} - 1 \le x/n$. (b) Use this fact to justify the remarks following (7).

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m18s  

---

## Solution

### (a)

Let $x>0$ and let $n$ be a positive integer. Set $y = x/n$, so $y>0$ and $x = ny$.

The desired inequality

$$
\sqrt[n]{1+x} - 1 \le \frac{x}{n}
$$

is equivalent to

$$
(1+x)^{1/n} \le 1 + \frac{x}{n},
$$

and, since both sides are positive, to

$$
1+x \le \left(1 + \frac{x}{n}\right)^n = (1+y)^n.
$$

Thus it suffices to prove

$$
(1+y)^n \ge 1+ny \quad \text{for } y>0.
$$

This is proved by induction on $n$.

For $n=1$, equality holds:

$$
(1+y)^1 = 1+y = 1+1\cdot y.
$$

Assume $(1+y)^n \ge 1+ny$. Multiplying both sides by $(1+y)>0$ gives

$$
(1+y)^{n+1} \ge (1+ny)(1+y) = 1 + (n+1)y + ny^2 \ge 1 + (n+1)y.
$$

Thus $(1+y)^{n+1} \ge 1+(n+1)y$, completing the induction.

Substituting $y=x/n$ yields

$$
(1+x)^{1/n} \le 1 + \frac{x}{n},
$$

hence

$$
\sqrt[n]{1+x} - 1 \le \frac{x}{n}.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎

### (b)

Let $b>1$ and let $k$ be a positive integer. Apply part (a) with

$$
x = b-1, \quad n = 10^k.
$$

Then

$$
\sqrt[10^k]{b} - 1 \le \frac{b-1}{10^k}.
$$

Since $\sqrt[10^k]{b} = b^{1/10^k}$, this becomes

$$
b^{1/10^k} - 1 \le \frac{b-1}{10^k}.
$$

In the construction surrounding (7), the gap between the upper and lower bounds is

$$
b^{n + d_1/10 + \cdots + d_k/10^k}\left(b^{1/10^k}-1\right).
$$

For the first factor, since

$$
n + \frac{d_1}{10} + \cdots + \frac{d_k}{10^k} < n+1,
$$

and $b>1$, monotonicity of exponentiation gives

$$
b^{n + d_1/10 + \cdots + d_k/10^k} < b^{n+1}.
$$

Combining this with the bound from part (a),

$$
b^{1/10^k}-1 \le \frac{b-1}{10^k},
$$

yields

$$
b^{n + d_1/10 + \cdots + d_k/10^k}\left(b^{1/10^k}-1\right)
< b^{n+1}\cdot \frac{b-1}{10^k}.
$$

This matches the estimate used after (7), since the right-hand expression tends to $0$ as $k \to \infty$, allowing arbitrary accuracy of $b^x$. ∎
