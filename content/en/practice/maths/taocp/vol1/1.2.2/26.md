**Exercise 26.** [*M27*] Find a rigorous upper bound on the error made by the algorithm in the previous exercise, based on the precision used in the arithmetic operations.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m49s

We restart from a consistent floating-point model and propagate the error through the iterative state update.

## 1. Correct error model

Assume IEEE-style relative rounding for each arithmetic operation:

$$
\operatorname{fl}(a \circ b) = (a \circ b)(1+\delta), \qquad |\delta| \le \varepsilon,
$$

for $\circ \in \{+,-,\text{shift}\}$. Only the addition update of $y$ is relevant for error growth.

Let the algorithm compute

$$
y^\ast = \sum_{j=1}^{N} c_{k_j}, \qquad c_{k_j} > 0,
$$

and define the computed iterates

$$
\hat{y}_0 = 0, \qquad \hat{y}_j = \operatorname{fl}(\hat{y}_{j-1} + c_{k_j}).
$$

Let the exact (mathematical) iterates be

$$
y_0 = 0, \qquad y_j = y_{j-1} + c_{k_j},
$$

so that $y_N = y^\ast$.

Define the error

$$
E_j = \hat{y}_j - y_j.
$$

## 2. One-step error recurrence

From the floating-point model,

$$
\hat{y}_j = (\hat{y}_{j-1} + c_{k_j})(1+\delta_j), \qquad |\delta_j| \le \varepsilon.
$$

Subtract the exact update $y_j = y_{j-1} + c_{k_j}$:

$$
E_j
= (\hat{y}_{j-1} + c_{k_j})(1+\delta_j) - (y_{j-1} + c_{k_j}).
$$

Insert $\hat{y}_{j-1} = y_{j-1} + E_{j-1}$:

$$
E_j
= (y_{j-1} + c_{k_j} + E_{j-1})(1+\delta_j) - (y_{j-1} + c_{k_j}).
$$

Expand:

$$
E_j
= E_{j-1}(1+\delta_j) + \delta_j (y_{j-1} + c_{k_j}).
$$

## 3. Absolute error bound recurrence

Taking absolute values and using $|\delta_j| \le \varepsilon$:

$$
|E_j|
\le (1+\varepsilon)|E_{j-1}| + \varepsilon (y_{j-1} + c_{k_j}).
$$

Since all $c_{k_j} > 0$, the exact partial sums are increasing and bounded by the final value:

$$
y_{j-1} + c_{k_j} = y_j \le y^\ast.
$$

Hence

$$
|E_j| \le (1+\varepsilon)|E_{j-1}| + \varepsilon y^\ast.
$$

with $E_0 = 0$.

## 4. Solving the recurrence

Unfold the recurrence:

$$
|E_N|
\le \varepsilon y^\ast \sum_{i=0}^{N-1} (1+\varepsilon)^i.
$$

This is a geometric sum:

$$
\sum_{i=0}^{N-1} (1+\varepsilon)^i
= \frac{(1+\varepsilon)^N - 1}{\varepsilon}.
$$

Thus

$$
|E_N|
\le y^\ast \big((1+\varepsilon)^N - 1\big).
$$

## 5. Final bound in terms of the problem variables

Since $y^\ast = \log_b x$ and $1 \le x < 2$, we have

$$
0 \le y^\ast < \log_b 2.
$$

Therefore,

$$
|\hat{y} - \log_b x|
= |E_N|
\le \log_b x \,\big((1+\varepsilon)^N - 1\big)
\le \log_b 2 \,\big((1+\varepsilon)^N - 1\big).
$$

For a simpler explicit form, use $(1+\varepsilon)^N \le e^{N\varepsilon}$:

$$
|\hat{y} - \log_b x|
\le \log_b 2 \,(e^{N\varepsilon} - 1).
$$

And for small $N\varepsilon$, the standard linearization gives:

$$
|\hat{y} - \log_b x|
\le N \varepsilon \log_b 2 \, e^{N\varepsilon}
= O(N\varepsilon).
$$

## 6. Final result

$$
\boxed{
|\hat{y} - \log_b x|
\le \log_b 2 \,\big((1+\varepsilon)^N - 1\big)
\le \log_b 2 \,(e^{N\varepsilon} - 1)
}
$$

This bound correctly accounts for accumulation and propagation of rounding error through all $N$ dependent updates, rather than treating errors as independent.
