**Exercise 9.** [*M23*] Given that $x$ and $y$ are rational, prove the laws of exponents under the assumption that the laws hold when $x$ and $y$ are integers.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m21s  

---

## Solution

Let $b>0$, and let $x=\frac{p}{q}$ and $y=\frac{r}{s}$, where $p,r\in\mathbb{Z}$ and $q,s$ are positive integers. By definition (6),

$$
b^{p/q}=\sqrt[q]{b^p}, \qquad b^{r/s}=\sqrt[s]{b^r}.
$$

The laws of exponents are assumed for integer exponents.

### First law: $b^{x+y}=b^x b^y$

Compute

$$
x+y=\frac{p}{q}+\frac{r}{s}=\frac{ps+rq}{qs}.
$$

Hence, by definition (6),

$$
b^{x+y}=b^{(ps+rq)/(qs)}=\sqrt[qs]{b^{ps+rq}}.
$$

Let $A=b^x b^y=b^{p/q}b^{r/s}$. To identify $A$, compute $A^{qs}$. Since $qs$ is a positive integer,

$$
A^{qs}=(b^{p/q})^{qs}(b^{r/s})^{qs}.
$$

Using the definition of rational exponents and integer exponent laws,

$$
(b^{p/q})^{q}=b^p,\quad \text{so}\quad (b^{p/q})^{qs}=\big((b^{p/q})^{q}\big)^s=(b^p)^s=b^{ps}.
$$

Similarly,

$$
(b^{r/s})^{s}=b^r,\quad \text{so}\quad (b^{r/s})^{qs}=\big((b^{r/s})^{s}\big)^q=(b^r)^q=b^{rq}.
$$

Therefore,

$$
A^{qs}=b^{ps}b^{rq}=b^{ps+rq},
$$

using the laws of exponents for integer exponents.

Thus $A$ is a positive real number whose $qs$th power equals $b^{ps+rq}$. By uniqueness of positive $m$th roots (Exercise 8),

$$
A=\sqrt[qs]{b^{ps+rq}}=b^{(ps+rq)/(qs)}=b^{x+y}.
$$

This completes the proof of the first law. ∎

### Second law: $(b^x)^y=b^{xy}$

Let $B=(b^x)^y=(b^{p/q})^{r/s}$. Compute $B^{qs}$.

Since $(r/s)\cdot qs=rq$, exponent rules for integer powers give

$$
B^{qs}=\big((b^{p/q})^{r/s}\big)^{qs}=(b^{p/q})^{rq}.
$$

Using the same reduction as above,

$$
(b^{p/q})^{q}=b^p \quad \Rightarrow \quad (b^{p/q})^{rq}=\big((b^{p/q})^{q}\big)^r=(b^p)^r=b^{pr}.
$$

Thus,

$$
B^{qs}=b^{pr}.
$$

Hence $B$ is the positive $qs$th root of $b^{pr}$. By uniqueness of roots,

$$
B=\sqrt[qs]{b^{pr}}=b^{pr/(qs)}=b^{(p/q)(r/s)}=b^{xy}.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎

## Notes

The argument reduces both identities to uniqueness of $m$th roots by raising candidate expressions to a common integer power $qs$, after which only integer exponent laws are used.
