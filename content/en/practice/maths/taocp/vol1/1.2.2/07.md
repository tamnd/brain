**Exercise 7.** [*M23*] Given that $x$ and $y$ are integers, prove the laws of exponents, starting from the definition given by Eq. (4).

**Solve time:** 2m49s

Let the exponentiation of an integer exponent be defined as in Eq. (4) of TAOCP:

For a fixed nonzero number $b$,

$$
b^0 = 1,\qquad b^{n+1} = b^n b \ \ (n \ge 0),
$$

and for $n > 0$,

$$
b^{-n} = \frac{1}{b^n}.
$$

All identities below are proved from these rules alone.

## 1. Basic consequence for nonnegative exponents

### Lemma 1

For all integers $m \ge 0$,

$$
b^{m+1} = b^m b.
$$

This is exactly the recursive definition.

### Lemma 2 (product law for nonnegative exponents)

For all integers $m,n \ge 0$,

$$
b^{m+n} = b^m b^n.
$$

**Proof by induction on $n$.**

For $n = 0$,

$$
b^{m+0} = b^m = b^m \cdot 1 = b^m b^0.
$$

Assume $b^{m+n} = b^m b^n$. Then

$$
b^{m+(n+1)} = b^{m+n+1} = b^{m+n} b.
$$

Using the induction hypothesis,

$$
b^{m+n+1} = (b^m b^n)b = b^m (b^n b) = b^m b^{n+1}.
$$

Thus the lemma holds.

## 2. Negative exponents and division identity

### Lemma 3

For $n > 0$,

$$
b^{-n} = \frac{1}{b^n}.
$$

This is the defining rule.

### Lemma 4 (quotient form)

For integers $m \ge n \ge 0$,

$$
b^{m-n} = \frac{b^m}{b^n}.
$$

**Proof.** From Lemma 2,

$$
b^m = b^{(m-n)+n} = b^{m-n} b^n.
$$

Since $b \ne 0$, divide both sides by $b^n$:

$$
b^{m-n} = \frac{b^m}{b^n}.
$$

## 3. Product law for all integers

We prove that for all integers $x,y$,

$$
b^{x+y} = b^x b^y.
$$

We proceed by cases.

### Case 1: $x \ge 0, y \ge 0$

This is Lemma 2.

### Case 2: $x \ge 0, y < 0$

Let $y = -n$ with $n > 0$. Then

$$
b^{x+y} = b^{x-n}.
$$

There are two subcases.

If $x \ge n$, then by Lemma 4,

$$
b^{x-n} = \frac{b^x}{b^n}.
$$

Using Lemma 3,

$$
\frac{1}{b^n} = b^{-n},
$$

so

$$
b^{x-n} = b^x b^{-n} = b^x b^y.
$$

If $x < n$, then $x-n = -(n-x)$, so by definition,

$$
b^{x-n} = b^{-(n-x)} = \frac{1}{b^{n-x}}.
$$

Using Lemma 4,

$$
b^n = b^{(n-x)+x} = b^{n-x} b^x,
$$

so

$$
\frac{1}{b^{n-x}} = \frac{b^x}{b^n} = b^x b^{-n} = b^x b^y.
$$

Thus the identity holds.

### Case 3: $x < 0, y \ge 0$

Symmetric to Case 2, giving $b^{x+y} = b^x b^y$.

### Case 4: $x < 0, y < 0$

Let $x = -m, y = -n$ with $m,n > 0$. Then

$$
b^{x+y} = b^{-(m+n)} = \frac{1}{b^{m+n}}.
$$

Using Lemma 2,

$$
\frac{1}{b^{m+n}} = \frac{1}{b^m b^n} = \frac{1}{b^m} \frac{1}{b^n} = b^{-m} b^{-n} = b^x b^y.
$$

Thus,

$$
b^{x+y} = b^x b^y
$$

for all integers $x,y$.

## 4. Power of a power

We prove for all integers $x,y$,

$$
(b^x)^y = b^{xy}.
$$

Again we use cases.

### Case 1: $y \ge 0$

Induction on $y$.

For $y = 0$,

$$
(b^x)^0 = 1 = b^0 = b^{x\cdot 0}.
$$

Assume $(b^x)^y = b^{xy}$. Then

$$
(b^x)^{y+1} = (b^x)^y b^x = b^{xy} b^x.
$$

By the product law already proved,

$$
b^{xy} b^x = b^{xy+x} = b^{x(y+1)}.
$$

### Case 2: $y < 0$

Let $y = -n$, $n > 0$. Then

$$
(b^x)^y = (b^x)^{-n} = \frac{1}{(b^x)^n}.
$$

From Case 1,

$$
(b^x)^n = b^{xn}.
$$

Hence

$$
(b^x)^{-n} = \frac{1}{b^{xn}} = b^{-xn} = b^{x(-n)} = b^{xy}.
$$

## Conclusion

From the recursive definition (4) alone, we have rigorously derived for all integers $x,y$:

$$
b^{x+y} = b^x b^y,\qquad (b^x)^y = b^{xy}.
$$

∎
