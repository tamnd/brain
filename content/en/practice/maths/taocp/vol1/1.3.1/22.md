**Exercise 22.** [*28*] Location 2000 contains an integer number, `X`. Write two programs that compute `X^{13}` and halt with the result in register `A`. One program should use the minimum number of MIX memory locations; the other should require the minimum execution time possible. Assume that `X^{13}` fits into a single word.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 4m39s

## Solution

Location $2000$ initially contains the integer $X$. We are to write two MIX programs that halt with $rA=X^{13}$. We assume that $X^{13}$ fits into one word.

Under standard MIX semantics, if two one-word numbers are multiplied, the ten-byte product is placed in the register pair $(A,X)$, with the high-order half in $A$ and the low-order half in $X$. Since every intermediate power is assumed to fit into one word, the high-order half is zero and the desired product appears in register $X$. Therefore after each multiplication we transfer the result to $A$ by

$$
\texttt{SLAX 5},
$$

which shifts the contents of $X$ into $A$. Hence each multiplication is followed by one shift instruction.

The optimization problems concern the number of memory locations and the number of multiplications. The additional shift instructions are unavoidable consequences of standard MIX multiplication semantics and do not affect the addition-chain arguments.

## Minimum number of memory locations

The original value $X$ must remain available until the final multiplication, so location $2000$ cannot be overwritten.

Suppose that no auxiliary location were available. After loading $X$, every multiplication changes the contents of the registers. Since no intermediate power can be stored anywhere and location $2000$ must continue to contain $X$, every new power must be obtained by multiplying the current value by $X$. Thus the only exponent sequence obtainable is

$$
1,2,3,4,\ldots ,
$$

and twelve multiplications would be required. Therefore at least one auxiliary location is necessary.

One auxiliary location is sufficient. Use location $2001$ to store $X^4$. Consider the addition chain

$$
1,2,4,8,12,13.
$$

The program is

```
3000  LDA 2000
3001  MUL 2000
3002  SLAX 5
3003  STA 2001
3004  MUL 2001
3005  SLAX 5
3006  STA 2001
3007  MUL 2001
3008  SLAX 5
3009  MUL 2001
3010  SLAX 5
3011  MUL 2000
3012  SLAX 5
3013  HLT
```

Trace:

- after 3002,

$$
rA=X^2;
$$

- after 3003, location $2001$ contains $X^2$;
- after 3005,

$$
rA=X^4;
$$

- after 3006, location $2001$ contains $X^4$;
- after 3008,

$$
rA=X^8;
$$

- after 3010,

$$
rA=X^{12},
$$

since $X^8\cdot X^4=X^{12}$;

- after 3012,

$$
rA=X^{13}.
$$

Thus one auxiliary location suffices.

Therefore the minimum number of data locations is two, namely locations $2000$ and $2001$.

## Minimum execution time

Execution time is minimized by minimizing the number of multiplications, because multiplication is by far the most expensive operation. We therefore determine the minimum possible number of multiplications.

After one multiplication the only possible exponent is

$$
2=1+1.
$$

After two multiplications the possible exponents are

$$
3,\qquad 4.
$$

From $3$ a third multiplication can produce

$$
4,\;5,\;6,
$$

while from $4$ it can produce

$$
5,\;6,\;8.
$$

Hence after three multiplications the attainable exponents are

$$
4,\;5,\;6,\;8.
$$

A fourth multiplication must add two exponents already present in the corresponding chain. Therefore the possible exponents after four multiplications are

$$
5,\;6,\;7,\;8,\;9,\;10,\;11,\;12,\;16.
$$

The exponent $13$ does not occur. Hence no addition chain of length $4$ exists, and at least five multiplications are necessary.

The chain

$$
1,2,3,6,12,13
$$

has length $5$, so five multiplications are sufficient.

Store $X$ in location $2001$ and $X^6$ in location $2002$. The program is

```
3000  LDA 2000
3001  STA 2001
3002  MUL 2000
3003  SLAX 5
3004  MUL 2001
3005  SLAX 5
3006  STA 2002
3007  MUL 2002
3008  SLAX 5
3009  STA 2002
3010  MUL 2002
3011  SLAX 5
3012  MUL 2001
3013  SLAX 5
3014  HLT
```

Trace:

- after 3003,

$$
rA=X^2;
$$

- after 3005,

$$
rA=X^3;
$$

- after 3006, location $2002$ contains $X^3$;
- after 3008,

$$
rA=X^6;
$$

- after 3009, location $2002$ contains $X^6$;
- after 3011,

$$
rA=X^{12};
$$

- after 3013,

$$
rA=X^{13}.
$$

Exactly five multiplications are used. Since four multiplications are impossible, this program has minimum execution time.

Hence:

- the minimum-memory program requires one auxiliary location, namely $2001$;
- the minimum-time program requires five multiplications and uses two auxiliary locations, $2001$ and $2002$.

$\square$
