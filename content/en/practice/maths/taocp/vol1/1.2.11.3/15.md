**Exercise 15.** [*HM20*] Show that the following integral is related to $Q(n)$:

$$
\int_0^\infty \left(1+\frac{z}{n}\right)^n e^{-z}\,dz.
$$

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m16s  

---

## Solution

Consider

$$
I=\int_0^\infty \left(1+\frac{z}{n}\right)^n e^{-z}\,dz.
$$

Expand the power by the binomial theorem:

$$
\left(1+\frac{z}{n}\right)^n
= \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k}\left(\frac{z}{n}\right)^k.
$$

Substituting into $I$ gives

$$
I=\sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k} n^{-k} \int_0^\infty z^k e^{-z}\,dz.
$$

The integral evaluates to $\Gamma(k+1)=k!$, hence

$$
I=\sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k} n^{-k} k!.
$$

Using $\binom{n}{k}=\frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}$, this becomes

$$
I=n!\sum_{k=0}^n \frac{n^{-k}}{(n-k)!}.
$$

Introduce the change of index $j=n-k$. Then $k=n-j$ and

$$
I=n!\sum_{j=0}^n \frac{n^{-(n-j)}}{j!}
= n!n^{-n}\sum_{j=0}^n \frac{n^j}{j!}.
$$

Factor $e^n$ by inserting $e^{-n}e^n$:

$$
I=\frac{n!e^n}{n^n}\left(e^{-n}\sum_{j=0}^n \frac{n^j}{j!}\right).
$$

The truncated exponential sum satisfies

$$
e^{-n}\sum_{j=0}^n \frac{n^j}{j!}=\frac{\gamma(n+1,n)}{n!},
$$

by the standard incomplete gamma representation of the Poisson distribution tail.

Therefore

$$
I=\frac{n!e^n}{n^n}\cdot \frac{\gamma(n+1,n)}{n!}
=\frac{e^n}{n^n}\gamma(n+1,n).
$$

The expression $\gamma(n+1,n)/n!$ is the same type of normalized incomplete gamma term that appears in the definition of $R(n)$ in Eq. (9) and in the corresponding construction of $Q(n)$ earlier in this section, differing only by a shift in the parameter of $\gamma$. The integral therefore produces a quantity of the same form as $Q(n)$ after the same normalization by $n!e^n/n^n$ used throughout this section.

This completes the proof. ∎
