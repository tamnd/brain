[Section 1.2.11.3: Some Asymptotic Calculations](../)

**Exercise 19.** [*HM30*] (Watson's lemma.) Show that if the integral

$$
C_n = \int_0^\infty e^{-nx} f(x)\,dx
$$

exists for all large $n$, and if $f(x)=O(x^\alpha)$ for $0 \le x \le r$, where $r>0$ and $\alpha>-1$, then

$$
C_n = O(n^{-1-\alpha}).
$$

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m02s  

---

Write

$$
C_n=\int_0^r e^{-nx}f(x)\,dx+\int_r^\infty e^{-nx}f(x)\,dx
= A_n+B_n.
$$

### 1. Estimate of $A_n$

Assume $|f(x)|\le Kx^\alpha$ for $0\le x\le r$, with $\alpha>-1$. Then

$$
|A_n|\le K\int_0^r e^{-nx}x^\alpha\,dx.
$$

With $u=nx$,

$$
\int_0^r e^{-nx}x^\alpha\,dx
=n^{-1-\alpha}\int_0^{nr} e^{-u}u^\alpha\,du
\le n^{-1-\alpha}\int_0^\infty e^{-u}u^\alpha\,du
= \Gamma(\alpha+1)n^{-1-\alpha}.
$$

Hence

$$
A_n = O(n^{-1-\alpha}).
$$

### 2. Estimate of $B_n$ without absolute integrability

Fix $N$ such that $C_N$ exists, i.e. the improper integral

$$
\int_0^\infty e^{-Nx}f(x)\,dx
$$

converges (not necessarily absolutely).

Define

$$
H(x)=e^{-Nx}f(x), \qquad G(t)=\int_r^t H(x)\,dx.
$$

Then $G(t)$ converges as $t\to\infty$, hence $G$ is bounded on $[r,\infty)$. Write

$$
B_n=\int_r^\infty e^{-nx}f(x)\,dx
=\int_r^\infty e^{-(n-N)x}H(x)\,dx.
$$

Let

$$
g_n(x)=e^{-(n-N)x},
$$

which is positive, decreasing, and tends to $0$.

Apply Abel–Dirichlet (integration by parts for Stieltjes integrals):

$$
\int_r^\infty g_n(x)\,dG(x)
= -g_n(r)G(r)-\int_r^\infty G(x)\,dg_n(x).
$$

Since $G$ is bounded, say $|G(x)|\le M$, and $g_n$ is decreasing,

$$
\left|\int_r^\infty g_n\,dG\right|
\le M g_n(r) + M\int_r^\infty (-dg_n(x)).
$$

But

$$
\int_r^\infty (-dg_n(x)) = g_n(r),
$$

so

$$
|B_n|\le 2M e^{-(n-N)r}.
$$

Thus

$$
B_n = O(e^{-nr}).
$$

### 3. Comparison with a power

For any $m>0$,

$$
e^{-nr} = O(n^{-m}).
$$

Taking $m=1+\alpha>0$,

$$
B_n = O(n^{-1-\alpha}).
$$

### 4. Conclusion

Combining $C_n=A_n+B_n$,

$$
C_n = O(n^{-1-\alpha}).
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
