**Exercise 5.** [*HM24*] Show that $R$ in Eq. (21) is $O(n^n e^{-n})$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m45s  

---

The errors in the previous solution stem from treating the Euler–Maclaurin remainder and the size of $f^{(3)}$ in a purely global way, and from introducing an unsupported “cancellation” step. A correct approach uses a local analysis of $f(x)=x^{n+\frac12}e^{-x}$ near its saddle point.

## 1. Proper control of the Euler–Maclaurin remainder

After two correction terms, the Euler–Maclaurin formula gives a remainder of the form

$$
R = \int_{0}^{n} \widetilde B_3(x)\, f^{(3)}(x)\,dx,
$$

where $\widetilde B_3(x)$ is a bounded periodic Bernoulli function. Hence there is a constant $C$ such that

$$
|R| \le C \int_0^n |f^{(3)}(x)|\,dx.
$$

Thus it suffices to bound the integral of $|f^{(3)}|$.

## 2. Localization of $f(x)$

Let

$$
\phi(x)=\log f(x)=\left(n+\tfrac12\right)\log x - x.
$$

Then

$$
\phi'(x)=\frac{n+\frac12}{x}-1,
\quad \text{so the maximum occurs at } x_0=n+\tfrac12.
$$

Also,

$$
\phi''(x)= -\frac{n+\frac12}{x^2},
\quad \text{so } \phi''(x_0)= -\frac{1}{n+O(1)}.
$$

Hence for $x=n+t\sqrt{n}$ with $|t|\le n^{1/6}$, a Taylor expansion gives

$$
\phi(x)=\phi(x_0) - \frac{t^2}{2} + O\!\left(\frac{|t|^3}{\sqrt{n}}\right).
$$

Therefore, uniformly in this range,

$$
f(x)=f(x_0)\, e^{-t^2/2 + O(n^{-1/6})}
= O\!\left(f(n)\, e^{-t^2/3}\right),
$$

since $f(x_0)\sim f(n)$.

This shows $f(x)$ is effectively supported in a window of width $O(\sqrt{n})$, and satisfies

$$
\int_0^n f(x)\,dx = O\!\big(f(n)\sqrt{n}\big).
$$

## 3. Size of derivatives

Differentiate

$$
f(x)=e^{\phi(x)}.
$$

Each derivative has the structure

$$
f^{(k)}(x)=f(x)\,P_k\!\big(\phi'(x),\phi''(x),\dots,\phi^{(k)}(x)\big),
$$

where $P_k$ is a polynomial with bounded coefficients.

On the central region $x=n+O(\sqrt{n})$,

$$
\phi'(x)=O\!\left(\frac{x-x_0}{n}\right)=O(n^{-1/2}),
\quad
\phi''(x)=O(n^{-1}),
\quad
\phi^{(j)}(x)=O(n^{-j+1}).
$$

Hence each derivative contributes a factor $n^{-1/2}$ at leading order, giving

$$
|f^{(3)}(x)| \le C\, n^{-3/2}\, f(x)
\quad \text{for } |x-n|=O(\sqrt{n}).
$$

Outside this region, $f(x)$ decays exponentially, so the same bound remains valid after adjusting constants.

## 4. Integrating the remainder

Using the localization of $f$,

$$
\int_0^n |f^{(3)}(x)|\,dx
\le C n^{-3/2} \int_0^n f(x)\,dx.
$$

From Section 2,

$$
\int_0^n f(x)\,dx = O\!\big(f(n)\sqrt{n}\big).
$$

Thus,

$$
\int_0^n |f^{(3)}(x)|\,dx
= O\!\left(n^{-3/2}\cdot f(n)\sqrt{n}\right)
= O\!\left(\frac{f(n)}{n}\right).
$$

Therefore,

$$
R = O\!\left(\frac{f(n)}{n}\right).
$$

## 5. Final simplification

Since

$$
f(n)=n^{n+\frac12}e^{-n},
$$

we obtain

$$
R = O\!\left(n^{n-\frac12}e^{-n}\right).
$$

In particular,

$$
R = O\!\left(n^n e^{-n}\right).
$$

## Conclusion

The remainder is smaller than $n^n e^{-n}$ by a factor of $n^{1/2}$, and this follows from a local saddle-point analysis combined with the Euler–Maclaurin integral remainder formula.
