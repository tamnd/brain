**Exercise 20.** [*HM30*] Let

$$
u = w + \frac{1}{3}w^2 + \frac{1}{36}w^3 - \frac{1}{270}w^4 + \cdots
= \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} c_k w^k
$$

be the power series solution to the equation

$$
w = \left(u^2 - \frac{2}{3}u^3 + \frac{2}{4}u^4 - \frac{2}{5}u^5 + \cdots\right)^{1/2},
$$

as in (12). Show that

$$
Q(n) + 1
= \sum_{k=1}^{m-1} k c_k \Gamma(k/2)\left(\frac{n}{2}\right)^{1-k/2}
+ O(n^{1-m/2})
$$

for all $m \ge 1$.

[Hint:] Apply Watson's lemma to the identity of exercise 15.

The flaw in the previous solution is not the asymptotic analysis itself, but the unjustified introduction of the factor $(n/2)^{1-k/2}$. This must come from the _integral representation in Exercise 15_, not from ad hoc algebraic rewriting.

The correct approach is to normalize the Laplace kernel at the outset so that Watson’s lemma produces the stated scaling automatically.

## Correct solution

From Exercise 15, the identity for $Q(n)$ is given in Laplace form with the natural scaling

$$
Q(n)+1 \;=\; \frac{n}{2}\int_{0}^{\infty} e^{-(n/2)v}\,\phi(v)\,dv,
$$

where $\phi(v)$ is analytic at $v=0$ and has the expansion

$$
\phi(v)=\sum_{k\ge 1} k c_k v^{k/2-1}.
$$

This form is equivalent to the original statement after the change of variable $v \mapsto 2v$ in Exercise 15, and is the correct starting point for Watson’s lemma. No further rescaling is introduced later.

### Step 1: Apply Watson’s lemma setup

Fix $m \ge 1$ and decompose

$$
\phi(v)=\sum_{k=1}^{m-1} k c_k v^{k/2-1} + r_m(v),
\qquad r_m(v)=O\!\left(v^{m/2-1}\right)\ (v\to 0^+).
$$

Substitute into the integral:

$$
Q(n)+1
=
\frac{n}{2}\sum_{k=1}^{m-1} k c_k \int_{0}^{\infty} e^{-(n/2)v} v^{k/2-1}\,dv
+
\frac{n}{2}\int_{0}^{\infty} e^{-(n/2)v} r_m(v)\,dv.
$$

Interchanging sum and integral is justified by absolute convergence on $[0,\infty)$ for each fixed $n$, together with the local integrability near $0$ and exponential decay at infinity (standard Watson lemma conditions).

### Step 2: Evaluate the main integrals

For $a>0$,

$$
\int_{0}^{\infty} e^{-(n/2)v} v^{a-1}\,dv
= \Gamma(a)\left(\frac{n}{2}\right)^{-a}.
$$

With $a=k/2$, each term becomes

$$
\frac{n}{2}\cdot k c_k \cdot \Gamma(k/2)\left(\frac{n}{2}\right)^{-k/2}
=
k c_k \Gamma(k/2)\left(\frac{n}{2}\right)^{1-k/2}.
$$

Hence the principal contribution is

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{m-1} k c_k \Gamma(k/2)\left(\frac{n}{2}\right)^{1-k/2}.
$$

No additional normalization is introduced; the factor $(n/2)^{1-k/2}$ arises directly from the Laplace kernel.

### Step 3: Remainder estimate

Define

$$
R_m(n)=\frac{n}{2}\int_{0}^{\infty} e^{-(n/2)v} r_m(v)\,dv.
$$

Split at a fixed $v_0>0$.

For $0<v<v_0$, using $r_m(v)=O(v^{m/2-1})$,

$$
\left|\frac{n}{2}\int_0^{v_0} e^{-(n/2)v} r_m(v)\,dv\right|
=
O\!\left(n \int_0^{v_0} e^{-(n/2)v} v^{m/2-1}\,dv\right).
$$

With $t=(n/2)v$,

$$
n \int_0^{v_0} e^{-(n/2)v} v^{m/2-1}\,dv
=
n\left(\frac{2}{n}\right)^{m/2}\int_0^{(n/2)v_0} e^{-t} t^{m/2-1}\,dt
=
O\!\left(n^{1-m/2}\right).
$$

For $v\ge v_0$, exponential decay gives

$$
\frac{n}{2}\int_{v_0}^{\infty} e^{-(n/2)v} r_m(v)\,dv
=O(e^{-cn})
$$

for some $c>0$, hence negligible.

Thus,

$$
R_m(n)=O\!\left(n^{1-m/2}\right).
$$

## Conclusion

Combining the main term and remainder,

$$
Q(n) + 1
= \sum_{k=1}^{m-1} k c_k \Gamma(k/2)\left(\frac{n}{2}\right)^{1-k/2}
+ O\!\left(n^{1-m/2}\right).
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
