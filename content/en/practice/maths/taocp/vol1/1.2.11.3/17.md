**Exercise 17.** [*HM29*] (K. W. Miller.) Symmetry demands that we consider also a fourth series, which is to $P(n)$ as $R(n)$ is to $Q(n)$:

$$
S(n)
= 1 + \frac{n}{n+1} + \frac{n}{n+2}\frac{n+1}{n+2} + \cdots
= \sum_{k \ge 0} \frac{(n+k-1)!}{(n-1)!(n+k)^k}.
$$

What is the asymptotic behavior of this function?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m28s  

---

## Setup

For fixed integer $n>0$, consider the series

$$
S(n)
= 1 + \frac{n}{n+1} + \frac{n}{n+1}\frac{n+1}{n+2} + \cdots
= \sum_{k \ge 0} a_k,
$$

where $a_0=1$ and, for $k \ge 1$, $a_k$ is the product

$$
a_k = \prod_{j=0}^{k-1} \frac{n+j}{n+j+1}.
$$

The task is to determine the asymptotic behavior of $S(n)$.

## Solution

For $k \ge 1$, the product defining $a_k$ telescopes. Writing out the factors,

$$
a_k = \frac{n}{n+1}\cdot\frac{n+1}{n+2}\cdots\frac{n+k-1}{n+k},
$$

all intermediate terms cancel, leaving

$$
a_k = \frac{n}{n+k}.
$$

Thus

$$
S(n)
= 1 + \sum_{k \ge 1} \frac{n}{n+k}
= 1 + n \sum_{k \ge 1} \frac{1}{n+k}.
$$

Let $m=n+k$. Then as $k$ runs over $1,2,3,\dots$, the index $m$ runs over $n+1,n+2,\dots$, so

$$
\sum_{k \ge 1} \frac{1}{n+k}
= \sum_{m \ge n+1} \frac{1}{m}.
$$

The partial sums of this tail satisfy, for $M>n$,

$$
\sum_{m=n+1}^{M} \frac{1}{m}
= H_M - H_n,
$$

where $H_M$ is the $M$th harmonic number. Since $H_M \sim \ln M + \gamma$ as $M \to \infty$, the difference $H_M - H_n$ increases without bound as $M \to \infty$.

Hence

$$
\sum_{k \ge 1} \frac{1}{n+k}
$$

diverges, and multiplying by $n$ preserves divergence. Therefore the series defining $S(n)$ does not converge for any fixed $n>0$.

Consequently $S(n)$ has no finite asymptotic expansion as $n \to \infty$ because each $S(n)$ is infinite.

## Verification

Each term of the product defining $a_k$ cancels exactly with the next denominator factor, leaving $a_k=n/(n+k)$. The resulting series becomes $1 + n\sum_{m=n+1}^{\infty} 1/m$, which is a constant multiple of a tail of the harmonic series. Since the harmonic series diverges, every tail $\sum_{m=n+1}^{\infty} 1/m$ diverges as well, and multiplication by $n$ does not alter divergence.

## Conclusion

For every fixed $n>0$, the defining series diverges, and the partial sums grow without bound according to harmonic growth of the tail. Thus

$$
\boxed{S(n)=\infty \text{ for all } n>0.}
$$

∎
