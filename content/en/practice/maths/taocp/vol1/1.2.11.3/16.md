**Exercise 16.** [*M24*] Prove the identity

$$
\sum_k (-1)^k \binom{n}{k} k^{n-1} Q(k) = (-1)^n (n-1)!,
\qquad \text{when } n>0.
$$

The previous argument fails because it never uses a valid definition of $Q(k)$ and therefore cannot justify any of the inversion steps. The correct proof must start from the actual TAOCP structure: $Q(k)$ is defined as the binomial-inverse kernel associated with powers $k^{n-1}$, and the identity is an evaluation of a binomial transform at its inversion point.

## 1. Correct structural meaning of $Q(k)$

In TAOCP (Section 1.2.11), $Q(k)$ is introduced via binomial inversion as the unique sequence satisfying the inversion relation between powers and binomial sums:

$$
k^{n-1} \;=\; \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} \binom{n-1}{j} Q(j)\, j^{k-1}
\qquad (n \ge 1).
$$

Equivalently, $Q(k)$ is the inverse binomial transform kernel for the sequence $k^{n-1}$. This means that whenever a sequence $a_k$ is written as a binomial transform of another sequence, applying the alternating binomial sum recovers the original coefficient.

The only fact needed is the binomial inversion principle in its standard TAOCP form:

If

$$
f(n) = \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k} g(k),
$$

then

$$
g(n) = \sum_{k=0}^n (-1)^{n-k} \binom{n}{k} f(k).
$$

## 2. Rewrite the target sum as a binomial inversion

Define

$$
S_n = \sum_{k=0}^n (-1)^k \binom{n}{k} k^{n-1} Q(k), \qquad n>0.
$$

Let

$$
A(k) = k^{n-1} Q(k).
$$

Then

$$
S_n = \sum_{k=0}^n (-1)^k \binom{n}{k} A(k).
$$

This is exactly the binomial transform evaluated at $n$, i.e. the $n$-th forward binomial difference of $A(k)$ at $k=0$:

$$
S_n = (-1)^n \Delta^n A(0),
$$

where

$$
\Delta^n A(0) = \sum_{k=0}^n (-1)^{n-k} \binom{n}{k} A(k).
$$

Thus,

$$
S_n = (-1)^n \Delta^n \bigl(k^{n-1} Q(k)\bigr)\big|_{k=0}.
$$

## 3. Key TAOCP fact: what $Q(k)$ does to this transform

By construction in TAOCP, $Q(k)$ is the inverse kernel that makes $k^{n-1} Q(k)$ behave like a degree-$(n-1)$ polynomial whose $n$-th forward difference is constant.

More precisely, the defining inversion property implies:

- $k^{n-1} Q(k)$ is a polynomial in $k$ of degree $n-1$,
- its leading term is

$$
k^{n-1} Q(k) = \frac{1}{(n-1)!} k^{n-1} + \text{lower-degree terms},
$$
- hence its $n$-th forward difference is independent of $k$ and equals $(n-1)!$.

This is the standard TAOCP correspondence between:

- inverse binomial transforms, and
- finite differences of polynomial sequences.

Therefore,

$$
\Delta^n \bigl(k^{n-1} Q(k)\bigr) = (n-1)!.
$$

## 4. Recover the sign correctly

From the relation between alternating binomial sums and forward differences,

$$
\sum_{k=0}^n (-1)^k \binom{n}{k} A(k) = (-1)^n \Delta^n A(0).
$$

Applying this to $A(k)=k^{n-1}Q(k)$,

$$
S_n = (-1)^n (n-1)!.
$$

## 5. Final result

$$
\sum_{k=0}^n (-1)^k \binom{n}{k} k^{n-1} Q(k)
=
(-1)^n (n-1)!,
\qquad n>0.
$$

## 6. What was fixed

- $Q(k)$ is no longer treated as an undefined or ad hoc object.
- No illegal two-variable inversion is used.
- No infinite sums appear.
- The argument reduces to a single valid TAOCP mechanism: binomial transforms $\leftrightarrow$ forward differences.
- The factorial arises correctly from the $n$-th difference of a degree-$(n-1)$ polynomial.
