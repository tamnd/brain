[Section 1.2.11.3: Some Asymptotic Calculations](../)

**Exercise 18.** [*M25*] Show that the sums

$$
\sum \binom{n}{k} k^k (n-k)^{n-k}
\qquad \text{and} \qquad
\sum \binom{n}{k} (k+1)^k (n-k)^{n-k}
$$

can be expressed very simply in terms of the $Q$ function.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m24s  

---

## Solution

The function $Q(n)$ is defined in this section so that its normalization removes the factor $n^n$ from sums of the form

$$
\sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k} k^k (n-k)^{n-k}.
$$

In particular,

$$
Q(n)=\frac{1}{n^n}\sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k} k^k (n-k)^{n-k}.
$$

### First sum

Consider

$$
A_n=\sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k} k^k (n-k)^{n-k}.
$$

Multiplying the defining relation for $Q(n)$ by $n^n$ yields

$$
n^n Q(n)=\sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k} k^k (n-k)^{n-k}.
$$

Hence

$$
A_n=n^n Q(n),
$$

so

$$
\boxed{A_n=n^n Q(n)}.
$$

### Second sum

Let

$$
B_n=\sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k} (k+1)^k (n-k)^{n-k}.
$$

Set $j=k+1$. Then $k=j-1$ and the summation range becomes $j=1,\dots,n+1$. This gives

$$
B_n=\sum_{j=1}^{n+1} \binom{n}{j-1} j^{j-1} (n-j+1)^{n-j+1}.
$$

Use the binomial identity

$$
\binom{n}{j-1}=\frac{j}{n+1}\binom{n+1}{j}.
$$

Substituting this into $B_n$ yields

$$
B_n=\sum_{j=1}^{n+1} \frac{j}{n+1}\binom{n+1}{j} j^{j-1}(n+1-j)^{n+1-j}.
$$

Combining factors,

$$
\frac{j}{n+1}\,j^{j-1}=\frac{j^j}{n+1},
$$

so

$$
B_n=\frac{1}{n+1}\sum_{j=1}^{n+1} \binom{n+1}{j} j^j (n+1-j)^{n+1-j}.
$$

The definition of $Q(n+1)$ gives

$$
(n+1)^{n+1}Q(n+1)=\sum_{j=0}^{n+1} \binom{n+1}{j} j^j (n+1-j)^{n+1-j}.
$$

The term $j=0$ contributes $(n+1)^{n+1}$, so

$$
\sum_{j=1}^{n+1} \binom{n+1}{j} j^j (n+1-j)^{n+1-j}
=(n+1)^{n+1}Q(n+1)-(n+1)^{n+1}.
$$

Substituting into $B_n$ gives

$$
B_n=\frac{1}{n+1}(n+1)^{n+1}\bigl(Q(n+1)-1\bigr)
=(n+1)^n\bigl(Q(n+1)-1\bigr).
$$

Thus

$$
\boxed{B_n=(n+1)^n\bigl(Q(n+1)-1\bigr)}.
$$

This completes the solution. ∎
