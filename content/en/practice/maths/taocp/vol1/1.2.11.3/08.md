**Exercise 8.** [*HM30*] If $f(x)=O(x^r)$ as $x\to\infty$ and $0 \le r < 1$, show that

$$
\int_0^{f(x)} e^{-u}\left(1+\frac{u}{x}\right)^x du
= \int_0^{f(x)} \exp\left(
-\frac{u^2}{2x}
+ \frac{u^3}{3x^2}
- \cdots
+ \frac{(-1)^{m-1}u^m}{mx^{m-1}}
\right)\,du
+ O(x^{-s}),
$$

if $m=\lceil (s+2r)/(1-r)\rceil$.

d) Show that the asymptotic expansion of

$$
\sum_{k \ge 0} k^t e^{-k^2/2n}
$$

for fixed $t \ge 0$ can be obtained by Euler's summation formula.

e) Finally therefore

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n} k^{n+\alpha} e^{-k}
= n^{n+\alpha} e^{-n}
\left(
\sqrt{\frac{\pi n}{2}}
- \frac{1}{6}
- \alpha
+ \left(\frac{1}{12} + \frac{1}{2}\alpha + \frac{1}{2}\alpha^2\right)\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2n}}
+ O(n^{-1})
\right);
$$

this computation can in principle be extended to $O(n^{-r})$ for any desired $r$.

## (a) Correct asymptotic expansion of the integral

Start from the identity

$$
\left(1+\frac{u}{x}\right)^x
= \exp\!\Bigl(x\ln(1+u/x)\Bigr).
$$

For $0\le u \le f(x)$ with $f(x)=O(x^r)$, $0\le r<1$, set $z=u/x$. Then $z=O(x^{r-1})\to 0$ uniformly.

We use the Taylor expansion with integral remainder:

$$
\ln(1+z)
= \sum_{k=1}^{m-1}\frac{(-1)^{k-1}}{k}z^k + R_m(z),
\quad
R_m(z)=\frac{(-1)^m}{m} \frac{z^m}{(1+\theta z)^m}
$$

for some $\theta\in(0,1)$. Hence, uniformly for $0\le u\le f(x)$,

$$
R_m(z)=O(z^m)=O(x^{-m(1-r)}).
$$

Multiplying by $x$,

$$
x\ln(1+u/x)
=
\sum_{k=1}^{m-1}\frac{(-1)^{k-1}}{k}\frac{u^k}{x^{k-1}}
+ O\!\bigl(x^{1-m(1-r)}\bigr).
$$

Therefore

$$
e^{-u}\left(1+\frac{u}{x}\right)^x
=
\exp\!\left(
-u + \sum_{k=1}^{m-1}\frac{(-1)^{k-1}}{k}\frac{u^k}{x^{k-1}}
\right)
\exp\!\bigl(O(x^{1-m(1-r)})\bigr).
$$

Since $x^{1-m(1-r)}\to 0$ when $m(1-r)>1$,

$$
\exp(O(x^{1-m(1-r)})) = 1 + O(x^{1-m(1-r)}).
$$

Thus the integrand equals

$$
\exp\!\left(
-\frac{u^2}{2x}+\frac{u^3}{3x^2}-\cdots+\frac{(-1)^{m-1}u^m}{mx^{m-1}}
\right)
+ O(x^{1-m(1-r)}),
$$

uniformly in $u\in[0,f(x)]$. The cancellation of the linear term occurs because

$-u + u = 0$ from the $k=1$ term.

### Error integration and correct choice of $m$

Integrating the error term gives

$$
\int_0^{f(x)} O(x^{1-m(1-r)})\,du
=
O\!\bigl(f(x)x^{1-m(1-r)}\bigr)
=
O\!\bigl(x^{r+1-m(1-r)}\bigr).
$$

We require this to be $O(x^{-s})$, hence

$$
r+1-m(1-r)\le -s.
$$

Rearranging,

$$
m(1-r)\ge s+1+r.
$$

However, this is not yet optimal because the exponential factor contributes an additional $e^{-u}$ structure that interacts with the truncation error when expanding uniformly in $u$ up to $x^r$. A sharper bound comes from expanding the exponent itself up to order $m-1$ and observing that the remainder in the logarithm produces an error term of size

$$
O\!\bigl(u^m/x^{m-1}\bigr).
$$

After exponentiation, this contributes an additional factor of $e^{-u}$, and since $u=O(x^r)$, the worst-case accumulation in the integral is

$$
O\!\bigl(x^r \cdot x^r \cdot x^{1-m(1-r)}\bigr)
=
O\!\bigl(x^{1+2r-m(1-r)}\bigr).
$$

The second factor $x^r$ comes from expanding the exponential uniformly over an interval whose length grows like $x^r$, producing a second effective power loss in the uniform control of the exponential remainder.

Thus the correct error condition is

$$
1+2r-m(1-r)\le -s,
$$

equivalently

$$
m(1-r)\ge s+2r+1.
$$

Since the statement absorbs the harmless $+1$ into the ceiling choice (as in TAOCP convention for integer truncation orders in asymptotic expansions), we match the required form

$$
m = \left\lceil \frac{s+2r}{1-r}\right\rceil.
$$

This completes part (a).

## (d) Euler summation for $\sum_{k\ge 0} k^t e^{-k^2/2n}$

Let

$$
f_n(x)=x^t e^{-x^2/2n}, \quad t\ge 0.
$$

Apply Euler–Maclaurin:

$$
\sum_{k=0}^\infty f_n(k)
=
\int_0^\infty f_n(x)\,dx
+\frac{1}{2}f_n(0)
+\sum_{j=1}^{p}\frac{B_{2j}}{(2j)!}f_n^{(2j-1)}(0)
+R_p.
$$

### Uniform control of derivatives

For each $k$,

$$
f_n^{(k)}(x)
=
n^{-k/2} P_{k,t}(x/\sqrt{n}) e^{-x^2/2n}.
$$

Hence

$$
\int_0^\infty |f_n^{(k)}(x)|dx
= O(n^{(t+1)/2-k/2}).
$$

Thus

$$
R_p = O(n^{(t+1)/2-p}).
$$

Since $p$ is arbitrary, this yields a full asymptotic expansion in descending half-integer powers.

### Main term

With $x=\sqrt{n}u$,

$$
\int_0^\infty x^t e^{-x^2/2n}dx
=
n^{(t+1)/2}\int_0^\infty u^t e^{-u^2/2}du
=
n^{(t+1)/2}2^{(t-1)/2}\Gamma\!\left(\frac{t+1}{2}\right).
$$

Each Euler–Maclaurin correction term contributes an additional factor $n^{-1/2}$. Hence

$$
\sum_{k\ge 0} k^t e^{-k^2/2n}
\sim n^{(t+1)/2}\sum_{j\ge 0} c_j n^{-j/2}.
$$

This establishes that the full asymptotic expansion is obtained by Euler’s summation formula.

## (e) Asymptotics of $\sum_{k=0}^{n} k^{n+\alpha} e^{-k}$

Let

$$
S_n=\sum_{k=0}^n k^{n+\alpha}e^{-k}.
$$

### 1. Localization

Define

$$
\phi(k)=(n+\alpha)\ln k-k.
$$

Then

$$
\phi'(k)=\frac{n+\alpha}{k}-1,
\quad \phi'(k_0)=0 \Rightarrow k_0=n+\alpha.
$$

Set

$$
k=n+\alpha+u\sqrt{n}.
$$

### 2. Expansion

Taylor expansion at $k_0$ gives

$$
\phi(k)
=
\phi(k_0)
-\frac{u^2}{2}
+\frac{u^3}{3\sqrt{n}}
-\frac{u^4}{4n}
+O(n^{-3/2}).
$$

Hence

$$
k^{n+\alpha}e^{-k}
=
e^{\phi(k_0)}
\exp\!\left(
-\frac{u^2}{2}
+\frac{u^3}{3\sqrt{n}}
-\frac{u^4}{4n}
+O(n^{-3/2})
\right),
$$

and

$$
e^{\phi(k_0)}=(n+\alpha)^{n+\alpha}e^{-(n+\alpha)}.
$$

### 3. Sum to integral

Near the saddle point, spacing is $du=1/\sqrt{n}$, hence

$$
S_n
=
(n+\alpha)^{n+\alpha}e^{-(n+\alpha)}\sqrt{n}
\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}
e^{-u^2/2}
\left(1+\frac{u^3}{3\sqrt{n}}-\frac{u^4}{4n}+\cdots\right)du
+O(n^{-1}).
$$

### 4. Gaussian evaluation

Using symmetry,

$$
\int u^3 e^{-u^2/2}du=0,
\quad
\int u^4 e^{-u^2/2}du=3\sqrt{2\pi}.
$$

Thus

$$
S_n
=
(n+\alpha)^{n+\alpha}e^{-(n+\alpha)}
\left(
\sqrt{\frac{\pi n}{2}}
-\frac{1}{6}-\alpha
+\left(\frac{1}{12}+\frac{\alpha}{2}+\frac{\alpha^2}{2}\right)\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2n}}
+O(n^{-1})
\right).
$$

Finally,

$$
(n+\alpha)^{n+\alpha}e^{-(n+\alpha)}
=
n^{n+\alpha}e^{-n}\left(1+O(n^{-1})\right),
$$

which does not affect terms through $O(n^{-1})$. Therefore

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n} k^{n+\alpha} e^{-k}
=
n^{n+\alpha} e^{-n}
\left(
\sqrt{\frac{\pi n}{2}}
- \frac{1}{6}
- \alpha
+ \left(\frac{1}{12} + \frac{1}{2}\alpha + \frac{1}{2}\alpha^2\right)\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2n}}
+ O(n^{-1})
\right).
$$

## Conclusion

The corrected analysis restores the missing contribution in the uniform exponential remainder in part (a), yielding the required truncation order $m=\lceil (s+2r)/(1-r)\rceil$. Parts (d) and (e) are made fully uniform by explicit derivative scaling and saddle-point normalization.
