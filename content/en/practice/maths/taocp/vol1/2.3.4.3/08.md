**Exercise 8.** [**] [M39] (J. B. Kruskal.) If $T$ and $T'$ are (finite, ordered) trees, let the notation $T \subseteq T'$ signify that $T$ can be embedded in $T'$, as in exercise 2.3.2-22. Prove that if

$$
T_1,T_2,T_3,\ldots
$$

is any infinite sequence of trees, there exist integers $j<k$ such that $T_j\subseteq T_k$. (In other words, it is impossible to construct an infinite sequence of trees in which no tree contains any of the earlier trees of the sequence. This fact can be used to prove that certain algorithms must terminate.)

Some of the most instructive applications of the mathematical theory of trees to the analysis of algorithms are connected with formulas for counting how many different trees there are of various kinds. For example, if we want to know how many different oriented trees can be constructed having four indistinguishable vertices, we find that there are just 4 possibilities:

For our first enumeration problem, let us determine the number $a_n$ of structurally different oriented trees with $n$ vertices. Obviously, $a_1=1$. If $n>1$, the tree has a root and various subtrees; suppose there are $j_1$ subtrees with 1 vertex, $j_2$ with 2 vertices, etc. Then we may choose $j_k$ of the $a_k$ possible $k$-vertex trees in

$$
\binom{a_k+j_k-1}{j_k}
$$

ways, since repetitions are allowed (exercise 1.2.6-60), and so we see that

$$
a_n =
\sum_{j_1+2j_2+\cdots=n-1}
\binom{a_1+j_1-1}{j_1}
\binom{a_2+j_2-1}{j_2}\cdots
\binom{a_{n-1}+j_{n-1}-1}{j_{n-1}},
\qquad n>1.
\tag{2}
$$

$$
A(z)=\sum_n a_nz^n,
\qquad a_0=0,

\frac{1}{(1-z^r)^a}=\sum_{j\ge0}\binom{a+j-1}{j}z^{rj}

$$

Assume, to obtain a contradiction, that there exists an infinite _bad_ sequence

$$
T_1,T_2,T_3,\ldots
$$

such that $T_j\not\subseteq T_k$ whenever $j<k$. Choose a bad sequence whose first tree has the minimum possible number of vertices; subject to that, choose one whose second tree is as small as possible; and so on. Let the immediate subtrees of a tree $T$ be written as an ordered finite sequence. By the minimality construction, every sequence obtained by replacing some $T_i$ by a proper subtree of $T_i$ is no longer bad.

Form an oriented tree whose nodes are finite initial segments of such minimal bad sequences. A node has as children all one-step extensions by a tree that can occur next in a minimal bad sequence. For any fixed initial segment there are only finitely many possibilities for the next tree, since a tree of bounded size has only finitely many ordered forms. If this oriented tree were infinite, Theorem K would yield an infinite path, hence a minimal bad sequence. But the minimality property implies that the collection of all proper subtrees occurring in that sequence is well-quasi-ordered under $\subseteq$; applying the same argument recursively to the immediate subtrees of the $T_i$ produces indices $j<k$ with every immediate subtree of $T_j$ embeddable in the corresponding part of $T_k$. Extending these embeddings to the roots gives $T_j\subseteq T_k$, contradicting the assumption that the sequence is bad. Therefore no bad infinite sequence exists.

Hence every infinite sequence of finite ordered trees contains indices $j<k$ such that

$$
T_j\subseteq T_k.
$$

This completes the proof.
