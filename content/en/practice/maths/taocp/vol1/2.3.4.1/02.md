[Section 2.3.4.1: Free Trees](../)

**Exercise 2.** [**] $$M20$$ Prove that if $V$ and $V'$ are vertices of a graph and if there is a walk from $V$ to $V'$, then there is a (simple) path from $V$ to $V'$.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 4m49s  

---

Let $(V_0,V_1,\ldots,V_n)$ be a walk from $V$ to $V'$. Among all walks from $V$ to $V'$, choose one of minimum length. If some vertex occurred twice in this walk, say $V_i=V_j$ with $0\le i<j\le n$, then the portion $(V_i,V_{i+1},\ldots,V_j)$ would be a closed walk. Deleting this portion would produce a shorter walk

$$
(V_0,\ldots,V_i,V_{j+1},\ldots,V_n)
$$

from $V$ to $V'$, because $V_i=V_j$ and $V_j$ is adjacent to $V_{j+1}$ when $j<n$. This contradicts the minimality of the chosen walk.

Therefore no vertex appears more than once in a minimum-length walk. Hence the vertices $V_0,V_1,\ldots,V_n$ are distinct, and by definition $(V_0,V_1,\ldots,V_n)$ is a path from $V$ to $V'$. This completes the proof.
