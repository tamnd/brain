**Exercise 9.** [**] $$M22$$ Edges $e_{13}$ and $e_{19}$ were split into two parts in Fig. 32, since a graph is not supposed to have two edges joining the same two vertices. However, if we look at the final result of the construction, this splitting into two parts seems quite artificial since $E'_{13}=E''_{13}$ and $E'_{19}=E''_{19}$ are two of the relations found in (6), while $E''_{13}$ and $E''_{19}$ are two of the independent variables. Explain how the construction could be generalized so that an artificial splitting of edges may be avoided.

The construction extends naturally to a multigraph, where several edges may join the same pair of vertices and loops are also permitted. A free subtree $G'$ is chosen exactly as before. For every edge $e$ not belonging to $G'$, adjoin $e$ to $G'$ and form the unique cycle consisting of $e$ together with the unique simple path in $G'$ between the endpoints of $e$, as in the argument preceding equation (3).

If two distinct edges, such as $e'_{13}$ and $e''_{13}$, join the same pair of vertices, and if one of them belongs to $G'$ while the other does not, the corresponding fundamental cycle consists simply of those two parallel edges traversed in opposite directions. The cycle therefore yields the relation $E'_{13}=E''_{13}$. Similarly, if a loop occurs at a vertex, the loop itself is a fundamental cycle. Thus the theory of fundamental cycles, the independence statement (4), and the uniqueness statement (5) remain valid without introducing artificial vertices. The splitting of $e_{13}$ and $e_{19}$ in Fig. 32 merely converts a multigraph into an ordinary graph; it is not essential to the method.
