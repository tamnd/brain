[Section 1.2.6: Binomial Coefficients](../)

**Exercise 38.** [*HM30*] (C. Ramus, 1834.) Generalizing the result of the preceding exercise, show that we have the formula

$$
\binom{n}{k} + \binom{n}{m+k} + \binom{n}{2m+k} + \cdots
= \frac{1}{m}\sum_{0 \le j < m}\left(2\cos\frac{j\pi}{m}\right)^n
\cos\frac{j(n-2k)\pi}{m}.
$$

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m35s  

---

## Setup

Let $n,m,k$ be integers with $m \ge 1$. Define

$$
S := \binom{n}{k} + \binom{n}{m+k} + \binom{n}{2m+k} + \cdots,
$$

where the sum ranges over all integers $r \in {0,\dots,n}$ such that $r \equiv k \pmod m$.

The goal is to prove

$$
S
= \frac{1}{m}\sum_{0 \le j < m}\left(2\cos\frac{j\pi}{m}\right)^n
\cos\frac{j(n-2k)\pi}{m}.
$$

## Solution

Let $\omega = e^{2\pi i/m}$. The root-of-unity filter identity

$$
\frac{1}{m}\sum_{j=0}^{m-1} \omega^{j(r-k)} =
\begin{cases}
1, & r \equiv k \pmod m,\\
0, & r \not\equiv k \pmod m
\end{cases}
$$

holds for all integers $r$, since it is a finite geometric sum.

Multiplying by $\binom{n}{r}$ and summing over $r$ gives

$$
S = \sum_{r=0}^{n} \binom{n}{r} \cdot \frac{1}{m}\sum_{j=0}^{m-1}\omega^{j(r-k)}.
$$

Interchanging sums yields

$$
S = \frac{1}{m}\sum_{j=0}^{m-1}\omega^{-jk}\sum_{r=0}^{n}\binom{n}{r}\omega^{jr}.
$$

By the binomial theorem (13),

$$
\sum_{r=0}^{n}\binom{n}{r}\omega^{jr} = (1+\omega^j)^n.
$$

Hence

$$
S = \frac{1}{m}\sum_{j=0}^{m-1}\omega^{-jk}(1+\omega^j)^n.
$$

Now write $\omega^j = e^{2\pi i j/m}$. Then

$$
1+\omega^j
= e^{\pi i j/m}\left(e^{-\pi i j/m}+e^{\pi i j/m}\right)
= 2e^{\pi i j/m}\cos\frac{j\pi}{m}.
$$

Raising to the $n$th power gives

$$
(1+\omega^j)^n
= \left(2\cos\frac{j\pi}{m}\right)^n e^{\pi i jn/m}.
$$

Also

$$
\omega^{-jk} = e^{-2\pi i jk/m}.
$$

Thus

$$
\omega^{-jk}(1+\omega^j)^n
=
\left(2\cos\frac{j\pi}{m}\right)^n
e^{\pi i j(n-2k)/m}.
$$

Therefore

$$
S = \frac{1}{m}\sum_{j=0}^{m-1}
\left(2\cos\frac{j\pi}{m}\right)^n
e^{\pi i j(n-2k)/m}.
$$

The left-hand side $S$ is real, so the imaginary part of the right-hand side vanishes. Pairing terms $j$ and $m-j$ shows that each non-real exponential term cancels its conjugate, leaving only real parts:

$$
e^{\pi i j(n-2k)/m} = \cos\frac{j(n-2k)\pi}{m} + i\sin\frac{j(n-2k)\pi}{m}.
$$

Taking real parts yields

$$
S
= \frac{1}{m}\sum_{j=0}^{m-1}
\left(2\cos\frac{j\pi}{m}\right)^n
\cos\frac{j(n-2k)\pi}{m}.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification

The root-of-unity filter contributes $1$ exactly for indices $r \equiv k \pmod m$ and $0$ otherwise, since the inner geometric sum equals $m$ when $\omega^{r-k}=1$ and equals $0$ otherwise.

The binomial theorem applies directly because $(1+\omega^j)^n$ expands as $\sum_{r=0}^n \binom{n}{r}\omega^{jr}$.

The identity

$$
1+e^{i\theta} = 2e^{i\theta/2}\cos(\theta/2)
$$

with $\theta = 2\pi j/m$ yields the factorization of $(1+\omega^j)^n$ into magnitude and phase.

Complex conjugate pairing of terms $j$ and $m-j$ ensures the imaginary parts cancel because cosine is even and sine is odd.

## Notes

The result is a discrete Fourier projection of binomial coefficients onto a residue class modulo $m$. The cosine expression arises from rewriting the exponential form of roots of unity into real trigonometric form. A more general form replaces cosine by complex exponentials and yields the same identity without taking real parts.
