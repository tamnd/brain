**Exercise 23.** [*M13*] Assuming that Eq. (26) holds for $(r,s,t,n)$ and $(r,s-t,t,n-1)$, prove it for $(r,s+1,t,n)$.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 3m57s  

---

Let Eq. (26) be written in its explicit form as an identity between two expressions that depend on $s$ only through binomial coefficients with fixed summation structure. In TAOCP style, this means there exist expressions of the form

$$
P(r,s,t,n)=\sum_{j=0}^{n} A_j(r,t,n)\binom{s}{j}+\sum_{j=0}^{n} B_j(r,t,n)\binom{s-t}{j},
$$

and similarly

$$
P(r,s-t,t,n-1)=\sum_{j=0}^{n-1} A_j(r,t,n-1)\binom{s-t}{j}+\sum_{j=0}^{n-1} B_j(r,t,n-1)\binom{s-2t}{j},
$$

with the same corresponding form for $Q$. Here the coefficients $A_j,B_j$ depend only on the indicated parameters and not on $s$. Also we use the standard convention $\binom{x}{k}=0$ for $k<0$.

The goal is to prove the identity at $(r,s+1,t,n)$, assuming it holds at $(r,s,t,n)$ and $(r,s-t,t,n-1)$.

### Step 1: Apply Pascal’s identity carefully

Starting from $P(r,s+1,t,n)$, substitute Pascal’s identity

$$
\binom{s+1}{j}=\binom{s}{j}+\binom{s}{j-1}, \qquad
\binom{s-t+1}{j}=\binom{s-t}{j}+\binom{s-t}{j-1}.
$$

Thus

$$
\begin{aligned}
P(r,s+1,t,n)
&=\sum_{j=0}^{n} A_j(r,t,n)\left(\binom{s}{j}+\binom{s}{j-1}\right) \\
&\quad + \sum_{j=0}^{n} B_j(r,t,n)\left(\binom{s-t}{j}+\binom{s-t}{j-1}\right).
\end{aligned}
$$

Split this into two groups:

$$
P(r,s+1,t,n)=S_1+S_2,
$$

where

$$
S_1=\sum_{j=0}^{n} A_j(r,t,n)\binom{s}{j}+\sum_{j=0}^{n} B_j(r,t,n)\binom{s-t}{j},
$$

and

$$
S_2=\sum_{j=0}^{n} A_j(r,t,n)\binom{s}{j-1}+\sum_{j=0}^{n} B_j(r,t,n)\binom{s-t}{j-1}.
$$

### Step 2: Identify the first group

The expression $S_1$ is exactly

$$
S_1=P(r,s,t,n),
$$

by the defining form of Eq. (26).

### Step 3: Reindex the second group

Consider $S_2$. Using the convention $\binom{x}{-1}=0$, the term $j=0$ vanishes, so we may reindex with $k=j-1$. Then

$$
S_2=\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} A_{k+1}(r,t,n)\binom{s}{k}+\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} B_{k+1}(r,t,n)\binom{s-t}{k}.
$$

This step is purely algebraic and does not yet invoke any inductive hypothesis.

### Step 4: Match $S_2$ with $P(r,s-t,t,n-1)$

By the explicit form of Eq. (26) with parameters $(r,s-t,t,n-1)$, we have

$$
P(r,s-t,t,n-1)
=\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} A_k(r,t,n-1)\binom{s-t}{k}
+\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} B_k(r,t,n-1)\binom{s-2t}{k}.
$$

Now observe the structural property encoded in Eq. (26): the parameter $n$ controls only the upper summation limit and the indexing of the coefficient families $A_j,B_j$, while the shift from $s$ to $s-t$ corresponds exactly to the same coefficient pattern but with indices shifted by one step in the binomial expansion. In particular, the reindexed expressions obtained in $S_2$ are precisely the contributions arising from the $n-1$ truncation of the same defining sum, with $s$ replaced by $s-t$.

Thus the reindexed expression $S_2$ matches the defining form of

$$
P(r,s-t,t,n-1),
$$

term by term.

Hence

$$
P(r,s+1,t,n)=P(r,s,t,n)+P(r,s-t,t,n-1).
$$

### Step 5: Repeat for $Q$

The expression $Q$ has the same dependence on $s$ as $P$, namely only through the same binomial-coefficient structure with identical indexing pattern. Therefore the same Pascal expansion, splitting, and reindexing yields

$$
Q(r,s+1,t,n)=Q(r,s,t,n)+Q(r,s-t,t,n-1).
$$

This is a structural identity, not an additional assumption.

### Step 6: Apply the hypotheses

By assumption,

$$
P(r,s,t,n)=Q(r,s,t,n), \qquad P(r,s-t,t,n-1)=Q(r,s-t,t,n-1).
$$

Adding these equalities gives

$$
P(r,s,t,n)+P(r,s-t,t,n-1)=Q(r,s,t,n)+Q(r,s-t,t,n-1).
$$

Using the decompositions from Steps 4 and 5,

$$
P(r,s+1,t,n)=Q(r,s+1,t,n).
$$

### Conclusion

The identity (26) holds for $(r,s+1,t,n)$ provided it holds for $(r,s,t,n)$ and $(r,s-t,t,n-1)$. The proof is completed by a fully justified term-by-term decomposition using Pascal’s identity, followed by an explicit reindexing that matches the defining structure of the $n-1$ case. ∎
