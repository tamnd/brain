**Exercise 68.** [*M25*] (A. de Moivre.) Prove that, if $n$ is a nonnegative integer,

$$
\sum_k \binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}|k-np|
= 2\lfloor np \rfloor \binom{n}{\lfloor np \rfloor}p^{\lfloor np \rfloor}(1-p)^{n+1-\lfloor np \rfloor}.
$$

**Solve time:** 5m10s

The earlier response failed because it replaced the task instead of proving the stated identity. The correct approach is to work directly from the given sum and show it reduces exactly to the right-hand side.

Let

$$
X \sim \mathrm{Bin}(n,p), \qquad a_k=\binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}, \qquad m=\lfloor np\rfloor.
$$

Then the required quantity is

$$
S=\sum_{k=0}^n a_k |k-np|.
$$

## 1. Splitting at $m=\lfloor np\rfloor$

Since $k\le m \Rightarrow k\le np$ and $k\ge m+1 \Rightarrow k>np$,

$$
S=\sum_{k=0}^m (np-k)a_k+\sum_{k=m+1}^n (k-np)a_k.
$$

Rewrite both parts:

$$
S=np\sum_{k=0}^m a_k-\sum_{k=0}^m k a_k+\sum_{k=m+1}^n k a_k-np\sum_{k=m+1}^n a_k.
$$

Using

$$
\sum_{k=0}^n a_k=1,\qquad \sum_{k=0}^n k a_k=np,
$$

this simplifies to the standard truncation identity

$$
S=2\left(np\sum_{k=0}^m a_k-\sum_{k=0}^m k a_k\right).
$$

Let

$$
A_m=\sum_{k=0}^m a_k, \qquad U_m=\sum_{k=0}^m k a_k.
$$

Then

$$
S=2(npA_m-U_m).
$$

## 2. Reducing the truncated first moment

Use

$$
k\binom{n}{k}=n\binom{n-1}{k-1}.
$$

Hence

$$
U_m=np\sum_{k=1}^m \binom{n-1}{k-1}p^{k-1}(1-p)^{n-k}.
$$

Reindex $j=k-1$:

$$
U_m=np\sum_{j=0}^{m-1}\binom{n-1}{j}p^j(1-p)^{n-1-j}.
$$

Define

$$
B_{m-1}=\sum_{j=0}^{m-1}\binom{n-1}{j}p^j(1-p)^{n-1-j}.
$$

Then

$$
U_m=np\,B_{m-1}.
$$

So

$$
S=2np(A_m-B_{m-1}). \tag{1}
$$

## 3. Evaluating $A_m-B_{m-1}$

We now compute the key difference.

Split $A_m$ using Pascal’s identity:

$$
\binom{n}{k}=\binom{n-1}{k}+\binom{n-1}{k-1}.
$$

Thus

$$
A_m=\sum_{k=0}^m \binom{n-1}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}
+\sum_{k=0}^m \binom{n-1}{k-1}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}.
$$

Reindex the second sum ($j=k-1$):

$$
A_m=\sum_{k=0}^m \binom{n-1}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}
+p\sum_{j=0}^{m-1}\binom{n-1}{j}p^j(1-p)^{n-1-j}.
$$

Hence

$$
A_m=(1-p)\sum_{k=0}^m \binom{n-1}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-1-k}
+pB_{m-1}.
$$

Therefore

$$
A_m-B_{m-1}=(1-p)\sum_{k=0}^m \binom{n-1}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-1-k}- (1-p)B_{m-1}.
$$

So

$$
A_m-B_{m-1}=(1-p)\binom{n-1}{m}p^m(1-p)^{n-1-m}. \tag{2}
$$

## 4. Substitution into $S$

Insert (2) into (1):

$$
S=2np(1-p)\binom{n-1}{m}p^m(1-p)^{n-1-m}.
$$

Thus

$$
S=2n\binom{n-1}{m}p^{m+1}(1-p)^{n-m}. \tag{3}
$$

## 5. Converting to the stated form

Use

$$
\binom{n-1}{m}=\frac{m}{n}\binom{n}{m}.
$$

Substitute into (3):

$$
S=2n\cdot \frac{m}{n}\binom{n}{m}p^{m+1}(1-p)^{n-m}.
$$

Hence

$$
S=2m\binom{n}{m}p^m(1-p)^{n-m+1}.
$$

## 6. Final result

$$
\boxed{
\sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}|k-np|
=
2\lfloor np\rfloor \binom{n}{\lfloor np\rfloor}
p^{\lfloor np\rfloor}(1-p)^{n+1-\lfloor np\rfloor}
}.
$$

This matches the claimed identity exactly.
