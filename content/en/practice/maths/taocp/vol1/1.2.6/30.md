**Exercise 30.** [*M24*] Show that there is a better way to solve Example 3 than the way used in the text, by manipulating the sum so that Eq. (26) applies.

Let Example 3 define

$$
S=\sum_{k} \binom{r}{k}\binom{s}{m+k},
$$

where the sum is over all integers $k$ for which the binomial coefficients are defined, i.e., $0\le k\le r$ and $0\le m+k\le s$.

The goal is to rewrite $S$ into the Vandermonde form required by Eq. (26):

$$
\sum_{k} \binom{\alpha}{k}\binom{\beta}{n-k}=\binom{\alpha+\beta}{n}.
$$

### Step 1: Shift the index to align one binomial coefficient

Let

$$
j=m+k \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad k=j-m.
$$

As $k$ runs over all integers, $j$ runs over those integers satisfying

$$
m \le j \le m+r.
$$

Substituting into $S$,

$$
S=\sum_{j=m}^{m+r} \binom{r}{j-m}\binom{s}{j}.
$$

### Step 2: Convert the first binomial coefficient into complementary form

Use the symmetry identity

$$
\binom{r}{j-m}=\binom{r}{r-(j-m)}=\binom{r}{r+m-j}.
$$

This identity is valid for all admissible $j$ since $0\le j-m\le r$ implies $0\le r+m-j\le r$.

Hence,

$$
S=\sum_{j=m}^{m+r} \binom{r}{r+m-j}\binom{s}{j}.
$$

### Step 3: Reindex to obtain complementary summation indices

Let

$$
k = r+m-j.
$$

Then

$$
j = r+m-k.
$$

When $j=m$, $k=r$.

When $j=m+r$, $k=0$.

Thus the sum becomes

$$
S=\sum_{k=0}^{r} \binom{r}{k}\binom{s}{r+m-k}.
$$

### Step 4: Match Vandermonde form

Now the summand has the structure

$$
\binom{r}{k}\binom{s}{(r+m)-k}.
$$

This is exactly of the form required by Eq. (26) with

$$
\alpha=r,\quad \beta=s,\quad n=r+m.
$$

Therefore, by Vandermonde’s identity (Eq. (26)),

$$
S=\binom{r+s}{r+m}.
$$

### Conclusion

By a direct index shift followed by a single symmetry transformation, the sum from Example 3 is converted exactly into Vandermonde convolution form, allowing Eq. (26) to be applied immediately without ad hoc manipulation of indices or informal rearrangements.

$$
\boxed{S=\binom{r+s}{r+m}}.
$$
