**Exercise 21.** [*M05*] Both sides of Eq. (25) are polynomials in $s$; why isn't that equation an identity in $s$?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m14s  

---

Both sides of Eq. (25) are polynomials in $s$, but the derivation of Eq. (25) establishes equality only under the restrictions imposed earlier in the section, namely for integer values of $s$ (and with the additional constraints on the other parameters present in the preceding equations). Equality of two polynomials at those admissible integer values does not by itself force equality of the polynomials for all real $s$, since a polynomial identity requires agreement for all real arguments, or equivalently agreement at infinitely many points with no restriction to a finite or constrained subset.

Since Eq. (25) is obtained through identities that are valid only under those integer restrictions, the equality holds on that restricted domain but does not automatically extend to all real $s$. Therefore the statement is not an identity in $s$, even though both sides are polynomial functions of $s$. ∎
