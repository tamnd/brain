[Section 1.2.6: Binomial Coefficients](../)

**Exercise 34.** [*M23*] Show that Abel's generalization, Eq. (16), of the binomial formula is true also for rising powers:

$$
(x+y)^{\overline{n}}
= \sum_k \binom{n}{k}x(x-kz+1)^{\overline{k-1}}(y+kz)^{\overline{n-k}}.
$$

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 4m34s  

---

Let

$$
R_n(x,y)=\sum_{k} \binom{n}{k}\,x\,(x-kz+1)^{\overline{k-1}}(y+kz)^{\overline{n-k}}.
$$

We prove

$$
R_n(x,y)=(x+y)^{\overline{n}}
$$

by induction on $n$. To avoid incorrect manipulations, we first remove the misleading algebra involving products of rising factorials.

## Step 1: Normalize the expression

Factor out $x$:

$$
R_n(x,y)=x\,T_n(x,y),
$$

where

$$
T_n(x,y)=\sum_k \binom{n}{k}(x-kz+1)^{\overline{k-1}}(y+kz)^{\overline{n-k}}.
$$

The identity to prove becomes

$$
(x+y)^{\overline{n}} = x\,T_n(x,y).
$$

Since

$$
(x+y)^{\overline{n+1}}=(x+y+n)(x+y)^{\overline{n}},
$$

it is enough to prove the equivalent recurrence

$$
T_{n+1}(x,y)=(x+y+n)\,T_n(x,y).
$$

## Step 2: Induction base

For $n=0$,

$$
T_0(x,y)= (x+1)^{\overline{-1}}(y)^{\overline{0}}=1,
$$

so both sides equal $1$. The statement holds.

## Step 3: Induction step

Assume

$$
T_n(x,y)=\sum_k \binom{n}{k}(x-kz+1)^{\overline{k-1}}(y+kz)^{\overline{n-k}}.
$$

We compute $(x+y+n)T_n(x,y)$:

$$
(x+y+n)T_n=\sum_k \binom{n}{k}(x-kz+1)^{\overline{k-1}}(y+kz)^{\overline{n-k}}(x+y+n).
$$

### Key correct decomposition

For each $k$, use the identity

$$
x+y+n = (x-kz+1 + k-1) + (y+kz + n-k).
$$

This identity is exact:

$$
(x-kz+1 + k-1) + (y+kz+n-k)
= x+y+n.
$$

We now split the sum into two parts.

## Step 4: First part (increasing the $x$-rising factorial)

Consider

$$
(x-kz+1 + k-1)(x-kz+1)^{\overline{k-1}}.
$$

By the defining property of rising factorials,

$$
a^{\overline{m}}(a+m)=a^{\overline{m+1}},
$$

with $a=x-kz+1$ and $m=k-1$, we get

$$
(x-kz+1)^{\overline{k-1}}(x-kz+1+k-1)
=(x-kz+1)^{\overline{k}}.
$$

Thus the first contribution becomes

$$
\sum_k \binom{n}{k}(x-kz+1)^{\overline{k}}(y+kz)^{\overline{n-k}}.
$$

Now reindex with $j=k+1$. Then $k=j-1$, and this term becomes

$$
\sum_j \binom{n}{j-1}(x-(j-1)z+1)^{\overline{j-1}}(y+(j-1)z)^{\overline{n-j+1}}.
$$

## Step 5: Second part (increasing the $y$-rising factorial)

Consider

$$
(y+kz+n-k)(y+kz)^{\overline{n-k}}.
$$

Using

$$
a^{\overline{m}}(a+m)=a^{\overline{m+1}},
$$

with $a=y+kz$, $m=n-k$, we obtain

$$
(y+kz)^{\overline{n-k+1}}.
$$

Thus the second contribution is

$$
\sum_k \binom{n}{k}(x-kz+1)^{\overline{k-1}}(y+kz)^{\overline{n-k+1}}.
$$

## Step 6: Combine and use binomial identities

We now combine:

- the reindexed first sum (shifted to $j=k+1$),
- the second sum (kept at index $k$).

Rename $j\to k$ in the first sum. We obtain two contributions to the coefficient of

$$
(x-kz+1)^{\overline{k-1}}(y+kz)^{\overline{n+1-k}}.
$$

They come from:

### (i) First sum

$$
\binom{n}{k-1}
$$

### (ii) Second sum

$$
\binom{n}{k}
$$

Hence the total coefficient is

$$
\binom{n}{k}+\binom{n}{k-1}=\binom{n+1}{k}.
$$

Boundary terms vanish because for $k=0$ or $k=n+1$, one of the binomial coefficients is zero and rising factorials of negative order do not appear.

## Step 7: Conclusion

We obtain

$$
T_{n+1}(x,y)=\sum_k \binom{n+1}{k}(x-kz+1)^{\overline{k-1}}(y+kz)^{\overline{n+1-k}}.
$$

Multiplying by $x$ restores the original form:

$$
(x+y)^{\overline{n+1}}
=
\sum_k \binom{n+1}{k}x(x-kz+1)^{\overline{k-1}}(y+kz)^{\overline{n+1-k}}.
$$

Thus the identity holds for $n+1$, completing the induction.

## Final conclusion

By induction, Abel’s generalization for rising powers is valid:

$$
(x+y)^{\overline{n}}
= \sum_k \binom{n}{k}x(x-kz+1)^{\overline{k-1}}(y+kz)^{\overline{n-k}}.
$$

∎
