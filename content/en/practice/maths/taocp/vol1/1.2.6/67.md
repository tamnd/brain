[Section 1.2.6: Binomial Coefficients](../)

**Exercise 67.** [*M20*] Prove the upper bound

$$
\binom{n}{k} \le \left(\frac{ne}{k}\right)^k,
\qquad n \ge k \ge 0.
$$

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m23s  

---

## Solution

For $k = 0$, the binomial coefficient satisfies $\binom{n}{0} = 1$. The right-hand expression is interpreted as $1$ since $\left(\frac{ne}{k}\right)^k$ equals $1$ at $k=0$ by the limiting convention $a^0 = 1$ for $a \ne 0$. Thus the inequality holds when $k = 0$.

Assume $n \ge k \ge 1$. From the factorial representation (5),

$$
\binom{n}{k} = \frac{n(n-1)\cdots(n-k+1)}{k!}.
$$

Each factor in the numerator satisfies $n - j \le n$ for $0 \le j \le k-1$, hence

$$
n(n-1)\cdots(n-k+1) \le n^k,
$$

so

$$
\binom{n}{k} \le \frac{n^k}{k!}.
$$

A lower bound for $k!$ is obtained from the exponential series. From

$$
e^k = \sum_{j=0}^{\infty} \frac{k^j}{j!},
$$

the term with $j = k$ gives

$$
e^k \ge \frac{k^k}{k!}.
$$

Rearranging yields

$$
k! \ge \frac{k^k}{e^k} = \left(\frac{k}{e}\right)^k.
$$

Substituting this into the previous inequality,

$$
\binom{n}{k} \le \frac{n^k}{(k/e)^k} = \left(\frac{ne}{k}\right)^k.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
