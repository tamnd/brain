[Section 1.2.6: Binomial Coefficients](../)

**Exercise 58.** [*M23*] In the notation of Eq. (40), prove the $q$-nomial theorem:

$$
(1+x)(1+qx)\cdots(1+q^{n-1}x)
= \sum_k \binom{n}{k}_q q^{k(k-1)/2}x^k.
$$

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m10s  

---

## Solution

Let

$$
P_n(x)=\prod_{j=0}^{n-1}(1+q^j x),
\qquad n\ge 0.
$$

Let

$$
S_n(x)=\sum_{k\ge 0}\binom{n}{k}_q q^{k(k-1)/2}x^k.
$$

The proof proceeds by showing that $P_n(x)=S_n(x)$ for all integers $n\ge 0$.

For $n=0$,

$$
P_0(x)=1,
\qquad
S_0(x)=\binom{0}{0}_q q^0 x^0=1,
$$

since $\binom{0}{0}_q=1$ and all other terms vanish by definition. Hence $P_0(x)=S_0(x)$.

Assume $P_{n-1}(x)=S_{n-1}(x)$ for some $n\ge 1$. Then

$$
P_n(x)=P_{n-1}(x)(1+q^{n-1}x).
$$

Substituting the induction hypothesis,

$$
P_n(x)=\left(\sum_{k\ge 0}\binom{n-1}{k}_q q^{k(k-1)/2}x^k\right)
+\left(\sum_{k\ge 0}\binom{n-1}{k}_q q^{k(k-1)/2+n-1}x^{k+1}\right).
$$

In the second sum, replace $k$ by $k-1$:

$$
\sum_{k\ge 1}\binom{n-1}{k-1}_q q^{(k-1)(k-2)/2+n-1}x^k.
$$

Thus the coefficient of $x^k$ in $P_n(x)$ equals

$$
\binom{n-1}{k}_q q^{k(k-1)/2}
+
\binom{n-1}{k-1}_q q^{(k-1)(k-2)/2+n-1}.
$$

Factor $q^{k(k-1)/2}$ from the second term by rewriting the exponent:

$$
(k-1)(k-2)/2+n-1
=
k(k-1)/2 + (n-k).
$$

Hence the coefficient becomes

$$
q^{k(k-1)/2}\left(
\binom{n-1}{k}_q
+
q^{n-k}\binom{n-1}{k-1}_q
\right).
$$

By Eq. (40), the $q$-binomial coefficients satisfy the recurrence

$$
\binom{n}{k}_q=\binom{n-1}{k}_q+q^{n-k}\binom{n-1}{k-1}_q.
$$

Therefore the coefficient of $x^k$ in $P_n(x)$ equals

$$
\binom{n}{k}_q q^{k(k-1)/2}.
$$

This matches the coefficient of $x^k$ in $S_n(x)$, so $P_n(x)=S_n(x)$.

This completes the proof. ∎
