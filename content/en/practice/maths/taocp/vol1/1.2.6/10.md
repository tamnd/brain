[Section 1.2.6: Binomial Coefficients](../)

**Exercise 10.** [*M25*] If $p$ is prime, show that:

1. $\binom{n}{p} \equiv \lfloor n/p \rfloor \pmod p$.
2. $\binom{p}{k} \equiv 0 \pmod p$, for $1 \le k \le p-1$.
3. $\binom{p-1}{k} \equiv (-1)^k \pmod p$, for $0 \le k \le p-1$.
4. $\binom{p+1}{k} \equiv 0 \pmod p$, for $2 \le k \le p-1$.
5. (E. Lucas, 1877.)

$$
\binom{n}{k}
\equiv \binom{\lfloor n/p \rfloor}{\lfloor k/p \rfloor}
\binom{n \bmod p}{k \bmod p}
\pmod p.
$$

6. If the $p$-ary number system representations of $n$ and $k$ are

$$
n = a_rp^r + \cdots + a_1p + a_0,\qquad
k = b_rp^r + \cdots + b_1p + b_0,
$$

then

$$
\binom{n}{k}
\equiv \binom{a_r}{b_r}\cdots\binom{a_1}{b_1}\binom{a_0}{b_0}
\pmod p.
$$

**Verified:** yes

## Solution

Let $p$ be prime. All congruences are taken modulo $p$.

### (2) $\binom{p}{k} \equiv 0$ for $1 \le k \le p-1$

From Eq. (7),

$$
\binom{p}{k} = \frac{p}{k}\binom{p-1}{k-1}.
$$

For $1 \le k \le p-1$, the integer $k$ is not divisible by $p$, hence $k$ is invertible modulo $p$. The factor $p$ in the numerator implies every such binomial coefficient is divisible by $p$, so

$$
\binom{p}{k} \equiv 0.
$$

### (1) $\binom{n}{p} \equiv \lfloor n/p \rfloor$

Write $n = qp + r$ with integers $q \ge 0$ and $0 \le r \le p-1$.

From the binomial theorem (13),

$$
(1+x)^p \equiv 1 + x^p.
$$

Raising to the $q$-th power gives

$$
(1+x)^{qp} \equiv (1+x^p)^q.
$$

Multiplying by $(1+x)^r$ yields

$$
(1+x)^n \equiv (1+x)^r (1+x^p)^q.
$$

The coefficient of $x^p$ on the right-hand side is obtained in two ways.

From $(1+x^p)^q$, choosing $x^p$ from one factor contributes $q$ terms, each contributing $1$ from $(1+x)^r$ at degree $0$, giving $q$.

Any term using a positive power from $(1+x)^r$ would require $x^{p-t}$ from $(1+x^p)^q$ with $1 \le t \le p-1$, which is impossible since powers in $(1+x^p)^q$ are multiples of $p$. The remaining contribution would involve a coefficient from $(1+x^r)$ of degree $p$, which equals $\binom{r}{p}=0$ because $r<p$.

Hence the coefficient of $x^p$ in $(1+x)^n$ equals $q$, so

$$
\binom{n}{p} \equiv q = \lfloor n/p \rfloor.
$$

### (3) $\binom{p-1}{k} \equiv (-1)^k$ for $0 \le k \le p-1$

From (13),

$$
(1+x)^p \equiv 1 + x^p.
$$

Thus, in the ring of formal power series modulo $p$,

$$
(1+x)^{p-1} \equiv \frac{1+x^p}{1+x}.
$$

For powers $x^k$ with $0 \le k \le p-1$, the term $x^p$ in the numerator does not contribute, so coefficients up to degree $p-1$ agree with those of

$$
\frac{1}{1+x} = \sum_{k \ge 0} (-1)^k x^k.
$$

Hence, for $0 \le k \le p-1$,

$$
\binom{p-1}{k} \equiv (-1)^k.
$$

### (4) $\binom{p+1}{k} \equiv 0$ for $2 \le k \le p-1$

Using the addition formula (9),

$$
\binom{p+1}{k} = \binom{p}{k} + \binom{p}{k-1}.
$$

For $2 \le k \le p-1$, both indices satisfy $1 \le k, k-1 \le p-1$, hence both terms are congruent to $0$ by (2). Therefore,

$$
\binom{p+1}{k} \equiv 0.
$$

### (5) Lucas congruence for one digit

Write

$$
n = qp + r,\qquad k = sp + t,
$$

with $0 \le r,t \le p-1$.

From the reduction

$$
(1+x)^n = (1+x)^{qp+r} \equiv (1+x^p)^q (1+x)^r,
$$

the coefficient of $x^{sp+t}$ is formed by selecting $x^{sp}$ from $(1+x^p)^q$ and $x^t$ from $(1+x)^r$.

The coefficient of $x^{sp}$ in $(1+x^p)^q$ equals $\binom{q}{s}$ since each choice of $s$ factors contributes $x^{sp}$. The coefficient of $x^t$ in $(1+x)^r$ equals $\binom{r}{t}$.

All other decompositions produce powers incompatible with the structure of $(1+x^p)^q$, hence contribute multiples of $p$ and vanish modulo $p$.

Therefore,

$$
\binom{n}{k} \equiv \binom{q}{s}\binom{r}{t}
= \binom{\lfloor n/p \rfloor}{\lfloor k/p \rfloor}
\binom{n \bmod p}{k \bmod p}.
$$

### (6) Base-$p$ product formula

Let

$$
n = a_r p^r + \cdots + a_1 p + a_0,\qquad
k = b_r p^r + \cdots + b_1 p + b_0,
$$

with $0 \le a_i, b_i \le p-1$.

Repeated application of (5) decomposes $(n,k)$ digit by digit. At each stage, the coefficient splits into a product of contributions from the highest base-$p$ digit and the remaining lower digits. Iterating this decomposition $r$ times yields

$$
\binom{n}{k} \equiv \prod_{i=0}^{r} \binom{a_i}{b_i}.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
