**Exercise 15.** [*M15*] Prove the binomial formula, Eq. (13).

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m07s  

---

## Solution

Induction on $r$ is used.

For $r=0$, $(x+y)^0 = 1$. From definition (3), $\binom{0}{0}=1$ and $\binom{0}{k}=0$ for $k \ne 0$. Hence

$$
\sum_k \binom{0}{k} x^k y^{-k} = \binom{0}{0} x^0 y^0 = 1,
$$

so the formula holds for $r=0$.

Assume that for a fixed integer $r \ge 0$,

$$
(x+y)^r = \sum_k \binom{r}{k} x^k y^{r-k}.
$$

Multiplication by $(x+y)$ gives

$$
(x+y)^{r+1} = \left(\sum_k \binom{r}{k} x^k y^{r-k}\right)(x+y).
$$

Distributing,

$$
(x+y)^{r+1}
= \sum_k \binom{r}{k} x^{k+1} y^{r-k}
+ \sum_k \binom{r}{k} x^k y^{r+1-k}.
$$

In the first sum, replace $k$ by $k-1$, so $k$ runs from $1$ to $r+1$:

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{r+1} \binom{r}{k-1} x^k y^{r+1-k}.
$$

The second sum is already indexed by $k$:

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{r} \binom{r}{k} x^k y^{r+1-k}.
$$

Both expressions are combined into a single sum over $k=0$ to $r+1$:

$$
(x+y)^{r+1}
= \sum_{k=0}^{r+1} \left(\binom{r}{k} + \binom{r}{k-1}\right)x^k y^{r+1-k},
$$

where $\binom{r}{-1}=0$ and $\binom{r}{r+1}=0$.

Using equation (9),

$$
\binom{r}{k} + \binom{r}{k-1} = \binom{r+1}{k}.
$$

Substitution yields

$$
(x+y)^{r+1} = \sum_k \binom{r+1}{k} x^k y^{r+1-k}.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
