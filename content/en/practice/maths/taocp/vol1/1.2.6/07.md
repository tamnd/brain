[Section 1.2.6: Binomial Coefficients](../)

**Exercise 7.** [*12*] If $n$ is a fixed positive integer, what value of $k$ makes $\binom{n}{k}$ a maximum?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m12s  

---

## Solution

Let $n$ be a fixed positive integer and consider the sequence $\binom{n}{k}$ for integers $k$, $0 \le k \le n$.

From Eq. (7) in Section 1.2.6,

$$
\binom{n}{k+1} = \frac{n-k}{k+1}\binom{n}{k},
\qquad 0 \le k < n.
$$

Hence the ratio

$$
\frac{\binom{n}{k+1}}{\binom{n}{k}} = \frac{n-k}{k+1}
$$

determines whether the sequence increases or decreases.

The inequality

$$
\frac{n-k}{k+1} \ge 1
$$

is equivalent to

$$
n-k \ge k+1 \;\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\; n \ge 2k+1 \;\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\; k \le \frac{n-1}{2}.
$$

Thus $\binom{n}{k+1} \ge \binom{n}{k}$ exactly when $k \le (n-1)/2$, and the reverse inequality holds when $k \ge (n-1)/2$.

Now distinguish the parity of $n$.

If $n = 2m$, then $k \le (n-1)/2 = m - \tfrac{1}{2}$ is equivalent to $k \le m-1$. For $0 \le k \le m-1$,

$$
\binom{n}{k+1} > \binom{n}{k},
$$

so the sequence is strictly increasing up to $k=m$. For $k=m$,

$$
\frac{\binom{n}{m+1}}{\binom{n}{m}} = \frac{2m-m}{m+1} = \frac{m}{m+1} < 1,
$$

so the sequence strictly decreases after $k=m$. Therefore $\binom{n}{k}$ attains its maximum uniquely at

$$
k = m = \frac{n}{2},
$$

and the maximum value is $\binom{n}{n/2}$.

If $n = 2m+1$, then $k \le (n-1)/2 = m$ gives

$$
\binom{n}{k+1} \ge \binom{n}{k} \quad \text{for } 0 \le k \le m.
$$

For $k=m$,

$$
\frac{\binom{n}{m+1}}{\binom{n}{m}} = \frac{2m+1-m}{m+1} = \frac{m+1}{m+1} = 1,
$$

so $\binom{n}{m+1} = \binom{n}{m}$. For $k \ge m+1$, the ratio $\frac{n-k}{k+1} < 1$, so the sequence strictly decreases thereafter.

Thus the maximum value is attained at two adjacent indices,

$$
k = m \quad \text{and} \quad k = m+1,
$$

and these give equal values.

In summary, the maximum of $\binom{n}{k}$ occurs at $k = \lfloor n/2 \rfloor$, and if $n$ is odd also at $k = \lceil n/2 \rceil$. This completes the proof. ∎
