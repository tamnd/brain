**Exercise 29.** [*M20*] Show that Eq. (34) is just a special case of the general identity proved in exercise 1.2.3--33.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m18s  

---

## Solution

Let Eq. (34) denote the identity

$$
\sum_k \binom{r}{k}\binom{s}{n-k} = \binom{r+s}{n},
$$

with the usual convention that terms with invalid binomial coefficients vanish.

From the binomial theorem,

$$
(1+x)^r = \sum_k \binom{r}{k} x^k,
\qquad
(1+x)^s = \sum_k \binom{s}{k} x^k.
$$

Multiplying these series gives

$$
(1+x)^r(1+x)^s
= \left(\sum_i \binom{r}{i} x^i\right)\left(\sum_j \binom{s}{j} x^j\right)
= \sum_n \left(\sum_k \binom{r}{k}\binom{s}{n-k}\right)x^n.
$$

On the other hand,

$$
(1+x)^r(1+x)^s = (1+x)^{r+s}
= \sum_n \binom{r+s}{n} x^n.
$$

Equality of formal power series implies equality of coefficients of $x^n$, hence

$$
\sum_k \binom{r}{k}\binom{s}{n-k} = \binom{r+s}{n},
$$

which is Eq. (34).

The general identity of Exercise 1.2.3, 33 asserts that the coefficient of $x^n$ in a product of two power series is the convolution of the coefficient sequences. Applying this identity to the series expansions of $(1+x)^r$ and $(1+x)^s$ yields exactly the left-hand side above, while direct expansion of $(1+x)^{r+s}$ yields the right-hand side. Therefore Eq. (34) is a direct specialization of that general coefficient-convolution identity. This completes the proof. ∎
