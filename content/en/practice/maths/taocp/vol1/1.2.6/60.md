**Exercise 60.** [*M23*] How many $k$-combinations of $n$ objects are there, if repetition is allowed?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m37s  

---

## Solution

Let the $n$ objects be labeled $1,2,\ldots,n$. A $k$-combination with repetition is a selection of $k$ elements from this set where each object may be chosen multiple times, and order is irrelevant. Such a combination is completely determined by the multiplicities

$$
x_1,x_2,\ldots,x_n,
$$

where $x_i \ge 0$ is the number of times object $i$ is chosen and

$$
x_1 + x_2 + \cdots + x_n = k.
$$

Thus the problem reduces to counting the number of integer solutions of this equation in nonnegative integers.

Introduce a sequence of $k$ stars and $n-1$ bars. A configuration consists of $k$ identical symbols $*$ and $n-1$ identical separators $|$. Each such configuration determines a unique solution $(x_1,\ldots,x_n)$ by letting $x_1$ be the number of stars before the first bar, $x_2$ the number of stars between the first and second bar, and so on, with $x_n$ the number of stars after the last bar.

Conversely, given any solution $(x_1,\ldots,x_n)$ with $x_i \ge 0$ and sum $k$, one constructs a unique configuration by writing $x_1$ stars, then a bar, then $x_2$ stars, then a bar, continuing until $x_n$ stars. These two constructions are inverse to each other, so they define a bijection between solutions and star-bar configurations.

Each configuration is a permutation of $k + n - 1$ symbols, consisting of $k$ stars and $n-1$ bars. The number of such permutations equals the number of ways to choose positions for the $k$ stars among $k+n-1$ slots, which is

$$
\binom{k+n-1}{k}.
$$

Therefore the number of $k$-combinations of $n$ objects with repetition allowed is

$$
\binom{n+k-1}{k} = \binom{n+k-1}{n-1}.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
