**Exercise 2.** [*00*] What is $0^0$?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m02s  

---

For integer exponents, the identity $a^0 = 1$ is defined for all $a \ne 0$, while $0^k = 0$ for all integers $k > 0$. These two rules do not determine a consistent value for $0^0$, since it lies at the intersection of both exceptional cases. Thus, within ordinary arithmetic definitions based solely on exponent laws, $0^0$ is not fixed.

In combinatorial contexts of this section, expressions such as $(x+y)^r$ are expanded so that the term with $k=0$ has coefficient $\binom{r}{0} x^0 y^r$, and consistency requires $x^0 = 1$ even when $x=0$. Interpreting $x^0$ as the empty product gives $0^0 = 1$ in this setting, and this convention preserves the validity of the binomial theorem and related identities involving empty products.

$\boxed{0^0 = 1}$
