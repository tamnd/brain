**Exercise 55.** [*M21*] Considering each of Stirling's triangles as matrices, determine their inverses.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 58s  

---

## Solution

Let $S(n,k)$ denote Stirling numbers of the second kind and $s(n,k)$ denote Stirling numbers of the first kind in the signed form.

Define two infinite lower-triangular matrices $S$ and $T$ by

$$
S_{n,k} = S(n,k), \qquad T_{n,k} = s(n,k),
\qquad n,k \ge 0,
$$

with the usual conventions that $S(n,k)=0$ and $s(n,k)=0$ when $k<0$ or $k>n$, and $S(0,0)=1$, $s(0,0)=1$.

The defining expansions of powers in terms of falling factorials and conversely are

$$
x^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} S(n,k)\, x^{\underline{k}}, \qquad n \ge 0,
$$

and

$$
x^{\underline{n}} = \sum_{k=0}^{n} s(n,k)\, x^k, \qquad n \ge 0.
$$

Substituting the second relation into the first gives

$$
x^n
= \sum_{k=0}^{n} S(n,k)\left(\sum_{m=0}^{k} s(k,m)\, x^m\right)
= \sum_{m=0}^{n}\left(\sum_{k=m}^{n} S(n,k)s(k,m)\right)x^m.
$$

Since this identity holds for all real $x$, the coefficients of $x^m$ must agree with those of the left-hand side, hence

$$
\sum_{k=m}^{n} S(n,k)s(k,m) = \delta_{n,m},
\qquad n,m \ge 0,
$$

where $\delta_{n,m}$ is the Kronecker delta.

This equation states exactly that the matrix product of $S$ and $T$ is the identity matrix:

$$
\sum_{k \ge 0} S(n,k)\, s(k,m) = \delta_{n,m}.
$$

Therefore $T$ is the inverse of $S$. Since both matrices are lower triangular with $S(n,n)=1$ and $s(n,n)=1$, the inverse is unique among lower-triangular matrices.

Interchanging the roles of the two expansions shows symmetrically that $S$ is also the inverse of $T$.

This completes the proof. ∎
