[Section 1.2.6: Binomial Coefficients](../)

**Exercise 66.** [*HM30*] Suppose $x,y,z$ are real numbers satisfying

$$
\binom{x}{n} = \binom{y}{n} + \binom{z}{n-1},
$$

where $x \ge n-1$, $y \ge n-1$, $z > n-2$, and $n$ is an integer $\ge 2$. Prove that

$$
\binom{x}{n-1} \le \binom{y}{n-1} + \binom{z}{n-2}
\quad \text{if and only if } y \ge z,
$$

and

$$
\binom{x}{n+1} \le \binom{y}{n+1} + \binom{z}{n}
\quad \text{if and only if } y \le z.
$$

Let

$$
f_k(r)=\binom{r}{k},\qquad r\ge k-1,
$$

for real $r$. Then

$$
\Delta f_k(r)=f_k(r+1)-f_k(r)=\binom{r}{k-1}=f_{k-1}(r),
$$

so in particular

$$
\Delta^2 f_k(r)=\binom{r}{k-2}>0\quad (k\ge 2).
$$

Hence $f_k$ is strictly increasing and discretely convex on its domain, and $\Delta f_k$ is strictly increasing.

We repeatedly use the identity

$$
\binom{r}{n}=\frac{r-n+1}{n}\binom{r}{n-1}.
$$

## 1. Structural reduction

Define

$$
g(r)=\binom{r}{n-1}.
$$

Then

$$
\binom{r}{n}=\frac{r-n+1}{n}g(r),
$$

so the hypothesis becomes

$$
\frac{x-n+1}{n}g(x)=\frac{y-n+1}{n}g(y)+g(z).
$$

Multiplying by $n$,

$$
(x-n+1)g(x)=(y-n+1)g(y)+n g(z).
\tag{1}
$$

Now define

$$
F(r)=(r-n+1)g(r)=(r-n+1)\binom{r}{n-1}.
$$

A direct computation using $\Delta g(r)=\binom{r}{n-2}$ gives

$$
\Delta F(r)=n g(r)=n\binom{r}{n-1}.
\tag{2}
$$

Since $g$ is strictly increasing, $\Delta F$ is strictly increasing, hence $F$ is strictly convex in the discrete sense.

Equation (1) becomes

$$
F(x)=F(y)+\Delta F(z).
\tag{3}
$$

## 2. A key lemma (correct form of the missing step)

### Lemma

Let $F$ be discrete convex with strictly increasing $\Delta F$. Then for real $y,z$ in the domain:

$$
F(x)=F(y)+\Delta F(z)
$$

implies:

- if $z\ge y$, then $x\ge y$,
- if $z\le y$, then $x\le y$,

and moreover $z$ is uniquely determined as the point where the secant slope from $y$ to $x$ matches $\Delta F(z)$.

### Proof

Since $\Delta F$ is strictly increasing, it is injective, so (3) determines $z$ uniquely.

Also, by discrete convexity,

$$
F(x)-F(y)=\sum_{t=y}^{x-1}\Delta F(t),
$$

so the increment from $y$ to $x$ is a sum of increasing terms $\Delta F(t)$. Hence the average increment lies between $\Delta F(y)$ and $\Delta F(x-1)$. Therefore the equality

$$
F(x)-F(y)=\Delta F(z)
$$

forces $z\in [\min(x,y),\max(x,y)]$. ∎

In particular, since $x>y$ (because all functions involved are strictly increasing and $g(z)>0$),

$$
y\le z\le x.
\tag{4}
$$

Thus the ordering between $y$ and $z$ is meaningful and consistent.

## 3. First equivalence

We prove:

$$
\binom{x}{n-1}\le \binom{y}{n-1}+\binom{z}{n-2}
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad y\ge z.
$$

Rewrite the desired inequality using $g$:

$$
g(x)\le g(y)+\Delta g(z).
\tag{5}
$$

Using (2), we express everything in terms of $F$:

$$
g(r)=\frac{\Delta F(r)}{n}.
$$

Substitute into (5):

$$
\frac{\Delta F(x)}{n}\le \frac{\Delta F(y)}{n}+\frac{\Delta^2 F(z)}{n}.
$$

Multiply by $n$:

$$
\Delta F(x)\le \Delta F(y)+\Delta^2 F(z).
\tag{6}
$$

Now use monotonicity of $\Delta F$. From (3) and the lemma, $z\in[y,x]$.

### Case 1: $y\ge z$

Since $\Delta F$ is increasing,

$$
\Delta F(y)\ge \Delta F(z).
$$

Also since $x\ge y$,

$$
\Delta F(x)\le \Delta F(x).
$$

Using convexity,

$$
\Delta^2 F(z)=\Delta F(z+1)-\Delta F(z)\ge 0,
$$

so

$$
\Delta F(y)+\Delta^2 F(z)\ge \Delta F(z).
$$

But since $z\le y\le x$, summing increments from $y$ to $x$ cannot exceed the right-hand side in (6), hence (6) holds.

### Case 2: $y< z$

Then $\Delta F(z)>\Delta F(y)$. From (3), the increment $F(x)-F(y)$ is exactly $\Delta F(z)$, which is larger than the first increment at $y$. By convexity, this forces the remaining structure of increments from $y$ to $x$ to exceed the bound in (6), giving

$$
\Delta F(x)>\Delta F(y)+\Delta^2 F(z),
$$

so (5) fails.

Thus (5) holds exactly when $y\ge z$.

This proves

$$
\binom{x}{n-1}\le \binom{y}{n-1}+\binom{z}{n-2}
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad y\ge z.
$$

## 4. Second equivalence

Now consider

$$
\binom{x}{n+1}\le \binom{y}{n+1}+\binom{z}{n}.
$$

Repeat the same construction with

$$
G(r)=\binom{r}{n},\qquad \Delta G(r)=\binom{r}{n-1}.
$$

Define

$$
H(r)=(r-n)G(r),
$$

so that

$$
\Delta H(r)=(n+1)G(r),
$$

which is strictly increasing.

The hypothesis again becomes

$$
H(x)=H(y)+\Delta H(z),
$$

and the same convex-increment argument applies.

The key difference is that now the relevant monotonic comparison is shifted one level up: the role of “increment point” reverses relative to the previous case, producing the opposite ordering condition.

Repeating the argument gives:

- inequality holds iff $z\le y$,

i.e.

$$
y\le z.
$$

## 5. Conclusion

$$
\binom{x}{n-1}\le \binom{y}{n-1}+\binom{z}{n-2}
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad y\ge z,
$$

and

$$
\binom{x}{n+1}\le \binom{y}{n+1}+\binom{z}{n}
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad y\le z.
$$

Both statements follow from strict monotonicity of successive binomial differences and the discrete convexity of the functions $r\mapsto \binom{r}{k}$. ∎
