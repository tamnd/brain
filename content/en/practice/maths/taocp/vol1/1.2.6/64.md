**Exercise 64.** [*M20*] Show that $\left\{{n \atop m}\right\}$ is the number of ways to partition a set of $n$ elements into $m$ nonempty disjoint subsets.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m13s

## Solution

Let $S(n,m)$ denote the number of ways to partition a set of $n$ elements into $m$ nonempty disjoint subsets. Let the underlying set be ${1,2,\dots,n}$.

Fix $n \ge 1$ and $m \ge 1$, and examine the position of the element $n$ in such a partition.

If ${n}$ forms a singleton subset, then the remaining $n-1$ elements must be partitioned into $m-1$ nonempty subsets. This contributes $S(n-1,m-1)$ partitions.

If $n$ is not a singleton, then $n$ must belong to one of the $m$ subsets formed by the remaining structure. After removing $n$, the remaining $n-1$ elements may be partitioned into $m$ nonempty subsets in $S(n-1,m)$ ways, and there are $m$ choices for the subset into which $n$ is inserted. This contributes $m,S(n-1,m)$ partitions.

These two cases are disjoint and exhaustive, giving

$$
S(n,m)=S(n-1,m-1)+m\,S(n-1,m).
$$

Initial conditions follow directly from the definition. A set with no elements has exactly one partition into zero subsets, hence $S(0,0)=1$. If $m>0$ and $n=0$, no partition into nonempty subsets exists, hence $S(0,m)=0$. If $n>0$ and $m=0$, no partition into zero nonempty subsets exists, hence $S(n,0)=0$.

The Stirling numbers of the second kind $\left{{n \atop m}\right}$ satisfy the same recurrence relation and the same initial conditions, as established earlier in Section 1.2.6. The recurrence for $\left{{n \atop m}\right}$ is

$$
\left\{{n \atop m}\right\}=\left\{{n-1 \atop m-1}\right\}+m\left\{{n-1 \atop m}\right\},
$$

with identical boundary values.

Since both $S(n,m)$ and $\left{{n \atop m}\right}$ are uniquely determined by this recurrence together with the same initial conditions, equality holds for all integers $n \ge 0$ and $m \ge 0$:

$$
S(n,m)=\left\{{n \atop m}\right\}.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
