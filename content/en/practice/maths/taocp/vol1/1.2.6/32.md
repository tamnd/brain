[Section 1.2.6: Binomial Coefficients](../)

**Exercise 32.** [*M20*] Show that $\sum_k \binom{n}{k}x^k = x^{\overline{n}}$, where $x^{\overline{n}}$ is the rising factorial power defined in Eq. 1.2.5--(19).

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m40s  

---

The stated identity is incorrect, so no proof of it can be completed as written. The error is not a gap in reasoning but a false equation.

## 1. Disproof of the stated identity

The exercise claims

$$
\sum_k \binom{n}{k} x^k = x^{\overline{n}},
\quad \text{where } x^{\overline{n}} = x(x+1)\cdots(x+n-1).
$$

Take $n=1$. Then

$$
\sum_k \binom{1}{k} x^k = \binom{1}{0}x^0 + \binom{1}{1}x^1 = 1 + x,
$$

while

$$
x^{\overline{1}} = x.
$$

Hence

$$
1+x \neq x,
$$

so the identity fails. Therefore the equation in the problem statement is false.

## 2. Correct identity corresponding to the left-hand side

The left-hand side is exactly the binomial expansion:

$$
\sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k} x^k = (1+x)^n.
$$

This follows directly from the binomial theorem (TAOCP Eq. (13)).

Thus the correct evaluation of the sum is

$$
\sum_k \binom{n}{k} x^k = (1+x)^n.
$$

## 3. Correct relation involving the rising factorial

The rising factorial $x^{\overline{n}}$ is defined by

$$
x^{\overline{n}} = x(x+1)\cdots(x+n-1),
\quad x^{\overline{0}} = 1.
$$

It is not equal to $(x+1)^n$ or $(1+x)^n$. In particular, it satisfies a different expansion:

$$
x^{\overline{n}} = \sum_{k=0}^n \left[{n \atop k}\right] x^k,
$$

where $\left[{n \atop k}\right]$ are the Stirling numbers of the first kind (TAOCP Eq. 1.2.6-(24)).

This is the correct connection between rising factorials and ordinary powers.

## 4. Conclusion

- The given identity is false.
- The binomial sum evaluates to $(1+x)^n$, not a rising factorial.
- The rising factorial expands in terms of Stirling numbers, not binomial coefficients.

Hence the original exercise statement cannot be proved because it is not valid as written.
