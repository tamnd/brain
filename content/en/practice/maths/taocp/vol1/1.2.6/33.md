**Exercise 33.** [*M20*] Show that the binomial formula is valid also when it involves factorial powers instead of the ordinary powers. In other words, prove that

$$
(x+y)^{\underline{n}} = \sum_k \binom{n}{k}x^{\underline{k}}y^{\underline{n-k}},
\qquad
(x+y)^{\overline{n}} = \sum_k \binom{n}{k}x^{\overline{k}}y^{\overline{n-k}}.
$$

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m24s  

---

## Solution

### Falling factorial identity

Proceed by induction on $n$.

For $n=0$, the identity reduces to

$$
(x+y)^{\underline 0} = 1
$$

and

$$
\sum_k \binom{0}{k} x^{\underline k} y^{\underline{0-k}} = \binom{0}{0} x^{\underline 0} y^{\underline 0} = 1,
$$

so both sides agree.

Assume that for some $n \ge 0$,

$$
(x+y)^{\underline n} = \sum_k \binom{n}{k} x^{\underline k} y^{\underline{n-k}}.
$$

Using $z^{\underline{n+1}} = (z-n)z^{\underline n}$ with $z=x+y$,

$$
(x+y)^{\underline{n+1}} = (x+y-n)(x+y)^{\underline n}.
$$

Substituting the inductive hypothesis,

$$
(x+y)^{\underline{n+1}} = (x+y-n)\sum_k \binom{n}{k} x^{\underline k} y^{\underline{n-k}}.
$$

Split the factor $x+y-n$ as

$$
x+y-n = (x-k) + (y-(n-k)).
$$

Then

$$
(x+y)^{\underline{n+1}}
= \sum_k \binom{n}{k}(x-k)x^{\underline k}y^{\underline{n-k}}
+ \sum_k \binom{n}{k}(y-(n-k))x^{\underline k}y^{\underline{n-k}}.
$$

Use the defining relations of falling factorials,

$$
(x-k)x^{\underline k} = x^{\underline{k+1}}, \qquad
(y-(n-k))y^{\underline{n-k}} = y^{\underline{n-k+1}}.
$$

The expression becomes

$$
\sum_k \binom{n}{k} x^{\underline{k+1}} y^{\underline{n-k}}
+
\sum_k \binom{n}{k} x^{\underline k} y^{\underline{n-k+1}}.
$$

In the first sum replace $k$ by $k-1$, giving

$$
\sum_k \binom{n}{k-1} x^{\underline k} y^{\underline{n-k+1}},
$$

where terms with $k=0$ vanish since $\binom{n}{-1}=0$.

Hence

$$
(x+y)^{\underline{n+1}}
=
\sum_k \left[\binom{n}{k} + \binom{n}{k-1}\right]
x^{\underline k} y^{\underline{n+1-k}}.
$$

Applying the addition formula $\binom{n}{k}+\binom{n}{k-1}=\binom{n+1}{k}$,

$$
(x+y)^{\underline{n+1}}
=
\sum_k \binom{n+1}{k} x^{\underline k} y^{\underline{n+1-k}}.
$$

This completes the induction for the falling factorial identity. ∎

### Rising factorial identity

Proceed by induction on $n$.

For $n=0$,

$$
(x+y)^{\overline 0} = 1
$$

and

$$
\sum_k \binom{0}{k} x^{\overline k} y^{\overline{0-k}} = 1,
$$

so the identity holds.

Assume that

$$
(x+y)^{\overline n} = \sum_k \binom{n}{k} x^{\overline k} y^{\overline{n-k}}.
$$

Using $z^{\overline{n+1}} = (z+n)z^{\overline n}$ with $z=x+y$,

$$
(x+y)^{\overline{n+1}} = (x+y+n)(x+y)^{\overline n}.
$$

Substitute the inductive hypothesis,

$$
(x+y)^{\overline{n+1}} = (x+y+n)\sum_k \binom{n}{k} x^{\overline k} y^{\overline{n-k}}.
$$

Decompose the factor as

$$
x+y+n = (x+k) + (y+(n-k)).
$$

Then

$$
(x+y)^{\overline{n+1}}
=
\sum_k \binom{n}{k}(x+k)x^{\overline k}y^{\overline{n-k}}
+
\sum_k \binom{n}{k}(y+(n-k))x^{\overline k}y^{\overline{n-k}}.
$$

Use the rising factorial relations

$$
(x+k)x^{\overline k} = x^{\overline{k+1}}, \qquad
(y+(n-k))y^{\overline{n-k}} = y^{\overline{n-k+1}}.
$$

This yields

$$
\sum_k \binom{n}{k} x^{\overline{k+1}} y^{\overline{n-k}}
+
\sum_k \binom{n}{k} x^{\overline k} y^{\overline{n-k+1}}.
$$

Reindex the first sum by replacing $k$ with $k-1$, giving

$$
\sum_k \binom{n}{k-1} x^{\overline k} y^{\overline{n-k+1}}.
$$

Hence

$$
(x+y)^{\overline{n+1}}
=
\sum_k \left[\binom{n}{k} + \binom{n}{k-1}\right]
x^{\overline k} y^{\overline{n+1-k}}.
$$

Applying $\binom{n}{k}+\binom{n}{k-1}=\binom{n+1}{k}$,

$$
(x+y)^{\overline{n+1}}
=
\sum_k \binom{n+1}{k} x^{\overline k} y^{\overline{n+1-k}}.
$$

This completes the induction for the rising factorial identity. ∎
