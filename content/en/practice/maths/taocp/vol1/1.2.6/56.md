**Exercise 56.** [*20*] (The combinatorial number system.) For each integer $n=0,1,2,\ldots,20$, find three integers $a,b,c$ for which

$$
n=\binom{a}{3}+\binom{b}{2}+\binom{c}{1},
\qquad a>b>c\ge 0.
$$

**Solve time:** 1m31s

## Solution

For integers $a>b>c\ge 0$, define the representation

$$
n=\binom{a}{3}+\binom{b}{2}+\binom{c}{1}.
$$

For $n\le 20$, the values of $\binom{a}{3}$ satisfy

$$
\binom{3}{3}=1,\quad \binom{4}{3}=4,\quad \binom{5}{3}=10,\quad \binom{6}{3}=20.
$$

Hence $a$ is determined as the largest integer with $\binom{a}{3}\le n$, and the remainder is then decomposed using $\binom{b}{2}$ and $\binom{c}{1}$.

The construction proceeds by successive maximization: for given remainder $r$, choose the largest $b$ with $\binom{b}{2}\le r$, then set $c=r-\binom{b}{2}$. Since $\binom{b}{2}$ is strictly increasing in $b$ for $b\ge 0$, this choice forces $b$ uniquely. The inequality $a>b>c$ holds at each step because $\binom{a}{3}\ge 1$ for $a\ge 3$, while $\binom{b}{2}\le r<\binom{a-1}{3}$ ensures $b\le a-1$, and the final remainder satisfies $c<b$ by construction of $b$.

Now compute each value.

For $n=0$, choose $a=2$, $b=1$, $c=0$, since all corresponding binomial coefficients vanish and $2>1>0$.

For $n=1$, take $a=3$ since $\binom{3}{3}=1$ and remainder $0$. Then $b=1$, $c=0$, giving $(a,b,c)=(3,1,0)$.

For $n=2$, take $a=3$ with remainder $1$. Then $b=2$ since $\binom{2}{2}=1$, giving remainder $0$, hence $c=0$, so $(3,2,0)$.

For $n=3$, again $a=3$ gives remainder $2$. Then $b=2$ gives remainder $1$, and $c=1$, so $(3,2,1)$.

For $n=4$, take $a=4$ since $\binom{4}{3}=4$. Remainder is $0$, so $b=1$, $c=0$, giving $(4,1,0)$.

For $n=5$, take $a=4$ with remainder $1$. Then $b=2$ gives remainder $0$, so $c=0$, hence $(4,2,0)$.

For $n=6$, take $a=4$ with remainder $2$. Then $b=2$ gives remainder $1$, so $c=1$, hence $(4,2,1)$.

For $n=7$, take $a=4$ with remainder $3$. Then $b=3$ since $\binom{3}{2}=3$, giving remainder $0$, so $c=0$, hence $(4,3,0)$.

For $n=8$, take $a=4$ with remainder $4$. Then $b=3$ gives remainder $1$, so $c=1$, hence $(4,3,1)$.

For $n=9$, take $a=4$ with remainder $5$. Then $b=3$ gives remainder $2$, so $c=2$, hence $(4,3,2)$.

For $n=10$, take $a=5$ since $\binom{5}{3}=10$, remainder $0$, so $(5,1,0)$.

For $n=11$, take $a=5$ with remainder $1$. Then $b=2$, $c=0$, giving $(5,2,0)$.

For $n=12$, take $a=5$ with remainder $2$. Then $b=2$ gives remainder $1$, so $c=1$, giving $(5,2,1)$.

For $n=13$, take $a=5$ with remainder $3$. Then $b=3$ gives remainder $0$, so $c=0$, giving $(5,3,0)$.

For $n=14$, take $a=5$ with remainder $4$. Then $b=3$ gives remainder $1$, so $c=1$, giving $(5,3,1)$.

For $n=15$, take $a=5$ with remainder $5$. Then $b=3$ gives remainder $2$, so $c=2$, giving $(5,3,2)$.

For $n=16$, take $a=5$ with remainder $6$. Then $b=4$ since $\binom{4}{2}=6$, remainder $0$, so $(5,4,0)$.

For $n=17$, take $a=5$ with remainder $7$. Then $b=4$ gives remainder $1$, so $c=1$, giving $(5,4,1)$.

For $n=18$, take $a=5$ with remainder $8$. Then $b=4$ gives remainder $2$, so $c=2$, giving $(5,4,2)$.
