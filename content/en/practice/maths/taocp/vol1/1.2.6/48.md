[Section 1.2.6: Binomial Coefficients](../)

**Exercise 48.** [*M25*] Show that

$$
\sum_{k \ge 0}\binom{n}{k}\frac{(-1)^k}{k+x}
= \frac{n!}{x(x+1)\cdots(x+n)}
= \frac{1}{x\binom{n+x}{n}},
$$

if the denominators are not zero.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m10s  

---

## Solution

Assume $x \notin {0,-1,-2,\ldots,-n}$ so that all denominators are nonzero.

Start from the identity

$$
\frac{1}{k+x}=\int_{0}^{1} t^{k+x-1}\,dt,
\qquad x>0,
$$

and note that both sides extend as meromorphic functions of $x$, so the resulting identity continues to hold wherever no pole occurs.

Substituting this into the sum gives

$$
\sum_{k \ge 0}\binom{n}{k}\frac{(-1)^k}{k+x}
= \sum_{k \ge 0}\binom{n}{k}(-1)^k \int_{0}^{1} t^{k+x-1}\,dt.
$$

Interchanging summation and integration yields

$$
\int_{0}^{1} t^{x-1}\sum_{k \ge 0}\binom{n}{k}(-t)^k\,dt.
$$

The inner sum is the binomial expansion of $(1-t)^n$ by Eq. (13), hence

$$
\sum_{k \ge 0}\binom{n}{k}(-t)^k=(1-t)^n.
$$

The expression reduces to

$$
\int_{0}^{1} t^{x-1}(1-t)^n\,dt.
$$

This integral equals the Beta function $B(x,n+1)$, which satisfies

$$
B(x,n+1)=\frac{\Gamma(x)\Gamma(n+1)}{\Gamma(x+n+1)}.
$$

Using $\Gamma(n+1)=n!$ and

$$
\Gamma(x+n+1)=x(x+1)\cdots(x+n)\,\Gamma(x),
$$

gives

$$
\int_{0}^{1} t^{x-1}(1-t)^n\,dt
= \frac{n!}{x(x+1)\cdots(x+n)}.
$$

Thus

$$
\sum_{k \ge 0}\binom{n}{k}\frac{(-1)^k}{k+x}
= \frac{n!}{x(x+1)\cdots(x+n)}.
$$

For the binomial coefficient form, apply Eq. (5) with non-integer upper argument:

$$
\binom{n+x}{n}
= \frac{(n+x)(n+x-1)\cdots(x+1)}{n!}
= \frac{x(x+1)\cdots(x+n)}{n!}.
$$

Taking reciprocals yields

$$
\frac{n!}{x(x+1)\cdots(x+n)}=\frac{1}{x\binom{n+x}{n}}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\sum_{k \ge 0}\binom{n}{k}\frac{(-1)^k}{k+x}
= \frac{n!}{x(x+1)\cdots(x+n)}
= \frac{1}{x\binom{n+x}{n}}.
$$

This completes the proof. ∎
