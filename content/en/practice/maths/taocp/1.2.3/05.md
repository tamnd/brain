**Exercise 5.** &#9654; [*HM20*] Prove that rule (a) is valid for arbitrary infinite series, provided that the series converge.

**Verified:** no

## Solution

Let $\sum_{R(i)} a_i$ and $\sum_{S(j)} b_j$ be two convergent infinite series. By definition, the series converge if the corresponding sequence of partial sums tends to a finite limit:

$\lim_{m \to \infty} \sum_{\substack{i \in R \\ i \le m}} a_i = A, \qquad \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{\substack{j \in S \\ j \le n}} b_j = B.$

Consider the product of the two series:

$\left(\sum_{R(i)} a_i \right) \left(\sum_{S(j)} b_j \right) = \lim_{m \to \infty} \sum_{i \le m,\, i \in R} a_i \;\; \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{j \le n,\, j \in S} b_j.$

For finite $m$ and $n$, the distributive law (4) holds:

$\left(\sum_{i \le m,\, i \in R} a_i\right) \left(\sum_{j \le n,\, j \in S} b_j\right) = \sum_{i \le m,\, i \in R} \sum_{j \le n,\, j \in S} a_i b_j.$

We now consider the limit as $m, n \to \infty$. Let

$S_{m,n} = \sum_{i \le m,\, i \in R} \sum_{j \le n,\, j \in S} a_i b_j.$

Since $\sum_{R(i)} a_i$ and $\sum_{S(j)} b_j$ converge, for any $\epsilon > 0$ there exist integers $M$ and $N$ such that

$\left| \sum_{i > M,\, i \in R} a_i \right| < \epsilon, \qquad \left| \sum_{j > N,\, j \in S} b_j \right| < \epsilon.$

Then for all $m \ge M$ and $n \ge N$, we have

$$
\begin{aligned}
\left| \sum_{i \in R} a_i \sum_{j \in S} b_j - S_{m,n} \right| 
&= \left| \sum_{i \in R} a_i \sum_{j \in S} b_j - \sum_{i \le m, i \in R} \sum_{j \le n, j \in S} a_i b_j \right| \\
&\le \left| \sum_{i > m, i \in R} a_i \sum_{j \in S} b_j \right| + \left| \sum_{i \le m, i \in R} \sum_{j > n, j \in S} a_i b_j \right| \\
&\le \left( \sum_{i > m, i \in R} |a_i| \right) \left( \sum_{j \in S} |b_j| \right) + \left( \sum_{i \le m, i \in R} |a_i| \right) \left( \sum_{j > n, j \in S} |b_j| \right).
\end{aligned}
$$

Both terms can be made arbitrarily small because the series converge absolutely. Hence, the limit exists and we can interchange the order of summation for the double series:

$\lim_{m \to \infty} \lim_{n \to \infty} S_{m,n} = \sum_{R(i)} \sum_{S(j)} a_i b_j.$

Therefore, for convergent series, we have

$\left(\sum_{R(i)} a_i\right) \left(\sum_{S(j)} b_j\right) = \sum_{R(i)} \sum_{S(j)} a_i b_j.$

By the same reasoning, we can drop the parentheses in the right-hand side, as in the finite case. This proves that the distributive law (4) is valid for arbitrary convergent infinite series.

This completes the proof.

∎
