**Exercise 22.** &#9654; [*20*] State the appropriate analogs of Eqs. (5), (7), (8), and (11) for *products* instead of sums.

**Verified:** no

## Solution

For products, the analog of Eq. (5) is

$\prod_{R(i)} a_i = \prod_{R(j)} a_j = \prod_{R(p(j))} a_{p(j)}.$

The conditions on the permutation $p(j)$ are the same as in Eq. (5), namely, for each integer $i$ satisfying $R(i)$ there must be exactly one integer $j$ satisfying $p(j)=i$.

The analog of Eq. (7) is

$\prod_{R(i)} \prod_{S(j)} a_{ij} = \prod_{S(j)} \prod_{R(i)} a_{ij}.$

Both sides represent the product of all factors $a_{ij}$ for which $R(i)$ and $S(j)$ are true.

The analog of Eq. (8) is obtained by replacing addition by multiplication:

$\prod_{R(i)} (b_i c_i) = \left(\prod_{R(i)} b_i\right)\left(\prod_{R(i)} c_i\right).$

This is a special case of the distributive law for products of products:

$$= \prod_{R(i)} (b_i c_i).$$

The analog of Eq. (11) is

= \left(\prod_{R(j)\text{ or }S(j)} a_j\right) \left(\prod_{R(j)\text{ and }S(j)} a_j\right).$$ To verify this identity, consider each value of $j$ separately. If exactly one of $R(j)$ and $S(j)$ is true, the factor $a_j$ appears once on each side. If both are true, the factor $a_j$ appears twice on each side. If neither is true, the factor $a_j$ appears on neither side. Hence the two products contain exactly the same factors with the same multiplicities. This completes the proof. ∎
