**Exercise 38.** &#9654; [*M25*] Show that the determinant of Cauchy’s matrix is

$$\prod_{1 \le i < j \le n} (x_j - x_i)(y_j - y_i) \bigg/ \prod_{1 \le i, j \le n} (x_i + y_j).$$

## Solution

Let

$$D_n=\det\left(\frac1{x_i+y_j}\right)_{1\le i,j\le n}.$$

We shall prove that

$$D_n=
\frac{\displaystyle\prod_{1\le i<j\le n}(x_j-x_i)(y_j-y_i)}
{\displaystyle\prod_{1\le i,j\le n}(x_i+y_j)}.
\eqno(1)$$

Multiply the $i$th row of the determinant by

$$\prod_{k=1}^n(x_i+y_k).$$

Factoring these quantities out of the rows gives

$$D_n=
\frac1{\displaystyle\prod_{1\le i,j\le n}(x_i+y_j)}
\det(a_{ij}),$$

where

$$a_{ij}=\frac{\prod_{k=1}^n(x_i+y_k)}{x_i+y_j}
=\prod_{\substack{1\le k\le n\\k\ne j}}(x_i+y_k).$$

Hence

$$D_n=
\frac{\Delta_n}
{\displaystyle\prod_{1\le i,j\le n}(x_i+y_j)},
\eqno(2)$$

where

$$\Delta_n=
\det\left(
\prod_{\substack{1\le k\le n\\k\ne j}}(x_i+y_k)
\right)_{1\le i,j\le n}.$$

For fixed $j$, the $j$th column is a polynomial in $x_i$ of degree $n-1$ whose leading coefficient is $1$. Therefore $\Delta_n$ is an alternating polynomial in the variables $x_1,\ldots,x_n$. Indeed, interchanging $x_r$ and $x_s$ interchanges rows $r$ and $s$, changing the sign of the determinant. Hence

$$x_s-x_r$$

divides $\Delta_n$ whenever $r<s$. Consequently,

$$\prod_{1\le r<s\le n}(x_s-x_r)$$

divides $\Delta_n$.

By the same argument, since interchanging $y_r$ and $y_s$ interchanges columns $r$ and $s$, the polynomial

$$\prod_{1\le r<s\le n}(y_s-y_r)$$

also divides $\Delta_n$.

Now each entry of $\Delta_n$ has degree $n-1$ in the variables $x_i$, and the determinant contains exactly one entry from each row. Hence the total degree of $\Delta_n$ in the variables $x_1,\ldots,x_n$ is at most

$$n(n-1).$$

The product

$$\prod_{1\le r<s\le n}(x_s-x_r)(y_s-y_r)$$

already has degree

$$\frac{n(n-1)}2+\frac{n(n-1)}2=n(n-1).$$

Therefore there exists a constant $C$, independent of the variables, such that

$$\Delta_n=
C\prod_{1\le r<s\le n}(x_s-x_r)(y_s-y_r).
\eqno(3)$$

It remains to determine $C$.

Consider the coefficient of

$$x_1^0x_2^1\cdots x_n^{n-1}$$

in $\Delta_n$. Since

$$a_{ij}=\prod_{k\ne j}(x_i+y_k),$$

the coefficient of $x_i^{m}$ in $a_{ij}$ is the elementary symmetric function of degree $n-1-m$ in the variables ${y_k:k\ne j}$.

In particular, the coefficient of $x_i^{i-1}$ in column $j$ is the elementary symmetric function

$$e_{n-i}^{(j)},$$

where the superscript indicates omission of $y_j$.

Hence the coefficient of

$$x_1^0x_2^1\cdots x_n^{n-1}$$

in $\Delta_n$ equals

$$\det(e_{n-i}^{(j)})_{1\le i,j\le n}.$$

This determinant is precisely the Vandermonde determinant in the variables $y_1,\ldots,y_n$:

$$\det(e_{n-i}^{(j)})_{1\le i,j\le n}
=
\prod_{1\le r<s\le n}(y_s-y_r).$$

Therefore the coefficient of

$$x_1^0x_2^1\cdots x_n^{n-1}$$

on the left side of (3) is

$$\prod_{1\le r<s\le n}(y_s-y_r).$$

On the right side of (3), the coefficient of the same monomial in

$$\prod_{1\le r<s\le n}(x_s-x_r)$$

is $1$, since the monomial arises uniquely by choosing $x_s$ from every factor $(x_s-x_r)$. Thus the coefficient on the right side of (3) is

$$C\prod_{1\le r<s\le n}(y_s-y_r).$$

Equating coefficients yields

$$C=1.$$

Substituting into (3) and then into (2), we obtain

$$D_n=
\frac{\displaystyle\prod_{1\le i<j\le n}(x_j-x_i)(y_j-y_i)}
{\displaystyle\prod_{1\le i,j\le n}(x_i+y_j)}.$$

Therefore

$$\boxed{
\det\left(\frac1{x_i+y_j}\right)_{1\le i,j\le n}
=
\frac{\displaystyle\prod_{1\le i<j\le n}(x_j-x_i)(y_j-y_i)}
{\displaystyle\prod_{1\le i,j\le n}(x_i+y_j)}
}.$$

This completes the proof.

∎
