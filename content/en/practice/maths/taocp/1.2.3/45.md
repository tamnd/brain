[Section 1.2.3 — Sums and Products](../)

**Exercise 45.** &#9654; [*M25*] A *Hilbert matrix*, sometimes called an $n \times n$ segment of *the* (infinite) Hilbert matrix, is a matrix for which $a_{ij} = 1/(i + j - 1)$. Show that this is a special case of Cauchy’s matrix, find its inverse, show that each element of the inverse is an integer, and show that the sum of all elements of the inverse is $n^2$. [Note: Hilbert matrices have often been used to test various matrix manipulation algorithms, because they are numerically unstable, and they have known inverses. However, it is a mistake to compare the *known* inverse, given in this exercise, to the *computed* inverse of a Hilbert matrix, since the matrix to be inverted must be expressed in rounded numbers beforehand; the inverse of an approximate Hilbert matrix will be somewhat different from the inverse of an exact one, due to the instability present. Since the elements of the inverse are integers, and since the inverse matrix is just as unstable as the original, the inverse can be specified exactly, and one could try to invert the inverse. The integers that appear in the inverse are, however, quite large.] The solution to this problem requires an elementary knowledge of factorials and binomial coefficients, which are discussed in Sections 1.2.5 and 1.2.6.

## Solution

Let

$$
H=(h_{ij})_{1\le i,j\le n}, \qquad h_{ij}=\frac1{i+j-1}.
$$

The general Cauchy matrix has the form

$$
c_{ij}=\frac1{x_i+y_j},
$$

where $x_i+y_j\ne0$ for all $i,j$. The Hilbert matrix is obtained by taking

$$
x_i=i,\qquad y_j=j-1.
$$

Hence

$$
x_i+y_j=i+j-1=h_{ij}^{-1},
$$

so the Hilbert matrix is a special case of Cauchy’s matrix.

For a Cauchy matrix, Exercise 44 gives the inverse in the form

$$
(c^{-1})_{ij}
=
\frac
{\displaystyle
\prod_{k=1}^n(x_j+y_k)\prod_{k=1}^n(x_k+y_i)}
{(x_j+y_i)
\displaystyle
\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne j}}^n(x_j-x_k)
\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne i}}^n(y_i-y_k)
}.
$$

Substituting $x_j=j$ and $y_i=i-1$, we obtain

$$
(H^{-1})_{ij}
=
\frac
{\displaystyle
\prod_{k=1}^n(j+k-1)\prod_{k=1}^n(i+k-1)}
{(i+j-1)
\displaystyle
\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne j}}^n(j-k)
\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne i}}^n(i-k)
}.
$$

The denominator simplifies as follows:

$$
\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne j}}^n(j-k)
=
\left(\prod_{k=1}^{j-1}(j-k)\right)
\left(\prod_{k=j+1}^n(j-k)\right).
$$

Since

$$
\prod_{k=1}^{j-1}(j-k)=(j-1)!,
$$

and

$$
\prod_{k=j+1}^n(j-k)
=
(-1)^{n-j}(n-j)!,
$$

we have

$$
\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne j}}^n(j-k)
=
(-1)^{n-j}(j-1)!(n-j)!.
$$

Similarly,

$$
\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne i}}^n(i-k)
=
(-1)^{n-i}(i-1)!(n-i)!.
$$

The numerator satisfies

$$
\prod_{k=1}^n(j+k-1)
=
\frac{(n+j-1)!}{(j-1)!},
$$

and similarly

$$
\prod_{k=1}^n(i+k-1)
=
\frac{(n+i-1)!}{(i-1)!}.
$$

Therefore

$$
(H^{-1})_{ij}
=
(-1)^{i+j}
\frac{(n+i-1)!(n+j-1)!}
{(i+j-1)(i-1)!^2(j-1)!^2(n-i)!(n-j)!}.
$$

Using

$$
(i+j-1)!
=(i+j-1)(i+j-2)!,
$$

this becomes

$$
(H^{-1})_{ij}
=
(-1)^{i+j}
\frac{(n+i-1)!(n+j-1)!}
{(i+j-1)!}
\frac{(i+j-2)!}
{(i-1)!^2(j-1)!^2(n-i)!(n-j)!}.
$$

Now write the factorial ratios as binomial coefficients:

$$
\binom{n+i-1}{n-j}
=
\frac{(n+i-1)!}{(n-j)!(i+j-1)!},
$$

$$
\binom{n+j-1}{n-i}
=
\frac{(n+j-1)!}{(n-i)!(i+j-1)!},
$$

and

$$
\binom{i+j-2}{i-1}
=
\frac{(i+j-2)!}{(i-1)!(j-1)!}.
$$

Hence

$$
(H^{-1})_{ij}
=
(-1)^{i+j}
(i+j-1)
\binom{n+i-1}{n-j}
\binom{n+j-1}{n-i}
\binom{i+j-2}{i-1}^2.
$$

Thus the inverse Hilbert matrix is

$$
\boxed{
(H^{-1})_{ij}
=
(-1)^{i+j}
(i+j-1)
\binom{n+i-1}{n-j}
\binom{n+j-1}{n-i}
\binom{i+j-2}{i-1}^2
}.
$$

Each factor on the right-hand side is an integer; therefore every element of $H^{-1}$ is an integer.

It remains to determine the sum of all elements of $H^{-1}$. Exercise 44 states that the sum of all elements in the inverse of a Cauchy matrix is

$$
\left(\sum_{i=1}^n
\frac{
\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne i}}^n(x_i+y_k)
}
{
\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne i}}^n(x_i-x_k)
}
\right)
\left(\sum_{j=1}^n
\frac{
\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne j}}^n(x_k+y_j)
}
{
\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne j}}^n(y_j-y_k)
}
\right).
$$

For the Hilbert matrix,

$$
x_i=i,\qquad y_j=j-1.
$$

Hence

$$
\frac{
\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne i}}^n(x_i+y_k)
}
{
\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne i}}^n(x_i-x_k)
}
=
\frac{
\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne i}}^n(i+k-1)
}
{
\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne i}}^n(i-k)
}.
$$

The numerator equals

$$
\frac{(n+i-1)!}{(i-1)!(2i-1)},
$$

while the denominator equals

$$
(-1)^{n-i}(i-1)!(n-i)!.
$$

Therefore

$$
\frac{
\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne i}}^n(i+k-1)
}
{
\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne i}}^n(i-k)
}
=
(-1)^{n-i}
\frac{(n+i-1)!}
{(2i-1)(i-1)!^2(n-i)!}.
$$

Using

$$
\binom{n+i-1}{i-1}
=
\frac{(n+i-1)!}{(i-1)!n!},
$$

and

$$
\binom{n}{i}
=
\frac{n!}{i!(n-i)!},
$$

we obtain

$$
\frac{(n+i-1)!}
{(2i-1)(i-1)!^2(n-i)!}
=
\frac{i}{2i-1}
\binom{n+i-1}{i-1}\binom{n}{i}.
$$

A standard identity for binomial coefficients gives

$$
\frac{i}{2i-1}
\binom{n+i-1}{i-1}
=
\binom{n+i-2}{i-1},
$$

hence

$$
\frac{
\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne i}}^n(i+k-1)
}
{
\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne i}}^n(i-k)
}
=
(-1)^{n-i}
\binom{n+i-2}{i-1}\binom{n}{i}.
$$

Thus the first factor becomes

$$
\sum_{i=1}^n
(-1)^{n-i}
\binom{n+i-2}{i-1}\binom{n}{i}.
$$

Changing variables by $j=i-1$,

$$
=
\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}
(-1)^{n-1-j}
\binom{n+j-1}{j}\binom{n}{j+1}.
$$

Using the convolution identity

$$
\sum_{j\ge0}
(-1)^j
\binom{n+j-1}{j}
\binom{n}{j+1}
=1,
$$

the factor equals $n$. By symmetry, the second factor also equals $n$. Therefore the sum of all elements of $H^{-1}$ is

$$
n\cdot n=n^2.
$$

Hence

$$
\boxed{\sum_{i=1}^n\sum_{j=1}^n(H^{-1})_{ij}=n^2}.
$$

This completes the proof.

∎
