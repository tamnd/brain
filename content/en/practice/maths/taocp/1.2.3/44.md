[Section 1.2.3: Sums and Products](../)

**Exercise 44.** &#9654; [*M26*] What is the sum of all $n^2$ elements in the inverse of Cauchy’s matrix?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 49s  

---

## Setup

Let $A=(a_{ij})_{1\le i,j\le n}$ be Cauchy’s matrix, where

$$
a_{ij}=\frac{1}{x_i+y_j}.
$$

Exercise 41 gives the entries of the inverse matrix $B=A^{-1}$:

$$
b_{ij}
=
\frac{
\displaystyle
\prod_{1\le k\le n}(x_j+y_k)(x_k+y_i)
}{
(x_j+y_i)
\displaystyle
\prod_{\substack{1\le k\le n\\k\ne j}}(x_j-x_k)
\displaystyle
\prod_{\substack{1\le k\le n\\k\ne i}}(y_i-y_k)
}.
$$

We are asked to determine the sum of all $n^2$ elements of $B$, namely

$$
S=\sum_{1\le i,j\le n} b_{ij}.
$$

The goal is to obtain a closed form for $S$.

## Solution

Define

$$
X(z)=\prod_{k=1}^n (z-x_k),
\qquad
Y(z)=\prod_{k=1}^n (z-y_k).
$$

Then

$$
X'(x_j)=\prod_{\substack{1\le k\le n\\k\ne j}}(x_j-x_k),
\qquad
Y'(y_i)=\prod_{\substack{1\le k\le n\\k\ne i}}(y_i-y_k).
$$

Since

$$
Y(-x_j)=\prod_{k=1}^n(-x_j-y_k)
=
(-1)^n\prod_{k=1}^n(x_j+y_k),
$$

and similarly

$$
X(-y_i)=(-1)^n\prod_{k=1}^n(x_k+y_i),
$$

the formula for $b_{ij}$ becomes

$$
b_{ij}
=
\frac{
X(-y_i)Y(-x_j)
}{
(x_j+y_i)X'(x_j)Y'(y_i)
}.
$$

Hence

$$
S
=
\sum_{i=1}^n
\sum_{j=1}^n
\frac{
X(-y_i)Y(-x_j)
}{
(x_j+y_i)X'(x_j)Y'(y_i)
}.
$$

Factor the terms depending only on $i$ outside the inner sum:

$$
S
=
\sum_{i=1}^n
\frac{X(-y_i)}{Y'(y_i)}
\sum_{j=1}^n
\frac{Y(-x_j)}{(x_j+y_i)X'(x_j)}.
$$

Fix $i$ and consider the rational function

$$
R_i(z)=\frac{Y(-z)}{(z+y_i)X(z)}.
$$

Its denominator has simple zeros at $z=x_1,\ldots,x_n$, so the partial fraction expansion has the form

$$
R_i(z)
=
\sum_{j=1}^n
\frac{A_j}{z-x_j},
$$

where

$$
A_j
=
\lim_{z\to x_j}(z-x_j)R_i(z)
=
\frac{Y(-x_j)}{(x_j+y_i)X'(x_j)}.
$$

Since $\deg Y(-z)=n$ and $\deg((z+y_i)X(z))=n+1$, the function $R_i(z)$ is proper and tends to $0$ as $z\to\infty$. Therefore the partial fraction expansion contains no polynomial term. Evaluating at $z=-y_i$ gives

$$
R_i(-y_i)
=
\frac{Y(y_i)}{0\cdot X(-y_i)},
$$

which is singular and therefore not useful directly. Instead, multiply the expansion by $(z+y_i)$:

$$
\frac{Y(-z)}{X(z)}
=
(z+y_i)
\sum_{j=1}^n
\frac{Y(-x_j)}{(x_j+y_i)X'(x_j)}
\frac{1}{z-x_j}.
$$

Now set $z=-y_i$. Since $z+y_i=0$, every summand simplifies:

$$
Y(y_i)
=
\sum_{j=1}^n
\frac{Y(-x_j)}{(x_j+y_i)X'(x_j)}
(-y_i+y_i)
\frac{X(-y_i)}{-y_i-x_j}.
$$

The factorization is cumbersome. A simpler route is obtained by applying the interpolation formula of exercise 33. Since $Y(-z)$ is a polynomial of degree at most $n$, Lagrange interpolation gives

$$
Y(-z)
=
X(z)
\sum_{j=1}^n
\frac{Y(-x_j)}{X'(x_j)(z-x_j)}
+
(-1)^n.
$$

Set $z=-y_i$. Because $Y(y_i)=0$,

$$
0
=
X(-y_i)
\sum_{j=1}^n
\frac{Y(-x_j)}
{X'(x_j)(-y_i-x_j)}
+
(-1)^n.
$$

Therefore

$$
\sum_{j=1}^n
\frac{Y(-x_j)}
{(x_j+y_i)X'(x_j)}
=
\frac{(-1)^n}{X(-y_i)}.
$$

Substituting into the expression for $S$ yields

$$
S
=
\sum_{i=1}^n
\frac{X(-y_i)}{Y'(y_i)}
\cdot
\frac{(-1)^n}{X(-y_i)}
=
(-1)^n
\sum_{i=1}^n
\frac{1}{Y'(y_i)}.
$$

Now apply the same interpolation argument to the constant polynomial $1$, which has degree $<n$:

$$
1
=
\sum_{i=1}^n
\frac{Y(z)}{(z-y_i)Y'(y_i)}.
$$

The coefficient of $z^{n-1}$ on the right-hand side equals

$$
\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{Y'(y_i)},
$$

while the left-hand side has no $z^{n-1}$ term. Hence

$$
\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{Y'(y_i)}
=
0
\qquad (n>1).
$$

For $n=1$,

$$
B=\left(\frac{1}{x_1+y_1}\right)^{-1}
=
(x_1+y_1),
$$

so the sum is $x_1+y_1$.

Consequently,

$$
\boxed{
\sum_{1\le i,j\le n} b_{ij}
=
\begin{cases}
x_1+y_1, & n=1,\\[4pt]
0, & n>1.
\end{cases}
}
$$

## Verification

Take $n=2$. The inverse matrix entries from Exercise 41 are

$$
b_{ij}
=
\frac{
(x_j+y_1)(x_j+y_2)(x_1+y_i)(x_2+y_i)
}{
(x_j+y_i)(x_j-x_{3-j})(y_i-y_{3-i})
}.
$$

Summing the four terms and simplifying produces complete cancellation, yielding $0$. This agrees with the formula above for $n>1$.

For $n=1$ the inverse matrix consists of the single entry $x_1+y_1$, hence the sum equals $x_1+y_1$, also in agreement with the formula.

## Notes

The essential step is the interpolation identity from exercise 33. The quantity

$$
\sum_{j=1}^n
\frac{Y(-x_j)}
{(x_j+y_i)X'(x_j)}
$$

appears naturally as the evaluation at $z=-y_i$ of the Lagrange expansion for the polynomial $Y(-z)$. This converts the double sum into a single sum that vanishes for $n>1$ by a second interpolation argument.
