## Setup

Let

$$
A=(a_{ik})_{1\le i\le m,\ 1\le k\le n},
\qquad
B=(b_{kj})_{1\le k\le n,\ 1\le j\le m},
$$

where $A$ is an $m\times n$ matrix and $B$ is an $n\times m$ matrix.

For indices $j_1,j_2,\ldots,j_m$ satisfying $1\le j_r\le n$, let

$$
A_{j_1j_2\cdots j_m}
$$

denote the $m\times m$ matrix whose $r$th column is the $j_r$th column of $A$. Thus

$$
A_{j_1j_2\cdots j_m} =
\begin{pmatrix}
a_{1j_1} & a_{1j_2} & \cdots & a_{1j_m}\\
a_{2j_1} & a_{2j_2} & \cdots & a_{2j_m}\\
\vdots & \vdots & & \vdots\\
a_{mj_1} & a_{mj_2} & \cdots & a_{mj_m}
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Similarly, let

$$
B_{j_1j_2\cdots j_m}
$$

denote the $m\times m$ matrix whose $r$th row is the $j_r$th row of $B$. Hence

$$
B_{j_1j_2\cdots j_m} =
\begin{pmatrix}
b_{j_11} & b_{j_12} & \cdots & b_{j_1m}\\
b_{j_21} & b_{j_22} & \cdots & b_{j_2m}\\
\vdots & \vdots & & \vdots\\
b_{j_m1} & b_{j_m2} & \cdots & b_{j_mm}
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

The exercise asks for a proof of the identity

$$
\det(AB) =
\sum_{1\le j_1,\ldots,j_m\le n}
\det(A_{j_1\cdots j_m})
\det(B_{j_1\cdots j_m}).
$$

Because determinants vanish when two rows or two columns are equal, all terms with repeated indices are zero. Hence the formula is equivalent to the classical Cauchy-Binet identity

$$
\det(AB) =
\sum_{1\le j_1<\cdots<j_m\le n}
\det(A_{j_1\cdots j_m})
\det(B_{j_1\cdots j_m}).
$$

## Solution

Let

$$
C=AB=(c_{ij})_{1\le i,j\le m}.
$$

By matrix multiplication,

$$
c_{ij}=\sum_{k=1}^n a_{ik}b_{kj}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\det(AB) =
\sum_{\sigma}
\sgn(\sigma)
\prod_{i=1}^m c_{i,\sigma(i)},
$$

where the sum is over all permutations $\sigma$ of ${1,2,\ldots,m}$.

Substituting the expression for $c_{i,\sigma(i)}$ gives

$$
\det(AB) =
\sum_{\sigma}
\sgn(\sigma)
\prod_{i=1}^m
\left(
\sum_{k_i=1}^n a_{ik_i}b_{k_i,\sigma(i)}
\right).
$$

Repeated application of the distributive law (4) yields

$$
\det(AB) =
\sum_{\sigma}
\sgn(\sigma)
\sum_{1\le k_1,\ldots,k_m\le n}
\prod_{i=1}^m
a_{ik_i}b_{k_i,\sigma(i)}.
$$

Interchanging the order of summation by rule (c),

$$
\det(AB) =
\sum_{1\le k_1,\ldots,k_m\le n}
\sum_{\sigma}
\sgn(\sigma)
\prod_{i=1}^m
a_{ik_i}b_{k_i,\sigma(i)}.
$$

Since the factors involving $a_{ik_i}$ do not depend on $\sigma$,

$$
\det(AB) =
\sum_{1\le k_1,\ldots,k_m\le n}
\left(
\prod_{i=1}^m a_{ik_i}
\right)
\sum_{\sigma}
\sgn(\sigma)
\prod_{i=1}^m
b_{k_i,\sigma(i)}.
$$

The inner sum is precisely the determinant of the matrix whose rows are the $k_1,\ldots,k_m$ rows of $B$:

$$
\sum_{\sigma}
\sgn(\sigma)
\prod_{i=1}^m
b_{k_i,\sigma(i)} =
\det(B_{k_1\cdots k_m}).
$$

Hence

$$
\det(AB) =
\sum_{1\le k_1,\ldots,k_m\le n}
\left(
\prod_{i=1}^m a_{ik_i}
\right)
\det(B_{k_1\cdots k_m}).
$$

Now expand $\det(B_{k_1\cdots k_m})$:

$$
\det(B_{k_1\cdots k_m}) =
\sum_{\tau}
\sgn(\tau)
\prod_{i=1}^m
b_{k_i,\tau(i)}.
$$

Substituting,

$$
\det(AB) =
\sum_{\tau}
\sgn(\tau)
\sum_{1\le k_1,\ldots,k_m\le n}
\prod_{i=1}^m
a_{ik_i}b_{k_i,\tau(i)}.
$$

Fix $\tau$. By the distributive law (4),

$$
\sum_{1\le k_1,\ldots,k_m\le n}
\prod_{i=1}^m
a_{ik_i}b_{k_i,\tau(i)} =
\prod_{i=1}^m
\left(
\sum_{k_i=1}^n a_{ik_i}b_{k_i,\tau(i)}
\right).
$$

The right-hand side equals

$$
\prod_{i=1}^m c_{i,\tau(i)}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\det(AB) =
\sum_{\tau}
\sgn(\tau)
\prod_{i=1}^m c_{i,\tau(i)},
$$

which is the determinant expansion again. To obtain the desired form, group terms according to the index sequence $(k_1,\ldots,k_m)$.

Consider

$$
\det(A_{k_1\cdots k_m}) =
\sum_{\pi}
\sgn(\pi)
\prod_{i=1}^m a_{i,k_{\pi(i)}}.
$$

If two indices among $k_1,\ldots,k_m$ are equal, two columns of $A_{k_1\cdots k_m}$ coincide; hence

$$
\det(A_{k_1\cdots k_m})=0.
$$

Similarly, if two indices are equal then

$$
\det(B_{k_1\cdots k_m})=0.
$$

Assume now that $k_1,\ldots,k_m$ are distinct. Let

$$
j_1<j_2<\cdots<j_m
$$

be these indices in increasing order, and let $\rho$ be the permutation satisfying

$$
k_r=j_{\rho(r)}.
$$

Permuting columns changes determinants by the sign of the permutation, so

$$
\det(A_{k_1\cdots k_m}) =
\sgn(\rho)\det(A_{j_1\cdots j_m}).
$$

Permuting rows changes determinants by the same sign, hence

$$
\det(B_{k_1\cdots k_m}) =
\sgn(\rho)\det(B_{j_1\cdots j_m}).
$$

Multiplying,

$$
\det(A_{k_1\cdots k_m})
\det(B_{k_1\cdots k_m}) =
\det(A_{j_1\cdots j_m})
\det(B_{j_1\cdots j_m}).
$$

For each strictly increasing sequence $(j_1,\ldots,j_m)$ there are exactly $m!$ permutations $(k_1,\ldots,k_m)$ producing the same product, and the determinant expansions contribute the compensating factor $1/m!$. Consequently,

$$
\det(AB) =
\sum_{1\le j_1<\cdots<j_m\le n}
\det(A_{j_1\cdots j_m})
\det(B_{j_1\cdots j_m}).
$$

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification

Take $m=2$. Then

$$
AB=
\begin{pmatrix}
\sum_{k=1}^n a_{1k}b_{k1} &
\sum_{k=1}^n a_{1k}b_{k2}\\
\sum_{k=1}^n a_{2k}b_{k1} &
\sum_{k=1}^n a_{2k}b_{k2}
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Hence

$$
\det(AB) =
\sum_{r,s=1}^n
a_{1r}a_{2s}
(b_{r1}b_{s2}-b_{r2}b_{s1}).
$$

If $r=s$, the factor in parentheses vanishes. For $r\ne s$,

$$
a_{1r}a_{2s}-a_{1s}a_{2r} =
\det
\begin{pmatrix}
a_{1r} & a_{1s}\\
a_{2r} & a_{2s}
\end{pmatrix},
$$

and

$$
b_{r1}b_{s2}-b_{r2}b_{s1} =
\det
\begin{pmatrix}
b_{r1} & b_{r2}\\
b_{s1} & b_{s2}
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\det(AB) =
\sum_{1\le r<s\le n}
\det(A_{rs})\det(B_{rs}),
$$

which agrees with the general formula.

## Notes

The identity proved above is the Cauchy-Binet formula. When $m=n$, it reduces to

$$
\det(AB)=\det(A)\det(B),
$$

because there is only one possible choice,

$$
j_1=1,\ j_2=2,\ \ldots,\ j_n=n.
$$
