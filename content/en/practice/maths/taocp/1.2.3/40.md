[Section 1.2.3: Sums and Products](../)

**Exercise 40.** [*M24*] Show that the inverse of VandermondeŌĆÖs matrix is given by

$$b_{ij} = \left( \sum_{\substack{1 \le k_1 < \cdots < k_{n-j} \le n \\ k_1, \ldots, k_{n-j} \ne i}} (-1)^{j-1} x_{k_1} \ldots x_{k_{n-j}} \right) \bigg/ x_i \prod_{\substack{1 \le k \le n \\ k \ne i}} (x_k - x_i).$$

DonŌĆÖt be dismayed by the complicated sum in the numerator, it is just the coefficient of $x^{j-1}$ in the polynomial $(x_1 - x) \ldots (x_n - x) / (x_i - x)$.

## Solution

Let $A=(a_{ij})_{1\le i,j\le n}$ be VandermondeŌĆÖs matrix,

$$
a_{ij}=x_i^{\,j-1}.
$$

We must show that the matrix $B=(b_{ij})$ defined by

$$
b_{ij} =
\left(
\sum_{\substack{1 \le k_1 < \cdots < k_{n-j} \le n \\
k_1,\ldots,k_{n-j}\ne i}}
(-1)^{j-1}x_{k_1}\cdots x_{k_{n-j}}
\right)
\bigg/
x_i\prod_{\substack{1\le k\le n\\k\ne i}}(x_k-x_i)
$$

is the inverse of $A$.

By the remark in the exercise, the numerator is the coefficient of $x^{j-1}$ in

$$
\frac{(x_1-x)\cdots(x_n-x)}{x_i-x}.
$$

Define

$$
P_i(x)=\frac{(x_1-x)\cdots(x_n-x)}{x_i-x}.
$$

Since the factor $(x_i-x)$ is removed, $P_i(x)$ is a polynomial of degree $n-1$. Write

$$
P_i(x)=\sum_{j=1}^n c_{ij}x^{j-1}.
$$

Then

$$
c_{ij}=
\sum_{\substack{1 \le k_1 < \cdots < k_{n-j} \le n \\
k_1,\ldots,k_{n-j}\ne i}}
(-1)^{j-1}x_{k_1}\cdots x_{k_{n-j}},
$$

hence

$$
b_{ij}=\frac{c_{ij}}
{x_i\displaystyle\prod_{\substack{1\le k\le n\\k\ne i}}(x_k-x_i)}.
$$

To prove that $B=A^{-1}$, it suffices to show that

$$
AB=I.
$$

The $(r,s)$ entry of $AB$ is

$$
\sum_{j=1}^n a_{rj}b_{js} =
\sum_{j=1}^n x_r^{\,j-1} b_{js}.
$$

Using the definition of $b_{js}$,

$$
\sum_{j=1}^n x_r^{\,j-1} b_{js} =
\sum_{j=1}^n
x_r^{\,j-1}
\frac{c_{js}}
{x_j\displaystyle\prod_{\substack{1\le k\le n\\k\ne j}}(x_k-x_j)}.
$$

Since

$$
P_j(x)=\sum_{s=1}^n c_{js}x^{s-1},
$$

the coefficient $c_{js}$ is attached to the power $x^{s-1}$. Therefore

$$
\sum_{s=1}^n c_{js}x_r^{\,s-1}=P_j(x_r).
$$

Hence the $(r,s)$ entry may be interpreted through the polynomial

$$
L_j(x)=
\frac{P_j(x)}
{x_j\displaystyle\prod_{\substack{1\le k\le n\\k\ne j}}(x_k-x_j)}.
$$

Indeed,

$$
L_j(x_r) =
\sum_{s=1}^n x_r^{\,s-1} b_{js}.
$$

Therefore

$$
(AB)_{rs} =
\sum_{j=1}^n a_{rj}b_{js} =
L_s(x_r).
$$

Now compute $L_s(x_r)$. Since

$$
P_s(x)=\prod_{\substack{1\le k\le n\\k\ne s}}(x_k-x),
$$

we have

$$
P_s(x_r)=0
\qquad (r\ne s),
$$

because one factor becomes $x_r-x_r=0$.

When $r=s$,

$$
P_s(x_s) =
\prod_{\substack{1\le k\le n\\k\ne s}}(x_k-x_s).
$$

Therefore

$$
L_s(x_r)=0
\qquad (r\ne s),
$$

and

$$
L_s(x_s) =
\frac{
\prod_{k\ne s}(x_k-x_s)
}{
x_s\prod_{k\ne s}(x_k-x_s)
} =
1.
$$

Hence

$$
L_s(x_r)=\delta_{rs}.
$$

Consequently,

$$
(AB)_{rs}=\delta_{rs},
$$

so

$$
AB=I.
$$

Since $A$ is nonsingular by Exercise 37, the matrix $B$ is the inverse of $A$. Therefore the inverse entries are

$$
\boxed{
b_{ij} =
\left(
\sum_{\substack{1 \le k_1 < \cdots < k_{n-j} \le n \\
k_1,\ldots,k_{n-j}\ne i}}
(-1)^{j-1}x_{k_1}\cdots x_{k_{n-j}}
\right)
\bigg/
x_i\prod_{\substack{1\le k\le n\\k\ne i}}(x_k-x_i)
}.
$$

This completes the proof.
