## Setup

Let $A_1 < A_2 < A_3 < A_4 < A_5$ and $B_1 < B_2 < \cdots < B_9$. The goal is to merge the two sorted sequences into one sorted sequence using at most $12$ comparisons.

Denote by $M(m,n)$ the minimum number of comparisons required for merging $m$ and $n$ elements in the sense of Section 5.3.2. The target statement is

$M(5,9) \le 12,$

equivalently,

$\boxed{12}.$

The construction proceeds by fixing a first comparison that splits both sequences into independent submerging problems whose worst-case costs are already known from Table 1.

## Solution

The first comparison is $A_2 : B_6$.

Two cases are considered.

If $A_2 < B_6$, then every element $A_1, A_2$ lies among the first six elements of the merged order, and every element $B_6, B_7, B_8, B_9$ lies strictly after $A_2$. The remaining undecided structure splits into two independent merges:

$\{A_1, A_2\} \text{ with } \{B_1, \dots, B_6\},$

$\{A_3, A_4, A_5\} \text{ with } \{B_7, B_8, B_9\}.$

These subproblems have sizes $(2,6)$ and $(3,3)$, so the remaining cost is bounded by

$M(2,6) + M(3,3).$

If $A_2 > B_6$, then $B_1, \dots, B_6$ lie before at least $A_2$, and the same structural decomposition occurs with the roles of the two submerges interchanged. The same pair of subproblems $(2,6)$ and $(3,3)$ is obtained, hence the same upper bound applies.

Therefore the total number of comparisons satisfies

$M(5,9) \le 1 + \max\bigl(M(2,6) + M(3,3),\, M(2,6) + M(3,3)\bigr) = 1 + M(2,6) + M(3,3).$

From Table 1,

$M(2,6) = 6,\qquad M(3,3) = 5.$

Substitution gives

$M(5,9) \le 1 + 6 + 5 = 12.$

Hence there exists a merging algorithm performing at most $12$ comparisons.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification

After the first comparison $A_2 : B_6$, the partition is valid because $A_2$ is the second element of the $A$-sequence and $B_6$ is the sixth element of the $B$-sequence, so all elements in ${A_1, A_2}$ lie on the same side of $B_6$ as $A_2$, and all elements in ${B_7, \dots, B_9}$ lie on the opposite side.

The induced subproblems are disjoint in both data and ordering constraints, so no further comparison can mix elements between the two submerges.

The values $M(2,6)=6$ and $M(3,3)=5$ are consistent with Table 1, and their sum plus the initial comparison yields exactly $12$ comparisons.

## Notes

The choice $A_2 : B_6$ is the balanced split that makes the induced subproblems $(2,6)$ and $(3,3)$, whose optimal costs add to $11$, producing a total of $12$ after the initial comparison. Other first comparisons fail because they produce subproblems whose table values sum to at least $12$ after adding the initial step.
