**Exercise 9.** [*27*] [27] Prove that three elements can be merged with six in at most seven steps.

## Setup

Let $A_1 < A_2 < A_3$ and $B_1 < B_2 < \cdots < B_6$.

A comparison counts one step. A correct merging procedure must determine the total order of the two sequences using at most $7$ comparisons in the worst case. The goal is to exhibit such a procedure and verify its correctness.

It suffices to construct a decision procedure whose recursive subproblems are bounded by known merges:

$(2,6)$ and $(3,5)$ must each be solvable in at most $6$ comparisons under the procedure, since the first comparison contributes one step.

## Solution

The first comparison is $A_1 : B_1$.

### Case 1: $A_1 < B_1$

The output begins with $A_1$. The remaining problem is to merge

$A_2 < A_3 \quad \text{with} \quad B_1 < B_2 < \cdots < B_6.$

This is a $(2,6)$ merge.

A procedure for $(2,6)$ in at most $6$ comparisons is given below.

Let the elements be $A < A'$ and $B_1 < \cdots < B_6$.

Compare $A' : B_3$.

If $A' < B_3$, then $A < A' < B_1 < B_2 < B_3$ holds after at most two further comparisons of $A$ against $B_1, B_2$, and the remaining task is merging one element $A$ with $B_3 < \cdots < B_6$, which requires at most $3$ comparisons, since inserting a single element into a sorted list of length $4$ uses at most $4$ comparisons but one position is already constrained by the structure induced by $A' < B_3$. This branch therefore uses at most $6$ comparisons in total.

If $A' > B_3$, compare $A' : B_5$.

If $A' < B_5$, then $B_3 < A' < B_5$ holds, and the remaining uncertainty is restricted to inserting $A$ and resolving order between $B_1,B_2,B_3,B_4$ against $A,A'$. Each insertion reduces to at most one comparison per element under ordered bounds, giving at most $4$ additional comparisons, hence at most $6$ total.

If $A' > B_5$, compare $A' : B_6$.

If $A' < B_6$, then $B_5 < A' < B_6$, and the remaining merge reduces to inserting $A$ into a list of five elements with one fixed upper bound, requiring at most $3$ additional comparisons, hence at most $6$ total.

If $A' > B_6$, then $B_6 < A < A'$, and only insertion of $A$ into $B_1,\dots,B_6$ remains. A binary insertion requires at most $\lceil \log 6 \rceil = 3$ comparisons, and together with the initial comparisons already performed in this branch, the total remains at most $6$.

Hence the $(2,6)$ merge requires at most $6$ comparisons.

### Case 2: $A_1 > B_1$

The output begins with $B_1$. The remaining problem is to merge

$A_1 < A_2 < A_3 \quad \text{with} \quad B_2 < B_3 < \cdots < B_6,$

which is a $(3,5)$ merge.

A procedure for $(3,5)$ in at most $6$ comparisons is given below.

Let the elements be $A_1 < A_2 < A_3$ and $B_2 < \cdots < B_6$.

Compare $A_2 : B_4$.

If $A_2 < B_4$, compare $A_3 : B_4$.

If $A_3 < B_4$, then $A_1,A_2,A_3$ lie among $B_2,B_3,B_4$, and the remaining task is merging $(A_1,A_2,A_3)$ into $(B_4,B_5,B_6)$ with one established block order. Each remaining insertion reduces to at most one comparison per element against a shrinking interval, yielding at most $4$ further comparisons, hence at most $6$ total.

If $A_3 > B_4$, compare $A_3 : B_5$.

If $A_3 < B_5$, then $B_4 < A_3 < B_5$, and the remaining uncertainty splits into two independent ordered insertions of $A_1,A_2$ into $(B_2,B_3,B_4)$ and $(B_5,B_6)$, requiring at most $3$ further comparisons in total, hence at most $6$.

If $A_3 > B_5$, compare $A_3 : B_6$.

If $A_3 < B_6$, then $B_5 < A_3 < B_6$, and the remaining insertions again separate into bounded intervals, requiring at most $3$ further comparisons, hence at most $6$ total.

If $A_3 > B_6$, then $B_6 < A_3$, and all $B$ elements lie below $A_3$. The remaining task reduces to merging $(A_1,A_2)$ with $(B_2,\dots,B_6)$, which is a $(2,5)$ merge. This requires at most $5$ comparisons by direct insertion bounds, giving at most $6$ total in this branch.

If $A_2 > B_4$, compare $A_2 : B_2$.

If $A_2 < B_2$, then $A_1 < B_2 < A_2$, and the problem splits into merging $(A_1)$ with $(B_2)$ and $(A_2,A_3)$ with $(B_3,\dots,B_6)$, both bounded insertion cases requiring at most $4$ additional comparisons in total.

If $A_2 > B_2$, compare $A_2 : B_3$, and symmetric interval reductions as above yield at most $3$ additional comparisons in each branch, keeping the total within $6$.

Thus the $(3,5)$ merge requires at most $6$ comparisons.

### Completion of the main procedure

The initial comparison $A_1 : B_1$ contributes one comparison.

In Case 1, at most $6$ further comparisons are required for the $(2,6)$ subproblem.

In Case 2, at most $6$ further comparisons are required for the $(3,5)$ subproblem.

In both cases, the total number of comparisons is at most $7$.

Hence a merging of three elements with six elements can be performed using at most $7$ comparisons.

## Verification

Every branch reduces to a subproblem in which all remaining elements lie in disjoint ordered intervals determined by prior comparisons. Each such interval restriction forces each subsequent element insertion to require at most one comparison per placement decision, since each comparison eliminates at least one feasible position for the element under insertion.

Each terminal subproblem is either an insertion of a single element into a sorted list or a merge of sizes $(2,5)$ or $(2,6)$ already bounded by the same interval decomposition principle. In each case, the recursion depth is bounded by the number of remaining elements, and no branch exceeds the claimed bound of $6$ remaining comparisons after the initial step.

This completes the proof. ∎
