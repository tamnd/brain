**Exercise 13.** [*M16*] [M16] Explain how to put weights on the horizontal line segments of a lattice, instead of the vertical segments, so that the sum of the horizontal weights on a lattice path is the number of inversions in the corresponding 2-ordered permutation.

## Solution

In Exercise 12, each inversion is identified with one unit square in the region between the lattice path and the staircase path. The weighting on the vertical segments assigns each unit square to its left edge. To obtain an equivalent weighting on the horizontal segments, assign each unit square to its lower edge instead.

This leads to a fixed weighting of the lattice, independent of the chosen path.

Consider the lattice whose vertices are $(i,j)$, where $0 \le i,j \le n$. Let $S$ denote the staircase path. For a horizontal lattice segment from

$$
(i,j)\ \text{to}\ (i+1,j),
$$

define its weight to be the number of unit squares immediately above that segment and below the staircase path. Equivalently, if the staircase lies at height $h(i)$ above the column between $x=i$ and $x=i+1$, then the horizontal segment at level $j$ receives the fixed weight

$$
w(i,j)=\max\{0,h(i)-j\}.
$$

This depends only on the position of the segment in the lattice and the fixed staircase path, not on the lattice path corresponding to the permutation.

Now let $P$ be any lattice path. Every unit square lying between $P$ and the staircase path has a unique lower horizontal edge, and that edge belongs to $P$. Conversely, if a horizontal edge of $P$ has weight $k$, then there are exactly $k$ such unit squares immediately above it.

Hence

$$
\sum_{\substack{\text{horizontal}\\\text{edges of }P}} w(e)
=
\#\{\text{unit squares between }P\text{ and the staircase}\}.
$$

Each unit square is counted exactly once, through its unique lower edge, so the sum of the horizontal weights is exactly the area between the path and the staircase.

By the result of Exercise 12, this area is the number of inversions of the corresponding $2$-ordered permutation. Therefore

$$
\boxed{\sum_{\text{horizontal edges of }P} w(e)=\text{number of inversions}.}
$$

Thus the required horizontal weighting is obtained by assigning to each horizontal lattice segment the number of unit squares lying directly above it and below the fixed staircase path. This weighting is intrinsic to the lattice and yields the inversion number for every corresponding lattice path.
