Let $a=h_{s+1}$ and $b=h_{s+2}$, with $\gcd(a,b)=1$. The file after the $a$- and $b$-passes is both $a$-ordered and $b$-ordered, meaning

$$
K_i \le K_{i+a}, \qquad K_i \le K_{i+b}
\quad \text{for all valid } i.
$$

During the $h_s$-pass, Algorithm D operates on the subsequences

$$
R_r = (R_{r+kh_s})_{k\ge 0}, \qquad 0 \le r < h_s.
$$

Let $B^{(s)}$ be the total number of moves. Each move equals an inversion in one such subsequence, so

$$
B^{(s)} = \sum_{r=0}^{h_s-1} B_r,
$$

where $B_r$ is the inversion count in $R_r$.

The goal is to show

$$
B^{(s)} = O\!\left(\frac{Nab}{h_s}\right).
$$

### 1. Key structural fact (correct use of the Frobenius condition)

Fix two indices $i<j$. If the index difference satisfies

$$
j-i = xa + yb \quad (x,y \ge 0),
$$

then repeated use of monotonicity gives

$$
K_i \le K_{i+a} \le K_{i+2a} \le \cdots \le K_j,
$$

so necessarily $K_i \le K_j$. Hence:

$$
j-i \in \langle a,b\rangle \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \text{no inversion between } i \text{ and } j.
$$

Therefore, any inversion $K_i > K_j$ must satisfy

$$
j-i \notin \langle a,b\rangle.
$$

By the Frobenius theorem, the largest nonrepresentable integer is $ab-a-b$. Hence every nonrepresentable integer lies in

$$
\{1,2,\dots,ab-a-b\}.
$$

So:

$$
K_i > K_j \implies j-i \le ab-a-b.
$$

This is the only correct way the Frobenius structure enters: it bounds _where inversions are allowed_, not how many occur in a probabilistic sense.

### 2. Transfer to an $h_s$-subsequence

Inside a fixed residue class $r \pmod{h_s}$, write

$$
i = r + kh_s, \qquad j = r + \ell h_s, \qquad k<\ell.
$$

Then

$$
j-i = (\ell-k)h_s.
$$

From the previous section, if $K_i > K_j$, then

$$
(\ell-k)h_s \le ab-a-b.
$$

Hence

$$
\ell-k \le \frac{ab-a-b}{h_s} = O\!\left(\frac{ab}{h_s}\right).
$$

So for a fixed element $R_{r+\ell h_s}$, only the preceding

$$
O\!\left(\frac{ab}{h_s}\right)
$$

positions in its $h_s$-chain can possibly form inversions with it.

This is the corrected core step: inversions are not classified by representability, but _representability forbids inversions beyond a bounded gap_, which is what produces the estimate.

### 3. Counting inversions in one residue class

Each residue class has

$$
m = \Theta\!\left(\frac{N}{h_s}\right)
$$

elements.

Each element participates in at most $O(ab/h_s)$ inversions (as the larger element of an inversion pair). Therefore,

$$
B_r = O\!\left(\frac{N}{h_s} \cdot \frac{ab}{h_s}\right).
$$

### 4. Summing over all residue classes

There are $h_s$ residue classes, so

$$
B^{(s)} = \sum_{r=0}^{h_s-1} B_r
= h_s \cdot O\!\left(\frac{N}{h_s} \cdot \frac{ab}{h_s}\right).
$$

Thus

$$
B^{(s)} = O\!\left(\frac{Nab}{h_s}\right).
$$

Substituting back $a=h_{s+1}$, $b=h_{s+2}$,

$$
B^{(s)} = O\!\left(\frac{N h_{s+1} h_{s+2}}{h_s}\right).
$$

### 5. What was corrected

The earlier proof failed because it tried to _classify inversions by representability_. The correct direction is the opposite:

- Representable gaps force monotone order, hence **exclude inversions**.
- Nonrepresentable gaps are the only possible locations of inversions.
- Frobenius theory is used only to bound the maximum size of such gaps.

This yields a direct combinatorial bound on how far an element can interact within its $h_s$-chain, which completes the proof.
