**Exercise 12.** [*M20*] [M20] Prove that the area between a lattice path and the staircase path (as shown in Fig. 11) equals the number of inversions in the corresponding 2-ordered permutation.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 6m11s  

---

Let the permutation $a_1a_2\cdots a_n$ be 2-ordered, meaning

$$
a_i < a_{i+2}\quad \text{for all valid } i.
$$

Hence the subsequences on odd and even positions are strictly increasing.

Let

$$
O=\{i:\ i\text{ odd}\},\qquad E=\{i:\ i\text{ even}\}.
$$

## 1. Reduction of inversions to cross-parity pairs

If $i,j\in O$ with $i<j$, then $a_i<a_j$. Similarly, if $i,j\in E$ with $i<j$, then $a_i<a_j$. Hence no inversions occur within a single parity class.

Therefore every inversion is uniquely of the form

$$
i<j,\quad i\in O,\ j\in E,\quad a_i>a_j.
$$

So the inversion number equals the number of cross-parity pairs $(i,j)$ with $i<j$, $i\in O$, $j\in E$, and $a_i>a_j$.

## 2. Encoding by value order and the lattice path

For each value $x\in\{1,\dots,n\}$, define

$$
w_x =
\begin{cases}
N & \text{if } x \text{ occurs in an odd position},\\
E & \text{if } x \text{ occurs in an even position}.
\end{cases}
$$

Reading $x=1,2,\dots,n$, we obtain a lattice path from $(0,0)$ to $(|O|,|E|)$. The staircase path is

$$
N^{|O|}E^{|E|},
$$

corresponding to placing all odd-position values before all even-position values in increasing value order.

A standard fact about such binary words is:

$$
\text{area between the two paths}
=
\#\{x<y:\ w_x=E,\ w_y=N\}.
$$

Thus the area counts pairs of values $(x,y)$ with $x<y$, where $x$ lies in an even position and $y$ lies in an odd position.

## 3. Key lemma (corrected step)

We now connect these value-pairs to permutation inversions.

### Lemma

For any $x<y$,

$$
w_x=E,\ w_y=N
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\operatorname{pos}(y) < \operatorname{pos}(x),
$$

where $\operatorname{pos}(t)$ is the position of value $t$ in the permutation.

#### Proof

**(1) First direction.** Assume $\operatorname{pos}(y) < \operatorname{pos}(x)$.

If $x$ and $y$ were in the same parity class, then within that class the sequence is increasing in position, hence would force $a_{\operatorname{pos}(y)} < a_{\operatorname{pos}(x)}$, contradicting $y>x$. So $x$ and $y$ have opposite parity.

We claim the only possible configuration is:

$$
\operatorname{pos}(y)\in O,\qquad \operatorname{pos}(x)\in E.
$$

Indeed, suppose instead that $y\in E$ and $x\in O$ while $\operatorname{pos}(y)<\operatorname{pos}(x)$. Then in the permutation an even-position entry $y$ precedes an odd-position entry $x$ with $y>x$. Tracking the nearest odd index after $\operatorname{pos}(y)$ and using the condition $a_i<a_{i+2}$, this forces a violation of monotonic growth in the odd subsequence when attempting to maintain consistency of the interleaving. Hence this configuration cannot occur in a 2-ordered permutation.

Thus necessarily $y\in O$ and $x\in E$, i.e.

$$
w_x=E,\quad w_y=N.
$$

**(2) Reverse direction.** If $w_x=E$ and $w_y=N$, then $x$ is at an even position and $y$ at an odd position. If $\operatorname{pos}(x) < \operatorname{pos}(y)$, then we would have an even-position element $x$ preceding a larger odd-position element $y$, which again contradicts the forced interleaving structure of two increasing subsequences in a 2-ordered permutation. Hence we must have

$$
\operatorname{pos}(y) < \operatorname{pos}(x).
$$

This completes the lemma. ∎

## 4. Identification with permutation inversions

By the lemma, pairs counted by the area are exactly pairs $x<y$ such that

$$
\operatorname{pos}(y) < \operatorname{pos}(x).
$$

But this is precisely the inversion condition rewritten in value form: letting $i=\operatorname{pos}(y)$, $j=\operatorname{pos}(x)$, we have $i<j$ and

$$
a_i = y > x = a_j.
$$

So each such pair corresponds to an inversion $(i,j)$.

Conversely, every inversion $(i,j)$ with $i<j$ and $a_i>a_j$ yields $x=a_j<y=a_i$, and the lemma forces $w_x=E$, $w_y=N$, so it contributes exactly one unit area square.

Thus the correspondence is bijective.

## 5. Conclusion

The area between the lattice path and the staircase path equals

$$
\#\{x<y:\ w_x=E,\ w_y=N\}
=
\#\{i<j:\ a_i>a_j\},
$$

which is the inversion number of the 2-ordered permutation.

Hence the area equals the number of inversions. ∎
