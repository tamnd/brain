[Section 5.2.1: Sorting by Insertion](../)

**Exercise 8.** [*10*] [10] Is Algorithm D a stable sorting algorithm?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 8m09s  

---

The goal is to show a **real execution of Algorithm D (Shellsort)** in which two equal keys are reversed before the final $h=1$ pass, so that the stable final insertion sort preserves the wrong order. That is sufficient to prove instability.

## Claim

Algorithm D is **not stable**.

## Key fact about a single $h$-pass

For a fixed increment $h$, Algorithm D performs insertion sort on each subsequence

$$
(R_i, R_{i+h}, R_{i+2h}, \dots).
$$

Two crucial properties hold:

- Each subsequence is stably sorted internally.
- Elements in different subsequences never interact during that pass.

However, elements _do move to different absolute positions_ within the full array, because each subsequence occupies scattered indices. This is where instability can arise.

## Construction of a counterexample

Use increment sequence $h=3,1$. It is enough to show instability already after the $h=3$ pass.

We construct records with keys:

- Two equal keys: $A_1, A_2$ both with key $5$
- Auxiliary keys chosen to force movement inside subsequences

Initial array:

$$
( A_1, D, A_2, B, E, C )
$$

with keys:

$$
(5, 7, 5, 1, 8, 9)
$$

So explicitly:

$$
\begin{aligned}
R_1 &= A_1(5), \quad R_2 = D(7), \quad R_3 = A_2(5), \\
R_4 &= B(1), \quad R_5 = E(8), \quad R_6 = C(9).
\end{aligned}
$$

## Perform the $h=3$ pass

The subsequences are:

### Subsequence 1: positions $1,4$

$$
(A_1(5), B(1))
$$

Insertion sort yields:

$$
(B(1), A_1(5))
$$

So $B$ moves to position 1, $A_1$ moves to position 4.

### Subsequence 2: positions $2,5$

$$
(D(7), E(8))
$$

Already sorted:

$$
(D(7), E(8))
$$

### Subsequence 3: positions $3,6$

$$
(A_2(5), C(9))
$$

Already sorted:

$$
(A_2(5), C(9))
$$

## Result after the $h=3$ pass

Interleaving the updated subsequences gives:

$$
(B, D, A_2, A_1, E, C).
$$

So the array is now:

$$
1:B,\; 2:D,\; 3:A_2,\; 4:A_1,\; 5:E,\; 6:C.
$$

## Observe the inversion of equal keys

Initially:

$$
A_1 \text{ (position 1) precedes } A_2 \text{ (position 3)}.
$$

After the $h=3$ pass:

$$
A_2 \text{ (position 3) precedes } A_1 \text{ (position 4)}.
$$

Thus the relative order of equal keys has been reversed.

## Final $h=1$ pass

The final pass is standard insertion sort on the whole array, which is **stable**. Therefore, it preserves the existing relative order of equal keys:

$$
A_2 \prec A_1 \quad \text{remains unchanged}.
$$

So the algorithm outputs a sequence in which the equal keys are reversed relative to their input order.

## Why this proves instability

Stability requires:

> If two keys are equal and $A_1$ precedes $A_2$ initially, then $A_1$ must precede $A_2$ in the final output.

We exhibited:

- Initial order: $A_1 \prec A_2$
- Final order: $A_2 \prec A_1$

Hence the requirement fails.

## Conclusion

Algorithm D (Shellsort) is **not stable**, because an $h>1$ pass can move equal keys across interleaved subsequences in such a way that their relative order is reversed before the final stable insertion sort, which then preserves the wrong order.
