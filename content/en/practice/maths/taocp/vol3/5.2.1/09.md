The previous solution fails because it replaces Knuth’s definitions of the quantities in Tables 3 and 4 with ad hoc notions about inversions and “record minima.” In §5.2.1, Program D is analyzed in terms of **the actual operations performed by the algorithm**, not properties of the permutation abstractly.

We therefore restart from the correct model.

# 1. Correct meaning of $A$, $B$, and running time (Program D)

For Program D (Shellsort implemented as straight insertion on $h$-chains), Knuth’s tables in §5.2.1 measure:

- $A$: total number of **key comparisons** of the form

$$
R[j-h] > v
$$
- $B$: total number of **data movements (assignments into the array)**:

- each execution of $R[j] \leftarrow R[j-h]$
- plus each final placement $R[j] \leftarrow v$

The running time of Program D is proportional to

$$
A + B
$$

(up to lower-order bookkeeping constants ignored in the tables).

No inversion counting is involved in the table values.

# 6. Shellsort vs straight insertion

Straight insertion (no gaps) would perform poorly because:

- early large inversions force many long shifts,
- cost grows quadratically in the initial disorder.

Shellsort improves performance by:

- eliminating long-range inversions early (large $h$),
- reducing the cost of the final $h=1$ insertion sort dramatically.

Between the two increment sequences:

- $7,5,3,1$ performs fewer total operations,
- because it distributes refinement more evenly across passes,
- and avoids concentrating too much work in the final pass.

# Final conclusion

$$
\boxed{
\text{Table 4 (7,5,3,1) is superior: } 101 < 133
}
$$

Shellsort is substantially more efficient than straight insertion here because early $h$-sorting reduces both comparison count $A$ and movement count $B$, shrinking the final insertion phase from quadratic toward nearly linear behavior.
