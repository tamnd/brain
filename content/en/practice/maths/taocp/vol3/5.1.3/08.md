[Section 5.1.3: Runs](../)

**Exercise 8.** [*M24*] [M24] (P. A. MacMahon.) Show that the probability that the first run of a sufficiently long permutation has length $l_1$, the second has length $l_2$, ..., and the $k$th has length $\ge l_k$, is

$$
\frac{1}{l_1! \cdots l_k!}.
$$

**Solve time:** 3m34s  

---

Let

$$
m=l_1+\cdots+l_k,
$$

and let $E(l_1,\dots,l_k)$ be the event that, in a random permutation

$$
a_1a_2\cdots a_n,
$$

the first run has length $l_1$, the second run has length $l_2$, $\dots$, the $(k-1)$st run has length $l_{k-1}$, and the $k$th run has length at least $l_k$.

Since $m$ is fixed and $n$ is sufficiently large, the event depends only on the relative order of

$$
a_1,\dots,a_m.
$$

Indeed, whether the first $k-1$ runs end at positions

$$
l_1,\ l_1+l_2,\ \dots,\ l_1+\cdots+l_{k-1}
$$

and whether the $k$th run continues through position $m$ is determined entirely by the comparisons among the first $m$ entries.

Hence we may replace $a_1,\dots,a_m$ by a uniformly random permutation of $\{1,\dots,m\}$. There are $m!$ possible relative orders, all equally likely.

Define

$$
s_i=l_1+\cdots+l_i,\qquad s_0=0.
$$

The event $E(l_1,\dots,l_k)$ is equivalent to the inequalities

$$
a_1<\cdots<a_{s_1}>a_{s_1+1}
<\cdots<a_{s_2}>a_{s_2+1}
<\cdots
>a_{s_{k-1}+1}<\cdots<a_{s_k}.
$$

Thus, within each block

$$
B_i=\{s_{i-1}+1,\dots,s_i\},
$$

the values are increasing, and for $i<k$ there is a descent

$$
a_{s_i}>a_{s_i+1}.
$$

We must count the number $N$ of permutations of $\{1,\dots,m\}$ satisfying these inequalities.

Consider the poset $P$ on the positions $1,\dots,m$ generated by the relations

$$
s_{i-1}+1 < s_{i-1}+2 < \cdots < s_i
\qquad (1\le i\le k),
$$

and

$$
s_i+1 < s_i
\qquad (1\le i<k).
$$

A permutation $a_1,\dots,a_m$ satisfies the required inequalities exactly when the assignment

$$
r\mapsto a_r
$$

is an order-preserving labeling of $P$. Therefore $N$ is the number of linear extensions of $P$.

Now observe the structure of $P$. Let

$$
b_i=s_{i-1}+1
$$

be the first position of block $B_i$. The covering relations are

$$
b_i<b_i+1<\cdots<s_i,
$$

and

$$
b_{i+1}<s_i.
$$

Hence the Hasse diagram is a rooted tree whose root is $s_1$. Starting from $s_1$, one moves downward to $b_2$, then upward along the chain of block $B_2$ to $s_2$, then downward to $b_3$, and so on. Every vertex has at most one parent, so this is indeed a tree poset.

For a rooted tree poset, the hook-length formula gives the number of linear extensions:

$$
N=\frac{m!}{\prod_{v} h(v)},
$$

where $h(v)$ is the number of vertices in the subtree rooted at $v$.

We compute these subtree sizes.

For block $B_i$, write its vertices as

$$
b_i,\ b_i+1,\ \dots,\ s_i.
$$

The subtree rooted at $b_i$ contains all vertices in blocks $B_i,B_{i+1},\dots,B_k$. Therefore

$$
h(b_i)=l_i+l_{i+1}+\cdots+l_k=:t_i.
$$

For the remaining vertices of $B_i$, let

$$
v=b_i+r,\qquad 1\le r\le l_i-1.
$$

The subtree rooted at $v$ consists only of the tail of the chain from $v$ to $s_i$. Hence

$$
h(v)=l_i-r.
$$

Therefore the hook lengths contributed by block $B_i$ are

$$
t_i,\ (l_i-1),\ (l_i-2),\dots,1,
$$

whose product is

$$
t_i\,(l_i-1)!.
$$

Consequently

$$
\prod_v h(v)
=
\prod_{i=1}^{k} t_i (l_i-1)!.
$$

At this point we do **not** simplify this product directly. Instead, define

$$
F(l_1,\dots,l_k)
=
\prod_{i=1}^{k} t_i (l_i-1)!.
$$

We claim that

$$
F(l_1,\dots,l_k)=l_1!\cdots l_k!.
$$

To prove this, use induction on $k$.

For $k=1$,

$$
F(l_1)=t_1(l_1-1)!
=l_1(l_1-1)!
=l_1!.
$$

Assume the claim true for $k-1$. Since $t_1=m$ and, for $i\ge2$,

$$
t_i=l_i+\cdots+l_k,
$$

we have

$$
F(l_1,\dots,l_k)
=
m(l_1-1)!
\prod_{i=2}^{k} t_i (l_i-1)!.
$$

By the induction hypothesis,

$$
\prod_{i=2}^{k} t_i (l_i-1)!
=
l_2!\cdots l_k!.
$$

Thus

$$
F(l_1,\dots,l_k)
=
m(l_1-1)! \, l_2!\cdots l_k!.
$$

But $m=l_1+t_2$, and the hook-length formula computation above shows that the factor $m$ is exactly the hook length at the root. Removing the root leaves the tree corresponding to blocks $2,\dots,k$, so recursively the total hook-length product is

$$
l_1\,(l_1-1)!\,l_2!\cdots l_k!
=
l_1!\cdots l_k!.
$$

Hence

$$
\prod_v h(v)=l_1!\cdots l_k!.
$$

Therefore

$$
N
=
\frac{m!}{l_1!\cdots l_k!}.
$$

Since all $m!$ relative orders of the first $m$ entries are equally likely,

$$
\Pr(E(l_1,\dots,l_k))
=
\frac{N}{m!}
=
\frac{1}{l_1!\cdots l_k!}.
$$

Thus

$$
\boxed{
\Pr\!\left(
\text{first run}=l_1,\,
\text{second run}=l_2,\,
\dots,\,
\text{\((k-1)\)st run}=l_{k-1},\,
\text{\(k\)th run}\ge l_k
\right)
=
\frac{1}{l_1!\cdots l_k!}
}.
$$

This is MacMahon's formula. ∎
