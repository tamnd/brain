**Exercise 7.** [*HM40*] [HM40] Is the probability distribution of runs, given by (14), asymptotically normal? (See exercise 1.2.10-13.)

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m38s  

---

## Setup

Let $A(n,k)=\left\langle \begin{matrix} n \ k \end{matrix} \right\rangle$ be the Eulerian number defined in Section 5.1.3, so that

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} A(n,k) z^k
$$

is the Eulerian polynomial for permutations of ${1,2,\dots,n}$. Let $K_n$ be the random variable counting the number of descents in a uniformly chosen permutation of ${1,2,\dots,n}$. Then

$$
\mathbb{P}(K_n=k)=\frac{A(n,k)}{n!}.
$$

We ask whether the distribution of $K_n$, after normalization by its mean and variance, tends to the normal law as $n\to\infty$.

From Section 5.1.3,

$$
\mathbb{E}(K_n)=\frac{n-1}{2}, \qquad \mathrm{Var}(K_n)=\frac{n+1}{12}.
$$

## Solution

Let

$$
A_n(z)=\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} A(n,k) z^k.
$$

A standard structural property of Eulerian polynomials is that all their zeros are real and negative. Write these zeros as $-r_1,\dots,-r_{n-1}$ with $r_i>0$, so that

$$
A_n(z)=A_n(1)\prod_{i=1}^{n-1}\left(1+\frac{z-1}{1+r_i}\right).
$$

Define

$$
p_i=\frac{1}{1+r_i}, \qquad 0<p_i<1.
$$

Then each factor has the form

$$
1+p_i(z-1)=(1-p_i)+p_i z,
$$

so

$$
\frac{A_n(z)}{A_n(1)}=\prod_{i=1}^{n-1}\bigl((1-p_i)+p_i z\bigr).
$$

This representation identifies $K_n$ as a sum of independent Bernoulli random variables:

$$
K_n \overset{d}{=} X_{n,1}+\cdots+X_{n,n-1},
$$

where $\mathbb{P}(X_{n,i}=1)=p_i$ and $\mathbb{P}(X_{n,i}=0)=1-p_i$.

The variance satisfies

$$
\sum_{i=1}^{n-1} p_i(1-p_i)=\frac{n+1}{12},
$$

hence the total variance tends to infinity linearly in $n$.

For each $i$,

$$
\mathbb{E}(X_{n,i})=p_i,\qquad \mathbb{E}\bigl|X_{n,i}-p_i\bigr|^3 \le 1.
$$

Let $s_n^2=\mathrm{Var}(K_n)=\frac{n+1}{12}$. Then

$$
\frac{1}{s_n^3}\sum_{i=1}^{n-1}\mathbb{E}\bigl|X_{n,i}-p_i\bigr|^3 \le \frac{n}{(n+1)^{3/2}} \to 0.
$$

The Lindeberg condition therefore holds, so by the Lindeberg–Feller central limit theorem,

$$
\frac{K_n-\mathbb{E}(K_n)}{\sqrt{\mathrm{Var}(K_n)}} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1).
$$

Thus the distribution of runs is asymptotically normal after centering by $\frac{n-1}{2}$ and scaling by $\sqrt{(n+1)/12}$.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification

The factorization used is valid because Eulerian polynomials have only real negative zeros, allowing decomposition into linear factors with positive coefficients after shifting by $z-1$. Each factor corresponds to a Bernoulli generating function, so the independence representation is exact.

The variance computation matches the known identity in Section 5.1.3. The Lindeberg ratio tends to $0$ because the third absolute moments are uniformly bounded while the variance grows without bound. This verifies the applicability of the Lindeberg–Feller theorem.

## Notes

The same conclusion follows from the general theorem that any sequence of distributions represented by sums of independent Bernoulli variables with diverging variance is asymptotically normal. Eulerian polynomials also admit proofs via the method of moments or via analytic combinatorics using saddle-point analysis of their exponential generating function.
