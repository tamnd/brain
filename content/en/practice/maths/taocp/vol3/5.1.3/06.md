[Section 5.1.3: Runs](../)

**Exercise 6.** [*M21*] [M21] Mr. B. C. Dull noticed that, by Eqs. (4) and (13),

$$
n! = \sum_{k>0} (-1)^k \sum_{j>0} \binom{j}{k} \binom{n+j}{n}.
$$

Carrying out the sum on $k$ first, he found that

$$
\sum_{k \ge 0} (-1)^k \binom{j}{k} = 0
$$

for all $j > 0$; hence $n! = 0$ for all $n > 0$. Did he make a mistake?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m11s  

---

The mistake is not a subtle issue of convergence or rearrangement of a well-defined double series. It is more basic: the expression is being manipulated as if both iterated sums exist, but the inner sum over $j$ is not defined for fixed $k$.

We analyze the expression as it is written:

$$
n! = \sum_{k>0} (-1)^k \sum_{j>0} \binom{j}{k}\binom{n+j}{n}.
$$

## 1. What is actually well-defined

Fix $k \ge 1$. Consider the inner sum

$$
\sum_{j>0} \binom{j}{k}\binom{n+j}{n}.
$$

For large $j$,

$$
\binom{j}{k} \sim \frac{j^k}{k!}, \qquad \binom{n+j}{n} \sim \frac{j^n}{n!},
$$

so the product behaves like a constant multiple of $j^{n+k}$. Hence the terms do not even tend to zero as $j \to \infty$ in any summable sense, and in particular the series

$$
\sum_{j>0} \binom{j}{k}\binom{n+j}{n}
$$

diverges for every fixed $k$.

Therefore the expression

$$
\sum_{k>0} (-1)^k \left(\sum_{j>0} \binom{j}{k}\binom{n+j}{n}\right)
$$

does not define a legitimate iterated sum in this order. The inner sum is not defined as a finite number.

This is the precise defect.

## 2. What happens if one illegally reverses the order

If one instead starts from the other iterated form,

$$
\sum_{j>0} \left(\sum_{k>0} (-1)^k \binom{j}{k}\binom{n+j}{n}\right),
$$

then for each fixed $j$,

$$
\sum_{k \ge 0} (-1)^k \binom{j}{k} = (1-1)^j = 0 \quad \text{for } j>0,
$$

and the inner sum is finite because $\binom{j}{k}=0$ for $k>j$. Hence this reordered computation gives $0$.

## 3. Why the conclusion $n!=0$ is invalid

The two iterated expressions

$$
\sum_{k>0}\sum_{j>0} a_{j,k}
\quad \text{and} \quad
\sum_{j>0}\sum_{k>0} a_{j,k}
$$

are not two evaluations of a single double series. Only the second is well-defined termwise.

The first requires convergence of

$$
\sum_{j>0} \binom{j}{k}\binom{n+j}{n}
$$

for each fixed $k$, which fails. Since the expression is not defined in the original order, there is no theorem permitting interchange of summation, nor any reason the two procedures must agree.

## 4. Conclusion

Mr. Dull’s mistake is assuming that the expression defines a legitimate double sum whose value is independent of summation order. In reality, the inner sum over $j$ diverges for every $k>0$, so the original expression is not well-defined. The computation that yields $0$ evaluates a different, reordered expression and cannot be applied to the original identity.

Hence no contradiction arises, and the step concluding $n!=0$ is invalid. ∎
