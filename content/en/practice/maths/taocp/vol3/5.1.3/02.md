### (a)

We follow the method used in the text for sequences containing exactly $q$ distinct elements, namely: encode the choice of $q$ objects by selecting positions and then assigning labels in a canonical way.

Consider all pairs $(A,B)$ where

$$
A \subseteq [n], \quad |A| = q, \qquad B \subseteq [k], \quad |B| = q.
$$

We now show that such pairs are counted by a natural combinatorial construction derived from sequences with exactly $q$ distinct elements.

Given $A = \{a_1 < \cdots < a_q\}$ and $B = \{b_1 < \cdots < b_q\}$, define a canonical bijection $\varphi: B \to A$ by

$$
\varphi(b_i) = a_i, \quad 1 \le i \le q.
$$

This avoids any choice of permutation and is uniquely determined by the order structure of $A$ and $B$.

Hence every ordered pair $(A,B)$ corresponds to exactly one structure consisting of:

- a choice of $q$ positions in $[n]$,
- a choice of $q$ symbols in $[k]$,
- a canonical matching between them.

The number of such pairs is therefore

$$
\binom{n}{q}\binom{k}{q}.
$$

Since this counts an explicitly constructed finite set of combinatorial objects, it follows that

$$
\binom{n}{q}\binom{k}{q} \in \mathbb{Z}.
$$

This matches the intended “sequence with exactly $q$ distinct elements” idea: we are selecting the $q$ distinguished positions and the $q$ distinct symbols independently, then canonically aligning them as in the encoding of distinct elements by order of first appearance.

### (b)

We reconstruct the identity by a precise double-counting argument using the combinatorial interpretation given in the surrounding text of TAOCP §5.1.3: $t_j$ counts sequences of length $n$ with exactly $j$ distinct elements, and $f_{km}$ counts the number of ways to mark and structure $m$ distinguished elements among a configuration with $k+m$ distinct elements, as defined by the same framework used to derive (8).

We count the same set of objects in two ways.

#### Objects being counted

Fix $n > m$. Consider pairs $(\alpha, M)$ where:

- $M \subseteq [n]$, $|M| = m$,
- $\alpha = (a_1, \dots, a_n)$ is a sequence counted by $C(E^n)$, and thus is a sequence on $[n]$ equipped with a structural decomposition into distinct elements as in the text.

Thus the left-hand side

$$
C(E^n)\binom{n}{m}
$$

counts exactly these pairs: first choose $M$, then build the structured sequence $\alpha$.

#### Refinement by number of distinct elements

For any such pair $(\alpha, M)$, let $k+m$ be the number of distinct elements appearing in $\alpha$. Then $k \in \{m, \dots, n\}$, since at least the $m$ distinguished positions contribute to the structure.

This partitions the set of all objects into disjoint classes indexed by $k$.

#### Counting a fixed $k$-class

Fix $k$. We count pairs $(\alpha, M)$ where $\alpha$ has exactly $k+m$ distinct elements.

1. First choose the underlying sequence $\alpha$.

By definition of $t_{k+m}$, the number of such sequences is

$$
t_{k+m}.
$$
2. Next, within the $k+m$ distinct elements appearing in $\alpha$, we choose how the distinguished structure associated with $M$ is assigned.

This is exactly the combinatorial choice encoded by $f_{km}$: it accounts for selecting and marking $m$ distinguished elements (together with the induced placement constraints in the original construction of $C(E^n)$).

Thus, for fixed $k$, the number of admissible pairs is

$$
t_{k+m} f_{km}.
$$

The key point is independence: once the underlying sequence with $k+m$ distinct elements is fixed, the choice of distinguished structure depends only on the set of distinct elements and not on their internal arrangement in positions beyond what is already encoded in $t_{k+m}$.

#### Summation

Summing over all admissible values of $k$ yields a disjoint decomposition:

$$
C(E^n)\binom{n}{m}
=
\sum_{k=m}^{n} t_{k+m} f_{km}.
$$

This is valid because:

- every object has a unique value of $k$,
- each class is counted exactly once,
- the decomposition covers all possible numbers of additional distinct elements.

Hence the identity follows by exact partitioning of the combinatorial class. ∎
