**Exercise 13.** [*05*] [05] If Simon Newcomb’s solitaire game is played with a standard bridge deck, ignoring face value but treating clubs < diamonds < hearts < spades, what is the average number of piles?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 3m27s  

---

Let the four suits be ordered

$$
\clubsuit < \diamondsuit < \heartsuit < \spadesuit,
$$

each appearing 13 times. A deal is a uniformly random permutation of this multiset.

### 1. Piles and descents

In Simon Newcomb’s solitaire (patience sorting with a fixed total order), the number of piles equals

$$
1 + D,
$$

where $D$ is the number of descents in the sequence of suits, i.e., the number of indices $k$ such that

$$
X_k > X_{k+1}.
$$

By linearity of expectation,

$$
\mathbb{E}[D] = \sum_{k=1}^{51} \mathbb{P}(X_k > X_{k+1}),
$$

and by symmetry every adjacent position has the same distribution, so it suffices to compute $\mathbb{P}(X_1 > X_2)$.

### 2. Distribution of an adjacent pair

View the permutation as sequential sampling without replacement from the multiset.

For suits $i,j$,

$$
\mathbb{P}(X_1 = i, X_2 = j) =
\frac{n_i}{52} \cdot \frac{n_j - \mathbf{1}_{i=j}}{51}.
$$

With $n_i = 13$ for all suits:

- If $i=j$,

$$
\mathbb{P}(i,i) = \frac{13}{52}\cdot \frac{12}{51} = \frac{156}{2652} = \frac{1}{17}.
$$

Summing over 4 suits,

$$
\mathbb{P}(\text{same suit}) = \frac{4}{17}.
$$

- If $i \neq j$,

$$
\mathbb{P}(i,j) = \frac{13}{52}\cdot \frac{13}{51} = \frac{169}{2652} = \frac{13}{204}.
$$

Thus every ordered pair of distinct suits has probability $13/204$.

### 3. Probability of a descent

A descent occurs when the first suit is larger than the second. Among the 4 suits, there are

$$
\binom{4}{2} = 6
$$

ordered pairs $(i,j)$ with $i>j$. Hence

$$
\mathbb{P}(X_1 > X_2) = 6 \cdot \frac{13}{204} = \frac{78}{204} = \frac{13}{34}.
$$

### 4. Expected number of descents

There are 51 adjacent positions, so

$$
\mathbb{E}[D] = 51 \cdot \frac{13}{34}.
$$

Since $51/34 = 3/2$,

$$
\mathbb{E}[D] = \frac{3}{2} \cdot 13 = \frac{39}{2}.
$$

### 5. Expected number of piles

Therefore,

$$
\mathbb{E}[\text{piles}] = 1 + \frac{39}{2} = \frac{41}{2}.
$$

### Final answer

$$
\boxed{\frac{41}{2}}.
$$
