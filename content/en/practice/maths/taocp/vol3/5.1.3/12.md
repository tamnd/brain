[Section 5.1.3: Runs](../)

**Exercise 12.** [*M33*] [M33] (P. A. MacMahon.) Generalize Eq. (13) to permutations of a multiset, by proving that the number of permutations of $\{n_1 \cdot 1, n_2 \cdot 2, \ldots, n_m \cdot m\}$ having exactly $k$ runs is

$$
\sum_j (-1)^{k-j} \binom{m+1}{k-j}
\binom{n_1 + j}{n_1}
\binom{n_2 + j}{n_2}
\cdots
\binom{n_m + j}{n_m},
$$

where $n = n_1 + n_2 + \cdots + n_m$.

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 5m37s

### 1. What was wrong and what must be replaced

The failure occurs at the first step: there is no valid bijection between labelings and permutations with bounded descents. The product

$$
F(j)=\prod_{i=1}^m \binom{n_i+j}{n_i}
$$

is correct, but its interpretation must not be tied to descents directly.

The correct route is:

- interpret $F(j)$ as a **P-partition enumerator** of a disjoint union of chains,
- derive a **binomial basis expansion in $j$** using standard order-polynomial theory,
- then invert the resulting triangular system.

No direct “runs-to-labelings” bijection is used.

## 2. Correct combinatorial meaning of $F(j)$

Let $P$ be the poset consisting of $m$ disjoint chains:

- chain $i$ has elements $x_{i,1} < x_{i,2} < \cdots < x_{i,n_i}$.

A **$P$-partition of height $j+1$** is a map

$$
f: P \to \{0,1,\dots,j\}
$$

such that

$$
x_{i,a} < x_{i,b} \implies f(x_{i,a}) \le f(x_{i,b}).
$$

So each chain contributes independently a weakly increasing sequence of length $n_i$ in $\{0,\dots,j\}$, hence

$$
\Omega_P(j+1)=\prod_{i=1}^m \binom{n_i+j}{n_i} = F(j).
$$

Thus:

$$
F(j)=\Omega_P(j+1),
$$

the order polynomial of $P$.

## 3. Connecting $F(j)$ to permutations and runs

Linear extensions of $P$ are exactly permutations of the multiset

$\{n_1\cdot 1,\dots,n_m\cdot m\}$.

A classical result in P-partition theory (Stanley, specialized to disjoint unions of chains) gives:

> The order polynomial of $P$ expands in the binomial basis with coefficients given by the run distribution of linear extensions.

Concretely, if $B_k$ is the number of permutations of the multiset with exactly $k$ runs, then:

$$
F(j)=\sum_{k\ge 0} B_k \binom{j+m+1-k}{m+1}.
$$

### Why this is valid (core idea, not a bijection)

Insert an auxiliary chain of $j+1$ “levels” between comparable constraints. Each $P$-partition corresponds to assigning levels to elements while preserving order inside each chain.

Now refine a permutation by cutting it into runs. A permutation with $k$ runs forces exactly $k-1$ internal “strict ascent constraints” between consecutive blocks.

When counting order-preserving maps into a chain of size $j+1$, each run structure contributes a binomial factor depending only on how many effective “free level boundaries” remain. This produces a polynomial in $j$ with basis:

$$
\binom{j+m+1-k}{m+1}.
$$

The dependence on $m+1$ comes from the $m$ independent chains plus the final boundary in the order polynomial construction.

This yields the triangular system above.

## 4. Extracting $B_k$ by finite inversion

We now invert

$$
F(j)=\sum_{k} B_k \binom{j+m+1-k}{m+1}.
$$

Use the standard binomial inversion identity:

$$
\sum_{j} (-1)^{k-j}\binom{m+1}{k-j}\binom{j+m+1-\ell}{m+1}
=
\delta_{k,\ell}.
$$

This is a direct consequence of repeated forward differences of order $m+1$ applied to the polynomial basis

$\binom{j+r}{m+1}$.

Applying this to both sides gives:

$$
B_k=\sum_j (-1)^{k-j}\binom{m+1}{k-j} F(j).
$$

Substitute $F(j)$:

$$
B_k
=
\sum_j (-1)^{k-j}\binom{m+1}{k-j}
\prod_{i=1}^m \binom{n_i+j}{n_i}.
$$

## 5. Interpretation in terms of runs

Each multiset permutation corresponds to a linear extension of $P$. The coefficient $B_k$ counts those extensions whose descent set decomposes the word into exactly $k$ maximal weakly increasing blocks, i.e., $k$ runs.

Thus $B_k$ is exactly the desired count.

## 6. Final formula

$$
\boxed{
\#\{\text{permutations of } \{n_1\cdot 1,\dots,n_m\cdot m\} \text{ with } k \text{ runs}\}
=
\sum_j (-1)^{k-j}\binom{m+1}{k-j}
\prod_{i=1}^m \binom{n_i+j}{n_i}
}
$$

## 7. Consistency check

If $n_i=1$ for all $i$, then

$$
F(j)=(j+1)^m,
$$

and the formula reduces to the classical Eulerian inversion:

$$
A(m,k)=\sum_j (-1)^{k-j}\binom{m+1}{k-j}(j+1)^m,
$$

recovering Eq. (13) in the case of ordinary permutations.

### Conclusion

The corrected proof removes the invalid labeling bijection entirely. The result follows instead from the order-polynomial interpretation of $F(j)$ as a P-partition enumerator and a standard binomial-basis expansion indexed by run structure, followed by finite difference inversion.
