[Section 5.2.3: Sorting by Selection](../)

**Exercise 6.** [**] [M25] What is the average number of comparisons performed by the algorithm of exercise 8, for random input?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m54s  

---

Let $N=m^{2}$. The algorithm partitions the input into $m$ groups of $m$ elements each. We separate the cost into two parts:

1. building and maintaining group maxima and repeatedly extracting the global maximum
2. the internal work required to replenish a group after one of its elements has been removed

The key point missing in the flawed solution is that the “replacement process” within each group is not a single bounded search per extracted element, but rather a complete incremental selection process inside each group over time.

## 1. Cost of maintaining the initial group maxima

Each group of size $m$ is scanned once to find its maximum. This requires exactly $m-1$ comparisons per group.

Hence the total initial cost is

$$
m(m-1)=m^{2}-m.
$$

In terms of $N$,

$$
m^{2}-m = N - \sqrt{N}.
$$

## 2. Cost of repeated extraction of the global maximum

We maintain an array of $m$ current group maxima (leaders). Each extraction step selects the largest among these $m$ values using a linear scan.

- Each global selection among $m$ leaders costs $m-1$ comparisons.
- This is done $N-1$ times.

Thus the leader-selection cost is

$$
(N-1)(m-1).
$$

## 3. Cost of replenishing group maxima (the crucial correction)

When the maximum of a group is extracted, that group loses one element and must reveal its next largest remaining element.

Importantly, **there is no persistent heap or tree inside groups** in this algorithm. The group is simply an unsorted array. Therefore, finding the next maximum in a group of size $k$ requires a fresh linear scan over the remaining $k$ elements.

So each group performs a full _selection-sort style process over time_:

- First maximum: $m-1$ comparisons
- Second maximum: $m-2$ comparisons
- $\dots$
- Last maximum: $0$ comparisons

Thus, over the lifetime of one group, the total number of comparisons is

$$
(m-1) + (m-2) + \cdots + 1 + 0 = \frac{m(m-1)}{2}.
$$

Since there are $m$ groups, the total intra-group cost is

$$
m \cdot \frac{m(m-1)}{2} = \frac{m^{2}(m-1)}{2}.
$$

This already includes the work previously (incorrectly) split into separate phases. No additional “replacement chain” accounting is needed.

## 4. Total number of comparisons

We now combine:

- Intra-group work:

$$
\frac{m^{2}(m-1)}{2}
$$
- Leader selection:

$$
(N-1)(m-1)
$$

So

$$
T(N) = \frac{m^{2}(m-1)}{2} + (N-1)(m-1).
$$

Substitute $m=\sqrt{N}$:

$$
\frac{N(\sqrt{N}-1)}{2} + (N-1)(\sqrt{N}-1).
$$

Expand:

First term:

$$
\frac{1}{2}N^{3/2} - \frac{1}{2}N
$$

Second term:

$$
N^{3/2} - N - \sqrt{N} + 1
$$

Add them:

$$
T(N) = \frac{3}{2}N^{3/2} - \frac{3}{2}N - \sqrt{N} + 1.
$$

## 5. Average-case justification

The number of comparisons depends only on:

- group sizes
- the deterministic scan order within each group
- the fixed linear selection among group maxima

No branch or comparison outcome changes the structure of future work: every input induces exactly the same sequence of scans and group reductions. Therefore the cost is identical for all permutations of the input.

Hence the average-case cost equals the exact cost computed above.

## Final result

$$
\boxed{
T(N) = \frac{3}{2}N^{3/2} - \frac{3}{2}N - \sqrt{N} + 1
= \frac{3}{2}N^{3/2} + O(N)
}
$$
