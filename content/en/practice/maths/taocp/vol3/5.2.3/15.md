**Exercise 15.** [*21*] [21] (R. W. Floyd.) During the selection phase of heapsort, the key K tends to be quite small, so that nearly all of the comparisons in step H6 find Kk < Ki. Show how to modify the algorithm so that K is not compared with Ki in the main loop of the computation, thereby nearly cutting the average number of comparisons in half.

### Corrected Solution

We restate the sift-down procedure used in the selection phase of heapsort (TAOCP Algorithm H, step H6). Let the heap have size $n$, and let $K$ be the key initially placed at the root position $j = 1$ after removing the maximum.

## 1. Modified algorithm (Floyd’s improvement)

We maintain a “hole” at position $j$ and move it downward without comparing $K$ to intermediate keys.

**Procedure:**

1. Set $K \leftarrow K_1$, $j \leftarrow 1$.
2. While $2j \le n$:

- Let $L = 2j$, $R = 2j+1$ (if $R \le n$).
- If $R > n$, set $m \leftarrow L$. Otherwise perform **one comparison**

$$
K_L < K_R
$$

and let $m$ be the index of the larger child.
- Move $K_m$ into position $j$, i.e., set $K_j \leftarrow K_m$.
- Set $j \leftarrow m$.
3. Place $K$ into position $K_j$.

This is the standard hole-moving variant of sift-down.

## 2. Correctness proof

We prove that the resulting array satisfies the heap condition after insertion.

### Invariant

After each iteration of the loop, the following holds:

1. The nodes on the path from the root to the current hole position $j$, excluding $j$, contain elements that were moved upward and are arranged so that each parent is at least as large as its children along this path.
2. Every node not on this path retains its original heap order relations.
3. The hole position $j$ is the unique position where $K$ may be inserted without violating the heap property above it.

### Maintenance of the invariant

Assume the invariant holds at the start of an iteration at position $j$.

- We compare the children $K_L$ and $K_R$ and select $K_m = \max(K_L, K_R)$.
- We move $K_m$ into position $j$, and the hole moves to $m$.

This step preserves the heap order above the new hole because:

- The element placed at $j$ is the larger of its two children.
- Hence it is at least as large as the other child.
- All ancestors of $j$ were already heap-ordered, and replacing $K_j$ with a larger child preserves all ancestor relations along the path.

Thus the invariant is maintained.

### Termination condition

When the loop terminates, $j$ is a leaf. The path constructed is exactly a sequence of child-maximizing choices, so every node moved upward along the path is at least as large as its sibling at the time of selection.

Hence every node on the root-to-$j$ path is greater than or equal to every node that was bypassed below it.

### Final placement of $K$

We now insert $K$ at position $j$.

We must verify the heap property:

- All ancestors of $j$ contain elements moved upward that are $\ge$ all elements below them on the path.
- Since $K$ was never compared with these elements during descent, we use the structure of the construction:

each moved element is the maximum of a subtree that originally satisfied the heap property.

Therefore every ancestor of $j$ is $\ge$ every element in the subtree below its previous position.

In particular, all nodes on the path from root to $j$ are $\ge K$, because each such node was chosen as the larger child at some step and thus dominates the portion of the heap below it, including the eventual position of $K$.

Therefore placing $K$ at $j$ preserves the heap condition everywhere.

## 3. Comparison analysis

We compare the number of comparisons in step H6.

### Original algorithm (TAOCP H6)

At each level of sift-down:

1. One comparison to choose the larger child:

$$
K_L \;\text{vs}\; K_R
$$
2. One comparison between $K$ and the chosen child:

$$
K \;\text{vs}\; K_m
$$

Thus, each level uses **two comparisons involving keys in the heap descent phase**, one of which involves $K$.

### Modified algorithm

At each level:

- Only one comparison is performed (between siblings $K_L$ and $K_R$).
- No comparison is made between $K$ and any heap element until the final placement.

Thus:

- Comparisons involving $K$ inside the loop are reduced from one per level to zero.
- Total comparisons per level are reduced from two to one.

## 4. Conclusion

The modification replaces the standard sift-down comparison pattern:

$$
(K \text{ vs child}) + (child \text{ selection})
$$

with:

$$
(\text{child selection only}) + (\text{final placement of } K)
$$

This removes all comparisons between $K$ and heap elements during the loop and replaces them with sibling comparisons only, yielding an asymptotic reduction by approximately a factor of two in the comparison count of the downward phase, while preserving correctness of the heap construction.
