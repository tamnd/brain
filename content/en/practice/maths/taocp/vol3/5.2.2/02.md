[Section 5.2.2: Sorting by Exchanging](../)

**Exercise 2.** [**] [M25]

(a) What is the minimum number of exchanges that will sort the permutation 376981452?

(b) In general, given any permutation $\pi = a_1 \dots a_n$ of $\{1,\dots,n\}$, let $\text{xch}(\pi)$ be the minimum number of exchanges that will sort $\pi$ into increasing order. Express $\text{xch}(\pi)$ in terms of “simpler” characteristics of $\pi$. (See exercise 5.1.4-41 for another way to measure the disorder of a permutation.)

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m04s  

---

(a) Write the permutation in disjoint cycle form:

$$
(1\,3\,6)(2\,7\,4\,9)(5\,8).
$$

A cycle of length $m$ can be restored to fixed points by exactly $m-1$ exchanges, since each exchange can increase the number of cycles by at most $1$, while exchanging one fixed element of the cycle with each of the remaining $m-1$ elements achieves this bound. Hence the three cycles above require at least

$$
(3-1)+(4-1)+(2-1)=6
$$

exchanges, and this lower bound is attained.

One optimal sequence is

$$
1 \leftrightarrow 6,
$$

$$
1 \leftrightarrow 3,
$$

$$
2 \leftrightarrow 9,
$$

$$
2 \leftrightarrow 4,
$$

$$
2 \leftrightarrow 7,
$$

$$
5 \leftrightarrow 8.
$$

Thus the minimum number of exchanges is

$$
\boxed{6}.
$$

(b) Let the disjoint cycle decomposition of $\pi$ contain $c$ cycles, counting $1$-cycles. If the cycle lengths are $m_1,\dots,m_c$, then

$$
m_1+\cdots+m_c=n.
$$

Each cycle of length $m_i$ requires at least $m_i-1$ exchanges, by the argument above, and this bound is attained by fixing one element of the cycle and exchanging it successively with each of the other $m_i-1$ elements. Therefore

$$
\operatorname{xch}(\pi)
=\sum_{i=1}^{c}(m_i-1)
=\sum_{i=1}^{c}m_i-c
=n-c.
$$

Hence

$$
\boxed{\operatorname{xch}(\pi)=n-\text{(number of cycles of }\pi\text{, including fixed points)}}.
$$
