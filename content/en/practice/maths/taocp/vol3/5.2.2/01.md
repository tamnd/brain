[Section 5.2.2: Sorting by Exchanging](../)

**Exercise 1.** [**] [M20] Let $a_1 \dots a_n$ be a permutation of $\{1,\dots,n\}$, and let $i$ and $j$ be indices such that $i < j$ and $a_i > a_j$. Let $a_1 \dots a_n'$ be the permutation obtained from $a_1 \dots a_n$ by interchanging $a_i$ and $a_j$. Can $a_1 \dots a_n'$ have more inversions than $a_1 \dots a_n$?

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 3m59s  

---

Let $a_1,\dots,a_n$ be a permutation, let $i<j$, and set

$$
x=a_i,\quad y=a_j,\quad x>y.
$$

Let $a'$ be obtained by swapping $x$ and $y$. We compare inversion counts, where an inversion is a pair $(p,q)$ with $p<q$ and $a_p>a_q$.

## 1. Which pairs can change

Only pairs involving positions $i$ or $j$ can change their inversion status. Thus we examine:

- pairs $(k,i)$ and $(k,j)$ for $k<i$,
- pairs $(i,k)$ and $(j,k)$ for $k>j$,
- pairs $(i,k)$ and $(k,j)$ for $i<k<j$,
- and the pair $(i,j)$.

All other pairs are unaffected.

## 2. Indices outside $[i,j]$

### Case $k<i$

Before swap:

$$
[k,i]:\ a_k>x,\quad [k,j]:\ a_k>y.
$$

After swap:

$$
[k,i]:\ a_k>y,\quad [k,j]:\ a_k>x.
$$

So the total contribution is

$$
\mathbf{1}[a_k>x]+\mathbf{1}[a_k>y]
$$

in both cases. Hence the net change is $0$.

### Case $k>j$

Before swap:

$$
[i,k]:\ x>a_k,\quad [j,k]:\ y>a_k.
$$

After swap:

$$
[i,k]:\ y>a_k,\quad [j,k]:\ x>a_k.
$$

Again the multiset of comparisons is identical, so the net change is $0$.

## 3. Indices between $i$ and $j$

Let $i<k<j$ and $z=a_k$. Only the pairs $(i,k)$ and $(k,j)$ matter.

Before swap:

$$
B_k = \mathbf{1}[x>z] + \mathbf{1}[z>y].
$$

After swap:

$$
A_k = \mathbf{1}[y>z] + \mathbf{1}[z>x].
$$

We analyze all possible positions of $z$ relative to $y<x$.

### Case 1: $z<y<x$

Before:

$$
x>z \Rightarrow 1,\quad z>y \Rightarrow 0 \quad \Rightarrow B_k=1.
$$

After:

$$
y>z \Rightarrow 1,\quad z>x \Rightarrow 0 \quad \Rightarrow A_k=1.
$$

So $A_k=B_k$.

### Case 2: $y<z<x$

Before:

$$
x>z \Rightarrow 1,\quad z>y \Rightarrow 1 \quad \Rightarrow B_k=2.
$$

After:

$$
y>z \Rightarrow 0,\quad z>x \Rightarrow 0 \quad \Rightarrow A_k=0.
$$

So $A_k = B_k - 2$.

### Case 3: $y<x<z$

Before:

$$
x>z \Rightarrow 0,\quad z>y \Rightarrow 1 \quad \Rightarrow B_k=1.
$$

After:

$$
y>z \Rightarrow 0,\quad z>x \Rightarrow 1 \quad \Rightarrow A_k=1.
$$

So $A_k=B_k$.

Hence for every $k\in(i,j)$, the contribution never increases and decreases by $2$ exactly when $y<z<x$.

Let $m$ be the number of indices $k\in(i,j)$ such that $y<a_k<x$. Then the total contribution from middle indices changes by $-2m$.

## 4. The pair $(i,j)$

Before swap:

$$
(i,j)\ \text{is an inversion since } x>y.
$$

After swap:

$$
(i,j)\ \text{is not an inversion since } y<x.
$$

So this contributes $-1$.

## 5. Total change

All other pairs contribute $0$, so

$$
\operatorname{inv}(a') - \operatorname{inv}(a) = -1 - 2m.
$$

Thus

$$
\operatorname{inv}(a') = \operatorname{inv}(a) - (1+2m) \le \operatorname{inv}(a)-1.
$$

In particular,

$$
\operatorname{inv}(a') < \operatorname{inv}(a),
$$

so the number of inversions cannot increase under such a swap.

## Conclusion

Swapping two entries $x>a_j$ and $y=a_j$ with $i<j$ strictly reduces the inversion count by an odd positive amount $1+2m$, and therefore it is impossible for the number of inversions to increase.
