[Section 5.1.4: Tableaux and Involutions](../)

**Exercise 6.** [*20*] [20] Given only the values of $r$ and $s$ after Algorithm S terminates, is it possible to restore $P$ to its original condition?

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 2m29s  

---

The proposed argument failed because it never produced a concrete noninjective instance and never verified that Algorithm S behaves consistently under the claimed modifications. A correct solution must exhibit two valid initial tableaux that both produce the same terminal coordinates $(r,s)$ under Algorithm S but are distinct.

We restart from the definition-level idea of Algorithm S (jeu de taquin type sliding): at each step the empty cell exchanges with the smaller of its eligible neighbors (typically the cell to the right or below), and the process continues until the empty cell reaches a terminal boundary position. The terminal coordinates depend only on where the path exits, not on which of the two local choices is taken at intermediate steps.

### Construction of a counterexample

It suffices to work with a $2\times 2$ Young diagram, since the smallest possible ambiguity already appears there.

Consider the shape

$$
\begin{matrix}
(1,1) & (1,2) \\
(2,1) & (2,2)
\end{matrix}
$$

and suppose Algorithm S starts with the empty cell at $(1,1)$. The terminal position in both cases will be $(2,2)$, regardless of the path taken.

We construct two valid tableaux.

#### Tableau $P$

$$
P =
\begin{matrix}
\infty & 2 \\
3 & 5
\end{matrix}
$$

More precisely, interpret $(1,1)$ as the empty position; the entries are:

$$
P(1,2)=2,\quad P(2,1)=3,\quad P(2,2)=5.
$$

This satisfies row and column strict increase:

$$
2<5,\quad 3<5.
$$

At $(1,1)$, Algorithm S compares the right and below entries:

$$
2 < 3,
$$

so the hole swaps with the right entry. The hole moves to $(1,2)$, then can only move down to $(2,2)$. Hence the path is right then down, terminating at $(2,2)$.

#### Tableau $P'$

Now swap the two critical entries:

$$
P' =
\begin{matrix}
\infty & 3 \\
2 & 5
\end{matrix}
$$

So

$$
P'(1,2)=3,\quad P'(2,1)=2,\quad P'(2,2)=5.
$$

This is still a valid tableau since:

$$
2<5,\quad 3<5,
$$

and column strictness holds.

Now at $(1,1)$, Algorithm S compares:

$$
3 \text{ (right)}, \quad 2 \text{ (down)},
$$

so it swaps with the smaller entry $2$. The hole moves down to $(2,1)$, then must move right to $(2,2)$. Hence the path is down then right, again terminating at $(2,2)$.

### Verification of the key properties

1. Both executions terminate at the same position:

$$
(r,s) = (2,2).
$$

1. The initial tableaux are different:

$$
P \neq P'.
$$

1. Both are valid Young tableaux.
2. The only difference is the local comparison at the starting cell, which changes the path but not the final exit point.

### Consequence

From the terminal data $(r,s)$ alone, one cannot determine whether the initial configuration was $P$ or $P'$. Both are compatible with the same terminal coordinates, so the mapping

$$
P \mapsto (r,s)
$$

is not injective.

### Conclusion

Since distinct initial tableaux can produce identical terminal coordinates $(r,s)$, the original tableau $P$ cannot be reconstructed uniquely from $(r,s)$ alone. Therefore, the answer to the exercise is negative.
