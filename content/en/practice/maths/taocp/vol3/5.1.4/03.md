**Exercise 3.** [**] [M18] (P. Erdős, G. Szekeres.) Prove that any permutation containing more than $n^2$ elements has a monotonic subsequence of length greater than $n$; but there are permutations of $n^2$ elements with no monotonic subsequences of length greater than $n$.

[Hint: See the previous exercise.]

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m03s  

---

Let $P$ be the tableau corresponding to a permutation of length $m$. By Exercise 2, the number of columns of $P$ equals the greatest length of an increasing subsequence, and the number of rows equals the greatest length of a decreasing subsequence. If $m>n^2$ and every increasing and decreasing subsequence had length at most $n$, then $P$ would have at most $n$ columns and at most $n$ rows. Hence $P$ would contain at most $n^2$ entries, since a tableau with at most $n$ rows and at most $n$ columns has at most $n^2$ positions. But $P$ contains exactly $m$ entries, a contradiction. Therefore every permutation with more than $n^2$ elements has either an increasing subsequence of length greater than $n$ or a decreasing subsequence of length greater than $n$.

For the converse, consider the permutation

$$
n,\ n-1,\ \ldots,\ 1,\ 2n,\ 2n-1,\ \ldots,\ n+1,\ \ldots,\ n^2,\ n^2-1,\ \ldots,\ n^2-n+1.
$$

It consists of $n$ consecutive decreasing blocks, each of length $n$, and every element of an earlier block is smaller than every element of a later block. An increasing subsequence can contain at most one element from each block, so its length is at most $n$. A decreasing subsequence must lie entirely within a single block, since elements increase from block to block, so its length is also at most $n$. Thus this permutation of $n^2$ elements has no monotonic subsequence of length greater than $n$. This completes the proof.
