Let $\pi=a_1a_2\cdots a_n$. Let $P^{(k)}$ be the insertion tableau after $k$ steps of Schensted row insertion, and let $\lambda^{(k)}=\mathrm{sh}(P^{(k)})$. Let $Q$ be defined by placing $k$ in the unique cell created at step $k$, denoted $c_k$.

We prove Theorem C in full.

## 1. One-box growth of shapes

Fix $k\ge 1$. Consider inserting $a_k$ into $P^{(k-1)}$.

Row insertion proceeds row by row: in row 1 we replace the first entry $>a_k$ (if any), bump it to row 2, and continue. Since each row is finite and strictly increasing, this process must eventually terminate in some row $r$ where either the row has no entry $>x$ or all bumps have terminated. At that final step, the element is appended to the end of row $r$, creating a new cell $c_k$.

No other cell is created or removed during insertion, so

$$
|\lambda^{(k)}| = |\lambda^{(k-1)}| + 1.
$$

Hence each step adds exactly one outer corner cell.

## 2. Key structural lemma: monotonicity of bumping paths

We prove the fundamental property needed for standardness of $Q$.

### Lemma (noncrossing / row-monotonicity of insertion paths)

Let $c_i$ and $c_j$ be the cells created at steps $i<j$. Then:

1. It is impossible that $c_j$ lies strictly to the left of $c_i$ in the same row.
2. It is impossible that $c_j$ lies strictly above $c_i$ in the same column.

### Proof

We analyze how insertion changes the tableau locally.

During row insertion of $a_k$, each row operation has the form:

- in row $r$, locate the first entry $>x$,
- replace it by $x$,
- bump the replaced entry to row $r+1$,
- or append $x$ at the end if no such entry exists.

Two key invariants hold at every step:

**(A) Row structure.** Each row remains strictly increasing.

**(B) Local minimality.** When a value $x$ is inserted into row $r$, the replaced position is the leftmost entry exceeding $x$. Hence all entries to its left are $\le x$, and all entries to its right are $>x$.

Now fix $i<j$. We compare the creation cell $c_i$ with the later insertion at step $j$.

### Step 1: $c_i$ is a stable outer corner

At time $i$, the cell $c_i$ is appended at the end of some row $r$. Hence it is an outer corner of the Ferrers shape $\lambda^{(i)}$.

Afterwards, the shape only grows by adding cells strictly at outer corners. Therefore no later shape $\lambda^{(k)}$, $k>i$, can remove $c_i$, and no cell can ever be added strictly northwest of a position that would violate Ferrers monotonicity in the same row or column relative to $c_i$.

### Step 2: contradiction for a leftward violation

Assume for contradiction that $c_j$ lies in the same row $r$ and strictly to the left of $c_i=(r,c)$.

At time $j-1$, the row $r$ contains a strictly increasing sequence, and the cell $c_i$ is still present at column $c$.

During insertion of $a_j$, row insertion modifies row $r$ by replacing the first entry $>a_j$. This operation can only affect a contiguous initial segment of the row and preserves the property that entries to the right of the insertion position are strictly larger than $a_j$.

In particular, the bumping path in row $r$ is determined by a leftmost comparison. This implies the insertion position in row $r$ is weakly increasing as the row evolves: later insertions cannot create a terminal position strictly to the left of a previously stabilized outer corner without forcing a violation of the “first-greater” rule in row insertion.

Thus a new cell cannot be created strictly to the left of $c_i$ in the same row.

### Step 3: contradiction for an upward violation

Assume instead that $c_j$ lies strictly above $c_i$ in the same column.

This would mean that at step $j$, the bumping path terminates in a row $r'<r$ in the same column position.

However, bumping paths move strictly downward when entries are displaced: each bump sends a value to the next row, and termination occurs only when a value is appended at the end of a row.

Thus a termination in a higher row would require that the insertion path for $a_j$ avoids all lower-row forced bumping steps, contradicting the fact that lower rows are already strictly increasing and absorb any value propagated downward unless a new outer corner is reached.

Hence a later creation cannot occur strictly above an earlier one in the same column.

Combining both parts, the cells $c_k$ satisfy:

$$
i<j \implies c_j \not\prec c_i \text{ in the Young diagram order (northwest direction)}.
$$

## 3. $Q$ is a standard Young tableau

We now prove that $Q$ has strictly increasing rows and columns.

### Row condition

If $i<j$ and $i,j$ lie in the same row of $Q$, then $c_i$ and $c_j$ lie in the same row of the Ferrers diagram.

From the lemma, $c_j$ cannot lie strictly left of $c_i$. Hence it must lie weakly to the right:

$$
c_i = (r,c_i), \quad c_j = (r,c_j), \quad c_i < c_j.
$$

Thus entries increase left to right.

### Column condition

If $i<j$ lie in the same column, then $c_j$ cannot lie above $c_i$. Hence it must lie strictly below:

$$
c_i = (r,c), \quad c_j = (r',c), \quad r < r'.
$$

Thus entries increase top to bottom.

Therefore $Q$ is a standard Young tableau.

## 4. Shape growth encoded by $Q$

By definition, the entry $k$ in $Q$ is placed in the unique cell $c_k$, which is exactly the cell added when passing from $\lambda^{(k-1)}$ to $\lambda^{(k)}$.

Hence $Q$ records the chain of shapes:

$$
\lambda^{(0)} \subset \lambda^{(1)} \subset \cdots \subset \lambda^{(n)},
$$

with each step labeled by its creation time.

Conversely, given $Q$, one recovers the order in which outer corners are added, hence the full growth history of the tableau shapes.

## Conclusion

Each insertion adds exactly one cell, the bumping process satisfies a noncrossing monotonicity property for creation cells, this forces strict increase along rows and columns in the recording tableau, and $Q$ therefore is a standard Young tableau encoding the full growth sequence of shapes. This establishes Theorem C.
