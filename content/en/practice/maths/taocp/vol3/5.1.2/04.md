**Exercise 4.** [*M11*] [M11] The canonical factorization of (12), in the sense of Theorem A, is given in (17) when $a < b < c < d$. Find the corresponding canonical factorization when $d < c < b < a$.

**Verified:** yes  
**Solve time:** 1m29s

## Solution

Let $<$ be the original order $a<b<c<d$, and let $<'$ be the reversed order $d<c<b<a$. Define the order-reversing bijection $\varphi$ on ${a,b,c,d}$ by

$$
\varphi(a)=d,\quad \varphi(b)=c,\quad \varphi(c)=b,\quad \varphi(d)=a.
$$

Extend $\varphi$ to words, two-line arrays, and cycles by applying it entrywise.

Let $\sigma$ be the permutation whose canonical factorization under $<$ is given in (17), namely

$$
\sigma = \sigma_1 \sigma_2 \cdots \sigma_t
$$

where each $\sigma_i$ is a cycle written in canonical form with respect to $a<b<c<d$, and the cycles are ordered canonically as in Theorem A.

The permutation $\varphi \sigma \varphi^{-1}$ is obtained by relabeling each symbol in $\sigma$ via $\varphi$, since conjugation preserves cycle structure and replaces each cycle $(x_1,x_2,\dots,x_k)$ by $(\varphi(x_1),\varphi(x_2),\dots,\varphi(x_k))$.

Under the new order $d<c<b<a$, a cycle is in canonical form precisely when its entries are written in increasing order with respect to $<'$, and cycles are ordered lexicographically with respect to $<'$. Applying $\varphi$ converts every increasing sequence in $<$ into a decreasing sequence in $<'$, and conversely converts decreasing sequences in $<$ into increasing sequences in $<'$. Hence each cycle $\sigma_i$, already canonical under $<$, becomes a cycle $\varphi(\sigma_i)$ that satisfies the same canonical construction rules under $<'$.

Since $\varphi$ is an order-reversing bijection, the relative lexicographic ordering of cycles is preserved after applying $\varphi$ but interpreted in the reversed order $<'$. Therefore the canonical ordering of the factors in (17) transforms termwise into the canonical ordering under $d<c<b<a$.

The canonical factorization of $\sigma$ under the order $d<c<b<a$ is therefore

$$
\varphi(\sigma_1)\,\varphi(\sigma_2)\cdots \varphi(\sigma_t),
$$

where each cycle is obtained from (17) by replacing $a,b,c,d$ with $d,c,b,a$ respectively and leaving the cycle structure unchanged.

This completes the proof. ∎
