Let

$$
\sigma_1,\sigma_2,\ldots,\sigma_t
$$

be cycles with disjoint internal repetition (each a single cycle on its support), and consider the product

$$
\Pi=\sigma_1\sigma_2\cdots\sigma_t.
$$

By Theorem C (TAOCP), two adjacent factors can be interchanged in a prime factorization if and only if the corresponding cycles are disjoint. Hence all prime factorizations of $\Pi$ are exactly the sequences obtained from $(\sigma_1,\ldots,\sigma_t)$ by adjacent swaps of disjoint cycles.

Define a relation on indices $x_1,\ldots,x_t$ by

$$
x_i \prec x_j \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad i<j \ \text{and}\ \sigma_i,\sigma_j \text{ share at least one letter}.
$$

We prove that prime factorizations of $\Pi$ are in bijection with linear extensions of this poset.

## 1. Structure of allowed permutations of factors

Consider any permutation $\pi=(\sigma_{i_1},\ldots,\sigma_{i_t})$ reachable from $(\sigma_1,\ldots,\sigma_t)$ by adjacent swaps of disjoint cycles.

### Lemma 1 (invariant constraint)

If $i<j$ and $\sigma_i$ and $\sigma_j$ share a letter, then in every reachable permutation, $\sigma_i$ must appear before $\sigma_j$.

**Proof.**

If $\sigma_i$ and $\sigma_j$ share a letter, they are not disjoint, so by Theorem C they can never be swapped when adjacent. Since adjacent swaps generate all permutations of factors, the relative order of such a pair can never be changed. Hence $i$ must remain to the left of $j$. ŌłÄ

Thus every reachable permutation respects the partial order $\prec$. Therefore every prime factorization induces a linear extension of $(\{x_i\},\prec)$.

## 2. From linear extensions to factorizations

We now prove the converse: every linear extension is achievable via allowed swaps.

Let

$$
(\sigma_{i_1},\ldots,\sigma_{i_t})
$$

be a linear extension of $\prec$. We show it can be obtained from $(\sigma_1,\ldots,\sigma_t)$ by adjacent swaps of disjoint cycles.

We use a standard adjacent-transposition argument (bubble-sort style), but we must verify that every required swap is allowed.

Start with the identity sequence and repeatedly reduce inversions relative to the target order. Suppose at some stage we have a sequence $S$, and there exists an adjacent inversion $(\sigma_a,\sigma_b)$ such that in the target order $\sigma_b$ must come before $\sigma_a$.

We claim $\sigma_a$ and $\sigma_b$ are disjoint.

Indeed, if they were not disjoint, then they share a letter. Without loss of generality assume their original indices satisfy $a<b$ or $b<a$; in either case, the definition of $\prec$ ensures that the earlier index among them precedes the later one in every linear extension. Hence the target linear extension cannot reverse their order. This contradicts that $(\sigma_{i_1},\ldots,\sigma_{i_t})$ is a linear extension.

Therefore every adjacent inversion relative to the target must be a pair of disjoint cycles, so the swap is allowed by Theorem C.

By repeatedly performing such swaps, we transform the initial sequence into the target linear extension. Hence every linear extension is realizable as a prime factorization.

### Conclusion of part 1

Prime factorizations of $\Pi$ are in bijection with linear extensions of $(\{x_i\},\prec)$. Therefore their number is equal.

## 3. Converse construction: realizing any partial order

Let $(P,\prec)$ be a finite poset on elements $x_1,\ldots,x_t$. We construct cycles $\sigma_1,\ldots,\sigma_t$ such that the induced relation matches $\prec$.

### Construction

For every comparable pair $i\neq j$ with $x_i \prec x_j$, introduce a distinct symbol $a_{ij}$. (One symbol per unordered comparable pair is sufficient; we keep orientation only for bookkeeping.)

Define:

$$
S_i=\{a_{ij}: x_i \prec x_j\}\ \cup\ \{a_{ji}: x_j \prec x_i\}.
$$

Let $\sigma_i$ be any cycle whose support is exactly $S_i$.

### Lemma 2 (correct induced overlap relation)

For $i\neq j$,

$$
\sigma_i \text{ and } \sigma_j \text{ share a letter } \Longleftrightarrow x_i \text{ and } x_j \text{ are comparable in } \prec.
$$

**Proof.**

- If $x_i \prec x_j$, then $a_{ij}\in S_i\cap S_j$, so the cycles intersect.
- If $x_j \prec x_i$, similarly $a_{ji}\in S_i\cap S_j$.
- If $x_i$ and $x_j$ are incomparable, no symbol involving $\{i,j\}$ is created, so $S_i\cap S_j=\varnothing$.

Thus intersection occurs exactly for comparable pairs. ŌłÄ

### Lemma 3 (induced partial order equals the original)

By definition,

$$
x_i \prec x_j \ \text{in the construction} \iff i<j \ \text{and}\ \sigma_i,\sigma_j \text{ share a letter}.
$$

By Lemma 2, this holds exactly when $x_i \prec x_j$ in the given poset (since we can relabel indices so that the index order is arbitrary).

Thus the induced relation is exactly the original partial order. ŌłÄ

No higher-order interactions occur because each symbol appears in exactly two cycles, so overlaps are purely pairwise and do not generate additional constraints.

## 4. Conclusion

We have established:

1. Adjacent swaps of cycles are allowed exactly when the cycles are disjoint (Theorem C).
2. Disjointness corresponds precisely to incomparability in the induced relation.
3. Reachable permutations of factors are exactly linear extensions of the partial order $(\{x_i\},\prec)$.
4. Every finite partial order can be realized by a suitable family of cycles.

Therefore, the number of distinct prime factorizations of

$$
\sigma_1\tau\sigma_2\tau\cdots\tau\sigma_t
$$

is exactly the number of topological sorts of the partial ordering, and conversely every partial order arises from such a construction.
