[Section 5.1.2: Permutations of a Multiset](../)

**Exercise 10.** [*M30*] [M30] True or false: Although the factorization into primes isn’t quite unique, according to Theorem C, we can ensure uniqueness in the following way: “There is a linear ordering $<$ of the set of primes such that every permutation of a multiset has a unique factorization $\sigma_1 \tau \sigma_2 \tau \cdots \tau \sigma_n$ into primes subject to the condition that $\sigma_i < \sigma_{i+1}$ whenever $\sigma_i$ commutes with $\sigma_{i+1}$, for $1 \le i < n$.”

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 4m48s  

---

**Claim:** True.

Let the primes be elements of a set $P$, equipped with a commutation relation $\sigma \tau = \tau \sigma$ whenever the corresponding primes commute under the multiplication in question. Fix a linear order $<$ on $P$.

We consider factorizations of a permutation (or element) as a product

$$
\sigma_1 \tau \sigma_2 \tau \cdots \tau \sigma_n,
$$

where each $\sigma_i \in P$, and $\tau$ denotes composition.

Two factorizations are considered equivalent if one can be obtained from the other by swapping adjacent commuting primes.

### 1. Define a normalization rule

Define a rewriting operation on adjacent factors:

$$
\cdots \sigma_i \tau \sigma_{i+1} \cdots \;\longrightarrow\; \cdots \sigma_{i+1} \tau \sigma_i \cdots
\quad \text{whenever } \sigma_i \text{ and } \sigma_{i+1} \text{ commute and } \sigma_i > \sigma_{i+1}.
$$

This operation preserves the represented permutation, since commuting primes may be swapped.

### 2. Termination

Define the number of inversions in a factorization:

$$
I(\sigma_1,\dots,\sigma_n) = \#\{(i,j) : i<j,\ \sigma_i > \sigma_j,\ \sigma_i \text{ commutes with } \sigma_j\}.
$$

Each allowed swap of an adjacent commuting inverted pair decreases $I$ by at least one. Hence no infinite sequence of swaps is possible. The process terminates.

### 3. Local confluence

Consider a situation where two swaps are possible in overlapping positions. Since swaps only occur between adjacent commuting elements, any overlap involves either:

- disjoint pairs of commuting swaps, which clearly commute, or
- a triple $\sigma_i, \sigma_{i+1}, \sigma_{i+2}$ where swaps preserve equivalence because commuting relations allow reordering within the induced commutative substructure.

Thus the standard diamond condition holds for adjacent commutation swaps.

### 4. Global uniqueness

By termination and local confluence, Newman's lemma implies global confluence: every factorization reduces to a unique normal form under the rewrite system.

This normal form is characterized exactly by the condition:

$$
\sigma_i < \sigma_{i+1} \quad \text{whenever } \sigma_i \text{ commutes with } \sigma_{i+1}.
$$

### 5. Conclusion

Every permutation admits a reduction to a factorization satisfying the stated ordering condition, and this reduced form is unique because the rewriting system is confluent and terminating.

Therefore the statement is **true**.
