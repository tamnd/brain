[Section 1.2.9: Generating Functions](../)

**Exercise 4.** [*M01*] Explain why Eq. (19) is a special case of Eq. (21).

**Verified:** no  
**Solve time:** 37s  

---

Equation (21) states that if $x^{t+1}=x^t+z$ and $x=1$ when $z=0$, then

$$
x^r=\sum_{k\ge0}\binom{r-kt}{k}\frac{r}{r-kt}z^k.
$$

Take $t=-1$. Then the defining equation becomes

$$
x^{0}=x^{-1}+z,
$$

hence

$$
1=\frac1x+z,
\qquad\text{so}\qquad
x=\frac1{1-z}.
$$

Therefore

$$
x^r=(1-z)^{-r}.
$$

Substituting $t=-1$ into Eq. (21) gives

$$
(1-z)^{-r}
=\sum_{k\ge0}\binom{r+k}{k}\frac{r}{r+k}z^k.
$$

Since

$$
\binom{r+k}{k}\frac{r}{r+k}
=\frac{(r+k)(r+k-1)\cdots(r+1)}{k!}\cdot\frac{r}{r+k}
=\frac{r(r+1)\cdots(r+k-1)}{k!}
=\binom{r+k-1}{k},
$$

we obtain

$$
(1-z)^{-r}
=\sum_{k\ge0}\binom{r+k-1}{k}z^k.
$$

Replacing $r$ by $-r$ yields

$$
(1+z)^r
=\sum_{k\ge0}\binom{r}{k}z^k,
$$

which is Eq. (19). ∎
