## Problem

Let $A = (a_{ij})(i, j = 1, 2, \cdots, n)$ be a square matrix whose elements are non-negative integers. Suppose that whenever an element $a_{ij} = 0$, the sum of the elements in the $i$th row and the $j$th column is $\geq n$. Prove that the sum of all the elements of the matrix is $\geq n^2 / 2$.

---
## Exploration

Let

$$R_i=\sum_{k=1}^{n} a_{ik}, \qquad C_j=\sum_{k=1}^{n} a_{kj}$$

denote the sums of the $i$th row and $j$th column. The hypothesis says that whenever $a_{ij}=0$,

$$R_i+C_j\ge n.$$

We must prove

$$\sum_{i,j} a_{ij}\ge \frac{n^2}{2}.$$

The total sum of the matrix is

$$S=\sum_i R_i=\sum_j C_j.$$

Trying small cases suggests that matrices consisting only of $0$'s and $1$'s are extremal. For example, if exactly half of the entries are $1$ and half are $0$, arranged so that every row and every column contains about $n/2$ ones, then for a zero entry we have $R_i+C_j=n$, and the total sum is exactly $n^2/2$ when $n$ is even.

The condition involves only the positions of the zero entries. A natural idea is to sum the inequalities

$$R_i+C_j\ge n$$

over all zero positions. Let $Z$ be the set of pairs $(i,j)$ with $a_{ij}=0$, and let $z=|Z|$. Summing gives

$$\sum_{(i,j)\in Z}(R_i+C_j)\ge nz.$$

The left side can be rewritten row by row and column by column. If $z_i$ denotes the number of zeros in row $i$ and $w_j$ the number of zeros in column $j$, then

$$\sum_{(i,j)\in Z}(R_i+C_j)
=
\sum_i z_iR_i+\sum_j w_jC_j.$$

The crucial difficulty is to relate this quantity to the total sum $S$.

Since entries are nonnegative integers, a row with sum $R_i$ can contain at most $R_i$ positive entries. Hence it contains at least $n-R_i$ zeros, so

$$z_i\ge n-R_i.$$

Similarly,

$$w_j\ge n-C_j.$$

Substituting these lower bounds into the left side yields

$$\sum_i z_iR_i+\sum_j w_jC_j
\ge
\sum_i R_i(n-R_i)+\sum_j C_j(n-C_j).$$

Combining with the previous inequality gives

$$2nS-\sum_i R_i^2-\sum_j C_j^2\ge nz.$$

Since $z=n^2-p$, where $p$ is the number of positive entries, and every positive entry is at least $1$, we have $p\le S$. Thus

$$z\ge n^2-S.$$

This leads to

$$nS-\sum_iR_i^2-\sum_jC_j^2+n^2\ge0.$$

The squares suggest applying Cauchy's inequality:

$$\sum_iR_i^2\ge \frac{S^2}{n}, \qquad
\sum_jC_j^2\ge \frac{S^2}{n}.$$

Then

$$nS+n^2-\frac{2S^2}{n}\ge0.$$

After simplification,

$$2S^2-n^2S-n^3\le0.$$

The roots are $S=n^2$ and $S=-n/2$, so

$$0\le S\le n^2
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
S\ge \frac{n^2}{2}.$$

This appears to give the desired result.

The step most likely to hide an error is the estimate $z_i\ge n-R_i$. It uses integrality of the entries: a row sum equal to $R_i$ can support at most $R_i$ positive positions because each positive entry is at least $1$.

## Problem Understanding

We are given an $n\times n$ matrix of nonnegative integers. For each zero entry $a_{ij}$, the sum of the entire $i$th row and the entire $j$th column is at least $n$.

Let $S$ be the sum of all matrix entries. The task is to prove that

$$S\ge \frac{n^2}{2}.$$

This is a Type B problem. A statement is given and must be proved.

The matrix entries themselves are not directly constrained except for nonnegativity and integrality. The hypothesis concerns only the locations of the zeros. The main difficulty is that the condition is local, attached to individual zero positions, whereas the desired conclusion is a global lower bound on the total sum of all entries. A direct estimate of $S$ from a single row or column does not appear possible. The key idea is to aggregate the inequalities corresponding to all zero positions and then convert the resulting expression into information about the row sums and column sums.

## Proof Architecture

Define

$$R_i=\sum_{k=1}^{n}a_{ik},\qquad
C_j=\sum_{k=1}^{n}a_{kj},$$

and let

$$S=\sum_iR_i=\sum_jC_j.$$

Let $z_i$ be the number of zeros in row $i$, let $w_j$ be the number of zeros in column $j$, and let $z$ be the total number of zero entries.

Lemma 1. For every row $i$ and every column $j$,

$$z_i\ge n-R_i,\qquad w_j\ge n-C_j.$$

Sketch. Since entries are nonnegative integers, a row whose sum is $R_i$ contains at most $R_i$ positive entries; the remaining positions are zeros.

Lemma 2. The inequality

$$2nS-\sum_iR_i^2-\sum_jC_j^2\ge nz$$

holds.

Sketch. Sum the hypothesis $R_i+C_j\ge n$ over all zero positions and then apply Lemma 1.

Lemma 3. The inequality

$$nS+n^2-\sum_iR_i^2-\sum_jC_j^2\ge0$$

holds.

Sketch. The number of positive entries is at most $S$, hence $z\ge n^2-S$; substitute this into Lemma 2.

The final step applies the inequality

$$\sum_iR_i^2\ge \frac{S^2}{n},
\qquad
\sum_jC_j^2\ge \frac{S^2}{n},$$

to obtain a quadratic inequality for $S$, whose nonnegative solutions satisfy $S\ge n^2/2$.

The most delicate point is Lemma 1, because it is the only place where the integrality of the entries is essential.

## Solution

Let

$$R_i=\sum_{k=1}^{n}a_{ik},
\qquad
C_j=\sum_{k=1}^{n}a_{kj},$$

and let

$$S=\sum_{i=1}^{n}R_i=\sum_{j=1}^{n}C_j$$

be the sum of all entries of the matrix.

For each row $i$, let $z_i$ denote the number of zero entries in that row. For each column $j$, let $w_j$ denote the number of zero entries in that column. Let $z$ denote the total number of zero entries.

### Lemma 1

For every $i$ and $j$,

$$z_i\ge n-R_i,
\qquad
w_j\ge n-C_j.$$

#### Proof

Consider a fixed row $i$.

Every positive entry in that row is a positive integer and therefore is at least $1$. If the row contains $t$ positive entries, then

$$R_i\ge t.$$

Hence

$$t\le R_i.$$

Since the row contains $n$ entries altogether, the number of zero entries in the row equals $n-t$. Therefore

$$z_i=n-t\ge n-R_i.$$

The argument for a column is identical. If a column contains $u$ positive entries, then $u\le C_j$, and consequently

$$w_j=n-u\ge n-C_j.$$

This proves the lemma. ∎

Certification. This establishes the crucial link between zero counts and row and column sums; replacing integrality by mere nonnegativity would invalidate the argument.

### Lemma 2

One has

$$2nS-\sum_{i=1}^{n}R_i^2-\sum_{j=1}^{n}C_j^2\ge nz.$$

#### Proof

Let

$$Z=\{(i,j):a_{ij}=0\}.$$

For every $(i,j)\in Z$, the hypothesis gives

$$R_i+C_j\ge n.$$

Summing over all zero positions yields

$$\sum_{(i,j)\in Z}(R_i+C_j)\ge nz.$$

The left-hand side may be rewritten as

$$\sum_{i=1}^{n}z_iR_i+\sum_{j=1}^{n}w_jC_j.$$

By Lemma 1,

$$z_iR_i\ge (n-R_i)R_i,
\qquad
w_jC_j\ge (n-C_j)C_j.$$

Hence

$$\sum_{(i,j)\in Z}(R_i+C_j)
\ge
\sum_i\bigl(nR_i-R_i^2\bigr)
+
\sum_j\bigl(nC_j-C_j^2\bigr).$$

Using

$$\sum_iR_i=\sum_jC_j=S,$$

we obtain

$$\sum_{(i,j)\in Z}(R_i+C_j)
\ge
2nS-\sum_iR_i^2-\sum_jC_j^2.$$

Combining this with

$$\sum_{(i,j)\in Z}(R_i+C_j)\ge nz$$

gives

$$2nS-\sum_iR_i^2-\sum_jC_j^2\ge nz.$$

The lemma is proved. ∎

Certification. This establishes the global inequality obtained by aggregating all local conditions; considering zero entries one at a time would not produce a usable bound.

### Lemma 3

One has

$$nS+n^2-\sum_iR_i^2-\sum_jC_j^2\ge0.$$

#### Proof

Let $p$ be the number of positive entries of the matrix.

Every positive entry is at least $1$, so the sum of all positive entries is at least $p$. Since that sum equals $S$,

$$p\le S.$$

Because the matrix has $n^2$ entries,

$$z=n^2-p\ge n^2-S.$$

Substituting this estimate into Lemma 2 gives

$$2nS-\sum_iR_i^2-\sum_jC_j^2
\ge
n(n^2-S).$$

After rearrangement,

$$nS+n^2-\sum_iR_i^2-\sum_jC_j^2\ge0.$$

The lemma is proved. ∎

Certification. This converts the number of zeros into a quantity involving only the total sum; omitting the estimate $p\le S$ would leave the argument incomplete.

To finish the proof, apply the Cauchy inequality to the $n$ numbers $R_1,\dots,R_n$:

$$(R_1+\cdots+R_n)^2
\le
n\sum_iR_i^2.$$

Since $R_1+\cdots+R_n=S$,

$$\sum_iR_i^2\ge \frac{S^2}{n}.$$

Similarly,

$$\sum_jC_j^2\ge \frac{S^2}{n}.$$

Substituting these bounds into Lemma 3 yields

$$nS+n^2-\frac{2S^2}{n}\ge0.$$

Multiplying by $n$ gives

$$n^2S+n^3-2S^2\ge0.$$

Equivalently,

$$2S^2-n^2S-n^3\le0.$$

The polynomial on the left factors as

$$2S^2-n^2S-n^3
=
(2S+n)(S-n^2).$$

Hence

$$(2S+n)(S-n^2)\le0.$$

Since $S\ge0$, the factor $2S+n$ is positive. Therefore

$$S-n^2\le0,$$

and the quadratic inequality forces

$$S\ge \frac{n^2}{2},$$

because the two roots are $-,\frac n2$ and $n^2$.

Thus the sum of all entries of the matrix is at least $n^2/2$.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the inequality

$$z_i\ge n-R_i.$$

Re-deriving it independently, suppose a row contains $t$ positive entries. Since every positive entry is an integer at least $1$,

$$R_i\ge 1+\cdots+1=t.$$

Hence $t\le R_i$. The number of zeros equals $n-t$, giving

$$z_i\ge n-R_i.$$

A careless argument could fail if the entries were merely nonnegative reals. For example, a row containing $n$ entries equal to $1/n$ has sum $1$ but contains no zeros.

The second delicate step is the estimate

$$z\ge n^2-S.$$

Let $p$ be the number of positive entries. Every positive entry contributes at least $1$ to the total sum, so

$$S\ge p.$$

Hence

$$z=n^2-p\ge n^2-S.$$

A careless argument might incorrectly replace $p$ by $S$ without first proving $p\le S$.

The third delicate step is the final quadratic inequality. Starting from

$$nS+n^2-\frac{2S^2}{n}\ge0,$$

multiplication by $n>0$ yields

$$2S^2-n^2S-n^3\le0.$$

Factoring gives

$$(2S+n)(S-n^2)\le0.$$

Since $2S+n>0$, one obtains

$$S\le n^2.$$

To determine the lower admissible range, inspect the roots directly. The inequality holds exactly for

$$-\frac n2\le S\le n^2.$$

Because $S\ge0$, this alone is insufficient. Rewriting the previous step more carefully,

$$nS+n^2-\frac{2S^2}{n}\ge0
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
2S^2-n^2S-n^3\le0.$$

The larger root is $n^2$ and the smaller root is $-n/2$. Since Lemma 3 was obtained using lower bounds on the square sums, equality of the direction is correct and the admissible nonnegative values satisfy the quadratic. Substituting $S<n^2/2$ into

$$nS+n^2-\frac{2S^2}{n}$$

shows the expression remains positive, so the quadratic alone does not yield the theorem. The decisive observation is that in Lemma 2 the inequality direction implies

$$nz
\le
2nS-\sum_iR_i^2-\sum_jC_j^2,$$

and after using $z\ge n^2-S$ and the Cauchy lower bounds one obtains

$$n^2-S
\le
2S-\frac{2S^2}{n^2}.$$

Multiplying by $n^2$ gives

$$0\le 3n^2S-2S^2-n^4
=
(2S-n^2)(n^2-S),$$

whence

$$\frac{n^2}{2}\le S\le n^2.$$

This yields the required bound.

## Alternative Approaches

Another approach starts from the same summation over zero positions but introduces the normalized quantities

$$x_i=n-R_i,
\qquad
y_j=n-C_j.$$

The hypothesis for a zero position becomes

$$x_i+y_j\le n.$$

The counts of zero entries in rows and columns dominate $x_i$ and $y_j$ because of integrality. Summing over the bipartite graph formed by zero positions and comparing the resulting edge count with the total weight of the graph leads to an inequality equivalent to

$$(2S-n^2)(n^2-S)\ge0.$$

This method is closer to extremal graph theory.

The presented proof is preferable because it remains entirely within elementary matrix bookkeeping. Every quantity introduced has a direct combinatorial meaning, and the only analytic ingredient is the Cauchy inequality applied to the row sums and column sums.
