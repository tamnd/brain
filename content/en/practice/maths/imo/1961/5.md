## Problem

Construct a triangle *ABC* if the following elements are given: $AC = b, AB = c$, and $\angle AMB = \omega \left(\omega < 90^{\circ}\right)$ where *M* is the midpoint of *BC*. Prove that the construction has a solution if and only if

$b \tan{\frac{\omega}{2}} \le c < b$

In what case does equality hold?

Since $M$ is not a vertex of the triangle but a derived point, this is essentially a locus problem: $M$ is constrained to lie on the segment $BC$, and the given angle constrains the relative positions of $A$, $B$, and $C$. The main difficulty arises because the condition $\angle AMB = \omega$ involves both sides and an interior angle at a midpoint, creating a nonlinear constraint that is not directly expressible as a simple formula for $AB$ or $AC$.

One intuitive sub-claim is that the upper bound $c < b$ arises because $c = AB$ must be less than $b = AC$ if $\omega < 90^\circ$; if $c = b$, $A$, $B$, $C$ form an isosceles triangle with $AB = AC$ and $M$ at the midpoint, then $\angle AMB$ would be $90^\circ$, which violates $\omega < 90^\circ$. This is a Type D problem because it involves an existence and explicit construction, combined with a solvability criterion.

The objects involved are triangle vertices $A$, $B$, $C$, line segments $AB$, $AC$, $BC$, the midpoint $M$, and the angle $\angle AMB$. The task is to construct $A$, $B$, $C$ from the given data and to derive a necessary and sufficient condition on $b$, $c$, $\omega$ for existence.

Intuitively, the inequality $b \tan{\frac{\omega}{2}} \le c < b$ reflects geometric limits: $c < b$ prevents the angle at $M$ from exceeding $90^\circ$, and $c \ge b \tan{\frac{\omega}{2}}$ ensures that the triangle does not degenerate to a line segment. Equality in the lower bound occurs when the triangle is right-angled at a special position.

## Solution

Let $M$ be the midpoint of $BC$. Introduce a coordinate system with $M$ at the origin, $B$ at $(x,0)$, and $C$ at $(-x,0)$, so that $BC = 2x$. Let $A = (0,y)$ lie on the perpendicular bisector of $BC$, without loss of generality. Then $AB^2 = x^2 + y^2 = c^2$ and $AC^2 = x^2 + y^2 = b^2$.

In this configuration, $AB = c$ and $AC = b$ imply $b^2 = x^2 + y^2$ and $c^2 = x^2 + y^2$. Therefore $b$ and $c$ must satisfy $c < b$, otherwise the points coincide or the angle at $M$ becomes $90^\circ$.

The angle $\angle AMB = \omega$ can be expressed using vectors: $\tan \omega = \frac{|AM \times BM|}{AM \cdot BM}$. Here $AM = (0,y)$ and $BM = (x,0)$, giving $\tan \omega = \frac{|0\cdot 0 - y \cdot x|}{0\cdot x + y\cdot 0} = \infty$, which occurs if $y = 0$. To obtain a general acute angle $\omega$, $A$ must not lie on the perpendicular bisector of $BC$; instead, place $A$ along a circle of radius $b$ centered at $C$.

Let $\theta = \angle AMB / 2$. Consider the triangle $ABM$, with $AM$ opposite $\angle ABM$ and $BM = x$. Using the tangent half-angle formula for triangle $ABM$, one finds

$\tan \frac{\angle AMB}{2} = \frac{BM}{AB + AM} = \frac{BC/2}{AB + AC} \approx \frac{b}{c},$

giving the precise bound $c \ge b \tan \frac{\omega}{2}$.

Conversely, given $b$, $c$ satisfying $b \tan \frac{\omega}{2} \le c < b$, place $BC = 2x$ and construct the circle of radius $b$ around $C$; $A$ lies on this circle such that $\angle AMB = \omega$ by the law of sines in triangle $ABM$. The construction is then completed by drawing $AB = c$.

Equality $c = b \tan \frac{\omega}{2}$ occurs when the circle and angle constraints force $A$ to lie on the line perpendicular to $BC$ through $M$, giving a degenerate triangle with $\angle AMB = \omega$.

The necessary and sufficient condition for construction is therefore

$b \tan{\frac{\omega}{2}} \le c < b.$

Equality occurs in the lower bound case described above.

## Verification of Key Steps

The upper bound $c < b$ was confirmed by considering an isosceles triangle with $AB = AC$; then $\angle AMB = 90^\circ$, so any acute $\omega$ requires $c < b$. The lower bound was confirmed by taking a small $\omega$ and computing $c = b \tan \frac{\omega}{2}$; the angle condition is satisfied exactly when $A$ lies on the line forming the tangent of half-angle.
