## Problem

Find all three-digit numbers for which one obtains, when dividing the number by 11, the sum of the squares of the digits of the initial number.

---
## Exploration

Let the three-digit number be

$$100a+10b+c,$$

where $a\in{1,\dots,9}$ and $b,c\in{0,\dots,9}$. The condition says that when this number is divided by $11$, the quotient equals

$$a^2+b^2+c^2.$$

Hence

$$100a+10b+c=11(a^2+b^2+c^2).$$

The first task is to understand the size restrictions. Since

$$a^2+b^2+c^2\le 9^2+9^2+9^2=243,$$

the right-hand side is at most $2673$, which does not help much. A better estimate comes from the fact that the left-hand side is only three digits. Since

$$100\le 100a+10b+c\le 999,$$

the quotient upon division by $11$ lies between

$$\frac{100}{11}\quad\text{and}\quad \frac{999}{11},$$

so

$$10\le a^2+b^2+c^2\le 90.$$

Trying small values of $a$ may help. Rearranging,

$$11b^2-10b+11c^2-c=100a-11a^2.$$

The right-hand side depends only on $a$. Compute it for the first several values:

$$\begin{aligned}
a=1&:\ 89,\\
a=2&:\ 156,\\
a=3&:\ 201,\\
a=4&:\ 224,\\
a=5&:\ 225,\\
a=6&:\ 204,\\
a=7&:\ 161,\\
a=8&:\ 96,\\
a=9&:\ 9.
\end{aligned}$$

The left-hand side is nonnegative and grows quadratically in $b,c$. Since

$$11b^2-10b=b(11b-10),$$

its values for $b=0,\dots,9$ are

$$0,1,24,69,136,225,336,469,624,801.$$

Similarly,

$$11c^2-c=c(11c-1)$$

gives

$$0,10,42,96,172,270,390,532,696,882.$$

Now test the possible right-hand sides. For $a=9$, the equation becomes

$$11b^2-10b+11c^2-c=9.$$

The second summand is either $0$ or at least $10$, so necessarily $c=0$. Then

$$11b^2-10b=9,$$

which has no digit solution.

For $a=8$, the equation becomes

$$11b^2-10b+11c^2-c=96.$$

Possible decompositions from the tables are

$$96=0+96,$$

giving $(b,c)=(0,3)$, and

$$96=96+0,$$

but $96$ never occurs in the first table. Hence $(a,b,c)=(8,0,3)$ works. Check:

$$803/11=73,\qquad 8^2+0^2+3^2=64+9=73.$$

Continue. For $a=7$, the target is $161$. Possible combinations fail. For $a=6$, the target is $204$. Since $172+24=196$ and $96+69=165$, nothing works. For $a=5$, the target is $225$. The decomposition

$$225=225+0$$

gives $(b,c)=(5,0)$, hence $550$. Check:

$$550/11=50,\qquad 5^2+5^2+0^2=50.$$

Testing the remaining $a$ gives no more solutions. The likely answer is

$$550,\ 803.$$

The dangerous step is proving exhaustively that no other decompositions exist. A careless search can miss a combination. The cleanest method is to tabulate all attainable values of the two quadratic expressions and check each target systematically.

## Problem Understanding

We seek all three-digit integers whose quotient upon division by $11$ equals the sum of the squares of their digits.

This is a Type A problem, because we must determine all objects satisfying a given condition. The solution must prove two directions: first, that the claimed numbers satisfy the condition; second, that no other three-digit numbers do.

The mathematical objects involved are decimal digits and a quadratic Diophantine equation. Writing the number as

$$100a+10b+c,$$

the condition becomes

$$100a+10b+c=11(a^2+b^2+c^2).$$

The difficulty lies in controlling all digit triples simultaneously. A naive brute-force check would require examining $900$ numbers. The structure of the equation reduces the problem to a finite search over much smaller sets of quadratic values.

The expected answer is

$$550\quad\text{and}\quad 803.$$

The equation separates naturally into a part depending only on $a$ and a part depending only on $b,c$, and the resulting numerical constraints become rigid enough that only two digit triples survive.

## Proof Architecture

The proof proceeds through three claims.

### Claim 1

If

$$100a+10b+c=11(a^2+b^2+c^2),$$

then

$$11b^2-10b+11c^2-c=100a-11a^2.$$

This follows by moving all terms involving $a$ to one side and all terms involving $b,c$ to the other.

### Claim 2

For digits $b,c\in{0,\dots,9}$, the possible values of

$$11b^2-10b$$

and

$$11c^2-c$$

can be listed explicitly, and comparison with the possible values of

$$100a-11a^2$$

for $a\in{1,\dots,9}$

shows that only two digit triples satisfy the equation.

This is the computational core of the argument. The main risk is missing a valid decomposition, so every possible value must be checked systematically.

### Claim 3

The numbers $550$ and $803$ indeed satisfy the original condition.

This requires direct substitution into the defining property.

The hardest direction is excluding all other numbers. The most fragile point is the exhaustive comparison of attainable quadratic values.

## Solution

Let the three-digit number be

$$N=100a+10b+c,$$

where

$$a\in\{1,2,\dots,9\},\qquad b,c\in\{0,1,\dots,9\}.$$

The hypothesis states that dividing $N$ by $11$ yields the sum of the squares of the digits. Hence

$$\frac{100a+10b+c}{11}=a^2+b^2+c^2.$$

Multiplying by $11$ gives

$$100a+10b+c=11a^2+11b^2+11c^2.$$

### Claim 1

Every solution satisfies

$$11b^2-10b+11c^2-c=100a-11a^2.$$

#### Proof

Starting from

$$100a+10b+c=11a^2+11b^2+11c^2,$$

move the terms involving $b,c$ to the left and the terms involving $a$ to the right:

$$11b^2-10b+11c^2-c=100a-11a^2.$$

This is exactly the desired identity. ∎

This establishes the separated form of the equation; without separating variables, the later finite analysis would become disorganized.

For each possible leading digit $a$, compute

$$100a-11a^2.$$

The values are

$$\begin{aligned}
a=1&:\ 89,\\
a=2&:\ 156,\\
a=3&:\ 201,\\
a=4&:\ 224,\\
a=5&:\ 225,\\
a=6&:\ 204,\\
a=7&:\ 161,\\
a=8&:\ 96,\\
a=9&:\ 9.
\end{aligned}$$

Next compute all possible values of

$$11b^2-10b$$

for digits $b$:

$$\begin{aligned}
b=0&:\ 0,\\
b=1&:\ 1,\\
b=2&:\ 24,\\
b=3&:\ 69,\\
b=4&:\ 136,\\
b=5&:\ 225,\\
b=6&:\ 336,\\
b=7&:\ 469,\\
b=8&:\ 624,\\
b=9&:\ 801.
\end{aligned}$$

Similarly, compute all possible values of

$$11c^2-c$$

for digits $c$:

$$\begin{aligned}
c=0&:\ 0,\\
c=1&:\ 10,\\
c=2&:\ 42,\\
c=3&:\ 96,\\
c=4&:\ 172,\\
c=5&:\ 270,\\
c=6&:\ 390,\\
c=7&:\ 532,\\
c=8&:\ 696,\\
c=9&:\ 882.
\end{aligned}$$

### Claim 2

The only digit triples satisfying the equation are

$$(a,b,c)=(5,5,0)\quad\text{and}\quad (8,0,3).$$

#### Proof

We compare each possible value of

$$100a-11a^2$$

with sums

$$(11b^2-10b)+(11c^2-c).$$

For $a=1$, the target value is $89$. Since the second table contains only

$$0,10,42,96,\dots,$$

and the first contains only

$$0,1,24,69,136,\dots,$$

no pair sums to $89$.

For $a=2$, the target value is $156$. The possible sums below $156$ are

$$0,1,10,11,24,34,42,43,52,66,69,79,96,97,106,111,120,136,146.$$

None equals $156$.

For $a=3$, the target value is $201$. Since $172$ is the largest second-table value below $201$, subtraction gives

$$201-172=29,$$

which is not in the first table. The other possibilities give

$$201-96=105,\quad 201-42=159,\quad 201-10=191,\quad 201-0=201,$$

none of which belong to the first table.

For $a=4$, the target value is $224$. Subtracting the second-table values gives

$$224,\ 214,\ 182,\ 128,\ 52.$$

None belongs to the first table.

For $a=5$, the target value is $225$. Since

$$225=225+0,$$

we obtain

$$b=5,\qquad c=0.$$

No other decomposition exists because

$$225-172=53,\quad 225-96=129,\quad 225-42=183,\quad 225-10=215,$$

and none of these numbers appears in the first table.

For $a=6$, the target value is $204$. Subtracting the second-table values gives

$$204,\ 194,\ 162,\ 108,\ 32.$$

None belongs to the first table.

For $a=7$, the target value is $161$. Subtracting the second-table values gives

$$161,\ 151,\ 119,\ 65.$$

None belongs to the first table.

For $a=8$, the target value is $96$. Since

$$96=0+96,$$

we obtain

$$b=0,\qquad c=3.$$

The remaining subtractions,

$$96-42=54,\qquad 96-10=86,\qquad 96-0=96,$$

do not yield values in the first table.

For $a=9$, the target value is $9$. Since every positive value in the second table is at least $10$, necessarily

$$c=0.$$

Then

$$11b^2-10b=9.$$

The first table shows that no digit $b$ satisfies this equation.

Thus the only solutions are

$$(a,b,c)=(5,5,0)\quad\text{and}\quad (8,0,3).$$

∎

This establishes exhaustively that no other digit triples satisfy the separated equation; the tempting shortcut of testing only plausible-looking triples would not prove completeness.

### Claim 3

The numbers $550$ and $803$ satisfy the original condition.

#### Proof

For $550$,

$$\frac{550}{11}=50,$$

while

$$5^2+5^2+0^2=25+25+0=50.$$

Hence $550$ satisfies the condition.

For $803$,

$$\frac{803}{11}=73,$$

while

$$8^2+0^2+3^2=64+0+9=73.$$

Hence $803$ satisfies the condition. ∎

This verifies directly that the surviving candidates are genuine solutions; without substituting back into the original condition, accidental solutions of a transformed equation could remain.

Hence the complete set of three-digit numbers satisfying the condition is

$$\boxed{550,\ 803}.$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The most delicate step is the exhaustive exclusion in Claim 2. Re-derive the case $a=4$ independently. The equation becomes

$$11b^2-10b+11c^2-c=224.$$

The possible values of

$$11c^2-c$$

not exceeding $224$ are

$$0,10,42,96,172.$$

Subtracting from $224$ gives

$$224,214,182,128,52.$$

Direct computation of

$$11b^2-10b$$

for $b=0,\dots,9$

yields

$$0,1,24,69,136,225,336,469,624,801,$$

and none matches. A careless argument could miss the value $172$ and falsely conclude too early.

Another delicate step is the case $a=8$. The equation becomes

$$11b^2-10b+11c^2-c=96.$$

The second summand can equal $96$ when $c=3$. Subtracting gives

$$96-96=0,$$

which corresponds to $b=0$. This yields the valid solution $803$. A careless search that checks only positive values of both summands would miss this solution.

A third delicate point is the transformation in Claim 1. Starting from

$$100a+10b+c=11a^2+11b^2+11c^2,$$

subtracting $10b+c$ from both sides gives

$$100a=11a^2+11b^2-10b+11c^2-c.$$

Then subtracting $11a^2$ yields

$$100a-11a^2=11b^2-10b+11c^2-c.$$

If the linear terms are moved with the wrong sign, the later tables become meaningless.

## Alternative Approaches

A different strategy uses congruences modulo $11$. Since

$$100a+10b+c\equiv a-b+c\pmod{11},$$

and the number is divisible by $11(a^2+b^2+c^2)$, we obtain

$$a-b+c\equiv 0\pmod{11}.$$

Because

$$-9\le a-b+c\le 18,$$

the only possibilities are

$$a-b+c=0\quad\text{or}\quad a-b+c=11.$$

Combining this with

$$100a+10b+c=11(a^2+b^2+c^2)$$

produces strong restrictions on the digits and reduces the search substantially.

Another approach bounds the quadratic terms directly. From

$$100a+10b+c=11(a^2+b^2+c^2),$$

rewrite as

$$11\left(a-\frac{50}{11}\right)^2+11\left(b-\frac{5}{11}\right)^2+11\left(c-\frac{1}{22}\right)^2=\text{constant}.$$

This shows that only digits near certain ranges are possible. The method is less efficient computationally, because the resulting constant is awkward and the remaining search is still nontrivial.

The main approach is preferable because it converts the problem into a finite decomposition problem with completely explicit tables, making completeness transparent.
