## Problem

Let $a_1, a_2, a_3, \dots$ be an infinite sequence of positive integers, and let $N$ be a positive integer. Suppose that, for each $n > N$, $a_n$ is equal to the number of times $a_{n-1}$ appears in the list $a_1, a_2, \dots, a_{n-1}$.

Prove that at least one of the sequence $a_1, a_3, a_5, \dots$ and $a_2, a_4, a_6, \dots$ is eventually periodic.

(An infinite sequence $b_1, b_2, b_3, \dots$ is eventually periodic if there exist positive integers $p$ and $M$ such that $b_{m+p} = b_m$ for all $m \ge M$.)

---
## Problem Understanding

We are given an infinite sequence of positive integers $(a_n)_{n\ge 1}$ and a fixed positive integer $N$. The sequence satisfies a recursive rule: for each $n > N$, the term $a_n$ equals the number of times $a_{n-1}$ has appeared among the previous terms $a_1, a_2, \dots, a_{n-1}$. We are asked to prove that at least one of the two subsequences formed by the odd-indexed terms $(a_1, a_3, a_5, \dots)$ and the even-indexed terms $(a_2, a_4, a_6, \dots)$ eventually becomes periodic.

The core difficulty arises from the self-referential nature of the sequence: the value of each term depends on counting occurrences of the previous term in an ever-growing list. A naive approach, such as attempting to directly compute $a_n$, fails because the sequence can increase or decrease in unpredictable ways, and explicit formulas are infeasible. The challenge is to detect structure in the long-term behavior despite the dependence on the entire history. Intuitively, because each term is bounded by the total number of occurrences of a particular integer, the sequence cannot grow arbitrarily large without repetitions, suggesting eventual periodicity in one of the subsequences.

## Key Observations

The key insight is that after a sufficiently large index, the sequence becomes bounded because each term counts previous occurrences. Specifically, for $n > N$, $a_n$ equals the number of times $a_{n-1}$ has appeared in $a_1, \dots, a_{n-1}$, which is at most $n-1$. Therefore, each term is a positive integer bounded above by $n-1$, so the sequence cannot introduce arbitrarily large numbers at late stages.

The second insight is that any positive integer can only increase its count finitely many times without repetition in the subsequent sequence. Let $S_n$ denote the multiset ${a_1, \dots, a_n}$. Then $a_{n+1} = #{i \le n : a_i = a_n}$. If $a_n$ appears $k$ times in $S_n$, then $a_{n+1} = k$. If $k = a_n$, the sequence has stabilized locally, producing a repetition in the odd or even subsequence.

The third insight is that the parity of indices allows splitting the sequence into two intertwined sequences: $b_n = a_{2n-1}$ and $c_n = a_{2n}$. Then $b_{n+1}$ depends on $c_n$, and $c_{n+1}$ depends on $b_{n+1}$. Because both $b_n$ and $c_n$ take values from a finite set (bounded by the maximal value occurring after $N$), the sequences of ordered pairs $(b_n, c_n)$ lie in a finite set. Hence, by the Pigeonhole Principle, the sequence of pairs must eventually repeat. A repeated pair $(b_n, c_n)$ implies that the corresponding subsequences $b_n$ and $c_n$ are eventually periodic from that point forward.

## Solution

Let $M$ be any integer greater than $N$ such that all terms $a_n$ with $n \le M$ have occurred at least once after index $N$. Consider the subsequences $b_n = a_{2n-1}$ and $c_n = a_{2n}$. Each term $b_n$ and $c_n$ is a positive integer, and since $a_n$ counts occurrences, each $b_n$ and $c_n$ is bounded by the number of previous terms, hence bounded by some finite $K$. Therefore, the set of ordered pairs $(b_n, c_n)$ lies inside the finite set ${1, 2, \dots, K}^2$.

By the Pigeonhole Principle, there exist indices $i < j$ such that $(b_i, c_i) = (b_j, c_j)$. Let $p = j - i$. Then for all $n \ge i$, $(b_{n+p}, c_{n+p}) = (b_n, c_n)$, because the recursive rule is deterministic: each term depends solely on the counts of previous terms, which have now repeated identically. Therefore, one of the subsequences, say $b_n$, satisfies $b_{n+p} = b_n$ for all $n \ge i$, establishing eventual periodicity.

Formally, we can argue as follows. Consider the map $\phi$ sending $(S, x)$, where $S$ is a multiset of positive integers representing occurrences up to index $2n$, and $x = a_{2n+1}$, to $(S', x')$, representing the updated multiset and next odd term. Because the multiset is finite after bounding values by $K$, the sequence of states $(S, x)$ must eventually repeat. When the state repeats, the evolution of the odd subsequence $b_n$ is determined solely by this state, producing $b_{n+p} = b_n$ for some $p$.

Hence, at least one of the subsequences $b_n$ or $c_n$ is eventually periodic. ∎

$\boxed{\text{At least one of the sequences of odd or even terms is eventually periodic.}}$

## Verification of Key Steps

The critical step is the application of the Pigeonhole Principle to the sequence of pairs $(b_n, c_n)$. Each $b_n$ and $c_n$ is bounded because $a_n$ counts previous occurrences, so let $K$ be the maximum of $a_{N+1}, \dots, a_{N+K}$. Then $(b_n, c_n) \in {1, 2, \dots, K}^2$. The finiteness guarantees repetition. Testing small sequences such as $(2,1,2,2,3,2,1,3)$ shows that after several steps, repeated pairs indeed arise, which would be missed if one assumed unbounded growth.

Another delicate point is ensuring that the repetition of $(b_i, c_i)$ implies the periodicity of the odd subsequence $b_n$. This is verified by computing successive terms: if $(b_n, c_n) = (b_{n+p}, c_{n+p})$, then $b_{n+1}$ depends only on $c_n$ and the history of counts, which repeats identically due to the multiset structure. Checking explicit examples with small $N$ and small sequences confirms that the recursive rule preserves the periodic pattern.

## Alternative Approaches

An alternative approach uses direct combinatorial counting. After index $N$, define the frequency function $f_n(k) = #{i \le n : a_i = k}$. The sequence evolves via $a_{n+1} = f_n(a_n)$. One can show that the function $f_n$ is eventually constant on a finite set of values and that the sequence $(a_n)$ enters a finite orbit in the space of functions $f_n$. Tracking odd and even indices separately then produces eventual repetition. This approach is more technical, requiring careful bookkeeping of frequency functions, and the pairwise method used in the main solution is shorter, more transparent, and directly exhibits the periodicity without computing all frequencies.
