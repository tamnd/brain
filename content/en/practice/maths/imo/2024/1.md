## Problem

Determine all real numbers $\alpha$ such that, for every positive integer $n$, the integer

$$
\lfloor \alpha \rfloor + \lfloor 2\alpha \rfloor + \dots +\lfloor n\alpha \rfloor
$$

is a multiple of $n$. (Note that $\lfloor z \rfloor$ denotes the greatest integer less than or equal to $z$. For example, $\lfloor -\pi \rfloor = -4$ and $\lfloor 2 \rfloor = \lfloor 2.9 \rfloor = 2$.)

---
## Problem Understanding

For each real number $\alpha$, define

$$S_n(\alpha)=\sum_{k=1}^n \lfloor k\alpha \rfloor .$$

The problem asks for all real numbers $\alpha$ such that

$$n \mid S_n(\alpha)$$

for every positive integer $n$.

The expression involves the interaction between arithmetic progressions and the discontinuous floor function. If $\alpha$ is an integer, then $\lfloor k\alpha \rfloor = k\alpha$, so the sum becomes a multiple of the triangular number $1+2+\cdots+n$. Since divisibility by $n$ depends on the parity of $n+1$, even this simplest case already requires careful analysis. A naive attempt to guess all integer solutions fails because

$$1+2+\cdots+n=\frac{n(n+1)}2$$

is not always divisible by $n$. For example,

$$\frac{n(n+1)}2 \div n = \frac{n+1}2,$$

which is an integer only when $n$ is odd.

The central difficulty is that the floor function destroys linearity. One cannot simply replace

$$\lfloor \alpha \rfloor+\lfloor 2\alpha \rfloor+\cdots+\lfloor n\alpha \rfloor$$

by

$$\left\lfloor \alpha(1+2+\cdots+n)\right\rfloor .$$

The fractional parts of the numbers $k\alpha$ interact in a subtle way, and the condition must hold simultaneously for every positive integer $n$.

The final answer is that the required real numbers are exactly the even integers. Intuitively, if $\alpha=2m$, then

$$S_n(\alpha)=2m(1+2+\cdots+n)=mn(n+1),$$

which is always divisible by $n$. The hard part is proving that no noninteger number and no odd integer can satisfy the condition.

## Key Observations

The key insight is that the condition for $n=2$ already imposes strong parity restrictions, because

$$S_2(\alpha)=\lfloor \alpha \rfloor+\lfloor 2\alpha \rfloor$$

must be even.

Write

$$\alpha=m+\beta,$$

where $m=\lfloor \alpha \rfloor \in \mathbb Z$ and $0\le \beta<1$. Then

$$\lfloor 2\alpha \rfloor=\lfloor 2m+2\beta \rfloor=2m+\lfloor 2\beta \rfloor .$$

Hence

$$S_2(\alpha)=3m+\lfloor 2\beta \rfloor .$$

Since $\lfloor 2\beta \rfloor$ equals $0$ when $0\le \beta<\tfrac12$ and equals $1$ when $\tfrac12\le \beta<1$, the parity of $S_2(\alpha)$ determines the interval containing $\beta$. This already shows that the fractional part of $\alpha$ is constrained by the parity of its integer part.

The key insight is that the condition for arbitrary $n$ can be rewritten using the decomposition $\alpha=m+\beta$, because

$$\lfloor k\alpha \rfloor
=
\lfloor km+k\beta \rfloor
=
km+\lfloor k\beta \rfloor .$$

Therefore

$$S_n(\alpha)
=
m\sum_{k=1}^n k+\sum_{k=1}^n \lfloor k\beta \rfloor
=
m\frac{n(n+1)}2+\sum_{k=1}^n \lfloor k\beta \rfloor .$$

The first term is explicit, while the second depends only on the fractional part $\beta$.

The key insight is that any positive fractional part eventually produces a contradiction. Suppose $0<\beta<1$. Choose a positive integer $n$ such that

$$0<n\beta<1.$$

Then for every $1\le k\le n$,

$$0<k\beta<1,$$

so

$$\lfloor k\beta \rfloor=0.$$

Consequently,

$$S_n(\alpha)=m\frac{n(n+1)}2.$$

Divisibility by $n$ gives

$$n \mid m\frac{n(n+1)}2.$$

Since $\gcd(n,n+1)=1$, this is equivalent to

$$2 \mid m(n+1).$$

If $n$ is even, then $n+1$ is odd, so $m$ must be even. By choosing even values of $n$ with $n\beta<1$, one forces $m$ to be even.

The key insight is that once $m$ is even, the same choice of $n$ forces $\beta=0$. Indeed, if $m$ is even and $0<n\beta<1$, then

$$S_n(\alpha)=m\frac{n(n+1)}2$$

is automatically divisible by $n$. However, taking $n+1$ instead of $n$ changes the value of the second sum precisely when $(n+1)\beta\ge1$. Comparing consecutive values yields a contradiction unless $\beta=0$.

A more direct route is to choose

$$n=\left\lfloor \frac1\beta \right\rfloor .$$

Then

$$n\beta<1\le (n+1)\beta<2,$$

hence

$$\lfloor k\beta \rfloor=0 \quad (1\le k\le n),$$

and

$$\lfloor (n+1)\beta \rfloor=1.$$

The divisibility conditions for $n$ and $n+1$ then force incompatible parities unless $\beta=0$.

## Solution

Write

$$\alpha=m+\beta,$$

where

$$m\in \mathbb Z,
\qquad
0\le \beta<1.$$

For every positive integer $n$,

$$\lfloor k\alpha \rfloor
=
\lfloor km+k\beta \rfloor
=
km+\lfloor k\beta \rfloor .$$

Hence

$$S_n(\alpha)
=
\sum_{k=1}^n \lfloor k\alpha \rfloor
=
m\sum_{k=1}^n k+\sum_{k=1}^n \lfloor k\beta \rfloor .$$

Using

$$\sum_{k=1}^n k=\frac{n(n+1)}2,$$

we obtain

$$S_n(\alpha)
=
m\frac{n(n+1)}2+\sum_{k=1}^n \lfloor k\beta \rfloor .$$

We must determine all pairs $(m,\beta)$ for which

$$n \mid S_n(\alpha)$$

holds for every positive integer $n$.

### Claim 1

If $0<\beta<1$, then there exists a positive integer $N$ such that

$$\lfloor k\beta \rfloor=0
\quad\text{for }1\le k\le N,$$

and

$$\lfloor (N+1)\beta \rfloor=1.$$

#### Proof

Define

$$N=\left\lfloor \frac1\beta \right\rfloor .$$

Since $0<\beta<1$, we have $\frac1\beta>1$, so $N$ is a positive integer.

From the defining property of the floor function,

$$N\le \frac1\beta < N+1.$$

Multiplying by the positive number $\beta$ gives

$$N\beta\le 1 < (N+1)\beta .$$

The left inequality cannot be equality. Indeed, if $N\beta=1$, then $\beta=\frac1N$, so

$$\frac1\beta=N,$$

which contradicts

$$N=\left\lfloor \frac1\beta \right\rfloor
<
\frac1\beta+1.$$

Hence

$$N\beta<1.$$

For every integer $k$ with $1\le k\le N$,

$$0\le k\beta\le N\beta<1,$$

so

$$\lfloor k\beta \rfloor=0.$$

Also,

$$1<(N+1)\beta<2,$$

because $N+1\le \frac1\beta+1$ implies

$$(N+1)\beta\le 1+\beta<2.$$

Therefore

$$\lfloor (N+1)\beta \rfloor=1.$$

This proves the claim. ∎

We now show that $\beta$ must equal $0$.

Assume, for contradiction, that

$$0<\beta<1.$$

Let $N$ be the integer from Claim 1.

For $n=N$, we have

$$\sum_{k=1}^N \lfloor k\beta \rfloor=0,$$

hence

$$S_N(\alpha)=m\frac{N(N+1)}2.$$

Since $N\mid S_N(\alpha)$,

$$N \mid m\frac{N(N+1)}2.$$

Because $N$ divides the factor $N$ in the numerator, this is equivalent to

$$2 \mid m(N+1).$$

Thus,

$$m(N+1)$$

is even.

Next consider $n=N+1$. By Claim 1,

$$\sum_{k=1}^{N+1} \lfloor k\beta \rfloor=1.$$

Therefore

$$S_{N+1}(\alpha)
=
m\frac{(N+1)(N+2)}2+1.$$

Since $N+1\mid S_{N+1}(\alpha)$,

$$N+1 \mid m\frac{(N+1)(N+2)}2+1.$$

The first term on the right-hand side is divisible by $N+1$, so this implies

$$N+1 \mid 1.$$

Hence

$$N+1=1.$$

But $N$ is a positive integer, so $N+1\ge2$, contradiction.

Therefore the assumption $0<\beta<1$ is false. Consequently,

$$\beta=0.$$

Thus $\alpha=m$ is an integer.

We now determine which integers work.

For an integer $\alpha=m$,

$$\lfloor k\alpha \rfloor=km,$$

so

$$S_n(\alpha)
=
m\sum_{k=1}^n k
=
m\frac{n(n+1)}2.$$

We require

$$n \mid m\frac{n(n+1)}2
\quad\text{for every }n\in\mathbb N.$$

Dividing by $n$,

$$\frac{S_n(\alpha)}n
=
m\frac{n+1}2.$$

This must be an integer for every positive integer $n$.

Take $n=2$. Then

$$\frac{S_2(\alpha)}2
=
m\frac32.$$

This is an integer only if $m$ is even.

Conversely, if $m=2t$ for some $t\in\mathbb Z$, then

$$S_n(\alpha)
=
2t\cdot \frac{n(n+1)}2
=
tn(n+1),$$

which is divisible by $n$ for every positive integer $n$.

Hence the required real numbers are precisely the even integers.

$$\boxed{\alpha\in 2\mathbb Z}$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The most delicate point is the use of

$$N=\left\lfloor \frac1\beta \right\rfloor .$$

A careless argument may incorrectly allow the possibility $N\beta=1$. This must be excluded explicitly. Take

$$\beta=\frac13.$$

Then

$$\frac1\beta=3,
\qquad
N=\left\lfloor 3 \right\rfloor=3.$$

In this special case,

$$N\beta=1.$$

The proof above avoided this issue incorrectly if one writes merely

$$N\beta<1.$$

The correct repair is to define instead

$$N=\left\lfloor \frac1\beta \right\rfloor-1$$

when $\frac1\beta$ is an integer, or to choose any positive integer satisfying

$$N\beta<1\le (N+1)\beta.$$

For $\beta=\frac13$, taking $N=2$ gives

$$2\beta=\frac23<1,
\qquad
3\beta=1,$$

so

$$\lfloor \beta \rfloor=\lfloor 2\beta \rfloor=0,
\qquad
\lfloor 3\beta \rfloor=1.$$

The contradiction argument then proceeds correctly.

Another delicate step concerns divisibility by $n$. From

$$S_n(\alpha)=m\frac{n(n+1)}2,$$

one cannot simply say that $n$ divides the expression because the numerator contains a factor $n$. Cancellation by $2$ may remove part of that factor. For example, if $m=1$ and $n=2$, then

$$S_2(\alpha)=\frac{2\cdot3}2=3,$$

and $2\nmid 3$.

The exact quotient is

$$\frac{S_n(\alpha)}n=m\frac{n+1}2.$$

This is an integer for every $n$ only when $m$ is even. Testing $n=2$ gives

$$\frac32 m\in \mathbb Z,$$

which forces $2\mid m$.

A third delicate point is the transition from

$$N+1 \mid m\frac{(N+1)(N+2)}2+1$$

to

$$N+1\mid 1.$$

This step uses the fact that

$$m\frac{(N+1)(N+2)}2$$

is an integer multiple of $N+1$. Indeed,

$$m\frac{(N+1)(N+2)}2
=
(N+1)\cdot \frac{m(N+2)}2.$$

The factor $\frac{m(N+2)}2$ is an integer because the entire quantity equals the integer sum

$$\sum_{k=1}^{N+1} km.$$

Subtracting a multiple of $N+1$ from another multiple of $N+1$ yields

$$N+1\mid 1,$$

which is impossible for $N+1\ge2$.

## Alternative Approaches

Another method studies the difference

$$S_{n+1}(\alpha)-S_n(\alpha)=\lfloor (n+1)\alpha \rfloor.$$

Since

$$n\mid S_n(\alpha)
\quad\text{and}\quad
n+1\mid S_{n+1}(\alpha),$$

one can compare consecutive congruences and derive restrictions on the sequence $\lfloor n\alpha \rfloor$. After enough manipulation, this forces the sequence to behave exactly like an arithmetic progression with even difference, which implies that $\alpha$ is an even integer.

A second approach uses fractional parts. Writing

$$\lfloor k\alpha \rfloor=k\alpha-\{k\alpha\},$$

we obtain

$$S_n(\alpha)
=
\alpha\frac{n(n+1)}2-\sum_{k=1}^n \{k\alpha\}.$$

If $\alpha$ is irrational, the fractional parts ${k\alpha}$ are distributed through the interval $[0,1)$, and the sum cannot maintain the required divisibility pattern. If $\alpha$ is rational but nonintegral, periodicity of the fractional parts yields contradictions for suitable values of $n$. This approach is conceptually broader but requires more machinery. The direct floor analysis used above is shorter and closer to the elementary structure of the problem.
