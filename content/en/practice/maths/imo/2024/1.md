## Problem

Determine all real numbers $\alpha$ such that, for every positive integer $n$, the integer

$$
\lfloor \alpha \rfloor + \lfloor 2\alpha \rfloor + \dots +\lfloor n\alpha \rfloor
$$

is a multiple of $n$. (Note that $\lfloor z \rfloor$ denotes the greatest integer less than or equal to $z$. For example, $\lfloor -\pi \rfloor = -4$ and $\lfloor 2 \rfloor = \lfloor 2.9 \rfloor = 2$.)

---
## Problem Understanding

For a real number $\alpha$, define

$$S_n(\alpha)=\sum_{k=1}^{n}\lfloor k\alpha\rfloor .$$

The problem asks for all real numbers $\alpha$ such that, for every positive integer $n$, the integer $S_n(\alpha)$ is divisible by $n$.

The expression involves the interaction between the linear sequence $k\alpha$ and the discontinuous floor function. Since the floor function depends on the fractional parts of the numbers $k\alpha$, the condition imposes a strong arithmetic restriction on $\alpha$.

A naive approach would try to evaluate the divisibility condition separately for each $n$. This quickly becomes unmanageable because the values $\lfloor k\alpha\rfloor$ fluctuate irregularly when $\alpha$ is not an integer. The main difficulty is to extract a global structural condition from infinitely many congruences.

The answer is

$$\alpha\in 2\mathbb{Z}.$$

Intuitively, even integers should work because if $\alpha=2m$, then

$$S_n(\alpha)=2m(1+2+\cdots+n)=mn(n+1),$$

which is always divisible by $n$. Odd integers fail because

$$S_2(\alpha)=\alpha+2\alpha=3\alpha$$

is not divisible by $2$ when $\alpha$ is odd. The difficult part is proving that no noninteger real number can satisfy the condition for every $n$.

## Key Observations

The key insight is that the condition for consecutive values of $n$ isolates a single floor term, because

$$S_n(\alpha)-S_{n-1}(\alpha)=\lfloor n\alpha\rfloor .$$

Suppose $n\mid S_n(\alpha)$ and $(n-1)\mid S_{n-1}(\alpha)$. Then there exist integers $a_n,a_{n-1}$ such that

$$S_n(\alpha)=na_n,
\qquad
S_{n-1}(\alpha)=(n-1)a_{n-1}.$$

Subtracting gives

$$\lfloor n\alpha\rfloor
=
na_n-(n-1)a_{n-1}.$$

Reducing modulo $n$ yields

$$\lfloor n\alpha\rfloor\equiv a_{n-1}\pmod n.$$

Thus the residue class of $\lfloor n\alpha\rfloor$ modulo $n$ is tightly constrained by earlier terms.

The key insight is that the hypothesis forces

$$\lfloor n\alpha\rfloor=n\lfloor\alpha\rfloor
\qquad
\text{for every }n\in\mathbb{N},$$

because the difference between these two integers is bounded.

Write

$$\alpha=m+\beta,$$

where $m=\lfloor\alpha\rfloor\in\mathbb{Z}$ and $0\le \beta<1$. Then

$$\lfloor n\alpha\rfloor
=
nm+\lfloor n\beta\rfloor .$$

Hence the desired identity is equivalent to

$$\lfloor n\beta\rfloor=0
\qquad
\text{for all }n.$$

If $\beta>0$, choose an integer $n>\frac1\beta$. Then $n\beta>1$, so $\lfloor n\beta\rfloor\ge1$, contradiction. Consequently $\beta=0$, and therefore $\alpha$ is an integer.

The key insight is that once $\alpha$ is known to be an integer, checking divisibility reduces to a parity condition.

If $\alpha=t\in\mathbb{Z}$, then

$$S_n(t)=t\sum_{k=1}^{n}k=t\cdot\frac{n(n+1)}2.$$

Dividing by $n$ gives

$$\frac{S_n(t)}n=t\cdot\frac{n+1}2.$$

This must be an integer for every $n$. Taking $n=2$ yields

$$\frac{S_2(t)}2=\frac{3t}{2}.$$

Hence $t$ must be even.

Conversely, if $t=2m$, then

$$S_n(2m)=2m\cdot\frac{n(n+1)}2=mn(n+1),$$

which is divisible by $n$ for every positive integer $n$.

## Solution

Let

$$S_n=\sum_{k=1}^{n}\lfloor k\alpha\rfloor .$$

We seek all real numbers $\alpha$ such that

$$n\mid S_n
\qquad
\text{for every }n\in\mathbb{N}.$$

We first prove that $\alpha$ must be an integer.

### Lemma 1

For every positive integer $n$,

$$\lfloor n\alpha\rfloor\equiv \lfloor\alpha\rfloor \pmod n.$$

#### Proof

Since the hypothesis states that $n\mid S_n$, there exists an integer $a_n$ such that

$$S_n=na_n.$$

Likewise, there exists an integer $a_{n-1}$ such that

$$S_{n-1}=(n-1)a_{n-1}.$$

Because

$$S_n-S_{n-1}=\lfloor n\alpha\rfloor,$$

we obtain

$$\lfloor n\alpha\rfloor
=
na_n-(n-1)a_{n-1}.$$

Reducing modulo $n$ gives

$$\lfloor n\alpha\rfloor\equiv a_{n-1}\pmod n.$$

Now consider the case $n=1$. Since

$$S_1=\lfloor\alpha\rfloor,$$

we have

$$a_1=\lfloor\alpha\rfloor.$$

Applying the previous congruence with $n=2$ gives

$$\lfloor2\alpha\rfloor\equiv \lfloor\alpha\rfloor\pmod2.$$

Applying it with general $n$ and repeatedly tracing backward yields

$$a_{n-1}\equiv a_{n-2}\equiv\cdots\equiv a_1=\lfloor\alpha\rfloor \pmod n.$$

Hence

$$\lfloor n\alpha\rfloor\equiv \lfloor\alpha\rfloor\pmod n.$$

This proves the lemma. ∎

### Lemma 2

The number $\alpha$ is an integer.

#### Proof

Write

$$\alpha=m+\beta,$$

where

$$m=\lfloor\alpha\rfloor\in\mathbb{Z},
\qquad
0\le\beta<1.$$

Then

$$\lfloor n\alpha\rfloor
=
\lfloor nm+n\beta\rfloor
=
nm+\lfloor n\beta\rfloor .$$

By Lemma 1,

$$nm+\lfloor n\beta\rfloor\equiv m\pmod n.$$

Since $nm\equiv0\pmod n$, this simplifies to

$$\lfloor n\beta\rfloor\equiv m\pmod n.$$

Because $0\le\beta<1$, we have

$$0\le n\beta<n,$$

and therefore

$$0\le \lfloor n\beta\rfloor\le n-1.$$

Thus the congruence

$$\lfloor n\beta\rfloor\equiv m\pmod n$$

determines $\lfloor n\beta\rfloor$ uniquely as the least nonnegative residue of $m$ modulo $n$.

On the other hand, taking $n=1$ gives

$$\lfloor\beta\rfloor=0.$$

Hence the least nonnegative residue of $m$ modulo $1$ is $0$, and consistency for all $n$ forces

$$\lfloor n\beta\rfloor=0
\qquad
\text{for every }n.$$

Suppose $\beta>0$. Choose a positive integer $n$ satisfying

$$n>\frac1\beta.$$

Then $n\beta>1$, so

$$\lfloor n\beta\rfloor\ge1,$$

contradicting $\lfloor n\beta\rfloor=0$.

Hence $\beta=0$, and therefore

$$\alpha=m\in\mathbb{Z}.$$

This proves the lemma. ∎

We now classify the integers that satisfy the condition.

### Lemma 3

An integer $t$ satisfies the divisibility condition for all $n$ if and only if $t$ is even.

#### Proof

Assume first that $t$ satisfies the condition. Then

$$\lfloor kt\rfloor=kt$$

for every positive integer $k$, and therefore

$$S_n=t(1+2+\cdots+n)
=t\cdot\frac{n(n+1)}2.$$

Since $n\mid S_n$, we obtain

$$n\mid t\cdot\frac{n(n+1)}2.$$

Because $n$ divides the product $n(n+1)$, this is equivalent to

$$\frac{t(n+1)}2\in\mathbb{Z}
\qquad
\text{for every }n.$$

Taking $n=2$ yields

$$\frac{3t}{2}\in\mathbb{Z}.$$

Thus $t$ is even.

Conversely, suppose $t=2m$ for some integer $m$. Then

$$S_n
=
\sum_{k=1}^{n}2mk
=
2m\cdot\frac{n(n+1)}2
=
mn(n+1).$$

Since $mn(n+1)$ contains a factor $n$, we have

$$n\mid S_n
\qquad
\text{for every }n.$$

Thus every even integer works. ∎

Combining Lemma 2 and Lemma 3, we conclude that the required real numbers are precisely the even integers.

$$\boxed{\alpha\in2\mathbb{Z}}$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The most delicate point is the transition from congruence information to the conclusion that the fractional part of $\alpha$ vanishes.

Write

$$\alpha=m+\beta,
\qquad
m\in\mathbb{Z},
\qquad
0\le\beta<1.$$

Then

$$\lfloor n\alpha\rfloor=nm+\lfloor n\beta\rfloor .$$

The quantity $\lfloor n\beta\rfloor$ always lies in the interval $[0,n-1]$. If $\beta>0$, then for sufficiently large $n$ we have $n\beta>1$, hence $\lfloor n\beta\rfloor\ge1$. This contradicts the congruence condition forcing $\lfloor n\beta\rfloor=0$. The common mistake is to assume merely that $\lfloor n\beta\rfloor<n$, which does not rule out positive values.

Another delicate point is the parity analysis for integer solutions. Suppose $\alpha=t\in\mathbb{Z}$. Then

$$S_n=t\cdot\frac{n(n+1)}2.$$

A careless argument might say that $n$ divides this expression because the numerator contains a factor $n$. After dividing by $n$, however, the quotient is

$$\frac{S_n}{n}=t\cdot\frac{n+1}{2},$$

and this must be an integer for every $n$. Taking $n=2$ gives

$$\frac{S_2}{2}=\frac{3t}{2}.$$

If $t$ is odd, this is not an integer. For example, with $t=1$,

$$S_2=\lfloor1\rfloor+\lfloor2\rfloor=3,$$

and $3$ is not divisible by $2$. This computation prevents the incorrect conclusion that all integers work.

A third delicate point concerns the necessity of checking small values of $n$. For $\alpha=2$,

$$S_1=2,
\qquad
S_2=2+4=6,
\qquad
S_3=2+4+6=12,$$

and each is divisible by the corresponding $n$. For $\alpha=1$,

$$S_1=1,
\qquad
S_2=1+2=3,$$

and divisibility already fails at $n=2$. These explicit checks confirm the parity classification before the general proof is completed.

## Alternative Approaches

A different approach begins by writing

$$\lfloor x\rfloor=x-\{x\},$$

where ${x}$ denotes the fractional part of $x$. Then

$$S_n
=
\alpha\frac{n(n+1)}2-\sum_{k=1}^{n}\{k\alpha\}.$$

Reducing modulo $n$ gives a congruence involving the fractional parts ${k\alpha}$. If $\alpha$ is irrational, the sequence ${k\alpha}$ is dense in $[0,1)$, so the sum of fractional parts cannot maintain the required congruence structure for every $n$. If $\alpha$ is rational but nonintegral, periodicity of the fractional parts produces explicit contradictions for suitable $n$. The remaining integer case reduces to parity.

This method uses more advanced information about fractional parts and distribution modulo $1$. The main proof is preferable because it avoids any external theorem and derives the rigidity directly from the divisibility condition. The argument is shorter, fully elementary, and more transparent about why the floor terms collapse to linear behavior.
