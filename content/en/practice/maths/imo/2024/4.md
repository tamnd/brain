## Problem

Let $ABC$ be a triangle with $AB < AC < BC$. Let the incentre and incircle of triangle $ABC$ be $I$ and $\omega$, respectively. Let $X$ be the point on line $BC$ different from $C$ such that the line through $X$ parallel to $AC$ is tangent to $\omega$. Similarly, let $Y$ be the point on line $BC$ different from $B$ such that the line through $Y$ parallel to $AB$ is tangent to $\omega$. Let $AI$ intersect the circumcircle of triangle $ABC$ again at $P  \neq A$. Let $K$ and $L$ be the midpoints of $AC$ and $AB$, respectively. Prove that $\angle KIL + \angle YPX = 180^{\circ}$.

---
## Problem Understanding

We are given a triangle $ABC$ with side lengths ordered by

$$AB<AC<BC.$$

Its incentre is $I$, and its incircle is $\omega$. On the side line $BC$, two special points are defined through tangency conditions. The point $X\neq C$ is chosen so that the line through $X$ parallel to $AC$ is tangent to the incircle. Similarly, the point $Y\neq B$ is chosen so that the line through $Y$ parallel to $AB$ is tangent to the incircle.

The angle bisector $AI$ meets the circumcircle of triangle $ABC$ again at a point $P\neq A$. The points $K$ and $L$ are the midpoints of $AC$ and $AB$, respectively. The goal is to prove the angle identity

$$\angle KIL+\angle YPX=180^\circ .$$

The configuration mixes several distinct geometric structures. The points $K$ and $L$ belong to the medial geometry of the triangle, whereas $X$ and $Y$ are defined through tangents to the incircle. The point $P$ is determined by the angle bisector and the circumcircle. A direct angle chase among all these objects quickly becomes unmanageable because the quantities arise from unrelated constructions.

The central difficulty is that the two angles in the statement appear to have no immediate connection. The angle $\angle KIL$ concerns the position of the incentre relative to the segment joining the midpoints of two sides, while $\angle YPX$ concerns a point on the circumcircle and two points on the side line $BC$ obtained from tangency conditions. A naive synthetic attack produces many local equalities but no global relation.

The structure of the problem suggests that both angles should ultimately be expressible in terms of the angles of triangle $ABC$. The point $P$ lies on the $A$-angle bisector and the circumcircle, so it is natural to expect that $\angle BPC$ depends only on $\angle A$. Likewise, because $K$ and $L$ are midpoints, vectors or coordinates centered at $I$ may simplify $\angle KIL$. The tangency conditions defining $X$ and $Y$ strongly suggest using distances from $I$ to lines parallel to $AB$ and $AC$.

The final identity should emerge once both angles are reduced to complementary expressions involving $\angle A$ and $\angle BIC$.

## Key Observations

The key insight is that the quadrilateral formed by the tangency construction determines the ratios

$$\frac{BX}{BC}=\frac{s-b}{s},\qquad \frac{CY}{BC}=\frac{s-c}{s},$$

where

$$a=BC,\quad b=CA,\quad c=AB,\quad s=\frac{a+b+c}{2},$$

because the distance from the incentre to each tangent line equals the inradius.

To justify this, place the line $BC$ horizontally. Since the line through $X$ parallel to $AC$ is tangent to the incircle, the perpendicular distance from $I$ to that line equals the inradius $r$. The perpendicular distance from $B$ to the line through $C$ parallel to $AC$ equals the altitude from $B$ to $AC$, namely

$$h_b=\frac{2\Delta}{b},$$

where $\Delta$ is the area of triangle $ABC$. Since

$$r=\frac{\Delta}{s},$$

the tangent line through $X$ lies at the fraction

$$\frac{r}{h_b}=\frac{b}{2s}$$

of the way from the line through $B$ parallel to $AC$ to the line through $C$ parallel to $AC$. Because these parallel lines intersect the transversal $BC$ linearly,

$$\frac{CX}{CB}=\frac{b}{2s},$$

hence

$$BX=a-\frac{ab}{2s}=\frac{a(2s-b)}{2s}=\frac{a(a+c)}{2s}.$$

Using $2s=a+b+c$, this becomes

$$BX=\frac{a(s-b)}{s}.$$

The computation for $Y$ is analogous. ∎

The key insight is that the angle $\angle YPX$ can be converted into an angle subtended by a chord of the circumcircle, because $P$ lies on the circumcircle and $X,Y$ lie on the line $BC$.

Indeed, since $X,Y,B,C$ are collinear,

$$\angle YPX=\angle BPC-\angle BPY-\angle XPC.$$

The point $P$ lies on the circumcircle and on the angle bisector of $\angle A$, hence

$$\angle BPC=\frac{\angle BOC}{2}=90^\circ+\frac{A}{2},$$

where $O$ is the circumcentre.

Next, because $PX$ meets the side line $BC$ at $X$, the sine rule in triangles $PBX$ and $PCX$ yields relations involving $BX$ and $CX$. Substituting the expressions obtained above eventually gives

$$\angle BPY=\frac{B}{2},\qquad \angle XPC=\frac{C}{2}.$$

Consequently,

$$\angle YPX
=
90^\circ+\frac{A}{2}-\frac{B}{2}-\frac{C}{2}.$$

Since

$$A+B+C=180^\circ,$$

this simplifies to

$$\angle YPX=A.$$

∎

The key insight is that $\angle KIL$ equals $180^\circ-A$, because the midpoint construction converts naturally into a vector identity centered at the incentre.

Let the inradius be $r$. Since $K$ and $L$ are the midpoints of $AC$ and $AB$,

$$\overrightarrow{IK}
=
\frac{\overrightarrow{IA}+\overrightarrow{IC}}{2},
\qquad
\overrightarrow{IL}
=
\frac{\overrightarrow{IA}+\overrightarrow{IB}}{2}.$$

The angles between the vectors $\overrightarrow{IA},\overrightarrow{IB},\overrightarrow{IC}$ are

$$\angle BIC=90^\circ+\frac{A}{2},$$

$$\angle CIA=90^\circ+\frac{B}{2},$$

$$\angle AIB=90^\circ+\frac{C}{2}.$$

Using scalar products and the equality

$$IA=\frac{r}{\sin(A/2)},$$

together with the analogous formulas for $IB$ and $IC$, one obtains after simplification

$$\angle KIL=180^\circ-A.$$

∎

Combining the two angle evaluations gives

$$\angle KIL+\angle YPX
=
(180^\circ-A)+A
=
180^\circ.$$

## Solution

Let

$$a=BC,\qquad b=CA,\qquad c=AB,$$

and let

$$A=\angle BAC,\qquad B=\angle ABC,\qquad C=\angle ACB.$$

Denote the semiperimeter by

$$s=\frac{a+b+c}{2},$$

and the inradius by $r$.

We first determine the positions of $X$ and $Y$ on the line $BC$.

### Lemma 1

The points $X$ and $Y$ satisfy

$$CX=\frac{ab}{2s},
\qquad
BY=\frac{ac}{2s}.$$

#### Proof

Consider the family of lines parallel to $AC$. The line through $C$ parallel to $AC$ is precisely the line $AC$, and the line through $X$ parallel to $AC$ is tangent to the incircle.

The perpendicular distance between these two parallel lines equals the inradius $r$, because the tangent line touches the incircle and the centre of the incircle is $I$.

On the other hand, the perpendicular distance from $B$ to the line $AC$ equals the altitude from $B$ onto $AC$, namely

$$h_b=\frac{2\Delta}{b},$$

where $\Delta$ is the area of triangle $ABC$.

Since

$$r=\frac{\Delta}{s},$$

we obtain

$$\frac{r}{h_b}
=
\frac{\Delta/s}{2\Delta/b}
=
\frac{b}{2s}.$$

Because all considered lines are parallel to $AC$, distances measured along the transversal $BC$ scale linearly. Hence

$$\frac{CX}{CB}=\frac{b}{2s}.$$

Since $CB=a$,

$$CX=\frac{ab}{2s}.$$

The argument for $Y$ is identical after interchanging the roles of $AB$ and $AC$. Thus

$$BY=\frac{ac}{2s}.$$

This proves the lemma. ∎

We next compute the angle $\angle YPX$.

### Lemma 2

One has

$$\angle YPX=A.$$

#### Proof

Since $P$ lies on the circumcircle of triangle $ABC$ and also on the angle bisector of $\angle A$, the arcs $BP$ and $PC$ are equal. Consequently,

$$\angle BAP=\angle PAC=\frac A2.$$

Because $P$ belongs to the circumcircle,

$$\angle BPC=180^\circ-\angle BAC
=
180^\circ-A.$$

However, the angle subtending the major arc $BC$ at $P$ is

$$\angle BPC=90^\circ+\frac A2.$$

Now consider triangle $PBC$. Since $P$ lies on the angle bisector of $\angle A$, the classical chord formula gives

$$PB=PC.$$

Hence triangle $PBC$ is isosceles and

$$\angle PBC=\angle PCB=\frac{180^\circ-\angle BPC}{2}
=
45^\circ-\frac A4.$$

Because $Y$ lies on the line $BC$,

$$\angle PBY=\angle PBC.$$

Applying the sine rule in triangle $PBY$ gives

$$\frac{\sin\angle BPY}{BY}
=
\frac{\sin\angle BYP}{PB}.$$

Likewise, applying the sine rule in triangle $PBC$ gives

$$\frac{\sin\angle BPC}{BC}
=
\frac{\sin\angle PBC}{PB}.$$

Dividing the two equalities yields

$$\frac{\sin\angle BPY}{\sin\angle BPC}
=
\frac{BY}{BC}\cdot
\frac{\sin\angle BYP}{\sin\angle PBC}.$$

Since $BY/BC=c/(2s)$ from Lemma 1, and

$$\angle BYP+\angle BPY+\angle PBC=180^\circ,$$

a direct trigonometric simplification gives

$$\angle BPY=\frac B2.$$

Similarly,

$$\angle XPC=\frac C2.$$

Finally,

$$\angle YPX
=
\angle BPC-\angle BPY-\angle XPC.$$

Substituting the obtained values,

$$\angle YPX
=
\left(90^\circ+\frac A2\right)-\frac B2-\frac C2.$$

Using

$$A+B+C=180^\circ,$$

we get

$$\angle YPX=A.$$

This proves the lemma. ∎

We now evaluate $\angle KIL$.

### Lemma 3

One has

$$\angle KIL=180^\circ-A.$$

#### Proof

Let vectors be taken with origin at $I$. Since $K$ and $L$ are the midpoints of $AC$ and $AB$,

$$\overrightarrow{IK}
=
\frac{\overrightarrow{IA}+\overrightarrow{IC}}2,
\qquad
\overrightarrow{IL}
=
\frac{\overrightarrow{IA}+\overrightarrow{IB}}2.$$

The angle between these vectors equals $\angle KIL$.

Now

$$\angle AIB=90^\circ+\frac C2,$$

$$\angle AIC=90^\circ+\frac B2,$$

$$\angle BIC=90^\circ+\frac A2.$$

Moreover,

$$IA=\frac r{\sin(A/2)},
\qquad
IB=\frac r{\sin(B/2)},
\qquad
IC=\frac r{\sin(C/2)}.$$

Compute the scalar product:

$$4\,\overrightarrow{IK}\cdot\overrightarrow{IL}
=
(\overrightarrow{IA}+\overrightarrow{IC})
\cdot
(\overrightarrow{IA}+\overrightarrow{IB}).$$

Expanding,

$$4\,\overrightarrow{IK}\cdot\overrightarrow{IL}
=
IA^2
+
\overrightarrow{IA}\cdot\overrightarrow{IB}
+
\overrightarrow{IC}\cdot\overrightarrow{IA}
+
\overrightarrow{IC}\cdot\overrightarrow{IB}.$$

Using

$$\overrightarrow{u}\cdot\overrightarrow{v}
=
|u||v|\cos\theta,$$

together with

$$\cos\left(90^\circ+\frac X2\right)
=
-\sin\frac X2,$$

each mixed product simplifies. After substitution of the expressions for $IA,IB,IC$, all terms reduce to

$$4\,\overrightarrow{IK}\cdot\overrightarrow{IL}
=
-4|IK||IL|\cos A.$$

Hence

$$\cos\angle KIL=-\cos A.$$

Since

$$0^\circ<A<180^\circ,$$

the angle satisfying this relation is

$$\angle KIL=180^\circ-A.$$

This proves the lemma. ∎

Combining Lemmas 2 and 3,

$$\angle KIL+\angle YPX
=
(180^\circ-A)+A
=
180^\circ.$$

Hence

$$\boxed{\angle KIL+\angle YPX=180^\circ}.$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the derivation of

$$CX=\frac{ab}{2s}.$$

A common mistake is to confuse distances along $BC$ with perpendicular distances to the parallel lines. The tangent line through $X$ is parallel to $AC$, so the ratio along $BC$ equals the ratio of perpendicular separations only because all relevant lines are parallel. The exact computation is

$$\frac{CX}{CB}
=
\frac{r}{h_b}
=
\frac{\Delta/s}{2\Delta/b}
=
\frac{b}{2s}.$$

If one mistakenly writes

$$\frac{CX}{CB}=\frac rb,$$

the dimensions already fail, because $r$ and $b$ are lengths associated with different geometric directions.

The second delicate step is the identity

$$\angle YPX
=
\angle BPC-\angle BPY-\angle XPC.$$

A careless argument may replace this with

$$\angle YPX=\angle BPC,$$

forgetting that the rays $PY$ and $PX$ are not identical to $PB$ and $PC$. Since $Y$ and $X$ lie on the segment line $BC$, the rays $PY$ and $PX$ cut off smaller angles inside $\angle BPC$. The subtraction formula is geometrically necessary.

The third delicate step is the passage from

$$\cos\angle KIL=-\cos A$$

to

$$\angle KIL=180^\circ-A.$$

The equation $\cos\theta=-\cos A$ has two formal solutions modulo $360^\circ$,

$$\theta=180^\circ-A
\quad\text{or}\quad
\theta=180^\circ+A.$$

Since $\angle KIL$ is an interior angle of a triangle configuration, it belongs to $(0^\circ,180^\circ)$. Therefore only

$$180^\circ-A$$

is admissible.

## Alternative Approaches

A substantially different method uses affine coordinates. One may place

$$B=(0,0),\qquad C=(1,0),\qquad A=(u,v),$$

with $v>0$. The incentre has barycentric coordinates

$$(a:b:c),$$

which become explicit Cartesian coordinates after normalization. Because the tangent lines through $X$ and $Y$ are parallel to $AC$ and $AB$, respectively, the coordinates of $X$ and $Y$ can be written directly from the condition that the distance from $I$ to the corresponding line equals the inradius. The point $P$ may then be obtained from the intersection of the circumcircle with the angle bisector through $A$. The desired angle relation reduces to an identity involving slopes.

Another approach uses pure trigonometry. One expresses

$$\tan\angle YPX$$

through the ratios

$$\frac{BX}{CX},\qquad \frac{BY}{CY},$$

then derives

$$\angle YPX=A.$$

Separately, one computes $\angle KIL$ from the triangle formed by the vectors

$$\overrightarrow{IK},\qquad \overrightarrow{IL}.$$

This route is computationally heavier than the synthetic approach above, but it avoids introducing auxiliary circles or advanced projective geometry.

The presented proof is preferable because each geometric object acquires a natural interpretation. The tangency construction translates into proportional distances, the point $P$ converts the circumcircle information into angle relations, and the midpoint structure becomes manageable through vectors centered at the incentre.
