## Problem

Turbo the snail plays a game on a board with 2024 rows and 2023 columns. There are hidden monsters in 2022 of the cells. Initially, Turbo does not know where any of the monsters are, but he knows that there is exactly one monster in each row except the first row and the last row, and that each column contains at most one monster.

Turbo makes a series of attempts to go from the first row to the last row. On each attempt, he chooses to start on any cell in the first row, then repeatedly moves to an adjacent cell sharing a common side. (He is allowed to return to a previously visited cell.) If he reaches a cell with a monster, his attempt ends and he is transported back to the first row to start a new attempt. The monsters do not move, and Turbo remembers whether or not each cell he has visited contains a monster. If he reaches any cell in the last row, his attempt ends and the game is over.

Determine the minimum value of $n$ for which Turbo has a strategy that guarantees reaching the last row on the $n^{th}$ attempt or earlier, regardless of the locations of the monsters.

## Problem Understanding

Turbo the snail must travel on a rectangular grid with $2024$ rows and $2023$ columns. Hidden monsters occupy exactly $2022$ cells. Every row except the first and the last contains exactly one monster, and every column contains at most one monster. Thus each of the rows $2,3,\dots,2023$ contains one monster, and exactly one column contains no monster at all.

Turbo repeatedly attempts to move from the first row to the last row. During an attempt he may move between edge-adjacent cells arbitrarily, including revisiting cells. If he ever enters a monster cell, the attempt ends immediately. The locations of discovered monsters remain known forever. Turbo succeeds once he reaches any cell in the last row.

The problem asks for the smallest integer $n$ such that Turbo can guarantee success by the $n$th attempt, regardless of the monster configuration.

The difficulty lies in the fact that Turbo initially knows nothing about the monster positions. A naive strategy such as choosing a column and moving straight downward fails badly, because a monster may appear somewhere in that column and terminate the attempt before useful information has been gathered. Since the monsters are arranged adversarially, the strategy must guarantee success for every possible placement.

The structural constraints are decisive. Since there are $2023$ columns and only $2022$ monsters, there exists exactly one column containing no monster. If Turbo knew that column, he could descend safely in a single attempt. The entire problem becomes a question of how many failed attempts are required to identify a safe column with certainty.

The answer is $2023$. Intuitively, Turbo may be forced to eliminate safe possibilities one by one. Each failed attempt can reveal at most one new monster column in a decisive way, and there are initially $2023$ candidate columns. The main task is to prove both that $2023$ attempts always suffice and that no strategy can guarantee success earlier.

## Key Observations

The key insight is that every failed attempt identifies at least one column that contains a monster, because the attempt ends only when Turbo enters a monster cell. Since each column contains at most one monster, once Turbo discovers a monster in a column, that entire column is permanently known to be unsafe. No additional monsters can occur there.

The key insight is that there exists exactly one safe column, because there are $2023$ columns and $2022$ monsters, with at most one monster in each column. Hence $2022$ columns contain one monster each, and the remaining column contains none.

The key insight is that if Turbo ever knows the safe column, then he can reach the last row immediately, because moving straight downward within that column never encounters a monster. Indeed, every monster lies in a different column, so the safe column contains no monster in any row.

The key insight is that an adversary can force Turbo to spend one failed attempt per unsafe column. Suppose Turbo has not yet ruled out several columns. During any attempt that does not already guarantee success, the adversary may place the encountered monster in a previously unconfirmed column while preserving all previous information and all constraints of the problem. Consequently Turbo cannot certify the safe column until all other columns have been shown unsafe.

These observations suggest a matching upper and lower bound of $2023$ attempts.

## Solution

We prove that the minimum number of attempts required is $2023$.

### Upper bound

We first describe a strategy that guarantees success within $2023$ attempts.

For each column $c$, let Turbo perform an attempt in which he starts at the cell in the first row of column $c$ and then moves straight downward through that column until either he encounters a monster or he reaches the last row.

We analyze this strategy carefully.

Since every column contains at most one monster, if column $c$ contains a monster, then during the attempt in column $c$ Turbo eventually reaches that monster and the attempt ends. Since there are exactly $2022$ monsters distributed among distinct columns, exactly $2022$ columns are unsafe and exactly one column is safe.

Whenever Turbo tests an unsafe column, the attempt fails and he learns that this column contains a monster. Hence after failing in $k$ distinct columns, he knows that those $k$ columns are unsafe.

After at most $2022$ failed attempts, Turbo has identified all unsafe columns. The remaining column contains no monster. On the next attempt he traverses that column from the first row to the last row without encountering a monster.

Thus Turbo always succeeds by the $2023$rd attempt.

Hence the desired minimum value satisfies

$$n \le 2023.$$

### Lower bound

We now prove that no strategy can guarantee success in fewer than $2023$ attempts.

Assume that Turbo follows an arbitrary strategy. We construct an adversarial placement process showing that Turbo may still fail during the first $2022$ attempts.

The adversary does not fix all monster positions initially. Instead, after observing Turbo's motion during each failed attempt, the adversary chooses where the encountered monster was located, while maintaining consistency with all previous information and with the rules of the problem.

We proceed inductively.

Suppose that before some attempt, fewer than $2022$ monster columns have been identified. Then at least two columns remain possible candidates for the safe column.

During the new attempt, Turbo moves through the grid according to his strategy. Since at least two columns remain unconfirmed, Turbo cannot restrict himself entirely to one already certified safe column, because no such column has yet been determined.

Consider the first time during this attempt that Turbo enters a cell in a column that has not previously been identified as unsafe. Denote this column by $c$.

The adversary now declares that this cell contains a monster. This immediately ends the attempt.

We must verify that this declaration is always consistent with the rules.

First, column $c$ was not previously known to contain a monster, so assigning a monster to column $c$ does not violate the condition that each column contains at most one monster.

Second, the chosen cell lies in some row other than the first row, because monsters never occur in the first row and Turbo began there. If the chosen row has not yet been assigned a monster previously, assigning a monster there preserves the condition that each row from $2$ through $2023$ contains exactly one monster.

Third, because fewer than $2022$ monster columns had previously been identified, there remains at least one unused monster row and at least one unused monster column, so the process can continue.

After this failed attempt, Turbo has learned only that column $c$ is unsafe. No safe column has yet been identified uniquely.

We now formalize the induction.

#### Claim 1

After any integer $k$ with $0 \le k \le 2022$, the adversary can arrange matters so that exactly $k$ columns have been identified as unsafe and Turbo has not yet reached the last row.

#### Proof

We use induction on $k$.

For $k=0$, no attempt has occurred yet, so the statement holds.

Assume the statement holds for some $k$ with $0 \le k \le 2021$. Thus exactly $k$ columns are known unsafe before the next attempt.

There are then $2023-k \ge 2$ columns not yet identified as unsafe. Turbo performs his next attempt according to his strategy. Let $c$ be the first previously unconfirmed column entered during this attempt. The adversary places a monster at that cell, terminating the attempt immediately.

The new monster occupies a previously unused column. Also, because only $k$ monster rows had been assigned previously, at least one eligible row remains available for the new monster. Hence all conditions remain satisfied.

After the attempt, exactly $k+1$ columns are known unsafe, and Turbo still has not reached the last row.

This completes the induction. ∎

Claim 1 with $k=2022$ shows that Turbo can be forced to fail during the first $2022$ attempts. Therefore no strategy guarantees success by attempt $2022$.

Hence

$$n \ge 2023.$$

Combining the upper and lower bounds gives

$$n=2023.$$

Therefore the minimum value is

$$\boxed{2023}.$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The most delicate point in the lower bound argument is the adversarial placement of monsters during the game. The adversary must remain consistent with a single valid board configuration.

Suppose after $k$ failed attempts, monsters have been assigned in exactly $k$ distinct rows and $k$ distinct columns. Since the rows $2,3,\dots,2023$ provide $2022$ available monster rows, when $k\le 2021$ there exists at least one unused row. Similarly, at least one unused column remains. Hence assigning one additional monster never violates the conditions. A careless argument might forget that rows also have restrictions, not only columns.

Another delicate step concerns the existence of exactly one safe column. There are $2023$ columns and $2022$ monsters. Because each column contains at most one monster, the monsters occupy $2022$ distinct columns. Thus precisely one column is unoccupied by monsters. A common mistake is to conclude merely that at least one safe column exists. The uniqueness is essential for the lower bound, because Turbo must eliminate all other columns before certainty is possible.

A further subtlety is why a straight descent through a safe column succeeds. The condition says every monster lies in a distinct column and in a row between $2$ and $2023$. If a column contains no monster, then every cell in that column is safe. Hence moving vertically through that column reaches row $2024$. A careless argument might assume that discovering one safe cell in a column implies the whole column is safe, which is false in general. The proof uses the stronger fact that the entire column contains no monster.

## Alternative Approaches

Another proof of the lower bound can be phrased using information theory. Initially, each of the $2023$ columns could be the unique safe column. Every failed attempt can eliminate at most one candidate, namely the column in which the encountered monster lies. Consequently after $2022$ failures at least one candidate remains unresolved, so success cannot yet be guaranteed. This argument is shorter, but it conceals the issue of consistency between different failed attempts. The adversarial construction used in the main proof makes that consistency explicit.

One may also formulate the problem graph-theoretically. The board is a grid graph, and each monster deletes one vertex. Since all monster vertices lie in different columns, the unique intact vertical column forms a complete path from the top row to the bottom row. The strategy of testing columns sequentially then becomes a search for the unique intact vertical path. This viewpoint gives useful intuition, but the direct combinatorial proof remains more transparent and closer to the structure of the problem.
