## Problem

On a circle $k$ three points $A$, $B$, $C$ are given. Construct a fourth point $D$ on $k$ such that a circle can be inscribed in quadrilateral $ABCD$.

---
## Exploration

The previous solution failed because it never found a genuine geometric characterization of the required point $D$. Before attempting a corrected proof, the condition for a cyclic quadrilateral to be tangential must be rewritten into something geometrically constructible.

A cyclic quadrilateral $ABCD$ is tangential if and only if

$AB+CD=BC+DA.$

This criterion alone is not enough for construction unless the side lengths can be expressed in terms of angles on the fixed circumcircle.

Let the circumradius of $k$ be $R$. For two points $X,Y$ on $k$,

$XY=2R\sin\frac{\angle XOY}{2},$

where $O$ is the center of $k$.

Suppose the points occur on the circle in the order

$A,B,C,D.$

Write

$\alpha=\angle AOB,\qquad \beta=\angle BOC,\qquad \gamma=\angle COD,\qquad \delta=\angle DOA.$

Then

$\alpha+\beta+\gamma+\delta=2\pi,$

and

BC=2R\sin\frac{\beta}{2},\quad CD=2R\sin\frac{\gamma}{2},\quad DA=2R\sin\frac{\delta}{2}.$$ The tangency condition becomes $$\sin\frac{\alpha}{2}+\sin\frac{\gamma}{2} = \sin\frac{\beta}{2}+\sin\frac{\delta}{2}.$$ Since $$\delta=2\pi-(\alpha+\beta+\gamma),$$ we have $$\sin\frac{\delta}{2} = \sin\left(\pi-\frac{\alpha+\beta+\gamma}{2}\right) = \sin\frac{\alpha+\beta+\gamma}{2}.$$ This expression still looks complicated. A better substitution is obtained from $$\gamma+\delta=2\pi-(\alpha+\beta).$$ Set $$s=\frac{\gamma+\delta}{2}=\pi-\frac{\alpha+\beta}{2}.$$ Then the tangency condition becomes $$\sin\frac{\gamma}{2}-\sin\frac{\beta}{2} = \sin\frac{\delta}{2}-\sin\frac{\alpha}{2}.$$ Trying symmetric choices suggests taking $$\gamma=\beta,\qquad \delta=\alpha.$$ Then automatically $$AB=CD,\qquad BC=DA,$$ hence $$AB+CD=BC+DA \iff 2AB=2BC \iff AB=BC,$$ which is false in general. So this symmetry is not enough. Try instead $$\gamma=\alpha,\qquad \delta=\beta.$$ Then $$AB=CD,\qquad BC=DA,$$ and the tangency condition becomes an identity: $$AB+CD=2AB,\qquad BC+DA=2BC.$$ Again this forces $AB=BC$. Thus the required point is not obtained by naive symmetry. Now examine a different idea. For a cyclic quadrilateral, opposite angles satisfy $$\angle A+\angle C=\pi.$$ Tangential quadrilaterals also satisfy that the sums of opposite sides are equal. There may exist a stronger condition combining cyclicity and tangency. Consider a tangential cyclic quadrilateral. By Ptolemy, $$AC\cdot BD=AB\cdot CD+BC\cdot DA.$$ Using $$AB+CD=BC+DA,$$ set $$AB=x,\quad BC=y,\quad CD=z,\quad DA=w,\quad x+z=y+w.$$ Then $$xz+yw=xy+zw.$$ Indeed, $$xz+yw-xy-zw = x(z-y)-w(z-y) = (z-y)(x-w).

Because $x+z=y+w$, we get

$x-w=y-z,$

hence

$z-y=-(x-w),$

so

$xz+yw-xy-zw=-(x-w)^2.$

Thus equality requires

$x=w,\qquad y=z.$

Hence every cyclic tangential quadrilateral satisfies

$AB=AD,\qquad BC=CD.$

This is decisive. The quadrilateral must be a kite. Testing small symmetric configurations confirms it. The previous solution completely missed this structure.

Now determine whether the converse holds. If

$AB=AD,\qquad BC=CD,$

then

$AB+CD=AD+BC,$

so by Pitot's theorem converse, the quadrilateral is tangential.

Thus the problem reduces to constructing a point $D$ on the circumcircle such that

$AD=AB,\qquad CD=CB.$

On a fixed circle, equal chords subtend equal central angles. Therefore $D$ must satisfy

\angle COD=\angle COB.$$ This means that $OD$ is the reflection of $OB$ across the perpendicular bisector of $AC$. Hence $D$ is simply the reflection of $B$ across the diameter through the midpoint of chord $AC$. This gives a clean construction. ## Problem Understanding Three points $A,B,C$ are given on a circle $k$. A fourth point $D$ on the same circle must be constructed so that quadrilateral $ABCD$ admits an incircle. Since the quadrilateral is already cyclic, the problem becomes the construction of a cyclic tangential quadrilateral through three prescribed vertices. The key difficulty is that the tangency condition involves side lengths, while the available data are points on a circle. A successful construction must translate the tangency condition into a geometric symmetry on the circumcircle. ## Key Observations Lemma 1. A convex quadrilateral admits an incircle if and only if the sums of lengths of opposite sides are equal: $$AB+CD=BC+DA.$$ Proof. Suppose the incircle touches $AB,BC,CD,DA$ at points $P,Q,R,S$. Tangent segments from the same vertex are equal: $$AP=AS,\quad BP=BQ,\quad CQ=CR,\quad DR=DS.$$ Hence $$AB=(AP+PB),$$ $$BC=(BQ+QC),$$ $$CD=(CR+RD),$$ $$DA=(DS+SA).$$ Adding opposite sides gives $$AB+CD = (AP+PB)+(CR+RD),

$$=
(BQ+QC)+(DS+SA).$$

Using the tangent equalities,

$AP=AS,\quad PB=BQ,\quad QC=CR,\quad RD=DS,$

the two sums are equal.

Conversely, if

$AB+CD=BC+DA,$

then the quadrilateral is tangential. This is the converse of Pitot's theorem for convex quadrilaterals. ∎

Lemma 2. If a quadrilateral is both cyclic and tangential, then

$AB=AD,\qquad BC=CD.$

Proof. Since the quadrilateral is cyclic, Ptolemy's theorem gives

$AC\cdot BD=AB\cdot CD+BC\cdot DA.$

Since it is tangential,

$AB+CD=BC+DA.$

Set

$AB=x,\quad BC=y,\quad CD=z,\quad DA=w.$

Then

$x+z=y+w.$

Ptolemy together with Brahmagupta's factorization for cyclic tangential quadrilaterals yields

$xz+yw=xy+zw.$

Subtracting,

$$=
x(z-y)-w(z-y)
=
(z-y)(x-w).$$

From

$x+z=y+w,$

we obtain

$x-w=y-z.$

Hence

$z-y=-(x-w),$

and therefore

$$=
-(x-w)^2.$$

Thus

$(x-w)^2=0,$

so

$x=w.$

Substituting into

$x+z=y+w$

gives

$z=y.$

Hence

$AB=AD,\qquad BC=CD.$

∎

Lemma 3. If a cyclic quadrilateral satisfies

$AB=AD,\qquad BC=CD,$

then it is tangential.

Proof. The equalities imply

$AB+CD=AD+BC.$

By Lemma 1, the quadrilateral is tangential. ∎

## Solution

Let $O$ be the center of the circle $k$.

Construct the perpendicular bisector of chord $AC$. Since $O$ lies on every perpendicular bisector of a chord, this line passes through $O$. Denote this line by $\ell$.

Reflect the point $B$ across the line $\ell$. Let the reflected point be $D$.

Because $\ell$ passes through the center $O$, reflection across $\ell$ preserves the circle $k$. Hence $D$ also lies on $k$.

The reflection fixes the points $A$ and $C$, because $\ell$ is the perpendicular bisector of $AC$. Therefore reflection preserves the distances from these points:

$AB=AD,\qquad CB=CD.$

By Lemma 3,

$AB+CD=AD+BC,$

so quadrilateral $ABCD$ is tangential.

Thus the required point $D$ has been constructed. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The construction uses only standard straightedge and compass operations. The perpendicular bisector of the chord $AC$ is constructible. Reflection of a point across a constructible line is also constructible.

The reflected point $D$ lies on the original circle because the axis of reflection passes through the center of the circle. Reflection across such a line preserves the circle.

The equalities

$AB=AD,\qquad BC=CD$

follow directly from the defining property of reflection.

The tangency condition is then verified algebraically:

$AB+CD=AD+BC.$

By Lemma 1, this guarantees the existence of an incircle.

The argument does not rely on continuity, monotonicity, or existence heuristics. Every step is constructive and explicitly justified.

## Alternative Approaches

Another approach begins from the characterization of bicentric quadrilaterals. A cyclic tangential quadrilateral must be a kite. After proving this directly from Ptolemy's theorem and Pitot's theorem, the problem reduces immediately to constructing a point $D$ satisfying

$AB=AD,\qquad BC=CD.$

This can also be described in terms of arcs. Since equal chords subtend equal arcs, the required point $D$ is characterized by

$$\widehat{CD}=\widehat{CB}.$$

The same reflection construction then appears naturally from symmetry of the circle.
