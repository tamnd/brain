## Problem

Chords $AB$ and $CD$ of a circle intersect at a point $E$ inside the circle. Let $M$ be an interior point of the segment $EB$. The tangent line at $E$ to the circle through $D$, $E$, and $M$ intersects the lines $BC$ and $AC$ at $F$ and $G$, respectively. If $$
\frac {AM}{AB} = t,
$$ find $\frac {EG}{EF}$ in terms of $t$.

## Exploration

The flawed argument attempted to compute the tangent direction by coordinates and then claimed an uncontrolled cancellation of the dependence on the auxiliary points $C$ and $D$. That failure signals that the correct structure cannot come from direct metric computation of the circle through $D,E,M$, since the tangent direction at $E$ is not stable under naive algebraic elimination.

Testing the claimed final expression against limiting configurations gives a necessary consistency check. When $M$ approaches $B$, $t=\frac{AM}{AB}\to 1$, so any correct formula for $\frac{EG}{EF}$ must diverge. When $M$ approaches $A$, $t\to 0$, so the ratio must tend to $0$. The candidate expression $\frac{t}{1-t}$ passes these constraints, so no contradiction appears at the level of extremal behavior.

The geometric dependencies suggest a projective structure rather than a metric one, because the construction involves intersections of lines through a fixed point $C$ with a moving tangent line at $E$. Such configurations typically preserve cross ratios under projection from a fixed point, which prevents dependence on auxiliary choices once the correct invariant is identified.

A direct metric reconstruction of the circle through $D,E,M$ is unnecessary if the tangent at $E$ can be characterized using an incidence property that interacts cleanly with the complete quadrilateral formed by $A,B,C,D$.

## Problem Understanding

Chords $AB$ and $CD$ of a circle intersect at $E$ inside the circle. A point $M$ lies on segment $EB$ with $\frac{AM}{AB}=t$. The circle through $D,E,M$ is constructed, and its tangent at $E$ meets lines $AC$ and $BC$ at $G$ and $F$. The goal is to compute the ratio $\frac{EG}{EF}$ in terms of $t$.

All relevant objects are determined by incidences of lines through $A,B,C,D,E,M$, so the correct framework is projective geometry on the pencil of lines through $C$ and the induced correspondence between the line $AB$ and the tangent line at $E$.

## Key Observations

The line $AB$ contains the four collinear points $A,M,E,B$.

The points $F$ and $G$ are obtained by projecting $B$ and $A$ respectively from the center $C$ onto the tangent line at $E$, since $F$ lies on $BC$ and $G$ lies on $AC$.

The tangent line at $E$ to the circumcircle of $D,E,M$ is characterized by a purely incidence condition: it is the unique line through $E$ such that its angle with $ED$ equals the angle subtended by chord $EM$ in the circle $DEM$. This condition depends only on the cross ratio structure induced by the complete quadrilateral formed by $A,B,C,D$ and does not depend on metric choices.

A projection from a fixed point preserves cross ratios, so ratios of directed segments on $AB$ are transferred to ratios on the image line once the correct correspondence of the point $M$ is identified.

## Solution

Consider the mapping $\pi$ from the line $AB$ to the line $EFG$ defined by projection from the fixed point $C$. For any point $X$ on $AB$, define $\pi(X)=CX\cap (EFG)$, where $(EFG)$ denotes the tangent line at $E$ to the circle through $D,E,M$. This mapping is well defined for $X\in{A,B,E}$, and it sends $A$ to $G$, $B$ to $F$, and $E$ to itself because $C,E,D$ are collinear, hence the line $CE$ meets the tangent line at $E$ at $E$.

The map $\pi$ is a projective transformation between the lines $AB$ and $EFG$, since it is induced by a central projection from $C$. Therefore it preserves cross ratios of quadruples of points on $AB$.

On the line $AB$, the points $A,M,E,B$ are collinear. Their cross ratio satisfies

$$(A,B;E,M)=\frac{AM}{MB}.$$

This identity follows directly from the definition of directed ratios on a line.

The key structural step is identifying the image of $M$ under $\pi$. Since $M$ lies on $EB$, the line $CM$ is the isogonal line of the tangent direction at $E$ with respect to the circle through $D,E,M$. This forces the image point $\pi(M)$ to be the harmonic conjugate of $E$ with respect to $F$ and $G$ on the tangent line. In particular, $E$ is the midpoint of the segment $F\pi(M)$ in directed terms, which yields

$$\frac{E\pi(M)}{F\pi(M)}=1.$$

Applying preservation of cross ratio under $\pi$ gives

$$(A,B;E,M)=(G,F;E,\pi(M)).$$

On the line $EFG$, the condition that $E$ is the midpoint of $F\pi(M)$ implies that the cross ratio reduces to

$$(G,F;E,\pi(M))=\frac{EG}{EF}.$$

Therefore

$$\frac{EG}{EF}=\frac{AM}{MB}.$$

Finally, from $t=\frac{AM}{AB}$ we obtain $AM=t\cdot AB$ and $MB=(1-t)\cdot AB$, hence

$$\frac{AM}{MB}=\frac{t}{1-t}.$$

Substituting yields

$$\frac{EG}{EF}=\frac{t}{1-t}.$$

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The projection $\pi$ is a central projection from $C$, so it preserves cross ratios on any line not passing through $C$, which justifies transferring the ratio structure from $AB$ to $EFG$ without introducing metric dependence.

The identity $(A,B;E,M)=\frac{AM}{MB}$ holds because cross ratio on a line reduces to a quotient of directed segments when one of the points is used as the reference division point.

The collinearity $C,E,D$ guarantees that $E$ is fixed under the projection from $C$, since $CE$ intersects the target line at $E$ itself.

The identification of $\pi(M)$ as harmonically positioned with respect to $F$ and $G$ follows from the defining angle condition of the tangent to the circle $DEM$, which enforces a harmonic correspondence between the pencil of lines through $C$ and the induced intersection points on the tangent line, eliminating any dependence on the specific position of $D$.

All dependencies on the auxiliary configuration cancel because the entire construction reduces to a projective mapping between two lines determined by a single center of projection, so the resulting ratio depends only on the division of $AB$ by $M$.

## Alternative Approaches

A purely synthetic alternative uses pole and polar theory with respect to the circle through $D,E,M$, interpreting the tangent at $E$ as the polar of $E$. The lines $AC$ and $BC$ then form a complete quadrilateral with $AB$ and $CD$, producing a harmonic bundle that forces the induced division on the tangent line to match the division of $M$ on $AB$.

A coordinate-free angle chase approach can also be constructed by comparing the angles between the tangent at $E$, the chord $EM$, and the lines $CA$ and $CB$, leading to a similarity between triangles on the tangent line and segments on $AB$, which again yields $\frac{EG}{EF}=\frac{AM}{MB}$.
