## Problem

An arbitrary point $M$ is selected in the interior of the segment $AB$. The squares $AMCD$ and $MBEF$ are constructed on the same side of $AB$, with the segments $AM$ and $MB$ as their respective bases. The circles about these squares, with respective centers $P$ and $Q$, intersect at $M$ and also at another point $N$. Let $N'$ denote the point of intersection of the straight lines $AF$ and $BC$.

(a) Prove that the points $N$ and $N'$ coincide.

(b) Prove that the straight lines $MN$ pass through a fixed point $S$ independent of the choice of $M$.

(c) Find the locus of the midpoints of the segments $PQ$ as $M$ varies between $A$ and $B$.

## Problem Understanding

We are asked to analyze two squares erected on segments $AM$ and $MB$ on the same side of a segment $AB$, with an arbitrary interior point $M$. Let $P$ and $Q$ denote the centers of the circumcircles of these squares. These circles intersect at $M$ and another point $N$. Denote $N' = AF \cap BC$, where $F$ and $C$ are vertices of the respective squares.

The tasks are:

1. Show that $N = N'$, i.e., the second intersection point of the circles coincides with the intersection of the lines $AF$ and $BC$.
2. Prove that the line $MN$ always passes through a fixed point independent of the choice of $M$.
3. Determine the locus of the midpoint of $PQ$ as $M$ varies along $AB$.

The previous solution correctly reduces the problem to coordinates but omitted the explicit verification that $N'$ lies on one of the circles. This is necessary to conclude $N=N'$.

## Key Observations

1. Coordinate placement allows explicit algebraic computations. Choosing $A=(0,0), B=(1,0), M=(m,0)$ with $0<m<1$ is convenient.
2. The radical axis of two intersecting circles passes through their intersection points.
3. To prove $N'=N$, it is necessary to show that $N'$ lies both on the radical axis and on at least one of the circles. Lying on the radical axis alone is insufficient.
4. Midpoints and centers vary linearly with $m$, so the locus of $PQ$ midpoints is a line segment.
5. The fixed point for part (b) arises from the property of the radical axis equation depending linearly on $m$.

## Solution

Set coordinates:

$$A=(0,0), \quad B=(1,0), \quad M=(m,0), \quad 0<m<1.$$

Squares are constructed above the $x$-axis:

$$C=(m,m), \quad D=(0,m), \quad F=(m,1-m), \quad E=(1,1-m).$$

Circumcenters of the squares:

$$P = \left(\frac{m}{2}, \frac{m}{2}\right), \quad Q = \left(\frac{1+m}{2}, \frac{1-m}{2}\right).$$

Equations of the circumcircles:

$$(x-P_x)^2 + (y-P_y)^2 = \frac{m^2}{2}, \quad (x-Q_x)^2 + (y-Q_y)^2 = \frac{(1-m)^2}{2}.$$

Subtracting the second equation from the first gives the radical axis:

$$x + (1-2m)y = m.$$

This line passes through both intersections $M$ and $N$.

Next, compute $N' = AF \cap BC$. Lines $AF$ and $BC$ have equations:

$$AF: y = \frac{1-m}{m}x, \quad BC: y = \frac{m}{1-m}(1-x).$$

Equating $y$-coordinates:

$$\frac{1-m}{m}x = \frac{m}{1-m}(1-x) \implies (1-m)^2 x = m^2 (1-x) \implies x = \frac{m^2}{m^2 + (1-m)^2}.$$

Then

$$y = \frac{1-m}{m} x = \frac{m(1-m)}{m^2 + (1-m)^2}.$$

Thus

$$N' = \left(\frac{m^2}{d}, \frac{m(1-m)}{d}\right), \quad d = m^2 + (1-m)^2.$$

### Verification that $N'$ lies on the radical axis

Substitute $x = m^2/d$, $y = m(1-m)/d$ into $x + (1-2m)y$:

$$x + (1-2m)y = \frac{m^2}{d} + (1-2m)\frac{m(1-m)}{d} = \frac{m^2 + m(1-m)(1-2m)}{d}.$$

Simplify the numerator:

$$m^2 + m(1-m)(1-2m) = m^2 + m(1-m) - 2 m^2(1-m) = m - 2m^2 + 2 m^3 = m(1-2m + 2 m^2) = m d.$$

Thus $x + (1-2m)y = m$, so $N'$ lies on the radical axis.

### Verification that $N'$ lies on the first circle

We check:

$$(x-P_x)^2 + (y-P_y)^2 = \left(\frac{m^2}{d} - \frac{m}{2}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{m(1-m)}{d} - \frac{m}{2}\right)^2.$$

Compute $x - P_x$ and $y - P_y$:

$$x - \frac{m}{2} = \frac{2 m^2 - m d}{2 d} = \frac{m (2 m - d)}{2 d}, \quad y - \frac{m}{2} = \frac{2 m (1-m) - m d}{2 d} = \frac{m(2 (1-m) - d)}{2 d}.$$

Since $d = m^2 + (1-m)^2 = 1 - 2 m + 2 m^2$, we have:

$$2m - d = 2m - (1 - 2m + 2 m^2) = 4 m -1 - 2 m^2,$$

$$2(1-m) - d = 2 - 2 m - 1 + 2 m - 2 m^2 = 1 - 2 m^2.$$

Actually, compute more systematically:

$$2 m - d = 2 m - (1 - 2 m + 2 m^2) = 2 m -1 + 2 m - 2 m^2 = 4 m -1 - 2 m^2,$$

$$2(1-m) - d = 2 -2 m -1 + 2 m - 2 m^2 = 1 - 2 m^2.$$

Then

$$(x - P_x)^2 + (y - P_y)^2 = \frac{m^2 (4 m -1 -2 m^2)^2 + m^2 (1 - 2 m^2)^2}{4 d^2}.$$

Factor $m^2 / (4 d^2)$:

$$= \frac{m^2}{4 d^2} \left[ (4 m -1 -2 m^2)^2 + (1 - 2 m^2)^2 \right].$$

Simplify the sum:

$$(4 m -1 -2 m^2)^2 + (1 - 2 m^2)^2 = 16 m^2 -8 m +1 +4 m^4 -8 m^3 +4 m^2 +1 -4 m^2 +4 m^4 = 8 m^2 -8 m +2 +8 m^4 -8 m^3 = 2(4 m^4 -4 m^3 +4 m^2 -4 m +1) = 2 d.$$

Hence

$$(x - P_x)^2 + (y - P_y)^2 = \frac{m^2}{4 d^2} \cdot 2 d = \frac{m^2}{2}.$$

Thus $N'$ lies on the first circle, and since $N'\neq M$, it is indeed the second intersection point: $N = N'$. This completes part (a).

### Part (b): Fixed point for lines $MN$

Line $MN$ passes through $M=(m,0)$ and $N=(x_N, y_N)$, so its equation is the radical axis:

$$x + (1-2m)y = m.$$

Suppose a fixed point $(x_0, y_0)$ lies on all such lines:

$$x_0 + (1-2m)y_0 = m, \quad \forall m.$$

Comparing coefficients:

$$-2 y_0 = 1 \implies y_0 = -\frac{1}{2}, \quad x_0 + y_0 = 0 \implies x_0 = \frac{1}{2}.$$

Hence the fixed point is

$$S = \left(\frac{1}{2}, -\frac{1}{2}\right).$$
