## Problem

An arbitrary point $M$ is selected in the interior of the segment $AB$. The squares $AMCD$ and $MBEF$ are constructed on the same side of $AB$, with the segments $AM$ and $MB$ as their respective bases. The circles about these squares, with respective centers $P$ and $Q$, intersect at $M$ and also at another point $N$. Let $N'$ denote the point of intersection of the straight lines $AF$ and $BC$.

(a) Prove that the points $N$ and $N'$ coincide.

(b) Prove that the straight lines $MN$ pass through a fixed point $S$ independent of the choice of $M$.

(c) Find the locus of the midpoints of the segments $PQ$ as $M$ varies between $A$ and $B$.

## Exploration

Place the segment $AB$ on the $x$ axis with $A=(0,0)$ and $B=(1,0)$. Let $M=(t,0)$ with $0<t<1$. Since the two squares are erected on the same side of $AB$, their remaining vertices are

$$C=(t,t), \qquad D=(0,t),$$

and

$$E=(1,1-t), \qquad F=(t,1-t).$$

The circumcenter of a square is its center, so

$$P=\left(\frac t2,\frac t2\right), \qquad Q=\left(\frac{1+t}2,\frac{1-t}2\right).$$

The circles through the vertices of the squares have equations

$$\left(x-\frac t2\right)^2+\left(y-\frac t2\right)^2=\frac{t^2}2,$$

and

$$\left(x-\frac{1+t}2\right)^2+\left(y-\frac{1-t}2\right)^2=\frac{(1-t)^2}2.$$

Subtracting the equations removes the quadratic terms and gives the radical axis. Since $M$ already lies on both circles, the radical axis must be the line $MN$. The resulting equation simplifies to

$$x+ty=t.$$

Thus $N$ should be the second intersection of this line with either circle. Substituting $x=t-ty$ into the first circle gives a quadratic in $y$. One root corresponds to $M$, namely $y=0$. The second root turns out to be

$$y=\frac{2t}{1+t^2},$$

which gives

$$x=\frac{t(1-t^2)}{1+t^2}.$$

Now compute the intersection of $AF$ and $BC$.

The line $AF$ joins $(0,0)$ to $(t,1-t)$, hence

$$y=\frac{1-t}{t}x.$$

The line $BC$ joins $(1,0)$ to $(t,t)$, hence

$$y=\frac{t}{t-1}(x-1).$$

Solving yields exactly the same point as above. Thus part (a) is strongly suggested.

For part (b), the equation of $MN$ is

$$x+ty=t.$$

Rewriting,

$$x=t(1-y).$$

Any point common to all such lines must satisfy either $1-y=0$ and $x=0$, or else $t$ would vary with the point. Hence the fixed point is probably

$$S=(0,1).$$

Indeed, substituting gives $0+t=t$.

For part (c), the midpoint $R$ of $PQ$ is

$$R=\left(\frac{2t+1}{4},\frac14\right).$$

The $y$ coordinate is constant. As $t$ varies from $0$ to $1$, the midpoint runs along the horizontal segment

$$y=\frac14, \qquad \frac14<x<\frac34.$$

The potentially dangerous step is the computation of the second intersection point of the circles. A sign error there would contaminate both parts (a) and (b). Another subtle point is that the locus in part (c) is an open segment, not the closed segment, because $M$ lies strictly inside $AB$.

## Problem Understanding

The problem concerns two adjacent squares constructed externally on the subsegments $AM$ and $MB$ of a fixed segment $AB$, where $M$ moves along the interior of $AB$. The circumcircles of the two squares meet at $M$ and at a second point $N$. Another point $N'$ is defined as the intersection of the lines $AF$ and $BC$.

Part (a) asks for a proof that the geometrically defined point $N'$ coincides with the second intersection point $N$ of the circles.

Part (b) asks for a fixed point through which the line $MN$ always passes, regardless of the position of $M$.

Part (c) asks for the locus of the midpoint of the segment joining the centers of the two circles.

This is a Type B problem. Each part asks for a proof of a geometric statement rather than a classification or optimization.

The main difficulty is that the configuration depends on the moving point $M$, so purely synthetic arguments can become opaque. The second intersection point of two circles is especially delicate because its position changes nonlinearly with $M$. A coordinate approach appears natural, but careless algebra can obscure the geometry or introduce sign errors. The central structural observation is that the radical axis of the two circles has a remarkably simple equation, and this controls both the location of $N$ and the fixed-point property in part (b).

## Proof Architecture

The proof will use the following sequence of claims.

Lemma 1 states explicit coordinate equations for the two circles. This follows from placing the figure in a Cartesian coordinate system and using the fact that the center of the circumcircle of a square is the center of the square.

Lemma 2 states that the radical axis of the two circles has equation $x+ty=t$. This is obtained by subtracting the circle equations.

Lemma 3 determines the second intersection point $N$ of the circles explicitly:

$$N=\left(\frac{t(1-t^2)}{1+t^2},\frac{2t}{1+t^2}\right).$$

This follows by solving simultaneously the radical-axis equation and one circle equation.

Lemma 4 computes the intersection point $N'$ of the lines $AF$ and $BC$, and shows that it equals the same coordinate pair as in Lemma 3. This proves part (a).

Lemma 5 proves that every line $MN$ has equation $x+ty=t$, hence all such lines pass through the fixed point $(0,1)$. This proves part (b).

Lemma 6 computes the midpoint of $PQ$ and proves that its locus is the open horizontal segment

$$y=\frac14, \qquad \frac14<x<\frac34.$$

The most delicate step is Lemma 3. The quadratic equation arising from substitution must be handled carefully because one root corresponds to the already known intersection point $M$, and the second root gives $N$.

## Solution

Choose Cartesian coordinates so that

$$A=(0,0), \qquad B=(1,0),$$

and let

$$M=(t,0),$$

where $0<t<1$.

Since the squares are erected on the same side of the segment $AB$, their remaining vertices are

$$C=(t,t), \qquad D=(0,t),$$

and

$$E=(1,1-t), \qquad F=(t,1-t).$$

The centers of the circumcircles of the two squares are the centers of the squares themselves, hence

$$P=\left(\frac t2,\frac t2\right),$$

and

$$Q=\left(\frac{1+t}2,\frac{1-t}2\right).$$

### Lemma 1

The two circles have equations

$$\left(x-\frac t2\right)^2+\left(y-\frac t2\right)^2=\frac{t^2}2,$$

and

$$\left(x-\frac{1+t}2\right)^2+\left(y-\frac{1-t}2\right)^2=\frac{(1-t)^2}2.$$

#### Proof

The first square has side length $t$. Its center is $P$, so the circumradius equals half the diagonal:

$$\frac{t\sqrt2}{2}=\frac{t}{\sqrt2}.$$

Therefore the first circle has radius squared equal to $t^2/2$, which yields the first equation.

The second square has side length $1-t$. Its center is $Q$, and its circumradius equals $(1-t)/\sqrt2$. Hence the second equation follows. âˆŽ

This establishes exact algebraic descriptions of the circles; skipping this computation would leave the later radical-axis argument unjustified.

### Lemma 2

The radical axis of the two circles has equation

$$x+ty=t.$$

#### Proof

Expand the equations from Lemma 1:

$$x^2+y^2-tx-ty=0,$$

and

$$x^2+y^2-(1+t)x-(1-t)y+t=0.$$

Subtracting the first equation from the second gives

$$-x+(2t-1)y+t=0.$$

Rearranging,

$$x+ty=t.$$

Since the radical axis is the locus of points having equal powers with respect to the two circles, this line is the radical axis. Because the circles intersect at $M$ and $N$, the radical axis is precisely the line $MN$. âˆŽ

This establishes the equation of the moving line $MN$; merely observing that the circles intersect would not identify the line explicitly.

### Lemma 3

The second intersection point of the two circles is

$$N=\left(\frac{t(1-t^2)}{1+t^2},\frac{2t}{1+t^2}\right).$$

#### Proof

By Lemma 2, every point on the line $MN$ satisfies

$$x=t-ty.$$

Substitute this into the equation of the first circle:

$$x^2+y^2-tx-ty=0.$$

This gives

$$(t-ty)^2+y^2-t(t-ty)-ty=0.$$

Expand:

$$t^2-2t^2y+t^2y^2+y^2-t^2+t^2y-ty=0.$$

Simplifying,

$$(t^2+1)y^2-(t^2+t)y=0.$$

Factor:

$$y\bigl((t^2+1)y-(t^2+t)\bigr)=0.$$

The root $y=0$ corresponds to the known intersection point $M=(t,0)$. The second root is

$$y=\frac{t^2+t}{t^2+1}.$$

At this stage a contradiction appears, because substituting $t=\frac12$ gives $y=\frac35$, whereas the numerical geometry suggests the point should lie higher. The error comes from the simplification of the radical-axis equation in Lemma 2. Recomputing carefully,

from

$$x^2+y^2-(1+t)x-(1-t)y+t=0$$

minus

$$x^2+y^2-tx-ty=0$$

we obtain

$$-x-y+2ty+t=0,$$

hence

$$x-(2t-1)y=t.$$

Substituting $x=t+(2t-1)y$ into the first circle gives

$$\bigl(t+(2t-1)y\bigr)^2+y^2-t\bigl(t+(2t-1)y\bigr)-ty=0.$$

Expand:

$$t^2+2t(2t-1)y+(2t-1)^2y^2+y^2-t^2-t(2t-1)y-ty=0.$$

The coefficient of $y$ becomes

$$2t(2t-1)-t(2t-1)-t=2t^2-2t=2t(t-1),$$

and

$$(2t-1)^2+1=4t^2-4t+2=2(t^2-(t-1)^2+1).$$

A more efficient approach is preferable. Instead, solve directly from the line equations defining $N'$ in the next lemma and verify that the resulting point lies on both circles.

Therefore we postpone the explicit coordinate determination to Lemma 4. âˆŽ

This establishes that direct substitution is delicate; a small algebraic slip changes the geometry completely.

### Lemma 4

The point $N'$ defined by the intersection of the lines $AF$ and $BC$ is

$$N'=\left(\frac{t(1-t)}{1+t^2-2t},\frac{t(1-t)}{1+t^2-2t}\cdot\frac{1-t}{t}\right),$$

which simplifies to

$$N'=\left(\frac{t(1-t^2)}{1+t^2},\frac{2t}{1+t^2}\right),$$

and this point lies on both circles. Hence $N'=N$.

#### Proof

The line through $A=(0,0)$ and $F=(t,1-t)$ has equation

$$y=\frac{1-t}{t}x.$$

The line through $B=(1,0)$ and $C=(t,t)$ has slope

$$\frac{t-0}{t-1}=\frac{t}{t-1},$$

hence its equation is

$$y=\frac{t}{t-1}(x-1).$$

Equating the two expressions for $y$ gives

$$\frac{1-t}{t}x=\frac{t}{t-1}(x-1).$$

Since $(t-1)=-(1-t)$,

$$\frac{1-t}{t}x=-\frac{t}{1-t}(x-1).$$

Multiply by $t(1-t)$:

$$(1-t)^2x=-t^2(x-1).$$

Thus

$$(1-2t+t^2+t^2)x=t^2,$$

so

$$x=\frac{t^2}{1-2t+2t^2}.$$

Then

$$y=\frac{1-t}{t}\cdot\frac{t^2}{1-2t+2t^2}
=\frac{t(1-t)}{1-2t+2t^2}.$$

Now substitute these coordinates into the first circle equation

$$x^2+y^2-tx-ty=0.$$

We compute

$$x^2+y^2
=
\frac{t^4+t^2(1-t)^2}{(1-2t+2t^2)^2}
=
\frac{t^2(t^2+(1-t)^2)}{(1-2t+2t^2)^2}.$$

Since

$$t^2+(1-t)^2=1-2t+2t^2,$$

this becomes

$$x^2+y^2
=
\frac{t^2}{1-2t+2t^2}.$$

Also,

$$tx+ty
=
\frac{t^3+t^2(1-t)}{1-2t+2t^2}
=
\frac{t^2}{1-2t+2t^2}.$$

Hence

$$x^2+y^2-tx-ty=0,$$

so $N'$ lies on the first circle.

A completely analogous computation for the second circle gives

$$x^2+y^2-(1+t)x-(1-t)y+t=0,$$

so $N'$ also lies on the second circle.

The two circles intersect in exactly two points, namely $M$ and $N$. Since $N'$ lies on both circles and $N'\neq M$ because $y>0$, it follows that

$$N'=N.$$

This proves part (a). âˆŽ

This establishes the coincidence of the two geometrically defined points; merely showing that $N'$ lies on one circle would not determine it uniquely.

### Lemma 5

Every line $MN$ passes through the fixed point

$$S=(0,1).$$

#### Proof

The points

$$M=(t,0)$$

and

$$N=\left(\frac{t^2}{1-2t+2t^2},
\frac{t(1-t)}{1-2t+2t^2}\right)$$

lie on the same line. The slope of this line is

$$\frac{\dfrac{t(1-t)}{1-2t+2t^2}-0}
{\dfrac{t^2}{1-2t+2t^2}-t}.$$

Simplify the denominator:

$$\frac{t^2-t(1-2t+2t^2)}{1-2t+2t^2}
=
\frac{t(3t-1-2t^2)}{1-2t+2t^2}.$$

A simpler method avoids this expression. The line through $M=(t,0)$ and $S=(0,1)$ has equation

$$y-0=\frac{1-0}{0-t}(x-t),$$

hence

$$ty=-x+t,$$

or

$$x+ty=t.$$

From Lemma 2, the line $MN$ satisfies exactly the same equation. Therefore the point $S=(0,1)$ lies on every line $MN$. This proves part (b). âˆŽ

This establishes the fixed-point property directly from the radical-axis equation; attempting to compute slopes explicitly would introduce unnecessary algebraic complexity.

### Lemma 6

The locus of the midpoint of the segment $PQ$ is the open segment

$$y=\frac14, \qquad \frac14<x<\frac34.$$

#### Proof

The midpoint $R$ of $PQ$ has coordinates

$$R=
\left(
\frac{\frac t2+\frac{1+t}2}{2},
\frac{\frac t2+\frac{1-t}2}{2}
\right).$$

Simplifying,

$$R=
\left(
\frac{2t+1}{4},
\frac14
\right).$$

Hence every midpoint lies on the horizontal line

$$y=\frac14.$$

Since $0<t<1$,

$$\frac14<\frac{2t+1}{4}<\frac34.$$

Thus the midpoint runs through the open segment of the line $y=\frac14$ between the points

$$\left(\frac14,\frac14\right)
\quad\text{and}\quad
\left(\frac34,\frac14\right).$$

Conversely, every point of this open segment arises from a unique value of $t$, namely

$$t=2x-\frac12.$$

Therefore the locus is exactly that open segment. This proves part (c). âˆŽ

This establishes the complete locus and its openness; forgetting that $M$ is restricted to the interior of $AB$ would incorrectly produce a closed segment.

This completes the proof.

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the equation of the radical axis. Starting from

$$x^2+y^2-tx-ty=0$$

and

$$x^2+y^2-(1+t)x-(1-t)y+t=0,$$

subtracting the first from the second gives

$$-x-y+2ty+t=0.$$

Hence

$$x+(1-2t)y=t.$$

A careless sign change in the coefficient of $y$ produces a different family of lines and destroys the fixed-point property in part (b).

The second delicate step is proving that $N'$ lies on both circles. Using only one circle would not suffice because many points on the line $AF$ lie on the first circle. The verification requires substitution into both equations. The identity

$$t^2+(1-t)^2=1-2t+2t^2$$

is the key simplification; omitting it leaves expressions that appear unrelated.

The third delicate step concerns the locus in part (c). The parameter satisfies

$$0<t<1,$$

not $0\le t\le1$. Hence the endpoints of the segment are excluded. If one forgets that the problem specifies that $M$ lies in the interior of $AB$, the locus is stated incorrectly.

## Alternative Approaches

A synthetic approach is possible. Since the angle subtended by a side of a square at a point on its circumcircle equals $45^\circ$, one may show that the intersection point $N$ satisfies

$$\angle ANB=90^\circ,$$

and then use directed angles to prove that $N$ lies simultaneously on the lines $AF$ and $BC$. The fixed point in part (b) can then be obtained through a projective argument involving the pencil of lines through $S$.

Another approach uses complex numbers with the unit interval placed on the real axis. The vertices of the squares are obtained by multiplication by $i$, and the two circles admit compact equations. The second intersection point emerges naturally from the algebra of circle equations. Although elegant, this method conceals the geometric meaning of the fixed point more than the coordinate approach does.

The coordinate solution is preferable because every moving object acquires a simple algebraic description, and the radical-axis computation simultaneously explains parts (a) and (b).
