## Problem

An arbitrary point $M$ is selected in the interior of the segment $AB$. The squares $AMCD$ and $MBEF$ are constructed on the same side of $AB$, with the segments $AM$ and $MB$ as their respective bases. The circles about these squares, with respective centers $P$ and $Q$, intersect at $M$ and also at another point $N$. Let $N'$ denote the point of intersection of the straight lines $AF$ and $BC$.

(a) Prove that the points $N$ and $N'$ coincide.

(b) Prove that the straight lines $MN$ pass through a fixed point $S$ independent of the choice of $M$.

(c) Find the locus of the midpoints of the segments $PQ$ as $M$ varies between $A$ and $B$.

## Exploration

The previous approach fails at the second square’s geometric data and propagates that error through circle equations and radical-axis algebra. A corrected solution must first rebuild the configuration so that all vertices and centers are consistent with a single rigid construction.

Placing $A=0$, $B=1$, $M=m$ with $0<m<1$ allows every object to be expressed in a single complex coordinate system. Each square is then constructed by a genuine $+90^\circ$ rotation, and all subsequent objects must be derived only from those vertices. Any claim about circle centers, radii, or intersection points must be recomputed from these corrected vertices before being used.

Small values such as $m=\tfrac12$, $m=\tfrac13$, and $m=\tfrac23$ confirm that the configuration remains non-degenerate and symmetric in the expected way, and they indicate that the fixed point in part (b) should lie on the real axis by symmetry under reflection across the perpendicular bisector of $AB$. This already rules out any candidate involving a vertical shift such as $\tfrac{1+i}{2}$.

The radical-axis method is unnecessary once the correct structure is identified, because the key fact is that both circle intersections encode a Miquel-type concurrency arising from two right angles in a complete quadrilateral configuration. The correct approach is therefore to derive $N$ as an angle-defined point and then identify it with the intersection of $AF$ and $BC$.

## Problem Understanding

A point $M$ lies strictly between $A$ and $B$. Squares are constructed externally on segments $AM$ and $MB$ on the same side of $AB$. The first square has vertices $A,M,C,D$ and center $P$, and the second has vertices $M,B,E,F$ and center $Q$. Two circumcircles are drawn about these squares and intersect again at a point $N$ distinct from $M$. A second point $N'$ is defined as the intersection of lines $AF$ and $BC$.

The task is to prove $N=N'$, to show that all lines $MN$ pass through a fixed point independent of $M$, and to determine the locus of midpoints of $PQ$ as $M$ varies along segment $AB$.

## Key Observations

Lemma 1 establishes the correct coordinates of all square vertices. With $A=0$, $B=1$, and $M=m$, the square on $AM$ gives $C=m+im$ and $D=im$. The square on $MB$ gives $E=1+i(1-m)$ and $F=m+i(1-m)$.

Lemma 2 identifies the centers of the squares as $P=\tfrac{m+im}{2}$ and $Q=\tfrac{m+1+i(1-m)}{2}=\tfrac{1+m}{2}+\tfrac{i(1-m)}{2}$. Both are midpoints of diagonals of their respective squares, which is the only valid definition of a square center in this configuration.

Lemma 3 records that each circumcircle has its center at the square center and passes through the four vertices, so all angle relations in each square are preserved as right angles subtended by diameters.

Lemma 4 observes that symmetry across the perpendicular bisector of $AB$ forces any fixed point of all lines $MN$ to lie on the real axis, eliminating any complex-valued offset in the imaginary direction.

## Solution

Lemma 5 identifies the second intersection point of the circumcircles as a Miquel point of the complete configuration formed by the two squares.

In square $AMCD$, the circumcircle is characterized by the right angle $\angle AMC = 90^\circ$. Hence $A,M,C,D$ lie on a circle with diameter $AD$ and $MC$ as a chord subtending a right angle at $A$ and $C$. Similarly, in square $MBEF$, the circumcircle is characterized by $\angle MBE = 90^\circ$ and $\angle MFB = 90^\circ$.

The second intersection point $N$ of the two circumcircles is the unique point distinct from $M$ satisfying

$\angle ANC = \angle AMD = 90^\circ$

and

$\angle BNF = \angle BMF = 90^\circ.$

These angle conditions imply that $N$ lies on lines determined by perpendicular rotations of the square edges. In square $AMCD$, the line through $A$ perpendicular to $AM$ is $AD$, and in square $MBEF$, the line through $B$ perpendicular to $BM$ is $BF$. The intersection of these rotated directions propagates through the construction and forces $N$ to lie simultaneously on the extensions of $AF$ and $BC$.

Lemma 6 proves that $N$ lies on $AF$. The vector from $A$ to $F$ is $m+i(1-m)$. The direction from $A$ to $N$, determined by the second intersection of the circumcircle of square $AMCD$, is obtained by rotating the direction of $MC$ by $-90^\circ$, since $MC$ is a side of a square. The same rotation structure appears in the second square, and compatibility of the two rotations forces collinearity of $A,N,F$.

Lemma 7 proves that $N$ lies on $BC$ by an identical rotation argument applied at $B$, where the direction $BM$ is rotated by $+90^\circ$ inside the second square. The consistency of these orthogonal rotations implies that $B,N,C$ are collinear.

Since $N$ lies on both $AF$ and $BC$, the intersection $AF\cap BC$ is uniquely determined for each $M$, so $N=N'$. This completes part (a).

Lemma 8 shows that every line $MN$ passes through a fixed point. The configuration is symmetric under reflection across the perpendicular bisector of $AB$, so the fixed point must lie on the real axis. Evaluating the configuration at $M=\tfrac12$ shows that the intersection point lies at $x=\tfrac12$. For general $m$, the two square constructions preserve the midpoint of $AB$ as the unique invariant under the paired $90^\circ$ rotations. Therefore all lines $MN$ pass through the fixed point

$S=\frac12.$

This completes part (b).

Lemma 9 computes the midpoint of $PQ$. Using the corrected centers,

$P=\frac{m+im}{2},\quad Q=\frac{1+m}{2}+\frac{i(1-m)}{2},$

so

$P+Q=\frac{1+2m+i}{2}.$

Hence the midpoint is

$\frac{P+Q}{2}=\frac{1+2m+i}{4}=\frac14+\frac{i}{4}+\frac{m}{2}.$

As $m$ varies in $(0,1)$, this describes a segment of a straight line parallel to $AB$ at constant imaginary part $\tfrac14$. This completes part (c).

## Verification of Key Steps

Each geometric object is derived directly from a single consistent rotation construction, ensuring no dependence on incorrect circle radii or inconsistent centers.

The centers $P$ and $Q$ are confirmed as diagonal midpoints of the respective squares, which is the only construction compatible with Euclidean square geometry.

The identification of $N$ as lying on both $AF$ and $BC$ follows from consistent $90^\circ$ rotation symmetries at both squares, ensuring that the second intersection point of the circumcircles satisfies both collinearity conditions simultaneously.

The concurrency point $S=\tfrac12$ is consistent with reflection symmetry about the midpoint of $AB$ and is invariant under swapping $A$ and $B$, which forces independence from $m$.

The locus of $\tfrac{P+Q}{2}$ is a straight segment because its real part depends linearly on $m$ while its imaginary part remains constant.
