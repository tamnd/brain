## Problem

An arbitrary point $M$ is selected in the interior of the segment $AB$. The squares $AMCD$ and $MBEF$ are constructed on the same side of $AB$, with the segments $AM$ and $MB$ as their respective bases. The circles about these squares, with respective centers $P$ and $Q$, intersect at $M$ and also at another point $N$. Let $N'$ denote the point of intersection of the straight lines $AF$ and $BC$.

(a) Prove that the points $N$ and $N'$ coincide.

(b) Prove that the straight lines $MN$ pass through a fixed point $S$ independent of the choice of $M$.

(c) Find the locus of the midpoints of the segments $PQ$ as $M$ varies between $A$ and $B$.

## Problem Understanding

The problem deals with a line segment $AB$, a point $M$ in its interior, and two squares constructed on $AM$ and $MB$ on the same side. Let $P$ and $Q$ be the circumcenters of these squares, and their circumcircles intersect at $M$ and another point $N$. Part (a) asks to prove that $N$ coincides with the intersection of lines $AF$ and $BC$. Part (b) asks to show that as $M$ varies, the line $MN$ passes through a fixed point $S$. Part (c) asks for the locus of the midpoints of $PQ$ as $M$ varies. This is a Type B problem, as all parts require proving existence or alignment statements. The difficulty lies in coordinating multiple geometric elements: square construction, circumcenters, circle intersections, and maintaining relations as $M$ varies. A naive coordinate approach might be messy; complex numbers or vector rotations by $90^\circ$ can simplify the geometry.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1: The circumcenter of a square constructed on a segment lies at the midpoint of its diagonal. Proof: By definition, the circumcenter is equidistant from all four vertices, which coincides with the intersection of the diagonals.

Lemma 2: The intersection $N'$ of lines $AF$ and $BC$ coincides with the second intersection point $N$ of the circumcircles of $AMCD$ and $MBEF$. Proof: Represent points with vectors and rotate segments by $90^\circ$ to map squares; show that both constructions yield the same coordinates for $N$.

Lemma 3: The line $MN$ passes through a fixed point $S$ independent of $M$. Proof: Use vector addition and the rotation structure to express $N$ as a linear function of $M$; identify $S$ as the limiting intersection of these lines when $M$ approaches $A$ or $B$.

Lemma 4: The midpoint of $PQ$ traces a straight line segment as $M$ varies along $AB$. Proof: Express $P$ and $Q$ as midpoints of diagonals depending linearly on $M$; the midpoint formula gives an affine map from $M$ to the midpoint of $PQ$.

The hardest part is Lemma 2, ensuring that the intersection of the lines and the second intersection of circles truly coincide for all $M$.
