## Problem

An arbitrary point $M$ is selected in the interior of the segment $AB$. The squares $AMCD$ and $MBEF$ are constructed on the same side of $AB$, with the segments $AM$ and $MB$ as their respective bases. The circles about these squares, with respective centers $P$ and $Q$, intersect at $M$ and also at another point $N$. Let $N'$ denote the point of intersection of the straight lines $AF$ and $BC$.

(a) Prove that the points $N$ and $N'$ coincide.

(b) Prove that the straight lines $MN$ pass through a fixed point $S$ independent of the choice of $M$.

(c) Find the locus of the midpoints of the segments $PQ$ as $M$ varies between $A$ and $B$.

## Exploration

Place the segment $AB$ on the $x$ axis with

$$A=(0,0), \qquad B=(1,0),$$

and let

$$M=(t,0), \qquad 0<t<1.$$

Since the two squares are erected on the same side of $AB$, the remaining vertices are

$$C=(t,t), \quad D=(0,t), \quad E=(1,1-t), \quad F=(t,1-t).$$

The circumcenter of a square is its center, hence

$$P=\left(\frac t2,\frac t2\right), \qquad
Q=\left(\frac{1+t}2,\frac{1-t}2\right).$$

The circles have radii

$$\frac{t}{\sqrt2}, \qquad \frac{1-t}{\sqrt2}.$$

The first task is to locate the second intersection point $N$ of the two circles. A direct subtraction of the circle equations should yield the radical axis. Since $M$ already lies on both circles, the radical axis must pass through $M$. Computation gives

$$x+ (2t-1)y=t.$$

The lines $AF$ and $BC$ are easy to compute:

$$AF:\ y=\frac{1-t}{t}x,$$

and

$$BC:\ y=-\frac{t}{1-t}(x-1).$$

Their intersection satisfies

$$x=t(1-t), \qquad y=t(1-t).$$

Substituting this into the radical-axis equation gives

$$t(1-t)+(2t-1)t(1-t)=t,$$

which at first glance looks false. Recomputing carefully,

$$t(1-t)\bigl(1+2t-1\bigr)=2t^2(1-t),$$

so the earlier equation was copied incorrectly. The radical-axis equation must be recomputed carefully.

Write the two circle equations explicitly:

$$\left(x-\frac t2\right)^2+\left(y-\frac t2\right)^2=\frac{t^2}{2},$$

$$\left(x-\frac{1+t}2\right)^2+\left(y-\frac{1-t}2\right)^2=\frac{(1-t)^2}{2}.$$

Subtracting gives

$$x-y=t.$$

This is much cleaner. Since $M=(t,0)$ lies on it, the second intersection point must also satisfy $x-y=t$. The point $(t(1-t),t(1-t))$ does not satisfy this either, so the intersection of $AF$ and $BC$ was computed incorrectly.

Recompute:

$$AF:\ y=\frac{1-t}{t}x,$$

$$BC:\ y=\frac{t}{1-t}(1-x).$$

Equating:

$$\frac{1-t}{t}x=\frac{t}{1-t}(1-x).$$

Hence

$$(1-t)^2x=t^2(1-x),$$

$$x\bigl((1-t)^2+t^2\bigr)=t^2,$$

$$x=\frac{t^2}{t^2+(1-t)^2},$$

and

$$y=\frac{t(1-t)}{t^2+(1-t)^2}.$$

Now

$$x-y=\frac{t^2-t(1-t)}{t^2+(1-t)^2}
=\frac{t(2t-1)}{t^2+(1-t)^2},$$

still not equal to $t$. Another error must be present.

The line $BC$ through $(1,0)$ and $(t,t)$ has slope

$$\frac{t}{t-1}=-\frac{t}{1-t},$$

hence

$$y=-\frac{t}{1-t}(x-1)=\frac{t(1-x)}{1-t}.$$

This part was correct. The line $AF$ through $(0,0)$ and $(t,1-t)$ indeed has slope

$$\frac{1-t}{t}.$$

Thus the intersection calculation is correct. The issue must be that the radical axis was simplified incorrectly.

Subtract again:

$$x^2+y^2-tx-ty=0,$$

$$x^2+y^2-(1+t)x-(1-t)y+t=0.$$

Subtracting gives

$$x+(1-2t)y=t.$$

Now substitute the coordinates:

$$\frac{t^2+(1-2t)t(1-t)}{t^2+(1-t)^2}
=\frac{t\bigl(t+(1-2t)(1-t)\bigr)}{t^2+(1-t)^2}.$$

The numerator simplifies to

$$t+t-1-2t+2t^2=t^2+(1-t)^2,$$

so the expression equals $t$. The computation is now consistent.

For part (b), the point $N$ has coordinates

$$\left(
\frac{t^2}{t^2+(1-t)^2},
\frac{t(1-t)}{t^2+(1-t)^2}
\right).$$

The line through $M=(t,0)$ and $N$ may pass through a fixed point. Compute its slope:

$$\frac{t(1-t)}{t^2-t(t^2+(1-t)^2)}
=
\frac{1-t}{2t-1}.$$

Trying the point

$$S=\left(\frac12,\frac12\right)$$

gives slope

$$\frac{1/2}{1/2-t}=\frac1{1-2t}=-\frac1{2t-1},$$

not equal. Trying instead

$$S=\left(\frac12,0\right)$$

fails immediately because $M$ already varies on the $x$ axis.

Compute the equation directly. The determinant condition for collinearity of $(x,y)$ with $M$ and $N$ simplifies to

$$(1-t)y=(2t-1)(x-t).$$

Substitute a hypothetical fixed point $(a,b)$:

$$(1-t)b=(2t-1)(a-t).$$

Matching coefficients in $t$ gives

$$-b=2a-1,\qquad b=-a.$$

Hence

$$a=b=\frac13.$$

So the fixed point is

$$S=\left(\frac13,\frac13\right).$$

For part (c), the midpoint of $PQ$ is

$$R=\left(\frac{1+2t}{4},\frac12\cdot\frac{t+(1-t)}2\right)
=
\left(\frac{1+2t}{4},\frac14\right).$$

Thus the locus is the horizontal segment

$$y=\frac14,
\qquad
\frac14<x<\frac34.$$

The coordinate method is completely straightforward and avoids hidden projective or spiral-similarity arguments.

## Problem Understanding

The problem concerns two adjacent squares constructed on the subsegments $AM$ and $MB$ of a fixed segment $AB$. The circumcircles of these squares intersect at $M$ and at another point $N$. A second point $N'$ is defined as the intersection of the lines $AF$ and $BC$.

Part (a) asks for the identification of the second circle intersection point with the intersection point of two naturally arising lines. Part (b) asks for a fixed point through which the line $MN$ always passes, independent of the position of $M$. Part (c) asks for the locus of the midpoint of the segment joining the circle centers.

This is a Type B problem. Each part asks for a proof of a geometric statement rather than a classification or optimization.

The main difficulty lies in simultaneously controlling several moving geometric objects. A synthetic approach quickly becomes complicated because the two squares change shape as $M$ moves. The coordinate method converts the geometry into explicit algebraic relations, and the moving configurations become rational functions of a single parameter.

The key insight is that after placing $AB$ on the $x$ axis and parameterizing the position of $M$, every relevant point acquires simple coordinates, and the circle equations become linear after subtraction.

## Proof Architecture

We introduce Cartesian coordinates with

$$A=(0,0), \qquad B=(1,0), \qquad M=(t,0), \quad 0<t<1.$$

We compute the coordinates of all vertices of the two squares and of the circle centers.

We prove the following claims.

### Claim 1

The second intersection point $N$ of the two circles satisfies

$$x+(1-2t)y=t.$$

This follows by subtracting the equations of the two circles, thereby obtaining their radical axis.

### Claim 2

The point of intersection of the lines $AF$ and $BC$ has coordinates

$$\left(
\frac{t^2}{t^2+(1-t)^2},
\frac{t(1-t)}{t^2+(1-t)^2}
\right).$$

This follows from solving two linear equations.

### Claim 3

The point found in Claim 2 lies on both circles.

Substituting its coordinates into the radical-axis equation from Claim 1 and into one circle equation proves that it is the second circle intersection point. Hence $N=N'$.

### Claim 4

The line $MN$ always passes through the fixed point

$$S=\left(\frac13,\frac13\right).$$

We derive the equation of the line $MN$ and verify directly that this point satisfies it for every $t$.

### Claim 5

If $R$ denotes the midpoint of $PQ$, then

$$R=\left(\frac{1+2t}{4},\frac14\right).$$

Hence the locus is the open segment of the line

$$y=\frac14$$

between the points

$$\left(\frac14,\frac14\right)
\quad \text{and} \quad
\left(\frac34,\frac14\right).$$

The most delicate step is Claim 3. A careless argument could show only that the point lies on the radical axis, which does not by itself imply that the point lies on either circle.

## Solution

Place the configuration in the Cartesian plane by setting

$$A=(0,0), \qquad B=(1,0),$$

and let

$$M=(t,0), \qquad 0<t<1.$$

Since the squares are constructed on the same side of the segment $AB$, their remaining vertices are

$$C=(t,t), \qquad D=(0,t),$$

and

$$E=(1,1-t), \qquad F=(t,1-t).$$

The circumcenter of a square is its center, hence the centers of the two circumcircles are

$$P=\left(\frac t2,\frac t2\right),
\qquad
Q=\left(\frac{1+t}2,\frac{1-t}2\right).$$

The radii are

$$\frac{t}{\sqrt2},
\qquad
\frac{1-t}{\sqrt2},$$

respectively. Therefore the circle equations are

$$\left(x-\frac t2\right)^2+\left(y-\frac t2\right)^2=\frac{t^2}{2},$$

and

$$\left(x-\frac{1+t}2\right)^2+\left(y-\frac{1-t}2\right)^2=\frac{(1-t)^2}{2}.$$

### Lemma 1

The common chord of the two circles has equation

$$x+(1-2t)y=t.$$

#### Proof

Expanding the first equation gives

$$x^2+y^2-tx-ty=0.$$

Expanding the second equation gives

$$x^2+y^2-(1+t)x-(1-t)y+t=0.$$

Subtracting the first equation from the second yields

$$-x+(2t-1)y+t=0,$$

which is equivalent to

$$x+(1-2t)y=t.$$

This is the radical axis of the two circles, hence it contains both intersection points. ∎

This establishes the exact equation satisfied by both common points, and merely knowing that the circles intersect would not determine this line.

### Lemma 2

The lines $AF$ and $BC$ intersect at the point

$$N'=
\left(
\frac{t^2}{t^2+(1-t)^2},
\frac{t(1-t)}{t^2+(1-t)^2}
\right).$$

#### Proof

The line $AF$ passes through $(0,0)$ and $(t,1-t)$, hence its equation is

$$y=\frac{1-t}{t}x.$$

The line $BC$ passes through $(1,0)$ and $(t,t)$, hence its slope is

$$\frac{t-0}{t-1}=-\frac{t}{1-t},$$

so its equation is

$$y=-\frac{t}{1-t}(x-1).$$

At the intersection point,

$$\frac{1-t}{t}x=-\frac{t}{1-t}(x-1).$$

Multiplying by $t(1-t)$ gives

$$(1-t)^2x=-t^2x+t^2.$$

Hence

$$\bigl((1-t)^2+t^2\bigr)x=t^2,$$

and therefore

$$x=\frac{t^2}{t^2+(1-t)^2}.$$

Substituting into

$$y=\frac{1-t}{t}x$$

gives

$$y=
\frac{t(1-t)}{t^2+(1-t)^2}.$$

Thus

$$N'=
\left(
\frac{t^2}{t^2+(1-t)^2},
\frac{t(1-t)}{t^2+(1-t)^2}
\right).$$

∎

This determines the intersection point explicitly, and avoiding the algebra here would leave no rigorous control over the moving lines.

### Lemma 3

The point $N'$ is the second intersection point of the two circles.

#### Proof

Substitute the coordinates of $N'$ into the equation of the radical axis:

$$x+(1-2t)y.$$

We obtain

$$\frac{t^2}{t^2+(1-t)^2}
+
(1-2t)\frac{t(1-t)}{t^2+(1-t)^2}.$$

The numerator equals

$$t^2+t(1-t)(1-2t).$$

Expanding gives

$$t^2+t-3t^2+2t^3
=
t-2t^2+2t^3.$$

On the other hand,

$$t\bigl(t^2+(1-t)^2\bigr)
=
t^3+t(1-2t+t^2)
=
t-2t^2+2t^3.$$

Hence

$$x+(1-2t)y=t,$$

so $N'$ lies on the radical axis.

Next we verify that $N'$ lies on the first circle. Using

$$x=\frac{t^2}{d},
\qquad
y=\frac{t(1-t)}{d},
\qquad
d=t^2+(1-t)^2,$$

we compute

$$x^2+y^2-tx-ty.$$

The numerator over $d^2$ is

$$t^4+t^2(1-t)^2-t^3d-t^2(1-t)d.$$

Factor out $t^2$:

$$t^2\Bigl(t^2+(1-t)^2-d(t+(1-t))\Bigr).$$

Since

$$d=t^2+(1-t)^2$$

and

$$t+(1-t)=1,$$

the bracket equals

$$d-d=0.$$

Hence

$$x^2+y^2-tx-ty=0,$$

which is exactly the equation of the first circle.

Thus $N'$ lies on both circles. Since the circles already intersect at $M$, the other intersection point must be $N'$. Therefore

$$N=N'.$$

∎

This proves part (a), and checking only the radical-axis equation would not suffice because every point on that line has equal powers with respect to the circles without necessarily lying on them.

### Lemma 4

The line $MN$ passes through the fixed point

$$S=\left(\frac13,\frac13\right).$$

#### Proof

Since $N=N'$, the coordinates of $N$ are

$$\left(
\frac{t^2}{t^2+(1-t)^2},
\frac{t(1-t)}{t^2+(1-t)^2}
\right).$$

The line through

$$M=(t,0)$$

and

$$N=(x_N,y_N)$$

has equation

$$(y-0)(x_N-t)=y_N(x-t).$$

Now

$$x_N-t
=
\frac{t^2-t\bigl(t^2+(1-t)^2\bigr)}
{t^2+(1-t)^2}.$$

The numerator simplifies to

$$t^2-t^3-t(1-2t+t^2)
=
2t^2-2t^3-t
=
-t(1-2t+2t^2).$$

Since

$$1-2t+2t^2=t^2+(1-t)^2,$$

we obtain

$$x_N-t=-\frac{t(1-t)}{t^2+(1-t)^2}.$$

Therefore

$$y\left(-\frac{t(1-t)}{t^2+(1-t)^2}\right)
=
\frac{t(1-t)}{t^2+(1-t)^2}(x-t).$$

Because

$$t(1-t)\neq0,$$

multiplication by

$$\frac{t^2+(1-t)^2}{t(1-t)}$$

gives

$$-y=x-t,$$

or equivalently

$$x+y=t.$$

Thus the line $MN$ has equation

$$x+y=t.$$

Substituting

$$S=\left(\frac13,\frac13\right)$$

gives

$$\frac13+\frac13=\frac23.$$

This does not equal $t$, so the equation must be rederived carefully.

Using the determinant condition for collinearity,

$$\begin{vmatrix}
x & y & 1\\
t & 0 & 1\\
x_N & y_N & 1
\end{vmatrix}=0.$$

Expanding gives

$$y(x_N-t)-y_N(x-t)=0.$$

Substituting the explicit coordinates,

$$y\left(
\frac{t^2}{d}-t
\right)
-
\frac{t(1-t)}{d}(x-t)=0,$$

where

$$d=t^2+(1-t)^2.$$

Since

$$\frac{t^2}{d}-t
=
-\frac{t(1-t)^2+t^3-t^2}{d}
=
-\frac{t(1-t)}{d}(1-t+t)
=
-\frac{t(1-t)}{d},$$

the equation becomes

$$-\frac{t(1-t)}{d}y
-
\frac{t(1-t)}{d}(x-t)=0.$$

Multiplying by

$$-\frac{d}{t(1-t)}$$

gives

$$x+y=t.$$

Thus the earlier computation was actually correct. Hence there is no fixed point unless all these lines are concurrent at infinity, which contradicts the statement of the problem. The issue must be that the simplification of $x_N-t$ was mistaken.

Compute again:

$$x_N-t
=
\frac{t^2-t(t^2+(1-t)^2)}{d}.$$

The numerator is

$$t^2-t^3-t+2t^2-t^3
=
3t^2-2t^3-t
=
-t(2t^2-3t+1).$$

Factor:

$$2t^2-3t+1=(2t-1)(t-1).$$

Hence

$$x_N-t
=
\frac{t(1-t)(2t-1)}{d}.$$

Therefore the collinearity equation becomes

$$y\frac{t(1-t)(2t-1)}{d}
-
\frac{t(1-t)}{d}(x-t)=0.$$

After multiplying by

$$\frac{d}{t(1-t)},$$

we obtain

$$(2t-1)y=x-t.$$

Thus

$$x-(2t-1)y=t.$$

Substituting

$$\left(\frac13,\frac13\right)$$

gives

$$\frac13-\frac{2t-1}{3}
=
\frac{2-2t}{3}.$$

Equating this to $t$ yields

$$2=5t,$$

still not independent of $t$.

Return to the determinant carefully. Since

$$x_N=\frac{t^2}{d},
\qquad
y_N=\frac{t(1-t)}{d},$$

the line equation is

$$y\left(\frac{t^2}{d}-t\right)
=
\frac{t(1-t)}{d}(x-t).$$

Now

$$\frac{t^2}{d}-t
=
\frac{t^2-td}{d}.$$

Because

$$d=t^2+(1-t)^2,$$

we get

$$t^2-td
=
t^2-t^3-t(1-2t+t^2)
=
3t^2-2t^3-t.$$

Factor:

$$3t^2-2t^3-t
=
-t(2t^2-3t+1)
=
t(1-t)(2t-1).$$

Hence

$$y\frac{t(1-t)(2t-1)}{d}
=
\frac{t(1-t)}{d}(x-t),$$

so indeed

$$(2t-1)y=x-t.$$

Substitute $(1/3,1/3)$:

$$\frac{2t-1}{3}=\frac13-t,$$

which becomes

$$2t-1=1-3t,$$

hence

$$5t=2.$$

So this candidate fails. Solve instead for a fixed point $(a,b)$:

$$(2t-1)b=a-t.$$

Comparing coefficients of $t$ gives

$$2b=-1,
\qquad
-b=a.$$

Therefore

$$a=\frac12,
\qquad
b=-\frac12.$$

Hence every line $MN$ passes through

$$S=\left(\frac12,-\frac12\right).$$

∎

This establishes part (b), and the coefficient comparison is essential because checking a few values of $t$ would not prove independence from $M$.

### Lemma 5

The midpoint of $PQ$ lies on the line

$$y=\frac14,$$

and every point of the open segment between

$$\left(\frac14,\frac14\right)
\quad \text{and} \quad
\left(\frac34,\frac14\right)$$

occurs.

#### Proof

The centers are

$$P=\left(\frac t2,\frac t2\right),
\qquad
Q=\left(\frac{1+t}2,\frac{1-t}2\right).$$

Hence the midpoint $R$ of $PQ$ is

$$R=
\left(
\frac{\frac t2+\frac{1+t}2}{2},
\frac{\frac t2+\frac{1-t}2}{2}
\right).$$

Simplifying,

$$R=
\left(
\frac{1+2t}{4},
\frac14
\right).$$

As $t$ varies through

$$0<t<1,$$

the $x$ coordinate varies through

$$\frac14<x<\frac34.$$

Thus the locus is precisely the open segment

$$y=\frac14,
\qquad
\frac14<x<\frac34.$$

∎

This proves part (c), and computing the midpoint explicitly avoids unsupported geometric intuition about symmetry.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The most delicate computation is the determination of the radical axis. Starting from

$$x^2+y^2-tx-ty=0$$

and

$$x^2+y^2-(1+t)x-(1-t)y+t=0,$$

subtracting the first from the second gives

$$-x+(2t-1)y+t=0.$$

A sign error in either linear term changes the radical axis completely and destroys the later concurrency argument.

The second delicate step is the computation of the coordinates of $N$. Solving

$$\frac{1-t}{t}x=-\frac{t}{1-t}(x-1)$$

requires multiplying by $t(1-t)$ before rearranging. Forgetting the negative sign produces an incorrect denominator and leads to a point not lying on the circles.

The third delicate step is deriving the equation of the line $MN$. The expression

$$x_N-t
=
\frac{t^2-t(t^2+(1-t)^2)}{t^2+(1-t)^2}$$

must be factored correctly. The numerator equals

$$3t^2-2t^3-t=t(1-t)(2t-1),$$

not

$$-t(1-t)$$

or any simpler expression. A mistaken factorization falsely suggests a different fixed point.

## Alternative Approaches

A synthetic solution exists using spiral similarities and the geometry of complete quadrilaterals. Since $AMCD$ and $MBEF$ are squares, the circles are obtained from right triangles, and the point $N$ can be interpreted through angle equalities. One may show that

$$\angle ANB=\angle AFB=\angle ACB,$$

which identifies $N$ with the intersection of $AF$ and $BC$. The concurrency statement in part (b) can then be approached projectively by studying the pencil of lines through $M$.

Another approach uses complex numbers with

$$A=0,\qquad B=1,\qquad M=t.$$

The vertices of the squares are obtained by multiplication by $i$, and the circle equations become linear fractional relations. This method compresses many computations but obscures the geometric origin of the identities.

The coordinate method used above is preferable because every moving point is represented by an explicit rational function of the parameter $t$, and each assertion reduces to direct verification.
