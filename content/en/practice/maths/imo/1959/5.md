## Problem

An arbitrary point $M$ is selected in the interior of the segment $AB$. The squares $AMCD$ and $MBEF$ are constructed on the same side of $AB$, with the segments $AM$ and $MB$ as their respective bases. The circles about these squares, with respective centers $P$ and $Q$, intersect at $M$ and also at another point $N$. Let $N'$ denote the point of intersection of the straight lines $AF$ and $BC$.

(a) Prove that the points $N$ and $N'$ coincide.

(b) Prove that the straight lines $MN$ pass through a fixed point $S$ independent of the choice of $M$.

(c) Find the locus of the midpoints of the segments $PQ$ as $M$ varies between $A$ and $B$.

## Problem Understanding

An arbitrary point $M$ is chosen on the segment $AB$. On the same side of $AB$, two squares $AMCD$ and $MBEF$ are constructed with bases $AM$ and $MB$. Let $P$ and $Q$ be the centers of the circumcircles of these squares. These circles intersect at $M$ and a second point $N$. Let $N' = AF \cap BC$.

The problem asks to prove that $N=N'$, to show that all lines $MN$ pass through a fixed point independent of $M$, and to determine the locus of the midpoint of $PQ$.

A coordinate approach is natural because the square constructions are linear in the parameter $t$, allowing all relevant objects to be expressed explicitly and compared.

We place

$$A=(0,0),\quad B=(1,0),\quad M=(t,0),\quad 0<t<1.$$

The squares constructed on $AM$ and $MB$ yield

$$C=(t,t),\quad D=(0,t),\quad E=(1,1-t),\quad F=(t,1-t).$$

The centers of the circumcircles are

$$P=\left(\frac t2,\frac t2\right),\qquad Q=\left(\frac{1+t}{2},\frac{1-t}{2}\right),$$

and both circles are easily expressed in quadratic form.

## Key Observations

The circumcircle of a square centered at $P$ with side aligned to the axes satisfies

$$x^2+y^2-tx-ty=0.$$

Similarly, the second circle is

$$x^2+y^2-(1+t)x-(1-t)y+t=0.$$

Subtracting gives the radical axis, which constrains all intersection points. This reduces the determination of $N$ to solving a linear relation substituted into one circle.

The intersection $N'$ of $AF$ and $BC$ is also determined by two linear equations, so the coincidence $N=N'$ is ultimately algebraic.

For part (b), the line $MN$ is obtained from the relation between $M$ and the second intersection $N$, but the expression involves division by $2t-1$, which requires special treatment when $t=\tfrac12$.

## Solution

### (a) Identification of $N$ and $N'$

The circle equations are

$$x^2+y^2-tx-ty=0,$$

$$x^2+y^2-(1+t)x-(1-t)y+t=0.$$

Subtracting the second from the first gives

$$tx+ty-(1+t)x-(1-t)y+t=0,$$

which simplifies to

$$x-(2t-1)y-t=0.$$

Hence every common point satisfies

$$x=t+(2t-1)y.$$

Substitute into the first circle:

$$[t+(2t-1)y]^2+y^2-t[t+(2t-1)y]-ty=0.$$

Expanding and simplifying yields

$$2(t^2+(1-t)^2)y^2+2t(t-1)y=0,$$

so

$$y=0 \quad \text{or} \quad y=\frac{t(1-t)}{t^2+(1-t)^2}.$$

The case $y=0$ corresponds to $M$. Thus the second intersection is

$$N=\left(\frac{t^2}{t^2+(1-t)^2},\frac{t(1-t)}{t^2+(1-t)^2}\right).$$

Now compute $N' = AF \cap BC$.

Line $AF$ through $A=(0,0)$ and $F=(t,1-t)$ is

$$y=\frac{1-t}{t}x.$$

Line $BC$ through $B=(1,0)$ and $C=(t,t)$ is

$$y=\frac{t}{t-1}(x-1).$$

Solving the system gives

$$(1-t)^2x=-t^2(x-1),$$

so

$$x=\frac{t^2}{t^2+(1-t)^2},\qquad
y=\frac{t(1-t)}{t^2+(1-t)^2}.$$

Hence $N'=N$, completing part (a).

### (b) Fixed point of the line $MN$

From part (a), for $t\neq \tfrac12$, we compute the slope of $MN$.

We have

$$M=(t,0),\qquad N=\left(\frac{t^2}{d},\frac{t(1-t)}{d}\right),\quad d=t^2+(1-t)^2.$$

Then

$$x_N-t=\frac{t^2-td}{d}.$$

Since $d=2t^2-2t+1$,

$$t^2-td=t(t-d)=t(t-2t^2+2t-1)=t(1-t)(2t-1).$$

Thus for $t\neq \tfrac12$,

$$\text{slope}(MN)=\frac{\frac{t(1-t)}{d}}{\frac{t(1-t)(2t-1)}{d}}=\frac{1}{2t-1}.$$

Hence

$$y=\frac{x-t}{2t-1},$$

or equivalently

$$x-(2t-1)y-t=0.$$

To find a fixed point $S=(a,b)$, we impose

$$a-(2t-1)b-t=0
\quad \text{for all } t\neq \tfrac12.$$

Rewriting,

$$a+b = t(2b+1).$$

Since the left-hand side is independent of $t$, the coefficient of $t$ must vanish:

$$2b+1=0 \Rightarrow b=-\frac12,$$

and then

$$a+b=0 \Rightarrow a=\frac12.$$

Thus the candidate fixed point is

$$S=\left(\frac12,-\frac12\right).$$

It remains to handle the degenerate case $t=\tfrac12$, where the above slope computation is invalid.

If $t=\tfrac12$, then

$$M=\left(\frac12,0\right),\quad
d=\frac14+\frac14=\frac12,$$

and

$$N=\left(\frac{\frac14}{\frac12},\frac{\frac14}{\frac12}\right)=\left(\frac12,\frac12\right).$$

Thus line $MN$ is the vertical line

$$x=\frac12.$$

The point $S=\left(\frac12,-\frac12\right)$ satisfies this equation, so it lies on $MN$ also in the degenerate case.

Therefore every line $MN$ passes through the fixed point

$$S=\left(\frac12,-\frac12\right).$$

### (c) Locus of the midpoint of $PQ$

The centers are

$$P=\left(\frac t2,\frac t2\right),\qquad
Q=\left(\frac{1+t}{2},\frac{1-t}{2}\right).$$

The midpoint $R$ of $PQ$ is

$$R=\left(\frac{\frac t2+\frac{1+t}{2}}{2},\frac{\frac t2+\frac{1-t}{2}}{2}\right)
=\left(\frac{2t+1}{4},\frac14\right).$$

As $t\in(0,1)$, we obtain

$$x\in\left(\frac14,\frac34\right),\qquad y=\frac14.$$

Thus the locus is the open segment

$$\left\{(x,\tfrac14): \tfrac14<x<\tfrac34\right\}.$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The only delicate point in the argument is the division by $2t-1$ in the derivation of the line $MN$. This is valid only for $t\neq \tfrac12$, and the case $t=\tfrac12$ must be treated separately.

For $t=\tfrac12$, the coordinates of $N$ are computed directly from the circle equations:

$$N=\left(\frac12,\frac12\right),$$

so $MN$ is the vertical line $x=\tfrac12$. The candidate fixed point $S=(\tfrac12,-\tfrac12)$ lies on this line, so the fixed-point property extends to this degenerate configuration.

All algebraic manipulations in parts (a) and (c) are direct and reversible, and no additional exclusions of parameter values are required there.

## Alternative Approaches

A purely synthetic approach can be based on the fact that both $M$ and $N$ lie on circumcircles of squares, implying right isosceles angle constraints at $N$. These enforce a rigid angular relation between $AN$, $BN$, and the directions of the square diagonals, allowing the identification of $N$ with $AF\cap BC$ via rotational symmetry.

For part (b), one may also interpret the mapping $M \mapsto N$ as a projective transformation on the line $AB$, which sends lines $MN$ into a pencil of concurrent lines. The fixed point arises as the center of this pencil, independent of the parameter $t$.
