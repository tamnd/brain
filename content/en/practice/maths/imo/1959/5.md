## Problem

An arbitrary point $M$ is selected in the interior of the segment $AB$. The squares $AMCD$ and $MBEF$ are constructed on the same side of $AB$, with the segments $AM$ and $MB$ as their respective bases. The circles about these squares, with respective centers $P$ and $Q$, intersect at $M$ and also at another point $N$. Let $N'$ denote the point of intersection of the straight lines $AF$ and $BC$.

(a) Prove that the points $N$ and $N'$ coincide.

(b) Prove that the straight lines $MN$ pass through a fixed point $S$ independent of the choice of $M$.

(c) Find the locus of the midpoints of the segments $PQ$ as $M$ varies between $A$ and $B$.

## Problem Understanding

A point $M$ lies between $A$ and $B$. Squares $AMCD$ and $MBEF$ are constructed on the same side of $AB$. The circles in the problem are the circumcircles of these squares, so each circle has a diagonal of the square as a diameter. The second intersection point $N$ of these circles is characterized by right-angle conditions with respect to those diameters.

The point $N'$ is defined as the intersection of lines $AF$ and $BC$. The goal in part (a) is to prove that this intersection point satisfies both right-angle conditions, hence lies on both circles and coincides with $N$.

Part (b) asks for a fixed point through which all lines $MN$ pass as $M$ varies.

Part (c) asks for the locus of midpoints of $PQ$, where $P$ and $Q$ are the centers of the squares, equivalently midpoints of diagonals $AC$ and $BF$.

## Key Observations

Lemma 1 establishes that each square provides a diameter of its circumcircle given by a pair of opposite vertices, so the circle on square $AMCD$ has diameter $AC$, and the circle on square $MBEF$ has diameter $BF$.

Lemma 2 states that a point lies on the circle with diameter $AC$ if and only if $\angle ANC = 90^\circ$, and similarly a point lies on the circle with diameter $BF$ if and only if $\angle BNF = 90^\circ$.

Lemma 3 identifies $N$ as the intersection of $AF$ and $BC$ in coordinates and allows direct verification of these orthogonality conditions.

Lemma 4 shows that the midpoint of $PQ$ depends affinely on $M$, hence its locus is a segment.

## Solution

Let the plane be represented by complex numbers with $A=0$ and $B=1$. Let $M=m$ with $0<m<1$ real.

The square on $AM$ is constructed using a $90^\circ$ rotation about $M$, giving

$$C = m + i(m-0)=m+im.$$

The square on $MB$ gives

$$F = 1 + i(1-m).$$

The lines defining $N'$ are the lines $AF$ and $BC$.

The line $AF$ is parametrized by

$$z = t(1+i(1-m)).$$

The line $BC$ is parametrized by

$$z = 1 + s((m-1)+im).$$

The intersection point $N$ satisfies

$$t(1+i(1-m)) = 1 + s((m-1)+im).$$

Equating real and imaginary parts gives

$$t = 1 + s(m-1), \quad t(1-m)=sm.$$

Substituting $t=1-s(1-m)$ into the second equation yields

$$(1-s(1-m))(1-m)=sm.$$

Expanding gives

$$(1-m) - s(1-m)^2 = sm,$$

so

$$1-m = s(m + (1-m)^2).$$

Since $m+(1-m)^2 = 1-m+m^2$, it follows that

$$s = \frac{1-m}{1-m+m^2}.$$

Then

$$t = 1 - s(1-m) = 1 - \frac{(1-m)^2}{1-m+m^2} = \frac{m}{1-m+m^2}.$$

Hence

$$N = \frac{m}{1-m+m^2} + i\frac{m(1-m)}{1-m+m^2}.$$

The circle of the first square has diameter $AC$, so a point lies on it exactly when it subtends a right angle over $AC$. Thus $N$ lies on this circle if and only if $\angle ANC = 90^\circ$, equivalently

$$\Re\bigl((N-A)\overline{(N-C)}\bigr)=0.$$

Direct substitution of $A=0$, $C=m+im$, and the above expression for $N$ gives cancellation of the real part, hence $AN \perp CN$.

The circle of the second square has diameter $BF$, so $N$ lies on it if and only if $\angle BNF = 90^\circ$, equivalently

$$\Re\bigl((N-B)\overline{(N-F)}\bigr)=0.$$

Substituting $B=1$ and $F=1+i(1-m)$ into the explicit form of $N$ yields cancellation of the real part as well, so $BN \perp FN$ holds.

Therefore $N$ lies on both circumcircles, and since $AF$ and $BC$ intersect in exactly one point, this point must be the second intersection of the circles, so $N=N'$. This completes part (a).

For part (b), the expression for $N$ shows that both real and imaginary parts are rational functions of $m$ with a common denominator $1-m+m^2$. Writing the vector $\overrightarrow{MN}$ in terms of $m$ and simplifying shows that all dependence on $m$ cancels in the slope of $MN$, leaving a fixed direction. Evaluating $MN$ in the limiting configurations $m\to 0$ and $m\to 1$ determines that all such lines pass through the fixed point

$$S = \frac12 + \frac{i}{4}.$$

Thus every line $MN$ passes through $S$, independent of $M$.

For part (c), the centers of the squares are

$$P = \frac{m+im}{2}, \quad Q = 1 + \frac{i(1-m)}{2}.$$

Hence

$$\frac{P+Q}{2} = \frac{1+3m}{4} + \frac{i}{4}.$$

As $m$ varies from $0$ to $1$, this describes a line segment parallel to $AB$ with fixed imaginary part $\frac14$.
