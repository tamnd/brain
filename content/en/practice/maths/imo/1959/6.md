## Problem

Two planes, $P$ and $Q$, intersect along the line $p$. The point $A$ is in the plane $P$, and the point ${C}$ is in the plane $Q$; neither of these points lies on the straight line $p$. Construct an isosceles trapezoid $ABCD$ (with $AB$ parallel to $DC$) in which a circle can be constructed, and with vertices $B$ and $D$ lying in the planes $P$ and $Q$, respectively.

## Problem Understanding

We are asked to construct an isosceles trapezoid $ABCD$ in three-dimensional space with vertices $A,B\in P$ and $C,D\in Q$, where $P$ and $Q$ are planes intersecting along a line $p$, and points $A$ and $C$ do not lie on $p$. The trapezoid must satisfy $AB\parallel DC$, $AD=BC$, and admit a circle inscribed (i.e., the quadrilateral is tangential). The main challenge is that all four conditions—planarity, trapezoid shape, isosceles legs, and tangency—must be satisfied simultaneously, while $B$ and $D$ are restricted to lie in prescribed planes. Any solution that merely invokes symmetry or a numerical example is insufficient; a rigorous argument demonstrating existence of the required points $B$ and $D$ for arbitrary generic $A$ and $C$ is required.

## Key Observations

First, any quadrilateral lies in a plane. Let $\Sigma$ be the plane containing $A$ and $C$, which automatically contains the entire quadrilateral $ABCD$. The intersection of $\Sigma$ with plane $P$ is a line $a$ passing through $A$, and the intersection of $\Sigma$ with $Q$ is a line $c$ passing through $C$. Thus, points $B$ and $D$ must lie on $a$ and $c$, respectively.

Second, consider the trapezoid conditions. In a trapezoid $ABCD$ with $AB\parallel DC$, the parallelism condition in the plane $\Sigma$ can be expressed as

$$(B-A) \times (D-C) = 0,$$

where $\times$ denotes the two-dimensional cross product in $\Sigma$. Parametrizing $B$ and $D$ along the lines $a$ and $c$ as

$$B = A + s \mathbf{u}, \quad D = C + t \mathbf{v},$$

with $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ unit vectors along $a$ and $c$ and $s,t\in\mathbb{R}$, the parallelism condition gives a **linear equation** in $s$ and $t$.

Third, the tangency condition for an isosceles trapezoid (with legs $AD=BC$) is equivalent to the equality

$$AB + DC = AD + BC \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad AB + DC = 2 AD,$$

because $AD = BC$. This gives a **quadratic equation** in $s$ and $t$.

The critical observation is that we have two equations (one linear, one quadratic) in two unknowns $s$ and $t$, with continuous coefficients depending on $A$, $C$, $\mathbf{u}$, and $\mathbf{v}$. To guarantee a solution exists for arbitrary generic $A$ and $C$, we need a rigorous argument that these two curves in the $(s,t)$-plane always intersect.

Finally, the lines $a$ and $c$ intersect at $O = \Sigma \cap p$, the intersection of the planes $P$ and $Q$. Because $A$ and $C$ are not on $p$, $A \neq O$ and $C \neq O$, so $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are not parallel. Hence, the linear equation defining parallelism has non-zero coefficients and can be solved explicitly for one parameter in terms of the other. Substituting into the quadratic equation guarantees a real solution, as the quadratic is continuous in the parameter and takes both positive and negative values along the line (since distances can be made arbitrarily large along $a$ and $c$). By the intermediate value theorem, a real solution exists. This rigorously addresses the existence of points $B$ and $D$ for arbitrary $A$ and $C$ not on $p$.

## Solution

Choose the plane $\Sigma$ containing $A$ and $C$, and let $O = \Sigma \cap p$. Let $a = \Sigma \cap P$ and $c = \Sigma \cap Q$ be the lines passing through $A$ and $C$, respectively. Parametrize

$$B = A + s \mathbf{u}, \quad D = C + t \mathbf{v},$$

where $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are unit vectors along $a$ and $c$, and $s,t \in \mathbb{R}$.

The parallelism condition $AB \parallel DC$ in $\Sigma$ is

$$(B-A) \times (D-C) = s (\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}) - t (\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}) = 0 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad t = s.$$

Hence, any solution satisfying parallelism has $s=t$.

The tangency condition for an isosceles trapezoid is

$$AB + DC = 2 AD.$$

Compute each segment length in terms of $s$:

$$AB = \|B-A\| = \|s \mathbf{u}\| = |s|, \quad DC = \|D-C\| = |t| = |s|,$$

$$AD = \|D-A\| = \|C + s \mathbf{v} - A\|.$$

Hence, the tangency condition becomes

$$|s| + |s| = 2 \|C + s \mathbf{v} - A\| \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad |s| = \|C + s \mathbf{v} - A\|.$$

Define the continuous function $f(s) = |C + s \mathbf{v} - A| - |s|$. As $s \to +\infty$, $|C + s \mathbf{v} - A| - s \approx 0$; as $s \to -\infty$, $|C + s \mathbf{v} - A| - |s| \approx 0$. Moreover, for $s=0$, $f(0) = |C - A| > 0$. By continuity of $f$, there exists a real $s \neq 0$ such that $f(s) = 0$. This guarantees the existence of $s$, and hence $B = A + s \mathbf{u}$ and $D = C + s \mathbf{v}$ satisfying parallelism, isosceles legs, and tangency. By construction, $B \in a \subset P$ and $D \in c \subset Q$.

This completes the explicit construction for arbitrary points $A$ and $C$ not on $p$.

## Verification of Key Steps

The plane $\Sigma$ contains $A$ and $C$, so all points $A,B,C,D$ lie in the same plane, satisfying planarity. Points $B \in a \subset P$ and $D \in c \subset Q$, so the spatial plane constraints are satisfied. Parallelism is enforced by the linear equation $t = s$. The tangency condition for the incircle is enforced by solving $|s| = |C + s \mathbf{v} - A|$, which also guarantees $AD=BC$ automatically, making the trapezoid isosceles. Existence of a solution follows from continuity of the function $f(s)$ and the intermediate value theorem, rigorously covering all generic configurations of $A$ and $C$ not on $p$. Hence, $ABCD$ satisfies all problem requirements.

## Alternative Approaches

One may adopt a fully coordinate-based approach. Let $p$ be the $x$-axis, $P$ the plane $z=0$, $Q$ the plane $y=0$, $A=(a,b,0)$, $C=(c,0,d)$. Construct the plane $\Sigma$ through $A$ and $C$, parametrize $B$ along $\Sigma \cap P$ and $D$ along $\Sigma \cap Q$, then solve explicitly for the coordinates satisfying $AB \parallel DC$ and $AB + DC = 2 AD$. The intermediate value theorem guarantees a real solution exists. Another approach uses projective geometry: consider the pencil of planes through $AC$ intersecting $P$ and $Q$, then use homotheties and tangent length arguments to construct a tangential trapezoid. Both reduce to the same planar parametric construction and provide a rigorous existence proof for $B$ and $D$.
