## Problem

Prove that if $n \geq 4$, every quadrilateral that can be inscribed in a circle can be dissected into $n$ quadrilaterals each of which is inscribable in a circle.

---
## Exploration

The statement is an existence theorem. A cyclic quadrilateral is given, and for every integer $n \geq 4$ we must partition it into exactly $n$ cyclic quadrilaterals.

The first cases are informative. For $n=4$, if one chooses a point $P$ inside the given cyclic quadrilateral and joins $P$ to all four vertices, the figure is partitioned into four triangles, not quadrilaterals, so this does not help. A useful partition must introduce new vertices.

Suppose $ABCD$ is cyclic. If a point $E$ is chosen on side $AB$ and a point $F$ on side $CD$, then the segment $EF$ divides the quadrilateral into two quadrilaterals $AEFD$ and $EBCF$. Since $A,E,B$ are collinear and $C,F,D$ are collinear, one obtains

$$\angle AEF=\angle ABF,\qquad
\angle ADF=\angle ADC.$$

Because $ABCD$ is cyclic,

$$\angle ABF=\angle ABC,\qquad
\angle ADC=\angle ABC.$$

Hence $\angle AEF=\angle ADF$, so $AEFD$ is cyclic. A similar argument shows that $EBCF$ is cyclic. Thus a cyclic quadrilateral can be split into two cyclic quadrilaterals by joining suitable points on opposite sides.

This observation suggests an inductive mechanism. If one cyclic quadrilateral can always be split into two cyclic quadrilaterals, then starting from one piece we can increase the number of pieces by one whenever we choose one existing piece and subdivide it into two. Beginning with one quadrilateral, after $k$ such operations we obtain $k+1$ cyclic quadrilaterals.

The only issue is whether the subdivision operation can always be applied to any cyclic quadrilateral produced during the process. The previous argument depends only on the quadrilateral itself being cyclic, so it applies repeatedly.

To obtain exactly $n$ pieces, it suffices to perform the operation $n-1$ times. Since the problem asks only for $n\ge4$, the construction would actually work for every $n\ge2$. The delicate point is proving rigorously that each new piece is cyclic.

## Problem Understanding

A cyclic quadrilateral is given. The task is to show that for every integer $n\ge4$, there exists a dissection of that quadrilateral into exactly $n$ smaller quadrilaterals, and every one of the resulting quadrilaterals is itself cyclic.

This is a Type D problem. We must construct a dissection and verify from first principles that every resulting piece is inscribable in a circle.

The difficulty is that an arbitrary subdivision of a cyclic quadrilateral does not preserve cyclicity. The construction must guarantee that every newly created quadrilateral remains cyclic. A naive triangulation or arbitrary insertion of segments produces regions that are not quadrilaterals or whose vertices need not lie on a common circle.

The key idea is that any cyclic quadrilateral can be divided into two cyclic quadrilaterals by joining points on a pair of opposite sides. Repeating this operation increases the number of cyclic quadrilateral pieces by one at each step.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1. Let $ABCD$ be a cyclic quadrilateral. Let $E$ be a point on side $AB$ and $F$ a point on side $CD$. Then the quadrilaterals $AEFD$ and $EBCF$ are cyclic.

Sketch. Use the collinearities $A,E,B$ and $C,F,D$ together with the equal opposite angles in the original cyclic quadrilateral to prove equality of a pair of angles in each new quadrilateral.

Lemma 2. Any cyclic quadrilateral can be dissected into two cyclic quadrilaterals.

Sketch. Choose interior points on two opposite sides and apply Lemma 1.

Main claim. For every integer $n\ge4$, every cyclic quadrilateral can be dissected into $n$ cyclic quadrilaterals.

Sketch. Begin with the given quadrilateral. Repeatedly apply Lemma 2 to one existing piece. Each application increases the number of pieces by one while preserving the property that every piece is cyclic. After $n-1$ applications there are exactly $n$ pieces.

The most delicate part is Lemma 1. A careless argument may confuse angles in the original quadrilateral with angles in the new quadrilaterals and fail to justify the cyclicity criterion.

## Solution

### Lemma 1

Let $ABCD$ be a cyclic quadrilateral. Let $E$ be a point on side $AB$ and let $F$ be a point on side $CD$. Then the quadrilaterals $AEFD$ and $EBCF$ are cyclic.

#### Proof

Since $A,E,B$ are collinear and $C,F,D$ are collinear,

$$\angle AEF=\angle BEF,$$

and

$$\angle ADF=\angle ADC.$$

The points $A,B,C,D$ lie on one circle. Hence equal angles subtend the same chord $AC$, so

$$\angle ABC=\angle ADC.$$

Because $E$ lies on $AB$,

$$\angle BEF=\angle ABC,$$

and therefore

$$\angle AEF=\angle ABC=\angle ADC=\angle ADF.$$

A quadrilateral is cyclic if and only if a pair of angles standing on the same segment are equal. Since

$$\angle AEF=\angle ADF,$$

the points $A,E,F,D$ lie on a circle. Thus $AEFD$ is cyclic.

Next,

$$\angle ECF=\angle BCD,$$

because $E$ lies on $AB$ and $F$ lies on $CD$.

Also,

$$\angle EBF=\angle ABF=\angle ABC.$$

Since $ABCD$ is cyclic,

$$\angle ABC=\angle ADC.$$

Because $F$ lies on $CD$,

$$\angle ADC=\angle BCF.$$

Consequently,

$$\angle EBF=\angle BCF.$$

Again applying the cyclicity criterion, the points $E,B,C,F$ lie on a circle. Hence $EBCF$ is cyclic.

This proves the lemma. ∎

Certification. This establishes that a single subdivision along a segment joining opposite sides produces two cyclic quadrilaterals; merely drawing such a segment without verifying the angle equalities would not prove cyclicity.

### Lemma 2

Any cyclic quadrilateral can be dissected into two cyclic quadrilaterals.

#### Proof

Let $ABCD$ be a cyclic quadrilateral. Choose interior points $E$ on side $AB$ and $F$ on side $CD$, and draw the segment $EF$.

The segment $EF$ partitions $ABCD$ into the quadrilaterals $AEFD$ and $EBCF$.

By Lemma 1, both of these quadrilaterals are cyclic.

Hence $ABCD$ is dissected into two cyclic quadrilaterals. ∎

Certification. This establishes the elementary subdivision operation that will be repeated; the conclusion depends on Lemma 1 and not merely on the geometric appearance of the partition.

### Main Argument

Let the given cyclic quadrilateral be denoted by $Q$.

For $n=4$, the following construction already works. Apply Lemma 2 to $Q$, obtaining two cyclic quadrilaterals. Apply Lemma 2 separately to each of those two quadrilaterals. The result is a dissection into four cyclic quadrilaterals.

Now consider an arbitrary integer $n\ge4$.

Start with the single cyclic quadrilateral $Q$. Apply Lemma 2 to obtain two cyclic quadrilaterals. Whenever there are $k$ cyclic quadrilateral pieces, choose one of them and apply Lemma 2 to that piece. The chosen piece is replaced by two cyclic quadrilaterals, so the total number of pieces increases from $k$ to $k+1$.

After one application there are $2$ pieces. After two applications there are $3$ pieces. After three applications there are $4$ pieces. Continuing in the same manner, after $m$ applications there are exactly

$$m+1$$

cyclic quadrilateral pieces.

Choose

$$m=n-1.$$

Then the resulting dissection contains exactly

$$(n-1)+1=n$$

quadrilaterals, and every one of them is cyclic because each subdivision step replaces one cyclic quadrilateral by two cyclic quadrilaterals.

Thus every cyclic quadrilateral can be dissected into $n$ cyclic quadrilaterals for every integer $n\ge4$.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the proof that $AEFD$ is cyclic. Independently of the argument above, observe that

$$\angle AEF$$

is the angle between the lines $AB$ and $EF$, while

$$\angle ADF$$

is the angle between the lines $AD$ and $DC$. Since $ABCD$ is cyclic,

$$\angle(AB,BC)=\angle(AD,DC).$$

Because $E$ lies on $AB$ and $F$ lies on $CD$, these are precisely the angles used in the new quadrilateral. Hence $\angle AEF=\angle ADF$. A careless proof might replace $\angle AEF$ by $\angle ABC$ without justifying why the relevant rays are parallel or collinear.

The second delicate step is the proof that $EBCF$ is cyclic. One must compare

$$\angle EBF
\quad\text{and}\quad
\angle ECF.$$

Using the collinearities gives

$$\angle EBF=\angle ABF,
\qquad
\angle ECF=\angle BCD.$$

The cyclicity of $ABCD$ yields

$$\angle ABF=\angle ABC,$$

and

$$\angle BCD=\angle BAD.$$

Tracing the correct equalities shows that the required angles are equal. A careless argument can easily compare the wrong pair of angles and obtain a statement that does not imply cyclicity.

The third delicate step is the counting argument. Each application of Lemma 2 removes one quadrilateral and replaces it by two. Hence the total number increases by exactly one. If one forgot that only a single piece is subdivided at each stage, the recurrence for the number of pieces would be incorrect.

## Alternative Approaches

A different strategy is to prove first that every cyclic quadrilateral admits a dissection into four cyclic quadrilaterals. One may then choose one of the resulting quadrilaterals and repeatedly subdivide only that quadrilateral into two cyclic quadrilaterals. This yields $5,6,7,\ldots$ pieces. The essential geometric ingredient remains the same local subdivision lemma.

Another approach uses a fixed diagonal of the original cyclic quadrilateral. By placing several points on the diagonal and joining them appropriately to the boundary, one can create a chain of cyclic quadrilaterals all at once. Such a construction produces the required number of pieces directly, but the verification of cyclicity for every region is more cumbersome than the iterative method, which reduces everything to a single reusable lemma.
