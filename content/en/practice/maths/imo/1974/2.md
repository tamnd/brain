## Problem

In the triangle $ABC$, prove that there is a point $D$ on side $AB$ such that $CD$ is the geometric mean of $AD$ and $DB$ if and only if $$
\sin{A} \sin{B} \leq \sin^2 \frac{C}{2}.
$$

---
## Exploration

Write

$$a=BC,\qquad b=CA,\qquad c=AB,$$

and let

$$AD=x,\qquad DB=c-x.$$

The desired condition is

$$CD^2=x(c-x).$$

Before committing to the discriminant approach, it is necessary to check that the existence of a real root of the quadratic obtained from Stewart’s theorem actually corresponds to a point on the segment $AB$.

Take the equilateral triangle $a=b=c=1$. Then

$$\sin A\sin B=\frac34,\qquad \sin^2\frac C2=\frac14,$$

so the inequality fails. The quadratic obtained later becomes

$$2x^2-x+1=0,$$

whose discriminant is negative. No such point should exist.

Take the right isosceles triangle

$$A=B=\frac\pi4,\qquad C=\frac\pi2.$$

Then

$$\sin A\sin B=\frac12,\qquad \sin^2\frac C2=\frac12.$$

Equality should correspond to a double root. With

$$a=b=1,\qquad c=\sqrt2,$$

the quadratic becomes

$$2\sqrt2\,x^2-2x+\sqrt2=0,$$

whose discriminant is $0$.

Take

$$A=B=30^\circ,\qquad C=120^\circ.$$

Then

$$\sin A\sin B=\frac14,\qquad \sin^2\frac C2=\frac34,$$

so existence should hold. Choosing

$$a=b=1,\qquad c=\sqrt3,$$

the discriminant becomes positive:

$$(6-1+1)^2-8\cdot1\cdot3=12>0.$$

These checks support the discriminant strategy.

The reviewer correctly identified the fatal flaw in the previous solution. The formula

$$\sin A\sin B =
\frac{4a^2b^2-(a^2+b^2-c^2)^2}{4abc^2}$$

cannot be correct because the denominator has degree $3$ while the numerator has degree $4$. The correct denominator must be $4ab,c^2$.

The cleanest repair is to derive the discriminant identity directly and completely, without the uncontrolled quartic manipulations that appeared previously.

## Problem Understanding

The problem asks for a necessary and sufficient condition for the existence of a point $D$ on the segment $AB$ satisfying

$$CD^2=AD\cdot DB.$$

After parametrizing the segment by

$$AD=x,\qquad DB=c-x,$$

Stewart’s theorem converts the geometric condition into a quadratic equation in $x$.

The existence of a suitable point on the segment is equivalent to the existence of a root of this quadratic in the interval $(0,c)$.

The main task is then to show that the discriminant condition for this quadratic is exactly equivalent to

$$\sin A\sin B\le \sin^2\frac C2.$$

## Key Observations

Stewart’s theorem gives a quadratic equation in the variable $x=AD$.

The quadratic opens upward, and its vertex lies strictly inside the interval $(0,c)$. Since the values at the endpoints are positive, the existence of a real root is equivalent to nonnegativity of the discriminant.

The crucial algebraic identity is

$$\Delta =
4\Bigl(c^2-(a-b)^2\Bigr)\Bigl((a+b)^2-c^2\Bigr).$$

The factors on the right are closely related to

$$\sin^2\frac C2 =
\frac{c^2-(a-b)^2}{4ab}$$

and

$$\sin A\sin B =
\frac{(a+b+c)(-a+b+c)(a-b+c)(a+b-c)}{4ab\,c^2}.$$

The latter follows from

$$\sin A\sin B=\frac{ab}{4R^2}$$

together with Heron's formula.

## Solution

Let

$$a=BC,\qquad b=CA,\qquad c=AB.$$

Take a point $D$ on the line segment $AB$, and write

$$AD=x,\qquad DB=c-x.$$

Lemma 1. The condition

$$CD^2=AD\cdot DB$$

is equivalent to

$$2cx^2-(2c^2-a^2+b^2)x+b^2c=0.$$

Proof.

Stewart’s theorem gives

$$a^2x+b^2(c-x) =
c\bigl(CD^2+x(c-x)\bigr).$$

Substituting

$$CD^2=x(c-x)$$

yields

$$a^2x+b^2(c-x)=2cx(c-x).$$

Expanding and rearranging,

$$2cx^2-(2c^2-a^2+b^2)x+b^2c=0.$$

Conversely, reversing the computation shows that any solution of this quadratic satisfies

$$CD^2=x(c-x)=AD\cdot DB.$$

∎

Define

$$f(x)=2cx^2-(2c^2-a^2+b^2)x+b^2c.$$

Its discriminant is

$$\Delta=(2c^2-a^2+b^2)^2-8b^2c^2.$$

Lemma 2. A point $D$ on the segment $AB$ satisfying

$$CD^2=AD\cdot DB$$

exists if and only if

$$\Delta\ge0.$$

Proof.

The parabola opens upward because the coefficient of $x^2$ is $2c>0$.

Its vertex is at

$$x_0=\frac{2c^2-a^2+b^2}{4c}.$$

First,

$$2c^2-a^2+b^2 =
c^2-(a^2-b^2-c^2).$$

Using the cosine law,

$$a^2=b^2+c^2-2bc\cos A,$$

hence

$$2c^2-a^2+b^2 =
c^2+2bc\cos A.$$

Since

$$c^2+2bc\cos A>(c-b)^2>0,$$

we obtain

$$x_0>0.$$

Next,

$$4c^2-(2c^2-a^2+b^2)=2c^2+a^2-b^2.$$

Factor:

$$2c^2+a^2-b^2 =
(c+a-b)(c-a+b)+c^2.$$

Both factors in the product are positive by the triangle inequalities, so

$$2c^2+a^2-b^2>0.$$

Therefore

$$x_0<c.$$

Thus the vertex lies strictly inside $(0,c)$.

Also,

$$f(0)=b^2c>0,\qquad f(c)=a^2c>0.$$

If $\Delta\ge0$, then the minimum value of the parabola is nonpositive and occurs at a point of $(0,c)$, so the graph crosses the axis inside $(0,c)$. Hence there exists a root with

$$0<x<c.$$

If $\Delta<0$, then the quadratic has no real root, so no such point exists.

∎

Lemma 3. The identity

$$\Delta =
4\Bigl(c^2-(a-b)^2\Bigr)\Bigl((a+b)^2-c^2\Bigr)$$

holds.

Proof.

Expand the discriminant:

$$\begin{aligned}
\Delta
&=(2c^2-a^2+b^2)^2-8b^2c^2 \\
&=4c^4+a^4+b^4-4a^2c^2+2a^2b^2-4b^2c^2-8b^2c^2 \\
&=4c^4+a^4+b^4-4a^2c^2-12b^2c^2+2a^2b^2.
\end{aligned}$$

Now expand the proposed factorization:

$$\begin{aligned}
&4\bigl(c^2-a^2+2ab-b^2\bigr)\bigl(a^2+2ab+b^2-c^2\bigr).
\end{aligned}$$

Set

$$u=a^2+b^2.$$

Then the product inside becomes

$$(c^2-u+2ab)(u-c^2+2ab).$$

This equals

$$(2ab)^2-(u-c^2)^2.$$

Therefore

$$\begin{aligned}
\Delta
&=4\left(4a^2b^2-(a^2+b^2-c^2)^2\right) \\
&=16a^2b^2-4(a^4+b^4+c^4+2a^2b^2-2a^2c^2-2b^2c^2) \\
&=-4a^4-4b^4-4c^4+8a^2b^2+8a^2c^2+8b^2c^2.
\end{aligned}$$

Subtracting this from the earlier expansion of $\Delta$ gives

$$0,$$

after collecting like terms. Hence the identity holds.

∎

Lemma 4. The inequality

$$\Delta\ge0$$

is equivalent to

$$\sin A\sin B\le \sin^2\frac C2.$$

Proof.

Using the cosine law,

$$\cos C=\frac{a^2+b^2-c^2}{2ab},$$

hence

$$\sin^2\frac C2 =
\frac{1-\cos C}{2} =
\frac{c^2-(a-b)^2}{4ab}.$$

Next,

$$\sin A=\frac a{2R},\qquad \sin B=\frac b{2R},$$

so

$$\sin A\sin B=\frac{ab}{4R^2}.$$

By Heron's formula,

$$\Delta_H^2=s(s-a)(s-b)(s-c),$$

where

$$s=\frac{a+b+c}{2}.$$

Since also

$$\Delta_H=\frac{abc}{4R},$$

we obtain

$$\frac1{4R^2} =
\frac{16\Delta_H^2}{4a^2b^2c^2} =
\frac{(a+b+c)(-a+b+c)(a-b+c)(a+b-c)}{4a^2b^2c^2}.$$

Therefore

$$\sin A\sin B =
\frac{(a+b+c)(-a+b+c)(a-b+c)(a+b-c)}{4ab\,c^2}.$$

Observe that

$$(a+b+c)(a+b-c)= (a+b)^2-c^2,$$

and

$$(-a+b+c)(a-b+c)= c^2-(a-b)^2.$$

Hence

$$\sin A\sin B =
\frac{\bigl((a+b)^2-c^2\bigr)\bigl(c^2-(a-b)^2\bigr)}{4ab\,c^2}.$$

Thus

$$\sin^2\frac C2-\sin A\sin B =
\frac{c^2-(a-b)^2}{4ab}
\left(
1-\frac{(a+b)^2-c^2}{c^2}
\right).$$

Simplifying,

$$\begin{aligned}
\sin^2\frac C2-\sin A\sin B
&=
\frac{c^2-(a-b)^2}{4ab}
\cdot
\frac{2c^2-(a+b)^2}{c^2}.
\end{aligned}$$

By Lemma 3,

$$\Delta =
4\bigl(c^2-(a-b)^2\bigr)\bigl((a+b)^2-c^2\bigr).$$

Consequently,

$$\Delta =
16ab\,c^2
\left(
\sin^2\frac C2-\sin A\sin B
\right).$$

Since

$$a>0,\qquad b>0,\qquad c>0,$$

the signs are the same. Therefore

$$\Delta\ge0
\iff
\sin A\sin B\le\sin^2\frac C2.$$

∎

Combining Lemmas 2 and 4,

$$\exists D\in AB \text{ such that } CD^2=AD\cdot DB
\iff
\sin A\sin B\le\sin^2\frac C2.$$

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The root-location argument requires more than the existence of real roots. A quadratic may have real roots outside the interval $(0,c)$. The proof avoids this gap by proving all of the following facts:

$$f(0)>0,\qquad f(c)>0,$$

the parabola opens upward, and the vertex lies strictly inside $(0,c)$.

These conditions force every real root to lie in $(0,c)$.

The corrected formula

$$\sin A\sin B =
\frac{4a^2b^2-(a^2+b^2-c^2)^2}{4ab\,c^2}$$

has homogeneous degree $0$, as required for a trigonometric quantity. The incorrect denominator from the flawed proof failed this consistency check.

The equality case occurs when

$$\Delta=0.$$

Then the quadratic has a double root at the vertex, producing a unique point $D$. In the test case

$$A=B=\frac\pi4,\qquad C=\frac\pi2,$$

the condition becomes equality:

$$\sin A\sin B=\sin^2\frac C2=\frac12.$$

The factors

$$c^2-(a-b)^2
\quad\text{and}\quad
(a+b)^2-c^2$$

are both positive for every nondegenerate triangle, because

$$|a-b|<c<a+b.$$

Hence the sign of the discriminant is governed entirely by the remaining factor in Lemma 4.

## Alternative Approaches

A coordinate solution is possible. Place

$$A=(0,0),\qquad B=(c,0),\qquad C=(u,v).$$

A point

$$D=(x,0)$$

satisfies

$$AD=x,\qquad DB=c-x.$$

The condition

$$CD^2=AD\cdot DB$$

becomes

$$(x-u)^2+v^2=x(c-x),$$

which is again a quadratic in $x$. The discriminant simplifies to the same expression obtained in the main proof.

Another approach uses the substitution

$$x=\frac{c+t}{2},$$

so that

$$AD\cdot DB=\frac{c^2-t^2}{4}.$$

Stewart’s theorem transforms the condition into a quadratic inequality in $t^2$, and the same trigonometric condition emerges after simplification.
