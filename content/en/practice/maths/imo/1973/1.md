## Problem

Point $O$ lies on line $g$; $\overrightarrow{OP_1}, \overrightarrow{OP_2}, \ldots, \overrightarrow{OP_n}$ are unit vectors such that points $P_1, P_2, \ldots, P_n$ all lie in a plane containing $g$ and on one side of $g$. Prove that if $n$ is odd, $$
\left\vert \overrightarrow{OP_1} + \overrightarrow{OP_2} + \ldots + \overrightarrow{OP_n} \right\vert \geq 1
$$ Here $\left\vert \overrightarrow{OM} \right\vert$ denotes the length of vector $\overrightarrow{OM}$.

---
## Exploration

For $n=1$, the statement is immediate because the sum is a unit vector. For $n=3$, take three unit vectors making angles $10^\circ,20^\circ,30^\circ$ with the positive direction of $g$. The sum points roughly in the same direction and has length much larger than $1$. To test sharpness, take vectors with angles $0^\circ,120^\circ,240^\circ$. Their sum is $0$, but these vectors are not all on the same side of a line. Hence the geometric restriction is essential.

The condition “all points lie on one side of $g$” means that all vectors make angles belonging to some interval of length $\pi$. Since the vectors are unit vectors, writing them as $(\cos \theta_i,\sin \theta_i)$ relative to coordinates adapted to $g$ seems natural. The second coordinates all have the same sign. A direct estimate on the Euclidean norm,

$$\left(\sum \cos\theta_i\right)^2+\left(\sum \sin\theta_i\right)^2,$$

does not immediately produce a lower bound of $1$.

Another possibility is to project onto a carefully chosen direction. If all vectors lie in a closed half-plane, then there exists a direction $u$ such that every scalar product $\overrightarrow{OP_i}\cdot u$ is nonnegative. If one could show that the sum of these projections is at least $1$, then the vector norm would also be at least $1$. The oddness of $n$ suggests pairing vectors symmetrically around a median direction.

Arrange the vectors by increasing angle:

$$0\le \theta_1\le \theta_2\le \cdots \le \theta_n\le \pi.$$

Since $n$ is odd, there is a middle vector, namely $\overrightarrow{OP_m}$ with $m=\frac{n+1}{2}$. Let its direction be $\theta_m$. Then for every $i$,

$$|\theta_i-\theta_m|\le \frac{\pi}{2},$$

because all angles lie inside an interval of length $\pi$. Hence

$$\cos(\theta_i-\theta_m)\ge 0.$$

Projecting the total sum onto the direction $\theta_m$ yields

$$\sum_{i=1}^n \cos(\theta_i-\theta_m).$$

The middle term contributes $1$, and all others contribute nonnegative quantities. Thus the projection is at least $1$, forcing the norm to be at least $1$.

The delicate point is proving rigorously that

$$|\theta_i-\theta_m|\le \frac{\pi}{2}$$

for every $i$. A careless argument could fail if the interval of allowed angles were not normalized correctly.

## Problem Understanding

We are given an odd number of unit vectors in a plane. All endpoints lie in the same half-plane determined by a line $g$ passing through the common origin $O$. The task is to prove that the vector sum cannot have length smaller than $1$.

This is a Type B problem. The statement is already specified, and the goal is to prove it rigorously.

The objects involved are vectors in a plane, their directions, and the Euclidean norm. The geometric condition constrains all directions to lie inside some interval of angular width at most $\pi$. The difficulty is that vector addition permits substantial cancellation, and without the half-plane condition the sum may even vanish. A naive coordinate computation leads to complicated trigonometric expressions with many cross terms. The core idea is instead to choose a special direction and estimate a projection of the total sum.

The oddness of $n$ strongly suggests selecting a middle direction among the ordered vectors. Relative to that direction, every other vector makes an angle at most $\frac{\pi}{2}$, so every projection onto the middle direction is nonnegative. The middle vector itself contributes exactly $1$.

## Proof Architecture

The proof will use two lemmas.

### Lemma 1

After choosing coordinates so that $g$ is the $x$-axis and the vectors lie in the upper half-plane, the direction angles satisfy

$$0\le \theta_1\le \theta_2\le \cdots \le \theta_n\le \pi.$$

If $m=\frac{n+1}{2}$, then

$$|\theta_i-\theta_m|\le \frac{\pi}{2}$$

for every $i$.

The reason is that at most $m-1$ vectors lie below $\theta_m$ and at most $m-1$ lie above it, while the total angular width is at most $\pi$.

### Lemma 2

Let $u$ be the unit vector in direction $\theta_m$. Then the scalar projection of the total vector sum onto $u$ equals

$$\sum_{i=1}^n \cos(\theta_i-\theta_m),$$

and every term in this sum is nonnegative, with the middle term equal to $1$.

The reason is the identity

$$(\cos\alpha,\sin\alpha)\cdot(\cos\beta,\sin\beta)=\cos(\alpha-\beta).$$

The hardest part is ensuring that every angle difference from the median direction is at most $\frac{\pi}{2}$. Any hidden mistake there would invalidate the sign of the cosine terms.

## Solution

Choose Cartesian coordinates in the plane so that the line $g$ is the $x$-axis and all points $P_1,\dots,P_n$ lie in the closed upper half-plane. Since each vector $\overrightarrow{OP_i}$ has length $1$, there exists an angle $\theta_i\in[0,\pi]$ such that

$$\overrightarrow{OP_i}=(\cos\theta_i,\sin\theta_i).$$

Reorder the vectors so that

$$0\le \theta_1\le \theta_2\le \cdots \le \theta_n\le \pi.$$

Since $n$ is odd, there exists an integer

$$m=\frac{n+1}{2}.$$

Consider the middle angle $\theta_m$.

### Lemma 1

For every $i\in{1,\dots,n}$,

$$|\theta_i-\theta_m|\le \frac{\pi}{2}.$$

Proof.

Suppose first that $i\ge m$. Then

$$0\le \theta_i-\theta_m\le \theta_n-\theta_m.$$

Because $\theta_m\ge \theta_1$,

$$\theta_n-\theta_m\le \theta_n-\theta_1.$$

Since all angles lie in $[0,\pi]$,

$$\theta_n-\theta_1\le \pi.$$

Now there are exactly

$$n-m=\frac{n-1}{2}$$

indices strictly larger than $m$, and exactly

$$m-1=\frac{n-1}{2}$$

indices strictly smaller than $m$. Thus $\theta_m$ is a median angle. Consequently,

$$\theta_m-\theta_1\le \frac{\theta_n-\theta_1}{2},
\qquad
\theta_n-\theta_m\le \frac{\theta_n-\theta_1}{2}.$$

Combining this with $\theta_n-\theta_1\le \pi$ yields

$$\theta_n-\theta_m\le \frac{\pi}{2}.$$

Hence

$$0\le \theta_i-\theta_m\le \frac{\pi}{2}.$$

The case $i\le m$ is analogous:

$$0\le \theta_m-\theta_i\le \theta_m-\theta_1\le \frac{\pi}{2}.$$

Therefore

$$|\theta_i-\theta_m|\le \frac{\pi}{2}$$

for all $i$. ∎

This establishes that every vector forms an angle at most $\frac{\pi}{2}$ with the middle vector; omitting the median argument would leave open the possibility of an obtuse angle.

### Lemma 2

Let

$$u=(\cos\theta_m,\sin\theta_m).$$

Then

$$\left(\sum_{i=1}^n \overrightarrow{OP_i}\right)\cdot u
=
\sum_{i=1}^n \cos(\theta_i-\theta_m)\ge 1.$$

Proof.

Using the coordinate expressions,

$$\overrightarrow{OP_i}\cdot u
=
(\cos\theta_i,\sin\theta_i)\cdot(\cos\theta_m,\sin\theta_m).$$

By the trigonometric identity

$$\cos A\cos B+\sin A\sin B=\cos(A-B),$$

this becomes

$$\overrightarrow{OP_i}\cdot u
=
\cos(\theta_i-\theta_m).$$

Therefore

$$\left(\sum_{i=1}^n \overrightarrow{OP_i}\right)\cdot u
=
\sum_{i=1}^n \cos(\theta_i-\theta_m).$$

By Lemma 1,

$$|\theta_i-\theta_m|\le \frac{\pi}{2},$$

hence

$$\cos(\theta_i-\theta_m)\ge 0$$

for every $i$. When $i=m$,

$$\cos(\theta_m-\theta_m)=1.$$

Thus the entire sum is at least $1$:

$$\sum_{i=1}^n \cos(\theta_i-\theta_m)\ge 1.$$

∎

This establishes a lower bound for the projection of the total vector sum onto the median direction; replacing the projection argument by coordinate expansion would obscure the positivity structure.

Let

$$S=\sum_{i=1}^n \overrightarrow{OP_i}.$$

Since $u$ is a unit vector,

$$S\cdot u\le |S|\,|u|=|S|.$$

Lemma 2 gives

$$S\cdot u\ge 1.$$

Hence

$$|S|\ge 1.$$

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the claim

$$|\theta_i-\theta_m|\le \frac{\pi}{2}.$$

Derive it independently. Since all angles lie in an interval of length at most $\pi$,

$$\theta_n-\theta_1\le \pi.$$

The middle angle $\theta_m$ divides the ordered sequence into two groups of equal size. Hence the larger of the two quantities

$$\theta_m-\theta_1,
\qquad
\theta_n-\theta_m$$

cannot exceed half of the total span. Therefore each is at most $\frac{\pi}{2}$. A careless argument might assume directly that every angle is within $\frac{\pi}{2}$ of the median without using the bound on the total span.

The second delicate step is converting the projection into cosine terms. Starting from

$$(\cos\alpha,\sin\alpha)\cdot(\cos\beta,\sin\beta),$$

expand directly:

$$\cos\alpha\cos\beta+\sin\alpha\sin\beta.$$

The cosine subtraction identity gives

$$\cos(\alpha-\beta).$$

A sign mistake here would reverse the angle difference and could incorrectly produce $\cos(\alpha+\beta)$.

The final delicate step is the inequality

$$S\cdot u\le |S|.$$

This is the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality:

$$|S\cdot u|\le |S||u|.$$

Since $|u|=1$,

$$S\cdot u\le |S|.$$

Using only $S\cdot u\ge 1$ without this comparison would not connect the projection estimate to the norm of $S$.

## Alternative Approaches

A different proof uses complex numbers. Represent the vectors by unit complex numbers

$$z_i=e^{i\theta_i},
\qquad
0\le \theta_i\le \pi.$$

Let $\theta_m$ be the median angle and multiply the whole sum by $e^{-i\theta_m}$. Then

$$e^{-i\theta_m}\sum_{i=1}^n z_i
=
\sum_{i=1}^n e^{i(\theta_i-\theta_m)}.$$

The real part equals

$$\sum_{i=1}^n \cos(\theta_i-\theta_m),$$

which is at least $1$. Since the modulus of a complex number is at least the absolute value of its real part,

$$\left|\sum_{i=1}^n z_i\right|\ge 1.$$

Another approach uses convex geometry. The supporting line orthogonal to the median vector defines a half-plane containing all vectors. The orthogonal projection of every vector onto the median direction is nonnegative, while the median vector itself contributes projection $1$. Summing projections yields the same lower bound. The coordinate-trigonometric proof is preferable because every step can be written explicitly and checked algebraically.
