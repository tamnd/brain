## Problem

Point $O$ lies on line $g$; $\overrightarrow{OP_1}, \overrightarrow{OP_2}, \ldots, \overrightarrow{OP_n}$ are unit vectors such that points $P_1, P_2, \ldots, P_n$ all lie in a plane containing $g$ and on one side of $g$. Prove that if $n$ is odd, $$
\left\vert \overrightarrow{OP_1} + \overrightarrow{OP_2} + \ldots + \overrightarrow{OP_n} \right\vert \geq 1
$$ Here $\left\vert \overrightarrow{OM} \right\vert$ denotes the length of vector $\overrightarrow{OM}$.

---
## Exploration

Consider a set of unit vectors $\overrightarrow{OP_1}, \ldots, \overrightarrow{OP_n}$ all lying in a plane and positioned on one side of a line $g$ through $O$. Since $n$ is odd, small cases suggest a symmetry argument: with $n=1$, the vector sum has length $1$, with $n=3$, if all vectors lie in the same half-plane, the triangle inequality implies the sum cannot collapse to a length below $1$. Attempting to place three unit vectors in a semicircle shows that the minimal resultant occurs when they are collinear or nearly collinear along the boundary, suggesting that the one-sidedness prevents cancellation below $1$. A naive attempt to distribute vectors in an arc slightly less than $180^\circ$ shows that odd numbers prevent perfect cancellation: the “central” vector always leaves a net component along the direction normal to $g$. Likely, the key lemma will assert that for any odd collection of unit vectors in a half-plane relative to a line through the origin, the projection of the sum along a suitable direction is at least $1$.

## Problem Understanding

We are asked to prove a lower bound on the magnitude of the sum of an odd number of unit vectors that lie in a common plane on one side of a given line. This is a Type B problem: a proof is required; the statement is already given, and no extremum or classification is requested. The vectors are geometric objects in $\mathbb{R}^2$ (without loss of generality, since the problem is planar), and the claim is that their vector sum cannot be shorter than $1$. The difficulty is that vectors can point in many directions, and one must argue globally rather than by individual case checking; a naive use of the triangle inequality is insufficient because oddness is critical and simple vector cancellation is constrained by the one-sidedness relative to the line.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1: For a set of unit vectors in a plane, the magnitude of their sum is at least the absolute value of the sum of their projections along any fixed direction. This is true because the projection along a unit vector is a linear functional, and taking absolute values yields a lower bound.

Lemma 2: If $n$ is odd, then among the projections of $n$ unit vectors in a half-plane defined by a line through $O$, at least one projection has the same sign as the median projection. This follows from the fact that an odd number of real numbers cannot all cancel each other symmetrically around zero.

Lemma 3: With all unit vectors on one side of $g$, the sum of projections along the direction perpendicular to $g$ has absolute value at least $1$. This is justified by ordering vectors according to angles relative to $g$ and applying Lemma 2.

The main proof uses Lemmas 1 through 3 to show that the magnitude of the sum of vectors is bounded below by the sum of their perpendicular projections, which is at least $1$. The most delicate step is Lemma 3, where the oddness of $n$ ensures that cancellation cannot drop the sum below $1$.

## Solution

Let the plane containing all points $P_1, \ldots, P_n$ be identified with $\mathbb{R}^2$, and let line $g$ pass through the origin $O$. Introduce coordinates such that $g$ is the $x$-axis and the half-plane containing all points $P_i$ is the set $y \ge 0$. For each $i$, denote the unit vector $\overrightarrow{OP_i}$ in coordinates as $(\cos \theta_i, \sin \theta_i)$ with $0 \le \theta_i \le \pi$.

Lemma 1: Let $\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_n$ be vectors in $\mathbb{R}^2$, and let $\mathbf{u}$ be a unit vector. Then

$$\left|\sum_{i=1}^n \mathbf{v}_i\right| \ge \left|\sum_{i=1}^n (\mathbf{v}_i \cdot \mathbf{u})\right|.$$

Proof of Lemma 1: The Cauchy-Schwarz inequality implies

$$\left|\sum_{i=1}^n \mathbf{v}_i\right| = \left| \sum_{i=1}^n (\mathbf{v}_i \cdot \mathbf{u}) \mathbf{u} + \sum_{i=1}^n (\mathbf{v}_i - (\mathbf{v}_i \cdot \mathbf{u}) \mathbf{u}) \right| \ge \left| \sum_{i=1}^n (\mathbf{v}_i \cdot \mathbf{u}) \mathbf{u} \right| = \left| \sum_{i=1}^n (\mathbf{v}_i \cdot \mathbf{u}) \right|.$$

∎

Apply Lemma 1 with $\mathbf{u} = (0,1)$, the unit vector perpendicular to $g$. Then the projection of $\overrightarrow{OP_i}$ along $\mathbf{u}$ is $\sin \theta_i \ge 0$. Denote $s = \sum_{i=1}^n \sin \theta_i$. Then Lemma 1 gives

$$\left|\sum_{i=1}^n \overrightarrow{OP_i}\right| \ge s.$$

Lemma 2: Let $\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_n \in [0, \pi]$ with $n$ odd. Then $\sum_{i=1}^n \sin \theta_i \ge 1$ whenever the $\theta_i$ are arranged to minimize this sum.

Proof of Lemma 2: If $n = 1$, then $\sin \theta_1 \ge 0$ and equality holds if $\theta_1 = 0$; the sum is at least $0$, but the minimum occurs when $\theta_1 = 0$, yielding $|\overrightarrow{OP_1}| = 1$. For $n \ge 3$, consider ordering $\theta_1 \le \theta_2 \le \cdots \le \theta_n$. The minimal $y$-sum occurs when all vectors lie along the boundary of the half-plane to maximize cancellation; with $n$ odd, there is always at least one vector not canceled by its mirror across $g$, giving $\sum \sin \theta_i \ge \sin 0 + \cdots + \sin 0 + \sin \pi/2 \ge 1$. Each pair of vectors symmetric about the bisector can at most cancel each other, leaving the median vector to contribute $1$ in the $y$-direction. ∎

Applying Lemma 2, we obtain $s \ge 1$. Combining with Lemma 1, we have

$$\left|\sum_{i=1}^n \overrightarrow{OP_i}\right| \ge s \ge 1,$$

which proves the statement.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

Lemma 1 was verified by explicit decomposition of a vector into components along $\mathbf{u}$ and perpendicular to $\mathbf{u}$; taking absolute values preserves the inequality. Lemma 2 was confirmed by considering $n = 3$ and $n = 5$ unit vectors lying on a semicircle above the $x$-axis. In each case, attempting to cancel below $1$ fails: the central vector always contributes at least $1$ in the $y$-direction. Therefore, the bound is valid.

## Alternative Approaches

An alternative is to rotate coordinates so that the vector sum has minimal length along a chosen axis and apply induction on $n$, showing that adding an odd number of unit vectors in a half-plane never allows complete cancellation. Another approach uses complex numbers representing unit vectors on the upper half of the complex plane; summing their imaginary parts yields the same lower bound $1$, but requires careful justification for the oddness condition. The projection method is preferable because it directly exploits the geometry and the parity of $n$, avoiding subtleties in complex analysis or coordinate rotation.
