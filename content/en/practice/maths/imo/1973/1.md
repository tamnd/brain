## Problem

Point $O$ lies on line $g$; $\overrightarrow{OP_1}, \overrightarrow{OP_2}, \ldots, \overrightarrow{OP_n}$ are unit vectors such that points $P_1, P_2, \ldots, P_n$ all lie in a plane containing $g$ and on one side of $g$. Prove that if $n$ is odd, $$
\left\vert \overrightarrow{OP_1} + \overrightarrow{OP_2} + \ldots + \overrightarrow{OP_n} \right\vert \geq 1
$$ Here $\left\vert \overrightarrow{OM} \right\vert$ denotes the length of vector $\overrightarrow{OM}$.

---
## Exploration

For $n=1$, the statement says that the length of a unit vector is at least $1$, which is equality. For $n=3$, place the vectors symmetrically with arguments $30^\circ$, $90^\circ$, $150^\circ$. Their sum is the vertical unit vector, again giving equality. If the vectors are nearly parallel, the sum has much larger norm. These examples suggest that $1$ is the best possible lower bound.

The condition that all points lie on one side of the line $g$ means that all vectors make angles with a fixed direction along $g$ lying in an interval of length $\pi$. Thus all vectors lie inside a closed half-plane through the origin. This strongly suggests projecting onto a suitable direction.

Take coordinates so that $g$ is the $x$-axis and all points lie in the upper half-plane. Write

$$\overrightarrow{OP_k}=(\cos\theta_k,\sin\theta_k),
\qquad 0\leq \theta_k\leq \pi.$$

Then the problem becomes

$$\left(\sum \cos\theta_k\right)^2+\left(\sum \sin\theta_k\right)^2\geq 1.$$

A direct algebraic attack seems unpleasant.

A more geometric idea is to sort the angles:

$$0\leq \theta_1\leq \theta_2\leq \cdots \leq \theta_n\leq \pi.$$

Since $n$ is odd, there is a middle angle $\theta_m$ with $m=\frac{n+1}{2}$. If one projects every vector onto the direction of $\overrightarrow{OP_m}$, every projection should be nonnegative because all angle differences lie in $[-\pi/2,\pi/2]$ after pairing appropriately. The sum of the projections might then be at least $1$.

Testing this idea on small cases clarifies the structure. Pair $\theta_{m-k}$ with $\theta_{m+k}$. Since

$$\theta_{m-k}\leq \theta_m\leq \theta_{m+k},$$

and both differences from $\theta_m$ add to at most $\pi$, one gets

$$\cos(\theta_{m-k}-\theta_m)+\cos(\theta_{m+k}-\theta_m)\geq 0.$$

The middle vector contributes exactly $1$. Hence the total projection onto $\overrightarrow{OP_m}$ is at least $1$. Since the norm of a vector is at least any of its projections onto a unit direction, the result follows.

The delicate point is proving rigorously that each paired contribution is nonnegative. A careless argument could fail because cosine becomes negative outside $[-\pi/2,\pi/2]$. The ordering of the angles and the fact that all vectors lie in a half-plane together force the relevant sum of deviations to stay within $\pi$.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem. A statement is given and must be proved.

We are given an odd number $n$ of unit vectors in a common plane. All endpoints lie on the same side of a line through the origin. The task is to prove that the magnitude of their vector sum cannot be smaller than $1$.

The objects involved are planar vectors, projections, and angular orderings. The condition that all endpoints lie on one side of a line means that all vector directions occupy an angular interval of width at most $\pi$.

A naive approach would square the norm and expand

$$\left|\sum_{k=1}^n \overrightarrow{OP_k}\right|^2
=
n+2\sum_{i<j}\cos(\theta_i-\theta_j).$$

The cross terms can be negative, and controlling them individually is difficult. The core difficulty is that many vectors may nearly cancel one another. The oddness of $n$ must be exploited in a structural way rather than through brute-force estimation.

The central insight is to choose the median direction among the vectors and project the total sum onto that direction. The vectors can then be paired symmetrically around the median angle, and each pair contributes a nonnegative amount to the projection. The unpaired median vector contributes exactly $1$.

## Proof Architecture

The proof will proceed through two lemmas.

Lemma 1 states that after choosing coordinates with $g$ as the $x$-axis, the vectors may be written as

$$(\cos\theta_k,\sin\theta_k),
\qquad 0\leq \theta_k\leq \pi,$$

and after reindexing,

$$0\leq \theta_1\leq \theta_2\leq \cdots \leq \theta_n\leq \pi.$$

This is true because all vectors lie in the same closed half-plane bounded by $g$.

Lemma 2 states that if $m=\frac{n+1}{2}$, then for every $k=1,\dots,m-1$,

$$\cos(\theta_{m-k}-\theta_m)+\cos(\theta_{m+k}-\theta_m)\geq 0.$$

This follows from the trigonometric identity

$$\cos u+\cos v=2\cos\frac{u+v}{2}\cos\frac{u-v}{2},$$

together with bounds on the ordered angles.

The main argument projects the total vector sum onto the unit direction $\overrightarrow{OP_m}$. Lemma 2 shows that every symmetric pair contributes nonnegatively, while the middle vector contributes exactly $1$. Hence the total projection is at least $1$. Since the magnitude of a vector dominates its projection onto any unit vector, the theorem follows.

The hardest part is Lemma 2. A careless proof may ignore the sign of cosine outside the interval $[-\pi/2,\pi/2]$.

## Solution

Choose coordinates so that the line $g$ is the $x$-axis, and all points $P_1,\dots,P_n$ lie in the closed upper half-plane.

### Lemma 1

There exist angles $\theta_1,\dots,\theta_n$ such that

$$\overrightarrow{OP_k}=(\cos\theta_k,\sin\theta_k),
\qquad 0\leq \theta_k\leq \pi,$$

for all $k$, and after reindexing,

$$0\leq \theta_1\leq \theta_2\leq \cdots \leq \theta_n\leq \pi.$$

#### Proof

Each vector $\overrightarrow{OP_k}$ has length $1$, so it can be written uniquely in the form

$$(\cos\theta_k,\sin\theta_k)$$

with $0\leq \theta_k<2\pi$.

Since $P_k$ lies in the closed upper half-plane, its $y$-coordinate is nonnegative. Hence

$$\sin\theta_k\geq 0.$$

For angles in $[0,2\pi)$, this inequality holds exactly when

$$0\leq \theta_k\leq \pi.$$

Thus every $\theta_k$ belongs to $[0,\pi]$.

Since finitely many real numbers can be arranged in nondecreasing order, we may relabel the vectors so that

$$0\leq \theta_1\leq \theta_2\leq \cdots \leq \theta_n\leq \pi.$$

This proves the lemma. ∎

This establishes a linear ordering of all vector directions; without this ordering, the later pairing argument would have no controlled sign.

Let

$$m=\frac{n+1}{2}.$$

Since $n$ is odd, $m$ is an integer and

$$1\leq m\leq n.$$

Define

$$S=\sum_{k=1}^n \overrightarrow{OP_k}.$$

We project $S$ onto the unit vector $\overrightarrow{OP_m}$.

### Lemma 2

For each integer $k$ satisfying $1\leq k\leq m-1$,

$$\cos(\theta_{m-k}-\theta_m)+\cos(\theta_{m+k}-\theta_m)\geq 0.$$

#### Proof

Fix such a $k$.

Because

$$\theta_{m-k}\leq \theta_m\leq \theta_{m+k},$$

the numbers

$$a=\theta_m-\theta_{m-k},
\qquad
b=\theta_{m+k}-\theta_m$$

are nonnegative.

Moreover,

$$a+b
=
\theta_{m+k}-\theta_{m-k}.$$

Since

$$0\leq \theta_{m-k}\leq \theta_{m+k}\leq \pi,$$

we obtain

$$0\leq a+b\leq \pi.$$

Now

$$\cos(\theta_{m-k}-\theta_m)+\cos(\theta_{m+k}-\theta_m)
=
\cos a+\cos b.$$

Using the identity

$$\cos a+\cos b
=
2\cos\frac{a+b}{2}\cos\frac{a-b}{2},$$

we analyze the two factors separately.

Since

$$0\leq a+b\leq \pi,$$

we have

$$0\leq \frac{a+b}{2}\leq \frac{\pi}{2},$$

hence

$$\cos\frac{a+b}{2}\geq 0.$$

Also,

$$-\pi\leq a-b\leq \pi,$$

so

$$-\frac{\pi}{2}\leq \frac{a-b}{2}\leq \frac{\pi}{2},$$

which yields

$$\cos\frac{a-b}{2}\geq 0.$$

Therefore

$$\cos a+\cos b\geq 0.$$

This proves the lemma. ∎

This establishes that every pair of vectors symmetrically placed around the median direction contributes a nonnegative amount to the chosen projection; omitting the trigonometric factorization would leave the sign uncontrolled.

We now complete the proof.

Because $\overrightarrow{OP_m}$ is a unit vector, the projection of $S$ onto $\overrightarrow{OP_m}$ equals

$$S\cdot \overrightarrow{OP_m}.$$

Using

$$\overrightarrow{OP_k}\cdot \overrightarrow{OP_m}
=
\cos(\theta_k-\theta_m),$$

we obtain

$$S\cdot \overrightarrow{OP_m}
=
\sum_{k=1}^n \cos(\theta_k-\theta_m).$$

Separate the middle term and pair the remaining terms symmetrically:

$$S\cdot \overrightarrow{OP_m}
=
1+\sum_{k=1}^{m-1}
\left[
\cos(\theta_{m-k}-\theta_m)
+
\cos(\theta_{m+k}-\theta_m)
\right].$$

By Lemma 2, every bracketed quantity is nonnegative. Hence

$$S\cdot \overrightarrow{OP_m}\geq 1.$$

For any vector $v$ and any unit vector $u$,

$$v\cdot u\leq |v|.$$

Applying this with

$$v=S,
\qquad
u=\overrightarrow{OP_m},$$

gives

$$|S|
\geq
S\cdot \overrightarrow{OP_m}
\geq 1.$$

Thus

$$\left|
\overrightarrow{OP_1}+\cdots+\overrightarrow{OP_n}
\right|
\geq 1.$$

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the claim that

$$0\leq a+b\leq \pi$$

in Lemma 2. Re-derive it directly:

$$a+b
=
(\theta_m-\theta_{m-k})+(\theta_{m+k}-\theta_m)
=
\theta_{m+k}-\theta_{m-k}.$$

Since every $\theta_j$ lies in $[0,\pi]$ and the sequence is ordered,

$$0\leq \theta_{m-k}\leq \theta_{m+k}\leq \pi.$$

Hence

$$0\leq \theta_{m+k}-\theta_{m-k}\leq \pi.$$

A careless argument might only use $a,b\geq 0$ and conclude nothing about the sign of $\cos\frac{a+b}{2}$.

The second delicate step is the inequality

$$|S|\geq S\cdot \overrightarrow{OP_m}.$$

Since $\overrightarrow{OP_m}$ is a unit vector,

$$S\cdot \overrightarrow{OP_m}
=
|S|\cos\phi,$$

where $\phi$ is the angle between $S$ and $\overrightarrow{OP_m}$. Because $\cos\phi\leq 1$,

$$S\cdot \overrightarrow{OP_m}\leq |S|.$$

A careless argument could forget that this inequality requires the second vector to have norm $1$.

The third delicate step is the symmetric pairing. Since

$$n=2m-1,$$

the indices

$$m-k,\qquad m,\qquad m+k$$

cover every integer from $1$ to $n$ exactly once when $k=1,\dots,m-1$. If one tried to pair vectors arbitrarily rather than around the median index, the angular differences could exceed $\pi$, and the cosine sum could become negative.

## Alternative Approaches

Another proof uses complex numbers. Write

$$z_k=e^{i\theta_k},
\qquad 0\leq \theta_k\leq \pi.$$

Let

$$\theta_m$$

be the median angle. Multiplying the total sum by $e^{-i\theta_m}$ rotates the configuration without changing magnitudes:

$$e^{-i\theta_m}\sum_{k=1}^n z_k
=
\sum_{k=1}^n e^{i(\theta_k-\theta_m)}.$$

The real part equals

$$\sum_{k=1}^n \cos(\theta_k-\theta_m),$$

which is at least $1$ by the same pairing argument. Since the modulus of a complex number is at least the absolute value of its real part,

$$\left|\sum z_k\right|\geq 1.$$

This proof is shorter but conceals the geometric meaning behind complex notation.

A second approach uses convexity. The set of all possible sums of unit vectors in a fixed half-plane is convex and symmetric about the perpendicular bisector direction. By continuously rotating vectors while preserving their order, one can show that the minimum norm occurs when the vectors collapse into symmetric pairs around a central vector. Every symmetric pair contributes a vector parallel to the middle direction, so the minimum possible norm is exactly the length of the unpaired central vector, namely $1$. This method gives stronger geometric intuition but requires a more elaborate extremal argument.
