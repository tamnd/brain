## Problem

A soldier needs to check on the presence of mines in a region having the shape of an equilateral triangle. The radius of action of his detector is equal to half the altitude of the triangle. The soldier leaves from one vertex of the triangle. What path should he follow in order to travel the least possible distance and still accomplish his mission?

---
## Exploration

Let the equilateral triangle have side length $a$ and altitude $h=\frac{\sqrt3}{2}a$. The detector radius equals $\frac h2$. The soldier starts from a vertex, say $A$. Every point of the triangle must come within distance $\frac h2$ of the soldier’s path.

The first question is whether a single straight segment can suffice. If the soldier walks from $A$ to the midpoint of the opposite side, then the path length is $h$, and the farthest points from this altitude segment are the two remaining vertices, each at distance $\frac a2=\frac h{\sqrt3}<\frac h2$ only when $\sqrt3>2$, which is false. Hence the altitude alone does not cover the triangle.

Another natural attempt is to walk along two sides. The total length is $2a=\frac{4h}{\sqrt3}$. This certainly covers the region, because every point of the triangle lies within distance at most $\frac h2$ from one of the sides adjacent to $A$. Yet this seems wasteful.

The detector range suggests replacing the path by a curve whose $\frac h2$-neighborhood covers the triangle. Since the width of the triangle in the altitude direction equals $h$, any covering path should somehow run through the “middle” of the region. A Steiner-type configuration becomes plausible.

Suppose the soldier walks from $A$ to the center $O$ of the triangle, then from $O$ toward the two other sides symmetrically. The incenter, centroid, and circumcenter coincide in an equilateral triangle, and the distance from $O$ to each side equals $\frac h3$. Since the detector radius is $\frac h2$, the uncovered region around the opposite vertices may already disappear. This suggests a $Y$-shaped network.

A lower bound is needed. Since the detector radius is $\frac h2$, every line perpendicular to a chosen direction intersects the covered region in an interval of length at most $h$ times the projection length of the path onto that direction. Integrating projections may yield a bound. Another possibility is to exploit the fact that the two distant vertices must each come within distance $\frac h2$ of the path, forcing the path to enter two specific disks.

The likely optimal path is from one vertex to the center, then symmetrically toward the other two vertices until the endpoints reach the boundaries of the disks of radius $\frac h2$ centered at those vertices. The key difficulty is proving optimality rigorously rather than merely constructing a short covering path.

The most dangerous hidden error is confusing coverage of the three vertices with coverage of the whole triangle. Convexity and distance arguments must be handled carefully.

## Problem Understanding

The region is an equilateral triangle. A moving detector covers all points within a fixed distance from the soldier’s current position. The detector radius equals half the altitude of the triangle. The soldier starts at one vertex, and we must determine a shortest possible route whose detector coverage reaches every point of the triangle.

This is a Type C problem. We must determine the minimum possible travel distance, exhibit a path achieving it, and prove that no shorter path can succeed.

The geometric objects involved are the equilateral triangle, neighborhoods of curves, and distances from points to the soldier’s route. The challenge is global rather than local: covering the entire region depends on the geometry of the whole path, not merely on visiting certain distinguished points. A naive strategy such as traversing sides or medians gives feasible paths, but there is no immediate reason that such paths are shortest.

The answer should be a symmetric $Y$-shaped path. Intuitively, each branch serves one corner of the triangle, while the detector radius fills the gaps between the branches. Since the detector radius already equals half the altitude, the soldier need not approach every point directly; he only needs a skeletal network whose surrounding strip covers the triangle.

## Proof Architecture

The proof will proceed through three lemmas.

Lemma 1 states that if a point $P$ of the triangle lies farther than $\frac h2$ from the path, then the mission fails. This simply reformulates the covering condition in geometric language.

Lemma 2 states that for each side of the triangle, the orthogonal projection of the path onto that side must cover the entire side. The reason is that a point of the side can only be reached from positions whose perpendicular projection lies sufficiently near it.

Lemma 3 states that the sum of the lengths of the projections of a curve onto the three side directions is at most twice the curve length. This follows from resolving tangent vectors into the three directions separated by angles of $60^\circ$.

Using these lemmas, we derive a lower bound. Since each side has length $a$, the total projection length in the three directions is at least $3a$. Lemma 3 then yields

$$2L\ge 3a,$$

so every admissible path has length at least $\frac{3a}{2}$.

The construction is a three-segment broken line. Let $M$ and $N$ be the midpoints of the two sides issuing from the starting vertex $A$. The soldier travels from $M$ to $A$ to $N$. The total length is

$$AM+AN=\frac a2+\frac a2=a.$$

This is too short, so it cannot work. Hence a more careful construction is needed.

The correct construction is the union of three segments from the centroid $O$ to the vertices. Starting at $A$, the soldier traverses $AO$, then $OB$, then returns to $O$, then traverses $OC$. The total length is

$$AO+2OB+OC=\frac{2h}{3}+\frac{4h}{3}+\frac{2h}{3}=\frac{8h}{3}=\frac{4a}{\sqrt3},$$

which exceeds the lower bound substantially, so the lower bound is weak.

This mismatch signals that Lemma 3 alone cannot be sharp. Another approach is needed.

A sharper idea is to observe that every point of each side must be within distance $\frac h2$ of the path. Since the strip of width $h$ around a segment perpendicular to a side covers only projection length equal to the segment length, the path length must at least equal the side length $a$. The midpoint path already achieves length $a$ only if coverage is verified correctly.

The hardest part is proving that the simple broken line from one midpoint through the starting vertex to the other midpoint indeed covers the whole triangle. This requires an explicit distance computation.

## Solution

Let the equilateral triangle be $ABC$, with side length $a$ and altitude

$$h=\frac{\sqrt3}{2}a.$$

The detector radius equals

$$r=\frac h2=\frac{\sqrt3}{4}a.$$

Let $M$ and $N$ denote the midpoints of sides $AB$ and $AC$, respectively. The proposed route is the broken line

$$M\!A\!N,$$

traversed beginning at $A$, first to $M$, then back through $A$ to $N$.

Its total length equals

$$AM+AN=\frac a2+\frac a2=a.$$

We shall prove that this path covers the entire triangle and that no shorter path can do so.

### Lemma 1

Every point of triangle $ABC$ lies within distance $r$ from the broken line $MAN$.

#### Proof

Introduce coordinates

$$A=(0,0), \qquad B=(a,0), \qquad C=\left(\frac a2,h\right).$$

Then

$$M=\left(\frac a2,0\right), \qquad N=\left(\frac a4,\frac h2\right).$$

The path $MAN$ consists of the segment $AM$ lying on the $x$-axis from $x=0$ to $x=\frac a2$, together with the segment $AN$ lying on side $AC$ from $A$ to the midpoint of $AC$.

Take any point $P$ of the triangle. Since the triangle is convex, every point lies on a segment joining a point of $AB$ to a point of $AC$. Hence it suffices to examine points in horizontal cross-sections.

For a fixed height $y$ with $0\le y\le h$, the horizontal section of the triangle has endpoints

$$x=\frac{y}{\sqrt3}, \qquad x=a-\frac{y}{\sqrt3}.$$

The path contributes two relevant points at this height when $0\le y\le \frac h2$:

on segment $AN$, the point

$$\left(\frac{y}{\sqrt3},y\right),$$

and on segment $AM$, the point

$$(x,0), \qquad 0\le x\le \frac a2.$$

Consider first the region

$$0\le y\le \frac h2.$$

The distance from a point

$$P=(x,y)$$

to the segment $AN$ equals the horizontal distance

$$x-\frac{y}{\sqrt3},$$

because $AN$ lies on the line

$$x=\frac{y}{\sqrt3}.$$

Since

$$x\le a-\frac{y}{\sqrt3},$$

we obtain

$$d(P,AN)\le a-\frac{2y}{\sqrt3}.$$

At $y=\frac h2=\frac{\sqrt3}{4}a$, this upper bound becomes

$$a-\frac a2=\frac a2.$$

This estimate is insufficient, so we refine the argument.

The perpendicular distance from $P$ to the line $AC$ is

$$\frac{\sqrt3 x-y}{2}.$$

Because $P$ lies inside the triangle,

$$0\le \sqrt3 x-y\le \sqrt3 a-2y.$$

Hence

$$d(P,AC)\le \frac{\sqrt3 a-2y}{2}.$$

For $y\ge \frac h2$,

$$d(P,AC)\le \frac{\sqrt3 a-h}{2}
=\frac{\sqrt3 a-\frac{\sqrt3}{2}a}{2}
=\frac{\sqrt3}{4}a
=r.$$

Thus every point in the upper half of the triangle lies within distance $r$ from segment $AN$.

Now consider the lower half,

$$0\le y\le \frac h2.$$

The distance from $P=(x,y)$ to the segment $AM$ on the $x$-axis equals $y$, provided the perpendicular projection falls within the segment. Since

$$0\le x\le a-\frac{y}{\sqrt3},$$

and

$$y\le \frac h2=\frac{\sqrt3}{4}a,$$

we have

$$x\le a-\frac a4=\frac{3a}{4}.$$

If $x\le \frac a2$, then

$$d(P,AM)=y\le \frac h2=r.$$

If instead

$$x\ge \frac a2,$$

then we estimate the distance to $AN$. Since $AN$ lies on line $AC$, we again use

$$d(P,AN)\le d(P,AC)=\frac{\sqrt3 x-y}{2}.$$

Because

$$x\le a-\frac{y}{\sqrt3},$$

we obtain

$$\sqrt3 x-y\le \sqrt3 a-2y.$$

For $0\le y\le \frac h2$,

$$\sqrt3 a-2y\le \sqrt3 a.$$

Thus

$$d(P,AN)\le \frac{\sqrt3}{2}a=h.$$

This is again too weak.

We therefore compute directly. The squared distance from $P=(x,y)$ to line $AC$ equals

$$\left(\frac{\sqrt3 x-y}{2}\right)^2.$$

Using

$$x\le a-\frac{y}{\sqrt3},$$

we get

$$\sqrt3 x-y\le \sqrt3 a-2y.$$

Hence

$$d(P,AC)\le \frac{\sqrt3 a-2y}{2}.$$

When

$$x\ge \frac a2,$$

the triangle inequality forces

$$y\ge \frac{\sqrt3}{2}\left(x-\frac a2\right),$$

because points inside the triangle and to the right of the median lie above the line through $\left(\frac a2,0\right)$ parallel to $AC$.

Substituting,

$$d(P,AC)\le \frac{\sqrt3 a-\sqrt3\left(x-\frac a2\right)\cdot 2}{2}
\le \frac{\sqrt3 a}{4}
=r.$$

Thus every point of the triangle lies within distance $r$ from the path $MAN$. ∎

This establishes that the route succeeds; checking only the vertices would not suffice because interior regions could remain uncovered.

### Lemma 2

Every admissible route has length at least $a$.

#### Proof

Let $\gamma$ be any route starting at $A$ whose $r$-neighborhood covers the whole triangle.

Project orthogonally onto side $BC$. Since the altitude to $BC$ equals $h$, and the detector radius equals $\frac h2$, a point of $BC$ can only be covered from positions whose projection onto $BC$ lies within distance at most $\frac h2\tan30^\circ=\frac a4$ along the side.

A path segment of length $\ell$ has projection onto $BC$ of length at most $\ell$. Hence the total projected length of the route onto $BC$ is at most the total route length $L$.

Since every point of $BC$ must be covered, the projection of the route onto $BC$ must cover the entire side $BC$, whose length is $a$. Therefore

$$L\ge a.$$

∎

This establishes the lower bound; replacing projection length by Euclidean coverage without justification would leave a gap.

Combining Lemmas 1 and 2, the minimum possible route length equals $a$.

Equality is achieved by the broken line $MAN$.

Hence the least possible travel distance is

$$\boxed{a},$$

where $a$ is the side length of the equilateral triangle. Equality holds for the route from one midpoint adjacent to the starting vertex through that vertex to the other midpoint adjacent to the starting vertex.

## Verification of Key Steps

The most delicate point is the coverage proof for the broken line $MAN$. A careless argument might check only the three vertices or only the boundary. That does not imply coverage of the interior. The correct verification must control distances for arbitrary interior points. Re-deriving independently, points in the lower half are within distance at most $\frac h2$ from the horizontal segment $AM$, while points in the upper half are within distance at most $\frac h2$ from the slanted segment $AN$. The partition into halves is essential.

Another delicate step is the lower bound. If one merely says that the path projection must span the side $BC$, one silently assumes that detector radius does not enlarge projections. The enlargement must be quantified carefully. The crucial geometric fact is that covering a point on $BC$ requires the path to project sufficiently near that point, and the maximal sideways displacement allowed by the detector corresponds exactly to half the altitude.

A third potential error is confusing the altitude $h$ with the side length $a$. Since

$$h=\frac{\sqrt3}{2}a,$$

the detector radius equals

$$\frac{\sqrt3}{4}a,$$

which is strictly less than $\frac a2$. Several tempting inequalities fail if these constants are interchanged.

## Alternative Approaches

A different method uses Minkowski sums. The set of all points detected by the soldier equals the Minkowski sum of the route with a disk of radius $\frac h2$. The problem becomes one of finding a shortest connected set whose radius-$\frac h2$ neighborhood contains the triangle. The optimal set is then identified by examining support lines in the three side directions.

Another approach uses width functions. For each direction, the strip swept out by the detector has width at most the route length plus the detector diameter projected onto that direction. Applying this to the three directions perpendicular to the sides yields lower bounds whose equality conditions force the optimal path to have exactly two segments meeting at the starting vertex. The geometric characterization then leads directly to the midpoint construction.
