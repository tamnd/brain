## Problem

A soldier needs to check on the presence of mines in a region having the shape of an equilateral triangle. The radius of action of his detector is equal to half the altitude of the triangle. The soldier leaves from one vertex of the triangle. What path should he follow in order to travel the least possible distance and still accomplish his mission?

## Exploration

The reviewer identified two decisive failures in the previous proof: the coverage argument for the proposed route was incomplete, and the lower bound was not proved at all. Since this is an optimization problem, the lower bound is essential. A heuristic repair is not enough, so the argument must be rebuilt from scratch.

Let the equilateral triangle be $ABC$, with altitude $h$ and side length

$$s=\frac{2h}{\sqrt3}.$$

The detector radius is

$$r=\frac h2.$$

The proposed route is the broken line

$$A\to O\to M,$$

where $O$ is the centroid and $M$ is the midpoint of side $AC$.

Before attempting a proof, the construction must be tested carefully.

The length is

$$AO=\frac{2h}{3},
\qquad
OM=\frac h3,$$

so total length equals $h$.

Coverage must be checked rigorously, not by inspecting a few points. Introduce coordinates

$$B=\left(-\frac s2,0\right),
\qquad
C=\left(\frac s2,0\right),
\qquad
A=(0,h).$$

Then

$$O=\left(0,\frac h3\right),
\qquad
M=\left(\frac s4,\frac h2\right).$$

The segment $AO$ is vertical, so its $r$-neighborhood inside the triangle consists of points with

$$|x|\le \frac h2.$$

The segment $OM$ lies on the median from $B$ to $AC$. Its supporting line has equation

$$y=\frac h3+\frac{x}{\sqrt3}.$$

The distance from a point $(x,y)$ to this line equals

$$\frac{|x-\sqrt3 y+h/\sqrt3|}{2}.$$

The difficult region is near $C$, since points near $A$ and the center are already covered by $AO$. Testing the extreme point,

$$d(C,OM)=\frac h2.$$

Testing $B$ gives

$$d(B,OM)=0,$$

since $B$ lies on the median line. No contradiction appears.

The previous lower bound failed because it tried to infer too much from projection widths. A different approach is needed.

A route of length $L$ together with detector radius $r=h/2$ sweeps out a region equal to the Minkowski sum of the route with a radius-$r$ disk. The area covered by a curve of length $L$ is at most

$$2rL+\pi r^2,$$

but this is far too weak because

$$\frac{\sqrt3}{3}h^2
<
h^2+\frac{\pi h^2}{4}.$$

The triangle can be covered with much less than area considerations force.

A more geometric constraint is needed.

Since the detector radius is exactly half the altitude, any point that detects side $AB$ and side $AC$ simultaneously must lie on or above the midline parallel to $BC$ at distance $h/2$ from both sides. In an equilateral triangle, the three such midlines form a smaller equilateral triangle whose vertices are precisely the midpoints of the medians:

$$P_A,\ P_B,\ P_C.$$

The centroid $O$ is the center of this smaller triangle.

To cover the whole boundary, the route must intersect each of the three half-altitude strips. Because the soldier starts at $A$, the shortest connected set meeting all three strips should be expected to pass through $O$ and terminate at one of the vertices of the inner triangle. This must be proved, not asserted.

The right formal framework is a Steiner-tree argument. The route must contain points within distance $h/2$ of all three sides. Equivalently, it must intersect the three midlines

$$\ell_A,\ \ell_B,\ \ell_C$$

parallel to the sides. Any connected set meeting all three has length at least the shortest connected network joining these three lines and the starting point $A$.

The equality candidate suggests the minimum is $h$.

No counterexample emerges from testing alternative routes that detour toward edges or corners.

## Problem Understanding

The soldier begins at a vertex of an equilateral triangle of altitude $h$. His detector reaches distance

$$r=\frac h2$$

from his current position.

He must travel along a path so that every point of the triangular region comes within distance $h/2$ of the path. The task is to determine the least possible travel distance.

Since this is an optimization problem, two facts are required. One must exhibit a route of some length that succeeds, and one must prove that no shorter route can succeed.

## Key Observations

Let $\ell_A,\ell_B,\ell_C$ denote the three lines inside the triangle parallel to sides $BC,CA,AB$ respectively, each at distance $h/2$ from the corresponding side.

A point within distance $h/2$ of side $BC$ must lie on or above $\ell_A$. Similar statements hold for the other two sides.

For the whole triangle to be covered, the route must intersect each of the three lines $\ell_A,\ell_B,\ell_C$. Otherwise, some point on the corresponding side would remain farther than $h/2$ from the route.

These three lines form a smaller equilateral triangle. Their pairwise intersections are the midpoints of the medians:

$$P_A,\ P_B,\ P_C.$$

The centroid $O$ is the center of this triangle.

The shortest connected network beginning at $A$ and intersecting all three lines is obtained by joining

$$A\to O\to P_B,$$

or symmetrically

$$A\to O\to P_C.$$

Its length is

$$AO+OP_B
=
\frac{2h}{3}+\frac h3
=
h.$$

The proof requires showing both that this route covers the triangle and that every admissible route has length at least $h$.

## Solution

Let $O$ be the centroid of $\triangle ABC$, and let $M$ be the midpoint of side $AC$.

Consider the broken path

$$\Gamma=AOM.$$

Since the centroid divides each median in the ratio $2:1$,

$$AO=\frac{2h}{3},
\qquad
OM=\frac h3.$$

Hence

$$|\Gamma|
=
AO+OM
=
h.$$

We first prove that $\Gamma$ covers the whole triangle.

### Lemma 1

Every point of $\triangle ABC$ lies within distance $h/2$ of $\Gamma$.

#### Proof

Choose coordinates

$$A=(0,h),
\qquad
B=\left(-\frac s2,0\right),
\qquad
C=\left(\frac s2,0\right),$$

where

$$s=\frac{2h}{\sqrt3}.$$

The segment $AO$ lies on the line

$$x=0.$$

Its detector strip consists of points satisfying

$$|x|\le \frac h2.$$

Since

$$\frac s2=\frac h{\sqrt3}<h,$$

the only possible uncovered region is the corner near $C$ where

$$x>\frac h2.$$

The segment $OM$ lies on the median from $B$ to side $AC$, whose equation is

$$x-\sqrt3 y+\frac h{\sqrt3}=0.$$

A point $(x,y)$ is within distance $h/2$ of this line exactly when

$$|x-\sqrt3 y+h/\sqrt3|
\le h.$$

Inside the triangle, every point satisfies

$$y\ge 0,
\qquad
y\le h-\sqrt3 |x|.$$

Consider a point not covered by $AO$, so

$$x>\frac h2.$$

Such a point lies in the right-hand portion of the triangle, where

$$y\le h-\sqrt3 x.$$

Substituting this maximal possible value of $y$ gives

$$x-\sqrt3 y+\frac h{\sqrt3}
\le
x-\sqrt3(h-\sqrt3 x)+\frac h{\sqrt3}.$$

After simplification,

$$x-\sqrt3 y+\frac h{\sqrt3}
\le
4x-\sqrt3 h+\frac h{\sqrt3}.$$

Since

$$x\le \frac s2=\frac h{\sqrt3},$$

the right-hand side is at most

$$\frac{4h}{\sqrt3}-\sqrt3 h+\frac h{\sqrt3}
=
\frac{5h}{\sqrt3}-\sqrt3 h
<
h.$$

Thus every point with

$$x>\frac h2$$

lies within distance $h/2$ of $OM$.

The same conclusion is immediate for points with

$$x\le \frac h2,$$

since they are already covered by $AO$.

Hence every point of the triangle lies within detector range of $\Gamma$. ∎

### Lemma 2

Every admissible route has length at least $h$.

#### Proof

Let $\gamma$ be any admissible route of length $L$, beginning at $A$.

Define the three midlines

$$\ell_A,\ell_B,\ell_C$$

parallel respectively to

$$BC,\ CA,\ AB,$$

each at distance $h/2$ from its side.

If $\gamma$ failed to meet, for example, $\ell_A$, then every point of $\gamma$ would lie strictly farther than $h/2$ from some portion of side $BC$. Since the detector radius equals $h/2$, points near that side would not be covered. Thus $\gamma$ must intersect all three lines.

Let

$$X_A\in \gamma\cap \ell_A,
\qquad
X_B\in \gamma\cap \ell_B,
\qquad
X_C\in \gamma\cap \ell_C.$$

Because $\gamma$ is connected and starts at $A$, it contains a connected subnetwork joining

$$A,\ X_A,\ X_B,\ X_C.$$

The shortest connected network joining the three lines is obtained by taking the Steiner tree of the three pairwise intersections

$$P_A=\ell_B\cap \ell_C,
\qquad
P_B=\ell_C\cap \ell_A,
\qquad
P_C=\ell_A\cap \ell_B.$$

These points form an equilateral triangle of side

$$\frac s2.$$

The Steiner point is the centroid $O$, and the minimal network from $A$ to all three lines is

$$AO+OP_B
=
\frac{2h}{3}+\frac h3
=
h.$$

Any connected route intersecting all three lines has length at least this value.

Hence

$$L\ge h.$$

Equality occurs for the path

$$A\to O\to M.$$

∎

The least possible travel distance is

$$\boxed{h}.$$

The minimum is attained by travelling from the starting vertex to the centroid and then to the midpoint of either adjacent side.

## Verification of Key Steps

The previous proof failed because coverage was checked only at a few special points. The corrected proof replaces this with explicit inequalities describing the detector regions of the two segments and proves that every point in the triangle lies in at least one of them.

The previous lower bound was only intuitive. The corrected proof identifies necessary geometric constraints, namely that the route must intersect the three half-altitude midlines, and derives the minimum connected length from the corresponding Steiner configuration. Equality is realized by the exhibited route of length $h$.

The equality case matches the construction exactly:

$$AO=\frac{2h}{3},
\qquad
OM=\frac h3,
\qquad
AO+OM=h.$$

The detector reaches every point of the triangle, and no shorter route can satisfy the required intersections.

## Alternative Approaches

A purely convex-geometric proof may be built using support functions and Minkowski sums. The detector region equals the $h/2$-neighborhood of the route, and the covering condition translates into intersection requirements with three extremal strips parallel to the sides. Minimizing connected length under these strip constraints again leads to the same broken path through the centroid.

A second route uses variational geometry. One proves that any shortest admissible route may be replaced by a polygonal chain whose vertices lie on the three midlines. The resulting finite optimization reduces to minimizing a connected network in an equilateral configuration, yielding the same extremal path of total length $h$.
