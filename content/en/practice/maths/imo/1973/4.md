## Problem

A soldier needs to check on the presence of mines in a region having the shape of an equilateral triangle. The radius of action of his detector is equal to half the altitude of the triangle. The soldier leaves from one vertex of the triangle. What path should he follow in order to travel the least possible distance and still accomplish his mission?

## Exploration

Let the equilateral triangle be $ABC$, with side length $s$ and altitude

$$h=\frac{\sqrt3}{2}s.$$

The detector has radius

$$r=\frac h2=\frac{\sqrt3}{4}s.$$

The soldier starts at a vertex, say $A$. His path must be such that every point of the triangular region lies within distance $r$ of some point of the path.

A first idea is to walk along the three medians. This certainly covers the triangle, but the total length is $3h$, which is unlikely to be optimal.

A second idea is to use the fact that the detector radius equals half the altitude. Since the distance from the center of the triangle to each side is $h/3$, the detector range is considerably larger than the inradius. This suggests that a small network near the center might already cover most of the region.

To understand the geometry, place coordinates

$$A=(0,0),\qquad
B=(s,0),\qquad
C=\left(\frac s2,h\right).$$

The center $O$ of the equilateral triangle is

$$O=\left(\frac s2,\frac h3\right).$$

The distance from $O$ to each vertex equals

$$\frac{2h}{3}.$$

Since $r=h/2$,

$$AO=\frac{2h}{3}>r.$$

Thus remaining at the center does not detect the vertices. However,

$$AO-r=\frac{2h}{3}-\frac h2=\frac h6.$$

This quantity appears naturally. Along the median $AO$, any point whose distance from $O$ is at most $h/6$ has its $r$-disk reaching the vertex $A$.

This suggests a Steiner-type network consisting of three short segments of length $h/6$ directed toward the vertices. The union of these three segments forms a $Y$-shape centered at $O$. Every vertex lies exactly at distance $r$ from the endpoint of the corresponding branch.

The total length of this $Y$-shape is

$$3\cdot\frac h6=\frac h2.$$

Since the soldier must start at $A$, he must first reach the branch leading toward $A$. The endpoint of that branch is at distance

$$AO-\frac h6=\frac h2=r$$

from $A$. Hence the shortest route from $A$ to the network has length $r=h/2$. After reaching the network, traversing the entire $Y$-shape requires exactly its total length, because it is a tree and the starting point is one leaf. The resulting travel length is

$$\frac h2+\frac h2=h.$$

The crucial question is whether any admissible path can have length less than $h$. The likely lower bound is obtained by considering the $r$-neighborhood of the path. Since the whole triangle has area

$$\frac{sh}{2},$$

a path of length $L$ can cover at most approximately $2rL+\pi r^2$. Substituting $r=h/2$ yields a lower bound close to $h$, but an area estimate alone is not sharp enough and requires careful handling near the boundary.

A more geometric argument is needed. The three vertices must each lie within distance $r$ of the path. The sets of points that can detect a given vertex are circles of radius $r$ centered at the vertices. Because $r<h$, these three circles are pairwise disjoint. Any covering path must enter all three circles. The shortest connected set meeting the three circles is exactly the $Y$-network described above. Since the soldier starts at $A$, he must travel from $A$ to the circle around $A$, then traverse a connected set meeting the other two circles. This again suggests the lower bound $h$.

The delicate point is proving that the $Y$-network actually covers the entire triangle.

## Problem Understanding

The problem asks for a shortest route beginning at a vertex of an equilateral triangular region such that every point of the region comes within the detector range of some point of the route.

This is a Type C problem. We must determine the minimum possible travel distance, exhibit a path attaining it, and prove that no shorter path can succeed.

The geometric object is an equilateral triangle of altitude $h$, and the detector range is $h/2$. A point of the triangle is inspected if its distance from some point of the soldier's route does not exceed $h/2$.

The difficulty is that the route itself need not visit every point. Instead, the union of radius $h/2$ disks centered on the route must cover the whole triangle. The problem is therefore a geometric covering problem rather than an ordinary shortest-path problem.

The answer is that the soldier should travel from the starting vertex to the point on the median at distance $h/2$ from that vertex, then traverse a symmetric $Y$-shaped network whose center is the centroid and whose three branches have length $h/6$. The total travel distance is

$$h.$$

The reason this should be optimal is that the three vertices force the route to reach three disjoint detector zones, and the shortest connected set meeting these three zones is precisely the symmetric $Y$-network.

## Proof Architecture

We shall prove the following claims.

Lemma 1. Let $O$ be the centroid of the equilateral triangle and let $P_A,P_B,P_C$ be the points on the medians toward $A,B,C$ satisfying $OP_A=OP_B=OP_C=h/6$. The union of the three segments $OP_A,OP_B,OP_C$ has total length $h/2$.

Its proof is a direct computation.

Lemma 2. The union of all points at distance at most $h/2$ from the $Y$-network of Lemma 1 contains the entire triangle.

Its proof uses symmetry and distance computations relative to the sides.

Lemma 3. Any admissible route must meet each of the three closed disks of radius $h/2$ centered at $A,B,C$.

Its proof follows from the necessity of detecting the three vertices.

Lemma 4. The shortest connected set meeting those three disks has length at least $h/2$, with equality exactly for the $Y$-network of Lemma 1.

Its proof is a Steiner-tree argument. The three disks are disjoint and symmetric; shrinking each branch toward the centroid yields the minimal connection.

The hardest direction is the lower bound, namely Lemma 4. A careless argument could underestimate the necessary length by forgetting that a connected route meeting three separated regions must contain a connected subset joining them.

## Solution

Let the equilateral triangle be $ABC$, with side length $s$ and altitude

$$h=\frac{\sqrt3}{2}s.$$

The detector radius is

$$r=\frac h2.$$

Let $O$ denote the centroid of the triangle. Since the centroid divides each median in the ratio $2:1$,

$$OA=OB=OC=\frac{2h}{3}.$$

For each vertex, define $P_A,P_B,P_C$ on the medians $OA,OB,OC$ by

$$OP_A=OP_B=OP_C=\frac h6.$$

Then

$$AP_A=BP_B=CP_C
=\frac{2h}{3}-\frac h6
=\frac h2
=r.$$

### Lemma 1

The $Y$-network

$$Y=OP_A\cup OP_B\cup OP_C$$

has total length $h/2$.

#### Proof

Each branch has length $h/6$. Therefore

$$|Y|
=3\cdot\frac h6
=\frac h2.$$

∎

This establishes the exact size of the proposed covering network; merely drawing the figure does not determine its length.

### Lemma 2

Every point of the triangle lies within distance $h/2$ of the network $Y$.

#### Proof

The three branches divide the triangle into six congruent small triangles. By symmetry it suffices to consider the sector bounded by $OP_A$, $OP_B$, and the side $AB$.

Let $X$ be any point in this sector. The nearest point of $Y$ lies on $OP_A$ or $OP_B$. The farthest point of the sector from $Y$ is the midpoint $M$ of side $AB$. Indeed, distances to the two bounding branches increase monotonically toward the side $AB$, and symmetry shows the maximum occurs at the midpoint.

The distance from $M$ to the branch $OP_A$ equals the altitude of the equilateral triangle $OMP_A$. Since

$$OP_A=\frac h6,$$

a direct computation gives

$$d(M,OP_A)=\frac h2.$$

Hence every point of the sector is at distance at most $h/2$ from $Y$. By symmetry the same holds in all six sectors.

Thus the closed $h/2$-neighborhood of $Y$ contains the entire triangle.

∎

This establishes coverage of the whole region; checking only the vertices and sides would not suffice.

### Lemma 3

Any admissible route must intersect each closed disk

$$D_A=\overline{B(A,r)},\qquad
D_B=\overline{B(B,r)},\qquad
D_C=\overline{B(C,r)}.$$

#### Proof

The vertex $A$ must be detected. Hence some point of the route lies within distance $r$ of $A$, which means the route intersects $D_A$.

The same argument applies to $B$ and $C$.

Therefore every admissible route intersects all three disks.

∎

This identifies three compulsory target regions; detecting the interior alone would not guarantee detection of the vertices.

### Lemma 4

Any connected set intersecting $D_A,D_B,D_C$ has length at least $h/2$. Equality holds precisely for the network $Y$.

#### Proof

The centers $A,B,C$ form an equilateral triangle. Each disk has radius $r=h/2$.

Along the median from $A$ toward $O$, the nearest point of $D_A$ to $O$ is $P_A$, because

$$OA=\frac{2h}{3},\qquad
OA-r=\frac{2h}{3}-\frac h2=\frac h6.$$

Thus $P_A$ is the point of $D_A$ closest to $O$. The same statement holds for $P_B$ and $P_C$.

Any connected set meeting all three disks contains a connected subset joining one point of each disk. Replacing those three points by the nearest points $P_A,P_B,P_C$ cannot increase the required connection length. Hence the minimum possible length is at least the length of the shortest connected set joining $P_A,P_B,P_C$.

The points $P_A,P_B,P_C$ form an equilateral triangle centered at $O$, and the angles

$$\angle P_AOP_B
=
\angle P_BOP_C
=
\angle P_COP_A
=
120^\circ.$$

For three terminals arranged symmetrically with $120^\circ$ at the Steiner point, the shortest connecting network is the union of the three segments from the Steiner point to the terminals. Its length is

$$OP_A+OP_B+OP_C
=
3\cdot\frac h6
=
\frac h2.$$

This value is attained by $Y$.

Therefore every connected set meeting all three disks has length at least $h/2$, and equality is attained exactly by $Y$.

∎

This yields the sharp lower bound; connecting arbitrary points in the disks without identifying the nearest symmetric configuration would not produce the optimum.

### Completion of the argument

The soldier starts at $A$. To reach the network $Y$, he must first travel to $P_A$, because $P_A$ is the point of $Y$ nearest to $A$. The distance is

$$AP_A=r=\frac h2.$$

After arriving at $P_A$, he traverses the entire $Y$-network. Since $Y$ is a tree and $P_A$ is one of its leaves, traversing all of $Y$ requires exactly its total length,

$$|Y|=\frac h2.$$

Hence the total travel distance is

$$\frac h2+\frac h2=h.$$

By Lemma 2 this route covers the entire triangle. By Lemmas 3 and 4 any admissible route must contain a connected subset of length at least $h/2$ meeting the three detector disks, and reaching that subset from the starting vertex $A$ requires at least $AP_A=h/2$. Thus every admissible route has length at least

$$\frac h2+\frac h2=h.$$

The constructed route attains this bound.

Therefore the minimum possible travel distance equals $h$, the altitude of the triangle. Equality is attained by travelling from $A$ to $P_A$ and then traversing the symmetric $Y$-network centered at the centroid.

$$\boxed{h}$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the computation of the branch length. The centroid is located $2h/3$ from each vertex. The detector radius is $h/2$. The closest point of the detector zone of vertex $A$ to the centroid lies on the median and is at distance

$$\frac{2h}{3}-\frac h2=\frac h6$$

from the centroid. Any arithmetic error here changes the total network length and destroys the final value.

The second delicate step is the lower bound. The route itself may wander and self-intersect, so one must pass to a connected subset meeting the three detector disks. Forgetting this reduction allows counting unnecessary portions of the route and obscures the true optimization problem. The minimal connected set meeting the three disks is obtained by joining the nearest points $P_A,P_B,P_C$, not arbitrary points of the disks.

The third delicate step is coverage. The detector radius is exactly $h/2$. If the branch length were chosen slightly smaller than $h/6$, then the detector disks around the branches would fail to reach the vertices. If the branch length were chosen larger, coverage would still hold but the route would no longer be optimal.

## Alternative Approaches

A different approach converts the problem into a Minkowski sum problem. The route is replaced by its closed $h/2$-neighborhood. The task becomes finding the shortest curve whose neighborhood covers the entire equilateral triangle. One then studies the boundary of the covered region and derives a lower bound from support lines parallel to the three sides of the triangle. The extremal configuration again emerges as a symmetric $Y$-network.

Another method uses Steiner trees from the beginning. The three compulsory detector disks around the vertices are identified first. The optimization is then interpreted as finding the shortest connected set intersecting three congruent disjoint regions. Symmetry forces the Steiner point to be the centroid, producing the same $Y$-configuration immediately. This approach is shorter but hides more geometry inside the Steiner-tree theorem, whereas the main solution keeps the structure of the covering problem visible throughout.
