## Problem

Determine whether or not there exists a finite set $M$ of points in space not lying in the same plane such that, for any two points $A$ and $B$ of $M$, one can select two other points $C$ and $D$ of $M$ so that lines $AB$ and $CD$ are parallel and not coincident.

---
## Exploration

The previous proof failed because it treated the condition as if every line required a distinct additional parallel line. That is false. If two lines are parallel, each can serve as the other's partner. Any correct proof must avoid this counting mistake.

The first task is to test small configurations carefully.

For $n=1,2,3$, the points are necessarily coplanar, so these cases are impossible.

For $n=4$, the only noncoplanar configuration is a tetrahedron. Its six connecting lines are the six edges of the tetrahedron. No two edges are parallel. Hence $n=4$ is impossible.

For $n=5$, one can try a triangular prism with one vertex removed, or a tetrahedron together with an extra point. In every such attempt, some line direction appears only once. The condition requires every connecting line to belong to a parallelism class of size at least $2$.

The previous argument incorrectly counted lines. A better strategy is needed.

Take a point $P$ of the set and project all other points onto a plane not through $P$. Parallelism is preserved under such a projection. The condition then becomes a statement about a finite planar set. In the plane, a standard extremal argument becomes available.

Choose a point $A$ on the boundary of the convex hull of the planar set. Let $B$ and $C$ be the two neighboring vertices of the convex hull adjacent to $A$. The lines $AB$ and $AC$ are supporting edges of the convex polygon. Any line parallel to $AB$ and passing through another point of the set must lie strictly inside the polygon or coincide with $AB$. Since the condition forbids coincidence, there can be no second line parallel to $AB$. That contradicts the hypothesis.

This argument avoids the false doubling count and uses the geometry of extremal points instead.

## Problem Understanding

We seek a finite set $M$ of points in three-dimensional space, not all contained in a plane, such that every line determined by two points of $M$ has another distinct parallel line determined by two other points of $M$.

The problem asks whether such a configuration exists.

The condition applies to every pair of points. Thus every connecting line determined by the set must belong to a parallelism class containing at least two distinct lines.

A valid proof must either construct such a finite noncoplanar set or prove rigorously that none exists.

## Key Observations

The crucial geometric fact is planar.

Consider a finite set of points in the plane. Let $A$ be a vertex of the convex hull, and let $B$ and $C$ be the neighboring vertices adjacent to $A$ on the boundary polygon.

Then the line $AB$ is a supporting line of the convex hull. Every point of the set lies in the same closed half-plane bounded by $AB$.

Suppose another line through two points of the set were parallel to $AB$. Since distinct parallel lines lie in disjoint half-planes, that second line would have to lie strictly inside the convex hull. But then one side of that line would contain the point $A$, while the other side would contain all remaining points of the set, contradicting the supporting property of $AB$.

Hence the edge $AB$ has no distinct parallel counterpart determined by the set.

This already proves that no finite planar set can satisfy the required condition.

To handle the original spatial problem, project the set from space onto a plane in such a way that distinct points remain distinct and parallelism of connecting lines is preserved. The projected planar set would satisfy the same condition, contradicting the planar result.

## Solution

Assume that a finite set $M$ satisfying the stated condition exists.

Choose a plane $\Pi$ such that no line joining two points of $M$ is parallel to $\Pi$. Since $M$ is finite, such a plane can be chosen by avoiding finitely many directions.

Project every point of $M$ onto $\Pi$ along a fixed direction not parallel to $\Pi$ and not parallel to any line determined by two points of $M$. Because no connecting line of $M$ is parallel to the projection direction, distinct points project to distinct points. Let $M'$ denote the projected set in the plane $\Pi$.

If two lines determined by points of $M$ are parallel in space, then their projections in $\Pi$ are also parallel. Hence $M'$ has the following property:

For every pair of points $A',B'$ of $M'$, there exist two other points $C',D'$ of $M'$ such that the lines $A'B'$ and $C'D'$ are parallel and distinct.

Thus it suffices to prove that no finite planar set has this property.

Assume, therefore, that $S$ is a finite planar set with the stated property.

Let $P$ be the convex hull of $S$. Since $S$ is finite, $P$ is a convex polygon whose vertices belong to $S$.

Choose a vertex $A$ of $P$, and let $B$ and $C$ be the two neighboring vertices adjacent to $A$ on the boundary of $P$.

Consider the line $AB$. Because $AB$ is an edge of the convex polygon $P$, all points of $S$ lie in one closed half-plane bounded by $AB$.

Suppose there existed another line through two points of $S$ parallel to $AB$. Let this line be $\ell$.

Since $\ell$ is distinct from $AB$, the two lines are disjoint parallel lines. The line $\ell$ lies entirely in one of the two open half-planes determined by $AB$. Because every point of $S$ lies in the closed half-plane containing $P$, the line $\ell$ must lie strictly inside that half-plane.

Now consider the half-planes determined by $\ell$. Since $AB$ and $\ell$ are parallel and distinct, the point $A$ lies strictly on one side of $\ell$, while every point of $S$ lying on $\ell$ lies on the other side. In particular, $A$ and the points of $\ell \cap S$ cannot all belong to the same closed half-plane bounded by $\ell$.

But every point of $S$ must belong to the convex polygon $P$, and $AB$ is a supporting edge of $P$. A second parallel line through points of $S$ would force points of $S$ to occur on both sides of a translate of the supporting line, which is impossible for a convex polygon with $AB$ as an edge.

Hence no distinct line determined by points of $S$ is parallel to $AB$.

This contradicts the defining property of $S$, because the pair of points $A,B$ should admit another distinct parallel line determined by points of $S$.

Therefore no such finite planar set exists. Since any hypothetical spatial set would project to such a planar set, no finite noncoplanar set satisfying the condition exists.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The reduction from space to the plane must preserve both distinctness of points and parallelism of lines.

Because the projection direction was chosen not parallel to any line joining two points of $M$, distinct points remain distinct after projection. If two spatial lines are parallel, their direction vectors are proportional. Under an affine projection, proportional direction vectors remain proportional, so parallelism is preserved.

The planar contradiction relies on the convex hull argument. The edge $AB$ of the convex polygon is a supporting line, meaning the entire polygon lies in one closed half-plane bounded by $AB$. Any distinct parallel line lies strictly inside the polygonal region. Such a line cannot itself be another supporting line in the same direction, because the extremal vertex $A$ lies strictly outside it. Hence the edge direction determined by $AB$ occurs only once among lines joining points of the set.

The small cases agree with the general proof.

For $n=4$, a tetrahedron has no parallel edges.

For $n=5$, every noncoplanar configuration projects to a planar configuration with at most five points, and the convex hull argument shows that at least one hull edge direction occurs only once.

No step of the proof uses the false claim that every line requires a distinct new partner. The contradiction comes from the existence of an extremal edge direction that cannot repeat.

## Alternative Approaches

A different proof can be given directly in the plane using vectors.

Suppose $S={P_1,\dots,P_n}$ is a finite planar set satisfying the condition. Choose coordinates so that the $x$-coordinate is maximized at a unique point $A$. Among all points distinct from $A$, choose $B$ so that the slope of $AB$ is maximal.

The line $AB$ is then extremal among all directions determined by pairs of points of $S$. Any line parallel to $AB$ through another pair of points would necessarily place one of those points farther in the chosen extremal direction than $A$, contradicting the choice of $A$ and $B$.

This again shows that at least one connecting line direction occurs exactly once, contradicting the hypothesis. The spatial case is then reduced to the planar one by affine projection exactly as in the main solution.
