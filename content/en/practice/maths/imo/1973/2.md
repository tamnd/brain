## Problem

Determine whether or not there exists a finite set $M$ of points in space not lying in the same plane such that, for any two points $A$ and $B$ of $M$, one can select two other points $C$ and $D$ of $M$ so that lines $AB$ and $CD$ are parallel and not coincident.

---
## Exploration

The reviewer identified a critical error in the previous proof: extremality inside a fixed parallel class does **not** force uniqueness. Before attempting a new argument, the first task is to test candidate principles against small cases and against configurations where the failed logic breaks.

For $n=1,2,3$, every finite set is coplanar, so the hypothesis cannot even be satisfied.

For $n=4$, any noncoplanar configuration is a tetrahedron. A tetrahedron has six edges, and no two are parallel. Hence every segment direction occurs exactly once, so the condition fails.

For $n=5$, a square pyramid provides a useful test. The base contributes parallel edge pairs, but every segment from the apex to a base vertex has no parallel mate. Thus the condition still fails.

The previous proof tried to show that an extreme representative of a direction class must be unique. A prism immediately falsifies that principle: exposed edges can have parallel copies. Any corrected argument must avoid claims of that type.

A different idea is to study **difference vectors**. The condition says that every segment vector $A-B$ must occur again, up to sign, from another pair of points. If one could find an “extreme” difference vector that is realized uniquely, the condition would fail.

The natural place to search for such a vector is the convex hull. Let $P=\operatorname{conv}(M)$ be the convex hull of $M$. Since $M$ is finite and not coplanar, $P$ is a three-dimensional polytope.

Take an edge $AB$ of $P$. Since $AB$ is an edge, there exists a linear functional $\varphi$ whose maximum over $P$ is attained exactly on $AB$. If some other segment $CD$ is parallel to $AB$, compare the values of $\varphi$ on its endpoints. Since $AB$ is parallel to $CD$, the difference vector $D-C$ is proportional to $B-A$, so $\varphi(D)-\varphi(C)$ must equal the corresponding proportional increment along the maximizing direction.

The critical question is whether this forces $C,D$ to lie on the maximizing edge itself. Testing against a prism is essential, because prisms contain parallel edges. In a prism, a side edge may have a parallel mate, but such an edge is **not** uniquely maximizing for any functional. By contrast, an edge exposed as the unique maximizing set for a functional behaves differently.

This suggests choosing not an arbitrary edge, but an edge that is the **entire maximizing set** of some linear functional and then proving that no distinct parallel segment can exist.

## Problem Understanding

The problem asks whether there exists a finite set $M$ of points in space, not all contained in one plane, such that for every pair of distinct points $A,B\in M$, there exist two other points $C,D\in M$ with the lines $AB$ and $CD$ parallel and distinct.

Equivalently, every line determined by two points of $M$ must belong to a parallel class containing at least two distinct lines.

To prove nonexistence, it suffices to show that every finite noncoplanar set contains at least one line whose direction occurs only once.

## Key Observations

Let $P=\operatorname{conv}(M)$ be the convex hull of $M$. Since $M$ is finite and not coplanar, $P$ is a three-dimensional polytope.

Lemma 1. There exists an edge $AB$ of $P$ and a linear functional $\varphi$ such that:

$$\varphi(X)\le \varphi(A)=\varphi(B)
\quad\text{for all }X\in P,$$

with equality if and only if $X\in AB$.

**Proof.** Since $P$ is a polytope, every edge is exposed. Choose any edge $AB$ of $P$. There exist two supporting faces meeting exactly in $AB$. Let $\varphi_1,\varphi_2$ be linear functionals defining these supporting planes, normalized so that each attains its maximum on the corresponding face.

Then for sufficiently small $\varepsilon>0$,

$$\varphi=\varphi_1+\varepsilon\varphi_2$$

attains its maximum over $P$ exactly on the edge $AB$. Thus $AB$ is the unique maximizing set of $\varphi$. ∎

Lemma 2. Let $AB$ and $CD$ be parallel segments with

$$D-C=\lambda(B-A),
\qquad \lambda\ne 0.$$

If a linear functional $\varphi$ attains its maximum exactly on the segment $AB$, then $CD$ must lie on the same line as $AB$.

**Proof.** Since $\varphi$ is linear,

$$\varphi(D)-\varphi(C)
=
\lambda\bigl(\varphi(B)-\varphi(A)\bigr).$$

Because $\varphi$ is constant on $AB$,

$$\varphi(B)-\varphi(A)=0,$$

hence

$$\varphi(D)=\varphi(C).$$

Thus $C$ and $D$ lie in the same level set of $\varphi$.

Since $\varphi$ achieves its maximum exactly on $AB$, every level set parallel to the maximizing one intersects $P$ in at most one segment parallel to $AB$. To justify this claim, suppose a second distinct segment $CD$ parallel to $AB$ existed in another level set. Then for every $t\in[0,1]$,

$$X_t=(1-t)A+tC,
\qquad
Y_t=(1-t)B+tD$$

belong to $P$ by convexity, and

$$Y_t-X_t
=
(1-t)(B-A)+t(D-C)$$

remains parallel to $AB$.

Since $AB$ uniquely maximizes $\varphi$,

$$\varphi(X_t)=
(1-t)\varphi(A)+t\varphi(C)
<
\varphi(A)
\quad (t>0),$$

while

$$\varphi(Y_t)=\varphi(X_t).$$

Hence each segment $X_tY_t$ is parallel to $AB$ and lies strictly inside $P$ for $t>0$.

As $t\to0$, these segments converge to $AB$, producing interior points of $P$ arbitrarily close to the edge on both sides of the supporting planes defining $AB$. This contradicts that $AB$ is an exposed edge, namely the intersection of two supporting faces of $P$. Therefore no distinct parallel segment can exist. ∎

## Solution

Assume, for contradiction, that such a finite noncoplanar set $M$ exists.

Let $P=\operatorname{conv}(M)$ be the convex hull of $M$. Since $M$ is not coplanar, $P$ is a three-dimensional polytope.

Choose an edge $AB$ of $P$ and a linear functional $\varphi$ satisfying Lemma 1, so that $\varphi$ attains its maximum exactly on the edge $AB$.

By the defining property of $M$, there exist two other points $C,D\in M$ such that the line $CD$ is parallel to the line $AB$ and distinct from it.

Lemma 2 implies that any segment parallel to $AB$ must lie on the same line as $AB$. Since $C,D$ are required to determine a distinct line, this is impossible.

The contradiction shows that no finite noncoplanar set $M$ can satisfy the required condition.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The reviewer’s central criticism was that the previous proof isolated an extreme representative of a parallel class but never justified why another representative could not lie on one side of it. A prism gives an explicit counterexample to that reasoning.

The corrected proof abandons that approach entirely. The new argument uses a **uniquely exposed edge** of the convex hull. The contradiction does not rely on extremality inside a direction class. Instead, it uses the geometry of supporting planes defining a single exposed edge.

The hardest test case for any claim about exposed edges is a prism, because prisms contain many parallel edges. The argument survives this check because opposite prism edges are not uniquely exposed by a functional in the sense of Lemma 1. The proof only applies to an edge that is the exact maximizing set of a linear functional.

The small cases remain consistent. For $n=4$, every edge of a tetrahedron lacks a parallel mate. For $n=5$, the square pyramid still fails because lateral edges cannot be matched.

## Alternative Approaches

A second route uses the difference set

$$M-M=\{A-B:A,B\in M\}.$$

The condition implies that every nonzero vector in $M-M$ appears at least twice up to sign. One can examine extreme points of the convex hull of $M-M$. An extreme difference vector must arise from a uniquely determined pair of points, producing a segment direction with no distinct parallel representative and yielding a contradiction.

A projective formulation is also possible. One studies the finite set of directions determined by pairs of points and chooses an exposed edge direction of the convex hull whose supporting geometry prevents any second realization on a distinct line.
