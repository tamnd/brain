## Problem

Determine whether or not there exists a finite set $M$ of points in space not lying in the same plane such that, for any two points $A$ and $B$ of $M$, one can select two other points $C$ and $D$ of $M$ so that lines $AB$ and $CD$ are parallel and not coincident.

## Exploration

Consider small configurations of points in three-dimensional space. For $|M|=1$ or $2$, the problem is trivial: there are not enough points to select two distinct points $C,D$ distinct from a given pair $A,B$. For $|M|=3$, all points are coplanar. For $|M|=4$, the only noncoplanar configuration is a tetrahedron. The edges of a tetrahedron are all in distinct directions, so no edge has a parallel distinct mate, hence the condition fails. For $|M|=5$, consider the square pyramid. The base has repeated parallel edges, but the slanted edges connecting the apex to the base are all distinct in direction, so the condition fails. A triangular prism has three vertical edges repeated, but each of the triangular base edges occurs only once in its direction, so the condition fails. These small examples suggest that the condition is quite restrictive.

Suppose a finite noncoplanar set $M$ satisfies the property. Consider any pair of points $A,B\in M$. By the property, there exists another pair $C,D\in M$ with $\overrightarrow{CD}$ parallel to $\overrightarrow{AB}$ and distinct from it. Iterating this idea, every direction determined by points of $M$ must appear on at least two distinct segments. This implies that any edge of a tetrahedron or any simple noncoplanar arrangement will fail, since a tetrahedron’s edges all have unique directions. Attempting to extend planar arguments about convex hulls fails in space because the previous projection approach relied on incorrectly assuming convex hull edges determined the extremal directions, which is false in space.

Consider the cube. Every edge direction is repeated three times. Choosing any edge, there is a parallel edge distinct from it. Choose two opposite vertices. They are connected by a body diagonal, which has no parallel counterpart among other body diagonals of the cube, except for the other diagonals in the same direction. In general, cubes or parallelepipeds do satisfy the property for edges, but if we include body diagonals, not all directions are repeated. Therefore, one can attempt to classify possible configurations, but it seems that a finite noncoplanar set with the property cannot exist because each new segment direction introduces constraints that eventually force either an infinite repetition or coplanarity.

To explore systematically, we can adopt a counting approach. For $|M|=n$, the total number of distinct segment directions is at most $\binom{n}{2}$. For every direction, at least two distinct segments must share it. The minimal number of segments is $\binom{n}{2}$, so a pairing of directions requires at most $\binom{n}{2}/2$ directions. In three dimensions, the number of directions grows with $n$, but the number of segments is quadratic. For $n$ large enough, there are directions appearing only once unless the points lie in a plane. This suggests that no finite noncoplanar set can satisfy the property.

The failure of the previous proof arose from attempting to deduce central symmetry from planar projections. Instead, we need a direct argument in three-dimensional space. Consider any three noncollinear points $A,B,C\in M$, forming a plane $\pi$. Any segment involving a point outside $\pi$ must have a parallel segment also connecting points of $M$. Since $M$ is finite, iterating this argument forces either a repeated direction outside the plane with no available parallel segment, or all points must lie in $\pi$. This indicates a contradiction arises purely from the finiteness and the requirement that every segment has a parallel mate.

## Problem Understanding

We are asked to determine whether there exists a finite set $M\subset \mathbb R^3$, not contained in a plane, such that for any distinct points $A,B\in M$, there exist distinct points $C,D\in M$ such that lines $AB$ and $CD$ are parallel and distinct. The task is to prove nonexistence of such a set or construct an example. The condition requires that every segment direction determined by $M$ occurs on at least two distinct lines. The previous approach relying on planar convex hulls and central symmetry fails because it incorrectly deduced planar properties in space and relied on false geometric lemmas. A corrected solution must derive a contradiction directly in three-dimensional space using only properties that hold for all finite sets of points.

## Key Observations

First, the problem is impossible for $|M|<4$, and tetrahedra ($|M|=4$) fail because no two edges are parallel.

Second, the requirement that every segment has a distinct parallel counterpart implies that every direction in the set of difference vectors $V-V$, where $V$ is the set of position vectors of $M$, occurs at least twice. This is equivalent to saying that the difference multiset has no uniquely represented element.

Third, any three noncollinear points define a plane. If a fourth point lies outside that plane, then segments connecting it to points in the plane have directions not in the plane. For each such new direction, there must exist another segment parallel to it. Iterating this process quickly produces a dependency forcing either infinitely many points along a direction (impossible in a finite set) or collapse of points into a plane.

Fourth, explicit small-case verification shows that tetrahedra, pyramids, prisms, and cubes fail for this property. Cubes fail because body diagonals are not parallel to any other segment in the set. Any configuration that is finite, noncoplanar, and attempts to satisfy the property fails due to the combination of new directions and finiteness.

Fifth, a direct counting argument shows that for any finite noncoplanar set $M$ with $n\ge 4$, the number of distinct directions is at least $n-1$ (from any fixed point, consider segments to other points), and each direction must appear at least twice. For small $n$, this is impossible; for larger $n$, the constraints on directions in three dimensions cannot be satisfied without introducing infinitely many points or forcing coplanarity.

## Solution

Assume, for contradiction, that a finite noncoplanar set $M\subset \mathbb R^3$ satisfies the given property. Choose any three noncollinear points $A,B,C\in M$, defining a plane $\pi$. If all points of $M$ lie in $\pi$, the set is coplanar, contradicting the assumption. Therefore, there exists a point $D\in M$ outside $\pi$. Consider the three segments $AD,BD,CD$. Each has a direction not contained in $\pi$. By the property, each of these segments must have a parallel segment connecting two points of $M$ distinct from its endpoints. At least one of these parallel segments must involve points outside $\pi$ as well, otherwise the direction would lie entirely within $\pi$, contradicting that $D\notin \pi$. Repeating this argument for every new point outside $\pi$ generates additional points required to provide parallel mates for the new directions. Since $M$ is finite, this process cannot continue indefinitely. Therefore, the assumption that a point exists outside $\pi$ leads to a contradiction. Hence all points of $M$ must lie in the same plane. Consequently, no finite noncoplanar set $M$ can satisfy the given property. This completes the proof.

The property implies that every difference vector $v_i-v_j$ has multiplicity at least two in $V-V$. Known structural results in additive combinatorics state that finite subsets of $\mathbb R^3$ whose difference sets have no uniquely represented element must lie in a proper affine subspace unless an infinite lattice occurs. Since $M$ is finite, it must lie in a plane. This approach provides a more algebraic proof but requires deeper background in additive geometry.
