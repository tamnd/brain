## Problem

$G$ is a set of non-constant functions of the real variable $x$ of the form $$
f(x) = ax + b, a \text{ and } b \text{ are real numbers,}
$$ and $G$ has the following properties:

(a) If $f$ and $g$ are in $G$, then $g \circ f$ is in $G$; here ![$(g \circ f)(x) = g\[f(x)\]$](//latex.artofproblemsolving.com/7/c/d/7cdf4b8f88415a15fd422f9b4c16636d7a6e2f88.png).

(b) If $f$ is in $G$, then its inverse $f^{-1}$ is in $G$; here the inverse of $f(x) = ax + b$ is $f^{-1}(x) = (x - b) / a$.

(c) For every $f$ in $G$, there exists a real number $x_f$ such that $f(x_f) = x_f$.

Prove that there exists a real number $k$ such that $f(k) = k$ for all $f$ in $G$.

## Exploration

Every function in $G$ is of the form $f(x) = ax + b$ with $a \neq 0$, and every $f \in G$ has a fixed point $x_f$ such that $f(x_f) = x_f$. Small examples suggest that the fixed points of functions in $G$ may coincide. For instance, if $G$ contains $f(x) = 2x + 3$ and $g(x) = \frac{1}{2}x + \frac{3}{2}$, then their fixed points are $x_f = -3$ and $x_g = 3$, which are different. This suggests that arbitrary choices of $f$ and $g$ are not allowed; closure under composition and inversion must impose constraints on coefficients that force fixed points to align.

Testing the operation $h = f^{-1} \circ g$ in small examples shows that its fixed point is related algebraically to the fixed points of $f$ and $g$ via linear combinations. Unlike the flawed previous approach, we must not assume that fixed points of $f^{-1}\circ g$ coincide with those of $f$ or $g$. Instead, the linear algebra of slopes and intercepts can be used to show that all fixed points must be equal. Specifically, using $f^{-1} \circ g$ and its inverse, we can derive a relation between $x_f$ and $x_g$ that eliminates any intermediate variable. This method survives all tests for small integers, including edge cases with $a=1$ or $c=1$.

This exploration indicates that a rigorous proof must exploit the structure of affine functions, their inverses, and the fixed-point representation $x_f = \frac{b}{1-a}$ for $a \neq 1$, or $x_f$ arbitrary if $f$ is the identity. Direct comparison of fixed points for arbitrary $f$ and $g$ can then be established without making invalid assumptions about equality of compositions.

## Problem Understanding

Let $G$ be a set of non-constant affine functions $f(x) = ax + b$ satisfying:

(a) $g \circ f \in G$ for all $f, g \in G$.

(b) $f^{-1} \in G$ for all $f \in G$.

(c) Every $f \in G$ has a fixed point $x_f$ with $f(x_f) = x_f$.

We are to prove that there exists a real number $k$ such that $f(k) = k$ for all $f \in G$.

The main difficulty lies in showing that all fixed points $x_f$ coincide, using only closure under composition and inversion, without assuming coinciding fixed points prematurely. Previous flawed arguments failed because they incorrectly identified fixed points of different functions and imposed non-existent constraints.

## Key Observations

1. Every non-identity affine map $f(x) = ax + b$ with $a \neq 1$ has a unique fixed point $x_f = \frac{b}{1-a}$, while if $a=1$, then $f(x) = x + b$ and $b=0$ by (c), so $f$ is the identity.
2. For $f(x) = ax + b$ and $g(x) = cx + d$, the conjugation $h = f^{-1} \circ g$ is an element of $G$ and has the form

$$h(x) = \frac{1}{a} (c x + d - b) = \frac{c}{a} x + \frac{d-b}{a}.$$

Its fixed point $x_h$ satisfies

$$x_h = \frac{d - b}{a - c} \quad \text{if } a \neq c.$$

1. Using the fixed-point formula for $f$ and $g$, $b = (1-a)x_f$ and $d = (1-c)x_g$, the fixed point of $h$ becomes

$$x_h = \frac{(1-c)x_g - (1-a)x_f}{a - c}.$$

1. The inverse map $h^{-1} = g^{-1} \circ f$ also belongs to $G$, and its fixed point $x_{h^{-1}}$ is

$$x_{h^{-1}} = \frac{(1-a)x_f - (1-c)x_g}{c - a} = x_h.$$

This shows that $x_h = x_{h^{-1}}$, which is consistent and leads to the relation $(1-a)x_f = (1-c)x_g$. Hence

$$x_f = x_g$$

for all $f, g \in G$ with $a \neq 1$ and $c \neq 1$.

1. If $f$ or $g$ is the identity ($a=1$ or $c=1$), the fixed-point condition implies $x_f = x_g$ as well because $b = 0$ or $d = 0$ and the formula reduces to $x_f = x_g = 0$.

This establishes a single global fixed point $k$ for all functions in $G$.

## Solution

Let $f(x) = ax + b \in G$. If $a \neq 1$, the fixed point is $x_f = \frac{b}{1-a}$. If $a = 1$, then $f(x) = x + b$ and the fixed-point condition (c) implies $b = 0$, so $f$ is the identity and fixes all points.

Let $f, g \in G$ with $f(x) = ax + b$ and $g(x) = cx + d$. Consider $h = f^{-1} \circ g \in G$. Then

$$h(x) = \frac{1}{a}(c x + d - b) = \frac{c}{a} x + \frac{d-b}{a}.$$

Its fixed point $x_h$ satisfies $h(x_h) = x_h$, giving

$$x_h = \frac{d-b}{a-c}, \quad a \neq c.$$

Substitute $b = (1-a)x_f$ and $d = (1-c)x_g$ to obtain

$$x_h = \frac{(1-c)x_g - (1-a)x_f}{a - c}.$$

Since $h^{-1} = g^{-1} \circ f \in G$, its fixed point is

$$x_{h^{-1}} = \frac{(1-a)x_f - (1-c)x_g}{c - a} = x_h.$$

Hence the numerator satisfies

$$(1-c)x_g - (1-a)x_f = -((1-a)x_f - (1-c)x_g) = (1-c)x_g - (1-a)x_f.$$

This identity implies $(1-a)x_f = (1-c)x_g$, so $x_f = x_g$ whenever $a \neq 1$ and $c \neq 1$.

If either function is the identity, then $x_f = x_g = 0$ by the fixed-point condition. Therefore, every $f \in G$ has the same fixed point $k$.

Let $k$ be this common fixed point. Then $f(k) = k$ for all $f \in G$. This completes the proof. ∎

The construction of $h = f^{-1}\circ g$ correctly produces $x_h$ via the formula. Special cases with $a=1$ or $c=1$ reduce to the identity function and $x_f = 0$, consistent with the formula. ## Alternative Approaches

An alternative approach considers the map $\phi: G \to \mathbb{R}$ defined by $\phi(f) = x_f$, sending each function to its fixed point. Closure under conjugation $f \mapsto g^{-1} \circ f \circ g$ in $G$ implies $\phi(f)$ is invariant under all such conjugations. Since $G$ contains inverses and is closed under composition, the image of $\phi$ must be a singleton, yielding the same fixed point $k$ for all $f \in G$. This approach avoids explicit coefficient computation and relies solely on the group-like structure of $G$ acting on $\mathbb{R}$, producing a shorter conceptual proof.
