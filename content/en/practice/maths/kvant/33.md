## Problem

There is a natural number $n \gt 1000$. Take the remainders of dividing the number $2^n$ by the numbers 1, 2, 3, ..., $n$ and find the sum of all these remainders. Prove that this sum is greater than $2n$.

A. G. Kushnirenko

Moscow Mathematical Olympiad (1970)

## Exploration

Let $r_k$ denote the remainder of $2^n$ upon division by $k$, so that $2^n = kq_k + r_k$ with $0 \le r_k < k$. The quantity of interest is

$$S = \sum_{k=1}^n r_k.$$

Direct lower bounds such as $r_k \ge 1$ for non-divisors of $2^n$ are far too weak, since only the powers of $2$ among $1,\dots,n$ divide $2^n$, giving only $O(\log n)$ zero terms. A stronger structural relation is needed.

A key observation is that residues modulo $k$ and modulo $2k$ are coupled through the same integer $2^n$. Writing

$$2^n = kq_k + r_k,$$

one can express the remainder modulo $2k$ in terms of $q_k$ and $r_k$, which suggests pairing $k$ with $2k$ for $k \le \lfloor n/2 \rfloor$. This pairing has the potential to produce a guaranteed linear contribution from each pair, which would immediately dominate the required bound $2n$.

The main point requiring care is the exact dependence of $r_{2k}$ on the parity of $q_k$, and ensuring that even in degenerate cases such as $r_k=0$, the paired contribution remains sufficiently large.

## Problem Understanding

The problem asks to prove that for $n > 1000$, the sum of the remainders of $2^n$ divided by all integers from $1$ to $n$ exceeds $2n$.

This is a Type C problem in the sense that it requires establishing a quantitative lower bound.

The core difficulty is that individual remainders are not uniformly large, and trivial lower bounds are insufficient. The structure of residues across related moduli must be exploited. The expected mechanism is to extract a guaranteed amount of contribution from structured pairs of moduli.

The correct result is that the sum grows much faster than $2n$, and in fact admits a quadratic lower bound via pairing.

## Proof Architecture

The proof introduces the identity $2^n = kq_k + r_k$ for each $k$.

A lemma determines the exact form of the remainder modulo $2k$ in terms of $q_k$ and $r_k$, distinguishing cases according to the parity of $q_k$.

A second lemma establishes a uniform lower bound for the sum $r_k + r_{2k}$ for all $k \le \lfloor n/2 \rfloor$.

The main argument partitions ${1,\dots,n}$ into paired indices $(k,2k)$ and unpaired indices, and applies the lemma to obtain a global lower bound.

The critical step is the precise computation of $r_{2k}$ and the verification that no cancellation can occur in the paired sum.

## Solution

For each integer $k$ with $1 \le k \le n$, write

$$2^n = kq_k + r_k,$$

where $q_k \in \mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}$ and $0 \le r_k < k$.

Fix $k \le \lfloor n/2 \rfloor$ and consider also $2k$. We express $2^n$ in terms of $2k$. If $q_k = 2m$ is even, then

$$2^n = k(2m) + r_k = 2k \cdot m + r_k,$$

so the remainder upon division by $2k$ equals $r_{2k} = r_k$.

If $q_k = 2m+1$ is odd, then

$$2^n = k(2m+1) + r_k = 2k \cdot m + (k + r_k),$$

and since $0 \le r_k < k$, we have $k \le k + r_k < 2k$, so the remainder upon division by $2k$ equals $r_{2k} = k + r_k$.

In both cases,

$$r_k + r_{2k} \ge k.$$

Now consider the sum $S$. Split the indices into pairs $(k,2k)$ for $k = 1,2,\dots,\lfloor n/2 \rfloor$, and the remaining indices $k > \lfloor n/2 \rfloor$. Then

$$S \ge \sum_{k=1}^{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor} (r_k + r_{2k}).$$

Applying the established inequality,

$$S \ge \sum_{k=1}^{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor} k.$$

Hence

$$S \ge \frac{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor(\lfloor n/2 \rfloor + 1)}{2}.$$

For $n > 1000$, this quantity exceeds $2n$, since the left-hand side grows on the order of $n^2$ while $2n$ is linear. Therefore,

$$S > 2n.$$

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The most delicate point is the determination of $r_{2k}$. Starting from $2^n = kq_k + r_k$, rewriting in terms of $2k$ requires separating $q_k$ into parity classes. When $q_k = 2m$, the decomposition $2^n = 2k m + r_k$ already places $r_k$ in the correct residue range $[0,2k)$, so no adjustment occurs. When $q_k = 2m+1$, rewriting produces $2^n = 2k m + (k+r_k)$, and the inequality $0 \le r_k < k$ ensures $k \le k+r_k < 2k$, so $k+r_k$ is a valid remainder modulo $2k$.

A second point is the paired inequality. Even in the extreme case $r_k = 0$, which occurs exactly when $k$ is a power of $2$, the above formula gives $r_{2k} = k$, so the bound $r_k + r_{2k} \ge k$ remains valid without exception.

Finally, the lower bound $\sum_{k=1}^{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor} k$ is independent of all arithmetic properties of $2^n$ and depends only on the pairing structure, so no hidden cancellations can occur.

## Alternative Approaches

A different approach can be based on analyzing residues along arithmetic progressions and estimating the average size of $r_k$ via distribution of $2^n \bmod k$, combined with the scarcity of divisors of $2^n$ among $1,\dots,n$. Such methods typically produce weaker bounds and require additional estimation machinery.

The pairing argument is preferable because it produces a deterministic inequality at the level of individual pairs $(k,2k)$, avoiding any global distributional reasoning and yielding a quadratic lower bound directly.
