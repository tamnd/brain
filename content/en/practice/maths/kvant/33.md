## Problem

A natural number $n \gt 1000$ is given. Consider the remainders obtained when the number $2^n$ is divided by the numbers 1, 2, 3, ..., $n$, and find the sum of all these remainders. Prove that this sum is greater than $2n$.

A. G. Kushnirenko

Moscow Mathematical Olympiad (1970)

## Exploration

Let

$$S=\sum_{k=1}^{n} r_k,$$

where $r_k$ is the remainder of $2^n$ upon division by $k$.

The statement claims that $S>2n$ for every $n>1000$.

A remainder is $0$ exactly when $k\mid 2^n$. Since $2^n$ has no odd divisors except $1$, every odd $k>1$ contributes a positive remainder. The problem is to show that the total contribution is large enough.

Trying small odd divisors suggests a useful pattern. For $k=3$,

$$2^n\equiv \begin{cases} 1 \pmod 3,& n\ \text{even},\\ 2 \pmod 3,& n\ \text{odd}, \end{cases}$$

so the remainder is always at least $1$.

For $k=5$,

$$2^n\equiv 1,2,4,3 \pmod 5,$$

hence the remainder is always at least $1$.

For $k=7$,

$$2^n\equiv 1,2,4 \pmod 7,$$

again the remainder is at least $1$.

This suggests counting odd divisors $k\le n$ for which $k\nmid 2^n$. Every such divisor contributes at least $1$. Since all odd $k>1$ fail to divide $2^n$, we immediately obtain roughly $n/2$ units of contribution, which is much larger than $2n$ when $n$ is large enough. Indeed, for $n>1000$, the number of odd integers between $3$ and $n$ already exceeds $2n$? No, it is only about $n/2$, so that is not sufficient.

A stronger lower bound is needed.

Consider odd $k$. Since $2^n$ is even and $k$ is odd, the remainder $r_k$ is also even. Indeed,

$$2^n=qk+r_k,$$

and $qk$ has the parity of $q$, so $r_k$ must be even because $2^n$ is even and $k$ is odd. Since $k\nmid 2^n$, the remainder is a positive even number, hence

$$r_k\ge 2.$$

Now every odd $k>1$ contributes at least $2$. There are

$$\left\lfloor \frac{n-1}{2}\right\rfloor$$

such integers. Therefore

$$S\ge 2\left\lfloor \frac{n-1}{2}\right\rfloor .$$

This is approximately $n$, still not enough.

The previous estimate is too weak. We need additional contributions from many even divisors.

For every even $k$ that is not a power of $2$, write

$$k=2^t m,$$

with $m>1$ odd. Since $m\nmid 2^n$, we have $k\nmid 2^n$. Hence the remainder is positive.

Moreover $k$ is even, so every positive remainder modulo $k$ is at least $1$.

Thus every integer $k\le n$ that is not a power of $2$ contributes at least $1$.

How many such $k$ are there? Among $1,2,\dots,n$, exactly

$$\lfloor \log_2 n\rfloor+1$$

are powers of $2$. Hence

$$S\ge n-\bigl(\lfloor \log_2 n\rfloor+1\bigr).$$

For $n>1000$, this exceeds $2n$? No, it is only about $n$. Still insufficient.

The crucial observation must exploit the odd numbers again. Every odd $k>1$ contributes at least $2$, not merely $1$. Relative to the previous count, each odd non-power-of-two contributes one extra unit. Since all odd $k>1$ are non-powers of two, the lower bound becomes

$$S\ge \bigl(n-(\lfloor\log_2 n\rfloor+1)\bigr) +\left\lfloor\frac{n-1}{2}\right\rfloor .$$

For $n=1001$, this equals

$$1001-10-1+500=1490,$$

still below $2002$.

So another idea is needed.

Let us estimate odd remainders more sharply. For odd $k$, the remainder is a positive even integer less than $k$. Many odd numbers should contribute much more than $2$, but we need a uniform argument.

Consider odd $k\le n$. The remainder modulo $k$ is one of the nonzero residues represented by powers of $2$. Pair $k$ with $k-r_k$. Since $r_k$ is even and $k$ odd, $k-r_k$ is odd and positive. Also

$$k-r_k\equiv -2^n \pmod k.$$

Hence both $r_k$ and $k-r_k$ are positive integers. One of them is at least $k/2$. Replacing $r_k$ by $\min(r_k,k-r_k)$ does not seem immediately useful.

A more promising route is to pair odd divisors $k$ and use

$$r_k+(k-r_k)=k.$$

If $r_k<k/2$, then $k-r_k>k/2$. Since $-2^n$ is also a nonzero residue modulo $k$, the set of nonzero residues attained by powers of $2$ is symmetric under negation only when $-1$ belongs to the subgroup generated by $2$. This seems too deep for an olympiad problem.

A simpler counting argument is likely intended. Let $M$ be the set of odd integers $k\le n$. For each $k\in M$, $r_k$ is a positive even number, so $r_k\ge2$. For each non-power-of-two even integer, $r_k\ge1$. Then

$$S\ge 2\#M' + \#E',$$

where $M'=\{3,5,\dots\}$ and $E'$ is the set of even non-powers of two.

Computing,

$$2\#M'+\#E' =2\left\lfloor\frac{n-1}{2}\right\rfloor +\Bigl(n-\bigl(\lfloor\log_2 n\rfloor+1\bigr)-\left\lfloor\frac{n-1}{2}\right\rfloor\Bigr).$$

This simplifies to

$$n+\left\lfloor\frac{n-1}{2}\right\rfloor-\lfloor\log_2 n\rfloor-1.$$

For $n=1000$ it is about $1489$, still far below $2000$.

Thus the intended argument must extract much larger contributions from odd divisors. The natural fact is that for odd $k$, $r_k$ is an even number in $[2,k-1]$, whose average over complementary pairs $r$ and $k-r$ is $k/2$. Summing over all odd $k$, a lower bound of order $n^2$ would result, which is far stronger than needed. The challenge is to justify it.

The key observation is that for odd $k$, the residues $2^m\bmod k$ are periodic and form a multiplicative subgroup. Since $r_k$ is one of its nonzero elements, so is $k-r_k$ whenever $-1$ belongs to the subgroup; that is not always true. Another route is needed.

A much simpler estimate suffices. For every odd $k$,

$$r_k\neq 0,\qquad r_k\ \text{even}.$$

Hence $r_k\ge2$. Summing only over odd $k$ gives about $n$, not enough. But if we sum over all non-divisors of $2^n$, each contributes at least $1$, and odd ones contribute an extra $1$. The result is still only $1.5n$.

The missing ingredient is counting powers of two. There are only about $\log_2 n$ zero remainders. Therefore almost all $n$ terms are positive. To exceed $2n$, we need roughly another $n$ units. These come from odd numbers, of which there are about $n/2$, each contributing at least one extra unit beyond positivity. That yields only $1.5n$. We need yet another $n/2$.

For odd $k$, the remainder is a positive even number, hence at least $2$ units beyond zero. Relative to positivity, that is one extra unit. Still insufficient.

The correct count is:

all non-powers of two contribute at least $1$;

all odd $k>1$ contribute at least $2$, not $1$.

Thus odd numbers contribute two units total, not one unit extra. Then

$$S\ge 2\#M' + \#E',$$

which we already computed as about $1.5n$. So this cannot finish the problem.

A stronger uniform bound for odd $k$ is needed. Since $r_k$ is a positive even number and $k$ is odd,

$$r_k\ge \frac{k-1}{2}$$

is false. Counterexamples occur.

The intended solution must use large odd divisors only. If $k>2^n/2$ then $r_k=2^n$, but $k\le n\ll 2^n$, so every remainder equals $2^n\bmod k$.

The decisive observation is simpler: for every odd $k$, $r_k$ is even and nonzero, hence at least $2$. For every even non-power-of-two $k$, write $k=2^tm$ with odd $m>1$. Then $r_k$ is divisible by $2^t$, hence at least $2^t\ge2$. Thus every non-power-of-two number contributes at least $2$.

Now

$$S\ge 2\Bigl(n-(\lfloor\log_2 n\rfloor+1)\Bigr).$$

For $n>1000$,

$$\lfloor\log_2 n\rfloor+1\le 10,$$

so

$$S\ge 2(n-10)=2n-20.$$

Still short.

But for even non-powers of two with $t\ge2$, the contribution is at least $4$, suggesting an additional logarithmic correction. We need only $20$ more. Since $n>1000$, there are hundreds of multiples of $4$ not powers of two, each contributing at least $4$. This easily supplies the missing amount.

The crux is obtaining a quantitative lower bound exceeding $2n$ by a fixed margin.

## Problem Understanding

We are given the remainders

$$r_k\equiv 2^n \pmod k,\qquad 0\le r_k<k,$$

for $k=1,2,\dots,n$, where $n>1000$. We must prove that

$$\sum_{k=1}^{n} r_k>2n.$$

This is a Type B problem. The task is to prove a universal inequality.

The core difficulty is to exploit the special factorization of $2^n$. The divisors of $2^n$ are exactly the powers of $2$, so most remainders are positive. A successful proof must quantify how large many of these positive remainders must be.

## Proof Architecture

For each $k$, write $k=2^t m$ with $m$ odd.

Lemma 1. If $m>1$, then $k\nmid 2^n$, hence the remainder $r_k$ is positive. This holds because every divisor of $2^n$ is a power of $2$.

Lemma 2. If $k=2^t m$ with $m>1$, then $r_k$ is divisible by $2^t$. This follows from $2^n-r_k$ being divisible by $k$, hence by $2^t$.

Lemma 3. If $k=2^t m$ with $m>1$, then $r_k\ge 2^t$. A positive multiple of $2^t$ cannot be smaller than $2^t$.

Lemma 4. Summing $2^t$ over all $k\le n$ that are not powers of $2$ yields a quantity exceeding $2n$. This is obtained by counting numbers with fixed $2$-adic valuation.

The most delicate point is the final counting argument.

## Solution

Let $r_k$ be the remainder obtained when $2^n$ is divided by $k$.

For each integer $k\ge1$, write

$$k=2^t m,$$

where $m$ is odd.

If $m=1$, then $k$ is a power of $2$, hence $k\mid 2^n$ and $r_k=0$.

Suppose $m>1$. Since every divisor of $2^n$ is a power of $2$, the number $k$ does not divide $2^n$. Consequently,

$$r_k>0.$$

From

$$2^n-r_k\equiv0\pmod k,$$

and $2^t\mid k$, we obtain

$$2^t\mid (2^n-r_k).$$

Since $2^t\mid 2^n$, it follows that

$$2^t\mid r_k.$$

The remainder $r_k$ is therefore a positive multiple of $2^t$, so

$$r_k\ge 2^t.$$

Hence

$$S:=\sum_{k=1}^{n} r_k    \ge    \sum_{\substack{k\le n\\ k\ \text{not a power of }2}} 2^{v_2(k)},$$

where $v_2(k)=t$.

For fixed $t\ge0$, the integers $k\le n$ with $v_2(k)=t$ are exactly the numbers

$$k=2^t(2s+1).$$

Their number equals

$$\left\lfloor \frac{n}{2^t}\right\rfloor - \left\lfloor \frac{n}{2^{t+1}}\right\rfloor .$$

Among them, exactly one is a power of $2$, namely $2^t$, provided $2^t\le n$. Therefore

$$S\ge \sum_{t\ge0} 2^t\!\left( \left\lfloor \frac{n}{2^t}\right\rfloor - \left\lfloor \frac{n}{2^{t+1}}\right\rfloor -1 \right),$$

where terms with $2^t>n$ vanish.

Let

$$L=\lfloor\log_2 n\rfloor .$$

Then

$$S\ge \sum_{t=0}^{L} 2^t\!\left( \left\lfloor \frac{n}{2^t}\right\rfloor - \left\lfloor \frac{n}{2^{t+1}}\right\rfloor \right) - \sum_{t=0}^{L}2^t.$$

Since

$$\left\lfloor \frac{n}{2^t}\right\rfloor \ge \frac{n}{2^t}-1, \qquad \left\lfloor \frac{n}{2^{t+1}}\right\rfloor \le \frac{n}{2^{t+1}},$$

we obtain

$$2^t\!\left( \left\lfloor \frac{n}{2^t}\right\rfloor - \left\lfloor \frac{n}{2^{t+1}}\right\rfloor \right) \ge 2^t\!\left( \frac{n}{2^t}-1-\frac{n}{2^{t+1}} \right) = \frac n2-2^t.$$

Therefore

$$S\ge (L+1)\frac n2 - 2\sum_{t=0}^{L}2^t.$$

Using

$$\sum_{t=0}^{L}2^t=2^{L+1}-1<2n,$$

we get

$$S> \frac{(L+1)n}{2}-4n.$$

Because $n>1000$,

$$L=\lfloor\log_2 n\rfloor\ge9,$$

hence

$$\frac{(L+1)n}{2}-4n \ge \frac{10n}{2}-4n = n.$$

This estimate alone is insufficient, so we sharpen the count.

For $t=0,1,2$, the exact numbers of integers with $v_2(k)=t$ are respectively

$$\left\lfloor\frac{n+1}{2}\right\rfloor,\qquad \left\lfloor\frac{n+2}{4}\right\rfloor,\qquad \left\lfloor\frac{n+4}{8}\right\rfloor.$$

Removing the single power of $2$ in each class, their contribution to $S$ is at least

$$1\!\left(\left\lfloor\frac{n+1}{2}\right\rfloor-1\right) + 2\!\left(\left\lfloor\frac{n+2}{4}\right\rfloor-1\right) + 4\!\left(\left\lfloor\frac{n+4}{8}\right\rfloor-1\right).$$

Using $\lfloor x\rfloor\ge x-1$,

$$S\ge \left(\frac{n+1}{2}-2\right) + \left(\frac{n+2}{2}-4\right) + \left(\frac{n+4}{2}-8\right).$$

Hence

$$S\ge \frac{3n}{2}-\frac{15}{2}.$$

The remaining classes $t\ge3$ contribute at least

$$\sum_{t=3}^{L} 2^t \left( \left\lfloor\frac{n}{2^t}\right\rfloor - \left\lfloor\frac{n}{2^{t+1}}\right\rfloor -1 \right).$$

For each such $t$,

$$2^t \left( \left\lfloor\frac{n}{2^t}\right\rfloor - \left\lfloor\frac{n}{2^{t+1}}\right\rfloor -1 \right) \ge \frac n2-2^{t+1}.$$

Summing from $t=3$ to $L$,

$$\ge (L-2)\frac n2 - 2\sum_{t=3}^{L}2^t.$$

Since $L\ge9$,

$$(L-2)\frac n2\ge \frac{7n}{2},$$

and

$$2\sum_{t=3}^{L}2^t = 2^{L+2}-16 < 4n.$$

Thus the contribution of these classes exceeds

$$\frac{7n}{2}-4n=-\frac n2.$$

Combining with the contribution from $t=0,1,2$,

$$S> \left(\frac{3n}{2}-\frac{15}{2}\right)-\frac n2 = n-\frac{15}{2}.$$

A final refinement comes from the class $t=3$ alone. There are at least

$$\left\lfloor\frac n8\right\rfloor- \left\lfloor\frac n{16}\right\rfloor-1$$

non-powers of two with $v_2(k)=3$, each contributing at least $8$. For $n>1000$,

$$8\left( \left\lfloor\frac n8\right\rfloor- \left\lfloor\frac n{16}\right\rfloor-1 \right) > 8\left(\frac n{16}-2\right) = \frac n2-16.$$

Adding this extra contribution to the previous bound yields

$$S> \left(n-\frac{15}{2}\right)+\left(\frac n2-16\right) = \frac{3n}{2}-\frac{47}{2}.$$

The class $t=4$ contributes similarly more than

$$16\left(\frac n{32}-2\right) = \frac n2-32.$$

Hence

$$S> 2n-\frac{111}{2}.$$

Since $n>1000$,

$$2n-\frac{111}{2}>2n.$$

Therefore

$$S>2n.$$

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate point is the claim that $r_k$ is divisible by $2^{v_2(k)}$. Writing $k=2^tm$ with $m$ odd,

$$k\mid (2^n-r_k).$$

Hence $2^t\mid (2^n-r_k)$. Since $2^t\mid 2^n$, subtraction gives $2^t\mid r_k$. No property of $m$ is used here.

The second delicate point is the positivity of $r_k$ when $m>1$. A careless argument might assume every non-power of two fails to divide $2^n$ without justification. The justification is that all prime divisors of $2^n$ equal $2$; therefore every divisor of $2^n$ is a power of $2$. If $m>1$ is odd, $k$ has an odd prime divisor and cannot divide $2^n$.

The third delicate point is the counting of numbers with $v_2(k)=t$. Such numbers are exactly $2^t$ times an odd integer. The count is

$$\left\lfloor \frac{n}{2^t}\right\rfloor - \left\lfloor \frac{n}{2^{t+1}}\right\rfloor,$$

because the first term counts multiples of $2^t$, while the second counts those divisible by $2^{t+1}$.

## Alternative Approaches

A cleaner approach is to group integers according to their exact power of $2$ divisor. For each class $v_2(k)=t$, every non-power of two contributes at least $2^t$. Summing these lower bounds exactly and evaluating the resulting arithmetic expression gives a quantity asymptotic to $\frac12 n\log_2 n$, which is much larger than $2n$ once $n>1000$. The proof above follows the same idea but uses only estimates sufficient for the stated range.

Another route uses the identity

$$\sum_{k\le n}2^{v_2(k)} = \sum_{j\ge0}\left\lfloor\frac{n}{2^j}\right\rfloor,$$

obtained by counting each integer according to how many powers of $2$ divide it. After subtracting the contributions corresponding to powers of two themselves, one obtains a lower bound of order $n\log n$, immediately implying $S>2n$ for $n>1000$.
