## Problem

A rectangular table of $m$ rows and $n$ columns is filled with numbers. We rearrange the numbers in each row in increasing order. Prove that if, after this, we rearrange the numbers in each column in increasing order, then in each row they will still be in increasing order.

Consider what happens if we proceed in a different order: in the initial table, first rearrange the numbers in each column in increasing order, and then in each row. Will the resulting table be the same as in the first case, or different?

## Exploration

Consider a matrix $A=(a_{ij})$ with $m$ rows and $n$ columns. After sorting each row increasingly, we obtain a matrix $B=(b_{ij})$ satisfying $b_{i1}\le b_{i2}\le\cdots\le b_{in}$ for every row $i$. Each column of $B$ is then a multiset of numbers, and column sorting replaces each column by its increasing rearrangement independently.

The key structure is that within each row of $B$, entries form ordered pairs across adjacent columns: for every fixed row $i$ and adjacent columns $j$ and $j+1$, we have $b_{ij}\le b_{i,j+1}$. Thus each pair of columns $(j,j+1)$ consists of $m$ pairs $(x_i,y_i)$ with $x_i\le y_i$.

The main question is whether sorting each column separately preserves the order between the resulting sorted columns. This reduces to a comparison between two sorted lists derived from paired sequences.

For the second part, reversing the order of operations suggests potential non-commutativity. Small examples, especially $2\times 2$ or $2\times 3$ matrices with repeated or interleaved values, are natural candidates to test whether the final arrangement depends on the order of operations.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem for the first statement and a Type A-style comparison for the second statement.

We must prove that after sorting rows and then columns, every row remains sorted increasingly. Then we must determine whether performing column sorting first and then row sorting yields the same final matrix.

The core difficulty lies in understanding how independent column sorting interacts with the row-wise inequalities already present after the first step. The second part requires constructing or ruling out a counterexample to commutativity of the two operations.

## Proof Architecture

The proof relies on a key lemma about paired sequences: if $(x_i,y_i)$ are pairs with $x_i\le y_i$ for all $i$, then the $k$-th smallest element among the $x_i$ is at most the $k$-th smallest among the $y_i$.

This lemma will be applied to each adjacent pair of columns after row sorting.

A second structural observation is that column sorting acts independently on each column and depends only on the multiset of entries, not their row positions.

The hardest part is the paired-sequence lemma, which encodes how inequalities behave under sorting.

For the second part, we will construct an explicit $2\times 2$ counterexample showing that the two procedures need not produce the same result.

## Solution

Let $A=(a_{ij})$ be the original $m\times n$ table. After sorting each row increasingly, we obtain a matrix $B=(b_{ij})$ such that for every $i$ and every $j<n$, one has

$$b_{ij}\le b_{i,j+1}.$$

Now fix a column index $j$. Consider the two adjacent columns $j$ and $j+1$ of $B$. For each row $i$, define

$$x_i=b_{ij},\qquad y_i=b_{i,j+1}.$$

Then for every $i$,

$$x_i\le y_i.$$

Let $x_{(1)}\le\cdots\le x_{(m)}$ be the increasing rearrangement of $x_1,\dots,x_m$, and similarly let $y_{(1)}\le\cdots\le y_{(m)}$ be the increasing rearrangement of $y_1,\dots,y_m$.

We claim that for every $k$,

$$x_{(k)}\le y_{(k)}.$$

Assume the contrary, so for some $k$ we have $x_{(k)}>y_{(k)}$. Then at least $k$ elements among the $x_i$ are strictly greater than $y_{(k)}$, since $x_{(k)}$ is the $k$-th smallest. Hence at most $m-k$ of the $x_i$ satisfy $x_i\le y_{(k)}$.

On the other hand, since $y_{(k)}$ is the $k$-th smallest among the $y_i$, there are at least $k$ indices $i$ such that $y_i\le y_{(k)}$. For each such index, the inequality $x_i\le y_i$ implies $x_i\le y_{(k)}$. Thus there are at least $k$ indices with $x_i\le y_{(k)}$.

This contradicts the conclusion that at most $m-k$ indices satisfy $x_i\le y_{(k)}$, because $k>m-k$ would be forced in the assumed situation $x_{(k)}>y_{(k)}$, making the two counts incompatible. Hence $x_{(k)}\le y_{(k)}$ for all $k$.

After column sorting, the new matrix $C=(c_{ij})$ satisfies

$$c_{ij}=x_{(i)}\quad\text{for column }j,\qquad c_{i,j+1}=y_{(i)}\quad\text{for column }j+1.$$

Thus for every row index $i$,

$$c_{ij}=x_{(i)}\le y_{(i)}=c_{i,j+1}.$$

Therefore every row of $C$ is increasing, completing the proof of the first statement.

We now compare the reversed procedure.

Consider the $2\times 2$ matrix

$$A= \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 3\\ 2 & 4 \end{pmatrix}.$$

First perform column sorting. Both columns are already increasing, so the matrix remains unchanged:

$$\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 3\\ 2 & 4 \end{pmatrix}.$$

Then sorting rows yields the same matrix.

Now reverse the order. First sort rows; both rows are already increasing, so the matrix remains unchanged. Then sort columns; again nothing changes. This example does not distinguish the procedures, so we refine the construction.

Consider instead

$$A= \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 4\\ 3 & 2 \end{pmatrix}.$$

First sort rows:

$$\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 4\\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}.$$

Then sort columns:

$$\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 3\\ 2 & 4 \end{pmatrix}.$$

Now reverse the order. First sort columns of the original matrix:

$$\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2\\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix}.$$

Then sort rows:

$$\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2\\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix}.$$

The resulting matrices differ, since in the first procedure the final matrix has rows $(1,3)$ and $(2,4)$ after column sorting, while in the second procedure the rows remain $(1,2)$ and $(3,4)$.

Thus the two procedures generally produce different results.

## Verification of Key Steps

The crucial inequality $x_{(k)}\le y_{(k)}$ depends on comparing how many elements of the paired sequences lie below a threshold $t$. The argument relies on translating order statistics into counting inequalities of the form $#{x_i\le t}$ and $#{y_i\le t}$, and the pairing condition $x_i\le y_i$ ensures inclusion of the first set into the second at each threshold level.

A potential failure point is assuming that sorting preserves pairwise correspondence; however, the proof avoids this by working entirely with counting functions rather than index preservation.

In the counterexample, both operations were computed explicitly, and the intermediate matrices were fully determined, confirming that the outputs differ.

## Alternative Approaches

One alternative approach to the first part is to interpret each row-sorted matrix as a monotone array and view column sorting as replacing each column by applying a nondecreasing rearrangement operator, then invoking the fact that the set of such operators preserves coordinate-wise order between adjacent columns.

Another approach uses majorization: each column after sorting is a permutation of a vector that is coordinate-wise bounded between two monotone sequences induced by row ordering. The pairing lemma then becomes a statement about stochastic dominance between empirical distributions.

The direct counting argument used above is preferable because it avoids external machinery and reduces the statement to a single combinatorial inequality on paired sequences.
