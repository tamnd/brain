## Problem

Prove that for any odd number $a$ there exists a natural number $b$ such that $2^b-1$ is divisible by $a$.

## Exploration

The statement asks for every odd positive integer $a$. Trying small cases:

For $a=3$, $2^2-1=3$.

For $a=5$, $2^4-1=15$.

For $a=7$, $2^3-1=7$.

For $a=9$, $2^6-1=63$.

For $a=15$, $2^4-1=15$.

The exponents appearing are not simply related to $a$, but in each case some power of $2$ leaves remainder $1$ modulo $a$.

Since $a$ is odd, $\gcd(a,2)=1$. Looking at the sequence of remainders

$$1,2,2^2,\dots,2^a$$

modulo $a$, there are $a+1$ numbers but only $a$ possible residue classes. Two of them must coincide. If

$$2^m\equiv 2^n \pmod a,\qquad m>n,$$

then

$$2^n(2^{m-n}-1)\equiv0\pmod a.$$

The crucial point is whether one may cancel $2^n$ modulo $a$. Since $a$ is odd, $2$ is relatively prime to $a$, hence so is $2^n$. Then $a$ must divide $2^{m-n}-1$.

The step most likely to hide an error is precisely this cancellation. If $a$ were even, it would fail. For example, modulo $6$ one has $2^2\equiv2\pmod6$, yet $6\nmid(2-1)$. Thus the oddness of $a$ is exactly what makes the argument work.

## Problem Understanding

We must show that for every odd natural number $a$, there exists a natural number $b$ such that

$$a\mid(2^b-1).$$

This is a Type D problem, an existence statement.

The core difficulty is producing an exponent $b$ for an arbitrary odd modulus $a$. Since the problem asks only for existence, it is enough to prove that some positive power of $2$ is congruent to $1$ modulo $a$.

The reason this should be true is that the powers of $2$ modulo $a$ can assume only finitely many residue classes. Two powers must eventually coincide, and because $2$ is invertible modulo every odd $a$, their difference in exponents yields a power congruent to $1$.

## Proof Architecture

First, among the $a+1$ numbers $1,2,2^2,\dots,2^a$, two have the same residue modulo $a$, because there are only $a$ residue classes modulo $a$.

Second, if $2^m\equiv2^n\pmod a$ with $m>n$, then $a$ divides $2^n(2^{m-n}-1)$.

Third, since $a$ is odd, $\gcd(a,2^n)=1$; hence from $a\mid2^n(2^{m-n}-1)$ it follows that $a\mid(2^{m-n}-1)$.

The hardest step is the third one, because the argument depends essentially on the coprimality of $a$ and $2^n$.

## Solution

Consider the $a+1$ integers

$$1,2,2^2,\dots,2^a.$$

Reduce them modulo $a$. There are only $a$ residue classes modulo $a$, so by the pigeonhole principle two of these numbers have the same residue. Hence there exist integers $m,n$ satisfying

$$0\le n<m\le a$$

and

$$2^m\equiv2^n\pmod a.$$

Subtracting, we obtain

$$2^m-2^n\equiv0\pmod a,$$

or

$$2^n(2^{m-n}-1)\equiv0\pmod a.$$

Thus

$$a\mid2^n(2^{m-n}-1).$$

Since $a$ is odd, $\gcd(a,2)=1$. Consequently,

$$\gcd(a,2^n)=1.$$

A standard divisibility property states that if $\gcd(a,c)=1$ and $a\mid cd$, then $a\mid d$. Applying it with

$$c=2^n,\qquad d=2^{m-n}-1,$$

gives

$$a\mid(2^{m-n}-1).$$

Therefore, with

$$b=m-n,$$

we have

$$a\mid(2^b-1).$$

A natural number $b$ with the required property exists for every odd natural number $a$.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the pigeonhole argument. The list

$$1,2,2^2,\dots,2^a$$

contains exactly $a+1$ integers. Modulo $a$ there are exactly $a$ residue classes, namely $0,1,\dots,a-1$. Hence two members of the list must have the same residue. No further assumptions are needed.

The second delicate step is passing from

$$a\mid2^n(2^{m-n}-1)$$

to

$$a\mid(2^{m-n}-1).$$

This uses $\gcd(a,2^n)=1$. Because $a$ is odd, no factor $2$ divides $a$, so $2^n$ is relatively prime to $a$. Without this fact the conclusion is false. For example,

$$6\mid2(3),$$

but $6\nmid3$.

A third point is ensuring that the resulting exponent is positive. Since the repeated residues come from distinct powers with $m>n$, the number

$$b=m-n$$

satisfies $b\ge1$, so it is indeed a natural number.
