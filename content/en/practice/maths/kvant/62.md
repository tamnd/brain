## Problem

Prove that for any odd number $a$, there exists a natural number $b$ such that $2^b-1$ is divisible by $a$.

## Exploration

Let $a$ be an odd natural number. We seek $b$ such that $2^b \equiv 1 \pmod a$. Small examples suggest the pattern: for $a=1$, any $b$ works; for $a=3$, $2^2=4 \equiv 1 \pmod 3$; for $a=5$, $2^4=16 \equiv 1 \pmod 5$; for $a=7$, $2^3=8 \equiv 1 \pmod 7$. This indicates that powers of $2$ modulo $a$ eventually repeat in a way that produces $1$.

The sequence of residues $2^0,2^1,2^2,\dots$ modulo $a$ takes values in a finite set of size $a$. Therefore two powers must coincide modulo $a$. If $2^i \equiv 2^j \pmod a$ with $i<j$, then $a \mid 2^i(2^{j-i}-1)$. The key point is that since $a$ is odd, $2^i$ is invertible modulo $a$, so the factor $2^i$ can be removed, leaving $a \mid 2^{j-i}-1$. This produces the desired exponent.

The delicate point is justifying the cancellation of $2^i$ modulo $a$, which depends on $\gcd(2,a)=1$, guaranteed by oddness.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type D problem, requiring construction of a natural number $b$ such that $2^b-1$ is divisible by a given odd natural number $a$.

The core difficulty is showing that a power of $2$ is congruent to $1$ modulo an arbitrary odd modulus without invoking heavy machinery, relying instead on finiteness of residue classes and invertibility of $2$ modulo $a$.

## Proof Architecture

The proof proceeds by considering the sequence of residues $2^0,2^1,\dots,2^a$ modulo $a$ and applying the pigeonhole principle to obtain a repetition.

A lemma establishes that if $2^i \equiv 2^j \pmod a$ with $i<j$, then $a \mid 2^{j-i}-1$, using the fact that $\gcd(2,a)=1$ allows cancellation of $2^i$ modulo $a$.

The main argument extracts $b=j-i$ and concludes $2^b \equiv 1 \pmod a$.

The most delicate point is the cancellation step, which depends critically on the oddness of $a$.

## Solution

Consider the $a+1$ integers $2^0,2^1,\dots,2^a$ and their residues modulo $a$. Since there are only $a$ possible residues modulo $a$, there exist indices $0 \le i < j \le a$ such that

$$2^i \equiv 2^j \pmod a.$$

This congruence implies that $a \mid (2^j - 2^i)$, hence

$$a \mid 2^i(2^{j-i}-1).$$

Since $a$ is odd, it satisfies $\gcd(a,2)=1$. Therefore $\gcd(a,2^i)=1$ for every natural number $i$. From $a \mid 2^i(2^{j-i}-1)$ and $\gcd(a,2^i)=1$, it follows that

$$a \mid (2^{j-i}-1).$$

Setting $b=j-i$, which is a positive integer, yields

$$2^b \equiv 1 \pmod a,$$

so $2^b-1$ is divisible by $a$.

This completes the proof.

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the application of the pigeonhole principle. The $a+1$ residues of $2^k$ modulo $a$ must contain a repetition because the residue system modulo $a$ has exactly $a$ elements, so two indices $i<j$ exist with equal residues.

The second delicate step is the cancellation of $2^i$ from the congruence $2^i(2^{j-i}-1) \equiv 0 \pmod a$. Since $a$ is odd, no prime divisor of $a$ equals $2$, so $2$ is invertible modulo $a$, and therefore $2^i$ is also invertible modulo $a$. This justifies deducing $2^{j-i}-1 \equiv 0 \pmod a$.

The final step is the positivity of $b=j-i$, which follows from strict inequality of indices in the chosen repetition.

## Alternative Approaches

An alternative proof uses the fact that the multiplicative group of units modulo $a$ has finite order $\varphi(a)$ and that $2$ lies in this group because $a$ is odd. By Lagrange’s theorem, the order of $2$ divides $\varphi(a)$, hence $2^{\varphi(a)} \equiv 1 \pmod a$.

Another approach constructs the same conclusion purely combinatorially by tracking the remainders of successive divisions of $2^k$ by $a$ and analyzing the induced directed graph on residues.
