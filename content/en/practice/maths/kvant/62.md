## Problem

Prove that for any odd number $a$ there exists a natural number $b$ such that $2^b-1$ is divisible by $a$.

## Exploration

Take several odd numbers and look for exponents $b$ such that $2^b-1$ is divisible by the number.

For $a=3$,

$2^2-1=3.$

For $a=5$,

$2^4-1=16-1=15,$

which is divisible by $5$.

For $a=7$,

$2^3-1=7.$

For $a=9$,

$2^6-1=64-1=63,$

which is divisible by $9$.

The exponents are not always equal to $a-1$, but several of them divide $a-1$. This suggests looking at the remainders of powers of $2$ modulo $a$.

Since $a$ is odd, none of the numbers

$1,2,2^2,2^3,\dots$

is divisible by $a$. Hence every power of $2$ leaves one of the nonzero residues modulo $a$. There are only finitely many such residues, namely $a-1$ of them. If we consider sufficiently many powers, two of them must leave the same remainder.

Suppose

$2^m \equiv 2^n \pmod a,$

with $m>n$. Then

$2^n(2^{m-n}-1)\equiv 0\pmod a.$

The dangerous step is cancelling $2^n$. Cancellation modulo $a$ is valid only if $2^n$ is relatively prime to $a$. Since $a$ is odd, $\gcd(2,a)=1$, hence $\gcd(2^n,a)=1$. Then cancellation is justified, giving

$2^{m-n}\equiv 1\pmod a.$

Thus $a$ divides $2^{m-n}-1$.

The crucial point is exactly the cancellation step. If $a$ were even, the argument would fail. For instance, modulo $6$,

$2^2\equiv 2 \pmod 6,$

but cancelling the factor $2$ would incorrectly give

$2\equiv 1 \pmod 6.$

## Problem Understanding

We must prove that for every odd natural number $a$, there exists a natural number $b$ such that

$a \mid (2^b-1).$

This is a Type B problem, a pure proof problem.

The core difficulty is showing that some power of $2$ becomes congruent to $1$ modulo $a$. Since there are only finitely many residue classes modulo $a$, repeated powers of $2$ must eventually repeat a remainder. The proof then depends on converting equality of two powers into a congruence of the form

$2^b\equiv 1\pmod a,$

and this requires justified cancellation modulo $a$.

## Proof Architecture

First lemma: among the $a$ numbers

$2^0,2^1,\dots,2^{a-1},$

two have the same remainder modulo $a$; this follows from the pigeonhole principle because there are only $a-1$ nonzero residue classes modulo an odd number $a$.

Second lemma: if

$2^m\equiv 2^n\pmod a$

with $m>n$ and $a$ odd, then

$2^{m-n}\equiv 1\pmod a;$

this follows because $\gcd(2^n,a)=1$, so cancellation modulo $a$ is valid.

Final step: from

$2^{m-n}\equiv 1\pmod a$

deduce that

$a\mid (2^{m-n}-1),$

which gives the required exponent $b=m-n$.

The most delicate point is the cancellation lemma. A careless argument may divide by $2^n$ without checking coprimality with $a$.

## Solution

Let

$1,2,2^2,\dots,2^{a-1}$

be the first $a$ powers of $2$ modulo the odd number $a$.

Since $a$ is odd,

$\gcd(2,a)=1.$

Hence no power of $2$ is divisible by $a$. Thus each of the numbers

$1,2,2^2,\dots,2^{a-1}$

has a nonzero remainder modulo $a$.

There are only $a-1$ nonzero residue classes modulo $a$, namely

$1,2,\dots,a-1.$

But we have $a$ numbers listed above. By the pigeonhole principle, two of them have the same remainder modulo $a$.

Hence there exist integers $m,n$ with

$0\le n<m\le a-1$

such that

$2^m\equiv 2^n\pmod a.$

Subtracting,

$2^m-2^n\equiv 0\pmod a,$

so

$2^n(2^{m-n}-1)\equiv 0\pmod a.$

Since $a$ is odd,

$\gcd(2^n,a)=1.$

Therefore $2^n$ has a multiplicative inverse modulo $a$, and we may cancel the factor $2^n$. We obtain

$2^{m-n}-1\equiv 0\pmod a.$

Thus

$a\mid (2^{m-n}-1).$

If we set

$b=m-n,$

then $b$ is a natural number and

$a\mid (2^b-1).$

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the counting argument. We considered the $a$ numbers

$2^0,2^1,\dots,2^{a-1}.$

Because $a$ is odd, none is congruent to $0$ modulo $a$. Thus these $a$ numbers occupy only the $a-1$ nonzero residue classes. Repetition is unavoidable.

The second delicate step is cancellation modulo $a$. From

$2^n(2^{m-n}-1)\equiv 0\pmod a$

we concluded

$2^{m-n}-1\equiv 0\pmod a.$

This is justified because

$\gcd(2^n,a)=1.$

If this coprimality failed, cancellation could be false. For example,

$2\cdot 2\equiv 2\cdot 1\pmod 6,$

but

$2\not\equiv 1\pmod 6.$

The oddness of $a$ is exactly what prevents this failure.

The third delicate point is ensuring that the exponent obtained is positive. Since $m>n$, the difference

$b=m-n$

satisfies

$b\ge 1,$

so it is indeed a natural number.

## Alternative Approaches

A different proof uses Euler's theorem. Since $a$ is odd,

$\gcd(2,a)=1.$

Euler's theorem gives

$2^{\varphi(a)}\equiv 1\pmod a,$

where $\varphi(a)$ is Euler's totient function. Hence

$a\mid (2^{\varphi(a)}-1),$

and one may take

$b=\varphi(a).$

This proof is shorter, but it relies on a deeper theorem. The pigeonhole argument proves the statement directly from elementary properties of congruences and finite residue classes, which makes it more suitable for a Kvant problem at this level.
