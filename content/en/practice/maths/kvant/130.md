## Problem

What is the greatest number of points that can be placed

1. in the plane;
2. in space,

so that none of the triangles with vertices at these points is obtuse?

G. A. Galperin

## Exploration

In the plane, three noncollinear points form a triangle. If every triangle determined by the chosen points must be nonobtuse, then every triple of points must form an acute or right triangle.

A first guess is that the vertices of a square work. Every triangle determined by four vertices of a square is an isosceles right triangle. Thus four points are possible.

Can five points be placed in the plane? The natural obstruction comes from convex hulls. If a set contains at least five points, either the convex hull has at least four vertices, or it is a triangle containing interior points. For a convex quadrilateral, one diagonal subtends an angle exceeding $90^\circ$ from the opposite vertex, so one of the triangles is obtuse. This suggests that four may be maximal.

For space, the vertices of a regular tetrahedron work. Every face is acute. More generally, every triangle formed by three vertices of a regular tetrahedron is an equilateral triangle. Thus four points are possible.

Can five points exist in space? A natural candidate is a regular tetrahedron together with its center, but a triangle formed by the center and two vertices has angle $109.47^\circ$ at the center, hence is obtuse. So that does not work.

The crucial question is how to prove that five points are impossible in space. A projection argument seems promising. If five points in space had the required property, choose one point $P$. The remaining four points must all lie in the half-spaces determined by planes through $P$ perpendicular to the segments joining $P$ to them; otherwise a triangle with vertex $P$ would contain an obtuse angle. Intersecting these half-spaces yields a convex cone with apex $P$. Projecting the other points onto a plane not through $P$ should produce four planar points with the analogous property. Since the planar maximum appears to be four, this alone is insufficient. A stronger argument is needed.

A better idea is to choose a vertex of the convex hull. Let $P$ be such a vertex. All other points lie in some open half-space bounded by a supporting plane through $P$. Orthogonally project the remaining points onto that supporting plane. Any angle at $P$ in space is not larger than the corresponding projected angle. Since all angles at $P$ are at most $90^\circ$, the projected points form a planar set in which every pair of rays from the projection of $P$ makes angle at most $90^\circ$. The planar problem then implies that there can be at most four projected points, hence at most five total points. To rule out five, one must show that four projected points already force a forbidden configuration. The extremal planar configuration of four points is a rectangle, and then lifting back to space leads to a contradiction. This is the delicate step.

The most likely place for an error is the transition from a hypothetical five-point spatial configuration to a four-point planar configuration and extracting a contradiction from the planar extremal case.

## Problem Understanding

We seek the largest possible size of a finite set of points such that every triangle whose vertices belong to the set is nonobtuse, meaning that all its angles are at most $90^\circ$.

This is a Type C problem. We must determine the maximum number of points in the plane and in space, exhibit configurations attaining these maxima, and prove that no larger configuration exists.

The core difficulty is obtaining sharp upper bounds. In the plane one must show that five points are impossible. In space one must show that five points are likewise impossible, despite the additional freedom of three dimensions.

The answer is that the maximum is $4$ in both cases. In the plane a square attains the bound. In space a regular tetrahedron attains the bound. Intuitively, these configurations are highly symmetric and all triangles determined by their vertices are right or acute, while any attempt to add another point forces an obtuse angle somewhere.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1. If $A,B,C,D$ are the vertices of a convex quadrilateral, then at least one of the triangles determined by these four vertices is obtuse. The sketch is that the angles of a convex quadrilateral sum to $360^\circ$, so one angle is at least $90^\circ$, and the diagonal through that vertex produces an obtuse angle in one of the adjacent triangles.

Lemma 2. Any set of five points in the plane contains four points that are the vertices of a convex quadrilateral. The sketch is that either the convex hull has at least four vertices, or the hull is a triangle and two points lie inside it; in the latter case two interior points together with two suitable hull vertices form a convex quadrilateral.

Lemma 3. Hence no planar configuration contains five points. Combining Lemmas 1 and 2 yields an obtuse triangle.

Lemma 4. Let $P$ be a vertex of the convex hull of a spatial configuration. Orthogonally project all other points onto a supporting plane through $P$. If every triangle of the spatial configuration is nonobtuse, then every triangle formed by the projected points and $P$ is nonobtuse in the plane. The sketch is that projection onto the supporting plane cannot decrease the scalar products determining angles at $P$.

Lemma 5. Four projected points together with $P$ would give five planar points with the nonobtuse-triangle property, contradicting the planar bound. The sketch is that every triangle among projected points corresponds to a triangle among the original points, while angles at $P$ remain nonobtuse by Lemma 4.

The hardest direction is the upper bound in space. Lemma 4 is the most delicate statement.

## Solution

First consider the plane.

A square provides an example with four points. Any triangle whose vertices are vertices of a square is an isosceles right triangle, hence no such triangle is obtuse. Thus at least four points are possible.

We show that five points are impossible.

Let five points be given. We claim that among them there are four vertices of a convex quadrilateral.

If the convex hull of the five points has at least four vertices, the claim is immediate.

Suppose instead that the convex hull is a triangle $ABC$. Then at least two of the five points lie in the interior of $ABC$; call them $X$ and $Y$. The segment $XY$ divides the triangle into two parts. At least one vertex of $ABC$ lies on each side of the line $XY$, since otherwise all three vertices would lie on the same side and the whole triangle would lie there as well. Choose vertices $A$ and $B$ on opposite sides of $XY$. Then the quadrilateral $AXBY$ is convex. Hence the claim holds in all cases.

Now let $PQRS$ be four points forming a convex quadrilateral. The interior angles of this quadrilateral sum to $360^\circ$. Consequently one of them is at least $90^\circ$; assume it is $\angle PQR$.

Consider the diagonal $PR$. In triangle $PQR$ the angle at $Q$ is at least $90^\circ$. If it is greater than $90^\circ$, this triangle is obtuse. If it equals $90^\circ$, then the remaining three angles of the quadrilateral sum to $270^\circ$, so one of them exceeds $90^\circ$; the corresponding triangle formed with the appropriate diagonal is obtuse. Thus a convex quadrilateral always determines an obtuse triangle.

Therefore any five points in the plane determine an obtuse triangle. The maximum number of points in the plane is

$$\boxed{4}.$$

Now consider space.

The four vertices of a regular tetrahedron provide a configuration in which every triangle is equilateral. Hence at least four points are possible.

We prove that five points are impossible.

Assume that a set $S$ of five points in space has the property that every triangle with vertices in $S$ is nonobtuse.

Choose a vertex $P$ of the convex hull of $S$. Let $\Pi$ be a supporting plane of the convex hull through $P$. Orthogonally project the other four points onto $\Pi$; denote the projections by $A',B',C',D'$.

Take any two of the original points, say $A$ and $B$. Since triangle $PAB$ is nonobtuse, the angle $\angle APB$ does not exceed $90^\circ$. Hence

$$(\overrightarrow{PA},\overrightarrow{PB})\ge 0.$$

Write

$$\overrightarrow{PA}=\overrightarrow{PA'}+\mathbf{u}, \qquad \overrightarrow{PB}=\overrightarrow{PB'}+\mathbf{v},$$

where $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are perpendicular to $\Pi$. Since all points lie in the same closed half-space bounded by $\Pi$, the vectors $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ have the same direction. Therefore

$$(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v})\ge 0.$$

Consequently,

$$(\overrightarrow{PA'},\overrightarrow{PB'}) = (\overrightarrow{PA},\overrightarrow{PB}) - (\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}) \ge 0.$$

Thus every angle between rays $PA',PB'$ is at most $90^\circ$.

Consider any three projected points, for example $A',B',C'$. The corresponding original triangle $ABC$ is nonobtuse. Orthogonal projection onto a plane cannot increase any scalar product of side vectors, so the projected triangle $A'B'C'$ is also nonobtuse.

Hence the five planar points

$$P,\ A',\ B',\ C',\ D'$$

have the property that every triangle determined by them is nonobtuse.

The planar part of the proof has shown that no such planar set can contain five points. This contradiction proves that no spatial set with the required property can contain five points.

Therefore the maximum number of points in space is

$$\boxed{4}.$$

The extremal configurations are a square in the plane and a regular tetrahedron in space.

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate point is the assertion that any five planar points contain four vertices of a convex quadrilateral. If the convex hull already has four vertices, nothing remains to prove. If the hull is a triangle, there must be two interior points. A line through these interior points cannot place all three vertices of the hull on the same side, because then the entire triangle would lie on that side, contrary to the fact that the line passes through interior points. Choosing one hull vertex on each side produces a convex quadrilateral. This covers all possibilities.

The second delicate point is the passage from space to the supporting plane. Writing each vector from $P$ as a sum of its projection onto the plane and a normal component gives

$$(\overrightarrow{PA},\overrightarrow{PB}) = (\overrightarrow{PA'},\overrightarrow{PB'}) + (\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}).$$

Because all points lie on the same side of the supporting plane, the normal components are nonnegative multiples of a common unit normal, so $(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v})\ge0$. Hence nonnegativity of the original scalar product implies nonnegativity after projection.

The third delicate point is that projected triangles remain nonobtuse. For a triangle, nonobtuseness is equivalent to nonnegativity of the three pairwise scalar products of edge vectors. Orthogonal projection replaces each edge vector by its planar component and subtracts a nonnegative quantity from the corresponding squared normal contribution. Thus a negative scalar product cannot be created by projection.

## Alternative Approaches

A more geometric planar proof uses the diameter theorem. Among finitely many planar points choose a pair at maximal distance. Every other point must lie in the closed disk having that segment as diameter; otherwise one of the distances would be larger. If five points existed, four would lie in that disk. Convexity considerations then force an obtuse triangle. This yields the same bound of four.

For space one may use spherical geometry. Fix a vertex $P$ of the convex hull and consider the directions from $P$ to all other points. The condition that every angle at $P$ be at most $90^\circ$ places all these directions in a hemisphere and makes them form a planar nonobtuse configuration on the unit sphere. The planar extremal result then gives the bound. The projection argument is preferable because it uses only elementary Euclidean geometry and directly reduces the spatial problem to the planar one.
