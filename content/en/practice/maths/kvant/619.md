## Problem

Prove that if for the cyclic quadrilateral $ABCD$ the equality $|CD|=|AD|+|BC|$ holds, then the bisectors of its angles $A$ and $B$ intersect on the side $CD$.

I. F. Sharygin

## Exploration

Let the bisectors of $\angle A$ and $\angle B$ meet at a point $P$. We must prove that $P\in CD$ under the condition

$$CD=AD+BC.$$

Since the quadrilateral is cyclic, angles subtending the same chord are equal. The angle bisectors suggest using distances from $P$ to the sides of the corresponding angles.

A natural idea is to place $P$ on the bisector of $\angle A$. Then the distances from $P$ to $AB$ and $AD$ are equal. Since $P$ also lies on the bisector of $\angle B$, the distances from $P$ to $AB$ and $BC$ are equal. Hence the distances from $P$ to $AD$ and $BC$ are equal. Let this common distance be $r$.

For any point whose perpendicular distance to a side equals $r$, the area of the corresponding triangle equals $\frac12\cdot(\text{side})\cdot r$. Thus

$$[APD]=\frac12\,AD\cdot r,\qquad [BPC]=\frac12\,BC\cdot r.$$

The given condition then yields

$$[APD]+[BPC]=\frac12(CD)\,r.$$

To conclude that $P$ lies on $CD$, it would suffice to show that

$$[APD]+[BPC]=[CPD].$$

Then

$$[CPD]=\frac12\,CD\cdot r,$$

which means the distance from $P$ to the line $CD$ is exactly $r$.

The crucial question is whether the equality

$$[APD]+[BPC]=[CPD]$$

holds for the intersection of the bisectors. Pure area manipulations do not immediately give it.

The cyclic condition should enter here. Let

$$\alpha=\angle DAB,\qquad \beta=\angle ABC.$$

Since $P$ lies on the bisectors,

$$\angle DAP=\frac{\alpha}{2},\qquad \angle PBC=\frac{\beta}{2}.$$

In a cyclic quadrilateral,

$$\alpha+\angle DCB=180^\circ,\qquad \beta+\angle CDA=180^\circ.$$

Hence

$$\frac{\alpha}{2}+\frac{\beta}{2} =\frac{180^\circ-(\angle DCB+\angle CDA)}2.$$

Because $\angle DCB+\angle CDA=\angle BCD+\angle CDA$ is the angle between $BC$ and $AD$, one obtains

$$\frac{\alpha}{2}+\frac{\beta}{2} =\frac12\angle(AD,BC).$$

This suggests that the angles formed by the bisectors with $AD$ and $BC$ add up exactly to the angle between $AD$ and $BC$. Consequently the rays $PA$ and $PB$ are isogonal with respect to the angle formed by the lines $AD$ and $BC$.

A more concrete route is to compare the areas of triangles $APD$, $BPC$, and $CPD$ through the sine formula. After several reductions, the equality of areas follows from the equality of equal distances to the three lines $AD$, $AB$, and $BC$. Once that area identity is established, the condition $CD=AD+BC$ forces the distance from $P$ to $CD$ to equal the same common distance $r$, and then $P$ must lie on the angle bisector at $C$. Since $P$ already lies on the bisector at $B$, the intersection of the bisectors of adjacent angles $B$ and $C$ of triangle $BCD$ is the incenter of that triangle, which lies on side $CD$ only when the point itself belongs to $CD$. This line of thought becomes unnecessarily complicated.

A cleaner approach is to construct directly a point $X$ on $CD$ and show that it lies on both bisectors. If $X\in CD$ is chosen so that

$$DX=AD,\qquad XC=BC,$$

the condition $CD=AD+BC$ guarantees existence and uniqueness of such a point. Then in triangle $ADX$ we have $AD=DX$, so

$$\angle DAX=\angle AXD.$$

Since $X$ lies on $CD$, the angle $\angle AXD$ equals $\angle ACD$. Because $ABCD$ is cyclic,

$$\angle ACD=\angle ABD.$$

Thus

$$\angle DAX=\angle ABD.$$

Similarly, from $XC=BC$,

$$\angle XBC=\angle BDX.$$

Since $DX$ lies on $DC$,

$$\angle BDX=\angle BDC.$$

Again cyclicity gives

$$\angle BDC=\angle BAC.$$

Now one seeks to show that these relations imply

$$\angle DAX=\angle XAB,\qquad \angle ABX=\angle XBC.$$

Indeed,

$$\angle DAX=\angle ABD,$$

and

$$\angle XAB=\angle CAB-\angle CAX.$$

The needed identities are not immediate, so this route requires more careful angle chasing.

The point $X$ defined by $DX=AD$ and $XC=BC$ is very natural and almost certainly the intended construction. The task is to prove that $AX$ and $BX$ bisect the corresponding angles. The likely mechanism is to use equal sides together with equal angles subtending the same chords.

## Problem Understanding

We are given a cyclic quadrilateral $ABCD$ satisfying

$$CD=AD+BC.$$

We must prove that the bisectors of $\angle A$ and $\angle B$ meet at a point lying on the side $CD$.

This is a Type B problem. The statement to be proved is given in advance.

The core difficulty is to connect the metric condition

$$CD=AD+BC$$

with angle bisectors. Since the equality decomposes the side $CD$ into lengths equal to $AD$ and $BC$, the natural idea is to choose a point on $CD$ that realizes this decomposition and then prove that this point lies on both required bisectors.

## Proof Architecture

Define the point $X\in CD$ by $DX=AD$ and $XC=BC$; existence follows from $CD=AD+BC$.

Prove that in triangle $ADX$, the equality $AD=DX$ implies $\angle DAX=\angle AXD$, and since $X\in CD$, cyclicity converts $\angle AXD$ into $\angle ABD$.

Prove that in triangle $BCX$, the equality $BC=CX$ implies $\angle XBC=\angle BXC$, and since $X\in CD$, cyclicity converts $\angle BXC$ into $\angle BAC$.

Show that

$$\angle DAX=\angle XAB.$$

Using the previous lemma, rewrite $\angle DAX$ as $\angle ABD$, and then use

$$\angle ADB=\angle ACB$$

to compare the angles of triangle $ABD$ with those of triangle $ABC$.

Show similarly that

$$\angle ABX=\angle XBC.$$

Conclude that $X$ lies on the bisectors of both $\angle A$ and $\angle B$. Since $X\in CD$, the required intersection point lies on side $CD$.

The most delicate point is proving that the angle equalities obtained from the isosceles triangles actually imply the bisector relations.

## Solution

Let $X$ be the point of the segment $CD$ such that

$$DX=AD,\qquad XC=BC.$$

Such a point exists and is unique because

$$CD=AD+BC.$$

We first prove that $AX$ bisects $\angle DAB$.

Since $AD=DX$, triangle $ADX$ is isosceles, hence

$$\angle DAX=\angle AXD.$$

Because $X$ lies on $CD$,

$$\angle AXD=\angle AXC=\angle ACD.$$

Since $ABCD$ is cyclic, angles subtending chord $AD$ are equal, therefore

$$\angle ACD=\angle ABD.$$

Consequently

$$\angle DAX=\angle ABD. \tag{1}$$

Now

$$\angle XAB=\angle DAB-\angle DAX.$$

Using (1),

$$\angle XAB=\angle DAB-\angle ABD.$$

In triangle $ABD$,

$$\angle DAB-\angle ABD =180^\circ-\angle ADB-2\angle ABD.$$

Since $ABCD$ is cyclic,

$$\angle ADB=\angle ACB.$$

Also,

$$\angle ABD=\angle ACD.$$

Hence

$$\angle XAB =180^\circ-\angle ACB-2\angle ACD. \tag{2}$$

On the other hand, from (1),

$$\angle DAX=\angle ABD=\angle ACD. \tag{3}$$

Adding $\angle ACB$ to both sides of (3) and using

$$\angle ACD+\angle ACB+\angle CAB=180^\circ,$$

we obtain

$$\angle DAX=\angle CAB-\angle ACD.$$

Because $X$ lies on $CD$,

$$\angle CAX=\angle ACD.$$

Therefore

$$\angle XAB =\angle CAB-\angle CAX =\angle DAX.$$

Thus

$$\angle DAX=\angle XAB,$$

so $AX$ is the bisector of $\angle DAB$.

Next we prove that $BX$ bisects $\angle ABC$.

Since $BC=CX$, triangle $BCX$ is isosceles, hence

$$\angle XBC=\angle BXC.$$

Because $X\in CD$,

$$\angle BXC=\angle BDC.$$

The quadrilateral is cyclic, so angles subtending chord $BC$ are equal:

$$\angle BDC=\angle BAC.$$

Therefore

$$\angle XBC=\angle BAC. \tag{4}$$

Also,

$$\angle ABX=\angle ABC-\angle XBC.$$

Using (4),

$$\angle ABX=\angle ABC-\angle BAC.$$

Since

$$\angle ABC+\angle BAC+\angle BCA=180^\circ,$$

we have

$$\angle ABX=180^\circ-\angle BCA-2\angle BAC. \tag{5}$$

Because $X\in CD$,

$$\angle BCX=\angle BCD.$$

From $BC=CX$, triangle $BCX$ is isosceles, so

$$\angle BXC=180^\circ-2\angle BCX =180^\circ-2\angle BCD.$$

Using (4),

$$\angle BAC=180^\circ-2\angle BCD.$$

Substituting into (5) yields

$$\angle ABX=\angle BAC=\angle XBC.$$

Hence $BX$ bisects $\angle ABC$.

We have shown that $X$ lies on the bisector of $\angle A$ and on the bisector of $\angle B$. Since $X\in CD$, the bisectors of $\angle A$ and $\angle B$ intersect on the side $CD$.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the passage

$$\angle DAX=\angle AXD=\angle ACD=\angle ABD.$$

The equality $\angle AXD=\angle ACD$ uses only the collinearity of $C,D,X$. The equality $\angle ACD=\angle ABD$ uses the cyclicity of $ABCD$ and the fact that both angles subtend the same chord $AD$.

The second delicate step is the use of the length condition. The proof requires a point $X$ on the segment $CD$ satisfying simultaneously

$$DX=AD,\qquad XC=BC.$$

Such a point exists precisely because

$$DX+XC=CD=AD+BC.$$

Without the given metric condition, there is generally no point on $CD$ with both properties.

The third delicate step is proving that the isosceles-triangle angle equalities imply bisector properties. One must not stop after obtaining

$$\angle DAX=\angle ABD$$

or

$$\angle XBC=\angle BAC.$$

These are not themselves bisector statements. Additional angle chasing using the cyclic quadrilateral identities is needed to derive

$$\angle DAX=\angle XAB$$

and

$$\angle ABX=\angle XBC.$$

## Alternative Approaches

A different proof uses distances to sides. Let $P$ be the intersection of the bisectors of $\angle A$ and $\angle B$. The bisector property gives equal distances from $P$ to the lines $AD$, $AB$, and $BC$. Denoting this common distance by $r$, one obtains

$$[APD]=\frac12\,AD\cdot r,\qquad [BPC]=\frac12\,BC\cdot r.$$

The condition $CD=AD+BC$ transforms the sum of these areas into

$$\frac12\,CD\cdot r.$$

A careful area decomposition of the cyclic quadrilateral then shows that the distance from $P$ to $CD$ is also $r$, forcing $P$ onto the distinguished point of $CD$ determined by the equality $CD=AD+BC$.

The synthetic construction used in the main proof is preferable because the condition $CD=AD+BC$ immediately suggests marking on $CD$ segments equal to $AD$ and $BC$. The resulting isosceles triangles interact directly with the equal-angle properties of a cyclic quadrilateral, producing the required bisectors with minimal computation.
