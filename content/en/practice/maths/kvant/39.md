## Problem

Prove that the nonnegative integers $x$, $y$ satisfy the equation $$x^2-mxy+y^2=1$$ (where $m$ is a given integer greater than 1) if and only if $x$ and $y$ are consecutive terms of the sequence $\phi_0=0$, $\phi_1=1$, $\phi_2=m$, $\phi_3=m^2-1$, $\phi_4=m^3-2m$, $\phi_5=m^4-3m^2+1$, $\ldots$, in which $\phi_{k+1}=m\phi_k-\phi_{k-1}$ for all $k\ge 1$.

For example, all integer solutions $0\le x\lt y$ of the equation $$x^2-3xy+y^2=1\tag1$$ are precisely the pairs $$(0,1);~(1,3);~(3,8);~(8,21);~\ldots\tag2$$ of consecutive terms of the sequence 0, 1, 3, 8, 21, 55, $\ldots$, defined by the conditions $\phi_0=0$, $\phi_1=1$, $\phi_{k+1}=3\phi_k-\phi_{k-1}$ for all $k\ge 1$. (This beautiful fact was used in the work of Yu. Matiyasevich devoted to Hilbert’s Tenth Problem, which was discussed in the previous issue of _Kvant_.)

## Exploration

Let

$$Q(x,y)=x^2-mxy+y^2.$$

The recurrence

$$\phi_{k+1}=m\phi_k-\phi_{k-1}$$

suggests that the quadratic form may be invariant under the transformation

$$(x,y)\mapsto (y,my-x).$$

Indeed,

$$Q(y,my-x) =y^2-my(my-x)+(my-x)^2 =x^2-mxy+y^2.$$

Thus the equation is preserved by this transformation.

Testing the first few consecutive pairs of the sequence,

$$(0,1),\ (1,m),\ (m,m^2-1),$$

gives

$$0^2-m\cdot0\cdot1+1^2=1,$$

$$1^2-m\cdot1\cdot m+m^2=1,$$

$$m^2-m^2(m^2-1)+(m^2-1)^2=1.$$

The pattern holds. The recurrence itself is exactly the transformation above applied repeatedly to the pair of consecutive terms:

$$(\phi_{k-1},\phi_k)\mapsto(\phi_k,\phi_{k+1}).$$

To prove the converse, start from an arbitrary nonnegative solution. Since

$$x^2-mxy+y^2=1,$$

and $m>1$, every solution except $(0,1)$ has $y>x>0$. Applying the inverse transformation

$$(x,y)\mapsto (mx-y,x)$$

preserves the equation. The crucial question is whether it decreases the larger coordinate. If $0<x<y$, then

$$mx-y>0$$

because

$$1=x^2-mxy+y^2=(y-mx/2)^2+\Bigl(1-\frac{m^2}{4}\Bigr)x^2,$$

and for $m\ge2$ this forces $y<mx$. Hence

$$0<mx-y<x.$$

The transformation sends $(x,y)$ to a smaller positive solution $(mx-y,x)$. Repeating must eventually reach $(0,1)$. Reversing the steps reconstructs the original solution as consecutive terms of the sequence.

The step most likely to conceal an error is the proof that every nontrivial solution satisfies

$$0<mx-y<x.$$

Without this strict inequality the descent argument fails.

## Problem Understanding

We must prove that the nonnegative integer solutions of

$$x^2-mxy+y^2=1, \qquad m>1,$$

are exactly the pairs formed by two consecutive terms of the sequence

$$\phi_0=0,\qquad \phi_1=1,\qquad \phi_{k+1}=m\phi_k-\phi_{k-1}.$$

This is a Type A problem. We must prove two directions. First, every pair of consecutive terms satisfies the equation. Second, every nonnegative solution of the equation arises in this way.

The core difficulty is the converse direction. One needs a reduction procedure that takes an arbitrary solution and produces a smaller solution while preserving the equation. The recurrence itself suggests the correct reduction:

$$(x,y)\mapsto(mx-y,x).$$

The claimed answer is that all solutions are

$$(\phi_k,\phi_{k+1}) \quad\text{and}\quad (\phi_{k+1},\phi_k), \qquad k\ge0,$$

and for solutions with $0\le x<y$ only the first family occurs.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1. If $(x,y)$ satisfies $x^2-mxy+y^2=1$, then $(y,my-x)$ and $(mx-y,x)$ also satisfy it.

Sketch. Direct substitution shows that the quadratic form is invariant under these transformations.

Lemma 2. Every consecutive pair $(\phi_k,\phi_{k+1})$ satisfies $x^2-mxy+y^2=1$.

Sketch. Verify the initial pair $(0,1)$, then use Lemma 1 and the recurrence.

Lemma 3. If $(x,y)$ is a nonnegative solution different from $(0,1)$, then $0<x<y$ and

$$0<mx-y<x.$$

Sketch. The equation excludes $x=y>0$ and $x=0$ except for $(0,1)$. Comparing $y$ with $mx$ and $(m-1)x$ yields the inequalities.

Lemma 4. Every nontrivial solution produces a strictly smaller positive solution $(mx-y,x)$.

Sketch. Lemma 3 gives positivity and strict decrease of the larger coordinate.

The hardest direction is proving that every solution comes from the sequence. The lemma most vulnerable to error is Lemma 3, because the descent depends on the strict inequalities.

## Solution

Let

$$Q(x,y)=x^2-mxy+y^2.$$

We first prove that every pair of consecutive terms of the sequence satisfies

$$Q(x,y)=1.$$

A direct computation gives

$$Q(y,my-x) =y^2-my(my-x)+(my-x)^2 =x^2-mxy+y^2 =Q(x,y).$$

Hence the transformation

$$T(x,y)=(y,my-x)$$

preserves the value of $Q$.

The initial pair is

$$(\phi_0,\phi_1)=(0,1),$$

and

$$Q(0,1)=1.$$

Assume

$$Q(\phi_{k-1},\phi_k)=1.$$

Since

$$\phi_{k+1}=m\phi_k-\phi_{k-1},$$

we have

$$(\phi_k,\phi_{k+1}) =T(\phi_{k-1},\phi_k).$$

Because $T$ preserves $Q$,

$$Q(\phi_k,\phi_{k+1}) = Q(\phi_{k-1},\phi_k) =1.$$

By induction,

$$Q(\phi_k,\phi_{k+1})=1$$

for all $k\ge0$. Thus every consecutive pair of the sequence is a solution.

We now prove the converse.

Let $(x,y)$ be a nonnegative integer solution of

$$x^2-mxy+y^2=1.$$

If $x=0$, then $y^2=1$, so $y=1$. Hence

$$(x,y)=(0,1),$$

which is the pair $(\phi_0,\phi_1)$.

Assume from now on that $(x,y)\ne(0,1)$. Then $x>0$.

The equation may be written as

$$y^2-mxy+(x^2-1)=0.$$

Since $y$ is an integer root of this quadratic polynomial, the other root is

$$mx-y.$$

By Vieta's formula,

$$y(mx-y)=x^2-1.$$

Since $x>0$, the right-hand side is nonnegative. It is positive because $x\ne1$ together with $y=0$ is impossible. Thus

$$mx-y>0.$$

Suppose that $y\le (m-1)x$. Then

$$mx-y\ge x.$$

Multiplying by $y>0$,

$$y(mx-y)\ge xy.$$

Since $m>1$ and $y>x$ will follow from the next calculation, it suffices to compare with $x^2-1$. From Vieta's relation,

$$x^2-1=y(mx-y)\ge xy.$$

Hence

$$x^2-1\ge xy.$$

Because $y\ge x$, the right-hand side is at least $x^2$, which is impossible. Therefore

$$y>(m-1)x.$$

Combining

$$y>(m-1)x$$

with

$$y<mx,$$

the latter following from $mx-y>0$, we obtain

$$0<mx-y<x.$$

Consequently the pair

$$(x',y')=(mx-y,x)$$

consists of nonnegative integers and satisfies

$$0\le x'<y'.$$

By the invariance of $Q$,

$$Q(x',y')=Q(x,y)=1.$$

Moreover,

$$y'=x<y,$$

so the larger coordinate has strictly decreased.

Starting from any nontrivial solution and repeatedly replacing $(x,y)$ by $(mx-y,x)$, we obtain a sequence of nonnegative solutions whose larger coordinate strictly decreases at each step. Since the larger coordinate is a positive integer, the process must terminate.

The only solution for which the process cannot continue is $(0,1)$. Hence after finitely many steps every solution reaches $(0,1)$.

Now reverse the process. The inverse transformation is

$$T(x,y)=(y,my-x).$$

Beginning with

$$(0,1)=(\phi_0,\phi_1),$$

successive applications of $T$ produce

$$(\phi_1,\phi_2), \ (\phi_2,\phi_3), \ldots,$$

because

$$(\phi_k,\phi_{k+1}) =T(\phi_{k-1},\phi_k).$$

Since every solution descends to $(0,1)$, reversing the descent shows that every solution is one of these consecutive pairs. Therefore the nonnegative integer solutions are exactly the pairs

$$(\phi_k,\phi_{k+1}), \qquad k\ge0.$$

$$\boxed{\text{The nonnegative integer solutions are precisely }(\phi_k,\phi_{k+1})\text{ for }k\ge0.}$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate point is the inequality

$$0<mx-y<x.$$

From the quadratic equation in $y$,

$$y^2-mxy+x^2-1=0,$$

the second root is $mx-y$, and

$$y(mx-y)=x^2-1.$$

Since $x>0$ and the solution is not $(0,1)$, the right-hand side is positive, giving $mx-y>0$. If $mx-y\ge x$, then

$$x^2-1=y(mx-y)\ge xy.$$

Because $mx-y\ge x$ implies $y\le(m-1)x$, combining with $mx-y>0$ yields $y>x$. Hence $xy\ge x^2$, contradicting $x^2-1\ge xy$. Thus $mx-y<x$.

The second delicate point is termination of the descent. The map

$$(x,y)\mapsto(mx-y,x)$$

preserves the equation and replaces the larger coordinate $y$ by the smaller positive integer $x$. Hence the sequence of larger coordinates is strictly decreasing:

$$y>x>x'>\cdots.$$

A strictly decreasing sequence of positive integers is finite.

The third delicate point is identifying the terminal solution. If $x=0$, then the equation becomes

$$y^2=1.$$

Among nonnegative integers this gives only

$$(0,1).$$

Thus every descent chain ends at the unique minimal solution.

## Alternative Approaches

A more algebraic proof uses the characteristic roots of the recurrence

$$t^2-mt+1=0.$$

Let

$$\alpha=\frac{m+\sqrt{m^2-4}}2,\qquad \beta=\frac{m-\sqrt{m^2-4}}2.$$

Then $\alpha\beta=1$, and the sequence $\phi_k$ admits a closed formula analogous to Binet's formula for Fibonacci numbers. One can show that

$$\phi_{k+1}^2-m\phi_k\phi_{k+1}+\phi_k^2=1.$$

Conversely, every solution corresponds to a unit in the quadratic ring generated by $\alpha$, leading to the same parametrization.

The descent argument is preferable here because it uses only elementary properties of the quadratic form and the recurrence. It avoids algebraic number theory and makes the structure of the solutions completely transparent.
