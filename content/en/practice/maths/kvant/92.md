## Problem

Petya is going to spend all 90 days of his vacation in the countryside and strictly follow this schedule: every second day (that is, every other day) he goes swimming in the lake, every third day he goes to the store for groceries, and every fifth day he solves mathematics problems. (On the first day, Petya did all three and got very tired.)

How many “pleasant” days will Petya have, when he has to go swimming but does not have to go to the store or solve problems? How many “boring” days will there be, when he has no tasks at all?

## Exploration

The activities occur periodically. Since Petya swims every second day and he swam on day $1$, swimming occurs on days congruent to $1 \pmod 2$. Shopping occurs on days congruent to $1 \pmod 3$, and solving problems occurs on days congruent to $1 \pmod 5$.

A pleasant day must satisfy

$$n\equiv 1 \pmod 2,$$

but

$$n\not\equiv 1 \pmod 3,\qquad n\not\equiv 1 \pmod 5.$$

There are $45$ odd days among the $90$ vacation days. From these, subtract the odd days that are also shopping days, subtract the odd days that are also problem-solving days, and then correct for double subtraction.

An odd shopping day satisfies

$$n\equiv 1 \pmod{\operatorname{lcm}(2,3)}=\pmod 6.$$

Among $1,\dots,90$ there are $15$ such days.

An odd problem-solving day satisfies

$$n\equiv 1 \pmod{10}.$$

There are $9$ such days.

Days satisfying all three conditions satisfy

$$n\equiv 1 \pmod{30},$$

hence there are $3$ such days: $1,31,61$.

Thus the number of pleasant days is

$$45-15-9+3=24.$$

For boring days, Petya does nothing. Such days are not swimming days, not shopping days, and not problem-solving days.

The schedule repeats every

$$\operatorname{lcm}(2,3,5)=30$$

days. It is convenient to count within one $30$-day block and multiply by $3$.

In days $1$ through $30$, inclusion-exclusion gives the number of days with at least one activity:

$$15+10+6-5-3-2+1=22.$$

Hence there are

$$30-22=8$$

boring days in each block of $30$ days, so in $90$ days there are

$$3\cdot 8=24$$

boring days.

The step most likely to hide an error is the inclusion-exclusion count for pleasant days, because the intersections must be counted with the correct periods $6$, $10$, and $30$.

## Problem Understanding

Petya spends $90$ days in the countryside. Swimming is done every second day starting with day $1$, shopping every third day starting with day $1$, and solving problems every fifth day starting with day $1$.

We must determine two quantities. A pleasant day is a day on which Petya swims but neither shops nor solves problems. A boring day is a day on which he has no activity at all.

This is a Type C problem, since specific numerical values must be determined and justified.

The main difficulty is handling the overlaps among the three periodic schedules correctly. Inclusion-exclusion and the periodicity of length $30$ provide the natural framework.

## Proof Architecture

The first claim is that swimming days are exactly the days congruent to $1$ modulo $2$, shopping days are exactly the days congruent to $1$ modulo $3$, and problem-solving days are exactly the days congruent to $1$ modulo $5$; this follows directly from the statement that day $1$ belongs to every schedule.

The second claim is that the number of pleasant days equals the number of days congruent to $1$ modulo $2$ but not congruent to $1$ modulo $3$ or $5$; inclusion-exclusion counts these days.

The third claim is that the schedules repeat every $30$ days; this follows because $30=\operatorname{lcm}(2,3,5)$.

The fourth claim is that, in any block of $30$ consecutive days, exactly $22$ days contain at least one activity; inclusion-exclusion yields this count.

The fifth claim is that each $30$-day block contains exactly $8$ boring days, hence three such blocks contain $24$ boring days.

The most delicate lemma is the inclusion-exclusion computation for pleasant days, where the intersections must be counted with periods $6$, $10$, and $30$.

## Solution

Swimming occurs on the days

$$1,3,5,\dots,$$

hence on exactly those days satisfying

$$n\equiv 1 \pmod 2.$$

Shopping occurs on the days satisfying

$$n\equiv 1 \pmod 3,$$

and solving problems occurs on the days satisfying

$$n\equiv 1 \pmod 5.$$

A pleasant day is a day satisfying

$$n\equiv 1 \pmod 2,$$

but not

$$n\equiv 1 \pmod 3,$$

and not

$$n\equiv 1 \pmod 5.$$

Among the numbers from $1$ to $90$, there are

$$45$$

days with

$$n\equiv 1 \pmod 2.$$

A day that is both a swimming day and a shopping day satisfies

$$n\equiv 1 \pmod 6.$$

The numbers

$$1,7,13,\dots,85$$

form an arithmetic progression of length

$$\frac{85-1}{6}+1=15.$$

A day that is both a swimming day and a problem-solving day satisfies

$$n\equiv 1 \pmod{10}.$$

The numbers

$$1,11,21,\dots,81$$

form an arithmetic progression of length

$$\frac{81-1}{10}+1=9.$$

A day satisfying all three schedules simultaneously satisfies

$$n\equiv 1 \pmod{30}.$$

The relevant days are

$$1,31,61,$$

so there are $3$ of them.

Applying inclusion-exclusion,

$$45-15-9+3=24.$$

Hence the number of pleasant days is

$$24.$$

Now count boring days. Since

$$\operatorname{lcm}(2,3,5)=30,$$

the entire pattern repeats every $30$ days. It suffices to count within one block of $30$ consecutive days.

In such a block, the number of swimming days is

$$\frac{30}{2}=15,$$

the number of shopping days is

$$\frac{30}{3}=10,$$

and the number of problem-solving days is

$$\frac{30}{5}=6.$$

The pairwise intersections contain

$$\frac{30}{6}=5,\qquad \frac{30}{10}=3,\qquad \frac{30}{15}=2$$

days respectively. The triple intersection contains

$$\frac{30}{30}=1$$

day.

Therefore the number of days with at least one activity is

$$15+10+6-5-3-2+1=22.$$

A $30$-day block therefore contains

$$30-22=8$$

boring days.

Since $90=3\cdot 30$, there are three identical blocks, so the total number of boring days is

$$3\cdot 8=24.$$

Thus Petya has $24$ pleasant days and $24$ boring days.

$$\boxed{\text{pleasant days }=24,\qquad \text{boring days }=24}$$

## Verification of Key Steps

For pleasant days, the intersection of swimming and shopping days must be counted using period $6$, not by multiplying counts. The congruences

$$n\equiv 1\pmod2,\qquad n\equiv1\pmod3$$

are equivalent to

$$n\equiv1\pmod6,$$

giving exactly $15$ such days in the interval $1\le n\le90$.

The correction term in inclusion-exclusion comes from days counted in all three schedules. Solving

$$n\equiv1\pmod2,\qquad n\equiv1\pmod3,\qquad n\equiv1\pmod5$$

gives

$$n\equiv1\pmod{30}.$$

Only the days $1,31,61$ lie in the interval, so the correction is $3$. Omitting this term would produce the incorrect value $21$.

For boring days, the periodicity argument depends on the least common multiple $30$. After $30$ days, every congruence class modulo $2$, $3$, and $5$ repeats simultaneously. Since $90=3\cdot30$, multiplying the count from one block by $3$ is fully justified.

## Alternative Approaches

One may count boring days directly within a $30$-day cycle. The days with no activity are exactly those whose residues modulo $30$ are not congruent to $1$ modulo $2$, $3$, or $5$. Listing the $30$ residues and removing those belonging to at least one schedule leaves $8$ residues. Repetition over three cycles then gives $24$ boring days.

Another approach to pleasant days is to work entirely modulo $30$. Among the $30$ residue classes, the swimming classes are the $15$ odd residues. Of these, $5$ are also shopping classes, $3$ are also problem-solving classes, and one residue class belongs to all three schedules. Hence

$$15-5-3+1=8$$

pleasant residues per cycle. Since there are three cycles in $90$ days, the total is

$$3\cdot 8=24.$$

This method emphasizes the period-$30$ structure from the beginning.
