## Problem

Petya plans to spend all 90 days of his vacation in the village and to strictly follow the following schedule: every second day (i.e., every other day) he goes swimming in the lake, every third day he goes to the store for groceries, and every fifth day he solves math problems. (On the first day, Petya did all three of these things and became very tired.) How many “pleasant” days will Petya have, when he needs to go swimming but does not need to go to the store and solve problems? How many “boring” days will there be, when he will have nothing to do at all?

## Exploration

Petya’s activities depend only on divisibility patterns shifted so that day $1$ satisfies all three conditions. Therefore swimming occurs on all days congruent to $1 \pmod{2}$, grocery shopping on all days congruent to $1 \pmod{3}$, and solving problems on all days congruent to $1 \pmod{5}$.

The structure is periodic with period $\mathrm{lcm}(2,3,5)=30$, so classification reduces to counting residue classes modulo $30$ inside the interval $1 \le n \le 90$.

Pleasant days require swimming while excluding both other activities, which translates into a set difference inside the odd numbers. Boring days require exclusion from all three sets simultaneously.

The main risk is incorrect handling of overlaps among arithmetic progressions, so intersections must be computed exactly using simultaneous congruences.

## Problem Understanding

The problem describes three periodic schedules on the first $90$ positive integers. Swimming occurs on every second day starting with day $1$, grocery shopping on every third day starting with day $1$, and solving math problems on every fifth day starting with day $1$.

The task is to count days when swimming occurs but neither of the other two activities occurs, and also days when none of the three activities occurs.

This is a Type A problem since it asks for explicit counts of two classes of integers defined by congruence conditions. The structure suggests reduction to modular arithmetic and inclusion–exclusion. The final answer will consist of two integers.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma identifies each activity as a congruence class modulo $2$, $3$, and $5$ respectively. This follows directly from the periodic description and the condition on day $1$.

The second lemma computes sizes of the individual sets of days satisfying each activity condition in $1 \le n \le 90$ by counting arithmetic progressions.

The third lemma determines pairwise intersections by solving systems of congruences and reducing them to single congruence classes modulo the least common multiples $6$, $10$, and $15$.

The fourth lemma determines the triple intersection by solving the simultaneous system modulo $30$.

The fifth lemma computes the number of pleasant days as the cardinality of the set difference between swimming days and the union of the other two activities restricted to swimming days.

The sixth lemma computes the number of boring days as the complement of the union of all three activity sets in $1 \le n \le 90$.

The most delicate part is the correct computation of intersections and ensuring no overcounting in inclusion–exclusion.

## Solution

Let $n$ be a day number with $1 \le n \le 90$.

Swimming occurs exactly on days satisfying $n \equiv 1 \pmod{2}$ since day $1$ is included and the pattern repeats every two days. Grocery shopping occurs exactly on days satisfying $n \equiv 1 \pmod{3}$. Solving math problems occurs exactly on days satisfying $n \equiv 1 \pmod{5}$.

The set of swimming days is the arithmetic progression $1,3,5,\dots,89$, which contains $45$ elements.

The set of shopping days is $1,4,7,\dots,88$. The largest term not exceeding $90$ is $88=1+29\cdot 3$, so this set contains $30$ elements.

The set of math days is $1,6,11,\dots,86$. The largest term not exceeding $90$ is $86=1+17\cdot 5$, so this set contains $18$ elements.

To compute intersections, first consider swimming and shopping. The system

$$n \equiv 1 \pmod{2}, \quad n \equiv 1 \pmod{3}$$

implies $n \equiv 1 \pmod{6}$. In $1 \le n \le 90$, these are $1,7,13,\dots,85$, which gives $15$ elements.

For swimming and math, the system

$$n \equiv 1 \pmod{2}, \quad n \equiv 1 \pmod{5}$$

implies $n \equiv 1 \pmod{10}$. In $1 \le n \le 90$, these are $1,11,21,\dots,81$, which gives $9$ elements.

For shopping and math, the system

$$n \equiv 1 \pmod{3}, \quad n \equiv 1 \pmod{5}$$

implies $n \equiv 1 \pmod{15}$. In $1 \le n \le 90$, these are $1,16,31,46,61,76$, which gives $6$ elements.

For all three activities simultaneously, the system implies $n \equiv 1 \pmod{30}$. In $1 \le n \le 90$, these are $1,31,61$, which gives $3$ elements.

A pleasant day is one where swimming occurs and neither of the other two activities occurs. This is the set

$$S \setminus (S \cap G \cup S \cap M).$$

Its cardinality equals

$$|S| - |S \cap G| - |S \cap M| + |S \cap G \cap M|.$$

Substituting the computed values yields

$$45 - 15 - 9 + 3 = 24.$$

A boring day is one where none of the activities occurs, that is the complement of $S \cup G \cup M$ in ${1,2,\dots,90}$. The size of the union is

$$|S| + |G| + |M| - |S \cap G| - |S \cap M| - |G \cap M| + |S \cap G \cap M|.$$

Substituting gives

$$45 + 30 + 18 - 15 - 9 - 6 + 3 = 66.$$

Hence the number of boring days is

$$90 - 66 = 24.$$

The pleasant days and boring days are therefore both counted as $24$.

$$\boxed{\text{pleasant days }=24,\ \text{boring days }=24}$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The most sensitive point is the reduction of simultaneous congruences. For swimming and shopping, any integer satisfying both $n \equiv 1 \pmod{2}$ and $n \equiv 1 \pmod{3}$ must differ from $1$ by a multiple of both $2$ and $3$, hence by a multiple of $6$. The converse holds because every number $1+6k$ satisfies both original congruences since $6k$ is divisible by $2$ and $3$.

For swimming and math, a similar argument shows that the difference from $1$ must be divisible by both $2$ and $5$, hence by $10$, and every $1+10k$ satisfies both conditions.

For shopping and math, the difference from $1$ must be divisible by both $3$ and $5$, hence by $15$, and every $1+15k$ satisfies both conditions.

For the triple intersection, the difference from $1$ must be divisible by $2$, $3$, and $5$, hence by $30$. The explicit enumeration $1,31,61$ confirms that exactly three values lie in the interval $1 \le n \le 90$.

Each inclusion–exclusion computation is validated by checking that every overlap has been subtracted exactly once and restored appropriately through the triple intersection term.

## Alternative Approaches

A different method uses periodicity modulo $30$ from the outset. One computes the pattern of activities on the $30$ residue classes congruent to $1$ through $30$, counts pleasant and boring classes within a single period, and then multiplies by $3$ since $90=3\cdot 30$. This approach avoids explicit inclusion–exclusion over the full interval but requires careful classification of residue classes in a full period.
