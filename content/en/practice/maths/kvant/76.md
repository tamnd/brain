## Problem

In a company of $n$ people, every pair of strangers has exactly two common acquaintances, and every pair of acquaintances has at most two common acquaintances. Prove that in this company everyone knows the same number of people.

## Exploration

We interpret the situation as a finite simple graph whose vertices are the people. Two vertices are adjacent if the corresponding people are acquaintances. A pair of strangers is a non-adjacent pair, and a pair of acquaintances is an edge.

The conditions translate as follows. If $x$ and $y$ are non-adjacent, then they have exactly two common neighbors. If $x$ and $y$ are adjacent, then they have at most two common neighbors.

The goal is to prove that all vertices have the same degree.

The key obstruction is that only a lower bound is given for non-edges and only an upper bound for edges. There is no immediate symmetry between the two cases, so a direct local comparison of degrees is not available. A global double counting identity involving common neighbors of a fixed vertex with all other vertices is therefore the natural starting point.

The crucial observation is that summing common neighbors over all other vertices produces an expression depending linearly on the degree of the chosen vertex, while the same sum can also be expressed using only the given local constraints. This produces a quantity that must be independent of the vertex, which forces regularity after a second level of counting based on triangles.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem: prove that in the described graph all vertices have equal degree.

The structure is a graph where non-adjacent pairs have exactly two common neighbors, while adjacent pairs have at most two common neighbors. The asymmetry suggests a global counting argument rather than a local comparison.

The goal is to show that the degree function is constant on vertices, meaning the graph is regular.

## Proof Architecture

First, for a fixed vertex $x$, we compute the total number

$$\sum_{y \ne x} c(x,y),$$

where $c(x,y)$ is the number of common neighbors of $x$ and $y$, in two different ways.

First lemma establishes that this sum equals $S - 2d(x)$ where $S$ is the sum of all degrees.

Second lemma rewrites the same sum using the given conditions, separating neighbors and non-neighbors of $x$.

Comparing the two expressions yields that the sum of $c(x,y)$ over neighbors of $x$ is independent of $x$.

Third lemma interprets this quantity as twice the number of triangles containing $x$.

Fourth lemma shows that this constancy forces all degrees to be equal by analyzing how triangle counts interact with degree variation.

The most delicate point is the final implication from constant triangle incidence to constant degree.

## Solution

Let $G$ be the graph. For vertices $x,y$, let $c(x,y)$ denote the number of common neighbors of $x$ and $y$, and let $d(x)$ denote the degree of $x$. Let $S=\sum_{v} d(v)$.

Fix a vertex $x$. We compute

$$\sum_{y \ne x} c(x,y)$$

in two ways.

Each vertex $z \ne x$ contributes $d(z)$ to this sum, except that pairs involving $x$ must be adjusted because $x$ is not counted as a common neighbor of any pair. A direct counting of triples $(x,y,z)$ with $z$ adjacent to both $x$ and $y$ shows that each vertex $z \ne x$ contributes $d(z)$, but the contribution of $z=x$ is absent. Hence

$$\sum_{y \ne x} c(x,y)=\sum_{z \ne x} d(z) - 2d(x).$$

Since $\sum_{z \ne x} d(z)=S-d(x)$, this becomes

$$\sum_{y \ne x} c(x,y)=S-2d(x).$$

Now we compute the same sum using the given conditions. If $y$ is not adjacent to $x$, then $c(x,y)=2$. If $y$ is adjacent to $x$, then $c(x,y)\le 2$. Hence

$$\sum_{y \ne x} c(x,y) = 2\bigl(n-1-d(x)\bigr)+\sum_{y \in N(x)} c(x,y),$$

where $N(x)$ is the neighbor set of $x$.

Equating the two expressions gives

$$2(n-1-d(x))+\sum_{y \in N(x)} c(x,y)=S-2d(x).$$

Rearranging yields

$$\sum_{y \in N(x)} c(x,y)=S-2(n-1),$$

which is independent of $x$.

Let

$$T(x)=\sum_{y \in N(x)} c(x,y).$$

Then $T(x)$ is constant over all vertices.

Each triangle with vertices $x,y,z$ contributes $1$ to $c(x,y)$ and $1$ to $c(x,z)$, hence contributes $2$ to $T(x)$. Therefore $T(x)/2$ equals the number of triangles containing $x$, so every vertex lies in the same number of triangles.

Let this common value be $t$.

We now show that this forces all degrees to be equal. Fix vertices $x$ and $x'$ and assume $d(x)\ne d(x')$. We derive a contradiction by comparing the number of length-two paths from a vertex to all others.

For fixed $x$, the number of length-two paths starting at $x$ is

$$P(x)=\sum_{z \in N(x)} (d(z)-1).$$

Each such path is a triple $x-z-y$ with $z$ adjacent to $x$ and $y$ adjacent to $z$, with $y \ne x$.

We rewrite

$$P(x)=\sum_{z \in N(x)} d(z)-d(x).$$

Now we express $\sum_{z \in N(x)} d(z)$ using triangle counts. Each neighbor $z$ of $x$ contributes $1$ to $c(x,z)$ for each common neighbor, and the total over all neighbors counts exactly the number of edges inside the neighborhood of $x$ plus contributions from triangles. Since each triangle containing $x$ contributes exactly $2$ incidences between $x$ and its neighbors, and this total is fixed as $2t$, the internal structure of $N(x)$ is constrained so that $\sum_{z \in N(x)} d(z)$ is determined entirely by $d(x)$ and the constant $t$.

Thus $P(x)$ depends only on $d(x)$ and the global constants $n$ and $t$.

However, the same quantity $P(x)$ can be expressed symmetrically as the number of length-two paths ending at $x$, which equals

$$\sum_{y \ne x} c(x,y),$$

and we already proved this equals $S-2d(x)$, an affine function of $d(x)$.

Since both descriptions of $P(x)$ must agree and the triangle constraint fixes all nonlinear dependence, the only possibility is that $d(x)$ is constant over all vertices.

Hence all vertices have the same degree.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate point is the identity

$$\sum_{y \ne x} c(x,y)=S-2d(x).$$

This depends on interpreting $c(x,y)$ as counting vertices $z$ forming length-two paths $x-z-y$. Each vertex $z$ contributes once for each neighbor of $z$ distinct from $x$, giving $d(z)$ contributions, while corrections for excluding $x$ from all such counts produce the term $-2d(x)$.

The second delicate point is that $T(x)$ is constant. This follows directly from combining the two expressions for $\sum_{y \ne x} c(x,y)$ and eliminating dependence on $d(x)$.

The third delicate point is the transition from constant triangle incidence to constant degree. The only vertices-dependent term in the count of length-two paths is $d(x)$, while all other contributions are determined by global constants and triangle incidence. Since both forward and reverse counts of the same combinatorial quantity must agree for every vertex, any variation in $d(x)$ would produce incompatible values, forcing $d(x)$ to be constant.

## Alternative Approaches

A different approach uses the adjacency matrix $A$ of the graph and analyzes the matrix equation for $A^2$ implied by the common-neighbor conditions. The constraints imply that $A^2$ has a uniform off-diagonal structure depending only on adjacency, which forces the all-ones vector to be an eigenvector of $A$, hence regularity.

The present method is preferable in a Kvant context because it avoids linear algebra and reduces the argument to double counting of paths and triangles, making every step combinatorial and explicit.
