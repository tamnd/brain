## Problem

In a company of $n$ people, every pair of strangers has exactly two common acquaintances, and every pair of acquaintances has no more than two common acquaintances. Prove that in this company, everyone is acquainted with the same number of people.

## Exploration

Let the acquaintance graph have vertex set equal to the people in the company. Two vertices are adjacent if the corresponding people are acquainted.

The conditions become:

For every nonadjacent pair of vertices, the number of common neighbors is exactly $2$.

For every adjacent pair of vertices, the number of common neighbors is at most $2$.

We must prove that the graph is regular.

A first guess is that the graph is strongly regular. That is not stated, because adjacent pairs need not have exactly the same number of common neighbors. The only fixed parameter is for nonadjacent pairs. Thus a direct appeal to strongly regular graph theory is unavailable.

Let $d(v)$ denote the degree of a vertex $v$. To prove regularity it is natural to compare two vertices $u$ and $v$.

Suppose first that $u$ and $v$ are not adjacent. They have exactly two common neighbors. Count the edges between the set of neighbors of $u$ and the set of neighbors of $v$. Every vertex other than the two common neighbors contributes in a controlled way because every nonneighbor pair has exactly two common neighbors. This suggests double counting.

A more promising route is to use the adjacency matrix $A$. The condition on nonadjacent pairs says that for $i\neq j$,

$$(A^2)_{ij}=2$$

whenever $A_{ij}=0$.

Hence

$$A^2=2(J-I-A)+B,$$

where $B$ has off-diagonal entries only on adjacent pairs, and those entries are at most $2$. Since the diagonal of $A^2$ is the degree sequence, perhaps one can obtain an eigenvalue argument. Yet the problem is elementary, so a purely combinatorial proof is preferable.

Take two vertices $u,v$. Let

$$X=N(u)\setminus N(v),\qquad Y=N(v)\setminus N(u),\qquad C=N(u)\cap N(v).$$

Then

$$d(u)-d(v)=|X|-|Y|.$$

The goal is to show $|X|=|Y|$.

Assume first that $u$ and $v$ are nonadjacent. Then $|C|=2$.

Consider a vertex $x\in X$. Since $x$ is not adjacent to $v$, the pair $(x,v)$ has exactly two common neighbors. One of them is $u$. Therefore $x$ is adjacent to exactly one vertex of $C\cup Y$. Summing over $x\in X$ counts edges from $X$ to $C\cup Y$. By symmetry, summing over $Y$ gives the same type of relation. The crucial point is to turn these local constraints into $|X|=|Y|$.

Let $C={c_1,c_2}$. For $x\in X$, the second common neighbor of $x$ and $v$ lies in $C\cup Y$. Thus each $x$ contributes exactly one incidence to $C\cup Y$. Hence the number of edges from $X$ to $C\cup Y$ equals $|X|$. Similarly the number of edges from $Y$ to $C\cup X$ equals $|Y|$.

Now count edges joining $X$ and $Y$. Let $e(X,Y)$ denote their number. If $a_i$ is the number of neighbors of $c_i$ in $X$, then

$$|X|=e(X,Y)+a_1+a_2.$$

Similarly, if $b_i$ is the number of neighbors of $c_i$ in $Y$,

$$|Y|=e(X,Y)+b_1+b_2.$$

Hence it suffices to prove

$$a_1+a_2=b_1+b_2.$$

But $c_i$ and $v$ are adjacent. Their common neighbors are precisely the neighbors of $c_i$ in $Y$ together with $u$ and the possible other vertex $c_{3-i}$. Since they have at most two common neighbors, one gets $b_i\le a$ small. A cleaner counting argument is needed.

The likely hidden structure is that for nonadjacent $u,v$, every vertex in $X$ is adjacent to exactly one of $c_1,c_2$, and every vertex in $Y$ is adjacent to exactly one of $c_1,c_2$. Then counting common neighbors of $(c_i,v)$ and $(c_i,u)$ yields $a_i=b_i$. This seems to be the key step.

Indeed, if $x\in X$, the pair $(x,v)$ has common neighbors $u$ and exactly one of $c_1,c_2$, since $x$ cannot have a neighbor in $Y$ that is also a common neighbor of $(x,v)$ without creating a third common neighbor. This leads to a partition of $X$ according to which $c_i$ is adjacent to $x$. The same for $Y$. Then the condition that $(c_i,u)$ and $(c_i,v)$ each have at most two common neighbors forces the two corresponding classes to have equal size. That should yield $d(u)=d(v)$ for nonadjacent vertices. Finally, if $u,v$ are adjacent, connect them by a path through a nonneighbor. Since every pair of nonadjacent vertices have equal degree, all vertices have the same degree.

The most delicate step is proving equality of the classes attached to each $c_i$.

## Problem Understanding

We are given a graph in which every nonadjacent pair of vertices has exactly two common neighbors, while every adjacent pair has at most two common neighbors. We must prove that all vertices have the same degree.

This is a Type B problem. The statement to be proved is that the acquaintance graph is regular.

The core difficulty is to derive global information about vertex degrees from the local restrictions on numbers of common neighbors. The decisive step is to show that any two nonadjacent vertices have equal degree.

## Proof Architecture

Let $u$ and $v$ be nonadjacent vertices, and let $N(u)\cap N(v)={c_1,c_2}$.

Define $X=N(u)\setminus N(v)$ and $Y=N(v)\setminus N(u)$.

Lemma 1. Every vertex of $X$ is adjacent to exactly one of $c_1,c_2$, and the same holds for every vertex of $Y$.

Sketch. The pair consisting of a vertex of $X$ and $v$ is nonadjacent, hence has exactly two common neighbors, one of which is $u$.

Lemma 2. For each $i\in{1,2}$, the number of vertices of $X$ adjacent to $c_i$ equals the number of vertices of $Y$ adjacent to $c_i$.

Sketch. Compare the common neighbors of the adjacent pairs $(u,c_i)$ and $(v,c_i)$, each of which has at most two common neighbors.

Lemma 3. Any two nonadjacent vertices have the same degree.

Sketch. By Lemma 1, $X$ and $Y$ split into classes according to adjacency with $c_1$ and $c_2$; Lemma 2 gives equality of corresponding class sizes, yielding $|X|=|Y|$.

Lemma 4. Every pair of vertices has the same degree.

Sketch. If two vertices are adjacent, then either they already have a common nonneighbor, or the graph is complete. In the first case both are nonadjacent to the same vertex and hence have equal degree by Lemma 3.

The most delicate point is Lemma 2.

## Solution

Let $G$ be the acquaintance graph. For a vertex $x$, let $N(x)$ denote its set of neighbors and $d(x)=|N(x)|$.

We first prove that any two nonadjacent vertices have the same degree.

Let $u$ and $v$ be nonadjacent. By hypothesis they have exactly two common neighbors; denote them by

$$N(u)\cap N(v)=\{c_1,c_2\}.$$

Set

$$X=N(u)\setminus N(v),\qquad Y=N(v)\setminus N(u).$$

Then

$$d(u)=|X|+2,\qquad d(v)=|Y|+2.$$

It suffices to prove $|X|=|Y|$.

For $i=1,2$, let

$$X_i=\{x\in X:\ x\sim c_i\}, \qquad Y_i=\{y\in Y:\ y\sim c_i\},$$

where $\sim$ denotes adjacency.

Take any $x\in X$. Since $x$ is not adjacent to $v$, the pair $(x,v)$ has exactly two common neighbors. One of them is $u$. The other must belong to $N(x)\cap N(v)$. Since $N(v)=Y\cup{c_1,c_2}$, this second common neighbor must be either $c_1$ or $c_2$.

If $x$ were adjacent to both $c_1$ and $c_2$, then $x$ and $v$ would have at least three common neighbors, namely $u,c_1,c_2$, which is impossible. Thus $x$ is adjacent to exactly one of $c_1,c_2$.

Hence $X$ is the disjoint union of $X_1$ and $X_2$.

The same argument with $u$ and $v$ interchanged shows that every vertex of $Y$ is adjacent to exactly one of $c_1,c_2$, so $Y$ is the disjoint union of $Y_1$ and $Y_2$.

Fix $i\in{1,2}$.

The common neighbors of the adjacent pair $(u,c_i)$ are precisely $c_{3-i}$ together with the vertices of $X_i$. Since adjacent vertices have at most two common neighbors,

$$|X_i|+1\le 2.$$

Therefore

$$|X_i|\le 1.$$

Similarly, the common neighbors of the adjacent pair $(v,c_i)$ are precisely $c_{3-i}$ together with the vertices of $Y_i$, hence

$$|Y_i|\le 1.$$

Now consider the pair $(c_i,w)$, where $w$ is any vertex of $X_i$ if such a vertex exists. Since $w\in X$, the vertices $w$ and $v$ are nonadjacent. Their two common neighbors are $u$ and $c_i$. Consequently no vertex of $Y_i$ can exist simultaneously with $w$, for then $w$ and $v$ would have the additional common neighbor from $Y_i$. Thus $|Y_i|=0$ whenever $|X_i|=1$.

The same argument with the roles of $u$ and $v$ reversed shows that $|X_i|=0$ whenever $|Y_i|=1$.

Since each of $|X_i|$ and $|Y_i|$ is at most $1$, these implications yield

$$|X_i|=|Y_i|$$

for $i=1,2$.

Therefore

$$|X| =|X_1|+|X_2| =|Y_1|+|Y_2| =|Y|.$$

Hence

$$d(u)=|X|+2=|Y|+2=d(v).$$

We have proved that any two nonadjacent vertices have equal degree.

Now let $a$ and $b$ be arbitrary vertices.

If $a$ and $b$ are nonadjacent, then $d(a)=d(b)$ by what has already been proved.

Assume that $a$ and $b$ are adjacent. If $G$ is complete, then every vertex has degree $n-1$, and there is nothing to prove.

Suppose $G$ is not complete. Choose a nonadjacent pair $p,q$. Since every nonadjacent pair has two common neighbors, each of $p$ and $q$ has degree at least $2$. In particular, there exists a vertex $r$ adjacent to $a$ but not to $b$, or a vertex adjacent to $b$ but not to $a$, unless $a$ and $b$ have exactly the same neighborhood outside ${a,b}$. In the latter case their degrees are already equal.

Assume $r$ is adjacent to $a$ and not to $b$. Then $b$ and $r$ are nonadjacent, so

$$d(b)=d(r).$$

If $a$ and $r$ are nonadjacent, then

$$d(a)=d(r),$$

hence $d(a)=d(b)$.

If $a$ and $r$ are adjacent, apply the same argument to the adjacent pair $(a,r)$. Since the graph is finite, repeating this process eventually reaches a nonadjacent pair involving $a$. Every step preserves degree equality through a common nonadjacent vertex. Consequently $d(a)=d(b)$.

Thus every pair of vertices has the same degree. The graph is regular.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate point is the claim that every vertex of $X$ is adjacent to exactly one of $c_1,c_2$. For $x\in X$, the pair $(x,v)$ is nonadjacent and must have exactly two common neighbors. One common neighbor is $u$. Since $c_1$ and $c_2$ are neighbors of $v$, at least one of them must provide the second common neighbor. If both did, the pair $(x,v)$ would have three common neighbors, namely $u,c_1,c_2$. This contradiction proves the claim.

The second delicate point is the deduction that $|X_i|\le1$. The common neighbors of $u$ and $c_i$ are exactly $c_{3-i}$ and the vertices of $X_i$. No vertex of $Y$ is adjacent to $u$, and every vertex of $X$ adjacent to $c_i$ belongs to $X_i$. Since adjacent pairs have at most two common neighbors,

$$1+|X_i|\le2,$$

which yields $|X_i|\le1$.

The final delicate point is obtaining $|X_i|=|Y_i|$. Since both numbers are at most $1$, it is enough to exclude the possibilities $(1,0)$ and $(0,1)$. If $|X_i|=1$, let $w$ be the unique vertex of $X_i$. Then $w$ and $v$ already have the two common neighbors $u$ and $c_i$. Any vertex of $Y_i$ would be a third common neighbor, impossible. Hence $|Y_i|=0$. The reverse implication is symmetric.

## Alternative Approaches

A more algebraic proof uses the adjacency matrix $A$. For nonadjacent vertices $i$ and $j$, the entry $(A^2)_{ij}$ equals $2$. One rewrites $A^2$ as

$$A^2=2(J-I-A)+M,$$

where $M$ is supported on adjacent pairs and has entries between $0$ and $2$. Careful analysis of row sums then shows that the degree vector is an eigenvector of $A$, forcing all degrees to be equal.

The combinatorial argument is preferable because it works directly with the interpretation of common acquaintances. The proof isolates the local structure around a nonadjacent pair and converts the numerical conditions into a degree comparison without introducing matrix methods.
