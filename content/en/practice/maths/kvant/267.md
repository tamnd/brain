## Problem

In the sequence of triples of integers (2, 3, 5), (6, 15, 10), $\ldots$ each triple is obtained from the previous one as follows: the first number is multiplied by the second, the second by the third, and the third by the first, and the resulting products form a new triple.

Prove that none of the numbers obtained in this way will be a perfect power of an integer: a square, cube, etc.

F. A. Bartenev

## Exploration

The construction keeps all numbers in the multiplicative semigroup generated by $2$, $3$, and $5$, since every new entry is a product of previous entries. Hence every number appearing in any triple has the form $2^a3^b5^c$ with nonnegative integers $a,b,c$.

A number is a perfect power if and only if the exponents $a,b,c$ have a common divisor greater than $1$. Thus the problem reduces to showing that for each coordinate in every triple, the corresponding exponent triple has greatest common divisor equal to $1$.

The evolution of exponents is linear: for each prime, the exponent vector across the three coordinates evolves under the same $3\times 3$ matrix

$$M=\begin{pmatrix} 1&1&0\\ 0&1&1\\ 1&0&1 \end{pmatrix},$$

so that exponent data for each prime evolves independently.

The key risk is that all exponents of a fixed coordinate could become simultaneously divisible by some $k>1$ at some step. This translates into a modular linear-algebra condition on rows of $M^t$.

Thus the essential task is to show that no row of $M^t$ can become the zero vector modulo any integer $k>1$.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem.

We are given a dynamical system on triples of integers defined by

$$(a,b,c)\mapsto (ab,bc,ca),$$

starting from $(2,3,5)$. We must prove that no number appearing in any coordinate of any iterate is a perfect power.

The structure suggests tracking prime exponents. Since only primes $2$, $3$, and $5$ ever appear, each number is of the form $2^a3^b5^c$, and being a perfect power is equivalent to $a,b,c$ having a common divisor greater than $1$. The task reduces to proving that no coordinate ever acquires exponent triples simultaneously divisible by any $k>1$.

## Proof Architecture

The exponent of each prime evolves independently under the same linear transformation $M$ on $\mathbb{Z}^3$.

For each time $t$, we represent exponent data as a matrix $A_t$, whose rows correspond to coordinates and columns correspond to primes.

We prove that $A_{t+1}=MA_t$ with

$$M=\begin{pmatrix} 1&1&0\\ 0&1&1\\ 1&0&1 \end{pmatrix}.$$

We show that a number in the sequence is a perfect $k$-th power if and only if the corresponding row of $A_t$ is zero modulo $k$.

We then split into two cases: $k$ odd and $k$ even. For odd $k$, invertibility of $M$ modulo $k$ prevents zero rows. For $k=2$, an explicit computation shows that the rows of $M^t$ permute cyclically and never vanish.

The most delicate point is the structure of $M^t$ modulo $2$, where invertibility fails and must be replaced by direct dynamical analysis.

## Solution

Each number produced from $(2,3,5)$ by the rule $(a,b,c)\mapsto (ab,bc,ca)$ is a product of powers of $2$, $3$, and $5$, since multiplication does not introduce new prime divisors. Hence every entry at every step has the form

$$2^a3^b5^c$$

for some nonnegative integers $a,b,c$.

A number of this form is a perfect $k$-th power with $k\ge 2$ if and only if $k$ divides each of $a$, $b$, and $c$. Thus it suffices to prove that for every time step and every coordinate, the associated exponent triple has greatest common divisor equal to $1$.

For each fixed prime $p\in{2,3,5}$, let $v_p(t)=(x_1(t),x_2(t),x_3(t))$ denote the exponents of $p$ in the three coordinates at time $t$. The recurrence rule implies

$$x_1(t+1)=x_1(t)+x_2(t),\quad x_2(t+1)=x_2(t)+x_3(t),\quad x_3(t+1)=x_3(t)+x_1(t).$$

Thus

$$v_p(t+1)=Mv_p(t),$$

where

$$M=\begin{pmatrix} 1&1&0\\ 0&1&1\\ 1&0&1 \end{pmatrix}.$$

Let $A_t$ be the $3\times 3$ matrix whose columns are $v_2(t)$, $v_3(t)$, and $v_5(t)$. Then $A_{t+1}=MA_t$. The $i$-th row of $A_t$ records the exponent triple of the $i$-th coordinate of the sequence.

A coordinate at time $t$ is a perfect $k$-th power if and only if all entries in the corresponding row of $A_t$ are divisible by $k$, which is equivalent to the row being the zero vector in $(\mathbb{Z}/k\mathbb{Z})^3$.

We now prove that no row of $A_t$ can be zero modulo any $k>1$.

First assume $k$ is odd. The determinant of $M$ is

$$\det M = 1\cdot(1\cdot 1-1\cdot 0)-1\cdot(0\cdot 1-1\cdot 1)=1-(-1)=2.$$

Since $\gcd(2,k)=1$, the matrix $M$ is invertible modulo $k$. Hence every power $M^t$ is also invertible modulo $k$, so no row of $M^t$ can be the zero vector in $(\mathbb{Z}/k\mathbb{Z})^3$. Therefore no coordinate exponent triple is simultaneously divisible by any odd $k>1$.

It remains to consider $k=2$. Reducing $M$ modulo $2$ gives

$$M \equiv \begin{pmatrix} 1&1&0\\ 0&1&1\\ 1&0&1 \end{pmatrix}\pmod 2.$$

A direct computation in $\mathbb{F}_2$ shows that rows evolve periodically under multiplication by $M$. Indeed, if the rows of a matrix $X$ are $r_1,r_2,r_3$, then multiplying by $M$ replaces them by

$$r_1' = r_1+r_2,\quad r_2'=r_2+r_3,\quad r_3'=r_3+r_1.$$

Starting from $M^0=I$, the rows are $(1,0,0)$, $(0,1,0)$, $(0,0,1)$, none of which is zero. For $M^1$, the rows are $(1,1,0)$, $(0,1,1)$, $(1,0,1)$, again none is zero.

Computing one further step,

$$(1,1,0)\mapsto (1,0,1),\quad (0,1,1)\mapsto (1,1,0),\quad (1,0,1)\mapsto (0,1,1),$$

so the rows of $M^2$ are a permutation of the rows of $M^1$. Hence the set of rows cycles among these three nonzero vectors for all $t$, and no row ever becomes zero modulo $2$.

Thus for every integer $k\ge 2$ and every time $t$, no row of $A_t$ is the zero vector modulo $k$. Therefore no coordinate ever has all exponents divisible by $k$, so no number in the sequence is a perfect power.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The reduction from perfect powers to divisibility of exponent triples relies on the unique prime factorization of integers, since $2$, $3$, and $5$ are the only primes that ever appear. A number $2^a3^b5^c$ is a $k$-th power precisely when each exponent is a multiple of $k$, because taking a $k$-th root divides all exponents by $k$.

The identification of exponent evolution with multiplication by $M$ follows directly from the rule $(a,b,c)\mapsto (ab,bc,ca)$, since exponent addition under multiplication yields linear combinations matching the rows of $M$.

The crucial modular step is the analysis for $k=2$. The computation showing that applying $M$ permutes the three nonzero row vectors in $\mathbb{F}_2^3$ ensures that no row can collapse to zero, since zero cannot be part of a nontrivial permutation cycle.

## Alternative Approaches

One alternative approach uses eigenstructure of $M$ over the integers and studies growth of exponent vectors via diagonalization over $\mathbb{C}$, then reduces modulo $k$ to exclude periodic vanishing. This method is more abstract and requires handling reducibility issues of $M$ modulo composite moduli.

Another approach interprets the system as a dynamical system on the projective space of exponent vectors, where perfect powers correspond to rational points with special rational ratios. The modular linear algebra approach used here is shorter and avoids spectral analysis while isolating the only obstruction at modulus $2$.
