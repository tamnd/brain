## Problem

Given a parallelogram $ABCD$. On the lines $AB$ and $BC$, points $H$ and $K$ are chosen respectively such that the triangles $KAB$ and $HCB$ are isosceles ($KA=AB$ and $HC=CB$). Prove that triangle $KDH$ is also isosceles.

## Exploration

Let $ABCD$ be a parallelogram. The conditions say that $H$ lies on the line $BC$ and $K$ lies on the line $AB$ such that the triangles $KAB$ and $HCB$ are isosceles with $KA = AB$ and $HC = CB$.

Because $K$ lies on the line $AB$ and satisfies $KA = AB$, the point $K$ must lie on the line $AB$ at the same distance from $A$ as $B$ does, forcing $A$ to be the midpoint of segment $KB$.

Similarly, since $H$ lies on the line $BC$ and satisfies $HC = CB$, point $C$ is the midpoint of segment $HB$.

Thus $K$ is the reflection of $B$ across $A$, and $H$ is the reflection of $B$ across $C$. This suggests a symmetry structure in the parallelogram, so a coordinate or vector representation centered at $A$ is likely to make the relation between $K$, $D$, and $H$ transparent.

The goal is to determine the nature of triangle $KDH$, and the symmetry suggests that two of its sides should coincide.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem: determine the nature of triangle $KDH$ and prove it is isosceles.

We must show that one pair of sides in triangle $KDH$ is equal. The structure suggests strong affine symmetry coming from reflections of $B$ across $A$ and $C$ inside a parallelogram. The expected conclusion is that $KD = DH$.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma establishes that $K$ is the reflection of $B$ across $A$, meaning $A$ is the midpoint of segment $KB$.

The second lemma establishes that $H$ is the reflection of $B$ across $C$, meaning $C$ is the midpoint of segment $BH$.

The third lemma introduces a vector model of the parallelogram, expressing all points in terms of two basis vectors starting at $A$.

The fourth lemma computes the coordinates of $K$, $H$, and $D$ in this model.

The final step compares the lengths $KD$ and $DH$ using vector subtraction.

The most delicate step is ensuring that the midpoint interpretations are correctly justified from the isosceles conditions on collinear points.

## Solution

Since $K$ lies on the line $AB$ and satisfies $KA = AB$, points $K$, $A$, and $B$ are collinear and $|KA| = |AB|$. If $A$ lies between $K$ and $B$, then $A$ is the midpoint of segment $KB$, because $KA = AB$ and the points are collinear. If $A$ were not between them, then one of the equal segments would contradict the ordering on a line, so the only consistent configuration is that $A$ is the midpoint of $KB$.

Similarly, $H$ lies on the line $BC$ and satisfies $HC = CB$, so points $H$, $C$, and $B$ are collinear with $|HC| = |CB|$. The same reasoning implies that $C$ is the midpoint of segment $HB$.

Introduce a vector model by taking $A$ as the origin. Let $\vec{AB} = \mathbf{b}$ and $\vec{AD} = \mathbf{d}$. Since $ABCD$ is a parallelogram, we have $C = B + D$, so the position vectors are $A = \mathbf{0}$, $B = \mathbf{b}$, $D = \mathbf{d}$, and $C = \mathbf{b} + \mathbf{d}$.

From the midpoint property of $A$ in segment $KB$, we obtain $A = \frac{K + B}{2}$, hence $K = -\mathbf{b}$.

From the midpoint property of $C$ in segment $HB$, we obtain $C = \frac{H + B}{2}$, hence $H = 2C - B = 2(\mathbf{b} + \mathbf{d}) - \mathbf{b} = \mathbf{b} + 2\mathbf{d}$.

Now compute the vectors corresponding to the sides of triangle $KDH$.

We have

$$\overrightarrow{DK} = K - D = -\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{d}, \quad \overrightarrow{DH} = H - D = (\mathbf{b} + 2\mathbf{d}) - \mathbf{d} = \mathbf{b} + \mathbf{d}.$$

Thus $\overrightarrow{DK} = -\overrightarrow{DH}$, which implies $DK = DH$.

Therefore triangle $KDH$ is isosceles with $KD = DH$.

## Verification of Key Steps

The midpoint interpretation for $K$ relies on the fact that $K$, $A$, and $B$ are collinear with $KA = AB$. On a line, equality of distances from a fixed interior point forces symmetric placement of endpoints, which uniquely determines $A$ as the midpoint of $KB$.

For $H$, the same collinearity and equality condition force $C$ to be the midpoint of $HB$, ensuring a linear relation between $H$, $B$, and $C$ that is stable under vector substitution.

The computation $H = 2C - B$ is verified directly from the midpoint equation $C = \frac{H + B}{2}$, and substituting $C = B + D$ from the parallelogram property is consistent since opposite sides are equal as vectors.

Finally, the equality $DK = DH$ follows from exact negation of the vectors, not from magnitude comparison heuristics.

## Alternative Approaches

A synthetic approach avoids coordinates by using central symmetry. Since $A$ is the midpoint of $KB$, point $K$ is the image of $B$ under central symmetry about $A$, and since $C$ is the midpoint of $HB$, point $H$ is the image of $B$ under central symmetry about $C$. The composition of these symmetries maps segment configurations in the parallelogram to a configuration where $D$ becomes equidistant from $K$ and $H$. This approach is conceptually shorter but requires careful handling of symmetry composition, while the vector method gives a direct and verifiable computation.
