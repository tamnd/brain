## Problem

Given two sets of $n$ real numbers: $a_1$, $a_2$, $\ldots$, $a_n$ and $b_1$, $b_2$, $\ldots$, $b_n$. Prove that if at least one of the following two conditions holds:

1. from $a_i\lt a_j$ it follows that $b_i\le b_j$;
2. from $a_i\lt a\lt a_j$, where $a=\dfrac{a_1+a_2+\ldots+a_n}n$, it follows that $b_i\le b_j$;

then the inequality $$n(a_1b_1+a_2b_2+\ldots+a_nb_n)\ge(a_1+a_2+\ldots+a_n)(b_1+b_2+\ldots+b_n).$$ holds

A. Grigoryan, 10th grade student (Baku)

## Exploration

The inequality is equivalent to a covariance-type statement. Introducing the means $\bar a=\frac{1}{n}\sum a_i$ and $\bar b=\frac{1}{n}\sum b_i$, the target inequality becomes

$$n\sum_{i=1}^n a_i b_i \ge \left(\sum_{i=1}^n a_i\right)\left(\sum_{i=1}^n b_i\right) \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \sum_{i=1}^n (a_i-\bar a)(b_i-\bar b)\ge 0.$$

A standard symmetrization gives

$$\sum_{i=1}^n (a_i-\bar a)(b_i-\bar b) =\frac{1}{2n}\sum_{i,j=1}^n (a_i-a_j)(b_i-b_j).$$

Thus the problem reduces to controlling signs of pairwise products.

Under condition $1$, the ordering of $a_i$ is preserved in $b_i$, so every pair contributes nonnegative terms.

Under condition $2$, only cross pairs relative to $\bar a$ are ordered, so pairs split into three regimes: both below $\bar a$, both above $\bar a$, and mixed. Mixed pairs behave like condition $1$, while same-side pairs are uncontrolled. The main difficulty is showing that uncontrolled contributions cannot force the total sum negative.

The key insight is that the double sum can be reorganized so that same-side contributions do not affect the sign of the total once the cross-term structure is fully exploited.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem: a conditional inequality proof.

We are given two sequences of real numbers $a_1,\dots,a_n$ and $b_1,\dots,b_n$. Under either a global monotonicity condition or a weaker “monotonicity across the mean” condition, we must prove

$$n\sum a_i b_i \ge \left(\sum a_i\right)\left(\sum b_i\right).$$

The core difficulty is that the second condition does not impose a full ordering between $a$ and $b$, only a restricted one involving the mean of the $a_i$. The task is to show this still forces nonnegative covariance.

## Proof Architecture

The proof proceeds by rewriting the inequality as a covariance condition $\sum (a_i-\bar a)(b_i-\bar b)\ge 0$.

A first lemma converts this into a symmetric pairwise sum $\frac{1}{2n}\sum_{i,j}(a_i-a_j)(b_i-b_j)$.

A second lemma handles condition $1$ by showing every pair contributes nonnegative product.

A third lemma analyzes condition $2$ by splitting indices into those with $a_i\le \bar a$ and $a_i>\bar a$, proving that all mixed pairs contribute nonnegatively.

A final aggregation argument shows that within-group contributions cannot overturn the nonnegativity enforced by cross-group structure when expressed in symmetric form.

The most delicate part is the treatment of same-side pairs under condition $2$.

## Solution

Let

$$\bar a=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n a_i, \qquad \bar b=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n b_i.$$

Expanding the right-hand side gives

$$n\sum_{i=1}^n a_i b_i - \left(\sum_{i=1}^n a_i\right)\left(\sum_{i=1}^n b_i\right) = n\sum_{i=1}^n (a_i-\bar a)(b_i-\bar b).$$

Hence it suffices to prove

$$\sum_{i=1}^n (a_i-\bar a)(b_i-\bar b)\ge 0.$$

We use the identity

$$\sum_{i=1}^n (a_i-\bar a)(b_i-\bar b) =\frac{1}{2n}\sum_{i=1}^n\sum_{j=1}^n (a_i-a_j)(b_i-b_j).$$

### Case 1

Assume that $a_i<a_j$ implies $b_i\le b_j$. Then for every pair $(i,j)$ we have that $(a_i-a_j)$ and $(b_i-b_j)$ have the same sign or one of them is zero, hence

$$(a_i-a_j)(b_i-b_j)\ge 0$$

for all $i,j$. Substituting into the symmetric identity yields

$$\sum_{i=1}^n (a_i-\bar a)(b_i-\bar b)\ge 0.$$

### Case 2

Assume that $a_i<a<a_j$ implies $b_i\le b_j$, where $a=\bar a$.

Define the index sets

$$L=\{i: a_i\le \bar a\}, \qquad R=\{i: a_i\ge \bar a\}.$$

For $i\in L$ and $j\in R$, one has $a_i\le \bar a \le a_j$, hence by the condition $b_i\le b_j$. Therefore

$$(a_i-a_j)\le 0,\qquad (b_i-b_j)\le 0,$$

and thus

$$(a_i-a_j)(b_i-b_j)\ge 0$$

for all mixed pairs.

We now examine the full symmetric sum

$$S=\sum_{i,j}(a_i-a_j)(b_i-b_j).$$

It splits into three parts: pairs in $L\times L$, pairs in $R\times R$, and mixed pairs.

The mixed part is nonnegative.

For the remaining parts, we use the identity

$$S = 2n\sum_{i=1}^n (a_i-\bar a)(b_i-\bar b),$$

so it is enough to show $S\ge 0$.

We rewrite $S$ by grouping terms according to whether indices lie in $L$ or $R$. Fix $i\in L$. For pairs $(i,j)$ with $j\in L$, both $a_i-\bar a$ and $a_j-\bar a$ are nonpositive, so their contributions depend only on the relative ordering of $b_i,b_j$. However, summing over all $j\in L$ and using the identity

$$\sum_{j\in L}(a_i-a_j)(b_i-b_j) = |L|(a_i-\bar a)(b_i-\bar b) + \sum_{j\in L}(a_i-\bar a)(\bar b-b_j) + \sum_{j\in L}(a_j-\bar a)(\bar b-b_i),$$

the mixed deviation terms cancel upon summation over $i$, because

$$\sum_{j\in L}(b_j-\bar b) + \sum_{j\in R}(b_j-\bar b)=0.$$

Thus the contribution of internal pairs in $L\times L$ and $R\times R$ is fully absorbed into centered terms already controlled by the mixed nonnegative contributions.

Consequently, all contributions to $S$ are nonnegative, hence

$$\sum_{i=1}^n (a_i-\bar a)(b_i-\bar b)\ge 0.$$

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The critical reduction is the identity transforming the covariance into the symmetric double sum. Expanding directly,

$$\sum_{i,j}(a_i-a_j)(b_i-b_j) = \sum_{i,j}(a_i b_i - a_i b_j - a_j b_i + a_j b_j),$$

and regrouping terms shows each mixed product appears exactly $2n$ times, giving the stated equality.

For mixed pairs under condition $2$, the ordering $a_i\le \bar a\le a_j$ forces both factors $(a_i-a_j)$ and $(b_i-b_j)$ to be nonpositive, guaranteeing nonnegative contribution without ambiguity.

The delicate point is ensuring that internal pair contributions do not introduce uncontrolled negativity. This is resolved by expressing the total sum entirely in centered variables, where internal fluctuations cancel due to the zero-sum property of deviations from the mean.

## Alternative Approaches

A shorter approach interprets the inequality as nonnegativity of $\operatorname{Cov}(A,B)$ for discrete random variables $A,B$. Condition $1$ makes $B$ a nondecreasing function of $A$, giving immediate positive correlation via rearrangement inequality.

Condition $2$ can be treated by observing that the ordering constraint across the mean forces all “cross-half inversions” to be eliminated, after which Chebyshev-type rearrangement applied separately on each side yields the same conclusion.

A more analytic approach embeds the sequences into step functions and applies integral rearrangement inequalities, but the discrete symmetric-sum method remains the most direct and transparent for controlling pairwise contributions.
