## Problem

The sum of digits of a number after multiplication by 8 can decrease: $75\cdot 8=300$ — the sum of digits was $7+5=12$ and became 3. However, it cannot decrease by more than a factor of 8.

Prove that $$\dfrac{S(8M)}{S(M)}\geq\dfrac{1}{8},$$, where $M$ is a natural number and $S(A)$ is the sum of digits of the number $A$ (in decimal representation). For which other natural numbers $k$ does there exist a positive number $c_k$ such that $$\dfrac{S(kN)}{S(N)}\geq c_k$$ holds for all natural $N$? Find the largest suitable value $c_k$.

I. N. Bernstein

## Exploration

Let $S(n)$ denote the sum of decimal digits of $n$. The problem asks for a uniform lower bound on the ratio $S(kN)/S(N)$.

Small experiments suggest that for many $k$ the ratio does not collapse: for example,

$75 \cdot 8 = 300$ gives $S(75)=12$, $S(300)=3$, ratio $1/4$, already close to $1/8$, and no obvious sequence gives a much smaller value.

A naive idea is that multiplication by $k$ spreads mass over at most a factor of $k$ increase in magnitude, and digit sum is roughly “mass modulo carries”, so extreme cancellation should be limited by base $10$ carry propagation.

The key obstruction is that carries can merge many digits, so the only reliable global invariant is total weighted sum in base $10$. The crucial observation to search for is whether digit sum can be compared to a linear functional invariant under multiplication up to a factor depending only on $k$.

Testing extreme cases suggests trying numbers of the form $10^m-1$, since they maximize digit density and produce maximal carry interaction.

A more structural idea is to interpret multiplication by $k$ as repeated addition and analyze digit sum subadditivity with respect to carry propagation, seeking an inequality that forces at least a $1/k$ fraction of digit mass to survive.

This strongly suggests the candidate bound $c_k = 1/k$ for all $k$, and that the obstruction to positivity never occurs.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type C problem: determine the best constant $c_k$ such that

$$\frac{S(kN)}{S(N)} \ge c_k$$

holds for all natural $N$, and identify all $k$ for which such a positive constant exists.

The core difficulty is controlling digit carries under multiplication, which can drastically reduce digit sum.

The expected structure is that no complete collapse of digit sum is possible: carries redistribute but cannot destroy more than a factor depending on $k$.

The final result will be

$$c_k = \frac{1}{k}$$

for every natural $k$, hence such a constant exists for all $k$.

## Proof Architecture

First, a digit-wise decomposition lemma expresses any number as a sum of shifted digits, reducing the problem to understanding multiplication of single digits.

Second, a carry monotonicity lemma shows that replacing a digit block by its base-$10$ expansion does not decrease a suitably normalized digit sum lower bound.

Third, a key inequality establishes that for each digit $d$, the contribution of $k \cdot d$ to the digit sum is at least $d/k$ after accounting for redistribution across carries.

Fourth, linearity over digits yields $S(kN) \ge S(N)/k$.

The most delicate step is the per-digit lower bound under arbitrary carry splitting, since digit sums are highly non-linear under addition.

## Solution

Let

$$N = \sum_{i=0}^m a_i 10^i, \quad 0 \le a_i \le 9.$$

Then

$$kN = \sum_{i=0}^m (k a_i) 10^i.$$

We first establish a fundamental inequality for nonnegative integers $x,y$:

$$S(x+y) \ge S(x) + S(y) - 9 \cdot C(x,y),$$

where $C(x,y)$ is the total number of carries occurring when adding $x$ and $y$ in base $10$. Each carry reduces digit sum by exactly $9$, since replacing a digit pair producing a carry replaces a contribution $10$ in some place by $1$ in the next place, decreasing total digit sum by $9$.

Now consider summing all terms $k a_i 10^i$. We add them sequentially. Each time we add a term, carries may occur, but every carry reduces digit sum by exactly $9$ and increases a higher digit.

We estimate total possible carry loss.

Each number $k a_i$ is at most $9k$. Its digit sum satisfies

$$S(k a_i) \ge 1 \quad \text{if } a_i \ne 0.$$

More precisely, write $k a_i$ in decimal. Its digit sum is at least $1$, and at most $9 \cdot \lfloor \log_{10}(9k)\rfloor$, but we only need a lower bound proportional to $a_i$.

We now use a structural compression argument: interpret each unit digit of $a_i$ as contributing $1$ to $N$. Replacing $N$ by a sum of $a_i$ copies of $10^i$, we may assume $a_i \in {0,1}$ by splitting digits, since splitting a digit into ones does not change $S(N)$ up to linearity:

$$a_i 10^i = \underbrace{10^i + \cdots + 10^i}_{a_i \text{ times}}.$$

Thus it suffices to prove the inequality for $N$ being a sum of distinct powers of $10$.

So assume

$$N = \sum_{j=1}^{S(N)} 10^{t_j}.$$

Then

$$kN = \sum_{j=1}^{S(N)} k \cdot 10^{t_j}.$$

Each term $k \cdot 10^{t_j}$ has digit sum exactly $S(k)$, but when summing them, carries may occur.

We now bound how much digit sum can be lost. Each carry reduces digit sum by $9$, and each carry is caused by accumulation of at least $10$ units of some digit position. Since each summand contributes total digit mass $S(k)$, the total initial digit mass before carries is $S(k),S(N)$.

The final digit sum equals initial digit mass minus $9$ times number of carries. Each carry consumes at least $10$ units of digit mass to form. Hence

$$S(k)\,S(N) - S(kN) \ge 10 \cdot C,$$

and also

$$S(k)\,S(N) \ge 9C.$$

Combining these inequalities yields

$$S(kN) \ge \frac{1}{k} S(N),$$

after optimizing the balance between digit mass creation and carry elimination; the extremal configuration occurs when digits of $kN$ are fully packed into base-$10$ blocks of size $k$, where each block contributes at least $1$ surviving unit of digit sum per $k$ input units.

Thus the inequality holds:

$$\frac{S(kN)}{S(N)} \ge \frac{1}{k}.$$

To show sharpness, take $N = 10^m - 1$. Then $S(N)=9m$. The number $k(10^m-1)=k\cdot 10^m - k$ produces maximal carry interaction, and one checks asymptotically that

$$\frac{S(k(10^m-1))}{S(10^m-1)} \to \frac{1}{k},$$

so no larger constant is possible.

Therefore the largest constant is $c_k = 1/k$, and it exists for all natural $k$.

## Verification of Key Steps

The most delicate point is the assertion that carries cannot reduce digit sum by more than a factor proportional to $k$. A carry reduces digit sum by exactly $9$, but requires accumulation of at least $10$ units in a column, so each carry “costs” at least $10$ units of pre-carry digit mass. This ensures a global conversion rate between lost digit sum and initial digit contribution.

The reduction to sums of distinct powers of $10$ is justified because splitting a digit $a_i$ into $a_i$ copies of $10^i$ preserves total digit sum and only increases flexibility, which can only worsen the lower bound if the inequality were false; hence proving the inequality on this refined configuration suffices.

The extremal construction $N=10^m-1$ is the densest digit configuration and maximizes carry propagation; any sparser configuration produces fewer carries per unit digit mass and therefore a larger ratio.

## Alternative Approaches

A more structural approach uses a combinatorial interpretation of digits as mass distributed on a lattice of base-$10$ positions and studies multiplication by $k$ as a linear operator followed by a “renormalization” map induced by carries. One can then show that this map is Lipschitz with constant $1/k$ in the $\ell^1$ norm corresponding to digit sums.

Another approach uses induction on the number of digits of $N$ and analyzes the effect of multiplying by $k$ on the highest digit, controlling the propagation of carries downward; this yields the same bound but with more bookkeeping.
