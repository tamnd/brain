## Problem

A sphere of radius 1 is given. Equal circles $\gamma_0$, $\gamma_1$, $\ldots$, $\gamma_n$ of radius $r$ ($n\ge3$) are placed on it. The circle $\gamma_0$ is tangent to all the circles $\gamma_1$, $\ldots$, $\gamma_n$; furthermore, the circles $\gamma_1$ and $\gamma_2$ are tangent to each other; $\gamma_2$ and $\gamma_3$; $\ldots$; $\gamma_n$ and $\gamma_1$.

For which $n$ is this possible? Compute the corresponding radius $r$.

Czechoslovak Mathematical Olympiad (XVIII)

## Exploration

Each circle on the sphere is the intersection of the sphere with a plane. Since all circles have the same radius $r$, all their planes are at the same distance from the center $O$ of the sphere. Let this distance be $h=\sqrt{1-r^2}$.

Let $A_i$ be the center of the circle $\gamma_i$ on its defining plane. Then $A_i$ lies on the radius perpendicular to that plane, hence $A_i$ is on the sphere of radius $h$ centered at $O$. Thus the configuration of equal circles on the unit sphere corresponds to a configuration of points $A_i$ on the sphere of radius $h$.

The first task is to translate tangency of circles into a condition on the points $A_i$. Consider two equal circles whose centers are $A$ and $B$. Their planes are perpendicular to $OA$ and $OB$ and lie at distance $h$ from $O$. If the angle $\angle AOB=\theta$, the distance from either circle center to the line of intersection of the two planes equals $h\tan(\theta/2)$. The circles are tangent precisely when this distance equals the circle radius $r$. Hence

$$h\tan\frac{\theta}{2}=r.$$

Since $h^2+r^2=1$, this becomes

$$\tan\frac{\theta}{2}=\frac{r}{h}, \qquad \sin\frac{\theta}{2}=r.$$

Thus tangency of two equal circles is equivalent to

$$\theta=2\arcsin r.$$

Let this angle be $\alpha$. Then every tangency relation among the circles becomes a distance relation on the sphere of radius $h$: the corresponding points are separated by the same central angle $\alpha$.

The point $A_0$ is at angular distance $\alpha$ from each $A_i$ ($i\ge1$). Hence all $A_1,\dots,A_n$ lie on the small circle on the sphere of radius $h$ centered at $A_0$. In addition, consecutive points $A_i,A_{i+1}$ are also at angular distance $\alpha$.

The geometry now becomes a spherical polygon problem. Because all $A_i$ lie on a circle around $A_0$ and consecutive distances are equal, they form a regular $n$-gon on that circle. Let $\varphi=2\pi/n$ be the central angle of this Euclidean circle.

The crucial step is to express the condition $A_iA_{i+1}=\alpha$ in terms of $\alpha$ and $\varphi$.

## Problem Understanding

We are given equal circles $\gamma_0,\gamma_1,\ldots,\gamma_n$ on the unit sphere. The circle $\gamma_0$ is tangent to each of the others, and the circles $\gamma_1,\ldots,\gamma_n$ form a cyclic chain of pairwise tangencies:

$$\gamma_1\sim\gamma_2\sim\cdots\sim\gamma_n\sim\gamma_1.$$

We must determine for which integers $n\ge3$ such a configuration exists and find the corresponding circle radius $r$.

This is a Type A problem. We must classify all possible $n$ and compute the corresponding $r$.

The central idea is to replace each circle by the point on the sphere of radius $\sqrt{1-r^2}$ that determines its plane. Tangency between equal circles then becomes a fixed angular distance between the corresponding points. The given configuration turns into a regular $n$-gon on a small circle of a sphere, and the resulting trigonometric condition determines $n$ and $r$ uniquely.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1. Two equal circles of radius $r$ on the unit sphere are tangent if and only if the angle between the corresponding radius vectors equals $\alpha$, where $\sin(\alpha/2)=r$; this follows from a direct computation in the two defining planes.

Lemma 2. The points $A_1,\dots,A_n$ corresponding to $\gamma_1,\dots,\gamma_n$ all lie on a small circle of the sphere of radius $h=\sqrt{1-r^2}$ centered at $A_0$; this follows because each is at angular distance $\alpha$ from $A_0$.

Lemma 3. Consecutive vertices on that small circle subtend equal arcs, hence $A_1,\dots,A_n$ form a regular $n$-gon on the small circle; this follows because every consecutive pair is separated by the same spherical distance $\alpha$.

Lemma 4. If $\varphi=2\pi/n$, then

$$\cos\alpha=\cos^2\alpha+\sin^2\alpha\cos\varphi.$$

This is obtained by computing the spherical distance between consecutive vertices of the regular polygon.

Lemma 5. Equation

$$\cos\alpha=\cos^2\alpha+\sin^2\alpha\cos\varphi$$

reduces to

$$\cos\alpha=\frac{\cos\varphi}{1-\cos\varphi}.$$

Combining this with $0<\alpha<\pi$ yields $\cos\varphi>0$, hence $n\ge5$, and then

$$r^2=\frac{1-\cos\alpha}{2} =\frac{1-2\cos\varphi}{2(1-\cos\varphi)}.$$

Lemma 6. Positivity of $r^2$ requires $\cos\varphi<\frac12$, while $\cos\alpha>0$ requires $\cos\varphi>0$. Thus

$$0<\cos\varphi<\frac12.$$

Among integers $n\ge3$, this occurs only for $n=5$.

The hardest point is Lemma 4, where the distance between consecutive vertices of the regular polygon must be computed correctly.

## Solution

Let $O$ be the center of the unit sphere.

Each circle of radius $r$ on the sphere is obtained by intersecting the sphere with a plane at distance

$$h=\sqrt{1-r^2}$$

from $O$.

For each circle $\gamma_i$, let $A_i$ be the foot of the perpendicular from $O$ to its plane. Then

$$OA_i=h,$$

so all points $A_i$ lie on the sphere of radius $h$ centered at $O$.

We first translate tangency into a condition on the points $A_i$.

Consider two equal circles corresponding to points $A$ and $B$. Let

$$\angle AOB=\alpha .$$

The two defining planes meet along a line. In the plane through $O$, $A$, and $B$, the distances from $A$ and $B$ to this line are both

$$h\tan\frac{\alpha}{2}.$$

The circles are tangent exactly when this quantity equals their radius $r$. Hence

$$h\tan\frac{\alpha}{2}=r.$$

Since $h^2+r^2=1$,

$$\tan\frac{\alpha}{2}=\frac{r}{h},$$

and therefore

$$\sin\frac{\alpha}{2}=r.$$

Thus two circles are tangent if and only if the corresponding points on the sphere of radius $h$ are separated by the central angle $\alpha$, where

$$r=\sin\frac{\alpha}{2}.$$

Because $\gamma_0$ is tangent to every $\gamma_i$,

$$\angle A_0OA_i=\alpha \qquad (i=1,\dots,n).$$

Hence all points $A_1,\dots,A_n$ lie on the small circle

$$S=\{X:\angle A_0OX=\alpha\}$$

of the sphere of radius $h$.

Also, since $\gamma_i$ is tangent to $\gamma_{i+1}$ (indices modulo $n$),

$$\angle A_iOA_{i+1}=\alpha .$$

The points $A_1,\dots,A_n$ lie on the same Euclidean circle $S$. Equal chord lengths on a circle cut off equal arcs, and all consecutive spherical distances are equal to $\alpha$. Hence the vertices divide $S$ into $n$ equal arcs. Therefore they form a regular $n$-gon on $S$.

Let

$$\varphi=\frac{2\pi}{n}.$$

Choose coordinates on the sphere of radius $h$ so that

$$A_0=(0,0,h).$$

Then the circle $S$ is the parallel with colatitude $\alpha$, and its points have coordinates

$$h(\sin\alpha\cos t,\ \sin\alpha\sin t,\ \cos\alpha).$$

Two consecutive vertices correspond to parameters differing by $\varphi$. Their unit direction vectors therefore have scalar product

$$\cos^2\alpha+\sin^2\alpha\cos\varphi .$$

Since their angular separation equals $\alpha$, we obtain

$$\cos\alpha = \cos^2\alpha+\sin^2\alpha\cos\varphi .$$

Using $\sin^2\alpha=1-\cos^2\alpha$,

$$\cos\alpha = \cos^2\alpha+(1-\cos^2\alpha)\cos\varphi .$$

Rearranging,

$$(1-\cos\varphi)\cos^2\alpha-\cos\alpha+\cos\varphi=0.$$

Factoring,

$$(\cos\alpha-1)\bigl((1-\cos\varphi)\cos\alpha-\cos\varphi\bigr)=0.$$

Since $\alpha>0$, we have $\cos\alpha\ne1$, hence

$$\cos\alpha=\frac{\cos\varphi}{1-\cos\varphi}.$$

Because $0<\alpha<\pi$, we must have

$$-1<\frac{\cos\varphi}{1-\cos\varphi}<1.$$

The right inequality gives $\cos\varphi<\frac12$, and positivity of the left-hand side gives $\cos\varphi>0$. Therefore

$$0<\cos\varphi<\frac12.$$

Since

$$\varphi=\frac{2\pi}{n},$$

we examine integers $n\ge3$.

For $n=3$, $\cos\varphi=-\frac12$.

For $n=4$, $\cos\varphi=0$.

For $n=5$, $\cos\varphi=\cos72^\circ=\frac{\sqrt5-1}{4}$, which lies between $0$ and $\frac12$.

For $n\ge6$, $\varphi\le60^\circ$, hence $\cos\varphi\ge\frac12$.

Thus the only possible value is

$$n=5.$$

Substituting

$$\cos\varphi=\frac{\sqrt5-1}{4}$$

into

$$\cos\alpha=\frac{\cos\varphi}{1-\cos\varphi},$$

we obtain

$$\cos\alpha=\frac{\sqrt5-1}{3-\sqrt5} =\frac{1+\sqrt5}{4}.$$

Finally,

$$r^2=\sin^2\frac{\alpha}{2} =\frac{1-\cos\alpha}{2} = \frac{1}{2}\left(1-\frac{1+\sqrt5}{4}\right) = \frac{3-\sqrt5}{8}.$$

Hence

$$r=\sqrt{\frac{3-\sqrt5}{8}}.$$

The configuration exists for $n=5$, since the preceding construction determines a regular pentagon on the small circle and all required angular distances equal $\alpha$.

Therefore

$$\boxed{\,n=5,\qquad r=\sqrt{\frac{3-\sqrt5}{8}}\, }.$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the tangency criterion. Let the two planes be at distance $h$ from the origin and make angle $\alpha$. In the cross-sectional plane through $O$, $A$, and $B$, the two planes become lines whose intersection is the trace of the common line of the planes. The distance from either center to this intersection equals $h\tan(\alpha/2)$. Tangency requires that this distance be exactly the radius $r$ of each circle. Substituting $h=\sqrt{1-r^2}$ gives

$$\tan(\alpha/2)=r/h,$$

hence

$$\sin(\alpha/2)=r.$$

Any omission of the cross-sectional computation would leave the central relation unjustified.

The second delicate step is deriving

$$\cos\alpha=\cos^2\alpha+\sin^2\alpha\cos\varphi.$$

The points $A_i$ lie on a parallel of the sphere of radius $h$. Writing explicit coordinates

$$h(\sin\alpha\cos t,\sin\alpha\sin t,\cos\alpha)$$

and taking a scalar product for parameters differing by $\varphi$ yields the formula directly. A common mistake is to treat the polygon as lying on a great circle, which would incorrectly give $\alpha=\varphi$.

The final delicate step is determining all $n$. From

$$\cos\alpha=\frac{\cos\varphi}{1-\cos\varphi}$$

and $0<\alpha<\pi$, one needs

$$0<\cos\varphi<\frac12.$$

Checking integers explicitly,

$$\cos\frac{2\pi}{4}=0,\qquad \cos\frac{2\pi}{5}\approx0.309,\qquad \cos\frac{2\pi}{6}=\frac12.$$

Hence only $n=5$ survives. Missing either endpoint condition would incorrectly admit $n=4$ or $n=6$.

## Alternative Approaches

A purely spherical-geometric approach avoids coordinates. The points $A_0,A_i,A_{i+1}$ form an isosceles spherical triangle with all three sides equal to $\alpha$. Applying the spherical law of cosines to this equilateral triangle yields the angle at $A_0$. Since the $n$ congruent triangles around $A_0$ fill a full angle $2\pi$, one obtains an equation relating $\alpha$ and $n$. Simplifying leads again to

$$\cos\alpha=\frac{\cos(2\pi/n)}{1-\cos(2\pi/n)}.$$

The coordinate method is preferable because it converts the problem immediately into the geometry of a regular polygon on a parallel of a sphere. The key relation emerges from a single scalar-product computation and the classification of admissible $n$ becomes transparent.
