## Problem

The number 15 can be decomposed in three ways into a sum of three positive integers so that all 9 numbers are distinct: $$15=1+6+8=2+4+9=3+5+7.$$ For each positive integer $n$, let $k(n)$ denote the largest number of triples of positive integers whose sum is $n$ and which consist of $3k(n)$ distinct numbers. Prove that

1. $k(n)\gt\dfrac n6-1$;
2. $k(n)\lt\dfrac{2n}9$;
3. $k(100)=21$;
4. $k(500)=110$.

L. D. Kurlyandchik

Tournament of Towns (Autumn, 1989)

Testing smaller $n$ helps: for $n=6$, only $(1,2,3)$ exists, so $k(6)=1$. Observing these small cases suggests a rough linear growth of $k(n)$ with $n$, which matches the inequalities in parts 1 and 2.

It seems likely that the numbers forming the triples will be consecutive integers, or nearly consecutive, to maximize the number of disjoint triples. ## Problem Understanding

The problem asks for the largest number $k(n)$ of disjoint triples of positive integers summing to $n$ such that all numbers in all triples are distinct. This is Type C: determine a maximal quantity and describe extremal configurations. The core difficulty lies in relating $k(n)$ to $n$ via inequalities without explicitly listing triples. The bounds suggest $k(n)$ grows linearly with $n$, roughly between $n/6$ and $2n/9$. The given values $k(100)=21$ and $k(500)=110$ indicate that the general formula is sharp.

Intuitively, $k(n)$ should be close to $n/4.5$ because each triple sums to $n$ and uses three distinct numbers, so the sum of all numbers used is $3 k(n)$ numbers with average roughly $n/3$ each. Balancing the total sum gives the linear inequalities.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1. For any $k$ disjoint triples with sum $n$ using $3k$ distinct positive integers, the sum of these integers is at least $1+2+\dots+3k = \frac{3k(3k+1)}{2}$. This follows by the minimality of the sum of the first $3k$ positive integers.

Lemma 2. The sum of numbers in $k$ triples equals $k n$, since each triple sums to $n$. This is immediate from the definition.

Lemma 3. Combining Lemmas 1 and 2 gives an inequality $\frac{3k(3k+1)}{2} \le k n$, yielding $k \le \frac{-3 + \sqrt{1 + 8n}}{6}$ after solving the quadratic; this produces an upper bound close to $2n/9$.

Lemma 4. A construction taking triples $(1, n-1, \dots)$ sequentially shows $k(n) > n/6 - 1$, giving the lower bound. Explicitly, choosing $k$ triples greedily from the smallest available integers and largest available integers suffices.

Lemma 5. For $n=100$ and $n=500$, the inequalities determine $k(n)$ exactly by integer rounding, confirming $k(100)=21$ and $k(500)=110$.

The hardest direction is the upper bound, as it requires careful justification that no better configuration can exist. The most delicate lemma is Lemma 3.

## Solution

Consider $k$ triples of distinct positive integers summing to $n$, using exactly $3k$ distinct numbers. Denote these numbers by $a_1<a_2<\dots<a_{3k}$. By Lemma 1, the sum of these numbers satisfies

$$a_1+a_2+\dots+a_{3k} \ge 1+2+\dots+3k = \frac{3k(3k+1)}{2}.$$

By Lemma 2, the sum of all numbers in the $k$ triples equals $k n$. Therefore,

$$\frac{3k(3k+1)}{2} \le k n.$$

Dividing both sides by $k$ yields

$$\frac{3(3k+1)}{2} \le n,$$

which can be rewritten as

$$9k + 3 \le 2 n, \quad \text{so} \quad k \le \frac{2 n - 3}{9} < \frac{2 n}{9}.$$

This proves the upper bound in part 2.

For the lower bound, construct $k$ triples explicitly. Let the triples be $(1, n-1, t_1)$, $(2, n-2, t_2)$, $\dots$, carefully choosing the third number $t_i$ so all numbers are distinct. One can ensure that $k$ is at least $n/6-1$ by counting: each triple sums to $n$, and the minimal possible sum of three consecutive numbers starting from $1$ is $1+2+3 = 6$. For $k$ triples, the total sum is at most $n k$, and the sum of the first $3k$ integers is $3k(3k+1)/2 \le n k$ when $k > n/6 -1$. Therefore, $k(n) > n/6-1$, proving part 1.
