## Problem

![Fig. 2](//www.kvant.digital/media/common/1970-03-045-02.png?_v=2)

Fig. 2

Some faces of a convex white polyhedron are painted black in such a way that no two black faces share an edge (Fig. 2). Prove that if at least one of the following conditions is satisfied:

1. the number of black faces is greater than half the total number of faces;
2. the total area of the black faces is greater than half the surface area of the polyhedron,

then no sphere can be inscribed in this polyhedron.

## Exploration

If a sphere is inscribed in a convex polyhedron, each face is tangent to the sphere. Let the tangency point on a face $F$ be $T_F$, and let $r$ be the radius of the sphere.

Take a vertex $V$ of the polyhedron. The faces meeting at $V$ form a polygonal cone tangent to the sphere. In the plane of any face containing $V$, the two edges issuing from $V$ are tangent lines from the same point $V$ to the circle obtained by intersecting that face with the sphere. Hence the tangent segments from $V$ to the tangency points on the adjacent faces are equal.

Denote this common length by $x_V$. Then every face acquires a decomposition. If a face has vertices $V_1,\dots,V_k$, the boundary of the face is divided into segments whose lengths are $x_{V_i}$ and $x_{V_{i+1}}$, so its perimeter equals

$$p(F)=2\sum_{i=1}^k x_{V_i}.$$

Summing over all faces, each vertex contributes to as many faces as meet there. If we sum only over black faces, no vertex can contribute more than half as many times as it contributes to all faces, because around every vertex two black faces cannot be adjacent in the cyclic order of faces. Thus the total perimeter of the black faces is at most half the total perimeter of all faces.

For a tangential polyhedron there is also an area formula analogous to the area formula for a tangential polygon:

$$A(F)=\frac r2\, p(F).$$

Indeed, the face can be triangulated by joining its tangency point to all vertices; each triangle has height $r$.

Hence area and perimeter are proportional for every face. Once the perimeter inequality is proved, the area inequality follows immediately.

The delicate point is the combinatorial statement at a vertex: among the faces incident with a vertex, at most half can be black. This must be justified carefully from the condition that no two black faces share an edge.

## Problem Understanding

We are given a convex polyhedron whose faces are colored black and white so that no two black faces share an edge. We must prove that if either the number of black faces exceeds half the total number of faces, or the total area of the black faces exceeds half the surface area of the polyhedron, then the polyhedron cannot possess an inscribed sphere.

This is a Type B problem. We must prove a stated implication.

The core difficulty is to derive a restriction on the distribution of black faces that holds for every polyhedron admitting an inscribed sphere. The restriction must simultaneously control both the number of black faces and their total area.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1. If a sphere is inscribed in the polyhedron, then for every vertex $V$ there exists a number $x_V$ such that on every face containing $V$, the tangent segment from $V$ to the face's point of tangency has length $x_V$; this follows from equality of tangent segments from an exterior point to a sphere.

Lemma 2. For every face $F$,

$$p(F)=2\sum_{V\in F}x_V.$$

This follows because each side of $F$ is the sum of the two tangent segments adjacent to its endpoints.

Lemma 3. Let $m_V$ be the number of faces incident with a vertex $V$, and let $b_V$ be the number of black faces incident with $V$. Then

$$b_V\le \frac{m_V}{2}.$$

Around $V$ the incident faces form a cycle; black faces cannot occupy adjacent positions on this cycle.

Lemma 4. If $P_B$ is the sum of the perimeters of the black faces and $P$ is the sum of the perimeters of all faces, then

$$P_B\le \frac P2.$$

This is obtained by summing the expressions from Lemma 2 and using Lemma 3 at each vertex.

Lemma 5. For every face $F$ tangent to the inscribed sphere of radius $r$,

$$A(F)=\frac r2\,p(F).$$

Triangulate the face by joining its tangency point to all vertices; every resulting triangle has height $r$.

The hardest direction is proving Lemma 4, because it requires translating the local restriction at each vertex into a global inequality.

The lemma most likely to fail under scrutiny is Lemma 3, since the cyclic arrangement of faces around a vertex must be used correctly.

## Solution

Assume that the polyhedron admits an inscribed sphere of radius $r$.

For each face $F$, let $T_F$ be its point of tangency with the sphere.

Fix a vertex $V$. Consider two faces containing $V$. In the plane of either face, the sphere cuts a circle tangent to the sides of the face. From the point $V$, the segments drawn to the tangency points on the two sides of that face are equal. Since all these tangent segments arise from the same point $V$ to the same sphere, their common length depends only on $V$. Denote it by $x_V$.

Let a face $F$ have consecutive vertices

$$V_1,V_2,\dots,V_k.$$

The side $V_iV_{i+1}$ is tangent to the sphere. Its tangency point divides the side into segments of lengths $x_{V_i}$ and $x_{V_{i+1}}$. Hence

$$|V_iV_{i+1}|=x_{V_i}+x_{V_{i+1}}.$$

Summing over all sides of $F$ gives

$$p(F) =\sum_{i=1}^k (x_{V_i}+x_{V_{i+1}}) =2\sum_{i=1}^k x_{V_i}.$$

Let $P_B$ be the sum of the perimeters of all black faces and $P$ the sum of the perimeters of all faces.

For a vertex $V$, let $m_V$ be the number of incident faces and $b_V$ the number of incident black faces. The faces incident with $V$ occur in a cyclic order around $V$. Two consecutive faces in this cycle share an edge through $V$. Since no two black faces share an edge, no two black faces are consecutive in the cycle. In a cycle of length $m_V$, a subset with no adjacent elements contains at most $m_V/2$ elements. Therefore

$$b_V\le \frac{m_V}{2}.$$

Using the perimeter formula,

$$P_B =2\sum_V b_Vx_V,$$

while

$$P =2\sum_V m_Vx_V.$$

Since $b_V\le m_V/2$ for every vertex,

$$P_B =2\sum_V b_Vx_V \le 2\sum_V \frac{m_V}{2}x_V =\sum_V m_Vx_V =\frac P2.$$

Now let $A(F)$ denote the area of a face $F$. Join $T_F$ to all vertices of $F$. This decomposes $F$ into triangles. In each such triangle the altitude from $T_F$ to the corresponding side equals the distance from the center of the inscribed sphere to the plane of $F$, namely $r$. Consequently,

$$A(F) =\frac r2\,p(F).$$

Summing over all black faces and over all faces gives

$$A_B=\frac r2\,P_B, \qquad A=\frac r2\,P.$$

Since $P_B\le P/2$,

$$A_B\le \frac r2\cdot \frac P2=\frac A2.$$

We have proved two inequalities for every polyhedron with an inscribed sphere:

$$P_B\le \frac P2, \qquad A_B\le \frac A2.$$

To obtain the statement about the number of faces, apply the same counting argument with all weights $x_V$ replaced by $1$. Let $N_B$ be the number of black faces and $N$ the total number of faces. Then

$$N_B=\sum_V \frac{b_V}{d_V}, \qquad N=\sum_V \frac{m_V}{d_V},$$

where $d_V=m_V$ is the number of faces incident with $V$. Using $b_V\le m_V/2$ at every vertex yields

$$N_B\le \frac N2.$$

Thus every polyhedron possessing an inscribed sphere satisfies

$$N_B\le \frac N2 \quad\text{and}\quad A_B\le \frac A2.$$

Hence if either the number of black faces exceeds half the total number of faces, or the area of the black faces exceeds half the surface area, the polyhedron cannot have an inscribed sphere.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the inequality $b_V\le m_V/2$. Around a vertex the incident faces form a cycle, not merely a set. Consecutive faces in that cycle share an edge through the vertex. Since two black faces are forbidden to share an edge, black faces correspond to vertices of a cycle with no adjacent selections. A cycle of length $m_V$ contains at most $\lfloor m_V/2\rfloor$ such vertices. Forgetting the cyclic structure would leave the inequality unjustified.

The second delicate step is the perimeter identity

$$p(F)=2\sum_{V\in F}x_V.$$

Each side contributes exactly two tangent segments, one from each endpoint. If a side between $V_i$ and $V_{i+1}$ were counted only once, the factor $2$ would disappear and the final inequality would be incorrect.

The third delicate step is the area formula

$$A(F)=\frac r2\,p(F).$$

Every triangle in the decomposition with apex at the tangency point has height $r$, because the tangency point lies in the face plane and the radius to that point is perpendicular to the face. Using any other height would destroy the proportionality between area and perimeter.

## Alternative Approaches

A different proof of the area statement uses polar duality. If an inscribed sphere exists, one may normalize it to the unit sphere. Each face corresponds to a tangent plane, hence to a point of the dual polyhedron on the sphere. The condition that no two black faces share an edge translates into an independent set in the dual graph. Around every vertex of the dual graph the independent set occupies at most half the available positions, leading directly to a weighted inequality for face areas.

The approach used above is preferable because it remains entirely elementary. It relies only on equal tangent lengths from a vertex to a sphere, a simple combinatorial observation around each vertex, and the standard formula $A=\frac r2 p$ for a tangential polygon. No duality theory is required.
