## Problem

A circle, its diameter $AB$, and a point $C$ on that diameter are given. Construct two points $X$ and $Y$ on the circle, symmetric with respect to the diameter $AB$, such that the line $YC$ is perpendicular to the line $XA$.

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad (1970, Grade 8)

## Exploration

Let the given circle have center $O$, with diameter $AB$. Since $X$ and $Y$ are symmetric with respect to the diameter $AB$, the chord $XY$ is perpendicular to $AB$ and its midpoint lies on $AB$.

Introduce coordinates. Let the circle be the unit circle, with

$$A=(-1,0),\qquad B=(1,0),$$

and let

$$C=(c,0),$$

where $-1<c<1$.

A point $X$ on the circle has coordinates

$$X=(u,v),\qquad u^2+v^2=1,$$

and then

$$Y=(u,-v).$$

The condition is that $YC\perp XA$. The slope of $XA$ equals

$$\frac{v}{u+1},$$

while the slope of $YC$ equals

$$\frac{-v}{u-c}.$$

Perpendicularity gives

$$\frac{v}{u+1}\cdot\frac{-v}{u-c}=-1.$$

Since $v^2=1-u^2$,

$$\frac{-(1-u^2)}{(u+1)(u-c)}=-1.$$

After simplification,

$$1-u^2=(u+1)(u-c).$$

Expanding,

$$1-u^2=u^2+(1-c)u-c,$$

hence

$$2u^2+(1-c)u-(1+c)=0.$$

Trying to factor,

$$2u^2+(1-c)u-(1+c) =(u+1)(2u-(1+c)).$$

Thus

$$u=-1 \quad\text{or}\quad u=\frac{1+c}{2}.$$

The root $u=-1$ corresponds to $X=A$, which makes the line $XA$ undefined and is a degenerate solution. The nondegenerate solution is

$$u=\frac{1+c}{2}.$$

This suggests the key geometric fact: the projection of $X$ onto $AB$ must be the point $D$ on $AB$ satisfying

$$AD=\frac{AC}{2}.$$

Indeed, in our coordinates

$$AD=u+1=\frac{3+c}{2}, \qquad AC=1+c.$$

Hence

$$AD=\frac{AB+AC}{2}.$$

Since $AB=2$ in the normalized coordinates, this is exactly the coordinate description.

The most delicate step is converting the perpendicularity condition into a geometric relation on the foot of the perpendicular from $X$ to $AB$, and then showing that this relation determines $X$ uniquely.

## Problem Understanding

We are given a circle with diameter $AB$ and a point $C$ on the diameter. We must construct points $X$ and $Y$ on the circle such that $Y$ is the reflection of $X$ across the line $AB$ and the line $YC$ is perpendicular to the line $XA$.

This is a Type D problem, a construction problem.

The difficulty is to translate the condition

$$YC\perp XA$$

into a relation involving only points on the diameter $AB$, because such relations are directly constructible.

The answer will be obtained by first locating the projection of $X$ onto $AB$. That projection turns out to be a point $D$ on $AB$ satisfying

$$AD=\frac{AB+AC}{2}.$$

Once $D$ is known, the perpendicular through $D$ meets the circle in the required symmetric pair $X,Y$.

## Proof Architecture

Let $D$ be the foot of the perpendicular from $X$ to $AB$; since $Y$ is the reflection of $X$ across $AB$, $D$ is also the midpoint of $XY$.

Lemma 1. The triangles $XAD$ and $YCD$ are right triangles sharing the leg $XD=YD$; the condition $XA\perp YC$ is equivalent to $AD\cdot CD=XD^2$.

The proof uses slopes in right triangles: the product of the slopes of $XA$ and $YC$ equals $-1$ precisely when the ratio identities reduce to $AD\cdot CD=XD^2$.

Lemma 2. Since $X$ lies on the circle with diameter $AB$, one has $XD^2=AD\cdot DB$.

This is the standard altitude theorem in the right triangle $AXB$.

Lemma 3. Combining the previous two relations yields $CD=DB$.

Substituting $XD^2=AD\cdot DB$ into $AD\cdot CD=XD^2$ and cancelling $AD$ gives the claim.

Lemma 4. The condition $CD=DB$ determines $D$ uniquely on the diameter and is equivalent to

$$AD=\frac{AB+AC}{2}.$$

This follows because $D$ is the midpoint of the segment $CB$.

The hardest step is Lemma 1, where the perpendicularity condition must be translated correctly into the product relation.

## Solution

Let $D$ be the foot of the perpendicular from $X$ to the diameter $AB$. Since $Y$ is the reflection of $X$ across $AB$, the point $D$ is also the foot of the perpendicular from $Y$ to $AB$, and

$$XD=YD.$$

Consider the right triangles $XAD$ and $YCD$. Their legs along the diameter are $AD$ and $CD$, and their vertical legs are $XD$ and $YD$.

The slope of the line $XA$ has absolute value

$$\frac{XD}{AD},$$

and the slope of the line $YC$ has absolute value

$$\frac{YD}{CD}.$$

Since the lines are perpendicular,

$$\frac{XD}{AD}\cdot\frac{YD}{CD}=1.$$

Using $XD=YD$, we obtain

$$AD\cdot CD=XD^2. \tag{1}$$

Next, since $AB$ is a diameter and $X$ lies on the circle, the angle

$$\angle AXB$$

is a right angle. The segment $XD$ is the altitude from the right angle to the hypotenuse $AB$. The altitude theorem for a right triangle gives

$$XD^2=AD\cdot DB. \tag{2}$$

Comparing (1) and (2),

$$AD\cdot CD=AD\cdot DB.$$

Because $AD\neq0$, it follows that

$$CD=DB.$$

Thus $D$ is the midpoint of the segment $CB$.

This observation immediately yields a construction.

Construct the midpoint $D$ of the segment $CB$. Through $D$ draw the line perpendicular to $AB$. Let this line meet the given circle at two points $X$ and $Y$.

The points $X$ and $Y$ are symmetric with respect to $AB$, because the perpendicular through $D$ intersects the circle in a chord whose midpoint lies on the diameter $AB$.

It remains to verify the perpendicularity condition. Since $D$ is the midpoint of $CB$,

$$CD=DB.$$

Applying the altitude theorem in the right triangle $AXB$,

$$XD^2=AD\cdot DB.$$

Replacing $DB$ by $CD$ gives

$$XD^2=AD\cdot CD.$$

Since $YD=XD$,

$$XD\cdot YD=AD\cdot CD.$$

Hence

$$\frac{XD}{AD}\cdot\frac{YD}{CD}=1.$$

The slopes of $XA$ and $YC$ are therefore negative reciprocals, so

$$XA\perp YC.$$

The required construction is:

$$\boxed{\text{Take }D\text{ to be the midpoint of }CB,\text{ and let }X,Y \text{ be the intersections of the perpendicular through }D\text{ to }AB \text{ with the circle.}}$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate point is the passage from perpendicularity to

$$AD\cdot CD=XD^2.$$

Choose coordinates with $AB$ as the $x$-axis and $D=(0,0)$. Then

$$X=(0,h),\qquad Y=(0,-h),\qquad A=(-a,0),\qquad C=(c,0).$$

The slopes are

$$m_{XA}=\frac{h}{a}, \qquad m_{YC}=\frac{h}{c}.$$

One line rises while the other falls, so perpendicularity is equivalent to

$$\frac{h^2}{ac}=1,$$

that is,

$$h^2=ac.$$

Returning to geometric notation yields

$$XD^2=AD\cdot CD.$$

The second delicate point is the use of the altitude theorem. In the right triangle $AXB$, the altitude from $X$ meets the hypotenuse $AB$ at $D$. Similarity of triangles $XAD$, $XDB$, and $AXB$ gives

$$\frac{AD}{XD}=\frac{XD}{DB},$$

hence

$$XD^2=AD\cdot DB.$$

No additional assumptions are involved.

The final verification is the converse. Starting from the constructed midpoint $D$ of $CB$, the equality $CD=DB$ combines with the altitude theorem to produce

$$XD^2=AD\cdot CD.$$

Reversing the first argument yields $XA\perp YC$. Thus the construction indeed satisfies all requirements.

## Alternative Approaches

A coordinate solution can be completed directly. Place the circle as

$$x^2+y^2=1,$$

with

$$A=(-1,0),\quad B=(1,0),\quad C=(c,0).$$

Writing

$$X=(u,v),\qquad Y=(u,-v),$$

the condition $YC\perp XA$ leads to

$$(u+1)(u-c)=1-u^2.$$

Factoring gives

$$(u+1)\bigl(2u-(1+c)\bigr)=0.$$

Discarding the degenerate root $u=-1$, one obtains

$$u=\frac{1+c}{2}.$$

This means that the projection $D$ of $X$ onto $AB$ is the midpoint of $CB$, which is exactly the construction found above.

The synthetic proof is preferable because every step corresponds to a ruler-and-compass operation and the midpoint of $CB$ emerges naturally from the geometry of the circle and the altitude theorem.
