## Problem

Given a circle, its diameter $AB$ and a point $C$ on this diameter. Construct on the circle two points $X$ and $Y$, symmetric with respect to the diameter $AB$, for which the line $YC$ is perpendicular to the line $XA$.

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad (1970, 8th grade)

## Exploration

Consider a circle with diameter $AB$ and a point $C$ on $AB$. The goal is to find points $X$ and $Y$ on the circle, symmetric with respect to $AB$, such that $YC \perp XA$. Reflecting a point across a diameter preserves the distance to the line perpendicular to the diameter and reverses vertical orientation. Let the circle have center $O$ and radius $R$, and place $AB$ along the $x$-axis with $A=(-R,0)$, $B=(R,0)$, and $C=(c,0)$. Then a generic point $X$ on the circle has coordinates $(x, \sqrt{R^2 - x^2})$, and its reflection $Y$ across $AB$ is $(x, -\sqrt{R^2 - x^2})$. The condition $YC \perp XA$ translates to the product of slopes of lines $YC$ and $XA$ being $-1$. Explicitly computing slopes yields a cubic equation in $x$ after simplification. Testing a few simple cases, such as $C$ at the midpoint $O$ or near $A$, suggests that there always exists a solution, and the points $X$ and $Y$ are uniquely determined except in degenerate cases when $C$ coincides with $A$ or $B$. The crucial point is ensuring that the cubic equation has a real root corresponding to a point on the semicircle above $AB$, since the reflection guarantees $Y$ lies on the opposite side.

## Problem Understanding

The problem asks to construct points $X$ and $Y$ on a circle with diameter $AB$, symmetric with respect to $AB$, such that the line through $Y$ and $C$ is perpendicular to the line through $X$ and $A$. This is a Type D problem, an existence and explicit construction problem. The core difficulty lies in translating the geometric perpendicularity and symmetry constraints into a concrete method for locating $X$ and $Y$ on the circle without relying on guesswork. Intuitively, the reflection symmetry across $AB$ simplifies the problem because once $X$ is chosen, $Y$ is determined, reducing the degrees of freedom to one. The perpendicularity condition then becomes a linear relation on the slope of $XA$, intersected with the circle, guaranteeing a unique point.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1: For a point $X$ on a circle with diameter $AB$, its reflection $Y$ across $AB$ has the same $x$-coordinate and opposite $y$-coordinate. Proof: coordinate reflection across a horizontal line changes only the vertical coordinate.

Lemma 2: Two lines $YC$ and $XA$ are perpendicular if and only if the product of their slopes equals $-1$. Proof: follows from the analytic geometry definition of perpendicular lines.

Lemma 3: For $C$ on $AB$, there exists a point $X$ on the circle such that the slopes of $XA$ and $YC$ satisfy $m_{XA} m_{YC}=-1$. Proof sketch: setting $X=(x, \sqrt{R^2-x^2})$, $Y=(x, -\sqrt{R^2-x^2})$, writing the slope condition leads to a cubic in $x$, which always has a real root since the function is continuous and changes sign at the endpoints $x=-R$ and $x=R$.

Lemma 4: Once $X$ is found, reflecting across $AB$ gives $Y$, and the perpendicularity condition is satisfied automatically by construction. Proof: algebraically follows from Lemma 2.

The hardest part is Lemma 3, ensuring that the cubic equation has a real solution corresponding to a point on the circle. It requires careful analysis of the endpoints and monotonicity of the function.

## Solution

Place the circle in a coordinate plane with $A=(-R,0)$, $B=(R,0)$, and let $C=(c,0)$ with $-R<c<R$. Let $X=(x, y)$ be a point on the circle above $AB$, so $y>0$ and $x^2 + y^2 = R^2$. Its reflection across $AB$ is $Y=(x, -y)$. The slope of $XA$ is $\dfrac{y-0}{x+R} = \dfrac{y}{x+R}$, and the slope of $YC$ is $\dfrac{-y-0}{x-c} = \dfrac{-y}{x-c}$. Setting their product equal to $-1$ yields

$\frac{y}{x+R} \cdot \frac{-y}{x-c} = -1.$

Simplifying gives

$\frac{-y^2}{(x+R)(x-c)} = -1 \implies y^2 = (x+R)(x-c).$

Substituting $y^2 = R^2 - x^2$, we obtain

$R^2 - x^2 = (x+R)(x-c) \implies R^2 - x^2 = x^2 + (R-c)x - Rc \implies -2x^2 - (R-c)x + R^2 + Rc = 0.$

Multiplying both sides by $-1$ yields

$2x^2 + (R-c)x - R(R+c) = 0.$

$2x^2 + (R-c)x - R(R+c) = 0.$
