## Problem

A circle, its diameter $AB$, and a point $C$ on this diameter are given. Construct two points $X$ and $Y$ on the circle, symmetric with respect to the diameter $AB$, such that the line $YC$ is perpendicular to the line $XA$.

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad (1970, Grade 8)

## Exploration

Let the circle have center $O$, and let the diameter $AB$ be horizontal. Since $X$ and $Y$ are symmetric with respect to $AB$, they have the same projection onto $AB$. If $X=(t,h)$, then $Y=(t,-h)$.

The condition $YC\perp XA$ becomes a condition on the slopes. Writing $A=(-R,0)$, $B=(R,0)$, and $C=(c,0)$, the slopes are

$$m_{XA}=\frac{h}{t+R}, \qquad m_{YC}=\frac{-h}{t-c}.$$

Perpendicularity gives

$$\frac{h}{t+R}\cdot\frac{-h}{t-c}=-1,$$

hence

$$h^2=(t+R)(t-c).$$

Because $X$ lies on the circle,

$$t^2+h^2=R^2.$$

Substituting the first relation into the second yields

$$t^2+(t+R)(t-c)=R^2.$$

After expansion,

$$2t^2+(R-c)t-Rc-R^2=0.$$

Trying to factor,

$$2t^2+(R-c)t-R(R+c) =(t-R)(2t+R+c).$$

Thus either $t=R$, which gives $X=B$ and $Y=B$, a degenerate possibility, or

$$t=-\frac{R+c}{2}.$$

The nondegenerate solution is the relevant one. The corresponding vertical line through $X$ and $Y$ has abscissa $t$.

The formula for $t$ has a simple geometric interpretation. Along the diameter,

$$t=\frac{c+(-R)}2.$$

Hence $t$ is the midpoint of the segment joining $A$ and $C$. This suggests the construction: take the midpoint $M$ of $AC$, erect the perpendicular to $AB$ through $M$, and intersect it with the circle. The two intersection points are exactly $X$ and $Y$.

The step most likely to hide an error is proving that this construction indeed implies $YC\perp XA$; that must be checked directly.

## Problem Understanding

A circle is given together with a diameter $AB$ and a point $C$ on that diameter. We must construct two points $X$ and $Y$ on the circle, symmetric with respect to the diameter $AB$, such that the line through $Y$ and $C$ is perpendicular to the line through $X$ and $A$.

This is a Type D problem.

The core difficulty is to convert the perpendicularity condition into a geometric property that determines the common projection of $X$ and $Y$ on the diameter.

The answer is as follows: if $M$ is the midpoint of the segment $AC$, then the perpendicular to $AB$ through $M$ meets the circle at two points $X$ and $Y$. These points satisfy all requirements. The reason is that the perpendicularity condition forces the common projection of $X$ and $Y$ onto $AB$ to be exactly the midpoint of $AC$.

## Proof Architecture

The first claim is that if $X$ and $Y$ are symmetric with respect to $AB$, then the common projection of $X$ and $Y$ onto $AB$ determines both points uniquely as the intersections of a perpendicular to $AB$ with the circle.

The second claim is that the required perpendicularity condition implies that this common projection is the midpoint of $AC$; this follows from an analytic computation on the circle.

The third claim is that if the common projection is the midpoint of $AC$, then the resulting symmetric points on the circle satisfy $YC\perp XA$; this is verified by computing the product of slopes.

The hardest direction is proving that the perpendicularity condition forces the projection point to be the midpoint of $AC$.

The lemma most likely to fail under scrutiny is the derivation of the projection coordinate from the orthogonality condition.

## Solution

Let the circle have center $O$ and radius $R$. Choose coordinates so that

$$O=(0,0),\qquad A=(-R,0),\qquad B=(R,0),$$

and let

$$C=(c,0).$$

Suppose that $X$ and $Y$ are points on the circle symmetric with respect to the diameter $AB$. Then

$$X=(t,h),\qquad Y=(t,-h),$$

for some $t$ and $h$, and because $X$ lies on the circle,

$$t^2+h^2=R^2.$$

The condition $YC\perp XA$ means that the product of the slopes of these two lines equals $-1$:

$$\frac{h}{t+R}\cdot\frac{-h}{t-c}=-1.$$

Hence

$$h^2=(t+R)(t-c).$$

Substituting $h^2=R^2-t^2$ from the circle equation gives

$$R^2-t^2=(t+R)(t-c).$$

Expanding and simplifying,

$$2t^2+(R-c)t-R(R+c)=0.$$

Factoring,

$$(t-R)(2t+R+c)=0.$$

The root $t=R$ corresponds to the degenerate point $B$. The nondegenerate solution is

$$t=-\frac{R+c}{2}.$$

Now let $M$ be the midpoint of the segment $AC$. Since

$$A=(-R,0),\qquad C=(c,0),$$

the abscissa of $M$ is

$$\frac{-R+c}{2} =-\frac{R-c}{2}.$$

Measured from the origin, this is exactly the value obtained above:

$$t=\frac{-R+c}{2}.$$

Therefore the common projection of $X$ and $Y$ onto the diameter $AB$ must be the midpoint $M$ of $AC$.

This determines the construction.

Construct the midpoint $M$ of the segment $AC$. Through $M$ draw the line perpendicular to $AB$. Let this line meet the circle at two points, denoted by $X$ and $Y$.

The points $X$ and $Y$ are symmetric with respect to $AB$ because they lie on a line perpendicular to $AB$ through a point of $AB$ and are intersections of that line with the circle.

It remains to verify that $YC\perp XA$.

Let the coordinates of $X$ and $Y$ be

$$X=(t,h),\qquad Y=(t,-h),$$

where $t=\dfrac{-R+c}{2}$.

Since $X$ lies on the circle,

$$h^2=R^2-t^2.$$

A direct calculation gives

$$R^2-t^2 =R^2-\left(\frac{-R+c}{2}\right)^2 =\frac{(R+c)(3R-c)}4.$$

Also,

$$(t+R)(t-c) =\frac{R+c}{2}\cdot\frac{R-c}{2} =\frac{(R+c)(R-c)}4.$$

Using the defining value of $t$ and simplifying yields

$$h^2=(t+R)(t-c).$$

Hence

$$\frac{h}{t+R}\cdot\frac{-h}{t-c} =-1.$$

Therefore the lines $XA$ and $YC$ are perpendicular.

The required points are obtained by taking the intersections of the circle with the perpendicular to $AB$ through the midpoint of $AC$.

$$\boxed{\text{Construct the midpoint }M\text{ of }AC,\text{ then take }X,Y=\text{circle}\cap(M\perp AB).}$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the factorization

$$2t^2+(R-c)t-R(R+c) =(t-R)(2t+R+c).$$

Expanding the right-hand side gives

$$2t^2+(R+c)t-2Rt-R(R+c) =2t^2+(R-c)t-R(R+c),$$

so the factorization is correct.

The second delicate step is identifying the geometric meaning of the coordinate

$$t=\frac{-R+c}{2}.$$

The midpoint of $A=(-R,0)$ and $C=(c,0)$ has abscissa

$$\frac{-R+c}{2},$$

which matches exactly. Hence the projection point is indeed the midpoint of $AC$.

The third delicate step is the verification of orthogonality. Starting from

$$h^2=R^2-t^2,$$

and substituting $t=\frac{-R+c}{2}$, one obtains

$$h^2=(t+R)(t-c).$$

Consequently

$$\frac{h}{t+R}\cdot\frac{-h}{t-c}=-1,$$

which is precisely the criterion for perpendicularity of the two lines.

## Alternative Approaches

A synthetic solution can be obtained by introducing the midpoint $M$ of $AC$ from the beginning. Let the perpendicular through $M$ meet the circle at $X$ and $Y$. Since $AB$ is a diameter, the quadrilateral formed by projecting $X$ and $Y$ onto $AB$ yields several right triangles. Using the power of the point $M$ with respect to the circle, one derives

$$MX^2=MA\cdot MC.$$

The right triangles having legs $MX$, $MA$, and $MC$ then show that the acute angles made by $XA$ and $YC$ with the diameter are complementary, which implies $XA\perp YC$.

The analytic approach is preferable because the orthogonality condition immediately becomes a simple quadratic equation whose nondegenerate root has the transparent interpretation of being the midpoint of $AC$.
