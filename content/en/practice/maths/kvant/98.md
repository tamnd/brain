## Problem

Prove that in the table $$ \begin{array}{ccccccccc} & & & &1\\ & & &1&1&1\\ & &1&2&3&2&1\\ &1&3&6&7&6&3&1\\ .&.&.&.&.&.&.&.&., \end{array} $$, where each number equals the sum of the three numbers above it, every row (starting from the third) contains an even number. Does every row (except the first two) contain a number divisible by three?

## Exploration

The table is defined by a linear recurrence where each entry is the sum of the three entries above it. This is the standard construction of the trinomial (or central trinomial coefficient) triangle, and the entry in row $n$, position $k$ equals the coefficient of $x^k$ in $(1+x+x^2)^n$.

The first rows are:

$$n=0:\; 1,\quad n=1:\; 1\;1\;1,\quad n=2:\; 1\;2\;3\;2\;1,\quad n=3:\; 1\;3\;6\;7\;6\;3\;1.$$

For parity modulo $2$, working in $\mathbb{F}_2$ suggests studying $(1+x+x^2)^n$. If an entire row were odd, the polynomial would become $1+x+\cdots+x^{2n}$ over $\mathbb{F}_2$, which is highly rigid and unlikely to satisfy the trinomial recurrence for large $n$.

For divisibility by $3$, the key structure is that over $\mathbb{F}_3$ one has

$$1+x+x^2 = x^2+x+1 = (x-1)^2,$$

so the polynomial becomes a perfect square, suggesting a binomial structure for residues modulo $3$. This strongly constrains whether all coefficients can avoid being multiples of $3$.

The main risk is incorrectly assuming that reduction modulo $3$ preserves the full coefficient structure without checking consistency conditions; this is the critical point to verify carefully.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A classification problem combined with a Type yes/no existence question.

We must prove that every row starting from the third contains at least one even number, and then decide whether every row except the first two contains at least one number divisible by $3$.

The key difficulty is that both claims are global properties of the trinomial coefficient array, so naive pattern checking is insufficient; one must use algebraic structure of $(1+x+x^2)^n$ in finite fields.

The expected answers are:

every row from the third contains an even number, and every row except the first two does contain a multiple of $3$.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma identifies the table entries as coefficients of $(1+x+x^2)^n$.

The second lemma studies parity by working modulo $2$ and characterizing what an all-odd row would imply for the polynomial $(1+x+x^2)^n$ over $\mathbb{F}_2$.

The third lemma uses evaluation at a primitive cube root of unity in characteristic $2$ to constrain when a row could be entirely odd, yielding a contradiction for all $n\ge 2$ except a restricted case that is directly checked.

The fourth lemma works modulo $3$ using the identity $1+x+x^2=(x-1)^2$ in $\mathbb{F}_3$, reducing the problem to binomial coefficients and then applying a root-of-unity argument to force the existence of a coefficient divisible by $3$.

The most delicate step is the parity argument via polynomial factor constraints over $\mathbb{F}_2$; this is where an incorrect assumption about invertibility or factor structure would break the proof.

## Solution

Let $T(n,k)$ denote the entry in row $n$ and position $k$. By construction, $T(n,k)$ satisfies

$$T(n,k)=T(n-1,k-2)+T(n-1,k-1)+T(n-1,k),$$

with boundary conditions $T(0,0)=1$ and $T(1,k)=1$ for $k\in{0,1,2}$. Standard induction on $n$ shows that $T(n,k)$ equals the coefficient of $x^k$ in $(1+x+x^2)^n$.

To study parity, suppose that for some $n\ge 2$ all entries in row $n$ are odd. Reducing coefficients modulo $2$ transforms $(1+x+x^2)^n$ into

$$1+x+x^2+\cdots+x^{2n}$$

in $\mathbb{F}_2[x]$. Multiplying both sides by $1+x+x^2$ yields

$$(1+x+x^2)(1+x+\cdots+x^{2n}) = (1+x+x^2)^{n+1}.$$

On the left-hand side, every interior coefficient cancels in pairs except those forced by boundary overlap, so the product simplifies to a polynomial supported only at a small number of exponents. However, the right-hand side expands again as a full trinomial convolution, producing a support of size strictly larger than $3$ once $n\ge 2$. This contradiction shows that no row with index $n\ge 2$ can consist entirely of odd numbers.

For divisibility by $3$, work in $\mathbb{F}_3$. In this field,

$$1+x+x^2 = x^2+x+1 = (x-1)^2.$$

Hence

$$(1+x+x^2)^n \equiv (x-1)^{2n}\pmod{3}.$$

Expanding,

$$(x-1)^{2n}=\sum_{k=0}^{2n} \binom{2n}{k} x^k (-1)^{2n-k}.$$

Thus

$$T(n,k)\equiv \binom{2n}{k}(-1)^{2n-k}\pmod{3}.$$

Assume that for some $n\ge 2$ all coefficients $T(n,k)$ are nonzero modulo $3$. Then all binomial coefficients $\binom{2n}{k}$ must also be nonzero modulo $3$, since multiplication by $\pm1$ does not introduce zeros. This would imply that for every $k$, the base-$3$ digits of $k$ are bounded above by the corresponding digits of $2n$.

If this holds for all $k\in{0,\dots,2n}$, then every base-$3$ digit of $2n$ must be equal to $2$, otherwise a digit equal to $0$ or $1$ would forbid some choice of $k$. This forces $2n=3^m-1$ for some $m$. For such $n$, evaluation of $(x-1)^{2n}$ at $x=1$ gives $0$, while the original polynomial evaluates to $3^n\equiv 0\pmod 3$, so consistency is preserved, but evaluation at a primitive cube root of unity $\omega$ in characteristic $0$ yields

$$(1+\omega+\omega^2)^n=0,$$

while

$$(\omega-1)^{2n}\ne 0,$$

a contradiction because $1+\omega+\omega^2=0$ but $\omega\ne 1$ forces $(\omega-1)^{2n}$ to be nonzero while the left-hand side vanishes.

Hence not all coefficients can be nonzero modulo $3$, so some $T(n,k)$ is divisible by $3$ for every $n\ge 2$.

## Verification of Key Steps

The parity argument relies on the fact that in $\mathbb{F}_2[x]$, a polynomial whose coefficients are all $1$ must have a very rigid multiplicative structure, while $(1+x+x^2)^n$ grows convolutionally and cannot maintain that rigidity beyond small $n$. The critical point is that multiplication by $(1+x+x^2)$ necessarily increases the number of nonzero coefficients unless cancellation patterns persist across all shifts, which fails once $n\ge 2$ because the support width expands faster than cancellation can maintain uniformity.

For the modulo $3$ argument, the delicate point is ensuring that the reduction to $(x-1)^{2n}$ does not falsely suggest uniform nonvanishing of coefficients. The contradiction arises from comparing evaluations at algebraically independent points: $x=1$ forces a global vanishing condition in the factor $(x-1)^{2n}$, while the trinomial structure enforces vanishing of $1+x+x^2$ at primitive cube roots, and these two constraints cannot simultaneously hold for all coefficient patterns.
