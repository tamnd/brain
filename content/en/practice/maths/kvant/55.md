## Problem

All natural numbers whose decimal representation has no more than $n$ digits are divided into two groups. The first group consists of all numbers with an odd sum of digits, and the second group — of all numbers with an even sum of digits. Prove that if $1\le k\lt n$, then the sum of $k$-th powers of all numbers in the first group equals the sum of $k$-th powers of all numbers in the second group.

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad (1970, 10th grade)

## Exploration

For $n=1$, the numbers are $0,1,\dots,9$. The numbers with even digit sum are

$0,2,4,6,8,$

and the numbers with odd digit sum are

$1,3,5,7,9.$

Their sums are both equal to $20$. Since the statement only concerns $k<n$, there is no nontrivial case here.

For $n=2$ and $k=1$, we must compare the sums of all two-digit-or-fewer numbers according to the parity of the digit sum. Writing numbers from $00$ to $99$, every number $10a+b$ has digit-sum parity opposite to that of $10a+(9-b)$, because

$a+b+a+(9-b)=2a+9$

is odd. Moreover,

$\bigl(10a+b\bigr)+\bigl(10a+9-b\bigr)=20a+9.$

Summing over all $b$ for fixed $a$, the odd and even classes balance perfectly. This suggests pairing numbers whose last digit is replaced by its complement to $9$.

The problem asks for equality of sums of $k$-th powers for every $k<n$. Pairing alone is insufficient for powers larger than $1$, because

$x^k-(x')^k$

## Problem Understanding

We consider all natural numbers having at most $n$ decimal digits, equivalently all integers from $0$ to $10^n-1$. They are divided into two classes according to whether the sum of their decimal digits is odd or even. The problem asks us to prove that for every integer $k$ satisfying $1\le k<n$, the sum of the $k$-th powers of the numbers in the odd class equals the corresponding sum for the even class.

This is a Type B problem. We must prove the stated identity.

The core difficulty is to encode the parity of the digit sum in a form compatible with powers of integers. Direct combinatorial pairing works poorly for higher powers. The decisive idea is to introduce the sign

$(-1)^{s(m)},$

where $s(m)$ is the digit sum, and then use a generating function that factorizes over decimal digits.

## Proof Architecture

Define

$\varepsilon(m)=(-1)^{s(m)},$

so that the desired equality becomes

$\sum_{m=0}^{10^n-1}\varepsilon(m)m^k=0.$

This reformulates the problem as a single signed sum.

Construct the exponential generating function

$F(t)=\sum_{m=0}^{10^n-1}\varepsilon(m)e^{mt}.$

The coefficient of $t^k$ in its Taylor expansion is

$\frac1{k!}\sum\varepsilon(m)m^k.$

Expand each integer in decimal notation,

$m=a_0+10a_1+\cdots+10^{n-1}a_{n-1},$

and use additivity of digit sums to factorize $F(t)$ into a product of $n$ factors.

Show that each factor vanishes at $t=0$. Since the product has $n$ such factors, $F(t)$ has a zero of order at least $n$ at the origin.

Deduce that the coefficients of $t^k$ for $k<n$ vanish, giving the desired identity.

The most delicate step is the factorization of $F(t)$ and the deduction about the order of vanishing at $t=0$.
