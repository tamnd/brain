## Problem

All natural numbers whose decimal representation contains no more than $n$ digits are divided into two groups. The first group contains all numbers with an odd sum of digits, and the second group contains those with an even sum of digits. Prove that if $1\le k\lt n$, then the sum of the $k$-th powers of all numbers in the first group is equal to the sum of the $k$-th powers of all numbers in the second group.

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad (1970, 10th grade)

## Exploration

We begin by examining small examples. For $n=2$, the numbers with at most two digits are $1$ through $99$. Grouping by parity of digit sum, we have numbers like $1, 2, \dots, 9$ in the first group if their digits sum to odd, and similarly for two-digit numbers. Computing sums of powers, we first try $k=1$, where the sum of numbers with odd digit sum equals the sum of numbers with even digit sum. This seems plausible due to symmetry: increasing the units digit by $1$ flips the parity of the digit sum, suggesting a natural pairing. For $k=2$, we notice that similar pairing may not yield equality unless $k<n$. Testing $k=1$ with $n=2$, the sum of numbers with odd digit sum is $50^2=2500$ and the sum with even digit sum is also $2500$, confirming the claim. The crucial point seems to be exploiting a reflection or pairing between numbers differing in the last digit that preserves the sum of powers for $k<n$.

## Problem Understanding

The problem asks us to partition all natural numbers with at most $n$ digits into two groups based on the parity of the sum of their digits, and to prove that for any positive integer $k$ with $1\le k<n$, the sum of the $k$-th powers of numbers in the first group equals that in the second group. This is a Type B problem, as it requires proving a stated equality without finding extremal values or classifications. The core difficulty lies in constructing a systematic way to pair numbers so that contributions to the sums cancel or match, particularly given the constraint $k<n$ which prevents higher-order terms from breaking the symmetry. The key insight appears to be the recursive structure of numbers in base $10$ and the parity-altering effect of increasing a digit by $1$.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1: For each $(k<n)$-digit number, increasing its units digit by $1$ modulo $10$ flips the parity of the digit sum. This is true because the units digit alone contributes $1$ to the sum, and parity changes with any odd increment.

Lemma 2: For numbers with at most $n$ digits, one can construct a bijection between numbers with odd and even digit sums by adding $1$ to the last digit in all positions except when a carry is needed; this preserves the total number of digits at most $n$ and ensures every number is paired.

Lemma 3: For any pair of numbers differing only in the last digit, their $k$-th powers satisfy the equality of sums for $1\le k<n$ because the binomial expansion shows that all terms involving the highest power of $10$ exceed the allowed $k$, and lower powers cancel symmetrically.

The hardest step is Lemma 3, as it requires careful justification that powers of $10$ appearing in the expansion do not disrupt equality for $k<n$. The bijection in Lemma 2 is straightforward but needs explicit handling of numbers ending in $9$.

## Solution

Let us denote by $S_1$ the set of natural numbers with at most $n$ digits whose sum of digits is odd, and by $S_2$ the set of numbers whose sum of digits is even. We aim to prove that for any $k$ with $1\le k<n$,

$$\sum_{x\in S_1} x^k = \sum_{x\in S_2} x^k.$$

We proceed by constructing a pairing. Any number $x$ with at most $n$ digits can be written in decimal form as

$$x = d_1 \cdot 10^{n-1} + d_2 \cdot 10^{n-2} + \dots + d_n,$$

where $0\le d_i\le 9$ and $d_1\neq 0$ if $x$ has exactly $n$ digits. Consider the operation of replacing the last digit $d_n$ by $d_n + 1$ modulo $10$.
