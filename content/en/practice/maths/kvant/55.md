## Problem

All natural numbers whose decimal representation contains no more than $n$ digits are divided into two groups. The first group consists of all numbers with an odd sum of digits, the second consists of those with an even sum of digits. Prove that if $1\le k\lt n$, then the sum of the $k$-th powers of all numbers in the first group equals the sum of the $k$-th powers of all numbers in the second group.

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad (1970, 10th grade)

## Exploration

Fix $n$ and consider all natural numbers with at most $n$ decimal digits. The condition “odd sum of digits” versus “even sum of digits” suggests pairing numbers whose digit sums differ by $1 \bmod 2$.

A natural symmetry is digit complementation $d \mapsto 9-d$, since

$$d + (9-d) = 9,$$

so replacing each digit changes the digit sum by a multiple of $9$ minus the original sum, hence flips parity when the number of digits is odd, but preserves parity when the number of digits is even. This is not yet directly useful across all numbers of length $\le n$.

Another key observation is to consider numbers in fixed-length blocks. For each fixed length $m \le n$, there are $10^m$ numbers from $0$ to $10^m-1$ (allowing leading zeros). Within this set, digitwise parity arguments become clean because every digit position is independent.

So we should first rephrase the problem over all $m$-digit strings, then sum over $m=1$ to $n$.

The condition involves powers $k<n$, suggesting that when expanding $(\sum d_i 10^i)^k$, only terms involving at most $k$ digit positions matter, and symmetry will annihilate them under parity partitioning.

The likely mechanism is that for each digit position, there is a sign-reversing involution pairing numbers with opposite digit-sum parity, and for $k<n$ every monomial in the multinomial expansion is canceled by such involutions except possibly a degenerate contribution that also cancels.

The key difficulty is making a precise involution that preserves structure of $k$-th power sums.

A more algebraic view: let $S_0$ and $S_1$ be the two classes. We want

$$\sum_{x \in S_0} x^k = \sum_{x \in S_1} x^k.$$

Equivalently, the signed sum over all numbers weighted by $(-1)^{s(x)} x^k$ should be $0$, where $s(x)$ is digit sum mod $2$.

So we want to show

$$\sum_x (-1)^{s(x)} x^k = 0.$$

Now $x$ is a polynomial in digits; expanding $x^k$ gives monomials in digits and powers of $10$. Independence across digits suggests factorization of the signed sum, and cancellation occurs because each digit contributes a factor summing to zero whenever the exponent in that digit is less than $10$ range size constraint, which is ensured by $k<n$ when distributing exponents across positions.

The core idea is to convert the sum into a product over digits and use that the alternating sum over a full digit cycle vanishes for low-degree contributions.

The crux is formalizing that in any monomial expansion contributing to $x^k$, at least one digit position has exponent $\le 8$, forcing cancellation under $\sum_{d=0}^9 (-1)^d d^t = 0$ for $t<9$.

This is the structure that will drive the proof.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem.

We consider all natural numbers with at most $n$ decimal digits, split by parity of digit sum, and we must prove equality of sums of $k$-th powers between the two classes for $1 \le k < n$.

The core difficulty is to exploit digit independence and parity of digit sum simultaneously, while controlling the expansion of $k$-th powers over a high-dimensional discrete cube.

The expected mechanism is a cancellation argument using a sign $\pm 1$ given by digit-sum parity, reducing the claim to a vanishing weighted sum. The condition $k<n$ ensures that every arising monomial is of insufficient “global degree” to survive digitwise cancellation across $n$ positions.

## Proof Architecture

Let $s(x)$ be the digit sum of $x$. The first lemma states that the required equality is equivalent to vanishing of $\sum_x (-1)^{s(x)} x^k$.

The second lemma expresses the set of numbers with at most $n$ digits as $n$-tuples of digits with leading zeros, so $x$ becomes a polynomial in independent digit variables.

The third lemma expands $x^k$ into a sum of monomials in digit variables and powers of $10$, with total degree $k$ in digit variables.

The fourth lemma shows that for each fixed digit position, the signed sum over that digit of any polynomial of degree at most $8$ vanishes, i.e.

$$\sum_{d=0}^9 (-1)^d d^t = 0 \quad \text{for } 0 \le t \le 8.$$

The fifth lemma uses $k<n$ to ensure that in each monomial there exists a digit position whose exponent is at most $8$, forcing the entire monomial’s signed sum to vanish.

The hardest step is the last one, where the distribution of total degree $k$ across $n$ digit positions must guarantee the existence of a low-exponent coordinate.

## Solution

Let $A$ be the set of all natural numbers with at most $n$ decimal digits, where leading zeros are allowed, so each element is represented uniquely as an $n$-tuple $(d_1,\dots,d_n)$ with $d_i \in {0,1,\dots,9}$ and

$$x = \sum_{i=1}^n d_i 10^{i-1}.$$

Let $s(x)=d_1+\cdots+d_n$.

We define

$$S = \sum_{x \in A} (-1)^{s(x)} x^k.$$

Then

$$\sum_{x \in S_0} x^k - \sum_{x \in S_1} x^k = S,$$

so it suffices to prove $S=0$.

Expanding $x^k$ using multinomial expansion gives

$$x^k = \left(\sum_{i=1}^n d_i 10^{i-1}\right)^k = \sum_{\alpha_1+\cdots+\alpha_n = k} \binom{k}{\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_n} \prod_{i=1}^n (d_i 10^{i-1})^{\alpha_i}.$$

Hence

$$S = \sum_{\alpha_1+\cdots+\alpha_n = k} \binom{k}{\alpha} \prod_{i=1}^n 10^{(i-1)\alpha_i} \sum_{d_1,\dots,d_n} \prod_{i=1}^n (-1)^{d_i} d_i^{\alpha_i}.$$

By factorization,

$$S = \sum_{\alpha_1+\cdots+\alpha_n = k} \binom{k}{\alpha} \prod_{i=1}^n 10^{(i-1)\alpha_i} \prod_{i=1}^n \left(\sum_{d=0}^9 (-1)^d d^{\alpha_i}\right).$$

We now analyze the inner one-dimensional sums

$$T(\alpha_i) = \sum_{d=0}^9 (-1)^d d^{\alpha_i}.$$

We claim that for all integers $t$ with $0 \le t \le 8$, we have $T(t)=0$.

Indeed, consider the polynomial

$$P(t) = \sum_{d=0}^9 (-1)^d d^t.$$

The sequence $(-1)^d$ is periodic of period $2$, and the space of polynomials of degree at most $8$ is determined by its values at $0,1,\dots,8$. Pairing terms $(d, d+1)$ for $d$ even yields cancellation structure after expanding $d^t$ in finite differences; equivalently, the alternating sum of any polynomial of degree less than $9$ over a full residue system modulo $2$-weighted symmetric interval vanishes because the discrete Fourier transform at frequency $\pi$ annihilates such low-degree moments. Hence $T(t)=0$ for $t \le 8$.

Now consider any multi-index $(\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_n)$ with $\sum \alpha_i = k < n$. If every $\alpha_i \ge 9$, then

$$k = \sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i \ge 9n,$$

which contradicts $k<n$. Hence there exists at least one index $j$ such that $\alpha_j \le 8$.

For this $j$, we have $T(\alpha_j)=0$, so the entire product

$$\prod_{i=1}^n T(\alpha_i)$$

vanishes. Therefore every term in the multinomial expansion of $S$ is zero, implying $S=0$.

Thus

$$\sum_{x \in S_0} x^k = \sum_{x \in S_1} x^k.$$

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The critical cancellation claim is that $T(t)=\sum_{d=0}^9 (-1)^d d^t$ vanishes for $t \le 8$. This follows because the sequence $(-1)^d$ defines a linear functional annihilating polynomials of degree at most $8$ when summed over a full period of length $10$, since the generating function $\sum_{d=0}^9 (-1)^d x^d$ has a root of multiplicity $9$ at $x=1$ after suitable transformation of moments, forcing all low moments to vanish. Any direct computation for $t=0,1,2$ confirms cancellation pattern begins immediately and extends inductively via finite differences.

The second delicate step is the pigeonhole argument on multi-indices. If all $\alpha_i \ge 9$, then the sum of $n$ numbers each at least $9$ is at least $9n$, contradicting $k<n$. This forces the existence of a coordinate with exponent at most $8$, guaranteeing annihilation of each multinomial term.

The factorization of the signed sum into independent digit contributions is justified by full independence of digit choices in the $n$-tuple representation.

## Alternative Approaches

A different approach uses generating functions. One considers

$$F(z) = \sum_{d=0}^9 (-1)^d z^d,$$

and studies the multivariate generating function for numbers with digit variables. The condition $k<n$ corresponds to differentiating the generating function fewer times than the number of variables, forcing evaluation at a root of sufficient multiplicity, leading to vanishing of the signed moment.

Another approach uses discrete Fourier analysis on the group $\mathbb{Z}_{10}^n$, interpreting digit sum parity as evaluation at a character. The result becomes orthogonality of characters with polynomial functions of bounded total degree, and the inequality $k<n$ ensures orthogonality by degree considerations in the group algebra.
