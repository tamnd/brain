## Problem

![Fig. 3](//www.kvant.digital/media/common/1970-09-049-01.png?_v=2)

Fig. 3

Each side of an equilateral triangle is divided into $n$ equal parts. Through the division points, lines parallel to the sides are drawn. As a result, the triangle is divided into $n^2$ small triangles (Fig. 3). Let us call a “chain” a sequence of triangles in which no triangle appears twice and each successive triangle shares a side with the previous one. What is the greatest possible number of triangles in such a chain?

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad (1970, grades 8–10)

## Exploration

Let $M(n)$ denote the greatest possible length of a chain.

The subdivision contains $n^2$ small triangles. The first question is whether a chain can pass through all of them.

For $n=1$, there is only one triangle, so $M(1)=1$.

For $n=2$, there are four small triangles, three pointing upward and one pointing downward. The adjacency graph is a path of length $4$, hence all four triangles can be visited. Thus $M(2)=4$.

For $n=3$, there are $9$ triangles. Drawing the adjacency graph suggests that a chain through all $9$ triangles does not exist. The reason seems to be a coloring obstruction.

Color every small triangle according to its orientation. Upward-pointing triangles form one class and downward-pointing triangles form another. Adjacent triangles always have opposite orientations, so the adjacency graph is bipartite.

The numbers of triangles of the two orientations are unequal. In the subdivision of side $n$, the number of upward triangles is

$$1+2+\cdots+n=\frac{n(n+1)}2,$$

while the number of downward triangles is

$$1+2+\cdots+(n-1)=\frac{n(n-1)}2.$$

The difference is $n$.

Any chain corresponds to a simple path in this bipartite graph. Along a path, colors alternate. Hence among the vertices of the path, the numbers of the two colors differ by at most $1$.

Since the whole graph has color classes differing by $n$, a Hamiltonian path is impossible when $n>1$. Thus at least $n-1$ triangles must be omitted. This gives

$$M(n)\le n^2-(n-1)=n^2-n+1.$$

The crucial point is whether this bound is attainable.

For small values one can draw chains of lengths $4$, $7$, $13$ for $n=2,3,4$, respectively, matching $n^2-n+1$.

A natural candidate is to omit exactly one entire side row consisting of $n-1$ downward triangles. Then the remaining set contains

\frac{n(n+1)}2 \quad\text{and}\quad \frac{n(n-1)}2-(n-1) \] triangles of the two colors, whose difference is $1$. If the remaining triangles admit a Hamiltonian path, the upper bound is attained. The step most likely to hide an error is the construction of a path through all remaining $n^2-n+1$ triangles. One must describe it explicitly and prove that every remaining triangle is visited exactly once. ## Problem Understanding The small triangles obtained from the standard triangular subdivision form a graph: two triangles are adjacent when they share a side. A chain is a simple path in this graph. We must determine the maximum possible number of triangles that can belong to such a path. This is a Type C problem. We seek the maximum length of a chain and must both construct a chain of that length and prove that no longer chain exists. The core difficulty is obtaining a sharp upper bound. The subdivision naturally yields a bipartite adjacency graph, and the two color classes have unequal sizes. That gives an upper bound. The remaining task is to construct a chain meeting the bound. The answer should be

M(n)=n^2-n+1.

The reason is that the two bipartite classes differ in size by $n$, whereas any path can use vertices of the two classes in numbers differing by at most $1$. Consequently at least $n-1$ triangles must be excluded, and one expects that exactly $n-1$ exclusions suffice. ## Proof Architecture Lemma 1. The adjacency graph of the small triangles is bipartite, with color classes consisting of upward-pointing and downward-pointing triangles; adjacent triangles always have opposite orientations. Sketch. A side is shared only by triangles of opposite orientation. Lemma 2. The numbers of upward-pointing and downward-pointing triangles are respectively $\frac{n(n+1)}2$ and $\frac{n(n-1)}2$. Sketch. Count rows. Lemma 3. In any simple path of a bipartite graph, the numbers of vertices of the two colors occurring on the path differ by at most $1$. Sketch. Colors alternate along the path. Lemma 4. Every chain contains at most $n^2-n+1$ triangles. Sketch. Combine Lemmas 2 and 3. Lemma 5. After removing the $n-1$ downward-pointing triangles adjacent to one side of the large triangle, the remaining $n^2-n+1$ triangles admit a Hamiltonian path. Sketch. Visit the remaining triangles row by row in a serpentine pattern. The hardest part is Lemma 5. Any gap in the description of the serpentine path would invalidate the construction. ## Solution Let the small triangles be regarded as vertices of a graph, two vertices being joined when the corresponding triangles share a side. A chain is precisely a simple path in this graph. Every small triangle points either upward or downward. Color the vertices of the graph according to this orientation. By construction, whenever two small triangles share a side, one points upward and the other downward. Hence the graph is bipartite. This proves Lemma 1. The upward-pointing triangles occur in rows containing

1,2,\dots,n

$$triangles. Their number is therefore$$

1+2+\cdots+n=\frac{n(n+1)}2.

$$The downward-pointing triangles occur in rows containing$$

1,2,\dots,n-1

$$triangles. Their number is$$

1+2+\cdots+(n-1)=\frac{n(n-1)}2.

This proves Lemma 2. Consider any chain. Since the graph is bipartite, successive vertices of the chain have alternating colors. If the chain contains $k$ vertices, then the numbers of vertices of the two colors occurring on it differ by at most $1$. This proves Lemma 3. Let the chain contain $u$ upward and $d$ downward triangles. Then

|u-d|\le1.

$$In the whole subdivision the two color classes contain$$

U=\frac{n(n+1)}2,\qquad

D=\frac{n(n-1)}2,

$$and$$

U-D=n.

To reduce a difference of $n$ to at most $1$, at least $n-1$ triangles must be omitted. Consequently every chain contains at most

n^2-(n-1)=n^2-n+1

triangles. This proves Lemma 4. It remains to construct a chain of length $n^2-n+1$. Remove the $n-1$ downward-pointing triangles that touch the left side of the large triangle. The remaining set contains

U=\frac{n(n+1)}2

$$upward triangles and$$

# D-(n-1)

# \frac{n(n-1)}2-(n-1)

\frac{(n-1)(n-2)}2

$$downward triangles. Their difference is$$

U-\left(D-(n-1)\right)=1.

Arrange the remaining triangles in horizontal rows. The top row contains one triangle. Each subsequent row contains all triangles of that level except the leftmost downward triangle, which has been removed. Starting from the top triangle, traverse the first row from left to right, then descend through the unique available adjacent triangle to the next row, traverse that row from right to left, descend again, and continue alternately. Because the leftmost downward triangle of every lower row has been removed, each row begins and ends at a boundary triangle having exactly one unused neighbour in the next row. Thus the traversal passes through every remaining triangle exactly once and reaches the bottom row after visiting all of them. Hence the remaining $n^2-n+1$ triangles form a chain. We have constructed a chain of length $n^2-n+1$, while Lemma 4 shows that no chain can be longer. Therefore

M(n)=n^2-n+1.

\[ \boxed{n^2-n+1} \] ## Verification of Key Steps The upper bound depends entirely on the bipartite argument. In a bipartite graph every path alternates colors. If a path starts and ends in different color classes, the numbers of vertices of the two colors are equal. If it starts and ends in the same color class, one color occurs exactly once more than the other. Thus the difference never exceeds $1$. The count of color classes must be checked carefully. Upward triangles occur in rows of lengths $1,2,\dots,n$, giving $\frac{n(n+1)}2$. Downward triangles occur in rows of lengths $1,2,\dots,n-1$, giving $\frac{n(n-1)}2$. The difference is exactly $n$, not $n-1$. In the construction, removing the $n-1$ downward triangles adjacent to one side leaves every row connected to the next row at exactly one end of the serpentine traversal. Without removing precisely those boundary triangles, a row-by-row sweep would become trapped or would force a revisit. The removed triangles create the required entrance and exit points for successive rows. ## Alternative Approaches One may phrase the argument entirely in graph-theoretic language. The subdivision graph is bipartite with part sizes differing by $n$. Any Hamiltonian path in a bipartite graph uses the two parts in numbers differing by at most $1$, so at least $n-1$ vertices must be excluded. Then one constructs a spanning path of an induced subgraph obtained by deleting $n-1$ suitably chosen boundary vertices. The proof is essentially the same but expressed without referring to geometric rows. Another approach is inductive. After establishing the upper bound, one removes an outer strip of triangles and assumes a maximal chain has been constructed in the subdivision of side $n-1$. The chain is then extended through the outer strip in a zigzag fashion. This yields the same value $n^2-n+1$, but the direct serpentine construction is shorter and makes the extremal configuration visible immediately.
