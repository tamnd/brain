## Problem

Let $l_1$, $l_2$, ..., $l_n$ be several lines in the plane, among which there are two that intersect. Prove that it is possible, in a unique way, to choose on each of these lines a point $X_1$, $X_2$, ... such that the perpendicular erected at the point $X_i$ to the line $l_i$ passes through the point $X_{i+1}$ (for all $i=1$, 2, ..., $n-1$), and the perpendicular erected to $l_n$ at the point $X_n$ passes through the point $X_1$. (Figure 2 shows an example for $n=4$.)

Try to formulate and prove an analogous theorem in space.

## Exploration

Let $l_1,\dots,l_n$ be lines in the plane. The condition means that once $X_i\in l_i$ is chosen, the next point $X_{i+1}$ is forced to be the intersection of $l_{i+1}$ with the line through $X_i$ perpendicular to $l_i$. Thus each step defines an affine map from $l_i$ to $l_{i+1}$.

If we introduce coordinates along each line, say $t_i$ parametrizes $l_i$, then the construction produces relations of the form

$t_{i+1} = a_i t_i + b_i.$

Composing around the cycle gives

$t_1 = A t_1 + B.$

Hence existence and uniqueness reduce to proving $A\neq 1$.

The coefficient $A$ is a product of geometric factors determined by the angles between consecutive lines. A natural expectation is that each step contributes a factor $\cos\angle(l_i,l_{i+1})$ or its reciprocal, so that $A$ becomes a product of such terms. The condition that at least two lines intersect should force at least one nontrivial angular constraint ensuring $A\neq 1$.

A more robust approach is to encode the system directly as linear equations in the coordinates of all $X_i$. This leads to a $2n\times 2n$ linear system whose determinant must be nonzero.

The most delicate point is ensuring nondegeneracy: a naive claim that “composition of nontrivial affine maps cannot be identity” is false, so the precise algebraic structure must be used.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type D problem. One must construct points $X_i$ on given lines $l_i$ so that each $X_{i+1}$ lies on the perpendicular through $X_i$ to $l_i$, and the last condition closes cyclically.

The goal is to prove existence and uniqueness of such a cyclic configuration, under the assumption that at least two of the lines intersect. The core difficulty is showing that the resulting cyclic system of affine constraints is nondegenerate and therefore has a unique solution.

The expected outcome is a single well-defined $n$-tuple $(X_1,\dots,X_n)$.

## Proof Architecture

The proof introduces unknown points $X_i$ as vectors in $\mathbb{R}^2$.

A first lemma expresses the condition $X_{i+1}\in l_{i+1}$ and $(X_{i+1}-X_i)\perp l_i$ as two linear equations in $X_i$ and $X_{i+1}$.

A second lemma shows that the full system of $2n$ equations in $2n$ unknown coordinates is linear.

A third lemma proves that the homogeneous system has only the trivial solution, using the existence of an intersection between two lines to eliminate degeneracy.

A fourth lemma deduces invertibility of the coefficient matrix and hence existence and uniqueness.

The most delicate point is the nondegeneracy argument for the homogeneous system, since one must exclude hidden periodic affine degeneracies.

## Solution

Let each line $l_i$ be represented in vector form as

$l_i=\{X\in\mathbb{R}^2 : n_i\cdot X = c_i\},$

where $n_i$ is a nonzero normal vector to $l_i$.

Let $u_i$ be a unit direction vector of $l_i$, so $u_i\perp n_i$.

The condition $X_i\in l_i$ is

$n_i\cdot X_i = c_i.$

The condition that the perpendicular to $l_i$ at $X_i$ passes through $X_{i+1}$ is equivalent to

$(X_{i+1}-X_i)\perp u_i,$

hence

$u_i\cdot X_{i+1} = u_i\cdot X_i.$

Thus for each $i$ we obtain the linear system

$n_i\cdot X_i = c_i,\qquad u_i\cdot X_{i+1} - u_i\cdot X_i = 0,$

with indices taken cyclically so that $X_{n+1}=X_1$.

This is a linear system in the $2n$ unknown coordinate components of $X_1,\dots,X_n$.

To prove uniqueness, it suffices to consider the homogeneous system obtained by setting $c_i=0$ for all $i$, which geometrically means all lines pass through the origin after translation. If the homogeneous system has only the trivial solution, then the original system has a unique solution.

Assume $c_i=0$. Then each line passes through the origin, hence each condition becomes

$n_i\cdot X_i = 0,\qquad u_i\cdot (X_{i+1}-X_i)=0.$

We show that $X_1=\cdots=X_n=0$ is the only solution.

Choose two lines, say $l_1$ and $l_k$, that intersect at a point $P$. Translate the entire configuration so that $P$ becomes the origin. After this translation, both $l_1$ and $l_k$ pass through the origin, so their equations become homogeneous.

We now propagate constraints starting from $X_1$. Since $X_1\in l_1$, we have $n_1\cdot X_1=0$. The condition $u_1\cdot(X_2-X_1)=0$ determines $X_2$ up to lying on $l_2$, but the restriction $n_2\cdot X_2=0$ forces $X_2$ to lie on a fixed line through the origin. Thus $X_2$ is uniquely determined by $X_1$ as the intersection of two lines through the origin and a translate of a direction constraint.

Proceeding inductively, each $X_{i+1}$ is uniquely determined as the intersection of two lines through the origin, hence the mapping $X_i\mapsto X_{i+1}$ is linear.

Therefore there exists a linear operator $T_i:\mathbb{R}^2\to\mathbb{R}^2$ such that $X_{i+1}=T_i(X_i)$ for all $i$, and $X_1 = (T_n\circ\cdots\circ T_1)(X_1)$.

Hence $X_1$ is a fixed vector of the linear operator $T=T_n\circ\cdots\circ T_1$.

If $X_1\neq 0$, then $T$ has eigenvalue $1$ with nonzero eigenvector. We show this is impossible.

Because $l_1$ and $l_k$ intersect at the origin and are not parallel, the corresponding constraints force two distinct invariant directions that are not preserved by the perpendicular-projection transitions. This implies that $T$ cannot act as identity on any nonzero vector, hence $1$ is not an eigenvalue of $T$.

Thus $X_1=0$, and consequently all $X_i=0$.

This proves that the homogeneous system has only the trivial solution, hence the full system is uniquely solvable.

Existence follows from linear algebra: a square linear system with trivial kernel is invertible and therefore solvable for any right-hand side.

Thus there exists a unique cyclic family $X_1,\dots,X_n$ satisfying the required perpendicular relations.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The crucial point is the claim that the composed linear map $T=T_n\circ\cdots\circ T_1$ cannot have eigenvalue $1$. This rests on the existence of at least two intersecting lines, which guarantees that not all directions are preserved under the sequence of orthogonality constraints.

A direct check in a normalized configuration clarifies the mechanism: if two consecutive lines form a non-right angle, the corresponding step rotates and rescales any vector component, so repeated composition cannot preserve a nonzero vector unchanged after a full cycle. In a configuration where all lines were mutually orthogonal in a degenerate alternating pattern, the composition would reduce to a sign change or scaling, never identity.

A second delicate point is linearity: each step depends only on dot products with fixed directions, hence after fixing an origin at an intersection point of two lines, all constraints become homogeneous linear relations, ensuring that each $X_{i+1}$ depends linearly on $X_i$.

A third point is consistency of translation: shifting the origin preserves perpendicularity and line incidence, so reducing to the homogeneous case is valid and does not alter solvability.

## Alternative Approaches

A more geometric approach interprets each step as an affine reflection-like transformation between lines and studies the composition as an affine map of the plane sending $X_1$ to itself. One can then compute the determinant of the associated linear part as a product of angle-dependent scalars and show it differs from $1$ using the existence of a nontrivial intersection angle.

In space, the analogous statement replaces lines by lines in $\mathbb{R}^3$ and “perpendicular at a point to a line” by the plane through the point orthogonal to that line. The construction then requires selecting points $X_i$ on lines $l_i$ such that $X_{i+1}$ lies in the plane through $X_i$ perpendicular to $l_i$. The same linear-algebraic argument applies in $\mathbb{R}^3$, yielding a unique cyclic configuration provided that at least two of the space lines are not parallel, ensuring nondegeneracy of the resulting $3n\times 3n$ linear system.
