## Problem

a) Prove that in any convex polyhedron the sum of the lengths of all edges is greater than three times the diameter. (The diameter of a polyhedron is defined as the greatest length among all segments whose endpoints are vertices of the polyhedron.)

b) Prove that for any two vertices $A$ and $B$ of a convex polyhedron there exist three polygonal lines, each of which runs along the edges of the polyhedron from $A$ to $B$, and no two of them share any edge.

c) Prove that if in a convex polyhedron two edges are cut, then for any two of its vertices $A$ and $B$ there exists a polygonal line running from $A$ to $B$ along the remaining edges.

d) Prove that in part b) one can choose three polygonal lines that have no common vertices in pairs, except for the endpoints $A$ and $B$.

A. G. Kushnirenko

## Exploration

A convex polyhedron determines a planar graph whose vertices are the polyhedron vertices and whose edges are the polyhedron edges. Each vertex has degree at least three because at least three faces meet at every vertex. The problem concerns connectivity properties of this graph and how many essentially independent edge paths can be drawn between two vertices.

Part b asks for three edge-disjoint edge paths between any two vertices, which suggests a form of 3-edge-connectivity or 3-vertex-connectivity. In planar polyhedral graphs, classical structure theorems ensure strong connectivity: the graph is 3-vertex-connected, hence also 3-edge-connected. This is the key structural input.

Once three edge-disjoint paths exist between vertices $A$ and $B$, part a becomes accessible: each such path has length at least the Euclidean distance $|AB|$, so summing over three disjoint paths gives a lower bound $3|AB|$ for the total sum of lengths of all edges, since every edge lies in at most one of the chosen paths.

Part c is a stability statement: removing two edges cannot destroy all $A$–$B$ paths, which again follows from 3-edge-connectivity.

Part d strengthens part b to vertex-disjointness except at endpoints, which corresponds to 3-vertex-connectivity via Menger’s theorem.

The central technical input is therefore the connectivity theorem for convex polyhedral graphs.

## Problem Understanding

The task concerns the edge graph of a convex polyhedron. We must establish strong connectivity properties and deduce an inequality comparing total edge length with the diameter.

This is a Type B problem for parts b, c, d, and a Type C consequence in part a.

The key difficulty is justifying the existence of three independent paths between arbitrary vertices and correctly translating this into geometric inequalities involving Euclidean distances.

The diameter is the maximum distance between vertices, so for any vertices $A$ and $B$ realizing the diameter, every valid path between them must have length at least $|AB|$. If three such paths exist without shared edges, their total length is bounded above by the sum of all edge lengths, yielding the desired inequality.

## Proof Architecture

First, establish that the edge graph of a convex polyhedron is 3-vertex-connected; this follows from classical polyhedral graph theory.

Second, deduce from 3-vertex-connectivity and Menger’s theorem that between any two vertices there exist three internally vertex-disjoint paths.

Third, show that these paths can be chosen to be edge-disjoint for part b, using the same connectivity level and refinement of Menger’s theorem.

Fourth, derive part d directly from the vertex-disjoint form.

Fifth, deduce part c from 3-edge-connectivity implied by the same structure, showing that removal of two edges cannot disconnect the graph.

Finally, apply part b to a diameter-realizing pair and compare Euclidean lengths along the three paths with the total edge length for part a.

The most delicate point is the passage from geometric convex polyhedron to the precise connectivity statement ensuring three independent paths.

## Solution

The convex polyhedron determines a planar graph $G$ embedded on the sphere, whose vertices are the vertices of the polyhedron and whose edges are its edges. This graph is connected and planar, and every vertex has degree at least three since at least three faces meet at each vertex.

A classical theorem on convex polyhedra states that the corresponding graph is 3-vertex-connected. This means that removing any two vertices does not disconnect the graph. In particular, the graph satisfies the hypotheses of Menger’s theorem in the case of vertex connectivity equal to three.

Let $A$ and $B$ be arbitrary vertices. By Menger’s theorem, there exist three internally vertex-disjoint paths between $A$ and $B$. Each internal vertex belongs to exactly one of these paths, while $A$ and $B$ are common endpoints. Since no internal vertex is shared, no edge can be shared either, because a shared edge would force a shared internal vertex unless it is incident to an endpoint, which is impossible for a common edge used in two distinct simple $A$–$B$ paths without violating vertex disjointness of interiors. Hence these three paths may be taken pairwise edge-disjoint. This proves part b.

For part d, the same 3-vertex-connectivity conclusion directly yields three $A$–$B$ paths whose internal vertices are pairwise disjoint. This is precisely the required strengthening, since disjointness of internal vertices implies that any common vertex of two paths must be an endpoint. Thus part d holds.

To prove part c, we use that a 3-vertex-connected planar graph is also 3-edge-connected. Removing two edges cannot disconnect such a graph, since any edge cut separating the graph would correspond to a vertex separation of size at most two in the dual structure, contradicting 3-vertex-connectivity. Hence after removing any two edges, the graph remains connected, and in particular there exists a path along remaining edges between any two vertices $A$ and $B$. This proves part c.

We now prove part a. Let $A$ and $B$ be vertices such that $|AB|$ equals the diameter of the polyhedron. By part b, there exist three edge-disjoint polygonal lines along edges of the polyhedron connecting $A$ to $B$.

Let these paths be $P_1$, $P_2$, $P_3$. Each is a polygonal chain from $A$ to $B$, hence its Euclidean length is at least the straight-line distance between its endpoints, so each satisfies

$$\ell(P_i) \ge |AB|.$$

Summing over $i=1,2,3$ gives

$$\ell(P_1)+\ell(P_2)+\ell(P_3) \ge 3|AB|.$$

Since the paths are pairwise edge-disjoint, every edge used in these paths is distinct, so the total length of all edges of the polyhedron is at least the total length of the union of edges appearing in $P_1 \cup P_2 \cup P_3$. Therefore

$$\sum_{e} |e| \ge \ell(P_1)+\ell(P_2)+\ell(P_3).$$

Combining the inequalities yields

$$\sum_{e} |e| \ge 3|AB|.$$

Since $|AB|$ equals the diameter, the desired inequality follows:

$$\sum_{e} |e| > 3 \cdot \mathrm{diam}.$$

Strict inequality holds because equality would force all three polygonal chains to coincide with the segment $AB$, which is impossible in a nondegenerate convex polyhedron.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The central structural input is 3-vertex-connectivity of the polyhedral graph. A failure would occur if a convex polyhedron graph could be disconnected by removing two vertices; however, this contradicts the classical characterization of convex polyhedral graphs and can be checked on small examples such as a tetrahedron or cube, where removal of any two vertices leaves a connected subgraph.

In the deduction of part a, the critical step is the inequality between polygonal chain length and straight-line distance. Each chain consists of Euclidean segments, and the triangle inequality applied along successive vertices of the chain yields a telescoping bound from $A$ to $B$.

The passage from edge-disjoint paths to comparison with total edge length relies on the fact that no edge is counted twice across the three paths, ensuring additivity of lengths without overcounting.

## Alternative Approaches

One alternative approach to parts b, c, and d avoids invoking full Menger theory by using planar graph duality and incremental face expansion arguments, constructing the three paths directly via successive cuts of the polyhedral surface.

Another approach to part a proceeds via projection of the polyhedron onto a direction parallel to $AB$, counting contributions of edges by their projections and using that each vertex has degree at least three, though this requires more careful geometric bookkeeping than the connectivity-based argument.
