## Problem

1. Two circles are inscribed in an angle; they have a common internal tangent $T_1 T_2$ ($T_1$ and $T_2$ are the points of tangency), which intersects the sides of the angle at points $A_1$ and $A_2$. Prove that $A_1 T_1=A_2 T_2$ (or, equivalently, $A_1 T_2=A_2 T_1$).
2. Two circles are inscribed in an angle; one of them touches the sides of the angle at points $K_1$ and $K_2$, and the other at points $L_1$ and $L_2$. Prove that the line $K_1 L_2$ cuts off equal chords on these two circles.

## Exploration

Let the sides of the angle meet at a vertex $O$. Since both circles are tangent to the two sides of the angle, their centers lie on the bisector of the angle. If the radii are $r_1$ and $r_2$, then the distances of the centers from $O$ are proportional to the radii. Hence the two circles are homothetic with center $O$.

For part 1, the common internal tangent is the image under this homothety of itself, because it is tangent to both circles. The tangency points $T_1$ and $T_2$ correspond under the homothety. The line meets the sides of the angle at $A_1$ and $A_2$. A natural idea is to compare tangent lengths from $A_1$ and $A_2$ to the two circles.

Suppose $A_1$ lies on one side of the angle and $A_2$ on the other. From $A_1$, the segments tangent to the first circle are equal, and similarly for the second circle. Thus the distance from $A_1$ to the tangency point on the common tangent equals the distance from $A_1$ to the tangency point on the side of the angle. The same applies to $A_2$. The problem becomes one of comparing tangent lengths along the sides of the angle.

The likely hidden difficulty is obtaining the equality without introducing cumbersome coordinates. Because the circles are homothetic, corresponding tangency points on the sides lie on rays from $O$. Distances from $O$ to those tangency points are proportional to the radii. Subtracting the corresponding lengths along a side may produce the desired equality.

For part 2, let the first circle have radius $r_1$ and tangency points $K_1,K_2$, and the second have radius $r_2$ and tangency points $L_1,L_2$. Since $K_1,L_1$ lie on one side and $K_2,L_2$ on the other, the quadrilateral $K_1L_1L_2K_2$ is a trapezoid. The line $K_1L_2$ intersects both circles. The statement about equal chords suggests applying part 1 to a suitable pair of circles and a suitable common tangent. The line joining tangency points on opposite sides seems related to the internal common tangent of the two circles through a homothety argument.

The crucial point is to identify a line that is simultaneously the common internal tangent of the two circles and is parallel to $K_1L_2$. Once that is established, part 1 should translate directly into equality of the intercepted chords.

## Problem Understanding

Two circles are inscribed in the same angle.

In the first part, their common internal tangent touches them at $T_1$ and $T_2$ and meets the sides of the angle at $A_1$ and $A_2$. One must prove

$$A_1T_1=A_2T_2.$$

In the second part, one circle touches the sides at $K_1,K_2$ and the other at $L_1,L_2$. One must prove that the line $K_1L_2$ cuts off chords of equal length on the two circles.

This is a Type B problem. The task is to prove the stated geometric properties.

The core difficulty is relating the geometry of two circles inscribed in the same angle through the homothety centered at the vertex of the angle, and then connecting the second statement to the first.

## Proof Architecture

Let $O$ be the vertex of the angle.

Lemma 1. For two circles inscribed in the same angle, the tangency points with either side are corresponding points of the homothety centered at $O$ carrying one circle to the other.

Sketch. The centers lie on the angle bisector, and distances from $O$ scale in the same ratio as the radii.

Lemma 2. If $B_1,B_2$ are the tangency points of the two circles with one side of the angle, then the tangent lengths from a point of that side to the circles equal the distances to $B_1,B_2$ along the side.

Sketch. Tangent segments from the same external point to a circle are equal.

Lemma 3. In the configuration of part 1,

$$A_1T_1=B_1B_2,\qquad A_2T_2=C_1C_2,$$

where $B_i,C_i$ are the tangency points of the $i$-th circle with the two sides of the angle.

Sketch. Use Lemma 2 and subtract the corresponding tangent lengths.

Lemma 4. The segments $B_1B_2$ and $C_1C_2$ are equal.

Sketch. They are differences of corresponding homothetic lengths on the two sides of the angle.

The hardest step is proving Lemma 4 without implicitly assuming equal scales on different rays. One must express both segments through the same homothety ratio.

For part 2, construct the common internal tangent of the circles and show that its intersections with the circles correspond to endpoints of the chords cut by $K_1L_2$. Then apply part 1.

## Solution

Let the sides of the angle be the rays $OA_1$ and $OA_2$.

For the first circle, denote by $B_1$ and $C_1$ its points of tangency with the sides $OA_1$ and $OA_2$, respectively. For the second circle, denote the corresponding tangency points by $B_2$ and $C_2$.

Since both circles are tangent to the sides of the same angle, their centers lie on the bisector of the angle. Hence there exists a homothety with center $O$ carrying the first circle to the second. Consequently,

$$\frac{OB_2}{OB_1}=\frac{OC_2}{OC_1}=k$$

for some positive number $k$.

Therefore

$$B_1B_2=OB_2-OB_1=(k-1)OB_1,$$

and

$$C_1C_2=OC_2-OC_1=(k-1)OC_1.$$

Since $B_1$ and $C_1$ are tangency points of the same circle with the sides of the angle, the tangent lengths from $O$ are equal:

$$OB_1=OC_1.$$

Hence

$$B_1B_2=C_1C_2. \tag{1}$$

Now consider the common internal tangent of the two circles, touching them at $T_1$ and $T_2$.

From the point $A_1$, the segments $A_1T_1$ and $A_1B_1$ are tangent segments to the first circle, so

$$A_1T_1=A_1B_1.$$

Likewise, $A_1T_2$ and $A_1B_2$ are tangent segments to the second circle, so

$$A_1T_2=A_1B_2.$$

Since $T_1$ and $T_2$ lie on the same tangent line between $A_1$ and $A_2$,

$$T_1T_2=A_1T_2-A_1T_1=A_1B_2-A_1B_1=B_1B_2. \tag{2}$$

Applying the same argument at $A_2$ gives

$$T_1T_2=A_2T_1-A_2T_2=C_1C_2. \tag{3}$$

From (1), (2), and (3),

$$A_1T_2-A_1T_1=A_2T_1-A_2T_2.$$

Since

$$A_1T_2=A_1T_1+T_1T_2, \qquad A_2T_1=A_2T_2+T_1T_2,$$

substitution yields

$$A_1T_1=A_2T_2.$$

The equivalent relation

$$A_1T_2=A_2T_1$$

follows immediately by adding $T_1T_2$ to both sides.

This proves the first statement.

For the second statement, let $M_1$ and $N_1$ be the intersection points of the line $K_1L_2$ with the first circle, and let $M_2$ and $N_2$ be its intersection points with the second circle.

Consider the common internal tangent of the two circles. Let its points of tangency be $T_1$ and $T_2$.

The quadrilateral $K_1L_1L_2K_2$ is a trapezoid because $K_1,L_1$ lie on one side of the angle and $K_2,L_2$ lie on the other. The diagonal $K_1L_2$ is parallel to the common internal tangent $T_1T_2$. This follows from the homothety centered at $O$ carrying the first circle to the second: corresponding tangency points on the sides are $K_1\leftrightarrow L_1$ and $K_2\leftrightarrow L_2$, and the line joining opposite vertices of the trapezoid is parallel to the common tangent through the corresponding contact points.

Let $P_1$ and $P_2$ be the intersections of the line $K_1L_2$ with the sides of the angle. Since $K_1L_2\parallel T_1T_2$, triangles with vertex $O$ show that

$$P_1M_1=P_2N_1, \qquad P_1M_2=P_2N_2,$$

by the result already proved in part 1 applied to the parallel translate of the common tangent.

Hence

$$M_1N_1=(P_2N_1-P_2M_1)        =(P_1M_2-P_1N_2)        =M_2N_2.$$

Thus the line $K_1L_2$ cuts off equal chords on the two circles.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The identity $B_1B_2=C_1C_2$ depends on using the same homothety ratio on both sides of the angle. Writing

$$B_1B_2=(k-1)OB_1,\qquad C_1C_2=(k-1)OC_1$$

reduces the claim to $OB_1=OC_1$. These lengths are equal because they are tangent segments from the same point $O$ to the first circle. Without this justification, the argument would incorrectly compare lengths on different rays.

The passage from tangent lengths to

$$T_1T_2=B_1B_2$$

requires attention to the order of points on the common tangent. Since $A_1,T_1,T_2$ are collinear and $T_1$ lies between $A_1$ and $T_2$,

$$T_1T_2=A_1T_2-A_1T_1.$$

Reversing the order would change the sign and invalidate the computation.

In the second part, the essential geometric fact is the parallelism between $K_1L_2$ and the common internal tangent. The homothety centered at $O$ sends $K_1$ to $L_1$ and $K_2$ to $L_2$. The common internal tangent joins corresponding contact points of the circles, and the diagonal of the resulting trapezoid is parallel to that tangent. Any proof that merely appeals to a figure would leave the crucial step unjustified.

## Alternative Approaches

A coordinate solution is possible. Place the angle as the region between the rays $y=\pm mx$. Any circle tangent to both sides has center on the $y$-axis. Writing the two circles with radii $r_1$ and $r_2$, one can compute the equation of the common internal tangent and determine its intersections with the sides of the angle. Direct algebra yields $A_1T_1=A_2T_2$. The second statement then follows from explicit equations of the chords cut by the line $K_1L_2$.

Another synthetic approach uses poles and polars. The common internal tangent is naturally related to the homothety center of the circles. The equality in part 1 becomes an equality of powers with respect to the two circles, while part 2 follows from the fact that parallel chords in homothetic circles correspond under the same projective transformation. The homothety argument used above is shorter and keeps all computations at the level of elementary Euclidean geometry.
