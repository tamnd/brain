## Problem

1. Find the number $k$ which is divisible by 2 and 9 and has exactly 14 divisors (including 1 and $k$).  
2. Prove that if 14 is replaced by 15, the problem has multiple solutions, whereas if 14 is replaced by 17, there are no solutions at all.  

E. B. Dynkin, S. A. Molchanov, A. L. Rozenthal, A. K. Tolpygo  

Mathematical Problems. 3rd ed. – Moscow: Nauka, 1971.

## Exploration

Let

$$k=\prod p_i^{a_i}$$

be the prime factorization of $k$. The number of positive divisors is

$$\tau(k)=\prod (a_i+1).$$

Since $k$ is divisible by $2$ and by $9$, we must have

$$k=2^{a}3^{b}\cdot m,$$

with $a\ge1$ and $b\ge2$.

For part 1 we need $\tau(k)=14$. Since

$$14=14\cdot1=7\cdot2,$$

the possible exponent patterns are

$$13,$$

or

$$6,1.$$

The pattern $13$ corresponds to $p^{13}$, which cannot be divisible by both $2$ and $3$.

The pattern $6,1$ corresponds to $p^6q$. Since both primes $2$ and $3$ must occur, the two primes are exactly $2$ and $3$. To satisfy $b\ge2$, the exponent $6$ must belong to $3$. This gives

$$k=2\cdot3^6.$$

The alternative $2^6\cdot3$ is not divisible by $9$.

For part 2 with $15$ divisors,

$$15=15\cdot1=5\cdot3.$$

Hence the exponent patterns are

$$14,$$

or

$$4,2.$$

The first is impossible because a single prime power cannot be divisible by both $2$ and $3$. The second gives numbers of the form

$$2^4 3^2,\qquad 2^2 3^4,$$

and also

$$2^4 3^2 r,\qquad 2^2 3^4 r$$

would have too many divisors, since an extra prime contributes a factor $2$. Thus at least two distinct solutions already appear:

$$2^4 3^2,\qquad 2^2 3^4.$$

For $17$ divisors,

$$17$$

is prime, so the only exponent pattern is

$$16.$$

Then $k$ must be a $16$th power of a single prime, impossible if $k$ is divisible by both $2$ and $3$.

The potentially delicate point is proving that the divisor-count factorizations exhaust all possibilities.

## Problem Understanding

We seek integers divisible by both $2$ and $9$ whose number of positive divisors equals a prescribed value.

This is a Type A problem. We must determine all solutions for divisor count $14$, show that replacing $14$ by $15$ yields more than one solution, and prove that replacing $14$ by $17$ yields no solution.

The core difficulty is translating the condition on the number of divisors into restrictions on the exponents in the prime factorization. Since

$$\tau\!\left(\prod p_i^{a_i}\right)=\prod(a_i+1),$$

the factorization of the required divisor count determines all possible exponent patterns.

The answer for the first part is

$$k=2\cdot3^6.$$

This is plausible because $14=7\cdot2$, forcing exponent pattern $(6,1)$, and the condition of divisibility by $9$ places the larger exponent on the prime $3$.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma states that if

$$k=\prod p_i^{a_i},$$

then

$$\tau(k)=\prod(a_i+1).$$

This follows from counting independent choices of exponents in each divisor.

The second lemma states that all integers with $14$ divisors have exponent pattern $(13)$ or $(6,1)$. This follows from the factorizations of $14$ into integers greater than $1$.

The third lemma states that all integers with $15$ divisors have exponent pattern $(14)$ or $(4,2)$. This follows from the factorizations of $15$.

The fourth lemma states that all integers with $17$ divisors have exponent pattern $(16)$. This follows because $17$ is prime.

The hardest direction is excluding all possibilities for divisor count $17$. The lemma most likely to fail under scrutiny is the classification of exponent patterns from the divisor-count factorization.

## Solution

Let

$$k=\prod_{i=1}^{r} p_i^{a_i}$$

be the prime factorization of a positive integer.

A divisor of $k$ has the form

$$\prod_{i=1}^{r} p_i^{b_i},$$

where

$$0\le b_i\le a_i.$$

For each $i$, there are $a_i+1$ possible choices of $b_i$. Since the choices are independent,

$$\tau(k)=\prod_{i=1}^{r}(a_i+1).$$

We apply this formula.

For part 1, suppose $\tau(k)=14$. Since

$$14=14\cdot1=7\cdot2,$$

the only possible exponent patterns are

$$(13)$$

or

$$(6,1).$$

The pattern $(13)$ corresponds to a number of the form $p^{13}$, involving only one prime. Such a number cannot be divisible by both $2$ and $3$, hence it is impossible.

Thus $k$ must have pattern $(6,1)$:

$$k=p^6q,$$

where $p$ and $q$ are distinct primes.

Because $k$ is divisible by both $2$ and $3$, the primes $p$ and $q$ must be exactly $2$ and $3$. Hence the only possibilities are

$$2^6\cdot3 \quad\text{or}\quad 2\cdot3^6.$$

The first number is not divisible by $9$, because the exponent of $3$ equals $1$. The second number is divisible by $9$, because the exponent of $3$ equals $6$.

Therefore the unique solution of part 1 is

$$k=2\cdot3^6=1458.$$

Now replace $14$ by $15$. Since

$$15=15\cdot1=5\cdot3,$$

the exponent patterns are $(14)$ and $(4,2)$.

The pattern $(14)$ gives a single prime power and cannot produce a number divisible by both $2$ and $3$.

The pattern $(4,2)$ yields

$$2^4\cdot3^2 \qquad\text{and}\qquad 2^2\cdot3^4.$$

For these numbers,

$$\tau(2^4\cdot3^2)=(4+1)(2+1)=15,$$

and

$$\tau(2^2\cdot3^4)=(2+1)(4+1)=15.$$

Both are divisible by $2$ and by $9$.

Since these two numbers are distinct, the modified problem has multiple solutions.

Finally, replace $14$ by $17$. Since $17$ is prime, the equation

$$\tau(k)=17$$

forces the exponent pattern $(16)$. Thus

$$k=p^{16}$$

for some prime $p$.

Such a number contains only one prime factor, so it cannot be divisible by both $2$ and $3$. Hence no solution exists.

Thus:

For divisor count $14$, the unique solution is

$$\boxed{k=2\cdot3^6=1458}.$$

For divisor count $15$, there are at least two solutions,

$$2^4\cdot3^2 \quad\text{and}\quad 2^2\cdot3^4.$$

For divisor count $17$, there are no solutions.

$$\boxed{k=1458}$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the passage from the divisor count to exponent patterns. For $\tau(k)=14$, every factor $(a_i+1)$ is at least $2$. The factorizations of $14$ into integers at least $2$ are only

$$14,\qquad 7\cdot2.$$

Hence the exponent patterns are only $(13)$ and $(6,1)$. No three-factor decomposition exists because

$$2\cdot2\cdot2=8,\qquad 2\cdot2\cdot3=12,\qquad 2\cdot2\cdot4=16.$$

The second delicate step is identifying the unique solution for divisor count $14$. After obtaining pattern $(6,1)$, the primes involved must be $2$ and $3$, since divisibility by both is required. The two assignments are

$$2^6\cdot3,\qquad 2\cdot3^6.$$

Checking divisibility by $9$ eliminates the first and retains the second.

The third delicate step is the nonexistence for divisor count $17$. Because $17$ is prime, any product

$$\prod(a_i+1)=17$$

contains only one factor greater than $1$. Hence there is exactly one prime in the factorization of $k$, giving

$$k=p^{16}.$$

A number of this form cannot be divisible simultaneously by $2$ and $3$.

## Alternative Approaches

One may work entirely backwards from the required divisor count. If $k$ is divisible by $2$ and $9$, write

$$k=2^a3^b m,$$

with $a\ge1$, $b\ge2$, and $(m,6)=1$. Then

$$\tau(k)=(a+1)(b+1)\tau(m).$$

For $\tau(k)=14$, the factor $b+1$ is at least $3$, so the only way to obtain $14=2\cdot7$ is

$$a+1=2,\qquad b+1=7,\qquad \tau(m)=1,$$

which immediately yields

$$k=2\cdot3^6.$$

The main solution is preferable because the same divisor-pattern classification handles all three values, $14$, $15$, and $17$, with a single uniform argument.
