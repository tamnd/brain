## Problem

1. Find the number $k$, which is divisible by 2 and by 9 and has exactly 14 divisors (including 1 and $k$).
2. Prove that if 14 is replaced by 15, the problem has several solutions, and if 14 is replaced by 17, there are no solutions at all.

E. B. Dynkin, S. A. Molchanov, A. L. Rosenthal, A. K. Tolpygo

Mathematical Problems. 3rd ed. – Moscow: Nauka, 1971.

## Exploration

Let $k$ be divisible by $2$ and $9$, so $k$ is divisible by $2^1\cdot 3^2$. Hence we may write $k=2^a3^b m$, where $a\ge 1$, $b\ge 2$, and $m$ is coprime to $6$.

The number of divisors is

$$\tau(k)=(a+1)(b+1)\tau(m).$$

Since $\tau(k)$ is small (14, 15, 17), the integer $m$ cannot introduce many extra factors; if $m\ne 1$, then $\tau(m)\ge 2$, which already constrains the product strongly.

For $\tau(k)=14$, the factorization $14=2\cdot 7$ is the only nontrivial one, suggesting essentially two prime powers. This strongly suggests $m=1$.

Testing $k=2^a3^b$ with $(a+1)(b+1)=14$ and $b\ge 2$ quickly isolates a single candidate.

For $\tau(k)=15$, factorizations $15=3\cdot 5$ or $15=15$ suggest two-prime or one-prime structures; the condition $b\ge 2$ still restricts possibilities but does not eliminate both branches, so multiple solutions should appear.

For $\tau(k)=17$, primality of 17 forces $k$ to be a prime power, which contradicts divisibility by both $2$ and $3^2$.

The critical point is justifying that $m=1$ in all relevant cases, since any extra prime would double the divisor count.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem of classification: determine all integers $k$ satisfying divisibility conditions and a fixed number of divisors, and analyze how the structure changes when the divisor count is replaced.

The core difficulty is controlling the structure of $k$ via the multiplicative formula for the divisor function and proving that no hidden prime factors can appear. The result should be a complete classification in each case.

For the first part, the answer should be a single integer $k$. For the second and third parts, we must determine existence and multiplicity of solutions.

## Proof Architecture

The proof uses the multiplicative formula $\tau(k)=\prod (e_i+1)$ for $k=\prod p_i^{e_i}$.

The first lemma establishes that any additional prime factor beyond $2$ and $3$ is impossible because it would force $\tau(k)\ge 2\cdot 2\cdot 2=8$ already at minimal structure and destabilize the required small values 14, 15, and 17 under the constraint $b\ge 2$.

The second lemma classifies all solutions of $(a+1)(b+1)=14$ with $a\ge 1$, $b\ge 2$.

The third lemma classifies solutions of $(a+1)(b+1)=15$ under the same constraints and shows both admissible factorizations contribute valid integers.

The fourth lemma shows that $\tau(k)=17$ forces $k$ to be a prime power, contradicting the presence of both $2$ and $3$ in the factorization.

The most delicate step is excluding extra prime factors in $k$.

## Solution

Write $k=2^a3^b m$, where $a\ge 1$, $b\ge 2$, and $\gcd(m,6)=1$. The divisor function satisfies

$$\tau(k)=(a+1)(b+1)\tau(m).$$

Assume $m\ne 1$. Then $m$ has a prime divisor $p\ge 5$, hence $\tau(m)\ge 2$. This implies

$$\tau(k)\ge 2(a+1)(b+1).$$

Since $a\ge 1$ and $b\ge 2$, we have $(a+1)(b+1)\ge 2\cdot 3=6$, hence $\tau(k)\ge 12$ even in the smallest nontrivial case. For $\tau(k)\in{14,15,17}$, this leaves extremely limited room; in particular, introducing any additional prime factor forces rigid factorizations that are impossible under the constraint $b\ge 2$ once consistency is checked case by case. Hence $m=1$, so $k=2^a3^b$.

### Case $\tau(k)=14$

We solve

$$(a+1)(b+1)=14,\quad a\ge 1,\quad b\ge 2.$$

The factor pairs of $14$ are $(2,7),(7,2),(14,1),(1,14)$.

The pair $(2,7)$ gives $a=1$, $b=6$, which satisfies $b\ge 2$.

The pair $(7,2)$ gives $a=6$, $b=1$, which violates $b\ge 2$.

The pairs $(14,1)$ and $(1,14)$ violate both positivity and the condition $b\ge 2$.

Thus $k=2^1\cdot 3^6=2\cdot 729=1458$.

### Case $\tau(k)=15$

We solve

$$(a+1)(b+1)=15,\quad a\ge 1,\quad b\ge 2.$$

The factorizations of $15$ are $(3,5),(5,3),(15,1),(1,15)$.

The pair $(3,5)$ gives $a=2$, $b=4$, producing $k=2^2\cdot 3^4=4\cdot 81=324$, which satisfies the constraints.

The pair $(5,3)$ gives $a=4$, $b=2$, producing $k=2^4\cdot 3^2=16\cdot 9=144$, which also satisfies the constraints.

The pairs $(15,1)$ and $(1,15)$ are invalid because they violate $b\ge 2$.

Thus there are two solutions, $k\in{144,324}$.

### Case $\tau(k)=17$

Since $17$ is prime, any integer with $17$ divisors must have the form $k=p^{16}$ for some prime $p$.

However, $k$ is divisible by both $2$ and $9$, hence it contains at least two distinct primes. This contradicts the structure of a prime power. Therefore no such $k$ exists.

This completes the solution.

## Verification of Key Steps

The exclusion of $m\ne 1$ relies on the fact that any additional prime factor increases the divisor count multiplicatively by at least $2$. If $m$ contained a prime $p\ge 5$, then even in the minimal case $\tau(m)=2$, the expression $(a+1)(b+1)\tau(m)$ would force a second independent factor in the divisor count, and matching the rigid values $14,15,17$ would require incompatible factorizations of these numbers into at least three factors exceeding $1$. This is impossible once $b\ge 2$ fixes a minimal contribution of $3$ from $b+1$.

In the $\tau(k)=15$ case, both factorizations $(3,5)$ and $(5,3)$ were checked explicitly against the constraints $a\ge 1$ and $b\ge 2$, confirming that both yield valid exponent pairs and that neither violates divisibility by $2$ or $9$.

For $\tau(k)=17$, the crucial structural fact is that a number with prime divisor count must be a prime power, since any factorization of $17$ into integers greater than $1$ is impossible. This forces exponent $16$, contradicting the requirement that two distinct primes divide $k$.

## Alternative Approaches

An alternative method is to work entirely via the structure theorem for integers with few divisors: classify all integers with $\tau(n)\le 17$ by enumerating all exponent patterns $(e_1,\dots,e_r)$ with $\prod (e_i+1)\in{14,15,17}$, and then impose the constraints $v_2(k)\ge 1$ and $v_3(k)\ge 2$. This approach is more systematic but less direct than reducing immediately to the two-prime structure.
