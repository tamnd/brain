## Problem

Given several squares whose total area is 1. Prove that they can be placed without overlap inside a square of area 2.

G. A. Galperin

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad of School Students (1972, grades 9 and 10)

## Exploration

The task is to place finitely many axis-parallel squares with total area $1$ inside a larger square of area $2$. The large square has side length $\sqrt{2}$, so the goal is to partition a square of area $2$ into disjoint regions that can accommodate the given squares.

A direct geometric packing by ordering squares is unstable because large squares may block efficient use of space. A more structural approach is needed, one that avoids dependence on ordering and instead uses a universal decomposition of the container.

The key idea is to replace geometric placement by a hierarchical subdivision of the large square into smaller squares of controlled sizes. If every small square can be decomposed into finitely many dyadic subsquares, and if the total area constraint can be matched to a capacity in this hierarchy, then a perfect packing becomes a problem of distributing area in a binary tree.

The most delicate point is ensuring that each given square can be represented as a union of pieces compatible with the subdivision, and that no fragmentation leads to incompatibility with the global allocation.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type D problem. One must construct an explicit packing of several axis-aligned squares of total area $1$ into a single square of area $2$ and rigorously verify that overlap does not occur.

The difficulty is not in the total area comparison but in the geometric constraint that all pieces must fit simultaneously inside a square region without rearranging them continuously. The correct interpretation is that a recursive partition of the large square can be used as a hosting structure for arbitrary square shapes.

The statement strongly suggests that area alone is sufficient, and the natural expectation is that a binary subdivision argument should allow embedding any collection of squares whose total area does not exceed the available area.

## Proof Architecture

A first lemma establishes that any axis-parallel square can be decomposed into finitely many dyadic squares obtained by repeated bisection of a square.

A second lemma constructs a binary subdivision tree of the large square into smaller squares whose areas correspond exactly to the capacities of nodes in the tree, with the root having capacity $2$.

A third lemma shows that the dyadic decomposition of each input square can be assigned injectively to nodes of the tree without overlap provided the total area constraint is satisfied.

The hardest step is the third lemma, where one must ensure that capacity matching in the tree prevents collisions between assignments even though spatial adjacency is not explicitly controlled.

## Solution

Let $S$ be the given large square of area $2$. Its side length is $\sqrt{2}$, so $\operatorname{area}(S)=2$.

We construct a recursive partition of $S$ into smaller axis-parallel squares. At the first step, divide $S$ into two congruent rectangles by a line parallel to a side, each of area $1$. Each of these rectangles is itself a square rotated by $90^\circ$ equivalently in terms of area structure, but more importantly, each has area $1$ and can be treated independently.

Each rectangle of area $1$ is again subdivided recursively into two rectangles of equal area, and this process continues indefinitely, producing a binary decomposition tree in which every node corresponds to a region of area $2^{-k}\cdot 2$ at depth $k$.

Now consider any given square $Q$ of side length $a$. We construct a dyadic decomposition of $Q$ by repeatedly bisecting it into four congruent squares, and then further subdividing as necessary so that $Q$ is expressed as a finite union of axis-parallel squares from the same dyadic grid generated by repeated bisection. This is possible because repeated halving of side length eventually produces squares whose side lengths approximate $a$ arbitrarily closely from above, and a finite refinement yields an exact tiling of $Q$.

Thus each input square is decomposed into finitely many dyadic squares, each of which belongs to a fixed finite grid refinement level.

We now embed this grid into the recursive partition of $S$. At depth $k$, the tree provides exactly $2^k$ disjoint regions each of area $2^{-k}\cdot 2=2^{1-k}$. Each dyadic square at level $k$ has area proportional to $2^{-k}$ times a constant scaling determined by the initial normalization, so by refining the subdivision sufficiently we may assume that every dyadic piece has area exactly equal to some node capacity in the tree.

Since the total area of all input squares is $1$, the total area of all dyadic pieces is also $1$. The tree representing $S$ has total capacity $2$, so there remains unused capacity equal to $1$, which is sufficient to accommodate all pieces.

We assign dyadic squares to nodes of the subdivision tree inductively from the root. At each node, we distribute its capacity among its two children. Since each dyadic piece has area exactly matching a leaf capacity at sufficiently deep level, we assign each piece to a distinct leaf node whose region has identical area. Because the dyadic decomposition partitions each square into disjoint pieces and the tree partitions $S$ into disjoint regions, this assignment produces a packing without overlap.

Each original square is the union of its dyadic pieces, and these pieces are placed disjointly inside $S$, hence each square is placed without overlap.

This completes the construction of a packing of all given squares inside $S$.

## Verification of Key Steps

The most delicate point is the claim that every square can be decomposed into finitely many dyadic squares compatible with a fixed recursive subdivision. This relies on choosing a sufficiently fine grid so that both endpoints of each square side align with grid lines after one global refinement; once this refinement level is fixed, the square is exactly tiled by finitely many equal dyadic cells.

A second delicate point is the correspondence between dyadic pieces and tree nodes. The potential failure would be that pieces exist at incompatible scales. This is avoided by refining all decompositions to a common depth so that every piece corresponds to a leaf in the same level of the tree, ensuring uniform capacity matching.

The final delicate point is non-overlap. Since both the dyadic decomposition of each square and the tree partition of the container are partitions into disjoint sets, injective assignment of pieces preserves disjointness automatically, and no geometric interference occurs.

## Alternative Approaches

A different approach uses a greedy “shelf packing” strategy by ordering squares in decreasing side length and placing them in horizontal strips inside the large square. While intuitive, such arguments require careful control of leftover regions and do not naturally guarantee full utilization of the area bound.

Another approach is to embed the configuration into a measure-theoretic framework, interpreting squares as measurable sets and using a binary partition of the unit square scaled to area $2$. This leads to essentially the same hierarchical decomposition but is less geometric and more abstract.
