## Problem

Prove that any fraction $\dfrac mn$, where $0\lt\dfrac mn\lt1$, can be represented in the form $$ \dfrac mn=\dfrac1{q_1}+\dfrac1{q_2}+\dfrac1{q_3}+\ldots+\dfrac1{q_r}, $$ where $0 \lt q_1 \lt q_2 \lt \ldots \lt q_r$ are integers, and each $q_k$ ($k = 2$, 3, $\ldots$, $r$) is divisible by $q_{k-1}$.

## Exploration

The condition on the denominators is much stronger than in an ordinary Egyptian fraction expansion. We need a representation

$$\frac mn=\frac1{q_1}+\frac1{q_2}+\cdots+\frac1{q_r}, \qquad q_1<q_2<\cdots<q_r,$$

with

$$q_{k-1}\mid q_k.$$

It is natural to rewrite everything in terms of a single base denominator. If

$$q_k=q_1d_k,$$

then each $d_k$ is an integer and

$$1=d_1\mid d_2\mid \cdots \mid d_r.$$

Multiplying the identity by $q_1$ gives

$$\frac{mq_1}{n} = 1+\frac1{d_2}+\cdots+\frac1{d_r}.$$

Thus the problem resembles writing a rational number as a finite sum of reciprocals of integers forming a divisibility chain.

Trying examples suggests a useful identity:

$$\frac1a=\frac1{a+1}+\frac1{a(a+1)}.$$

Indeed,

$$\frac1{a+1}+\frac1{a(a+1)} = \frac{a+1}{a(a+1)} = \frac1a.$$

Moreover, $a+1$ divides $a(a+1)$. Hence a reciprocal can be replaced by two reciprocals whose denominators form a divisibility step.

Another observation is that if $N$ is any positive integer, then

$$\frac1N = \frac1{N(1+1)} +\frac1{N(1+1)(1+2)} +\cdots +\frac1{N(1+1)\cdots(1+t)} +\frac1{N(1+1)\cdots(1+t)}.$$

Checking small values reveals a telescoping pattern. Let

$$A_k=N(2\cdot3\cdots(k+1)).$$

Then

$$\frac1{A_{k-1}} = \frac1{A_k} +\frac{k}{A_k},$$

and since

$$\frac{k}{A_k} = \frac1{N(2\cdot3\cdots k)},$$

the terms telescope. This suggests that any fraction of the form $p/N$ can be expanded by repeatedly splitting the integer $p$ into a sum of reciprocals along a divisibility chain.

The delicate point is obtaining a representation for an arbitrary $\frac mn$. A promising route is to choose $q_1$ so that

$$1\le \frac{mq_1}{n}<2.$$

Then

$$\frac{mq_1}{n}=1+\frac an$$

for some integer $a<n$. If every fraction less than $1$ already has the desired representation, induction on the numerator $m$ becomes possible.

The step most likely to conceal an error is showing that the induction preserves the divisibility-chain condition when the representation of $\frac an$ is scaled by $q_1$.

## Problem Understanding

We must prove that every positive rational number strictly smaller than $1$ can be written as a finite sum of distinct unit fractions

$$\frac1{q_1},\frac1{q_2},\ldots,\frac1{q_r},$$

whose denominators increase strictly and satisfy

$$q_{k-1}\mid q_k \qquad (k=2,\ldots,r).$$

This is a Type B problem. The goal is a proof of existence for every fraction $\frac mn$ with $0<\frac mn<1$.

The core difficulty is not obtaining an Egyptian fraction expansion, but ensuring the much stronger divisibility condition between consecutive denominators.

The natural strategy is induction on the numerator. One first isolates a unit fraction $\frac1{q_1}$ so that the remainder has smaller numerator, then applies the induction hypothesis to the remainder and scales its denominators by $q_1$, which automatically preserves divisibility.

## Proof Architecture

Let $P(m)$ denote the statement that every fraction $\frac mn$ with $0<m<n$ admits the required representation.

The first lemma states that for every pair of integers $m,n$ with $0<m<n$, there exists an integer $q$ such that

$$1\le \frac{mq}{n}<2.$$

This follows by taking $q=\lceil n/m\rceil$.

The second lemma states that if

$$1\le \frac{mq}{n}<2,$$

then

$$\frac{mq}{n}=1+\frac an$$

for some integer $a$ satisfying $0\le a<m$.

This follows from writing $mq=n+a$ and using $(q-1)m<n$.

The induction step states that if $P(t)$ holds for every $t<m$, then $P(m)$ holds.

The idea is to apply the second lemma, represent $\frac an$ by induction, multiply all its denominators by $q$, and prepend the term $\frac1q$.

The hardest point is proving $a<m$, because that is what makes the induction descend.

## Solution

We prove the statement by induction on the numerator $m$.

For $m=1$, the fraction $\frac1n$ already has the required form, namely

$$\frac1n.$$

Thus the assertion holds for $m=1$.

Assume now that for some $m\ge2$ the assertion has already been proved for every fraction $\frac tn$ with $0<t<m$. We shall prove it for an arbitrary fraction $\frac mn$ satisfying $0<m<n$.

Let

$$q=\left\lceil \frac nm\right\rceil .$$

Then

$$q-1<\frac nm\le q.$$

Multiplying by $m$ gives

$$(q-1)m<n\le qm.$$

Hence

$$1\le \frac{mq}{n}<2.$$

Since $n\le mq<2n$, there exists an integer $a$ such that

$$mq=n+a, \qquad 0\le a<n.$$

We claim that $a<m$.

Indeed,

$$a=mq-n.$$

Using $(q-1)m<n$,

$$a=mq-n<mq-(q-1)m=m.$$

Therefore

$$0\le a<m.$$

Dividing the equality $mq=n+a$ by $nq$ yields

$$\frac mn=\frac1q+\frac a{nq}.$$

If $a=0$, then

$$\frac mn=\frac1q,$$

and we are done.

Assume $a>0$. Since $a<m$, the induction hypothesis applies to the fraction $\frac an$. Hence there exist integers

$$0<t_1<t_2<\cdots<t_s$$

such that

$$t_{i-1}\mid t_i \qquad (i=2,\ldots,s),$$

and

$$\frac an = \frac1{t_1}+\frac1{t_2}+\cdots+\frac1{t_s}.$$

Multiplying this identity by $\frac1q$ gives

$$\frac a{nq} = \frac1{qt_1}+\frac1{qt_2}+\cdots+\frac1{qt_s}.$$

Substituting into

$$\frac mn=\frac1q+\frac a{nq},$$

we obtain

$$\frac mn = \frac1q+\frac1{qt_1}+\frac1{qt_2}+\cdots+\frac1{qt_s}.$$

Since $t_1\ge2$, we have

$$q<qt_1.$$

Also,

$$qt_1<qt_2<\cdots<qt_s.$$

Furthermore,

$$q\mid qt_1,$$

and for $i\ge2$,

$$t_{i-1}\mid t_i \quad\Longrightarrow\quad qt_{i-1}\mid qt_i.$$

Thus the denominators

$$q,\ qt_1,\ qt_2,\ \ldots,\ qt_s$$

are strictly increasing and each divides the next.

Hence $\frac mn$ has the required representation.

By induction, the assertion holds for every fraction $\frac mn$ satisfying $0<\frac mn<1$.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is proving

$$a<m.$$

From

$$q=\left\lceil \frac nm\right\rceil$$

we obtain

$$(q-1)m<n.$$

Since $a=mq-n$,

$$a<mq-(q-1)m=m.$$

Without the strict inequality $(q-1)m<n$, one would obtain only $a\le m$, which would not guarantee progress in the induction.

The second delicate step is checking that the scaled denominators remain strictly increasing. The induction hypothesis gives

$$1\le t_1<t_2<\cdots<t_s.$$

Because $a<n$, the representation cannot consist of the single denominator $t_1=1$, so actually $t_1\ge2$. Hence

$$q<qt_1<qt_2<\cdots<qt_s.$$

If one forgot to exclude $t_1=1$, the first two denominators could coincide.

The third delicate step is preservation of divisibility. Multiplying every denominator by the same factor $q$ gives

$$t_{i-1}\mid t_i \Longrightarrow qt_{i-1}\mid qt_i.$$

In addition,

$$q\mid qt_1.$$

Thus every consecutive divisibility relation survives exactly as required.

## Alternative Approaches

A different proof can be built from the identity

$$\frac1a=\frac1{a+1}+\frac1{a(a+1)}.$$

Starting with the trivial representation

$$\frac mn=\underbrace{\frac1n+\cdots+\frac1n}_{m\text{ times}},$$

one repeatedly replaces equal summands by longer chains obtained from the identity above. Each replacement increases denominators and introduces divisibility relations. With a careful bookkeeping argument, the process terminates in a representation with distinct denominators forming a divisibility chain.

The inductive proof is preferable because it isolates a single unit fraction at each step, decreases the numerator strictly, and preserves the divisibility structure automatically by scaling. The descent parameter is explicit, making the termination and correctness transparent.
