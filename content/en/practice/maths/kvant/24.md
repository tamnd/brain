## Problem

Prove that any fraction $\dfrac mn$, where $0\lt\dfrac mn\lt1$, can be represented in the form $$\dfrac mn=\dfrac1{q_1}+\dfrac1{q_2}+\dfrac1{q_3}+\ldots+\dfrac1{q_r},$$, where $0 \lt q_1 \lt q_2 \lt \ldots \lt q_r$ are integers, and each $q_k$ ($k = 2$, 3, $\ldots$, $r$) is divisible by $q_{k-1}$.

## Exploration

The condition on the denominators is much stronger than in the usual Egyptian fraction problem. If

$$q_1\mid q_2\mid q_3\mid\cdots\mid q_r,$$

then after dividing by $q_1$ we obtain

$$\frac{m}{n} = \frac1{q_1} \left( 1+\frac1{a_2}+\frac1{a_2a_3}+\cdots \right),$$

where the $a_i$ are integers greater than $1$. This suggests that a recursive construction should be possible.

Trying small examples:

$$\frac23=\frac12+\frac16,$$

and $6$ is divisible by $2$.

Also,

$$\frac35=\frac12+\frac1{10}.$$

For

$$\frac47,$$

one gets

$$\frac47=\frac12+\frac1{14}.$$

These examples arise from taking the largest unit fraction not exceeding the given fraction.

Suppose

$$\frac{m}{n},\qquad 0<\frac{m}{n}<1.$$

Choose

$$q=\left\lceil\frac{n}{m}\right\rceil .$$

Then

$$\frac1q\le \frac{m}{n}<\frac1{q-1}.$$

Subtracting gives

$$\frac{m}{n}-\frac1q=\frac{mq-n}{nq}.$$

Because $q=\lceil n/m\rceil$, we have

$$0\le mq-n<m.$$

Hence the new numerator is strictly smaller than $m$.

This is the crucial point. If every remainder can be written in the required form with denominators beginning at some integer, then multiplying all those denominators by $q$ will preserve the divisibility chain and place the new terms after $1/q$.

The likely induction parameter is the numerator $m$. Since the new numerator is less than $m$, the process must terminate.

## Problem Understanding

We must prove that every rational number between $0$ and $1$ can be expressed as a finite sum of distinct unit fractions

$$\frac1{q_1}+\frac1{q_2}+\cdots+\frac1{q_r},$$

where

$$0<q_1<q_2<\cdots<q_r,$$

and each denominator divides the next:

$$q_{k-1}\mid q_k \qquad (k=2,\dots,r).$$

This is a Type B problem, a pure existence proof.

The core difficulty is showing that the divisibility condition can be maintained throughout a recursive decomposition. A natural greedy subtraction reduces the numerator, but one must verify that the representation of the remainder can be transformed so that every new denominator is divisible by the first chosen denominator.

## Proof Architecture

We prove the statement by induction on the numerator $m$.

The first lemma is that for $q=\lceil n/m\rceil$ one has

$$0\le mq-n<m.$$

This follows directly from the defining inequalities for the ceiling function.

The second lemma is that the remainder

$$\frac{m}{n}-\frac1q = \frac{mq-n}{nq}$$

has numerator strictly smaller than $m$.

This is an immediate consequence of the first lemma.

The third lemma is that if a fraction admits the required representation with denominators

$$t_1<t_2<\cdots<t_s,$$

satisfying

$$t_i\mid t_{i+1},$$

then multiplying all denominators by a fixed positive integer $q$ preserves these properties.

Divisibility is preserved because

$$qt_i\mid qt_{i+1}.$$

The hardest part is verifying that after adjoining the term $1/q$, the inequalities

$$q<qt_1<qt_2<\cdots$$

hold, ensuring a strictly increasing sequence.

## Solution

We proceed by induction on the numerator $m$.

For $m=1$, the fraction is

$$\frac1n,$$

which already has the required form, namely with $r=1$ and $q_1=n$.

Assume now that the statement has been proved for every fraction

$$\frac{a}{b}, \qquad 0<\frac{a}{b}<1,$$

whose numerator satisfies $a<m$, and consider a fraction

$$\frac{m}{n}, \qquad 0<\frac{m}{n}<1.$$

Let

$$q=\left\lceil\frac{n}{m}\right\rceil .$$

By the definition of the ceiling function,

$$q-1<\frac{n}{m}\le q.$$

Multiplying by $m$ gives

$$m(q-1)<n\le mq.$$

Hence

$$0\le mq-n<m.$$

Set

$$p=mq-n.$$

Then

$$0\le p<m,$$

and

$$\frac{m}{n} = \frac1q+\frac{p}{nq}.$$

If $p=0$, then

$$\frac{m}{n}=\frac1q,$$

and the proof is complete.

Assume $p>0$. Since $p<m$, the induction hypothesis applies to the fraction

$$\frac{p}{nq}.$$

Therefore there exist integers

$$0<t_1<t_2<\cdots<t_s$$

such that

$$t_i\mid t_{i+1}\qquad(i=1,\dots,s-1),$$

and

$$\frac{p}{nq} = \frac1{t_1}+\frac1{t_2}+\cdots+\frac1{t_s}.$$

Multiplying every denominator by $q$ yields

$$\frac{p}{nq} = \frac1{qt_1}+\frac1{qt_2}+\cdots+\frac1{qt_s}.$$
