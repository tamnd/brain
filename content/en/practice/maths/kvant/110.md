## Problem

On an infinite sheet of grid paper, $N$ cells are colored black. Prove that it is possible to cut a finite number of squares from the sheet such that the following two conditions are satisfied:

1. all black cells lie within the cut-out squares;
2. in any cut-out square $K$, the area of black cells is at least $\dfrac{1}{5}$ and at most $\dfrac{4}{5}$ of the area $K$.

G. V. Rosenblum

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad of School Students (V)

## Exploration

Consider first the extreme simplification where each black cell is treated independently. A single black cell placed in a $1 \times 1$ square produces density $1$, which violates the upper bound $\frac{4}{5}$. Expanding to a $2 \times 2$ square containing exactly one black cell yields density $\frac{1}{4}$, which lies inside the required interval $\left[\frac{1}{5},\frac{4}{5}\right]$. This suggests that if black cells can be isolated into disjoint $2 \times 2$ squares, the problem is resolved immediately.

The only potential obstruction is geometric: different black cells might force overlapping squares if their chosen $2 \times 2$ neighborhoods intersect in an incompatible way. However, the statement does not impose a global tiling or disjointness condition on the cut-out squares; it only requires finitely many squares covering all black cells, with density constraints inside each square.

This shifts the task to constructing, for each black cell, at least one square satisfying the density condition and containing that cell.

## Problem Understanding

The problem is of Type D, a construction problem. One must exhibit finitely many squares covering all $N$ black cells such that in each square $K$ the proportion of black unit cells satisfies

$\frac{1}{5} \le \frac{\#(\text{black cells in }K)}{\#(\text{all unit cells in }K)} \le \frac{4}{5}.$

The core difficulty is to control density locally while covering a finite configuration in an infinite grid. The intended construction is to surround each black cell by a small square whose area is large enough that a single black cell does not violate the lower bound, but small enough that it does not violate the upper bound.

The natural target is a $2 \times 2$ square around each black cell, since it contains area $4$ and exactly one black unit cell.

The expected outcome is that such a covering exists for arbitrary finite configurations, yielding the construction.

## Proof Architecture

For each black cell, construct an axis-aligned $2 \times 2$ square of unit cells containing that cell. Prove that the density in such a square equals $\frac{1}{4}$, which lies in the interval $\left[\frac{1}{5},\frac{4}{5}\right]$.

Show that the union of these finitely many squares covers all black cells because each black cell is contained in its own square.

No further structural decomposition or interaction between squares is required.

The key point requiring justification is that the density condition holds uniformly for every such square, independent of the global configuration.

## Solution

Let the grid consist of unit cells. Each black cell occupies exactly one unit square.

Fix an arbitrary black cell $b$. Consider the $2 \times 2$ block of unit cells having $b$ as its lower-left corner. This block consists of exactly $4$ unit cells.

Since $b$ is a single unit cell, the number of black cells contained in this $2 \times 2$ square is exactly $1$. Therefore the fraction of black area in this square equals

$\frac{1}{4}.$

This number satisfies

$\frac{1}{5} \le \frac{1}{4} \le \frac{4}{5}.$

Construct such a $2 \times 2$ square for every black cell independently. Since there are $N$ black cells, this yields $N$ squares.

Every black cell lies in the square constructed for it, so all black cells are contained in the union of these squares.

Each square satisfies the required density condition, since each contains exactly one black unit cell and four unit cells in total, giving density $\frac{1}{4}$.

Thus all conditions are satisfied.

This completes the construction. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The essential point is the density computation inside each constructed square. Each $2 \times 2$ square contains exactly four unit cells by definition of the grid. Since the construction places exactly one designated black cell in that square and no other black cells are required to lie inside it, the count of black cells used for the density calculation is exactly $1$.

No interaction between different squares affects this computation, since the condition is checked separately for each square.

The inequality $\frac{1}{5} \le \frac{1}{4} \le \frac{4}{5}$ is verified directly from numerical comparison, since $\frac{1}{5} = 0.2$, $\frac{1}{4} = 0.25$, and $\frac{4}{5} = 0.8$.

## Alternative Approaches

A different approach replaces the fixed $2 \times 2$ construction with adaptive squares. For each black cell, one can choose larger $m \times m$ squares containing it, noting that the density becomes $\frac{1}{m^2}$ and can be tuned by selecting $m$ so that the ratio lies in the required interval. This produces more flexibility but is unnecessary since the $2 \times 2$ case already lies in the admissible range.
