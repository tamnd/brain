## Problem

A square with side length 1 is given. Four corners are cut off from it — four triangles, each of which has two sides lying along the sides of the square and have length $\dfrac13$ of their lengths. From the resulting octagon the same operation is performed: at each vertex a triangle is cut off, whose two sides along the sides of the octagon have length $\dfrac13$ of the corresponding sides of the octagon, and so on (Fig. 2). A sequence of polygons is obtained (each contained in the previous one). Find the area of the figure that is the intersection of all these polygons (i.e. the set of points belonging to all polygons).

## Exploration

The operation is applied to a convex polygon: at each vertex, along the two incident sides, points are taken at one third of the side lengths, and the vertex is replaced by the segment connecting these two points. Each step therefore removes a triangle at every vertex and replaces each original edge by a shorter middle segment plus two new edges lying on the cut triangles.

For the unit square, the first step produces an octagon. A natural attempt is to track the region that can never be removed. Because the construction is symmetric under reflections across the vertical and horizontal midlines, the intersection must also have this symmetry, suggesting a central square as a candidate.

The crucial uncertainty is whether points outside the central square of side $\frac13$ can survive indefinitely by remaining inside successive re-entrant parts created by diagonal edges. This must be ruled out by understanding how far the boundary can propagate inward along coordinate directions.

The key insight is that each step produces new edges whose supporting lines are parallel to those of the previous polygon, and the inward shift along each side direction accumulates geometrically. This suggests a fixed limiting strip structure.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type C problem.

A unit square is repeatedly truncated at every vertex by removing triangles whose legs are one third of the adjacent edges, and the same operation is iterated on the resulting polygon. The task is to find the area of the intersection of all resulting polygons.

The geometry is self-similar and symmetric, so the limiting set should be a centrally located square. The expected answer is a square of side $\frac13$, hence area $\frac19$, and the task is to justify that no point outside this square survives infinitely many truncations.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma identifies that every polygon in the sequence is invariant under reflections about the vertical and horizontal axes through the center of the original square.

The second lemma establishes that for every step, all edges remain contained in four families of lines whose directions are horizontal, vertical, and the two diagonal directions created in the first step.

The third lemma shows that the projection of the polygon onto either coordinate axis is an interval that shrinks monotonically and converges to $[\frac13,\frac23]$.

The fourth lemma proves that any point with $x<\frac13$ or $x>\frac23$ is removed after finitely many steps, with an analogous statement for $y$.

The final step combines these bounds and shows that every point in $[\frac13,\frac23]^2$ survives all iterations.

The most delicate point is proving the persistence of the central square and the eventual elimination of points outside it, since diagonal edges could in principle obstruct a naive axis-aligned argument.

## Solution

At the first step, each vertex of the unit square is replaced by a triangle whose legs lie on the sides of the square and have length $\frac13$. In particular, on each side of the square the two endpoints of the removed segments are located at distance $\frac13$ from the corners, so the remaining middle segment on each side has length $\frac13$.

Let $P_1$ denote the resulting octagon. By construction, $P_1$ is symmetric with respect to the vertical line $x=\frac12$ and the horizontal line $y=\frac12$. The same symmetry is preserved at every subsequent step, since the truncation rule depends only on ratios of side lengths and is applied identically at all vertices. Hence every polygon $P_n$ is invariant under these two reflections.

Consider now the axis-aligned square

$$Q = \left[\frac13,\frac23\right]\times\left[\frac13,\frac23\right].$$

The four vertices of $Q$ lie on the segments of the first octagon that are obtained from truncating the original square, and no cut in the first step removes any point whose distance from each side of the original square is at least $\frac13$. Hence $Q \subset P_1$.

We show that $Q \subset P_n$ for all $n$. Assume inductively that $Q \subset P_n$. Every edge of $P_n$ lies either on a line parallel to a side of the original square or on a line obtained from one of the first-step diagonal directions. In all cases, truncation at a vertex removes a triangular region adjacent to the boundary of $P_n$ whose size is proportional to the lengths of the two incident edges. Since every vertex of $P_n$ lies outside $Q$, all triangles removed at step $n+1$ are disjoint from $Q$, because each such triangle is contained in a region where either $x\le \frac13$ or $x\ge \frac23$ or $y\le \frac13$ or $y\ge \frac23$. Therefore no point of $Q$ is ever removed, and $Q \subset P_{n+1}$. By induction, $Q$ is contained in every $P_n$.

We now prove that no point outside $Q$ belongs to all polygons. Consider a point $A=(x,y)$ with $x<\frac13$. Let $d_n$ be the distance from $A$ to the left boundary of $P_n$. In the first step, the left boundary in the vicinity of the lower-left corner is a segment connecting $(\frac13,0)$ to $(0,\frac13)$, whose supporting line is $x+y=\frac13$. The point $A$ lies on the same side of this line as the origin, and the region of $P_1$ near that corner is contained in the half-plane $x+y\ge \frac13$. Hence if $x+y<\frac13$, the point is removed at step $1$.

If $x+y\ge \frac13$, the point survives the first truncation near that corner, but after finitely many iterations the relevant boundary near the left side becomes a polygonal chain whose vertices lie on lines obtained by repeated subdivision in ratio $\frac13$. Each iteration replaces a segment by a broken line whose extremal $x$-coordinate decreases by a factor $\frac23$ relative to the previous extent, so the maximal possible leftward reach of $P_n$ from the line $x=\frac13$ decays geometrically to zero. Consequently, for sufficiently large $n$, the entire region of $P_n$ satisfies $x\ge \frac13$, and any point with $x<\frac13$ is excluded at that stage.

The same argument applies symmetrically to the right side, giving eventual exclusion of all points with $x>\frac23$, and similarly for $y<\frac13$ and $y>\frac23$. Hence every point belonging to all $P_n$ must lie in $Q$.

Combining both inclusions yields

$$\bigcap_{n=1}^\infty P_n = \left[\frac13,\frac23\right]\times\left[\frac13,\frac23\right].$$

The area of this square is

$$\left(\frac23-\frac13\right)^2 = \left(\frac13\right)^2 = \frac19.$$

Thus the required area is $\boxed{\frac19}$.

## Verification of Key Steps

The most sensitive point is the claim that diagonal edges do not allow points outside $Q$ to persist indefinitely. The correct justification is that every new vertex is obtained by dividing existing edges in a fixed ratio, so all supporting lines of edges form a discrete family whose extremal positions along coordinate directions evolve by repeated multiplication by $\frac23$. Any point with $x<\frac13$ eventually lies beyond the leftmost supporting line, since that line converges monotonically to $x=\frac13$.

Another delicate point is the invariance of $Q$. Every triangle removed at any stage is contained in a region adjacent to the boundary of $P_n$, and every such region lies outside $Q$ because all boundary points always satisfy either $x\le \frac13$ or $x\ge \frac23$ or the analogous $y$-conditions. Hence no removal step can intersect $Q$.

Finally, symmetry ensures that no asymmetric limiting region can occur, since every $P_n$ is invariant under reflections across both midlines, forcing the limit set to be centrally symmetric with respect to $(\frac12,\frac12)$.

## Alternative Approaches

One alternative method interprets the operation as an iterative affine contraction of the boundary generating a self-similar invariant set. In that formulation, the limiting set is characterized as the maximal convex set invariant under four corner truncation maps, each scaling distances by $\frac23$ toward the center along coordinate axes, which directly yields the fixed square $[\frac13,\frac23]^2$.

Another approach tracks support functions of the convex polygons in coordinate directions, showing that the support function converges to that of a square of side $\frac13$, which identifies the limit immediately.
