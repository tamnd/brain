## Problem

Prove that $N$ points on the plane can always be covered by several non-overlapping circles, the sum of whose diameters is less than $N$, and the distance between any two of which is greater than 1. (The distance between two circles is understood as the distance between their nearest points.)

V. I. Arnold

## Exploration

For $N=1$, a circle of diameter $0$ centered at the point covers it. The sum of diameters is $0<1$.

For $N=2$, if the points are farther than $1$ apart, two circles of diameter $0$ work. If their distance is at most $1$, one circle whose diameter equals that distance covers both points, and its diameter is $<2$.

The condition that the circles be pairwise at distance greater than $1$ suggests grouping nearby points together. A natural graph appears: join two points whenever their distance is at most $1$. Then points belonging to different connected components of this graph are separated by more than $1$. Indeed, if two components contained points at distance at most $1$, those points would be connected by an edge and the components would merge.

Thus each connected component may be treated independently. The problem becomes: given a connected component containing $m$ points, can we cover all its points by a single circle of diameter less than $m$?

A connected graph on $m$ vertices contains a spanning tree with $m-1$ edges. Every edge has length at most $1$. Hence the total length of the tree is at most $m-1$.

The crucial point is to relate the diameter of the point set to the length of such a tree. Take two points realizing the diameter of the component. In a tree there is a unique path between them. Its length is at most the total tree length, hence at most $m-1$. Therefore the diameter of the point set is at most $m-1$.

Once the diameter is bounded by $m-1$, a theorem of Jung is unnecessary. Any finite set of diameter $D$ can be covered by a circle of diameter $D$: choose a pair of points at distance $D$ and take the closed disk having that segment as diameter. This statement is false in general, so that route fails. For an equilateral triangle of side $D$, the third vertex lies outside that disk.

A different idea is needed. The diameter of the set is at most $m-1$. A classical fact says that every set of diameter $D$ is contained in a circle of radius at most $D/\sqrt3$, hence diameter at most $2D/\sqrt3<D$. This is Jung's theorem in the plane. Then the covering circle has diameter strictly less than $D$, and hence strictly less than $m$.

This seems to give exactly what is needed. The only non-elementary ingredient is Jung's theorem. Since the problem is due to Arnold, that is likely acceptable. The step most likely to hide an error is the passage from the connected component to the bound $D\le m-1$.

## Problem Understanding

We are given $N$ points in the plane. We must show that there exists a collection of pairwise non-overlapping circles covering all the points such that the distance between any two circles is greater than $1$, and the sum of all circle diameters is less than $N$.

This is a Type D problem, an existence statement. We must construct suitable circles and verify all required properties.

The core difficulty is obtaining a global bound on the sum of diameters. The separation condition suggests decomposing the point set into clusters connected by distances at most $1$. For a cluster containing $m$ points, connectivity forces its geometric diameter to be at most $m-1$. A covering circle can then be chosen with diameter strictly smaller than $m$. Summing over all clusters yields a total strictly smaller than $N$.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1. If we form a graph on the given points by joining pairs whose distance is at most $1$, then any two points belonging to different connected components are farther than $1$ apart. The reason is that a pair at distance at most $1$ would itself be an edge joining the components.

Lemma 2. If a connected component contains $m$ points, then the diameter of those $m$ points does not exceed $m-1$. A spanning tree has $m-1$ edges, each of length at most $1$, and the distance between any two vertices is bounded by the length of the unique tree path joining them.

Lemma 3. Any planar set of diameter $D$ is contained in a circle of diameter at most $2D/\sqrt3$. This is Jung's theorem for the plane.

Lemma 4. For a component with $m$ points, there exists a circle covering all its points whose diameter is strictly less than $m$. Combine Lemmas 2 and 3.

The hardest direction is proving the diameter bound for each component and converting it into the required global inequality. The lemma most likely to fail under scrutiny is Lemma 2, because one must justify carefully why the Euclidean distance between two vertices is bounded by the total length of the tree path.

## Solution

Construct a graph $G$ whose vertices are the given $N$ points. Two vertices are joined by an edge whenever the distance between the corresponding points is at most $1$.

Let the connected components of $G$ contain $m_1,m_2,\dots,m_k$ vertices. Then

$$m_1+m_2+\cdots+m_k=N.$$

Consider two distinct connected components. If there were points, one from each component, whose distance were at most $1$, those two points would be joined by an edge of $G$. The two components would then be connected to each other, which is impossible. Hence every point of one component is at distance greater than $1$ from every point of the other component.

Fix one component containing $m$ vertices. Let $D$ be the diameter of the corresponding set of points.

Choose a spanning tree $T$ of this component. The tree $T$ has exactly $m-1$ edges. Since every edge of $G$ joins points at distance at most $1$, every edge of $T$ has length at most $1$. Therefore the total length of all edges of $T$ is at most

$$m-1.$$

Take any two vertices of the component. In the tree $T$ there is a unique path joining them. The Euclidean distance between the corresponding points does not exceed the length of that path, by repeated application of the triangle inequality. Since the path length is at most the total length of the tree,

$$\text{distance between the two points}\le m-1.$$

Taking the maximum over all pairs of points in the component gives

$$D\le m-1.$$

Now apply Jung's theorem in the plane: every set of diameter $D$ is contained in a circle of diameter at most

$$\frac{2D}{\sqrt3}.$$

Hence our component is contained in a circle whose diameter $\delta$ satisfies

$$\delta\le \frac{2D}{\sqrt3} \le \frac{2(m-1)}{\sqrt3}.$$

Since $2/\sqrt3<1$,

$$\delta< m.$$

Thus each component containing $m_i$ points can be covered by a circle of diameter $\delta_i<m_i$.

Construct one such circle for each connected component. Every point is covered. Because points belonging to different components are mutually farther than $1$ apart, and each circle contains only points from a single component, the distance between any two of these circles is greater than $1$. In particular, the circles are non-overlapping.

Finally,

$$\sum_{i=1}^{k}\delta_i < \sum_{i=1}^{k}m_i = N.$$

The constructed circles satisfy all requirements. This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the claim that different connected components are separated by distances greater than $1$. Suppose two points from different components were at distance at most $1$. By the definition of the graph, they would be connected by an edge. That edge itself would join the two components, contradicting maximality of connected components. No additional geometric argument is needed.

The second delicate step is the estimate $D\le m-1$. Let $A$ and $B$ be arbitrary vertices of the component. In the spanning tree, the unique path from $A$ to $B$ consists of edges $e_1,\dots,e_r$. The triangle inequality gives

$$|AB| \le |e_1|+\cdots+|e_r|.$$

Each $|e_j|\le1$, and the path uses at most all $m-1$ tree edges, so

$$|AB| \le r \le m-1.$$

Taking the maximum over all pairs yields $D\le m-1$. A careless argument might incorrectly identify the Euclidean distance with the graph distance; only the inequality obtained from the triangle inequality is valid.

The third delicate step is the final summation. For a component of size $m_i$, Jung's theorem yields

$$\delta_i\le \frac{2(m_i-1)}{\sqrt3}<m_i.$$

The strict inequality is essential. Summing strict inequalities gives

$$\sum \delta_i<\sum m_i=N.$$

If one had only obtained $\delta_i\le m_i$, the conclusion would not follow.

## Alternative Approaches

Instead of citing Jung's theorem in its standard form, one may use the equivalent statement that every planar set of diameter $D$ is contained in a circle of radius at most $D/\sqrt3$. The remainder of the proof is unchanged.

Another route is to prove directly that a connected component with $m$ points is contained in a circle of diameter strictly less than $m$ by analyzing a minimal enclosing circle. If the boundary of the minimal circle is determined by two points, its diameter equals the diameter of the set and is at most $m-1$. If it is determined by three boundary points, the radius equals the circumradius of an acute triangle whose longest side is at most $m-1$, yielding a diameter at most $2(m-1)/\sqrt3$. This reproduces the planar case of Jung's theorem inside the proof. The main solution is preferable because it isolates the geometric fact in a standard theorem and leaves the combinatorial structure of the argument transparent.
