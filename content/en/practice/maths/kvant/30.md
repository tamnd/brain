## Problem

Prove that $N$ points in the plane can always be covered by several pairwise disjoint circles whose total sum of diameters is less than $N$ and such that the distance between any two of them is greater than 1. (The distance between two circles is understood to be the distance between their nearest points.)

V. I. Arnold

## Exploration

For $N=1$, a circle of diameter $0$ centered at the point works. For $N=2$, if the points are farther than $1$ apart, two circles of diameter $0$ suffice. If they are at distance at most $1$, one circle containing both points has diameter at most $1<2$.

The condition about distances between circles suggests grouping nearby points together. A natural graph appears: join two points whenever their distance is at most $1$. Connected components of this graph are separated from each other by distances greater than $1$, because otherwise an edge would connect the components.

Suppose one connected component contains $k$ points. Since the component is connected, it contains a spanning tree with $k-1$ edges, each of length at most $1$. If all points of the component could be covered by a circle whose diameter does not exceed the total length of those tree edges, then that diameter would be at most $k-1$.

This looks promising because summing over all components would give

$$\sum (k_i-1)=N-m,$$

where $m$ is the number of components. Since $m\ge 1$, the total would be at most $N-1<N$.

The crucial point is the geometric statement. Given a connected set of points joined by a tree whose total edge length is $L$, why must all vertices lie in a circle of diameter at most $L$? A stronger statement is true: any tree of total length $L$ has diameter at most $L$, where diameter means the largest distance between two vertices measured in the plane. Indeed, for any two vertices, the unique path in the tree has length at most $L$, and the straight-line distance between the vertices does not exceed the length of that path. Hence all vertices lie in a set of diameter at most $L$. Then a classical theorem of Jung is unnecessary; a much simpler fact suffices: every set of diameter $D$ is contained in a circle of diameter $D$. Take a pair of points realizing the diameter and use the disk having them as endpoints of a diameter.

That yields the desired estimate.

## Problem Understanding

We are given $N$ points in the plane. We must construct finitely many pairwise disjoint circles covering all the points. The distance between any two circles must be greater than $1$. The sum of the diameters of all circles must be strictly less than $N$.

This is a Type D problem. We must explicitly construct the circles and verify all required properties.

The core difficulty is obtaining a global bound $<N$ for the sum of diameters while simultaneously ensuring that distinct circles are separated by more than $1$.

The natural idea is to partition the points into clusters determined by the graph whose edges connect pairs of points at distance at most $1$. Different clusters are automatically separated by more than $1$, and a connected cluster of $k$ points can be covered by a circle of diameter at most $k-1$.

## Proof Architecture

Let $G$ be the graph on the given points in which two vertices are adjacent if their distance is at most $1$; connected components of $G$ determine the clusters.

Lemma 1. Any two points belonging to different connected components of $G$ are at distance greater than $1$; otherwise the corresponding edge would connect the components.

Lemma 2. If a connected component contains $k$ vertices, then there exists a tree on those vertices whose every edge has length at most $1$ and whose total edge length is at most $k-1$; take a spanning tree of the component.

Lemma 3. If a finite set of points is contained in a tree of total edge length $L$, then the diameter of the set is at most $L$; the Euclidean distance between two vertices does not exceed the length of the unique tree path joining them.

Lemma 4. Every finite set of diameter at most $D$ is contained in a circle of diameter $D$; choose two points at maximal distance and take the disk with that segment as a diameter.

The hardest step is Lemma 4, because one must justify that every point of the set lies in the chosen disk.

## Solution

Construct a graph $G$ whose vertices are the given $N$ points. Join two vertices by an edge whenever the distance between the corresponding points is at most $1$.

Let the connected components of $G$ contain $k_1,k_2,\dots,k_m$ vertices, where

$$k_1+k_2+\cdots+k_m=N.$$

By construction, if two points belong to different connected components, their distance is greater than $1$. Indeed, if some pair of points from different components were at distance at most $1$, the corresponding edge would join those components, contradicting their maximality.

Fix one connected component containing $k$ vertices. Choose a spanning tree $T$ of this component. Since every edge of the component has length at most $1$, every edge of $T$ has length at most $1$. Because a tree on $k$ vertices has $k-1$ edges, the total length of all edges of $T$ is at most

$$k-1.$$

Let this total length be $L$.

Consider any two vertices $A$ and $B$ of the component. In the tree $T$ there is a unique path joining them. The length of that path is at most $L$. The Euclidean distance $AB$ does not exceed the length of any polygonal path connecting $A$ and $B$, hence

$$AB\le L.$$

Thus every pair of vertices of the component is at distance at most $L$. The diameter of the set of vertices is therefore at most $L$.

We now show that every finite set of diameter at most $L$ is contained in a circle of diameter $L$.

Choose two points $P,Q$ of the set whose distance is maximal. Then $PQ\le L$. Let $M$ be the midpoint of $PQ$, and let $r=L/2$. Consider the circle centered at $M$ with radius $r$.

Take any point $X$ of the set. Since $PQ$ is a diameter of a circle of radius $PQ/2$, the angle $\angle PXQ$ is at most a right angle when $X$ lies on or inside that circle. Algebraically,

$$XP^2+XQ^2\le PQ^2$$

is equivalent to

$$MX^2\le \frac{PQ^2}{4},$$

hence every point whose distances from $P$ and $Q$ do not exceed $PQ$ lies in the disk with diameter $PQ$. Since $PQ$ is the diameter of the set, we have

$$XP\le PQ,\qquad XQ\le PQ,$$

so every point of the set lies in that disk. Because $PQ\le L$, the same set is contained in the concentric disk of radius $L/2$. Therefore it is contained in a circle of diameter $L$.

Applying this to our component, all its vertices are contained in a circle whose diameter does not exceed $L\le k-1$.

Perform this construction for every connected component. Let the resulting circles have diameters $d_1,\dots,d_m$. Then

$$d_i\le k_i-1,$$

and therefore

$$d_1+\cdots+d_m    \le (k_1-1)+\cdots+(k_m-1)    = N-m.$$

Since $m\ge 1$,

$$N-m\le N-1<N.$$

Hence the total sum of diameters is less than $N$.

It remains to verify the separation condition. Each circle contains only points from a single connected component. If two circles correspond to different components and had distance at most $1$, then some point of one circle and some point of the other would be at distance at most $1$, contradicting the fact that every pair of points from different components are farther than $1$ apart. Thus the distance between any two circles is greater than $1$.

We have constructed pairwise disjoint circles covering all $N$ points, with mutual distances greater than $1$ and total sum of diameters less than $N$.

$$\boxed{\text{The circles obtained from the connected components of }G\text{ satisfy all requirements.}}$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate point is the estimate for a connected component. A careless argument might claim that a connected component of $k$ points has diameter at most $k-1$ because adjacent points are at distance at most $1$. This is insufficient. The correct argument uses a spanning tree. Any path between two vertices consists of edges whose total length does not exceed the total length $L$ of the whole tree, and $L\le k-1$. The Euclidean distance between the endpoints is bounded by the path length, giving diameter at most $L$.

The second delicate point is the existence of a circle of diameter $L$ containing a set of diameter at most $L$. One must justify why choosing a diametral pair $P,Q$ works. For any point $X$ of the set, the inequalities $XP\le PQ$ and $XQ\le PQ$ imply that $X$ lies in the disk with diameter $PQ$. This follows from

$$XP^2+XQ^2=2MX^2+\frac{PQ^2}{2},$$

together with $XP^2+XQ^2\le 2PQ^2$. Hence

$$MX^2\le \frac{PQ^2}{4}.$$

Thus every point lies within distance $PQ/2$ of $M$.

The third delicate point is the separation of circles. The graph construction guarantees that every pair of points from different components are farther than $1$ apart. Since each circle contains only points from one component, the nearest points of two different circles cannot be at distance at most $1$; otherwise the corresponding component points would also be at distance at most $1$, producing an edge between components.

## Alternative Approaches

One can replace the spanning-tree argument by constructing, inside each connected component, a polygonal chain visiting all vertices. The total length of such a chain can be chosen at most $k-1$. The diameter of the component is then bounded by the length of the chain, and the rest of the proof proceeds as above.

Another route uses the minimal spanning tree of each component. Its total length is at most $k-1$ because every edge has length at most $1$. The diameter estimate then follows from the fact that the distance between two vertices is bounded by the length of the unique path in the tree. This is essentially the same geometric idea, but the spanning-tree formulation used in the main proof is simpler because no optimality property of the tree is needed.
