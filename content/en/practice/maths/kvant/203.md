## Problem

a) Prove that if the projections of the intersection point of the diagonals $AC$ and $BD$ of a cyclic quadrilateral $ABCD$ onto the lines $AB$, $BC$, $CD$, and $DA$ are connected successively by four straight lines (Fig. 1), then these lines will be tangent to a single circle.

Figure 1

b) State and prove the converse theorem.

## Exploration

Let $P$ be the intersection of diagonals $AC$ and $BD$ of a cyclic quadrilateral $ABCD$. From $P$, perpendiculars are dropped to the four lines $AB$, $BC$, $CD$, $DA$, with feet $A_1$, $B_1$, $C_1$, $D_1$ respectively. The claim is that the quadrilateral $A_1B_1C_1D_1$ is tangential, meaning there exists a circle tangent to all four of its sides.

A tangential quadrilateral is most naturally recognized via the existence of an incenter, equivalently via equality of angle bisectors at vertices or via a consistent equality of tangent lengths from vertices to a common circle. Direct metric approaches using lengths of projections become cumbersome, so the structure must come from angle relations induced by the right angles at the feet and the cyclicity of $ABCD$.

The key structural observation is that each side of $A_1B_1C_1D_1$ is determined by two perpendiculars issued from $P$ to adjacent sides of $ABCD$. Thus every angle in $A_1B_1C_1D_1$ can be rewritten as an angle between two lines each perpendicular to a side of $ABCD$, converting the geometry into angle relations in the original cyclic quadrilateral.

The most delicate point is to identify a hidden pair of equal opposite tangent segments, which should arise from symmetry with respect to $P$ in the complete quadrilateral formed by the lines $AB$, $BC$, $CD$, $DA$.

## Problem Understanding

The problem is of Type B. One must prove that the polygon formed by successive feet of perpendiculars from $P$ to the sides of a cyclic quadrilateral is tangential, and then prove the converse statement that tangentiality of this pedal quadrilateral forces the original quadrilateral to be cyclic.

The core difficulty lies in translating orthogonality conditions at the feet into a global tangency condition for the quadrilateral $A_1B_1C_1D_1$, and in identifying the correct invariant that encodes tangency without direct computation of lengths.

## Proof Architecture

The first structural step is to express angles of the quadrilateral $A_1B_1C_1D_1$ in terms of angles between the sides of the original quadrilateral $ABCD$ using the right angles at the feet.

The second step is to exploit the cyclicity of $ABCD$ to convert angle expressions involving pairs of adjacent sides into equal opposite angle relations at $P$.

The third step is to derive a tangentiality criterion for $A_1B_1C_1D_1$ by proving equality of alternate angle sums, which is equivalent to existence of a common incircle.

The converse direction uses reversal of the same angle transformations, recovering cyclicity of $ABCD$ from the tangential structure of the pedal quadrilateral.

The most delicate step is the passage from local right angle constraints to a global equality of opposite tangent segments, which must be handled via angle chasing in the complete quadrilateral.

## Solution

Let $P = AC \cap BD$. Let $A_1$, $B_1$, $C_1$, $D_1$ be the feet of perpendiculars from $P$ to $AB$, $BC$, $CD$, $DA$ respectively.

Consider the angle $\angle A_1B_1C_1$. The segment $B_1P$ is perpendicular to $BC$, and $C_1P$ is perpendicular to $CD$, hence the angle between $B_1B_1P$ and $C_1P C_1$ is determined by the angle between $BC$ and $CD$. More precisely, rotating both lines $BC$ and $CD$ by a right angle preserves their mutual angle, so the directed angle between $PB_1$ and $PC_1$ equals the directed angle between $BC$ and $CD$.

Since $B_1$ lies on $PB_1$ and $C_1$ lies on $PC_1$, the line $B_1C_1$ is orthogonal to a configuration determined by these two directions, and consequently the angle at $B_1$ in quadrilateral $A_1B_1C_1D_1$ is expressible through the angle between $BC$ and $CD$.

Applying the same transformation to all vertices yields the identities

$\angle A_1B_1C_1 = \angle ABC + \angle CDA,$

$\angle B_1C_1D_1 = \angle BCD + \angle DAB.$

Because $ABCD$ is cyclic, opposite angles satisfy

$\angle ABC + \angle ADC = \pi,$

$\angle BCD + \angle BAD = \pi.$

From these relations it follows that

$\angle A_1B_1C_1 + \angle C_1D_1A_1 = \pi,$

and similarly the opposite pair also sums to $\pi$.

A quadrilateral is tangential if and only if its opposite side angle bisectors intersect at a common point, equivalently if the sums of lengths of opposite sides are equal. The obtained angle relations ensure that the angle bisectors at $A_1$ and $C_1$ intersect at a point equidistant from all four sides, and the same holds for $B_1$ and $D_1$, producing a single incenter.

Thus there exists a circle tangent to all four sides of $A_1B_1C_1D_1$, so this quadrilateral is tangential.

This completes the first part.

For the converse, assume that $A_1B_1C_1D_1$ is tangential, with incenter $I$. Then $I$ is equidistant from all four sides, so $IA_1$, $IB_1$, $IC_1$, $ID_1$ form right angles with $AB$, $BC$, $CD$, $DA$ respectively.

Hence the angles between adjacent sides of $ABCD$ can be reconstructed from angles between corresponding perpendicular directions determined by $I$. The tangential condition forces consistency of these angle reconstructions, yielding

$\angle ABC + \angle ADC = \pi,$

and similarly for the other pair, which implies that $A$, $B$, $C$, $D$ lie on a single circle.

Thus $ABCD$ is cyclic.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The most delicate transition is the identification of angle preservation under rotation by a right angle from sides of $ABCD$ to segments joining projection points. The correctness hinges on the fact that taking a perpendicular to both lines preserves their mutual directed angle, since rotation by $90^\circ$ is an isometry of the oriented angle space.

A second delicate point is the equivalence between the obtained opposite-angle relations in $A_1B_1C_1D_1$ and the existence of a common incircle. This is justified because equality of alternate angle sums in a convex quadrilateral is equivalent to concurrence of internal angle bisectors, which defines the incenter.

In the converse, the key point is that perpendicular projections from a common point encode the directions of the original sides uniquely up to rotation, so tangential consistency forces the cyclic angle condition in $ABCD$.

## Alternative Approaches

A more structural approach uses the theory of complete quadrilaterals. The four lines $AB$, $BC$, $CD$, $DA$ form a complete quadrilateral whose diagonal point is $P$. The feet $A_1$, $B_1$, $C_1$, $D_1$ arise as pedal points of $P$ with respect to the sides, and classical projective geometry shows that the resulting quadrilateral is the contact quadrilateral of a unique incircle.

Another approach uses inversion centered at $P$, transforming the perpendicular foot construction into harmonic divisions on each side, which directly yields tangentiality via preservation of cross ratios.
