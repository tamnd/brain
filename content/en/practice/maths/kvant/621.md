## Problem

A $n$-gon is circumscribed about a circle. An arbitrary point $P$ inside the circle is connected to all its vertices and points of tangency. The resulting $2n$ triangles are colored alternately red and blue. Prove that the product of the areas of the red triangles is equal to the product of the areas of the blue triangles.

U. Alla

## Exploration

Label the vertices of the circumscribed $n$-gon by $V_1,\dots,V_n$ and the corresponding points of tangency by $T_1,\dots,T_n$, where $T_i$ lies on side $V_iV_{i+1}$ (indices modulo $n$). The $2n$ triangles are

$$PV_1T_1,\ PT_1V_2,\ PV_2T_2,\ PT_2V_3,\dots .$$

Because the coloring alternates around the boundary, every triangle of the form $PV_iT_i$ has one color and every triangle of the form $PT_iV_{i+1}$ has the other color.

Let the inscribed circle have center $O$ and radius $r$. Since $T_i$ is the tangency point on side $V_iV_{i+1}$, the line $V_iV_{i+1}$ is tangent to the circle at $T_i$. Hence $OT_i\perp V_iV_{i+1}$.

The area of $PV_iT_i$ equals

$$\frac12\,V_iT_i\cdot d(P,V_iV_{i+1}),$$

and the area of $PT_iV_{i+1}$ equals

$$\frac12\,T_iV_{i+1}\cdot d(P,V_iV_{i+1}).$$

The same distance factor appears in both triangles adjacent to $T_i$. Taking ratios gives

$$\frac{[PV_iT_i]}{[PT_iV_{i+1}]} =\frac{V_iT_i}{T_iV_{i+1}}.$$

Now a standard property of tangents from a common vertex yields

$$V_iT_{i-1}=V_iT_i.$$

Thus

$$\prod_{i=1}^n \frac{V_iT_i}{T_iV_{i+1}} = \frac{\prod_{i=1}^n V_iT_i} {\prod_{i=1}^n T_iV_{i+1}}.$$

After reindexing the denominator,

$$\prod_{i=1}^n T_iV_{i+1} = \prod_{i=1}^n V_iT_{i-1}.$$

By the tangent-length property, the numerator and denominator are equal. Hence the product of all these ratios is $1$, which is exactly the desired statement.

The only point that could conceal an error is the identification of the two color classes. Since the coloring alternates cyclically and the triangles appear in the order

$$PV_iT_i,\ PT_iV_{i+1},\ PV_{i+1}T_{i+1},$$

all triangles of the first form indeed receive one color and all of the second form the other.

## Problem Understanding

We are given an $n$-gon circumscribed about a circle. A point $P$ is chosen anywhere inside the circle. Joining $P$ to every vertex and every tangency point divides the polygon into $2n$ triangles. These triangles are colored alternately red and blue around the polygon.

The task is to prove that the product of the areas of all red triangles equals the product of the areas of all blue triangles.

This is a Type B problem, a pure proof.

The core difficulty is to relate areas of adjacent triangles to geometric data on the sides of the circumscribed polygon and then exploit the equal tangent lengths from each vertex.

## Proof Architecture

Let $A_i=[PV_iT_i]$ and $B_i=[PT_iV_{i+1}]$.

The first lemma is that all $A_i$ have one color and all $B_i$ have the other color. This follows from the cyclic order of the triangles and the alternating coloring.

The second lemma is

$$\frac{A_i}{B_i}=\frac{V_iT_i}{T_iV_{i+1}}.$$

Both triangles have bases on the same side $V_iV_{i+1}$ and hence have the same altitude from $P$.

The third lemma is

$$V_iT_i=V_iT_{i-1}.$$

This is the equality of tangent lengths drawn from the same vertex of a circumscribed polygon to the inscribed circle.

Multiplying the ratios from the second lemma and using the third lemma yields

$$\prod A_i=\prod B_i.$$

The step most likely to fail under scrutiny is the product simplification, because the denominator must be reindexed correctly before applying the tangent-length equality.

## Solution

Let the vertices of the circumscribed polygon be $V_1,\dots,V_n$, indexed cyclically, and let $T_i$ be the point at which the inscribed circle touches the side $V_iV_{i+1}$.

The triangles obtained after joining $P$ to all vertices and tangency points occur around the boundary in the order

$$PV_1T_1,\ PT_1V_2,\ PV_2T_2,\ PT_2V_3,\dots, PV_nT_n,\ PT_nV_1.$$

Since the coloring alternates, every triangle of the form $PV_iT_i$ has one color and every triangle of the form $PT_iV_{i+1}$ has the other color.

Denote

$$A_i=[PV_iT_i], \qquad B_i=[PT_iV_{i+1}],$$

where $[\cdot]$ denotes area.

Both triangles $PV_iT_i$ and $PT_iV_{i+1}$ have their bases on the same line $V_iV_{i+1}$. Their common altitude is the distance from $P$ to that line. Hence

$$A_i=\frac12\,V_iT_i\cdot h_i, \qquad B_i=\frac12\,T_iV_{i+1}\cdot h_i,$$

where $h_i=d(P,V_iV_{i+1})$.

Therefore

$$\frac{A_i}{B_i} = \frac{V_iT_i}{T_iV_{i+1}}.$$

Multiplying over all $i$ gives

$$\prod_{i=1}^{n}\frac{A_i}{B_i} = \frac{\prod_{i=1}^{n}V_iT_i} {\prod_{i=1}^{n}T_iV_{i+1}}.$$

Reindexing the denominator,

$$\prod_{i=1}^{n}T_iV_{i+1} = \prod_{i=1}^{n}V_iT_{i-1},$$

with indices taken modulo $n$.

For each vertex $V_i$, the segments $V_iT_{i-1}$ and $V_iT_i$ are tangent segments from the same external point $V_i$ to the inscribed circle. Hence

$$V_iT_{i-1}=V_iT_i.$$

Consequently,

$$\prod_{i=1}^{n}V_iT_i = \prod_{i=1}^{n}V_iT_{i-1}.$$

Substituting into the previous product yields

$$\prod_{i=1}^{n}\frac{A_i}{B_i}=1.$$

Thus

$$\prod_{i=1}^{n}A_i = \prod_{i=1}^{n}B_i.$$

Since the $A_i$ are exactly the triangles of one color and the $B_i$ are exactly the triangles of the other color, the product of the areas of the red triangles equals the product of the areas of the blue triangles.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

For the area ratio, write

$$A_i=\frac12(V_iT_i)h_i, \qquad B_i=\frac12(T_iV_{i+1})h_i.$$

The altitude $h_i$ is the distance from $P$ to the line containing the side $V_iV_{i+1}$. Both triangles use that same line as their base line. Replacing $h_i$ by different altitudes would invalidate the ratio.

For the tangent-length identity, the point $V_i$ lies outside the inscribed circle, and the two segments from $V_i$ to the tangency points on the adjacent sides are tangent to the same circle. The theorem on equal tangent segments gives

$$V_iT_{i-1}=V_iT_i.$$

Using any other pair of tangency points would not be justified.

For the product computation,

$$\prod_{i=1}^{n}T_iV_{i+1} = (T_1V_2)(T_2V_3)\cdots(T_nV_1).$$

Renaming the index $i+1$ as $i$ gives

$$(V_2T_1)(V_3T_2)\cdots(V_1T_n) = \prod_{i=1}^{n}V_iT_{i-1}.$$

This is the precise form needed to match the tangent-length relation at each vertex.

## Alternative Approaches

A projective or logarithmic approach is possible. Taking logarithms of the areas converts the desired equality into a statement that the alternating sum of the logarithms of the $2n$ areas is zero. Using the common-altitude relation on each side, this alternating sum reduces to

$$\sum_{i=1}^{n}\log(V_iT_i) - \sum_{i=1}^{n}\log(V_iT_{i-1}),$$

which vanishes because $V_iT_i=V_iT_{i-1}$ for every $i$.

The direct product argument is preferable because it works entirely with elementary area formulas and the standard tangent-length theorem, making the cancellation transparent and avoiding any auxiliary transformations.
