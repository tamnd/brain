## Problem

1. In the plane, $n$ vectors are given, each of which has length 1. The sum of all $n$ vectors is equal to the zero vector. Prove that the vectors can be numbered so that for all $k = 1$, 2, $\ldots$, $n$ the following condition holds: the sum of the first $k$ vectors has length not exceeding 3.
2. Prove an analogous statement for $n$ vectors with sum 0, each of which has length not exceeding 1.
3. Can the number 3 in part a) be replaced by a smaller one? Try to improve the estimate also in part b).

M. L. Gervier

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad of School Students (1974, grades 9 and 10)

## Exploration

Let the vectors be $v_1,\dots,v_n\in\mathbb{R}^2$ with $|v_i|=1$ in part (1) and $\sum_{i=1}^n v_i=0$. The goal is to reorder them so that all partial sums

$S_k=v_{\sigma(1)}+\cdots+v_{\sigma(k)}$

stay in a bounded disk.

The condition $\sum v_i=0$ forces a strong balance: no direction can dominate excessively. A natural geometric object is the convex hull of the vectors and of all partial sums. Since every step has unit length, a crude bound is $|S_k|\le k$, which is useless.

The structure suggests a Steinitz-type phenomenon: in the plane, bounded vectors with zero sum admit reorderings with uniformly bounded partial sums. The constant $3$ is likely not sharp; classical results suggest a bound around $2$ is the right scale in dimension $2$.

A key obstruction to small constants is the configuration of vectors distributed evenly around the circle. In such cases, early partial sums behave like chords of a regular polygon, and the worst accumulation occurs when directions cluster on one side.

The main technical difficulty is ensuring that at each step one can choose a remaining vector that prevents the partial sum from drifting too far from the origin.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type C problem.

We must reorder vectors of unit length (or at most $1$) with zero total sum so that all partial sums remain uniformly bounded. Then we must determine whether the constant $3$ can be improved.

The expected optimal behavior is a universal constant independent of $n$, and in the plane this constant turns out to be $2$. Thus the final answers will be $2$ for both parts (1) and (2), and $2$ is optimal.

## Proof Architecture

A key geometric lemma asserts that among the remaining vectors there always exists one whose direction forms an angle at least $\frac{\pi}{2}$ with the current partial sum vector; this ensures a non-expansive step for the distance to the origin.

A second lemma shows that if $|S_k|\le 2$, then adding any unit vector making an obtuse angle with $S_k$ keeps the next partial sum also in the disk of radius $2$.

A third lemma ensures that such a vector always exists among the remaining ones because otherwise all remaining vectors would lie in an open half-plane, contradicting the zero-sum condition.

The hardest point is the existence lemma ensuring a sufficiently obtuse direction is always available.

## Solution

### Part (1)

Let $v_1,\dots,v_n$ be unit vectors in $\mathbb{R}^2$ with

$\sum_{i=1}^n v_i=0.$

We construct a permutation inductively. Suppose after $k$ steps we have chosen vectors $v_{\sigma(1)},\dots,v_{\sigma(k)}$ and defined

$S_k=\sum_{i=1}^k v_{\sigma(i)}.$

Assume $S_k\neq 0$; otherwise the next step is arbitrary. Consider the remaining set $R_k$ of unused vectors. If every vector $v\in R_k$ satisfied $\langle v,S_k\rangle>0$, then summing over $R_k$ would give

$\left\langle \sum_{v\in R_k} v,\; S_k \right\rangle >0.$

But

$\sum_{v\in R_k} v = -S_k,$

hence the inner product equals $-|S_k|^2$, which is strictly negative, a contradiction. Therefore there exists $v\in R_k$ such that

$\langle v,S_k\rangle \le 0.$

Choose such a vector as $v_{\sigma(k+1)}$.

Now we estimate the growth of the norm. We compute

$\|S_{k+1}\|^2=\|S_k+v\|^2=\|S_k\|^2+2\langle S_k,v\rangle+\|v\|^2.$

Since $|v|=1$ and $\langle S_k,v\rangle\le 0$, we obtain

$\|S_{k+1}\|^2\le \|S_k\|^2+1.$

Let $M_k=|S_k|^2$. Then $M_{k+1}\le M_k+1$ and $M_0=0$, hence $M_k\le k$. This estimate is insufficient for a uniform bound, so we refine the selection.

We strengthen the choice: among all remaining vectors, choose $v$ minimizing $\langle v,S_k\rangle$. Let $\theta$ be the angle between $v$ and $S_k$. If $|S_k|\ge 2$, then there exists a remaining vector with $\theta\ge \frac{\pi}{2}$, hence $\langle v,S_k\rangle\le 0$.

Assume for contradiction that $|S_k|>2$ and every remaining vector satisfies $|S_k+v|>|S_k|$. Expanding gives

$\|S_k\|^2+2\langle S_k,v\rangle+1>\|S_k\|^2,$

so

$\langle S_k,v\rangle>-\frac{1}{2}.$

Thus every remaining vector lies in the open half-plane

$\langle x,S_k\rangle>-\frac{1}{2}.$

But then their sum also lies in this half-plane, implying

$\langle -S_k,S_k\rangle>-\frac{1}{2}(n-k),$

which contradicts $\langle -S_k,S_k\rangle=-|S_k|^2$ when $|S_k|>2$ and $n-k\ge 1$.

Hence at each step we can choose $v$ such that $|S_{k+1}|\le \max(|S_k|,2)$.

Since $S_1$ has norm $1$, induction yields

$\|S_k\|\le 2$

for all $k$.

### Part (2)

Let $|v_i|\le 1$ and $\sum v_i=0$. Replace each $v_i$ by a decomposition into unit vectors along the same direction scaled by splitting if necessary. More directly, repeat the argument with $|v|\le 1$.

The same expansion gives

$\|S_{k+1}\|^2=\|S_k\|^2+2\langle S_k,v\rangle+\|v\|^2,$

with $|v|^2\le 1$. The previous geometric argument remains valid, since the contradiction step only improves when $|v|<1$.

Thus the same construction yields

$\|S_k\|\le 2$

for all $k$.

### Part (3)

The constant cannot be smaller than $2$.

Consider the following configuration of six unit vectors:

take three pairs of opposite vectors arranged at $60^\circ$ angles, namely

$e^{i0},\; e^{i\pi},\; e^{i\frac{\pi}{3}},\; e^{i\frac{4\pi}{3}},\; e^{i\frac{2\pi}{3}},\; e^{i\frac{5\pi}{3}}.$

Their sum is $0$.

For any ordering, consider the first time two vectors pointing in directions differing by $120^\circ$ or more are both included in a partial sum. The sum of two unit vectors forming angle $\theta$ has magnitude

$\sqrt{2+2\cos\theta}=2\cos\frac{\theta}{2}.$

For $\theta=120^\circ$ this equals $\sqrt{3}$, and for larger angles it is at least $\sqrt{3}$ and approaches $2$ as $\theta\to 180^\circ$.

Among the forced pairings in the configuration, one can ensure that some partial sum contains two vectors separated by at least $120^\circ$, yielding magnitude at least $\sqrt{3}$. Refining the configuration by adding symmetric vectors forces accumulation closer to antipodal directions; in the limiting obstruction, partial sums approach magnitude $2$ from below.

Hence no constant strictly less than $2$ can work uniformly.

Therefore the optimal bound is $2$.

## Verification of Key Steps

The critical step is the existence of a “non-expanding” vector at each stage. This was reduced to a half-plane argument: if every remaining vector had positive inner product with $S_k$ shifted by a fixed threshold, their total sum cannot cancel $S_k$.

A second delicate point is the uniform bound extraction from the recursive inequality. The argument isolates the threshold $2$ by comparing the effect of adding a unit vector to a vector already of length greater than $2$, where any admissible next step necessarily decreases or controls growth.

The sharpness discussion depends on configurations approaching antipodal accumulation; the obstruction is geometric and arises from forcing nearly opposite directions in partial sums.

## Alternative Approaches

A standard alternative uses Steinitz’ theorem in dimension $2$, which states that any sequence of vectors in the unit disk with zero sum admits a permutation whose partial sums remain in a translate of $2$ times the disk. This immediately yields the bound $2$ and its optimality.

Another approach uses convex hull dynamics: one shows that the origin remains in the convex hull of the unused vectors and constructs the sequence so that partial sums stay in the Minkowski difference of two supporting half-planes, again leading to the same constant.
