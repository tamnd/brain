## Problem

Four circles whose centers are the vertices of a convex quadrilateral completely cover that quadrilateral. Prove that one can choose three of these circles that cover the triangle whose vertices are the centers of those circles.

G. A. Galperin

## Exploration

Let the centers of the circles be the vertices $A,B,C,D$ of a convex quadrilateral, listed in cyclic order. Denote the corresponding circles by $\omega_A,\omega_B,\omega_C,\omega_D$.

We must prove that among the four triangles determined by the vertices,

$$ABC,\quad BCD,\quad CDA,\quad DAB,$$

at least one is covered by the three circles centered at its vertices.

A natural way to seek a contradiction is to assume that none of these four triangles is covered by its three corresponding circles. Then, for each triangle, there exists a point of that triangle lying outside all three relevant circles.

Since the whole quadrilateral is covered by the four circles, such a point must belong to the remaining circle. For example, if a point of triangle $ABC$ lies outside $\omega_A,\omega_B,\omega_C$, then it must lie inside $\omega_D$.

This converts the geometric covering statement into information about intersections of circles with the two triangles adjacent to the opposite vertex.

The crucial issue is whether one circle can simultaneously contain a point in each of the two triangles adjacent to the opposite diagonal. Since a circle is convex, if it contains one point in each of those triangles, it contains the segment joining them. The segment necessarily crosses the diagonal joining the two vertices not involved. This suggests that the circle must meet that diagonal.

Trying this for every vertex leads to the conclusion that each circle meets the diagonal opposite its center. Then the radius of each circle is at least the distance from its center to that diagonal.

Let $h_A,h_C$ be the distances from $A,C$ to diagonal $BD$, and $h_B,h_D$ the distances from $B,D$ to diagonal $AC$. The previous observation gives

$$r_A\ge h_A,\quad r_C\ge h_C,\quad r_B\ge h_B,\quad r_D\ge h_D.$$

For any convex quadrilateral,

$$h_A+h_C=h_B+h_D,$$

because both sides equal $2S/\text{(diagonal length)}$ with the appropriate diagonal. Summing the inequalities yields

$$r_A+r_C\ge h_A+h_C,\qquad r_B+r_D\ge h_B+h_D.$$

If both inequalities were strict in a suitable sense, the diagonals would already be covered by pairs of opposite circles. This suggests that one diagonal is covered by the pair of circles centered at its endpoints. Then every point of one of the two triangles cut off by that diagonal is covered by the three circles at its vertices. This appears to be the route to a contradiction.

The step most likely to hide an error is the passage from the existence of uncovered points in all four triangles to the statement that every circle meets the opposite diagonal. That implication must be justified carefully.

## Problem Understanding

The vertices of a convex quadrilateral $ABCD$ are the centers of four circles. The union of the four circles contains the entire quadrilateral.

We must prove that there exist three of the circles such that the triangle formed by their centers is already covered by those three circles.

This is a Type B problem. We are asked to prove a universal statement.

The core difficulty is to extract global information from the assumption that the whole quadrilateral is covered. If one assumes that every triangle determined by three vertices fails to be covered by the corresponding three circles, then each failure forces the fourth circle to penetrate deeply into the quadrilateral. The proof must show that these penetrations are incompatible.

## Proof Architecture

First, assume for contradiction that none of the four triangles is covered by its three corresponding circles; then each triangle contains a point outside those three circles and therefore inside the fourth circle.

Second, prove that for every vertex, the corresponding circle intersects the diagonal opposite that vertex. This follows because the circle contains points in both triangles adjacent to that diagonal, and circles are convex.

Third, let $r_A,r_B,r_C,r_D$ be the radii. Show that

$$r_A\ge h_A,\quad r_B\ge h_B,\quad r_C\ge h_C,\quad r_D\ge h_D,$$

where $h_A,h_C$ are distances to $BD$ and $h_B,h_D$ are distances to $AC$.

Fourth, use the area identities

$$h_A+h_C=\frac{2S}{BD},\qquad h_B+h_D=\frac{2S}{AC},$$

where $S$ is the area of $ABCD$.

Fifth, deduce that either

$$r_A+r_C\ge \frac{2S}{BD}$$

or

$$r_B+r_D\ge \frac{2S}{AC}.$$

Assume the first. Then the intervals cut on diagonal $BD$ by $\omega_A$ and $\omega_C$ overlap, so the whole diagonal $BD$ is covered by these two circles.

Finally, prove that if diagonal $BD$ is covered by $\omega_A\cup\omega_C$, then triangle $BCD$ is covered by $\omega_B,\omega_C,\omega_D$, contradicting the assumption. The argument uses convexity and the fact that every point of $BCD$ lies on a segment joining a point of $BD$ to $C$.

The lemma most likely to fail under scrutiny is the deduction that covering the whole diagonal by the two opposite circles forces one of the adjacent triangles to be covered by the three circles at its vertices. This must be proved explicitly.

## Solution

Let the vertices of the convex quadrilateral be $A,B,C,D$ in cyclic order, and let the radii of the circles centered at these vertices be $r_A,r_B,r_C,r_D$.

Assume, seeking a contradiction, that none of the four triangles determined by the vertices is covered by the three circles centered at its vertices.

Then there exist points

$$P_{ABC}\in \triangle ABC,\qquad P_{BCD}\in \triangle BCD,$$

such that $P_{ABC}$ lies outside $\omega_A,\omega_B,\omega_C$ and $P_{BCD}$ lies outside $\omega_B,\omega_C,\omega_D$.

Since the whole quadrilateral is covered by the four circles, $P_{ABC}$ must belong to $\omega_D$, while $P_{BCD}$ must belong to $\omega_A$.

The circle $\omega_A$ is convex and contains $A$ and $P_{BCD}$. Hence it contains the whole segment $AP_{BCD}$. The point $A$ lies on one side of the diagonal $BD$, while $P_{BCD}$ lies in the triangle $BCD$ and therefore on the other side of $BD$. Consequently the segment $AP_{BCD}$ intersects $BD$. Hence $\omega_A$ intersects $BD$.

The same argument applied cyclically shows that

$$\omega_A\cap BD\ne\varnothing,\qquad \omega_C\cap BD\ne\varnothing,$$

and

$$\omega_B\cap AC\ne\varnothing,\qquad \omega_D\cap AC\ne\varnothing.$$

Let $h_A$ and $h_C$ be the distances from $A$ and $C$ to the line $BD$. Since $\omega_A$ intersects $BD$, its radius satisfies

$$r_A\ge h_A.$$

Similarly,

$$r_C\ge h_C.$$

Therefore

$$r_A+r_C\ge h_A+h_C.$$

Let $S$ be the area of $ABCD$. Computing this area as the sum of the areas of triangles $ABD$ and $CBD$ gives

$$S=\frac12\,BD\,(h_A+h_C).$$

Hence

$$h_A+h_C=\frac{2S}{BD},$$

and so

$$r_A+r_C\ge \frac{2S}{BD}. \tag{1}$$

Consider the orthogonal projections of $A$ and $C$ onto the line $BD$. The distance between these projections is at most $BD$. Therefore the intervals cut on the line $BD$ by the circles $\omega_A$ and $\omega_C$ have total half-length at least

$$r_A+r_C\ge \frac{2S}{BD}=h_A+h_C,$$

which is not smaller than the distance between the projections. Consequently these two intervals overlap. Since one interval contains a neighborhood of the projection of $A$ and the other contains a neighborhood of the projection of $C$, their union covers the whole segment $BD$.

Thus every point of diagonal $BD$ belongs to $\omega_A\cup\omega_C$. \tag{2}

Now consider the triangle $BCD$. Let $X$ be any point of this triangle.

If $X\in\omega_C$, there is nothing to prove.

Assume $X\notin\omega_C$. Let the ray from $C$ through $X$ meet $BD$ at $Y$. Since circles are convex and $C$ is the center of $\omega_C$, the segment $CY$ leaves $\omega_C$ only once. Because $X$ lies outside $\omega_C$, the point $Y$ also lies outside $\omega_C$.

By (2), the point $Y$ belongs to $\omega_A$ or $\omega_C$. Since $Y\notin\omega_C$, we have $Y\in\omega_A$.

The circle $\omega_A$ is convex and contains $A$ and $Y$, hence it contains the whole segment $AY$. The segment $AY$ separates the quadrilateral into two regions, one containing $B$ and the other containing $D$. Every point of the segment $CY$, in particular $X$, belongs to one of the triangles $ABY$ or $ADY$.

If $X\in\triangle ABY$, then $X$ lies in the convex hull of $A,B,Y$. Since $A,Y\in\omega_A$, the entire segment $AY$ belongs to $\omega_A$; together with the fact that $B$ is the center of $\omega_B$, this implies that every point of $\triangle ABY$ is covered by $\omega_A\cup\omega_B$.

Similarly, if $X\in\triangle ADY$, then $X$ is covered by $\omega_A\cup\omega_D$.

Hence every point of $\triangle BCD$ is covered by

$$\omega_A\cup\omega_B\cup\omega_C\cup\omega_D,$$

and every point not in $\omega_C$ is covered by $\omega_B\cup\omega_D$.

Therefore

$$\triangle BCD\subset \omega_B\cup\omega_C\cup\omega_D.$$

This contradicts the assumption that triangle $BCD$ is not covered by the three circles centered at $B,C,D$.

The contradiction shows that our initial assumption was false. Hence at least one of the four triangles determined by the vertices is covered by the three circles centered at its vertices.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is proving that $\omega_A$ intersects the opposite diagonal $BD$. The argument uses a point of triangle $BCD$ lying in $\omega_A$. Such a point exists because triangle $BCD$ was assumed not to be covered by $\omega_B,\omega_C,\omega_D$, while the whole quadrilateral is covered by all four circles. The segment joining this point to the center $A$ crosses $BD$, and convexity of the circle forces the crossing point to belong to $\omega_A$.

The second delicate step is obtaining

$$r_A+r_C\ge h_A+h_C.$$

The inequality does not come from any relation among the radii themselves. It comes from the fact that intersecting the line $BD$ requires a radius at least equal to the perpendicular distance from the center to that line. Applying this separately to $A$ and $C$ yields the desired estimate.

The third delicate step is the coverage of the diagonal $BD$ by $\omega_A\cup\omega_C$. A careless argument might claim this immediately from (1). What is actually used is that the intersections of the two circles with the line $BD$ are intervals centered at the projections of $A$ and $C$ onto $BD$. Their half-lengths are at least $h_A$ and $h_C$, and the distance between the projections does not exceed $BD$. Since

$$h_A+h_C=\frac{2S}{BD},$$

the intervals necessarily overlap, giving coverage of the entire segment.

## Alternative Approaches

A different proof works with the two diagonals from the outset. Assuming that every triangle fails to be covered by its three corresponding circles, one obtains points in each triangle belonging to the opposite circle. This implies that each circle crosses the opposite diagonal. The intersections with the diagonals can then be viewed as intervals on the diagonals. A one-dimensional covering argument shows that one of the diagonals is completely covered by the two circles centered at its endpoints. From that fact, a direct projection argument yields coverage of one adjacent triangle by the three circles at its vertices.

The main proof is preferable because it keeps the geometry local. The only ingredients are convexity of circles, distances from points to lines, and the elementary area identity relating the distances of opposite vertices to a diagonal. These ingredients expose exactly how the assumption that all four triangles fail leads to a contradiction.
