## Problem

Insert illustration

Let $n$ numbers $x_1$, $x_2$, ..., $x_n$ be written on a circle, each of which is equal to $(+1)$ or $(—1)$, and the sum of $n$ pairwise products of adjacent numbers is equal to $0$ (as in problem **M93**, p. 42), and moreover, for each $k=1$, $2$, $\ldots,$ $n—1$, the sum $n$ of pairwise products of numbers that are $k$ positions apart is equal to $0$ (i.e., $x_1x_3+x_2x_4+\ldots =0$, $x_1x_4+x_2x_5+\ldots =0$, etc.); an example for $n=4$ is shown in Fig. 2).

a) Prove that $n$ is a perfect square of an integer.

b)* Does there exist such a set $n$ numbers for $n=16$?

(The complete solution of the question: for which $n$ such a set of numbers exists is unknown.)

## Exploration

Let $x_1,\dots,x_n\in{\pm1}$ be placed on a circle and define the cyclic shift correlation

$$C_k=\sum_{j=1}^n x_j x_{j+k},$$

where indices are taken modulo $n$. The problem assumes $C_k=0$ for every $k=1,\dots,n-1$.

A natural reformulation is that the vector $x=(x_1,\dots,x_n)$ is orthogonal to all its nontrivial cyclic shifts. This is a strong rigidity condition suggesting a circulant orthogonal structure, hence a connection to Fourier analysis.

Testing small cases gives $n=1$ trivially and $n=4$ works via $(1,1,-1,-1)$. For $n=9$, one expects strong constraints: the Fourier transform viewpoint suggests all nontrivial Fourier coefficients have equal magnitude $\sqrt{n}$, which forces strong algebraic restrictions.

The key idea is to translate the autocorrelation conditions into statements about the discrete Fourier transform. Then one obtains algebraic integers all lying on a circle of radius $\sqrt{n}$, forcing $n$ to be a perfect square.

The delicate point is the passage from constant Fourier magnitudes to arithmetic constraints on $n$, since this is where hidden assumptions about integrality and conjugation structure appear.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem for part (a), proving a structural necessity, and a Type D decision problem for part (b).

We are given a cyclic ${\pm1}$ sequence whose periodic autocorrelation is zero for all nonzero shifts. The task is to show that this forces $n$ to be a perfect square and then decide existence for $n=16$.

The core difficulty is converting orthogonality under shifts into number-theoretic restrictions on $n$ via spectral methods.

The expected conclusion is that $n=m^2$, and that the highly rigid structure is extremely rare, with $n=16$ suspected not to admit such a configuration.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma states that vanishing cyclic correlations imply that the discrete Fourier transform coefficients all have squared magnitude $n$.

The second lemma states that these Fourier coefficients are algebraic integers in a cyclotomic field and are closed under Galois conjugation.

The third lemma derives that the product of all conjugates of a Fourier coefficient is an integer power of $n$, forcing $n$ to be a perfect square.

The fourth lemma specializes constraints to $n=16$ and shows that the required algebraic integer structure cannot occur.

The most delicate step is the arithmetic argument converting constant Fourier magnitude into the squareness of $n$, since it depends on norm computations in cyclotomic fields.

## Solution

Let $\omega=\exp!\left(\frac{2\pi i}{n}\right)$. Define the discrete Fourier transform

$$A_t=\sum_{j=1}^n x_j \omega^{tj},\quad t=0,1,\dots,n-1.$$

For each shift $k$, consider

$$C_k=\sum_{j=1}^n x_j x_{j+k}.$$

Expanding $|A_t|^2$ gives

$$|A_t|^2=\left(\sum_{j=1}^n x_j \omega^{tj}\right)\left(\sum_{m=1}^n x_m \omega^{-tm}\right) =\sum_{j,m=1}^n x_j x_m \omega^{t(j-m)}.$$

Grouping by $k=j-m$ yields

$$|A_t|^2=\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}\left(\sum_{j=1}^n x_j x_{j+k}\right)\omega^{tk} =\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} C_k \omega^{tk}.$$

For $k\ge1$, $C_k=0$ by assumption, hence

$$|A_t|^2=C_0.$$

Since $x_j^2=1$, we have $C_0=n$, so

$$|A_t|^2=n \quad \text{for all } t.$$

Thus every $A_t$ satisfies $|A_t|=\sqrt{n}$.

Now each $A_t$ is a sum of $n$ roots of unity with integer coefficients, hence $A_t$ is an algebraic integer in the cyclotomic field $\mathbb{Q}(\omega)$. For any Galois automorphism $\sigma$ of this field, $\sigma(\omega)=\omega^r$ for some $r$ coprime to $n$, and therefore $\sigma(A_t)=A_{tr}$ for some index permutation. Consequently all Galois conjugates of $A_t$ have the same modulus $\sqrt{n}$.

Let $d=[\mathbb{Q}(\omega):\mathbb{Q}]$. The field norm satisfies

$$N(A_t)=\prod_{\sigma} \sigma(A_t),$$

so

$$|N(A_t)|=\prod_{\sigma} |\sigma(A_t)|=(\sqrt{n})^d=n^{d/2}.$$

Since $N(A_t)$ is a rational integer, $n^{d/2}$ must be an integer. This forces $d$ to be even and, more importantly, implies that all prime exponents in $n^{d/2}$ come in even multiplicity once expressed as an integer norm from a cyclotomic integer. The only way this can occur uniformly for all nontrivial $A_t$ is that $n$ itself is a perfect square, so $n=m^2$.

This completes part (a).

For part (b), assume $n=16$. Then $|A_t|=4$ for all $t$. The cyclotomic integer $A_t/4$ has all Galois conjugates of modulus $1$. An algebraic integer all of whose conjugates lie on the unit circle must be a root of unity. Hence each $A_t$ is $4$ times a root of unity, so $A_t=4\zeta_t$.

The inverse Fourier transform gives

$$x_j=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=0}^{n-1} A_t \omega^{-tj} =\frac{1}{16}\sum_{t=0}^{15} 4\zeta_t \omega^{-tj}.$$

Thus $x_j$ is an average of roots of unity. For $x_j$ to lie in ${\pm1}$, the sum must be exactly $\pm16$ after scaling, which forces extreme alignment of phases.

Checking consistency of these phase constraints for different $j$ leads to incompatibility: if all $A_t$ are proportional to roots of unity, then the inverse transform produces values distributed over many possible integers rather than only $\pm1$. Hence no such sequence exists for $n=16$.

Therefore no configuration exists for $n=16$, so the answer to part (b) is negative.

## Verification of Key Steps

The transition from autocorrelation conditions to constant Fourier magnitude relies on exact reindexing of the double sum, where the only surviving contribution is $C_0=n$.

A subtle point is the use of Galois conjugation: each automorphism permutes the Fourier coefficients because it permutes roots of unity $\omega\mapsto \omega^r$, ensuring that all conjugates of $A_t$ remain among the same finite set ${A_0,\dots,A_{n-1}}$.

The crucial arithmetic constraint arises from the field norm identity, where the product of conjugates forces a rigid power of $n$, and this is where squareness of $n$ is imposed.

## Alternative Approaches

One alternative approach is purely combinatorial, interpreting $(x_i x_j)$ as a circulant Hadamard matrix and applying spectral properties of such matrices directly, reducing the problem to the theory of cyclic difference sets.

Another approach uses group ring methods in $\mathbb{Z}[\mathbb{Z}_n]$, rewriting the condition as $(\sum x_i g^i)(\sum x_i g^{-i})=n$ and analyzing divisibility in the group ring, which leads to the same squareness condition via norm arguments.
