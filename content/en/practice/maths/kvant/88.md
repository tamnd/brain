## Problem

What condition must the coefficients $a$, $b$, $c$ of the equation $x^3+ax^2+bx+c=0$ satisfy so that its three roots form an arithmetic progression?

M. F. Bezborodnikov

## Exploration

Let the roots of $x^3 + ax^2 + bx + c = 0$ form an arithmetic progression. Any three-term arithmetic progression can be written in the form $\alpha - d$, $\alpha$, $\alpha + d$, where $\alpha, d \in \mathbb{R}$ and $d$ may be zero.

Using Vieta’s formulas, the sum of the roots equals $-(a)$, the sum of pairwise products equals $b$, and the product equals $-c$.

From the root representation, the sum is $3\alpha$, so $\alpha$ is determined by $a$. Substituting this into the expressions for $b$ and $c$ should eliminate $\alpha$ and $d$ and produce a single algebraic relation between $a$, $b$, and $c$.

The main task is to eliminate $d^2$ consistently and ensure the resulting condition is both necessary and sufficient.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem. The goal is to determine all triples $(a,b,c)$ for which the roots of the cubic polynomial form an arithmetic progression.

The core difficulty lies in expressing the Vieta relations in terms of an arithmetic progression parametrization and eliminating the free parameters.

The expected result is a single algebraic constraint relating $a$, $b$, and $c$, which ensures that such a progression exists.

## Proof Architecture

The roots are written as $\alpha - d$, $\alpha$, $\alpha + d$, which parametrizes all arithmetic progressions.

The first lemma identifies $\alpha$ in terms of $a$ using the sum of roots.

The second lemma expresses $b$ in terms of $\alpha$ and $d^2$ via pairwise products.

The third lemma expresses $c$ in terms of $\alpha$ and $d^2$ via the product of roots.

The fourth step eliminates $d^2$ to obtain a relation involving only $a$, $b$, and $c$.

The most delicate step is the consistent elimination of $d^2$ without introducing extraneous constraints or losing equivalence.

## Solution

Let the roots of the cubic polynomial be $\alpha - d$, $\alpha$, and $\alpha + d$, where $d \in \mathbb{R}$.

By Vieta’s formulas, the sum of the roots equals $-a$, hence

$$(\alpha - d) + \alpha + (\alpha + d) = 3\alpha = -a.$$

It follows that

$$\alpha = -\frac{a}{3}.$$

The sum of pairwise products equals $b$. We compute

$$(\alpha - d)\alpha + (\alpha - d)(\alpha + d) + \alpha(\alpha + d).$$

Expanding each term gives

$$\alpha^2 - \alpha d + \alpha^2 - d^2 + \alpha^2 + \alpha d.$$

Combining yields

$$3\alpha^2 - d^2 = b.$$

Hence

$$d^2 = 3\alpha^2 - b.$$

The product of the roots equals $-c$, so

$$(\alpha - d)\alpha(\alpha + d) = \alpha(\alpha^2 - d^2) = -c.$$

Thus

$$\alpha^3 - \alpha d^2 = -c.$$

Substituting $d^2 = 3\alpha^2 - b$ gives

$$\alpha^3 - \alpha(3\alpha^2 - b) = -c.$$

Expanding,

$$\alpha^3 - 3\alpha^3 + \alpha b = -c,$$

which simplifies to

$$-2\alpha^3 + \alpha b = -c.$$

Hence

$$c = 2\alpha^3 - \alpha b.$$

Substituting $\alpha = -\frac{a}{3}$ gives

$$\alpha^3 = -\frac{a^3}{27}, \quad \alpha^2 = \frac{a^2}{9}.$$

Then

$$c = 2\left(-\frac{a^3}{27}\right) - \left(-\frac{a}{3}\right)b.$$

This simplifies to

$$c = -\frac{2a^3}{27} + \frac{ab}{3}.$$

Multiplying by $27$ yields

$$27c = -2a^3 + 9ab.$$

Rearranging,

$$2a^3 - 9ab - 27c = 0.$$

Thus the coefficients must satisfy

$$\boxed{2a^3 - 9ab - 27c = 0}.$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate point is the computation of the pairwise products. Each term was expanded directly from the roots $\alpha - d$, $\alpha$, $\alpha + d$, and cancellation of the linear terms in $d$ occurs exactly once in the sum, leaving $3\alpha^2 - d^2$ without omission or duplication.

The second delicate point is the elimination of $d^2$. The expression for $d^2$ comes uniquely from the relation $b = 3\alpha^2 - d^2$, ensuring no loss of equivalence when substituted into the product formula.

The third delicate point is the substitution $\alpha = -a/3$, which is forced by the linear Vieta relation and introduces no branching cases, since every arithmetic progression of three numbers admits a unique midpoint.

## Alternative Approaches

An alternative method uses the observation that three numbers form an arithmetic progression if and only if twice the middle root equals the sum of the other two roots, then expressing this condition directly in terms of symmetric polynomials of the roots. This leads to the same elimination of parameters but proceeds entirely within symmetric polynomial identities rather than explicit parametrization.

The parametrization method is preferable because it reduces the structure to two parameters $\alpha$ and $d$, making the elimination of constraints transparent and computationally direct.
