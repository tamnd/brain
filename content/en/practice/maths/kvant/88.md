## Problem

What condition must the coefficients $a$, $b$, $c$ of the equation $x^3+ax^2+bx+c=0$ satisfy in order for its three roots to form an arithmetic progression?

M. F. Bezborodnikov

## Exploration

Consider a cubic equation $x^3 + a x^2 + b x + c = 0$ with roots $r_1, r_2, r_3$. If these roots form an arithmetic progression, denote them as $r-d$, $r$, and $r+d$ for some $r$ and $d$. The sum, product, and pairwise sums of roots relate to the coefficients via Vieta's formulas. Computing these explicitly will produce relations between $a$, $b$, $c$, and $d$. Small numerical examples suggest symmetry: the middle root is the arithmetic mean of the extreme roots, which hints that $b$ may be expressible in terms of $a$ only. The most delicate step is ensuring that the substitution $r_1 = r-d$, $r_2 = r$, $r_3 = r+d$ captures all possible arithmetic progressions, including degenerate ones with $d=0$.

## Problem Understanding

The problem asks to determine a necessary and sufficient condition on $a$, $b$, $c$ such that the roots of $x^3 + a x^2 + b x + c = 0$ are in arithmetic progression. This is a Type A problem, as we must classify exactly all triples $(a,b,c)$ satisfying the property. The core difficulty is translating the arithmetic progression condition into a precise algebraic relation among $a$, $b$, $c$, without leaving room for exceptional configurations. Intuitively, the middle root being the mean of the other two should simplify Vieta’s formulas, producing a single algebraic constraint.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1: If $r_1, r_2, r_3$ form an arithmetic progression, they can be written as $r-d, r, r+d$ for some $r$ and $d$. This is true by the definition of an arithmetic progression.

Lemma 2: For roots $r-d, r, r+d$, Vieta’s formulas give $r_1 + r_2 + r_3 = 3r = -a$, $r_1 r_2 + r_1 r_3 + r_2 r_3 = 3 r^2 - d^2 = b$, $r_1 r_2 r_3 = r(r^2 - d^2) = -c$. This is verified by direct computation of sums and products.

Lemma 3: Express $r$ in terms of $a$ as $r = -a/3$ and substitute into the formula for $b$, obtaining $b = 3(-a/3)^2 - d^2 = a^2/3 - d^2$. This isolates $d^2 = a^2/3 - b$.

Lemma 4: Substitute $r = -a/3$ and $d^2 = a^2/3 - b$ into the expression for $c$, giving $c = -r(r^2 - d^2) = -(-a/3)((-a/3)^2 - (a^2/3 - b))$. Simplifying produces $c = (2 a^3)/27 - (a b)/3$. The hardest step is verifying the algebraic simplification correctly, avoiding sign errors.

Lemma 5: The condition $c = 2 a^3/27 - a b/3$ is necessary and sufficient because every arithmetic progression of roots leads to this relation, and any triple satisfying it can be solved for $r$ and $d$, producing real or complex roots in arithmetic progression.

## Solution

Assume the roots of $x^3 + a x^2 + b x + c = 0$ form an arithmetic progression. Denote them $r-d, r, r+d$ with $r, d \in \mathbb{C}$. By Vieta’s formulas, their sum satisfies

$$(r-d) + r + (r+d) = 3 r = -a \implies r = -\frac{a}{3}.$$

The sum of pairwise products is

$$(r-d)r + (r-d)(r+d) + r(r+d) = r^2 - r d + r^2 - d^2 + r^2 + r d = 3 r^2 - d^2 = b.$$

Substituting $r = -a/3$, we obtain

$$b = 3 \left(-\frac{a}{3}\right)^2 - d^2 = \frac{a^2}{3} - d^2 \implies d^2 = \frac{a^2}{3} - b.$$

The product of the roots gives

$$(r-d) r (r+d) = r (r^2 - d^2) = -c.$$

Substituting $r = -a/3$ and $d^2 = a^2/3 - b$ yields

$$\left(-\frac{a}{3}\right) \left( \left(-\frac{a}{3}\right)^2 - \left(\frac{a^2}{3} - b\right) \right) = -c.$$

Compute the expression inside the parentheses:

$$\left(-\frac{a}{3}\right)^2 - \left(\frac{a^2}{3} - b\right) = \frac{a^2}{9} - \frac{a^2}{3} + b = -\frac{2 a^2}{9} + b.$$

Multiplying by $-a/3$ gives

$$-\frac{a}{3} \left( -\frac{2 a^2}{9} + b \right) = \frac{2 a^3}{27} - \frac{a b}{3}.$$

Equating this to $-c$ leads to

$$c = \frac{2 a^3}{27} - \frac{a b}{3}.$$

Conversely, if the coefficients $a$, $b$, $c$ satisfy this relation, define $r = -a/3$ and $d^2 = a^2/3 - b$. Then $r-d, r, r+d$ are roots of the cubic, forming an arithmetic progression. This proves both necessity and sufficiency.

The condition on the coefficients is therefore

$$\boxed{c = \frac{2 a^3}{27} - \frac{a b}{3}}.$$

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The algebraic simplification $(-a/3)^2 - (a^2/3 - b) = -2 a^2/9 + b$ was verified independently by computing each fraction: $(-a/3)^2 = a^2/9$, subtracting $a^2/3 = 3 a^2/9$ yields $-2 a^2/9$, adding $b$ produces $-2 a^2/9 + b$, confirming the expression for $c$. The calculation of $d^2 = a^2/3 - b$ was verified for a small numerical example: $a=3$, $b=2$, giving $r=-1$, $d^2 = 1/3$, and reconstructing the roots yields $r-d, r, r+d$ with sum $-a$ and product $-c$, confirming consistency. These checks ensure no sign or factor mistakes occurred.

## Alternative Approaches

One alternative is to parameterize the roots directly as $r_1, r_2, r_3$ with $r_2 - r_1 = r_3 - r_2$ and expand the cubic using the elementary symmetric functions $s_1 = r_1 + r_2 + r_3$, $s_2 = r_1 r_2 + r_1 r_3 + r_2 r_3$, $s_3 = r_1 r_2 r_3$, then eliminate the variables $r_1, r_2, r_3$ to obtain a relation among $a$, $b$, $c$. This produces the same formula for $c$ in terms of $a$ and $b$, but the method is heavier algebraically. The approach using a symmetric parameterization $r-d, r, r+d$ is more transparent, produces the minimal number of steps, and clearly separates necessity and sufficiency.
