## Problem

1. A bucket contains 12 L of milk. Using only 5 L and 7 L containers, how can you divide the milk into two equal parts?
2. Solve the general problem: for which $a$ and $b$ is it possible to split $(a+b)$ L of milk in half using only $a$ L, $b$ L, and $(a+b)$ L containers?

V. V. Ushakov

## Exploration

For the specific problem, let the 12 L bucket initially contain all the milk, and let the 5 L and 7 L containers be empty.

A few trials show that filling the 7 L container from the bucket leaves 5 L in the bucket. Pouring from the 7 L container into the 5 L container fills the latter and leaves 2 L in the 7 L container. Emptying the 5 L container back into the bucket gives contents $(7,0,5)=(2,0,10)$ after the transfer. Filling the 5 L container again from the 2 L remaining in the 7 L container gives $(0,2,10)$. Pouring the 2 L into the bucket produces $(0,0,12)$ again, so that attempt merely returns to the start.

A more systematic view is needed. Let $x$ be the amount in the $a$-container. Since the total amount of milk is fixed and equal to $a+b$, the state is determined by the pair consisting of the amounts in the $a$- and $b$-containers. Every operation transfers milk until one container becomes empty or the other becomes full. This is exactly the same structure as the classical water-jug problem.

For $(a,b)=(5,7)$, the target is to obtain 6 L in one container and 6 L in another. Since neither auxiliary container has capacity 6, the only possibility is that one of the 5 L and 7 L containers contains 1 L or 5 L while the bucket contains 6 L. Trying to create 1 L is natural. Starting from $(0,0,12)$:

$$(0,7,5)\to(5,2,5)\to(0,2,10)\to(2,0,10)\to(2,7,3)$$

then

$$(5,4,3)\to(0,4,8)\to(4,0,8)\to(4,7,1)\to(5,6,1).$$

Now the 7 L container contains 6 L and the bucket contains 1 L. Pouring the 5 L from the small container into the bucket yields $(0,6,6)$, which solves part 1.

For the general problem, classical jug theory suggests that the attainable quantities are governed by $d=\gcd(a,b)$. Since every transfer changes amounts by multiples of $d$, every quantity appearing in any container must be a multiple of $d$. To split the total amount $a+b$ into equal halves, we need the quantity

$$\frac{a+b}{2}$$

to occur somewhere. Hence a necessary condition is

$$d\mid \frac{a+b}{2}.$$

Is it sufficient? Write $a=da_1$, $b=db_1$ with $\gcd(a_1,b_1)=1$. Then the condition becomes

$$2\mid (a_1+b_1).$$

Since coprime integers have the same parity only when both are odd, this means $a_1$ and $b_1$ must both be odd.

Testing examples supports this. For $(2,4)$, we have $d=2$ and $(a+b)/2=3$, not divisible by $2$, and a split is impossible. For $(3,5)$, $d=1$, and a split is possible. For $(6,10)$, $d=2$ and $(a+b)/2=8$, divisible by $2$, and scaling the $(3,5)$ construction suggests a solution.

The crucial point is proving sufficiency. One must show that whenever $d\mid (a+b)/2$, the amount $(a+b)/2$ can indeed be produced in one of the containers. The standard jug theorem gives every multiple of $d$ between $0$ and $a+b$ that is compatible with the capacity bounds, and here $(a+b)/2$ always fits in at least one of the auxiliary containers because it lies between $a$ and $b$ or equals one of them.

## Problem Understanding

We have three containers of capacities $a$, $b$, and $a+b$. Initially the largest container holds $a+b$ liters of milk and the other two are empty. Milk may be transferred between containers. The goal is to divide the milk into two equal parts.

This is a Type A problem. We must determine exactly for which pairs $(a,b)$ such a division is possible.

For the special case $(a,b)=(5,7)$, we must exhibit a sequence of transfers producing two portions of 6 L each.

For the general problem, the answer is that the division is possible exactly when

$$\gcd(a,b)\mid \frac{a+b}{2}.$$

Equivalently, after dividing $a$ and $b$ by their greatest common divisor, the resulting coprime numbers are both odd. The reason is that jug operations preserve divisibility by $\gcd(a,b)$, while the target quantity is $(a+b)/2$.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1. During every sequence of transfers, the amount of milk in each container is a multiple of $d=\gcd(a,b)$; this follows because all capacities are multiples of $d$ and every transfer changes amounts by multiples of $d$.

Lemma 2. If a split into two equal parts is achieved, then $d\mid (a+b)/2$; this follows from Lemma 1 because one of the equal parts has size $(a+b)/2$.

Lemma 3. In the three-container system, every multiple of $d$ between $0$ and $\max(a,b)$ can be produced in one of the two smaller containers; this is the standard Bézout property of the jug problem.

Lemma 4. If $d\mid (a+b)/2$, then the quantity $(a+b)/2$ can be produced in one of the two smaller containers; this follows from Lemma 3 and the fact that $(a+b)/2\le \max(a,b)$.

The hardest direction is sufficiency. The most delicate point is Lemma 3, because it must justify that the desired multiple of $d$ can actually be obtained by transfers.

## Solution

For the first part, start with

$$(0,0,12),$$

where the entries denote the amounts in the 5 L, 7 L, and 12 L containers.

Perform the following transfers:

$$(0,0,12)\to(0,7,5)\to(5,2,5)\to(0,2,10)\to(2,0,10)$$

$$\to(2,7,3)\to(5,4,3)\to(0,4,8)\to(4,0,8)$$

$$\to(4,7,1)\to(5,6,1).$$

Finally pour the 5 L from the first container into the bucket:

$$(5,6,1)\to(0,6,6).$$

The milk has been divided into two equal parts of 6 L.

Now consider arbitrary positive integers $a$ and $b$, and let

$$d=\gcd(a,b).$$

We determine exactly when a division into two equal parts is possible.

First, we prove necessity.

Every container capacity is a multiple of $d$. Initially the amounts are

$$0,\quad 0,\quad a+b,$$

all multiples of $d$.

Suppose at some stage all amounts are multiples of $d$. During a transfer, milk moves until either the source becomes empty or the destination becomes full. The transferred quantity is the difference between two multiples of $d$, hence is itself a multiple of $d$. Consequently the new amounts are again multiples of $d$.

By induction over the sequence of transfers, every amount occurring in every container is always a multiple of $d$.

If the milk is divided into two equal parts, each part equals

$$\frac{a+b}{2}.$$

Since this quantity must occur as the amount of milk in some container, it must be a multiple of $d$. Hence

$$d\mid \frac{a+b}{2}.$$

This proves necessity.

Next, we prove sufficiency.

Assume that

$$d\mid \frac{a+b}{2}.$$

Without loss of generality let $a\le b$. Then

$$\frac{a+b}{2}\le b.$$

In the classical jug process using capacities $a$ and $b$, starting with the $b$-container full and repeatedly pouring from one jug into the other, the amounts obtained in the $b$-container are precisely the residues

$$0,d,2d,\ldots,b,$$

taken modulo $b$. Since $\gcd(a,b)=d$, Bézout's theorem implies that every multiple of $d$ between $0$ and $b$ appears during this process.

Because $(a+b)/2$ is a multiple of $d$ and satisfies

$$0\le \frac{a+b}{2}\le b,$$

there exists a reachable state in which one of the two smaller containers contains exactly

$$\frac{a+b}{2}$$

liters.

The total amount of milk is always $a+b$, so the remaining milk equals

$$(a+b)-\frac{a+b}{2} = \frac{a+b}{2}.$$

Thus the milk is divided into two equal parts.

We have shown that the division is possible whenever

$$d\mid \frac{a+b}{2},$$

and impossible otherwise.

Writing

$$a=da_1,\qquad b=db_1,\qquad \gcd(a_1,b_1)=1,$$

the condition becomes

$$2\mid(a_1+b_1).$$

Since $a_1$ and $b_1$ are coprime, this is equivalent to both $a_1$ and $b_1$ being odd.

Hence the complete answer is

$$\boxed{\text{The division is possible exactly when }\gcd(a,b)\mid\frac{a+b}{2}.}$$

Equivalently,

$$\boxed{\frac{a}{\gcd(a,b)}\text{ and }\frac{b}{\gcd(a,b)}\text{ are both odd}.}$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the divisibility invariant. A careless argument might claim only that the total amount of milk is fixed. That is insufficient. The actual invariant is stronger: every individual container always contains a multiple of $d$. The induction works because every transfer amount is determined by reaching either capacity or emptiness, both of which involve differences of multiples of $d$.

The second delicate step is the claim that every multiple of $d$ up to $b$ is reachable. For $\gcd(a,b)=d$, the successive amounts obtained by repeatedly pouring correspond to the sequence

$$0,a,2a,3a,\ldots \pmod b.$$

Since $a/d$ and $b/d$ are coprime, these residues run through all multiples of $d$ modulo $b$. Hence no admissible multiple of $d$ is missed.

The third delicate step is verifying that the target quantity lies within the capacity range of a smaller container. Assuming $a\le b$,

$$\frac{a+b}{2}\le b.$$

Thus the target amount can indeed be stored in the $b$-container. Without this check, the reachability statement would not automatically imply a valid configuration.

## Alternative Approaches

A different proof models the state by the amount $x$ in the $a$-container. Every pouring operation changes $x$ by adding or subtracting $a$ modulo $b$. The set of reachable values is then the cyclic subgroup of $\mathbb Z/b\mathbb Z$ generated by $a$, which consists precisely of the multiples of $\gcd(a,b)$. The target state exists exactly when $(a+b)/2$ belongs to this subgroup, yielding the same criterion.

Another approach uses Bézout's identity directly. If

$$d=\gcd(a,b)$$

and

$$\frac{a+b}{2}=kd,$$

then there exist integers $m,n$ with

$$ma+nb=d.$$

Multiplying by $k$ gives an expression for the target amount as an integer combination of $a$ and $b$. The standard jug construction realizing Bézout combinations then produces exactly $(a+b)/2$ liters in one container. The invariant-based proof is preferable because it establishes necessity and sufficiency within the same framework and makes the role of the greatest common divisor transparent.
