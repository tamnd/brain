## Problem

In a trihedral angle, the bisectors of the plane angles are constructed. Prove that the pairwise angles between these three bisectors are all obtuse, or all acute, or all right angles.

E. G. Gotman

## Exploration

Let $ \vec{a}, \vec{b}, \vec{c}$ be nonzero vectors along the three edges of the trihedral angle, emanating from the vertex. Each plane angle is formed by a pair of these vectors, so the three plane angles are determined by the pairs $(\vec{a},\vec{b})$, $(\vec{b},\vec{c})$, and $(\vec{c},\vec{a})$.

The bisector of the angle between two unit vectors is naturally directed along their sum. Thus the bisectors in the three faces are directed along $\vec{a}+\vec{b}$, $\vec{b}+\vec{c}$, and $\vec{c}+\vec{a}$.

The goal reduces to understanding the angles between these three vectors in space. Computing their mutual scalar products suggests strong symmetry, and the expression $(\vec{a}+\vec{b})\cdot(\vec{b}+\vec{c})$ expands into terms involving pairwise scalar products of $\vec{a},\vec{b},\vec{c}$ plus a constant term coming from unit lengths.

The key structural question is whether these scalar products are equal for all pairs of bisectors, and how their sign controls whether the angles are acute, right, or obtuse.

## Problem Understanding

The problem asks to prove that the three angles formed pairwise by the bisectors of the plane angles of a trihedral angle are either all acute, all right, or all obtuse.

This is a Type B statement. The core difficulty lies in translating a spatial geometric configuration into a symmetric algebraic structure and showing that all pairwise angles between the bisectors are governed by a single scalar expression.

## Proof Architecture

Let $\vec{a}, \vec{b}, \vec{c}$ be unit vectors along the edges of the trihedral angle. The bisectors are represented by $\vec{u}=\vec{a}+\vec{b}$, $\vec{v}=\vec{b}+\vec{c}$, and $\vec{w}=\vec{c}+\vec{a}$.

The first lemma is that each plane angle bisector is directed along the sum of the corresponding unit edge vectors, since the bisector in a plane angle between unit vectors bisects the angle symmetrically.

The second lemma is that the scalar products $\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v}$, $\vec{v}\cdot\vec{w}$, and $\vec{w}\cdot\vec{u}$ are all equal and have the same expression in terms of pairwise scalar products of $\vec{a},\vec{b},\vec{c}$.

The third lemma is that the sign of this common scalar product determines whether all pairwise angles between bisectors are acute, right, or obtuse.

The most delicate step is establishing that all three scalar products coincide exactly.

## Solution

Let $\vec{a}, \vec{b}, \vec{c}$ be unit vectors directed along the edges of the trihedral angle from the vertex, so the plane angles of the trihedral angle are the angles between the pairs $(\vec{a},\vec{b})$, $(\vec{b},\vec{c})$, and $(\vec{c},\vec{a})$.

The bisector of the angle between two unit vectors $\vec{a}$ and $\vec{b}$ in their plane is directed along $\vec{a}+\vec{b}$. Indeed, any vector proportional to $\vec{a}+\vec{b}$ lies in the plane spanned by $\vec{a},\vec{b}$ and has equal scalar products with $\vec{a}$ and $\vec{b}$, since

$$(\vec{a}+\vec{b})\cdot \vec{a} = 1 + \vec{a}\cdot\vec{b}, \quad (\vec{a}+\vec{b})\cdot \vec{b} = 1 + \vec{a}\cdot\vec{b}.$$

Thus the bisectors of the three plane angles are directed along

$$\vec{u}=\vec{a}+\vec{b}, \quad \vec{v}=\vec{b}+\vec{c}, \quad \vec{w}=\vec{c}+\vec{a}.$$

We compute the scalar product of two bisectors:

$$\vec{u}\cdot \vec{v} = (\vec{a}+\vec{b})\cdot(\vec{b}+\vec{c}).$$

Expanding,

$$\vec{u}\cdot \vec{v} = \vec{a}\cdot\vec{b} + \vec{a}\cdot\vec{c} + \vec{b}\cdot\vec{b} + \vec{b}\cdot\vec{c}.$$

Since $\vec{b}$ is a unit vector, $\vec{b}\cdot\vec{b}=1$, hence

$$\vec{u}\cdot \vec{v} = 1 + \vec{a}\cdot\vec{b} + \vec{b}\cdot\vec{c} + \vec{c}\cdot\vec{a}.$$

Similarly,

$$\vec{v}\cdot \vec{w} = (\vec{b}+\vec{c})\cdot(\vec{c}+\vec{a}) = \vec{b}\cdot\vec{c} + \vec{b}\cdot\vec{a} + \vec{c}\cdot\vec{c} + \vec{c}\cdot\vec{a},$$

so

$$\vec{v}\cdot \vec{w} = 1 + \vec{a}\cdot\vec{b} + \vec{b}\cdot\vec{c} + \vec{c}\cdot\vec{a}.$$

Finally,

$$\vec{w}\cdot \vec{u} = (\vec{c}+\vec{a})\cdot(\vec{a}+\vec{b}) = \vec{c}\cdot\vec{a} + \vec{c}\cdot\vec{b} + \vec{a}\cdot\vec{a} + \vec{a}\cdot\vec{b},$$

hence

$$\vec{w}\cdot \vec{u} = 1 + \vec{a}\cdot\vec{b} + \vec{b}\cdot\vec{c} + \vec{c}\cdot\vec{a}.$$

All three scalar products are equal; denote their common value by

$$S = 1 + \vec{a}\cdot\vec{b} + \vec{b}\cdot\vec{c} + \vec{c}\cdot\vec{a}.$$

The angle $\theta$ between any two bisectors satisfies

$$\cos\theta = \frac{S}{|\vec{u}||\vec{v}|}.$$

The denominators are positive, so the sign of $\cos\theta$ is determined by the sign of $S$. Since the same expression governs every pair of bisectors, all three angles share the same sign of cosine.

If $S>0$, all three angles are acute. If $S=0$, all three are right angles. If $S<0$, all three are obtuse angles.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The crucial structural point is that each bisector direction is exactly a sum of the two defining unit vectors. This relies on the characterization of angle bisectors via equal scalar products: a vector $\vec{x}$ lies on the bisector of the angle between unit vectors $\vec{a}$ and $\vec{b}$ precisely when $\vec{x}\cdot\vec{a}=\vec{x}\cdot\vec{b}$, which holds for $\vec{x}=\vec{a}+\vec{b}$ by direct computation.

The second delicate point is the coincidence of the three scalar products. Each expansion contains exactly the same set of pairwise products $\vec{a}\cdot\vec{b}$, $\vec{b}\cdot\vec{c}$, $\vec{c}\cdot\vec{a}$ together with a single unit term coming from squaring one vector in each product, guaranteeing identical expressions.

The final point is the reduction of angle type to the sign of a single scalar expression, which follows from positivity of norms in the cosine formula.

## Alternative Approaches

A geometric alternative uses the unit sphere centered at the vertex. The three edge directions define three points on the sphere, and the bisectors correspond to midpoints of the spherical arcs between these points. The statement then becomes a property of a triangle on the sphere whose edge midpoints form a triangle with equi-sign inner products. This approach is more geometric but ultimately reduces to the same vector identity, while the vector method is more direct and less dependent on spherical geometry machinery.
