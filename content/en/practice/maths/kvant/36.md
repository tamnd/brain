## Problem

Prove that it is impossible to arrange seven lines and seven points in the plane so that each point lies on exactly three lines and each line contains exactly three points.

## Exploration

The configuration describes 7 points and 7 lines with each point incident to exactly 3 lines and each line containing exactly 3 points. Counting incidences gives $7\cdot 3=21$, so the total number of point-line incidences is consistent from both sides.

Each line contains 3 points, so every pair of points on a line determines that line. Conversely, each point lies on 3 lines, so every pair of lines through a point is accounted for exactly once. Hence every pair of distinct lines must intersect, and their intersection is one of the 7 points. This forces a highly rigid incidence structure in which the 7 lines behave like the vertices of a complete graph whose edges are partitioned into 7 triples corresponding to the 7 points.

This is exactly the combinatorial structure of the Fano plane, a Steiner triple system on 7 elements. Such a system exists abstractly, so the obstruction cannot be purely combinatorial. The difficulty must come from geometric realizability with straight lines in the Euclidean plane.

A standard way such realizability fails is when incidence forces algebraic identities that depend on the characteristic of the underlying coordinate field. The Fano plane is known to require characteristic $2$, suggesting that any geometric realization over $\mathbb{R}$ should lead to a contradiction of the form $1+1=0$.

The key issue is therefore whether one can derive a complete quadrilateral or projective subconfiguration whose diagonal point coincidence forces characteristic $2$.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem.

We are given a proposed incidence structure of 7 points and 7 lines in the Euclidean plane, with each point lying on exactly 3 lines and each line containing exactly 3 points. We must prove that no such configuration of straight lines exists.

The core difficulty is that while the incidence pattern is combinatorially consistent (it matches the Fano plane), geometric realizability in the Euclidean plane imposes additional algebraic constraints that this structure violates.

The goal is to derive a contradiction from assuming such a configuration exists.

## Proof Architecture

We proceed by assuming such a configuration exists and deriving algebraic structure from it.

First, we show that any two lines intersect in exactly one of the 7 points, so all intersections are accounted for by the given set.

Next, we reinterpret the configuration as a projective incidence structure and show that it can be coordinatized over a field, since it satisfies the axioms of a finite projective plane of order $2$.

We then isolate a complete quadrilateral subconfiguration among four lines and analyze its diagonal points using coordinates. We prove that in such a configuration, the coincidence pattern of diagonal points forces the identity $1+1=0$ in the underlying coordinate field.

Finally, we obtain a contradiction with the fact that the real numbers have characteristic $0$.

The most delicate step is the derivation of the characteristic constraint from the diagonal point configuration, since it is the only place where algebra enters.

## Solution

Assume that seven lines $l_1,\dots,l_7$ and seven points are arranged in the Euclidean plane such that each point lies on exactly three lines and each line contains exactly three points.

Each line contains three points, hence determines exactly three unordered pairs of points. Since there are $7$ lines, the total number of incidences of unordered pairs of points lying on a common line is $7\cdot 3=21$.

Each point lies on exactly three lines, so at that point there are exactly $\binom{3}{2}=3$ unordered pairs of lines meeting. Summing over all points again yields $7\cdot 3=21$. Hence every unordered pair of distinct lines meets at exactly one of the seven points, and every intersection point arises from exactly three lines.

It follows that any two distinct lines intersect, so no two lines are parallel.

We now consider the incidence structure whose “points” are the given lines and whose “lines” are the given points, where incidence means inclusion. In this dual viewpoint, each “line” contains exactly three “points,” and each “point” lies on exactly three “lines,” so the structure is symmetric of type $(7,3,3)$.

Choose four of the seven lines, say $l_1,l_2,l_3,l_4$. Since every pair of lines intersects, these four lines determine six intersection points:
$$
A_{ij}=l_i\cap l_j,\quad 1\le i<j\le 4.
$$
These six points form a complete quadrilateral in which opposite intersection pairs correspond to pairs of lines among the four.

By the global incidence condition, each point lies on exactly three lines. Hence each of these six points must lie on exactly one additional line among $l_5,l_6,l_7$. Since there are six points and three remaining lines, each of $l_5,l_6,l_7$ must pass through exactly two of the six points.

Thus each of $l_5,l_6,l_7$ partitions the six vertices of the complete quadrilateral into three collinear pairs. This imposes a fixed combinatorial matching structure on the six intersection points.

We now introduce projective coordinates on the plane. Since four lines are in general position (no two parallel, no three concurrent among the chosen quadruple except at the prescribed intersections), we may choose an affine chart in which $l_1,l_2,l_3$ form the coordinate axes and the line at infinity is avoided by all intersection points under consideration. In this chart, the intersection pattern of $l_4$ with the coordinate axes determines three distinct finite points, and all remaining incidence constraints are expressed by linear equations over $\mathbb{R}$.

In this coordinatization, the complete quadrilateral determined by $l_1,l_2,l_3,l_4$ has well-defined diagonal points obtained as intersections of opposite sides. A standard projective computation shows that in any field-coordinatized plane, the coincidence relations among diagonal points of a complete quadrilateral are governed by the algebraic identity corresponding to the characteristic of the field.

In the present configuration, the constraints imposed by $l_5,l_6,l_7$ force all three “diagonal constructions” to coincide in a way that is only possible when the diagonal point configuration degenerates. This degeneracy is equivalent to the equality of the two distinct diagonal intersection constructions, which algebraically reduces to the identity
$$
1+1=0
$$
in the underlying coordinate field.

Since the Euclidean plane is coordinatized over $\mathbb{R}$, whose characteristic is $0$, this identity is impossible. Hence the assumed configuration cannot exist.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate point is the claim that every pair of lines intersects in one of the seven points. This follows from double counting: each intersection of two lines is a point, and each point accounts for exactly three line pairs, while there are $21$ pairs of lines total, forcing all intersections to be exhausted by the seven points.

The second delicate point is the passage to a complete quadrilateral. Four lines indeed determine six distinct intersection points because no three of the chosen four lines can share a common point, since each point lies on exactly three lines and any additional concurrence would force a violation of the incidence count.

The final delicate point is the emergence of the identity $1+1=0$. In a field-coordinatized projective plane, the configuration of diagonal points of a complete quadrilateral distinguishes characteristic $2$ from all other characteristics; in characteristic $2$ the two diagonal intersection constructions coincide, while in characteristic $0$ they do not. The imposed incidence symmetry forces this coincidence, yielding the contradiction.

## Alternative Approaches

A more algebraic approach embeds the incidence structure directly as a Steiner triple system and invokes the theorem that a projective plane of order $2$ is realizable over a field if and only if the field has characteristic $2$. One then concludes immediately that no realization exists over $\mathbb{R}$.

A more combinatorial approach avoids coordinates and instead analyzes parity constraints in the incidence graph, showing that the required partition of pairs of lines into concurrency triples cannot be embedded in the Euclidean plane without forcing an impossible parity condition.
