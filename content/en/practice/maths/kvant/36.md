## Problem

Prove that it is impossible to arrange seven lines and seven points in the plane so that exactly three lines pass through each point and exactly three points lie on each line.

## Exploration

Let the seven points be the vertices of a bipartite incidence graph. One part consists of the seven points, the other part of the seven lines. A point is joined to a line when the point lies on that line. The condition says that every point is incident with exactly three lines and every line contains exactly three points.

The total number of incidences is then $7\cdot 3=21$.

Suppose such a configuration exists. Any two distinct points determine at most one line. Since every line contains three points, each line contributes $\binom{3}{2}=3$ pairs of points lying on a common line. With seven lines, the total number of point-pairs lying on a common line, counted with multiplicity, is $7\cdot 3=21$.

There are exactly $\binom{7}{2}=21$ pairs of points altogether. Hence every pair of points must lie on a common line, and no pair can be counted twice. Thus every pair of points determines a unique line of the configuration.

This already resembles the projective plane of order $2$, the Fano plane, which indeed has seven points and seven lines with three points on each line and three lines through each point. The difficulty is that the Fano plane is not realizable by ordinary lines in the Euclidean plane. A contradiction must come from ordinary planar geometry.

Choose one of the seven lines, call it $\ell$. It contains three of the points. The remaining four points are not on $\ell$. Since every pair of points lies on a line of the configuration, every pair among these four points must determine one of the remaining six lines. There are $\binom{4}{2}=6$ such pairs, so each of the six remaining lines is determined by exactly one pair of the four points.

Now consider incidences with the three points on $\ell$. Each of these points lies on two additional lines besides $\ell$, giving $3\cdot 2=6$ incidences between the six remaining lines and the three points on $\ell$. Since there are six remaining lines, each such line must contain exactly one point of $\ell$.

Take the four points outside $\ell$, say $A,B,C,D$. The six remaining lines are $AB,AC,AD,BC,BD,CD$. Each contains exactly one point of $\ell$. Let the three points on $\ell$ be $P,Q,R$.

Among the three lines through $A$, namely $AB,AC,AD$, each must contain one of $P,Q,R$. Since $A$ already lies on exactly three lines total, these three lines are distinct and must use all three of $P,Q,R$. Thus after relabeling,

$$AB\ni P,\qquad AC\ni Q,\qquad AD\ni R.$$

Applying the same reasoning to $B$, the lines $AB,BC,BD$ must also contain $P,Q,R$ once each. Since $AB$ already contains $P$, the lines $BC$ and $BD$ must contain $Q$ and $R$ in some order. Assume

$$BC\ni R,\qquad BD\ni Q.$$

Now inspect $C$. The lines through $C$ are $AC,BC,CD$. The first two contain $Q$ and $R$, so $CD$ must contain $P$.

Finally, inspect $D$. The lines through $D$ are $AD,BD,CD$. These contain $R,Q,P$ respectively, as required.

Thus

$$P=AB\cap CD,\qquad Q=AC\cap BD,\qquad R=AD\cap BC.$$

But for four points $A,B,C,D$ in the plane, the three intersection points

$$AB\cap CD,\quad AC\cap BD,\quad AD\cap BC$$

cannot be collinear. They form the three diagonal points of a complete quadrilateral. Since $P,Q,R$ all lie on $\ell$, contradiction.

The crucial point is the non-collinearity of the three diagonal points.

## Problem Understanding

We are asked to prove that no configuration of seven ordinary plane lines and seven plane points can satisfy simultaneously the conditions that every point lies on exactly three of the lines and every line contains exactly three of the points.

This is a Type B problem. The goal is to derive a contradiction from the assumed existence of such a configuration.

The core difficulty is to extract enough combinatorial structure from the incidence conditions to force a geometric configuration that is impossible for ordinary lines in the plane.

## Proof Architecture

First, show that every pair of the seven points lies on a unique line of the configuration. This follows from counting pairs of points on lines.

Second, fix one line $\ell$ and denote by $A,B,C,D$ the four points not on $\ell$. Show that each of the six remaining lines contains exactly one point of $\ell$. This follows from counting incidences with the three points on $\ell$.

Third, label the three points of $\ell$ by $P,Q,R$ and determine systematically which of these points lies on each of the lines $AB,AC,AD,BC,BD,CD$. The incidence conditions force

$$P=AB\cap CD,\quad Q=AC\cap BD,\quad R=AD\cap BC.$$

Fourth, use the theorem on a complete quadrilateral: the three diagonal points

$$AB\cap CD,\quad AC\cap BD,\quad AD\cap BC$$

are not collinear.

The hardest step is the last one, because it is the only genuinely geometric ingredient.

## Solution

Assume that such a configuration exists.

Since each of the seven points lies on exactly three lines, the total number of incidences equals

$$7\cdot 3=21.$$

The same count is obtained from the lines, because each of the seven lines contains exactly three points.

Each line contains exactly three points and therefore contains

$$\binom{3}{2}=3$$

pairs of points. Since there are seven lines, the total number of pairs of points lying on a common line, counted over all lines, is

$$7\cdot 3=21.$$

On the other hand, there are exactly

$$\binom{7}{2}=21$$

pairs of points altogether.

Two distinct points determine at most one line. Hence each pair of points can contribute at most once to the preceding count. Since the count already equals the total number of pairs, every pair of points must lie on a line of the configuration, and no pair can lie on two different lines.

Thus every pair of points determines a unique line of the configuration.

Choose one of the seven lines and call it $\ell$. Let its three points be $P,Q,R$. Let the remaining four points be $A,B,C,D$.

Among $A,B,C,D$ there are

$$\binom{4}{2}=6$$

pairs:

$$AB,\ AC,\ AD,\ BC,\ BD,\ CD.$$

Each pair determines a unique line. Since $\ell$ contains none of $A,B,C,D$, these six lines are distinct and are exactly the six lines other than $\ell$.

Each of the points $P,Q,R$ lies on exactly three lines. One of them is $\ell$, so each lies on exactly two further lines. Hence the six lines distinct from $\ell$ contain altogether

$$3\cdot 2=6$$

incidences with the points $P,Q,R$.

Every one of those six lines contains three points. It already contains a pair from ${A,B,C,D}$, so it can contain at most one of $P,Q,R$. Since there are six incidences distributed among six lines, every one of the six lines contains exactly one of $P,Q,R$.

Consider the three lines through $A$:

$$AB,\ AC,\ AD.$$

Point $A$ lies on exactly three lines, namely these. Since each of them contains one of $P,Q,R$, the points $P,Q,R$ must occur once each on these three lines. After relabeling,

$$AB\ni P,\qquad AC\ni Q,\qquad AD\ni R.$$

Now consider $B$. The lines through $B$ are

$$AB,\ BC,\ BD.$$

Again the points $P,Q,R$ must occur once each. Since $AB$ already contains $P$, the lines $BC$ and $BD$ contain $Q$ and $R$ in some order. Interchanging the names of $Q$ and $R$ if necessary, we may assume

$$BC\ni R,\qquad BD\ni Q.$$

The lines through $C$ are

$$AC,\ BC,\ CD.$$

The first two already contain $Q$ and $R$, so $CD$ must contain $P$.

Consequently,

$$P=AB\cap CD.$$

Similarly,

$$Q=AC\cap BD,$$

and

$$R=AD\cap BC.$$

Thus the three points $P,Q,R$ are exactly the three diagonal points of the complete quadrilateral determined by the four points $A,B,C,D$.

A standard theorem of projective geometry states that the three diagonal points of a complete quadrilateral are not collinear. Therefore the points

$$AB\cap CD,\qquad AC\cap BD,\qquad AD\cap BC$$

cannot lie on a single line.

But $P,Q,R$ all lie on $\ell$ by construction. This contradiction shows that the assumed configuration cannot exist.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the counting of pairs. There are $21$ pairs of points in total. Each line contributes exactly $3$ pairs, giving $21$ pair-occurrences. Since a pair of points determines at most one line, no pair can contribute more than once. Equality of the two totals forces every pair to contribute exactly once. If even one pair failed to lie on a configuration line, the total contribution would be at most $20$.

The second delicate step is proving that every line other than $\ell$ contains exactly one of $P,Q,R$. A line determined by a pair among $A,B,C,D$ already contains two points outside $\ell$. Since each line contains exactly three configuration points, it can contain at most one of $P,Q,R$. The six lines other than $\ell$ must accommodate exactly six incidences with $P,Q,R$, because each of $P,Q,R$ lies on two such lines. Hence every one of the six lines contains exactly one of $P,Q,R$.

The geometric step is the identification

$$P=AB\cap CD,\quad Q=AC\cap BD,\quad R=AD\cap BC.$$

Once these equalities are established, $P,Q,R$ become the three diagonal points of a complete quadrilateral. The theorem that these diagonal points are non-collinear is precisely the fact that excludes a realization by ordinary plane lines.

## Alternative Approaches

The combinatorial counting shows that any hypothetical configuration would have exactly the incidence structure of the seven-point projective plane, often called the Fano plane, namely every pair of points lies on a unique line and every pair of lines meets in a unique point. One may then invoke the classical theorem that the Fano plane is not realizable by ordinary lines in the real projective plane. This yields an immediate contradiction.

The main proof is preferable because it avoids importing the full theory of finite projective planes. After the counting argument, only one geometric fact is needed, namely the non-collinearity of the three diagonal points of a complete quadrilateral. This keeps the argument elementary and self-contained.
