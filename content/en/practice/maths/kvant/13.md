## Problem

Prove that if the difference between the largest and the smallest of the $n$ real numbers $a_1$, $a_2$, $\ldots$, $a_n$ is equal to $d$, and the sum of the absolute values of all $\dfrac{n(n-1)}{2}$ pairwise differences of these numbers $$ \sum_{i \lt j} |a_i - a_j| $$ is equal to $s$, then $$ (n-1)d \le s \le \frac{n^2}{4}d. $$.

## Exploration

For $n=2$ there is only one pairwise difference, so $s=d$. Both inequalities become equalities.

For $n=3$, let the numbers be $0,x,d$ with $0\le x\le d$. Then

$$s=x+(d-x)+d=2d.$$

The lower bound gives $2d\le s$, and the upper bound gives $s\le \frac94d$. Here $s=2d$, so the lower bound is attained for every configuration.

For $n=4$, let the numbers be $0,0,d,d$. Then

$$s=4d=\frac{4^2}{4}d,$$

so the upper bound is attained. This suggests that the maximum occurs when as many numbers as possible are placed at the two endpoints of the interval of length $d$.

The lower bound seems to come from fixing the smallest and largest numbers. If $m=\min a_i$ and $M=\max a_i$, then $M-m=d$. Every other number lies between them. For each intermediate number $a_k$,

$$(M-a_k)+(a_k-m)=d.$$

Summing these identities together with the difference $M-m=d$ already produces $(n-1)d$. Since all remaining pairwise differences are nonnegative, this gives the lower bound.

For the upper bound, order the numbers:

$$a_1\le a_2\le \cdots\le a_n.$$

Then

$$s=\sum_{i<j}(a_j-a_i).$$

Writing each difference as a sum of consecutive gaps

$$x_k=a_{k+1}-a_k,$$

the coefficient of $x_k$ in $s$ is the number of pairs separated by that gap, namely $k(n-k)$. Since

$$\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}x_k=d,$$

the problem becomes maximizing

$$\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}k(n-k)x_k$$

subject to $x_k\ge0$ and $\sum x_k=d$. The maximum coefficient is $\max k(n-k)$, which equals $n^2/4$ when $n$ is even and $(n^2-1)/4$ when $n$ is odd. Since $(n^2-1)/4<n^2/4$, the desired upper bound follows.

The step most likely to hide an error is the passage from pairwise differences to the gap representation and the computation of the coefficient $k(n-k)$.

## Problem Understanding

We are given $n$ real numbers whose largest value exceeds the smallest by $d$. Let

$$s=\sum_{i<j}|a_i-a_j|$$

be the sum of all pairwise absolute differences. We must prove that

$$(n-1)d\le s\le \frac{n^2}{4}d.$$

This is a Type B problem, a pure proof.

The core difficulty is to relate the global quantity $s$ to the single parameter $d$. The lower bound requires showing that a certain amount of pairwise difference is unavoidable once the range is fixed. The upper bound requires identifying how large the total pairwise difference can become under the same constraint.

## Proof Architecture

First, choose indices corresponding to the minimum and maximum elements, and prove that the differences involving these two elements already contribute $(n-1)d$ to $s$.

Second, order the numbers increasingly and introduce the consecutive gaps

$$x_k=a_{k+1}-a_k.$$

Express every pairwise difference as a sum of appropriate gaps.

Third, prove that in the resulting expression

$$s=\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}k(n-k)x_k.$$

The coefficient $k(n-k)$ counts the pairs whose interval contains the $k$-th gap.

Fourth, use

$$\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}x_k=d$$

and the bound

$$k(n-k)\le \frac{n^2}{4}$$

to obtain the desired upper estimate.

The hardest part is establishing the exact coefficient $k(n-k)$ in the gap expansion.

## Solution

Let

$$m=\min(a_1,\dots,a_n), \qquad M=\max(a_1,\dots,a_n).$$

Then

$$M-m=d.$$

Choose indices $p,q$ such that

$$a_p=m,\qquad a_q=M.$$

For every index $k\ne p,q$,

$$|a_k-m|+|M-a_k| =(a_k-m)+(M-a_k) =M-m=d,$$

because $m\le a_k\le M$.

Summing over all $k\ne p,q$ gives

$$\sum_{k\ne p,q}\bigl(|a_k-m|+|M-a_k|\bigr) =(n-2)d.$$

Adding also the pair $(p,q)$ contributes

$$|M-m|=d.$$

Hence the pairwise differences involving at least one of the extreme elements contribute

$$(n-2)d+d=(n-1)d.$$

All remaining pairwise differences are nonnegative, so

$$s\ge (n-1)d.$$

This proves the lower bound.

For the upper bound, reorder the numbers:

$$a_1\le a_2\le \cdots\le a_n.$$

Since the numbers are ordered,

$$|a_j-a_i|=a_j-a_i \qquad (i<j),$$

and therefore

$$s=\sum_{i<j}(a_j-a_i).$$

Define the consecutive gaps

$$x_k=a_{k+1}-a_k \qquad (k=1,\dots,n-1).$$

Then

$$x_k\ge0,$$

and

$$\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}x_k=a_n-a_1=d.$$

For $i<j$,

$$a_j-a_i=\sum_{k=i}^{j-1}x_k.$$

Substituting into the expression for $s$,

$$s=\sum_{i<j}\sum_{k=i}^{j-1}x_k.$$

Interchanging the order of summation, we determine the coefficient of a fixed gap $x_k$. The term $x_k$ appears exactly for those pairs $(i,j)$ satisfying

$$i\le k<j.$$

There are $k$ possible choices for $i$ and $n-k$ possible choices for $j$. Hence $x_k$ occurs exactly

$$k(n-k)$$

times. Therefore

$$s=\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}k(n-k)x_k.$$

For every $k$,

$$k(n-k)\le \frac{n^2}{4},$$

because

$$4k(n-k)=n^2-(n-2k)^2\le n^2.$$

Thus

$$s =\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}k(n-k)x_k \le \frac{n^2}{4}\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}x_k = \frac{n^2}{4}d.$$

Combining the two estimates,

$$(n-1)d\le s\le \frac{n^2}{4}d.$$

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

For the lower bound, the crucial identity is

$$|a_k-m|+|M-a_k|=d.$$

A careless argument might use absolute values incorrectly. The identity holds because $m\le a_k\le M$, so both absolute values may be removed:

$$|a_k-m|=a_k-m,\qquad |M-a_k|=M-a_k.$$

Their sum is exactly $M-m=d$.

For the gap representation, the delicate point is the coefficient of $x_k$. The condition that $x_k$ appears in $a_j-a_i$ is

$$i\le k<j.$$

There are exactly $k$ choices for $i$ among $1,\dots,k$ and exactly $n-k$ choices for $j$ among $k+1,\dots,n$. Multiplying gives $k(n-k)$. Missing one of these inequalities would produce an incorrect coefficient.

For the final estimate, one must justify

$$k(n-k)\le \frac{n^2}{4}.$$

The identity

$$4k(n-k)=n^2-(n-2k)^2$$

shows this directly. Equality occurs only when $n-2k=0$, that is, when $k=n/2$ and $n$ is even.

## Alternative Approaches

After ordering the numbers,

$$s=\sum_{i<j}(a_j-a_i) =\sum_{r=1}^{n}(2r-n-1)a_r.$$

One may derive this by collecting the coefficient of each $a_r$. Since $a_1\le a_r\le a_n$ and $a_n-a_1=d$, maximizing this linear expression under the ordering constraints forces every $a_r$ to move to one of the endpoints of the interval $[a_1,a_n]$. The positive coefficients are assigned the value $a_n$, the negative coefficients the value $a_1$. This yields the maximum

$$\left\lfloor\frac n2\right\rfloor \left\lceil\frac n2\right\rceil d \le \frac{n^2}{4}d.$$

The gap method is preferable because it produces the upper bound immediately from the simple identity

$$s=\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}k(n-k)x_k,$$

and the coefficient bound follows from a single quadratic inequality.
