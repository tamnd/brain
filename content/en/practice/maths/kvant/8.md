## Problem

Two people play the following game. From a pile containing 25 matches, they take turns taking 1, 2, or 3 matches. The winner is the player who, at the end of the game, after all the matches have been taken, has an even number of matches.

Who wins with correct play, the first player or the second? How should that player play in order to win? How does the answer change if the winner is the player who has taken an odd number of matches?

Investigate this game in the general case when there are $2n+1$ matches and it is permitted to take any number of matches from 1 to $m$.

## Exploration

The winner is not determined by who takes the last match. The winner is determined by the parity of the number of matches taken by one player. Since the total number of matches is odd, the two players always finish with opposite parities. If one player has taken an even number of matches, the other has taken an odd number. Hence exactly one player is the winner.

The state of the game is not determined solely by the number of matches remaining. We must also know the parity of the number already taken by one player. Let the parity of the first player's total be denoted by $p_1$ and that of the second player's total by $p_2$.

For the specific game with $25$ matches and moves $1,2,3$, it is useful to work backward. Let a position be written as $(r,p_1,p_2)$, where $r$ matches remain and it is the next player's turn.

When $r=0$, the game is over. Since $25$ is odd, the only possible terminal parity pairs are $(0,1)$ and $(1,0)$. If the winner is the player with an even number of matches, then $(0,1)$ is winning for the first player and $(1,0)$ is winning for the second.

Computing the first few positions gives a striking pattern. If the winner is the player with an even number of matches, then with $r$ matches remaining and both parities equal to $0$, the position is winning for the player to move when $r\equiv1\pmod4$ and losing when $r\equiv0\pmod4$. The positions repeat with period $4$.

This suggests that the crucial quantity is not the exact number of remaining matches but its residue modulo $m+1$. For the ordinary take-away game with moves $1,\dots,m$, the losing positions are multiples of $m+1$. The parity condition may merely determine which parity configuration should be paired with those residues.

The step most likely to conceal an error is the passage from small examples to the general game with $2n+1$ matches and moves $1,\dots,m$. The parity of a player's score changes precisely when that player takes an odd number of matches, so any proof must track both the residue class of the remaining pile and the parity information.

The key observation is that because the total number of matches is odd, specifying the parity of one player's total automatically determines the parity of the other's at the end. Thus the game may be viewed as a game whose objective is to force a prescribed parity for the first player's total.

## Problem Understanding

We are given a take-away game. Starting from an odd number of matches, players alternately remove between $1$ and $m$ matches. The game ends when no matches remain.

The winner is determined not by the last move but by the parity of the total number of matches collected by a specified player. In the first version, the winner is the player whose total number of matches is even. In the second version, the winner is the player whose total number of matches is odd.

This is a Type D problem. We must determine which player has a winning strategy and describe that strategy, first for $25$ matches and moves $1,2,3$, then for the general game with $2n+1$ matches and moves $1,\dots,m$.

The core difficulty is that the state depends simultaneously on the number of matches remaining and on the parity of the accumulated scores.

The answer will be that the game reduces to the usual take-away game modulo $m+1$. The winning player maintains a suitable parity while forcing the pile through the standard sequence of multiples of $m+1$.

## Proof Architecture

Let $N=2n+1$.

First, prove that at every terminal position the two players have opposite parities, because their totals sum to the odd number $N$.

Second, reformulate the game as one in which the first player's parity at the end determines the winner.

Third, prove that whenever two consecutive moves remove a total of $m+1$ matches, the number of remaining matches decreases by exactly $m+1$ while control of the parity can be transferred in a predictable way.

Fourth, prove that positions with a multiple of $m+1$ matches remaining are the analogue of losing positions in the ordinary take-away game.

Fifth, show that if $N$ is not divisible by $m+1$, the first player can move to a multiple of $m+1$ and thereafter answer every move $k$ by $m+1-k$.

Sixth, compute the parity effect of one complete pair of complementary moves and determine which parity objective is achieved.

The most delicate point is proving that the parity objective and the modulo-$m+1$ strategy are compatible throughout the game.
