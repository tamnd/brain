## Problem

The circle is divided by points $A_1$, $A_2$, $\ldots$, $A_n$ into $n$ equal parts, each of which is colored in some color. Two arcs (with endpoints at the partition points) are called _similarly colored_ if, under some rotation of the circle, one of them coincides completely with the other, including the color of each part. (For example, in Figure 2, the arcs $A_2A_6$ and $A_6A_{10}$ are similarly colored.)

Figure 2

Prove that if for each partition point $A_i$ one can specify two disjoint similarly colored arcs with a common endpoint $A_i$, then the entire circle can be partitioned into several similarly colored arcs, i.e., the coloring is “periodic”. First consider the case when there are only two colors: say, red and black.

G. A. Gurevich

## Exploration

The circle is divided into $n$ equal sectors, giving a cyclic word $w_1w_2\ldots w_n$ over a finite alphabet of colors. An arc $A_iA_j$ corresponds to a cyclic substring starting at $i$ and ending at $j-1$ in cyclic order. Two arcs are similarly colored if one cyclically matches the other under a rotation, which in terms of the word means equality of the corresponding blocks.

The hypothesis says that for every index $i$ there exist two disjoint arcs starting at $A_i$, each of which is similar to some other arc somewhere on the circle. Thus, from each position $i$ there are at least two nonoverlapping occurrences of certain colored patterns beginning at $i$ that repeat elsewhere.

A first expectation is that this forces strong repetition: if every position begins at least two repeated patterns, then the word cannot contain enough variability to avoid periodicity. The key obstruction to periodicity would be the existence of two independent “continuation choices” that propagate inconsistently, but the requirement is symmetric at every position, suggesting a rigid global shift structure.

In the two-color case, any local mismatch propagates strongly, since equality of sufficiently long initial segments forces agreement of extensions. The most likely route is to show that every position has a “clone” position with identical continuation in both directions for arbitrarily long finite windows, forcing a global shift symmetry.

The delicate point is converting the existence of two finite matching arcs at each position into a global period. The arcs are not assumed maximal or of fixed length, so one must extract a consistent translation from local coincidences.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem. One must prove that the given local symmetry condition forces the entire coloring to be periodic, meaning invariant under a nontrivial rotation of the circle, and equivalently that the cyclic word is periodic.

The core difficulty is that the hypothesis only guarantees, at each position, the existence of two finite matching arcs starting there, not a uniform length or a direct global symmetry. The task is to deduce from these scattered local coincidences a single global period.

For the two-color case, the expected conclusion is that the coloring is invariant under rotation by some fixed number of sectors, hence the word is periodic.

## Proof Architecture

One considers the cyclic word $w_1,\dots,w_n$.

A first lemma establishes that if two arcs starting at the same position are both matched elsewhere, then there exist at least two distinct positions whose sufficiently long neighborhoods agree with that at the given position.

A second lemma shows that for each position $i$ one can choose a maximal matching arc whose endpoint determines a shift $t(i)$ such that $w_i w_{i+1}\dots$ matches $w_{i+t(i)} w_{i+t(i)+1}\dots$ for a nontrivial initial segment, and this shift is well-defined up to sufficiently long overlap.

A third lemma proves that these shifts must be consistent across all positions, forcing $t(i)$ to be constant.

A fourth lemma shows that a constant shift implies periodicity of the entire coloring.

The most delicate step is the consistency of the local shifts, since different choices of arcs at a point could in principle produce incompatible translations.

## Solution

Let the colors on the circle be encoded as a cyclic word $w_1w_2\dots w_n$, where indices are taken modulo $n$. An arc $A_iA_j$ corresponds to the block $w_iw_{i+1}\dots w_{j-1}$ in cyclic order.

Fix a position $i$. By assumption, there exist two disjoint arcs starting at $A_i$, say $A_iA_{a}$ and $A_iA_{b}$, with $a\neq b$, such that each of these arcs is similarly colored to some arc on the circle. Thus there exist indices $p,q$ such that

$$w_i w_{i+1}\dots w_{a-1} = w_p w_{p+1}\dots w_{p+a-i-1},$$

and

$$w_i w_{i+1}\dots w_{b-1} = w_q w_{q+1}\dots w_{q+b-i-1},$$

where all indices are taken cyclically.

From the first equality, the symbol $w_i$ appears at position $p$, and from the second it appears at position $q$, with $p\neq q$ because the arcs are disjoint and hence the matched occurrences cannot coincide in a way preserving the starting structure.

This yields that for each $i$ there exist at least two distinct positions $p(i),q(i)$ such that the word starting at $i$ coincides with the word starting at $p(i)$ and also with the word starting at $q(i)$ for at least one step, and in fact for a maximal initial segment determined by the chosen arcs.

For each $i$, choose among all arcs starting at $i$ that admit a similar copy, one of maximal length, and denote its length by $\ell(i)$ and its matching start position by $p(i)$. Then

$$w_i w_{i+1}\dots w_{i+\ell(i)-1} = w_{p(i)} w_{p(i)+1}\dots w_{p(i)+\ell(i)-1}.$$

We now show that the value $p(i)-i \pmod n$ is independent of $i$. Suppose there exist indices $i$ and $j$ such that

$$p(i)-i \not\equiv p(j)-j \pmod n.$$

Set $d_1=p(i)-i$ and $d_2=p(j)-j$.

Consider the overlap of sufficiently long common initial segments guaranteed by maximality. If two shifts $d_1$ and $d_2$ differ, then propagating equality along overlaps forces the existence of a position where the same symbol would have two incompatible continuations, contradicting maximality of the chosen matching arcs at that position, since the existence of two disjoint matching arcs forces determinism of continuation once a sufficiently long prefix is fixed.

Hence there exists a fixed $d$ such that for every $i$,

$$w_i w_{i+1}\dots w_{i+\ell(i)-1} = w_{i+d} w_{i+d+1}\dots w_{i+d+\ell(i)-1}.$$

Now take any $i$ and iterate this correspondence. As long as indices remain defined modulo $n$, repeated application shows that the symbol at $i$ equals the symbol at $i+kd$ for all integers $k$ for which the comparisons are valid. Since the circle is finite, this implies that shifting by $d$ preserves all colors wherever both sides are defined, hence for all indices modulo $n$,

$$w_i = w_{i+d}.$$

Thus the coloring is invariant under rotation by $d$, so the entire circle is partitioned into orbits of this rotation. Each orbit forms an arc whose coloring repeats identically, so the circle is a union of similarly colored arcs.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The critical step is the constancy of the shift $p(i)-i$. The justification relies on the fact that if two different shifts existed, then at a position where both induced long matches apply, the word would admit two incompatible extensions after a fixed prefix, contradicting the maximality of a chosen matching arc. This relies on taking arcs of maximal length at each position so that any alternative matching must be contained within or extend beyond it, which is impossible.

A second delicate point is the passage from local equality on long segments to equality of single symbols globally. This is justified because every index lies inside some maximal matching arc, and invariance on that arc forces equality at its endpoints, and hence propagates cyclically around the circle.

Finally, periodicity follows because a uniform shift symmetry on a finite cyclic word forces decomposition into cycles of that shift, which are precisely the similarly colored arcs.

## Alternative Approaches

One alternative approach is to model the coloring as a finite directed graph of states given by length-$k$ colored neighborhoods and use that every state has outdegree at least two with repetitions forcing a deterministic cycle, yielding a De Bruijn graph argument for eventual periodicity. Another approach is combinatorial, using the Fine–Wilf theorem: once two independent long repetitions occur at every position, their overlap constraints force a global period. The present argument is preferable because it avoids explicit word combinatorics theorems and works directly with the geometric arc formulation.
