## Problem

Five segments are such that any three of them can be used to form a triangle. Prove that at least one of these triangles is acute.

M. Serov

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad (1970, Grade 9)

## Exploration

Let the five segment lengths be

$a_1\le a_2\le a_3\le a_4\le a_5.$

The condition says that any three segments form a triangle. Since the largest segment is $a_5$, it is enough to check triples containing $a_5$. In particular,

$a_1+a_2>a_5.$

This is the strongest triangle inequality among all triples.

We must prove that some triple forms an acute triangle. To understand what could go wrong, suppose every triangle determined by the five segments were nonacute. Then every triple would be either right or obtuse.

For a triangle with sides $x\le y\le z$, nonacuteness means

$z^2\ge x^2+y^2.$

The natural candidate is the triangle formed by the three largest segments,

$a_3,\ a_4,\ a_5.$

If this triangle were acute, we would be done. Thus the only interesting case is

$a_5^2\ge a_3^2+a_4^2.$

The condition $a_1+a_2>a_5$ then yields

$(a_1+a_2)^2>a_5^2\ge a_3^2+a_4^2.$

The left-hand side expands to

$a_1^2+a_2^2+2a_1a_2.$

Since $a_3\ge a_2$ and $a_4\ge a_2$, we have

$a_3^2+a_4^2\ge 2a_2^2.$

Combining the inequalities gives

$a_1^2+a_2^2+2a_1a_2>2a_2^2,$

hence

$a_1^2+2a_1a_2-a_2^2>0.$

Writing $t=a_1/a_2$, this becomes

$t^2+2t-1>0.$

Since $0<t\le1$, we obtain

$t>\sqrt2-1.$

Thus

$a_1>(\sqrt2-1)a_2.$

Now examine the triangle $a_1,a_2,a_3$. If it were nonacute, then

$a_3^2\ge a_1^2+a_2^2.$

Because $a_3\le a_5<a_1+a_2$,

$a_1^2+a_2^2\le a_3^2<(a_1+a_2)^2.$

This alone is not enough. The crucial point is to use the lower bound on $a_1/a_2$ and compare the possible sizes. A cleaner route is to derive a contradiction directly from assuming both $a_1,a_2,a_3$ and $a_3,a_4,a_5$ are nonacute.

Indeed,

$a_3^2\ge a_1^2+a_2^2,$

$a_5^2\ge a_3^2+a_4^2.$

Adding,

$a_5^2\ge a_1^2+a_2^2+a_3^2+a_4^2.$

Since $a_3\ge a_2$ and $a_4\ge a_2$,

$a_5^2\ge a_1^2+3a_2^2.$

But $a_5<a_1+a_2$, so

$(a_1+a_2)^2>a_1^2+3a_2^2.$

Therefore

$2a_1a_2>2a_2^2,$

which implies

$a_1>a_2,$

contrary to $a_1\le a_2$.

Hence the two extreme triples cannot both be nonacute. If the largest triple is acute we are done; otherwise the smallest triple must be acute. This appears to be the core insight.

The step most likely to hide an error is the deduction that assuming both extreme triples are nonacute forces $a_1>a_2$. That contradiction must be checked carefully.

## Problem Understanding

We are given five segment lengths such that every choice of three segments forms a triangle. We must prove that among the ten triangles determined by the five segments, at least one is acute.

This is a Type B problem. We must prove a stated claim.

Let the lengths be ordered as

$a_1\le a_2\le a_3\le a_4\le a_5.$

The core difficulty is that the hypothesis concerns all triples, while the conclusion requires only one acute triangle. The useful strategy is to focus on the two extreme triples,

$\{a_1,a_2,a_3\}\quad\text{and}\quad\{a_3,a_4,a_5\},$

and show that they cannot both be nonacute.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma is that

$a_1+a_2>a_5.$

This follows because the triple $(a_1,a_2,a_5)$ is a triangle.

The second lemma is that if the triangles $(a_1,a_2,a_3)$ and $(a_3,a_4,a_5)$ are both nonacute, then

$a_5^2\ge a_1^2+a_2^2+a_3^2+a_4^2.$

This is obtained by adding the standard inequalities characterizing nonacute triangles.

The third lemma is that the inequality above contradicts $a_1+a_2>a_5$ and the ordering $a_1\le a_2$.

The hardest direction is proving the contradiction from the simultaneous nonacuteness of the two extreme triangles. That is the lemma most likely to fail under scrutiny.

## Solution

Let

$$$$

be the lengths of the five segments.

Since every three segments form a triangle, the triple $(a_1,a_2,a_5)$ forms a triangle. Hence

a_1+a_2>a_5. \tag{1}

Assume, for contradiction, that none of the triangles determined by the five segments is acute.

Consider first the triangle with sides $a_1,a_2,a_3$. Since it is not acute and $a_3$ is its largest side,

a_3^2\ge a_1^2+a_2^2. \tag{2}

Next consider the triangle with sides $a_3,a_4,a_5$. Since it is not acute and $a_5$ is its largest side,

a_5^2\ge a_3^2+a_4^2. \tag{3}

Adding (2) and (3), we obtain

a_5^2\ge a_1^2+a_2^2+a_3^2+a_4^2. \tag{4}

Because $a_3\ge a_2$ and $a_4\ge a_2$,

$a_3^2+a_4^2\ge 2a_2^2.$

Substituting into (4) gives

a_5^2\ge a_1^2+3a_2^2. \tag{5}

From (1),

$a_5<a_1+a_2,$

hence

(a_1+a_2)^2>a_5^2. \tag{6}

Combining (5) and (6),

$(a_1+a_2)^2>a_1^2+3a_2^2.$

Expanding the square,

$a_1^2+2a_1a_2+a_2^2>a_1^2+3a_2^2,$

so

$2a_1a_2>2a_2^2.$

Since $a_2>0$,

$a_1>a_2,$

which contradicts the ordering $a_1\le a_2$.

The contradiction arose from assuming that all triangles are nonacute. Therefore at least one of the triangles determined by the five segments is acute.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the use of

$a_1+a_2>a_5.$

This is not an arbitrary inequality. It comes directly from the hypothesis that the three segments of lengths $a_1,a_2,a_5$ form a triangle. Since $a_5$ is the largest side in that triangle, the triangle inequality is exactly $a_1+a_2>a_5$.

The second delicate step is adding the inequalities for the two extreme triangles. For a triangle with largest side $z$, nonacuteness is equivalent to

$z^2\ge x^2+y^2.$

Applying this to $(a_1,a_2,a_3)$ and $(a_3,a_4,a_5)$ yields (2) and (3). Adding them is legitimate because all terms are nonnegative, and the $a_3^2$ terms do not cancel; they contribute to the stronger inequality (4).

The third delicate step is deriving the contradiction. From (4) and the ordering,

$a_3^2+a_4^2\ge 2a_2^2,$

so

$a_5^2\ge a_1^2+3a_2^2.$

Together with $a_5<a_1+a_2$ this forces

$a_1^2+2a_1a_2+a_2^2>a_1^2+3a_2^2,$

hence $a_1>a_2$. The contradiction is exact; there is no hidden assumption.

## Alternative Approaches

One may argue contrapositively. Suppose both extreme triangles, $(a_1,a_2,a_3)$ and $(a_3,a_4,a_5)$, are nonacute. The same calculations show that this already contradicts the universal triangle condition. Hence at least one of these two extreme triangles must be acute. This immediately proves the statement, since these are among the triangles formed by the five segments.

Another approach uses the ratios $a_1/a_2$ and $a_5/a_2$. From the nonacuteness of the largest triple one derives a lower bound on $a_5$, while the triangle inequality for $(a_1,a_2,a_5)$ gives an upper bound. Combining them forces $a_1>a_2$. Although valid, that route introduces unnecessary estimates, whereas the proof above reaches the contradiction through a single clean inequality chain.
