## Problem

Five segments are such that any three of them can form a triangle. Prove that at least one of these triangles is acute-angled.

M. Serov

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad (1970, 9th grade)

## Exploration

Let the segment lengths be

$$a_1 \le a_2 \le a_3 \le a_4 \le a_5 .$$

The condition says that every triple forms a triangle. Since the largest side in any triple must be smaller than the sum of the other two, it is enough to check triples containing the largest segments. In particular,

$$a_5<a_1+a_2,$$

because otherwise the triple $(a_1,a_2,a_5)$ would fail to form a triangle.

The claim asks for an acute triangle among the $\binom52$ possible triples. A triangle with sides $x\le y\le z$ is acute exactly when

$$z^2<x^2+y^2.$$

To disprove the statement one would assume that every triangle is right or obtuse, hence for every triple

$$z^2\ge x^2+y^2.$$

The strongest restriction should come from triples with large sides. Consider

$$(a_3,a_4,a_5).$$

If this triangle were nonacute, then

$$a_5^2\ge a_3^2+a_4^2.$$

Since $a_3\ge a_2$ and $a_4\ge a_1$,

$$a_3^2+a_4^2\ge a_1^2+a_2^2.$$

Hence

$$a_5^2\ge a_1^2+a_2^2.$$

But from the triangle condition,

$$a_5<a_1+a_2.$$

Squaring gives

$$a_5^2<(a_1+a_2)^2=a_1^2+a_2^2+2a_1a_2.$$

These inequalities are not contradictory.

A sharper comparison is needed. Since

$$a_5<a_1+a_2\le 2a_2,$$

we get

$$a_5^2<4a_2^2.$$

If also

$$a_5^2\ge a_3^2+a_4^2\ge 2a_2^2,$$

there is still no contradiction.

Try another route. Suppose every triangle is nonacute. Then in particular

$$a_3^2\ge a_1^2+a_2^2$$

from the triple $(a_1,a_2,a_3)$, and

$$a_4^2\ge a_1^2+a_3^2$$

from $(a_1,a_3,a_4)$. Combining,

$$a_4^2\ge 2a_1^2+a_2^2.$$

Continuing,

$$a_5^2\ge a_1^2+a_4^2\ge 3a_1^2+a_2^2.$$

This still does not directly contradict $a_5<a_1+a_2$.

A better recursive choice is

$$a_3^2\ge a_1^2+a_2^2,$$

$$a_4^2\ge a_2^2+a_3^2\ge a_1^2+2a_2^2,$$

$$a_5^2\ge a_3^2+a_4^2\ge 2a_1^2+3a_2^2.$$

Hence

$$a_5>\sqrt{5}\,a_2>2a_2.$$

But the triangle condition gives

$$a_5<a_1+a_2\le 2a_2,$$

a contradiction.

The crucial point is the recursive buildup of lower bounds. One must choose the triples carefully so that the coefficients grow fast enough.

## Problem Understanding

We are given five segment lengths such that every choice of three lengths can serve as the sides of a triangle. We must prove that among all these triangles, at least one is acute.

This is a Type B problem. The task is to prove the stated assertion.

The core difficulty is that the hypothesis concerns only existence of triangles, while the conclusion concerns angle type. A direct geometric argument is not apparent. The effective strategy is to assume all triangles are nonacute and derive increasingly strong inequalities among the side lengths until they contradict the triangle condition.

## Proof Architecture

Order the segment lengths as

$$a_1\le a_2\le a_3\le a_4\le a_5.$$

This allows every triangle condition and every acute-obtuse condition to be written with the largest side identified.

Assume for contradiction that every triangle formed by the segments is right or obtuse. Then for every triple $x\le y\le z$,

$$z^2\ge x^2+y^2.$$

This converts the geometric condition into algebraic inequalities.

Apply this to the triples $(a_1,a_2,a_3)$, $(a_2,a_3,a_4)$, and $(a_3,a_4,a_5)$. Combining the resulting inequalities recursively yields

$$a_5^2\ge 2a_1^2+3a_2^2.$$

Since $a_1^2\ge0$, this implies

$$a_5^2>4a_2^2,$$

hence

$$a_5>2a_2.$$

Use the triangle condition for the triple $(a_1,a_2,a_5)$:

$$a_5<a_1+a_2\le2a_2.$$

This contradicts the previous inequality.

The most delicate step is obtaining a lower bound strong enough to force $a_5>2a_2$. A weaker chain of inequalities would not contradict the triangle condition.

## Solution

Let the segment lengths be

$$a_1\le a_2\le a_3\le a_4\le a_5.$$

Assume that none of the triangles formed by these segments is acute. Then every such triangle is right or obtuse.

For a triangle with side lengths $x\le y\le z$, being right or obtuse means

$$z^2\ge x^2+y^2.$$

Apply this to the triangle with sides $a_1,a_2,a_3$. Since $a_3$ is the largest side,

$$a_3^2\ge a_1^2+a_2^2.$$

Next consider the triangle with sides $a_2,a_3,a_4$. Since $a_4$ is the largest side,

$$a_4^2\ge a_2^2+a_3^2.$$

Substituting the previous inequality,

$$a_4^2\ge a_2^2+a_1^2+a_2^2=a_1^2+2a_2^2.$$

Now consider the triangle with sides $a_3,a_4,a_5$. Since $a_5$ is the largest side,

$$a_5^2\ge a_3^2+a_4^2.$$

Using the inequalities already obtained,

$$a_5^2\ge (a_1^2+a_2^2)+(a_1^2+2a_2^2) =2a_1^2+3a_2^2.$$

Since $a_1>0$,

$$a_5^2>4a_2^2.$$

Therefore

$$a_5>2a_2.$$

On the other hand, the segments $a_1,a_2,a_5$ also form a triangle. Hence

$$a_5<a_1+a_2.$$

Because $a_1\le a_2$,

$$a_1+a_2\le2a_2.$$

Thus

$$a_5<2a_2,$$

contradicting the inequality $a_5>2a_2$.

The contradiction arose from assuming that all triangles were right or obtuse. Hence at least one of the triangles formed by the five segments is acute.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate point is the translation from “nonacute” to an algebraic inequality. For a triangle with ordered sides $x\le y\le z$, the angle opposite $z$ is nonacute exactly when

$$z^2\ge x^2+y^2.$$

This is the converse direction of the Pythagorean theorem. The ordering of the sides is essential; applying the inequality to a nonmaximal side would be invalid.

The second delicate point is the recursive estimate. From

$$a_3^2\ge a_1^2+a_2^2$$

and

$$a_4^2\ge a_2^2+a_3^2,$$

one must substitute correctly:

$$a_4^2\ge a_2^2+(a_1^2+a_2^2)=a_1^2+2a_2^2.$$

Then

$$a_5^2\ge a_3^2+a_4^2$$

becomes

$$a_5^2\ge (a_1^2+a_2^2)+(a_1^2+2a_2^2) =2a_1^2+3a_2^2.$$

If one instead used weaker inequalities such as $a_4^2\ge2a_2^2$, the final estimate would only give

$$a_5^2\ge a_1^2+3a_2^2,$$

which does not force $a_5>2a_2$.

The final contradiction also requires strictness. Since the three segments $a_1,a_2,a_5$ form a genuine triangle,

$$a_5<a_1+a_2,$$

not merely $\le$. This yields

$$a_5<2a_2,$$

which directly contradicts $a_5>2a_2$.

## Alternative Approaches

A different proof can be organized through repeated use of the Fibonacci recurrence. Assume again that every triangle is nonacute. Then for consecutive triples,

$$a_{k+2}^2\ge a_{k+1}^2+a_k^2.$$

Starting from $a_1,a_2$, the minimal possible growth under these inequalities resembles the Fibonacci sequence. After three steps one obtains

$$a_5^2\ge2a_1^2+3a_2^2,$$

which is already incompatible with the triangle inequality for $(a_1,a_2,a_5)$.

Another approach uses contradiction with maximality. Among all triples choose one with largest side $a_5$. If this triangle is nonacute, then

$$a_5^2\ge a_i^2+a_j^2$$

for some $i,j<5$. Repeating the argument for the larger of $a_i,a_j$ creates a rapidly increasing chain of lower bounds, eventually exceeding the linear upper bound imposed by the triangle inequality. This method is conceptually similar but less transparent than the direct recursive computation above.
