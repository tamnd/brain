## Problem

Five segments are such that any three of them can form a triangle. Prove that at least one of these triangles is acute-angled.

M. Serov

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad (1970, 9th grade)

## Exploration

Consider five positive real numbers representing the lengths of segments such that any three of them can form a triangle. If all triangles were right or obtuse, then each set of three sides must satisfy the triangle inequality with the largest side at least as long as the other two combined, otherwise an acute triangle would form. Attempting to arrange the segments so that all triangles are non-acute suggests extreme inequalities among the lengths, with one side potentially dominating others. For example, if four sides are nearly equal and one side is significantly longer, then some triangle formed with the long side and two shorter ones may be obtuse, but the remaining triples could still form acute triangles. Testing small numerical cases shows that distributing lengths too evenly produces an acute triangle, while creating one very long segment reduces the number of non-acute triangles but cannot eliminate all acute ones. The most likely critical step is showing that no assignment of lengths can prevent the existence of an acute triangle without violating the condition that any three lengths form a triangle.

## Problem Understanding

The problem asks to prove that among five segments where any three form a triangle, at least one triangle is acute-angled. This is a Type B problem since a statement is given to be proved. The core difficulty lies in handling all configurations of five numbers and ruling out the possibility that all triangles are non-acute. Intuitively, the triangle inequality forces constraints on the relative sizes of segments; if one tries to make all triangles right or obtuse, the inequalities become incompatible for all five segments simultaneously. The main challenge is formalizing this observation to a rigorous argument.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1: In any triangle, if the largest side squared is greater than or equal to the sum of the squares of the other two, the triangle is right or obtuse; otherwise it is acute. This is the standard characterization from the law of cosines.  

Lemma 2: Among five positive numbers such that any three form a triangle, the largest number cannot be greater than the sum of any two smaller numbers of a triple that excludes it. This ensures the triangle inequality holds for all triples.  

Lemma 3: If all triangles formed from five numbers are right or obtuse, the largest number must be at least the sum of the other two in every triple it appears, but Lemma 2 restricts the largest number to be less than the sum of two others in some triple, producing a contradiction. This is the crux of the argument.  

The hardest step is Lemma 3, as it requires checking the interplay of the largest number with multiple triples and showing that an attempt to avoid acute triangles inevitably violates the triangle inequality in at least one triple.

## Solution

Let the five segment lengths be $a < b < c < d < e$. Any triangle formed by three segments satisfies the triangle inequality. Denote by $T(x,y,z)$ the triangle with sides $x \le y \le z$. By Lemma 1, $T(x,y,z)$ is acute if and only if $z^2 < x^2 + y^2$ and is right or obtuse if $z^2 \ge x^2 + y^2$. Suppose for contradiction that all triangles formed from $a,b,c,d,e$ are non-acute, meaning that for every triple, the square of the largest side is greater than or equal to the sum of the squares of the other two.

Consider the triangle with the largest side $e$ and the next two largest sides $d$ and $c$. Since we are assuming non-acute triangles, we have

$$e^2 \ge d^2 + c^2.$$

Similarly, consider the triangle with $e$ and the smallest two sides $a$ and $b$:

$$e^2 \ge a^2 + b^2.$$

Adding these two inequalities gives

$$2e^2 \ge (d^2 + c^2) + (a^2 + b^2).$$

Now examine the triangle with $d$, $a$, and $b$. Since this triangle is also assumed non-acute, the largest side is $d$, and thus

$$d^2 \ge a^2 + b^2.$$

Similarly, the triangle with $c$, $a$, and $b$ satisfies

$$c^2 \ge a^2 + b^2.$$

Summing these last two inequalities yields

$$c^2 + d^2 \ge 2(a^2 + b^2).$$

Combining with the previous inequality produces

$$2 e^2 \ge (c^2 + d^2) + (a^2 + b^2) \ge 2(a^2 + b^2) + (a^2 + b^2) = 3(a^2 + b^2).$$

Hence, $e^2 \ge \frac{3}{2} (a^2 + b^2)$. On the other hand, the triangle $a,b,e$ must satisfy the triangle inequality

$$a + b > e,$$

which implies

$$(a+b)^2 > e^2.$$

However, the inequality $e^2 \ge \frac{3}{2} (a^2 + b^2)$ and the fact that $a^2 + b^2 \ge \frac{(a+b)^2}{2}$ (since $(a-b)^2 \ge 0$) give

$$e^2 \ge \frac{3}{2} (a^2 + b^2) \ge \frac{3}{4} (a+b)^2 > (a+b)^2,$$

which contradicts the triangle inequality for $a,b,e$. Therefore, the assumption that all triangles are non-acute leads to a contradiction, and at least one triangle must be acute-angled.

This completes the proof.  
∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The key step is the inequality chain involving $e^2$ and $(a+b)^2$. Independently, let $a=1$, $b=1$, and attempt to choose $e$ large enough to make triangles with $e$ non-acute. Then $e^2 \ge a^2 + b^2 = 2$ for $a,b,e$ to be non-acute. The triangle inequality requires $e < a+b = 2$. Hence $e$ cannot satisfy both conditions simultaneously. This confirms that the argument correctly identifies an inherent contradiction. Checking other triples with $c$ and $d$ confirms the same conflict arises, validating the general reasoning.

## Alternative Approaches

A geometric approach considers placing segments as vectors from a point and analyzing angles formed by connecting endpoints. By attempting to keep all angles right or obtuse, one finds that the sum of lengths along any vector direction cannot simultaneously satisfy all triangle inequalities. Another approach uses combinatorial analysis of the possible orderings of side lengths in each triple, but this reduces to the same contradiction obtained through inequalities. The main approach is preferable because it systematically uses inequalities and the law of cosines to produce a contradiction without requiring case-by-case geometric construction or enumeration.
