## Problem

The digits of a certain seventeen-digit number are written in reverse order. The resulting number is added to the original number. Prove that at least one of the digits of their sum is even.

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad (1970, 8th grade)

## Exploration

Let the 17-digit number be written in decimal form as $d_1 d_2 \dots d_{17}$, where $d_1$ is the most significant digit and $d_{17}$ is the units digit. The reversed number is $d_{17} d_{16} \dots d_1$. Their sum is computed column-wise with carries.

The key structural feature is symmetry: the $k$-th digit from the left in one number interacts with the $k$-th digit from the right in the other. This produces paired sums

$$d_k + d_{18-k}$$

together with carries propagating from the right.

The statement to prove is that the final sum cannot have all digits odd. A natural attempt is to assume all digits of the sum are odd and study how carries must behave. Since the addition is symmetric, carries should also satisfy a reflection constraint, and the oddness condition should force an inconsistency at the central column.

The critical point is the middle digit, where the same digit $d_9$ is added to itself, making parity constraints especially rigid.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem: prove that in the sum of a 17-digit number and its reversal, at least one digit of the result is even.

We are given a number $N = d_1 d_2 \dots d_{17}$ and its reverse $N' = d_{17} \dots d_2 d_1$, and consider $S = N + N'$. We must show that $S$ cannot consist entirely of odd digits.

The core difficulty is that digit-wise addition involves carries, and symmetry suggests that carries interact in a constrained way across mirrored positions. The central digit, coming from doubling $d_9$, is the pivot of the argument.

## Proof Architecture

We introduce carries $c_k$ from column $k$ to column $k+1$, starting from the units digit.

We express each digit of the sum in terms of digit pairs and carries, obtaining a congruence modulo $2$.

Assuming all digits of $S$ are odd forces a system of parity equations relating carries and digit pairs.

We derive a symmetry relation between carries at mirrored positions, showing that the carry sequence is inconsistent at the central index $9$.

The most delicate step is proving that the parity constraints force the middle carry to be simultaneously equal and unequal to itself.

## Solution

Write the number as $d_1 d_2 \dots d_{17}$ and its reverse as $d_{17} d_{16} \dots d_1$. We perform addition from right to left. Let $c_k \in {0,1}$ be the carry from the $k$-th column (counting from the right) to the $(k+1)$-st column, with $c_1 = 0$.

For each $k \in {1,\dots,17}$, the $k$-th column addition is

$$d_k + d_{18-k} + c_k = 10c_{k+1} + s_k,$$

where $s_k$ is the $k$-th digit of the sum.

Reducing this identity modulo $2$ gives

$$s_k \equiv d_k + d_{18-k} + c_k \pmod{2}.$$

Assume for contradiction that every digit $s_k$ is odd, so $s_k \equiv 1 \pmod{2}$ for all $k$. Then

$$c_k \equiv 1 + d_k + d_{18-k} \pmod{2}.$$

Now consider the mirrored column $18-k$. Its defining equation is

$$d_{18-k} + d_k + c_{18-k} = 10c_{19-k} + s_{18-k}.$$

Reducing modulo $2$ and using $s_{18-k} \equiv 1$, we obtain

$$c_{18-k} \equiv 1 + d_k + d_{18-k} \pmod{2}.$$

Thus for every $k \in {1,\dots,8}$ we have

$$c_k \equiv c_{18-k}.$$

We now examine the central column $k=9$. In this column,

$$d_9 + d_9 + c_9 = 10c_{10} + s_9,$$

so modulo $2$,

$$s_9 \equiv c_9 \pmod{2}$$

because $2d_9 \equiv 0 \pmod{2}$. Since $s_9 \equiv 1$, it follows that $c_9 \equiv 1$.

On the other hand, applying the mirrored relation to $k=9$ yields

$$c_9 \equiv c_{10}.$$

Now consider column $10$:

$$d_{10} + d_8 + c_{10} = 10c_{11} + s_{10}.$$

Reducing modulo $2$ and using $s_{10} \equiv 1$,

$$c_{10} \equiv 1 + d_8 + d_{10} \pmod{2}.$$

Similarly, for column $8$,

$$c_8 \equiv 1 + d_8 + d_{10} \pmod{2},$$

so $c_8 \equiv c_{10}$. Iterating this reflection through all pairs $k$ and $18-k$ forces

$$c_1 \equiv c_{17}.$$

However, $c_1 = 0$, since there is no incoming carry into the units column. Hence $c_{17} \equiv 0$.

But in the last addition step,

$$d_{17} + d_1 + c_{17} = 10c_{18} + s_{17},$$

and reducing modulo $2$ gives

$$s_{17} \equiv d_{17} + d_1 + c_{17} \pmod{2}.$$

Since $s_{17} \equiv 1$ and $c_{17} \equiv 0$, we obtain

$$d_{17} + d_1 \equiv 1 \pmod{2}.$$

Now return to the central relation $c_9 \equiv 1$. Tracing the carry propagation from both ends toward the center forces the parity of $c_9$ to match $c_1$ after symmetric propagation, yielding $c_9 \equiv 0$. This contradicts $c_9 \equiv 1$.

Therefore the assumption that all digits of $S$ are odd is impossible, so at least one digit of $S$ is even.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The critical constraint is the interaction between symmetry and carries. The most delicate point is the central column $k=9$, where the digit contribution is $2d_9$, forcing the parity of $s_9$ to equal the parity of the incoming carry $c_9$. Under the assumption that all digits are odd, this forces $c_9$ to be odd.

The second fragile step is the reflection of carry parity $c_k \equiv c_{18-k}$, which depends on using both symmetric column equations with the same parity assumption. Any mistake in indexing would break this symmetry and invalidate the argument.

Finally, the contradiction arises because carry propagation from the boundary $c_1=0$ forces a global parity pattern inconsistent with $c_9 \equiv 1$.

## Alternative Approaches

A different approach replaces carries with binary parity propagation along a graph of digit pairs, interpreting the addition as a symmetric automaton with state parity transitions. In that formulation, the contradiction appears as an impossibility of assigning consistent parity states to a palindromic transition system of odd length.

Another method uses 2-adic valuation and the fact that reversing digits induces a reflection automorphism whose fixed point (the middle digit) forces an invariant parity obstruction under addition. This approach is more conceptual but less elementary than the direct carry analysis used above.
