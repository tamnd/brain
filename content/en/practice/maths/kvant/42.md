## Problem

The digits of a certain seventeen-digit number are written in reverse order. The resulting number is added to the original one. Prove that at least one of the digits of their sum is even.

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad (1970, Grade 8)

## Exploration

Let the original seventeen-digit number be

$$a_{17}a_{16}\ldots a_2a_1,$$

where $a_{17}\neq0$. Its reverse is

$$a_1a_2\ldots a_{16}a_{17}.$$

The statement claims that when these two numbers are added, the resulting sum must contain at least one even digit.

A natural attempt is to assume the contrary, namely that every digit of the sum is odd, and derive a contradiction.

Consider how addition is performed. The digits added in symmetric positions are the same pair: in the units column we add $a_1+a_{17}$, in the tens column we add $a_2+a_{16}$, and so on. The middle column contains $a_9+a_9=2a_9$.

The middle column immediately looks special. Since $2a_9$ is even, the digit written there depends entirely on the carry from the previous column. If all digits of the final sum are odd, then the middle digit must be odd. Hence the carry into the middle column must be odd, and since a carry is either $0$ or $1$, it must be $1$.

What happens to carries around the center? Let $c_k$ be the carry from column $k$ to column $k+1$, counting from the right. The digit written in column $k$ is

$$a_k+a_{18-k}+c_{k-1}\pmod{10}.$$

If that digit is odd, then

$$a_k+a_{18-k}+c_{k-1}$$

is odd. Since $a_k+a_{18-k}=a_{18-k}+a_k$, the parity condition in the symmetric column $18-k$ involves the same digit sum but the carry $c_{17-k}$.

Thus, if all written digits are odd, then

$$c_{k-1}\equiv c_{17-k}\pmod 2.$$

Because carries are only $0$ or $1$, this gives

$$c_{k-1}=c_{17-k}.$$

Taking $k=9$ yields

$$c_8=c_8,$$

which is tautological, but from the middle-column parity we already found $c_8=1$.

Then the symmetry relation forces

$$c_0=c_{17}=1.$$

Yet $c_0=0$ because there is no carry into the units column. This contradiction should finish the proof.

The step most likely to hide an error is the deduction that odd digits in symmetric columns force equality of the corresponding carries. That parity computation must be written carefully.

## Problem Understanding

A seventeen-digit number is added to the number obtained by reversing its digits. We must prove that the resulting sum has at least one even digit.

This is a Type B problem. We are asked to prove a universal statement.

The core difficulty is that the digits of the original number are arbitrary. A direct examination of the sum is impossible. The decisive observation is that the addition process is symmetric: the same pair of digits is added in two columns equidistant from the ends, so parity conditions on the resulting digits impose symmetry conditions on the carries.

## Proof Architecture

Let $c_k$ denote the carry from the $k$-th column to the $(k+1)$-st column, with $c_0=0$.

For each column $k$, the written digit is congruent modulo $2$ to $a_k+a_{18-k}+c_{k-1}$. This holds because reducing modulo $10$ does not change parity.

If every digit of the sum is odd, then for every $k$ the quantity $a_k+a_{18-k}+c_{k-1}$ is odd.

Comparing columns $k$ and $18-k$, the common term $a_k+a_{18-k}$ implies that $c_{k-1}$ and $c_{17-k}$ have the same parity; since each carry is either $0$ or $1$, they are equal.

The middle column is column $9$. Since $2a_9$ is even, an odd digit there forces $c_8=1$.

The carry symmetry then yields $c_0=c_{17}=1$, contradicting $c_0=0$.

The lemma most likely to fail under scrutiny is the symmetry relation $c_{k-1}=c_{17-k}$, so its parity derivation must be checked carefully.

## Solution

Assume, for contradiction, that every digit of the sum is odd.

Write the original seventeen-digit number as

$$a_{17}a_{16}\ldots a_2a_1,$$

and let

$$a_1a_2\ldots a_{16}a_{17}$$

be its reversal.

Number the columns from right to left. Let $c_k$ be the carry from column $k$ to column $k+1$. Thus

$$c_k\in\{0,1\},$$

and

$$c_0=0.$$

In column $k$, where $1\le k\le17$, we add the digits $a_k$ and $a_{18-k}$ together with the incoming carry $c_{k-1}$. Hence the digit written in that column has the same parity as

$$a_k+a_{18-k}+c_{k-1},$$

because subtracting a multiple of $10$ does not change parity.

Since every digit of the sum is assumed odd, for every $k$ we have

$$a_k+a_{18-k}+c_{k-1}\equiv1\pmod2.$$

Replacing $k$ by $18-k$ gives

$$a_{18-k}+a_k+c_{17-k}\equiv1\pmod2.$$

Subtracting these congruences modulo $2$, we obtain

$$c_{k-1}\equiv c_{17-k}\pmod2.$$

Each carry is either $0$ or $1$, so equal parity implies equality:

$$c_{k-1}=c_{17-k}.$$

This holds for every $k$.

Now consider the middle column, $k=9$. There the two added digits are both $a_9$, so

$$a_9+a_9=2a_9$$

is even. The digit written in the ninth column has the same parity as

$$2a_9+c_8.$$

Our assumption says that this digit is odd. Since $2a_9$ is even, it follows that

$$c_8=1.$$

Applying the relation $c_{k-1}=c_{17-k}$ with $k=9$ gives

$$c_8=c_8.$$

Using the same relation repeatedly, we see that all carries paired symmetrically about the center are equal. Since $c_8=1$, we obtain in particular

$$c_0=c_{17}=1.$$

But $c_0=0$ by definition, because there is no carry into the first column.

This contradiction shows that our assumption was false. Hence the sum cannot consist entirely of odd digits. Therefore at least one digit of the sum is even.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate point is the parity of a written digit. If the total in a column is $T$, the written digit is either $T$ or $T-10$. These numbers have the same parity because $10$ is even. Thus the parity of the written digit is exactly the parity of $T=a_k+a_{18-k}+c_{k-1}$.

The second delicate point is the deduction

$$c_{k-1}=c_{17-k}.$$

From the assumption that both symmetric output digits are odd, we obtain

$$a_k+a_{18-k}+c_{k-1}\equiv1\pmod2,$$

and

$$a_k+a_{18-k}+c_{17-k}\equiv1\pmod2.$$

Subtracting gives

$$c_{k-1}\equiv c_{17-k}\pmod2.$$

Since carries can only be $0$ or $1$, parity equality forces actual equality.

The third delicate point is the middle column. The quantity $2a_9$ is always even. If the middle output digit is odd, then the incoming carry must be odd. The only possible odd carry is $1$, so $c_8=1$.

## Alternative Approaches

A different proof can be phrased entirely in terms of parity. If all digits of the sum were odd, then every column total would be odd. For symmetric columns the digit sums $a_k+a_{18-k}$ are identical, so the corresponding incoming carries must have the same parity. Since carries are $0$ or $1$, the carry sequence is symmetric about the middle column.

The middle column contains $2a_9$, an even number. To produce an odd digit there, the incoming carry must be $1$. Symmetry of the carries then propagates this value to the outermost carry, forcing the nonexistent initial carry to equal $1$. This contradiction completes the argument.

The main proof is preferable because it introduces the carry notation explicitly and converts the symmetry of the addition into precise equalities, making every step of the contradiction transparent.
