## Problem

In the table $m\times n$, numbers are written such that for any two rows and any two columns, the sum of the numbers at two opposite vertices of the rectangle formed by them equals the sum of the numbers at the other two vertices. Some of the numbers have been erased, but from the remaining ones the erased values can be restored. Prove that at least $(n+m-1)$ numbers remain.

## Exploration

Let the entry in row $i$ and column $j$ be $a_{ij}$. The condition on every rectangle with corners $(i,j),(i,j'),(i',j),(i',j')$ is

$$a_{ij}+a_{i'j'}=a_{ij'}+a_{i'j}.$$

This is the classical vanishing of the discrete mixed second difference, suggesting that differences between columns do not depend on the row and differences between rows do not depend on the column.

Fixing a reference row $i_0$, the relation

$$a_{ij}-a_{i_0j}=a_{ij'}-a_{i_0j'}$$

shows that the difference between two columns is independent of the row, which strongly indicates a decomposition $a_{ij}=x_i+y_j$.

If such a representation exists, each known entry gives an equation $x_i+y_j=a_{ij}$. The unknowns are $x_1,\dots,x_m,y_1,\dots,y_n$, but adding a constant to all $x_i$ and subtracting it from all $y_j$ preserves all values, so there is one degree of freedom.

Thus the system effectively has $m+n-1$ independent parameters. The statement that all erased values can be restored uniquely suggests that the known entries impose enough constraints to determine all parameters up to that single freedom, which is possible only if the graph of known entries connecting rows and columns is connected. A connected bipartite graph on $m+n$ vertices has at least $m+n-1$ edges, matching the claim.

The critical point is justifying rigorously that the rectangle condition forces the additive decomposition and that uniqueness of completion forces connectivity of the constraint graph.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem. One must prove a lower bound on the number of remaining entries under a structural constraint on the table.

The rectangle condition implies the table has additive rank $1$ structure, meaning each entry can be written as a sum of a row parameter and a column parameter. The reconstruction condition implies that the partial data uniquely determines this structure. The goal is to show that at least $m+n-1$ entries must remain to ensure uniqueness.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma establishes that the rectangle condition implies the existence of functions $x_i$ and $y_j$ such that $a_{ij}=x_i+y_j$, proven by fixing a reference row and showing column differences are consistent.

The second lemma states that any two completions consistent with a given set of known entries correspond to solutions of a system $x_i+y_j=a_{ij}$ on a bipartite graph, and uniqueness of completion is equivalent to connectivity of this graph.

The third lemma shows that a connected bipartite graph on $m+n$ vertices has at least $m+n-1$ edges, with equality attained exactly for trees.

The most delicate step is the equivalence between uniqueness of reconstruction and connectivity, since it requires constructing distinct solutions when a disconnected component exists.

## Solution

The rectangle condition implies that for any indices $i,i',j,j'$, the equality

$$a_{ij}+a_{i'j'}=a_{ij'}+a_{i'j}$$

holds. Fix a row index $i_0$. For any $i,j,j'$, applying the condition with $(i,i_0,j,j')$ gives

$$a_{ij}-a_{i_0j}=a_{ij'}-a_{i_0j'}.$$

The right-hand side does not depend on $j$, hence for each $i$ there exists a number $x_i$ such that $a_{ij}-a_{i_0j}=x_i$ for all $j$. Similarly, defining $y_j=a_{i_0j}$ yields

$$a_{ij}=x_i+y_j.$$

Thus every admissible filling of the table has the form $a_{ij}=x_i+y_j$ for some vectors $(x_i)$ and $(y_j)$.

Each known entry $(i,j)$ provides an equation

$$x_i+y_j=a_{ij}.$$

Consider the bipartite graph whose left vertices correspond to rows $1,\dots,m$, right vertices correspond to columns $1,\dots,n$, and where an edge $(i,j)$ is present exactly when $a_{ij}$ is known. Each connected component of this graph determines the variables within it up to an additive constant, since replacing $x_i$ by $x_i+c$ and $y_j$ by $y_j-c$ for all vertices in a component preserves all equations inside that component.

If the graph has at least two connected components, choose one component and add $1$ to all $x_i$ in that component and subtract $1$ from all $y_j$ in the same component. This transformation leaves all known equations valid, since every edge lies entirely within a component, but changes all values in that component, producing a different completion. This contradicts the assumption that the erased values can be restored uniquely.

Therefore the bipartite graph of known entries is connected. A connected graph on $m+n$ vertices has at least $m+n-1$ edges, since any spanning tree has exactly $m+n-1$ edges and every connected graph contains a spanning tree.

Since edges correspond exactly to remaining entries, at least $m+n-1$ numbers remain.

$\boxed{m+n-1}$

## Verification of Key Steps

The derivation of the form $a_{ij}=x_i+y_j$ uses a fixed reference row and shows that all row differences relative to it are constant across columns; this is justified directly by applying the rectangle identity, ensuring no hidden dependence remains on the chosen column.

The argument that disconnectedness yields non-uniqueness relies on modifying one connected component by adding a constant to all row variables and subtracting it from all column variables inside that component. This preserves all equations on known entries because no equation links different components; hence any such modification produces a distinct valid completion, establishing contradiction with uniqueness.

The bound $m+n-1$ follows from the fact that any connected graph contains a spanning tree, and a tree on $m+n$ vertices has exactly $m+n-1$ edges, which is independent of any algebraic structure and purely combinatorial.

## Alternative Approaches

An alternative proof interprets the condition as stating that the matrix has rank $1$ after subtracting a reference row, reducing the problem to linear algebra: the space of such matrices has dimension $m+n-1$, and uniqueness of completion requires at least that many independent constraints. This approach is shorter but less transparent than the graph-theoretic argument, which directly reflects how entries constrain one another through shared rows and columns.
