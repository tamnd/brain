## Problem

You are looking at a clock. How many positions of the hands are there from which it is impossible to determine the time if one does not know which hand is the hour hand and which is the minute hand?

(_It is assumed that the position of each hand can be determined exactly, but it is not possible to observe how the hands move._)

### Exploration

Represent a clock position by the ordered pair $(h,m)$, where $h$ is the hour hand angle and $m$ is the minute hand angle, measured in minutes around the dial, so both lie in $\mathbb Z_{720}$ with 720 minute-units per full turn. For a real time $t$ (in minutes after 12:00, modulo 720), the minute hand is at $m=t$, while the hour hand is at $h=t/12$ modulo 60, i.e. modulo 720 as $h=t/12$.

Given an unordered observed pair $\{a,b\}$, ambiguity means there are two distinct times $t_1
eq t_2$ such that either $(h(t_1),m(t_1))=(a,b)$ and $(h(t_2),m(t_2))=(b,a)$, or vice versa. So the real question is: when does swapping the two hand positions produce another valid clock state?

Write the clock equations for a time $t$:

$$m=t,\qquad h=t/12\pmod{60}.$$

Suppose swapping gives another valid time $s$:

$$s=h(t)=t/12,\qquad s/12\equiv m(t)=t\pmod{60}.$$

Substitute $s=t/12$ into the second congruence:

$$\frac{t}{144}\equiv t\pmod{60}.$$

Multiply by 144:

$$t\equiv 144t\pmod{8640},$$

hence

$$143t\equiv0\pmod{8640}.$$

Because $\gcd(143,8640)=1$, this forces $t\equiv0\pmod{8640}$. Modulo 720, that means $t=0$, i.e. both hands at 12:00. The swapped configuration is the same configuration, not a second distinct time. So there are no genuinely ambiguous positions.

The only place a hidden error could creep in is the modular arithmetic: the hour hand lives modulo 60 minute-units while the minute hand lives modulo 720, and one must convert them consistently before multiplying congruences.

### Problem Understanding

We observe the two hand positions exactly, but the hands are unlabeled: we do not know which is the hour hand and which is the minute hand, and we cannot watch them move. We must count the clock positions from which the time cannot be uniquely determined.

This is a Type A (classification/counting) problem. We must characterize all ambiguous positions and count them.

The answer should be $0$. Intuitively, if swapping the two observed positions almost never produces another physically possible clock state, then the labeling is forced. The only fixed point of the swap is when the hands coincide, but that gives the same time, not two different times.

### Proof Architecture

1. Lemma 1. A time $t$ (minutes after 12:00, modulo 720) has minute hand position $m=t$ and hour hand position $h=t/12$ modulo 60.
2. Lemma 2. If swapping the two observed hand positions also gives a valid clock state, then the corresponding time $t$ satisfies $143t\equiv0\pmod{8640}$.
3. Lemma 3. Since $\gcd(143,8640)=1$, Lemma 2 implies $t\equiv0\pmod{8640}$, hence modulo 720 the only possibility is $t=0$.
4. Lemma 4. The configuration at $t=0$ has both hands at 12, so swapping the hands does not produce a second distinct time.

The hardest direction is showing that any ambiguous position would force the congruence in Lemma 2. The most scrutiny-worthy step is handling the two different moduli (60 for the hour-hand position in minute-units around the 12-hour dial, and 720 for full time modulo 12 hours) without mixing them incorrectly.

### Solution

Measure angles in minute-units around the dial: one full revolution is $60$ units for positions on the clock face, while time itself is measured modulo $720$ minutes (12 hours).

For a time $t$ minutes after 12:00, taken modulo $720$:

- the minute hand is at position $m=t\pmod{60}$, equivalently at angle determined by $t$ modulo 60;
- the hour hand is at position $h=t/12\pmod{60}$.

Suppose an observed unlabeled pair of hand positions is ambiguous. Then there exist two distinct times $t$ and $s$ such that the ordered hand positions at time $s$ are obtained from those at time $t$ by swapping the hands:

$$m(s)=h(t),\qquad h(s)=m(t).$$

Using the formulas above, these become

$$s\equiv \frac{t}{12}\pmod{60},\qquad \frac{s}{12}\equiv t\pmod{60}.$$

Substitute the first congruence into the second. Working modulo 60,

\frac{1}{12}\left(\frac{t}{12} ight)\equiv t,

that is,

$$\frac{t}{144}\equiv t\pmod{60}.$$

To clear denominators, multiply the congruence by $144$. Since multiplying a congruence modulo 60 by 144 yields a congruence modulo $60\cdot144=8640$, we obtain

$$t\equiv144t\pmod{8640},$$

hence

$$143t\equiv0\pmod{8640}.$$

Now $143=11\cdot13$, and $8640=2^6\cdot3^3\cdot5$. These share no common prime factors, so $\gcd(143,8640)=1$. Therefore the congruence forces

$$t\equiv0\pmod{8640}.$$

Reducing modulo $720$, the only possibility is $t\equiv0\pmod{720}$, i.e. the clock is at 12:00.

At 12:00 the two hands coincide. Swapping them gives the same ordered pair, so there is not a second distinct time producing the same unlabeled observation. Thus no clock position is genuinely ambiguous.

The number of positions from which it is impossible to determine the time is

$$\boxed{0}.$$

### Verification of Key Steps

1. Deriving the congruence correctly. A common mistake is to write everything modulo 60 and then multiply by 144 as if the modulus stayed 60. The safe route is:

$$\frac{t}{144}-t\in60\mathbb Z \;\Longrightarrow\; t-144t\in8640\mathbb Z \;\Longrightarrow\; 143t\equiv0\pmod{8640}.$$

This avoids any ambiguity about how the modulus changes when denominators are cleared.

2. Why the coprimality conclusion is valid. Since $143=11\cdot13$ and $8640=2^6\cdot3^3\cdot5$, we have $\gcd(143,8640)=1$. Therefore multiplication by 143 is invertible modulo 8640, so $143t\equiv0\pmod{8640}$ implies $t\equiv0\pmod{8640}$.

3. Why the coincidence at 12:00 does not count as ambiguity. Ambiguity requires two different times compatible with the same unlabeled observation. At $t=0$ both hands are at 12, and swapping them leaves the configuration unchanged. There is no second distinct time. A careless argument that counts any fixed point of the swap would incorrectly report one ambiguous position.

### Alternative Approaches

A geometric version works directly with angles. Let the minute hand angle be $6t^\circ$ and the hour hand angle be $frac12 t^\circ$, with $t$ in minutes modulo 720. If swapping the observed angles also corresponds to a valid time $s$, then

$$6s\equiv 	frac12 t\pmod{360},\qquad 	frac12 s\equiv 6t\pmod{360}.$$

Substituting $s\equiv t/12$ from the first relation into the second gives $t/24\equiv 6t\pmod{360}$, hence $143t\equiv0\pmod{8640}$ after clearing denominators exactly as above.

The main solution is preferable because it separates time modulo 720 from position modulo 60 explicitly, making the modular arithmetic and the uniqueness conclusion easier to audit.
