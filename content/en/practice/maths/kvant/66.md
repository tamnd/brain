## Problem

Here are several examples where the sum of the squares of $k$ consecutive positive integers is equal to the sum of the squares of the next $k-1$ positive integers: $$\begin{gathered} 3^2+4^2=5^2,\\ 36^2+37^2+38^2+39^2+40^2=41^2+42^2+43^2+44^2,\\ 55^2+56^2+57^2+58^2+59^2+60^2=61^2+62^2+63^2+64^2+65^2. \end{gathered}$$

Find a general formula covering all such cases.

A. I. Milovanov

## Exploration

We are asked to find sequences of consecutive positive integers whose squares sum in a very particular pattern: the sum of the squares of $k$ consecutive integers equals the sum of the squares of the next $k-1$ integers. The examples given suggest that the sequences start near each other and that $k$ increases by one each time.

Let $a$ be the first integer of the $k$-term sequence. Then the sum of squares of $k$ consecutive numbers starting from $a$ is

$\sum_{i=0}^{k-1} (a+i)^2 = \sum_{i=0}^{k-1} (a^2 + 2ai + i^2) = k a^2 + 2 a \sum_{i=0}^{k-1} i + \sum_{i=0}^{k-1} i^2.$

The sum of the first $k-1$ squares after this sequence, starting at $a+k$, is

$\sum_{i=0}^{k-2} (a+k+i)^2 = (k-1)(a+k)^2 + 2(a+k) \sum_{i=0}^{k-2} i + \sum_{i=0}^{k-2} i^2.$

Setting these equal yields a quadratic equation in $a$ and $k$. Simplifying the sums $\sum_{i=0}^{n} i = n(n+1)/2$ and $\sum_{i=0}^{n} i^2 = n(n+1)(2n+1)/6$ produces an explicit Diophantine equation.

Trying small $k$ confirms the examples: $k=2$ gives $a=3$, $k=5$ gives $a=36$, $k=6$ gives $a=55$. The differences between starting numbers and $k$ are reminiscent of triangular numbers. This suggests a formula involving triangular numbers.

The crucial point is the algebraic simplification of the sums into a quadratic Diophantine equation and solving it for $a$ in terms of $k$. Any miscalculation in the sums or coefficients will yield incorrect candidates.

## Problem Understanding

The problem asks to classify all sequences of consecutive positive integers where the sum of the squares of $k$ consecutive integers equals the sum of the squares of the next $k-1$ consecutive integers. This is a Type A problem: a complete characterization of all solutions is required.

The core difficulty is to reduce the problem to a manageable Diophantine equation in $a$ and $k$ and then find a general solution for all positive integers. Initial exploration suggests that the solution involves triangular numbers or quadratic forms.

Intuitively, the pattern of examples suggests a recurrence or explicit formula connecting $a$ and $k$ through a quadratic relationship, which must be derived rigorously.

Lemma 2: The sum of squares of $k-1$ consecutive integers starting at $a+k$ is $(k-1)(a+k)^2 + (k-1)(k-2)(a+k) + \frac{(k-1)(k-2)(2k-3)}{6}$. Verified similarly.

## Solution

Let $a$ be the first number of the $k$ consecutive integers. The sum of their squares is

$\sum_{i=0}^{k-1} (a+i)^2 = k a^2 + 2a \sum_{i=0}^{k-1} i + \sum_{i=0}^{k-1} i^2 = k a^2 + 2a \cdot \frac{(k-1)k}{2} + \frac{(k-1)k(2k-1)}{6} = k a^2 + k(k-1)a + \frac{k(k-1)(2k-1)}{6}.$

The sum of squares of the next $k-1$ integers starting at $a+k$ is

\begin{align*}

\sum_{i=0}^{k-2} (a+k+i)^2 &= (k-1)(a+k)^2 + 2(a+k) \sum_{i=0}^{k-2} i + \sum_{i=0}^{k-2} i^2 \

&= (k-1)(a+k)^2 + 2(a+k)\cdot \frac{(k-2)(k-1)}{2} + \frac{(k-2)(k-1)(2k-3)}{6} \

&= (k-1)(a+k)^2 + (k-1)(k-2)(a+k) + \frac{(k-1)(k-2)(2k-3)}{6}.

\end{align*}

Equating the two sums gives

$k a^2 + k(k-1)a + \frac{k(k-1)(2k-1)}{6} = (k-1)(a+k)^2 + (k-1)(k-2)(a+k) + \frac{(k-1)(k-2)(2k-3)}{6}.$

Expanding the right-hand side:

\begin{align*}

(k-1)(a+k)^2 &= (k-1)(a^2 + 2ak + k^2) = (k-1)a^2 + 2(k-1)ak + (k-1)k^2, \

(k-1)(k-2)(a+k) &= (k-1)(k-2)a + (k-1)(k-2)k, \

\frac{(k-1)(k-2)(2k-3)}{6} &= \frac{(k-1)(k-2)(2k-3)}{6}.

\end{align*}

Adding these terms:

$(k-1)a^2 + 2(k-1)ak + (k-1)k^2 + (k-1)(k-2)a + (k-1)(k-2)k + \frac{(k-1)(k-2)(2k-3)}{6}.$

Combining like terms for $a^2$, $a$, and constants, and subtracting the left-hand side yields

\begin{align*}

& k a^2 - (k-1)a^2 + k(k-1)a - 2(k-1)ak - (k-1)(k-2)a + \frac{k(k-1)(2k-1)}{6} - (k-1)k^2 - (k-1)(k-2)k - \frac{(k-1)(k-2)(2k-3)}{6} = 0.

\end{align*}

Simplifying each coefficient:

For $a^2$: $k - (k-1) = 1$, giving $a^2$.

For $a$: $k(k-1) - 2(k-1)k - (k-1)(k-2) = k^2 - k - 2k^2 + 2k - k^2 + 3k - 2 = -2k^2 + 4k - 2 = -2(k-1)^2$.

For the constants:

\begin{align*}

\frac{k(k-1)(2k-1)}{6} - (k-1)k^2 - (k-1)(k-2)k - \frac{(k-1)(k-2)(2k-3)}{6} &= \frac{k(k-1)(2k-1) - 6(k-1)k^2 - 6(k-1)(k-2)k - (k-1)(k-2)(2k-3)}{6} \

&= \frac{-6(k-1)^3}{6} = -(k-1)^3.

\end{align*}

$a^2 - 2(k-1)^2 a - (k-1)^3 = 0.$
