## Problem

Here are several examples where the sum of squares of $k$ consecutive natural numbers equals the sum of squares of $k-1$ subsequent natural numbers: $$\begin{gathered} 3^2+4^2=5^2,\\ 36^2+37^2+38^2+39^2+40^2=41^2+42^2+43^2+44^2,\\ 55^2+56^2+57^2+58^2+59^2+60^2=61^2+62^2+63^2+64^2+65^2. \end{gathered}$$

Find the general formula encompassing all such cases.

A. I. Milovanov

## Exploration

Let the first block contain $k$ consecutive natural numbers starting from $a$, and the second block contain $k-1$ consecutive natural numbers starting from $a+k$. The equality becomes

$\sum_{i=0}^{k-1}(a+i)^2=\sum_{i=0}^{k-2}(a+k+i)^2.$

Expanding both sides leads to a quadratic equation in $a$. The left-hand side is

$k a^2 + a k(k-1) + \frac{(k-1)k(2k-1)}{6}.$

The right-hand side expands as

$(k-1)(a+k)^2 + (a+k)(k-1)(k-2) + \frac{(k-2)(k-1)(2k-3)}{6}.$

Subtracting the right-hand side from the left produces a quadratic in $a$ whose discriminant simplifies unexpectedly. The structure suggests that the discriminant becomes a perfect square in $k$, which would yield a clean parametric family rather than isolated solutions. Testing the given examples supports this: for $k=2,5,6$ the starting values are $3,36,55$, and these match a quadratic expression in $k$.

The critical point is whether the resulting quadratic in $a$ indeed has a discriminant that is a perfect square for all $k$, and whether the admissible root is always positive.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem: determine all pairs of blocks of consecutive natural numbers such that the sum of squares of $k$ consecutive numbers equals the sum of squares of the next $k-1$ consecutive numbers.

The structure of the examples indicates a one-parameter family indexed by $k$. The task is to derive the general starting value $a$ in terms of $k$ and verify that it produces all solutions.

The expected outcome is an explicit formula for $a(k)$.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma expresses the sum of squares of consecutive integers in closed form using standard summation identities.

The second lemma computes the difference between the two sums and reduces it to a quadratic equation in $a$ with coefficients depending on $k$.

The third lemma simplifies the discriminant of this quadratic and shows it is a perfect square, enabling exact solution.

The final step selects the admissible root and verifies that it generates valid natural-number blocks.

The most delicate step is the simplification of the discriminant, where cancellation must be handled exactly to reveal the square structure.

## Solution

Let the first block be $a,a+1,\dots,a+k-1$ and the second block be $a+k,a+k+1,\dots,a+2k-2$. The equality to be solved is

$\sum_{i=0}^{k-1}(a+i)^2=\sum_{i=0}^{k-2}(a+k+i)^2.$

Using standard summation formulas,

$\sum_{i=0}^{k-1}(a+i)^2 = k a^2 + a k(k-1) + \frac{(k-1)k(2k-1)}{6},$

and

$\sum_{i=0}^{k-2}(a+k+i)^2 = (k-1)(a+k)^2 + (a+k)(k-1)(k-2) + \frac{(k-2)(k-1)(2k-3)}{6}.$

Equating and expanding the second expression yields

$(k-1)a^2 +2a k(k-1) + (k-1)k^2 + a(k-1)(k-2) + k(k-1)(k-2) + \frac{(k-2)(k-1)(2k-3)}{6}.$

Subtracting the right-hand side from the left-hand side gives a quadratic equation in $a$. The coefficient of $a^2$ becomes

$k-(k-1)=1,$

so the quadratic term is $a^2$.

The coefficient of $a$ simplifies as

$k(k-1)-(k-1)(3k-2)=(k-1)(2-2k)=-2(k-1)^2.$

The constant term simplifies after grouping the remaining parts:

$\frac{(k-1)k(2k-1)}{6}-2k(k-1)^2-\frac{(k-2)(k-1)(2k-3)}{6}=-(2k-1)(k-1)^2.$

Hence $a$ satisfies

$a^2-2(k-1)^2 a-(2k-1)(k-1)^2=0.$

Solving the quadratic equation gives

$a=\frac{2(k-1)^2\pm\sqrt{4(k-1)^4+4(2k-1)(k-1)^2}}{2}.$

Factoring inside the square root,

$4(k-1)^2\bigl((k-1)^2+2k-1\bigr)=4(k-1)^2 k^2,$

so the square root equals $2k(k-1)$. Thus

$a=(k-1)^2 \pm k(k-1).$

This yields two candidates:

$a=(k-1)(2k-1) \quad \text{or} \quad a=-(k-1).$

Only the positive value produces natural numbers, so

$a=(k-1)(2k-1).$

Therefore all solutions are given by

$\sum_{i=0}^{k-1}\bigl((k-1)(2k-1)+i\bigr)^2=\sum_{i=0}^{k-2}\bigl((k-1)(2k-1)+k+i\bigr)^2,\quad k\ge 2.$

Hence the general formula is

$\boxed{a=(k-1)(2k-1)}.$

## Verification of Key Steps

The most sensitive step is the simplification of the constant term in the quadratic reduction. Recomputing directly from grouped expressions shows that all terms factor a common $(k-1)$ and reduce to a linear polynomial in $k$ after cancellation. The remaining expression combines with the contribution $-2k(k-1)^2$ to produce $-(2k-1)(k-1)^2$, matching the coefficient required for the discriminant to become a perfect square.

The discriminant computation is independently confirmed by expanding

$4(k-1)^4+4(2k-1)(k-1)^2=4(k-1)^2\bigl((k-1)^2+2k-1\bigr)=4k^2(k-1)^2,$

which forces a square root $2k(k-1)$ and eliminates any ambiguity in the solution structure.

Substituting $a=(k-1)(2k-1)$ into the original equality for $k=2$ gives $3^2+4^2=5^2$, and for $k=5$ gives $36^2+\cdots+40^2=41^2+\cdots+44^2$, confirming correctness in representative cases.

## Alternative Approaches

A different method begins by shifting the second sum to align indices and expressing the difference as a polynomial in $k$ and $a$ using Faulhaber-type expansions. The resulting expression again reduces to a quadratic in $a$ whose discriminant collapses to a perfect square, but this approach hides the cancellation structure that makes the solution transparent.

The direct expansion method is preferable because it isolates the algebraic simplification leading to the perfect square discriminant in a single step, making the parametric form of $a$ unavoidable rather than guessed.
