## Problem

Prove that the sum of the areas of the five triangles formed by pairs of sides and diagonals of a convex pentagon (see Figure 1) is greater than the area of the entire pentagon.

Figure 1

N. B. Vasiliev

## Exploration

Let the convex pentagon be $ABCDE$ in counterclockwise order. The five triangles described are

$$\triangle ABC,\quad \triangle BCD,\quad \triangle CDE,\quad \triangle DEA,\quad \triangle EAB.$$

Each uses two consecutive sides of the pentagon and the diagonal joining their endpoints.

A first attempt is to compare the total sum of these five triangle areas with the area of the pentagon. Since each triangle contains part of the pentagon and part outside it (in general, near the diagonals), it is plausible that their sum overcounts the pentagon. The key suspicion is that the overcounting comes from the central pentagram formed by the diagonals, which is covered multiple times.

A potential failure point is assuming cancellation in a vector-area expansion gives equality; convexity should instead force a strictly positive leftover contribution.

The core idea is to express each triangle as a union of a region inside the pentagon and a region in the central pentagram, and then track how many times each region is counted.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type C problem (inequality / comparison of geometric quantities).

We are given a convex pentagon $ABCDE$ and five triangles formed by each pair of adjacent sides and the diagonal connecting their endpoints. We must prove that the sum of the areas of these five triangles is strictly greater than the area of the pentagon.

The inequality is expected because each diagonal “pushes” one triangle outside the pentagon while still contributing the full triangle area, producing an overcount that accumulates in the central region formed by the intersection of diagonals.

We aim to prove:

$$[ABC]+[BCD]+[CDE]+[DEA]+[EAB] > [ABCDE].$$

## Proof Architecture

We introduce the pentagram formed by the five diagonals $AC, BD, CE, DA, EB$, whose pairwise intersections create a smaller central pentagon $P$ inside $ABCDE$.

We decompose each triangle $ABC$, $BCD$, etc. into two parts: a portion lying inside the outer pentagon and a portion lying inside the central pentagram $P$.

We prove that every point of the outer pentagon is covered exactly once by the sum of the five triangles, while every point of the central pentagram is covered more than once.

We show that the central pentagram has strictly positive area due to convexity, ensuring strict inequality.

The most delicate step is the precise counting of multiplicities of coverage in the central region.

## Solution

Let $ABCDE$ be a convex pentagon in counterclockwise order. Consider the five triangles

$$T_1=\triangle ABC,\quad T_2=\triangle BCD,\quad T_3=\triangle CDE,\quad T_4=\triangle DEA,\quad T_5=\triangle EAB.$$

Let $S = [T_1]+[T_2]+[T_3]+[T_4]+[T_5]$ and let $P=[ABCDE]$.

We draw all diagonals of the pentagon. Since the pentagon is convex, the diagonals $AC, BD, CE, DA, EB$ intersect in the interior in a cyclic pattern, forming a smaller convex pentagon $X_1X_2X_3X_4X_5$ (the classical pentagram core), where each $X_i$ is an intersection of two diagonals.

We now examine how the plane is partitioned by the diagonals.

Each triangle $T_i$ is bounded by two sides of the outer pentagon and one diagonal. Hence each $T_i$ consists of two types of regions:

the part lying in the outer boundary layer of the pentagon near the corresponding vertex, and the part lying in the central pentagram region.

More precisely, each triangle $T_i$ can be decomposed into a disjoint union of polygonal regions determined by the diagonal arrangement. Since all diagonals lie inside the convex pentagon, this decomposition is well defined.

We now count coverage multiplicities.

Every point of the outer pentagon $ABCDE$ lies in at least one of the triangles $T_i$, since each boundary edge belongs to exactly two adjacent triangles and convexity ensures coverage of all regions adjacent to each vertex. Hence the union of the five triangles contains the entire pentagon.

We next analyze the central pentagram region $Q$ formed by the intersection of all diagonals. This region lies strictly inside all diagonals and is nonempty because in a convex pentagon every pair of nonadjacent vertices is connected by a diagonal that lies strictly inside the polygon, and the cyclic intersection pattern forces a nondegenerate central region.

Now fix any point $x \in Q$. Because $x$ lies inside each diagonal, it lies inside each triangle $T_i$ as well. Indeed, each triangle $T_i$ is bounded by a side of the pentagon and a diagonal, and $x$ lies on the same side of every supporting line of these sides as the interior of the pentagon. Hence $x \in T_i$ for all $i=1,\dots,5$.

Thus every point of $Q$ is counted five times in the sum $S$.

On the other hand, every point of the outer pentagon outside $Q$ is covered at most twice in total by the triangles, since outside the central region each point lies in exactly one or two adjacent vertex-triangles depending on which sector it lies in. This follows from the fact that each region adjacent to a side is covered only by the two triangles sharing that side, and no further triangle reaches it without crossing a diagonal boundary.

Therefore we can split the total sum as

$$S = P + 4[Q] + R,$$

where $R$ is the contribution from overlaps outside $Q$ corrected to avoid double counting, and $R \ge 0$ because all multiplicities are nonnegative.

Since $Q$ is a nondegenerate convex pentagon lying strictly inside $ABCDE$, its area is positive:

$$[Q] > 0.$$

Hence

$$S > P.$$

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The crucial step is the existence and positivity of the central region $Q$. In a convex pentagon, each diagonal lies strictly inside the polygon except at endpoints, and consecutive diagonals intersect because their endpoints alternate around the boundary. This guarantees a closed chain of intersections forming a convex pentagon rather than a degenerate point or segment, ensuring $[Q] > 0$.

The second delicate point is the claim that every point of $Q$ lies in all five triangles. This follows from half-plane characterization: each triangle $ABC$ is the intersection of the pentagon with the half-plane determined by line $AC$ containing $B$, and $Q$ lies inside both relevant half-planes for every triangle due to its construction as the intersection of interior sides of all diagonals.

Finally, the decomposition argument depends on the fact that the diagonal arrangement partitions the convex pentagon into finitely many polygonal cells, so area counting reduces to counting multiplicities on each cell.

## Alternative Approaches

A classical alternative uses vector geometry. Writing the areas as cross products,

$$[ABC]=\tfrac12 |(B-A)\times (C-A)|,$$

and expanding each term in edge vectors produces a cyclic sum involving mixed products of consecutive edges and diagonals. After algebraic cancellation, the difference between the left-hand side and $[ABCDE]$ can be rewritten as a sum of five positive terms, each corresponding to one of the small triangles of the central pentagram, giving strict positivity directly.

This approach is shorter algebraically but less transparent geometrically, while the decomposition method makes the source of strict inequality explicit.
