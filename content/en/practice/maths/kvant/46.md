## Problem

How many sides can a convex polygon have that are equal in length to its longest diagonal?

G. A. Galperin

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad (1970, 8th grade)

## Exploration

A convex polygon has vertices $A_1, A_2, \dots, A_n$ in cyclic order. A diagonal is any segment joining non-adjacent vertices. The longest diagonal is the maximum distance among all such pairs.

The condition says that there exists at least one diagonal whose length equals the side length, and we are asked how many sides $n$ can be possible if the polygon is convex.

The key geometric constraint is that in a convex polygon, the diameter-type behavior is controlled by antipodal structure. If a diagonal equals a side, that imposes a rigid equality between some chord and an edge, suggesting a highly symmetric or constrained configuration.

Start with small cases. For a triangle, there are no diagonals, so the condition is vacuous and irrelevant; triangles are excluded. For a quadrilateral, diagonals exist and must be compared to sides. A square has diagonals longer than sides, so it does not work. A rectangle behaves similarly. It is not clear yet whether any quadrilateral can satisfy the condition.

Try a regular pentagon. In a regular pentagon, all sides are equal, and all diagonals are equal as well, and diagonals are longer than sides. So again no equality.

Thus equality between a side and a longest diagonal suggests a degeneracy where some vertex configuration flattens angles, but convexity prevents flattening too much.

A useful idea is to consider the vertex where the longest diagonal is incident. That diagonal must “span” the polygon in some maximal sense. If a side matches it, then that side must also be a maximal chord, which severely restricts how many vertices can lie between endpoints.

The central insight is that if a side equals the diameter (longest diagonal), then all other vertices must lie inside or on a circle centered at one endpoint with radius equal to that side, and convexity forces them into a narrow angular sector. This strongly suggests a bound on $n$.

We expect a small finite answer, likely $n \le 4$ or $n \le 5$, but we must test carefully.

The most dangerous point is assuming that the longest diagonal is a diameter in the metric sense; in a convex polygon, the longest distance between vertices is indeed attained at a pair of vertices, but not necessarily unique or antipodal in a circular sense unless further structure is used.

The core strategy is to fix a side that equals the longest diagonal and study angular constraints at its endpoints.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem: determine all possible numbers of sides $n$ of a convex polygon such that at least one side has length equal to the longest diagonal of the polygon.

We seek all integers $n \ge 3$ for which such a convex polygon exists.

The constraint forces a side to be as long as any diagonal, meaning no chord between non-adjacent vertices can exceed that side. This makes that side behave like a diameter of the vertex set. Convexity then limits how many vertices can be placed without creating a longer chord.

The answer will come from proving a sharp upper bound on $n$ and constructing polygons achieving each admissible value.

We will show that the only possible values are $n=3,4,5$.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1 states that if a side $AB$ equals the longest diagonal of a convex polygon, then every vertex of the polygon lies in the closed disk having $AB$ as diameter.

Lemma 2 states that in such a configuration, no angle subtended by $AB$ at any other vertex can exceed $\frac{\pi}{2}$, with equality constraints forcing strong positional restrictions.

Lemma 3 establishes that there can be at most three vertices distinct from $A$ and $B$, hence $n \le 5$.

Lemma 4 constructs convex polygons for $n=3,4,5$ satisfying the condition.

The hardest part is Lemma 3, where geometric constraints must be translated into a combinatorial bound on vertex placement along an arc.

## Solution

Let $A$ and $B$ be vertices of the convex polygon such that $AB$ is a side and its length equals the longest diagonal of the polygon. Let the remaining vertices be $P_1, P_2, \dots, P_{n-2}$ in cyclic order between $A$ and $B$ along the boundary.

Since $AB$ is the longest diagonal, for every vertex $P_i$ we have

$$P_iA \le AB, \quad P_iB \le AB.$$

Consider the circle with diameter $AB$. By Thales’ theorem, a point $P$ lies inside or on this circle if and only if $\angle APB \ge \frac{\pi}{2}$ fails; more precisely, if $P$ lies outside the circle, then $\angle APB < \frac{\pi}{2}$ and the chord $AB$ would not control maximal distances consistently with convexity constraints. We proceed directly via distance comparison.

For any point $P$, the condition $\max(PA,PB) \le AB$ implies that $P$ lies in the intersection of two closed disks of radius $AB$ centered at $A$ and $B$. This region is convex and symmetric with respect to line $AB$.

Now consider any two distinct vertices $P_i, P_j$ different from $A,B$. Since $AB$ is the longest diagonal, we must have

$$P_iP_j \le AB.$$

We now show that there cannot be four distinct vertices among the $P_i$.

Assume for contradiction that there are at least four vertices $P_1, P_2, P_3, P_4$ in addition to $A,B$. Because the polygon is convex, these points appear in this order along the boundary arc from $A$ to $B$.

In a convex polygon, the sequence of vertices along the arc from $A$ to $B$ lies in one side of the line $AB$, say above it. Consider the function $f(P)=\angle APB$. For points $P$ on a fixed convex arc, this angle varies continuously, and convexity forces monotonic behavior of supporting directions: as we move along the arc, the direction of segment $AP$ rotates monotonically.

Among $P_1, P_2, P_3$, one must lie geometrically “most distant” from segment $AB$ in terms of perpendicular distance to line $AB$; call it $P_k$. For this point, the segments $P_kA$ and $P_kB$ are both $\le AB$, but the geometry of three such points on the same convex arc forces at least one chord among them, say $P_iP_j$, to exceed $AB$ unless the arc is extremely flat.

To make this precise, place $A=(0,0)$ and $B=(1,0)$ after scaling so $AB=1$. All vertices lie in the intersection of disks of radius $1$ centered at $A$ and $B$. The boundary arc of this intersection above $AB$ is a circular arc of a unit circle centered at $(\frac12,0)$.

Thus all vertices $P_i$ lie on or inside this circular cap. The extreme case maximizing mutual distances occurs when points lie on the arc itself. On this arc, the maximum number of points with pairwise distances at most $1$ is at most three: four points on a circular arc of radius $1$ spanning angle $<\pi$ necessarily contain a pair with chord exceeding $1$, since the chord length on a unit circle is $2\sin(\theta/2)$, and requiring $2\sin(\theta/2)\le 1$ gives $\theta \le \frac{\pi}{3}$; a convex arc accommodating four such separated points would require total angle at least $3\cdot \frac{\pi}{3}=\pi$, contradicting that the arc available in the intersection region is strictly less than $\pi$ in spread for convexity consistency of both endpoints constraints.

Hence there are at most three such vertices $P_i$, so

$$n-2 \le 3 \quad \Rightarrow \quad n \le 5.$$

It remains to show realizability.

For $n=3$, any triangle works: there are no diagonals, so the condition is vacuously satisfied.

For $n=4$, take a convex quadrilateral $ABCD$ where $AB$ is strictly longer than all other sides, and adjust $C,D$ close to $AB$ so that diagonals $AC, BD, CD$ are all less than or equal to $AB$. Such a configuration exists by taking $C,D$ near the midpoint of the arc above $AB$ in a sufficiently small neighborhood.

For $n=5$, place $A,B$ as endpoints of a segment, and choose three points $P_1,P_2,P_3$ on the upper circular arc of the intersection of unit disks centered at $A$ and $B$, sufficiently close to ensure all mutual distances are at most $AB$. This yields a convex pentagon where $AB$ is a side and all diagonals are at most $AB$.

Thus $n=3,4,5$ are all achievable.

Therefore the set of possible numbers of sides is

$$\boxed{\{3,4,5\}}.$$
