## Problem

How many sides can a convex polygon have whose length is equal to the length of its longest diagonal?

G. A. Galperin

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad (1970, Grade 8)

## Exploration

Let the convex polygon have vertices $A_1,A_2,\dots,A_n$ in cyclic order. Denote by $L$ the length of its longest diagonal. The condition says that every side of the polygon also has length $L$.

The first question is whether such polygons exist for large $n$. A regular pentagon does not work, because its side is shorter than its diagonal. A square works: the side length equals the diagonal length only if the square degenerates, so an ordinary square does not satisfy the condition. An equilateral triangle has no diagonals.

Try a quadrilateral. If all sides have the same length $L$ and the longest diagonal also has length $L$, then both diagonals are at most $L$. In a rhombus with side $L$, the diagonals satisfy

$$d_1^2+d_2^2=4L^2.$$

If both $d_1,d_2\le L$, then

$$d_1^2+d_2^2\le 2L^2,$$

contradicting the identity. Hence no convex quadrilateral works.

This suggests that perhaps no convex polygon with $n\ge4$ exists.

Take any vertex $A_i$. The two adjacent sides $A_{i-1}A_i$ and $A_iA_{i+1}$ both have length $L$. The triangle $A_{i-1}A_iA_{i+1}$ has third side $A_{i-1}A_{i+1}$, which is a diagonal when $n\ge4$. Since $L$ is the length of the longest diagonal,

$$A_{i-1}A_{i+1}\le L.$$

In a triangle whose two sides are $L$ and whose third side is at most $L$, the angle at the common vertex is at most $60^\circ$.

Thus every interior angle of the polygon is at most $120^\circ$.

For a convex $n$-gon the sum of interior angles equals $(n-2)180^\circ$. If each is at most $120^\circ$, then

$$(n-2)180^\circ\le 120^\circ n,$$

which gives

$$n\le6.$$

Now examine $n=6$. Then the angle sum is $720^\circ$, exactly $6\cdot120^\circ$, so every interior angle must equal $120^\circ$. Then each triangle $A_{i-1}A_iA_{i+1}$ has sides $L,L$ and included angle $120^\circ$, hence its third side is

$$\sqrt{L^2+L^2-2L^2\cos120^\circ} =\sqrt{3}\,L>L,$$

contradicting that this third side is a diagonal and $L$ is the maximum diagonal length.

Hence $n\le5$.

For $n=5$, the angle sum is $540^\circ$. Since each angle is at most $120^\circ$, equality forces every angle to be $108^\circ$. Then each short diagonal $A_{i-1}A_{i+1}$ has length

$$\sqrt{L^2+L^2-2L^2\cos108^\circ}>L,$$

again impossible.

Therefore $n\le4$.

The quadrilateral case was already ruled out. Thus only $n=3$ remains, and a triangle indeed satisfies the condition vacuously because it has no diagonals. The likely answer is $3$.

The step most likely to hide an error is the deduction that every interior angle is at most $120^\circ$ from the condition on the diagonal $A_{i-1}A_{i+1}$.

## Problem Understanding

We seek all possible numbers of sides of a convex polygon whose side length is equal to the length of its longest diagonal.

This is a Type A problem. We must determine every possible value of $n$ and prove that no other value can occur.

The core difficulty is extracting a global restriction on the polygon from the local fact that every side has the same length as the longest diagonal. The crucial observation is that, at each vertex, the diagonal joining the two neighboring vertices cannot exceed the common side length, which forces every interior angle to be at most $120^\circ$.

The answer is $n=3$. A triangle has no diagonals, so the condition is satisfied vacuously. For polygons with at least four sides, the angle restriction becomes too strong.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1. If $n\ge4$, then every side has the same length $L$, where $L$ is the length of the longest diagonal; this is merely a restatement of the condition.

Lemma 2. If $n\ge4$, every interior angle of the polygon is at most $120^\circ$; in the triangle formed by two adjacent sides and the diagonal joining their other endpoints, the third side is at most $L$, and the law of cosines bounds the included angle.

Lemma 3. A convex polygon with all interior angles at most $120^\circ$ satisfies $n\le6$; compare the angle sum $(n-2)180^\circ$ with $120^\circ n$.

Lemma 4. The cases $n=6$ and $n=5$ are impossible; the angle-sum condition forces all interior angles to be respectively $120^\circ$ and $108^\circ$, and then the law of cosines gives a diagonal longer than $L$.

Lemma 5. The case $n=4$ is impossible; applying Lemma 2 gives all angles at most $120^\circ$, but the angle sum $360^\circ$ forces all four angles to be exactly $90^\circ$, and then a diagonal has length $\sqrt2,L>L$.

The hardest direction is excluding all polygons with $n\ge4$. The most delicate lemma is Lemma 2, where the angle bound is extracted from the diagonal condition.

## Solution

Let the common side length and the length of the longest diagonal be $L$.

We determine all possible numbers $n$ of sides of the polygon.

Assume first that $n\ge4$. Let $A_{i-1},A_i,A_{i+1}$ be three consecutive vertices.

Since every side has length $L$,

$$A_{i-1}A_i=A_iA_{i+1}=L.$$

The segment $A_{i-1}A_{i+1}$ is a diagonal of the polygon. Since $L$ is the length of the longest diagonal,

$$A_{i-1}A_{i+1}\le L.$$

Let $\alpha_i$ be the interior angle at $A_i$. Applying the law of cosines in triangle $A_{i-1}A_iA_{i+1}$,

$$A_{i-1}A_{i+1}^2 = L^2+L^2-2L^2\cos\alpha_i = 2L^2(1-\cos\alpha_i).$$

Since $A_{i-1}A_{i+1}\le L$,

$$2L^2(1-\cos\alpha_i)\le L^2.$$

Dividing by $L^2$ gives

$$2(1-\cos\alpha_i)\le1,$$

hence

$$\cos\alpha_i\ge\frac12.$$

The angle in the triangle at $A_i$ is the exterior supplement of the polygonal interior angle, namely $180^\circ-\alpha_i$. Thus

$$\cos(180^\circ-\alpha_i)\ge\frac12,$$

which yields

$$180^\circ-\alpha_i\le60^\circ,$$

and therefore

$$\alpha_i\le120^\circ.$$

Every interior angle of the polygon is at most $120^\circ$.

Summing over all vertices,

$$(n-2)180^\circ = \sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i \le120^\circ n.$$

Hence

$$180n-360\le120n,$$

so

$$60n\le360,$$

and therefore

$$n\le6.$$

We now exclude $n=6$.

For a convex hexagon,

$$\sum \alpha_i=(6-2)180^\circ=720^\circ.$$

Since each $\alpha_i\le120^\circ$ and there are six angles, equality in

$$\sum\alpha_i\le6\cdot120^\circ=720^\circ$$

forces

$$\alpha_1=\cdots=\alpha_6=120^\circ.$$

Consider three consecutive vertices. In the corresponding triangle the angle at the middle vertex equals

$$180^\circ-120^\circ=60^\circ.$$

The law of cosines gives

$$A_{i-1}A_{i+1}^2 = L^2+L^2-2L^2\cos60^\circ = L^2,$$

so

$$A_{i-1}A_{i+1}=L.$$

Thus every such diagonal has length $L$.

Then triangle $A_{i-1}A_iA_{i+1}$ is equilateral. Repeating this for each $i$, all consecutive triples of vertices form equilateral triangles. The polygon must follow successive edges turning by $60^\circ$ each time, which after six sides closes as a regular hexagon. A regular hexagon has long diagonals of length $2L$, larger than $L$, contradicting the assumption that the longest diagonal has length $L$.

Hence $n\ne6$.

Next consider $n=5$.

The angle sum equals

$$(5-2)180^\circ=540^\circ.$$

Since every angle is at most $120^\circ$, at least one angle satisfies

$$\alpha_i\ge108^\circ.$$

For that vertex,

$$180^\circ-\alpha_i\le72^\circ.$$

Applying the law of cosines in triangle $A_{i-1}A_iA_{i+1}$,

$$A_{i-1}A_{i+1}^2 = L^2+L^2-2L^2\cos(180^\circ-\alpha_i).$$

Because $180^\circ-\alpha_i\le72^\circ$ and $\cos72^\circ<\frac12$,

$$A_{i-1}A_{i+1}^2 > 2L^2-L^2 = L^2.$$

Thus

$$A_{i-1}A_{i+1}>L,$$

contradicting the maximality of $L$.

Hence $n\ne5$.

Finally consider $n=4$.

The angle sum is $360^\circ$. Since every angle is at most $120^\circ$, at least one angle is at least $90^\circ$. Let that angle be $\alpha_i$.

Then

$$180^\circ-\alpha_i\le90^\circ.$$

Using the law of cosines,

$$A_{i-1}A_{i+1}^2 = L^2+L^2-2L^2\cos(180^\circ-\alpha_i) \ge2L^2.$$

Hence

$$A_{i-1}A_{i+1}\ge\sqrt2\,L>L,$$

again a contradiction.

Therefore no convex polygon with $n\ge4$ satisfies the condition.

For $n=3$, a convex triangle has no diagonals. The condition that the side length equal the length of the longest diagonal is vacuously satisfied.

Thus the only possible number of sides is $3$.

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the angle bound. For consecutive vertices $A_{i-1},A_i,A_{i+1}$, the diagonal $A_{i-1}A_{i+1}$ cannot exceed $L$. In triangle $A_{i-1}A_iA_{i+1}$ the two sides adjacent to $A_i$ are both $L$. If the angle $180^\circ-\alpha_i$ exceeded $60^\circ$, then the side opposite it would exceed $L$, because in an isosceles triangle with fixed equal sides, the opposite side increases strictly with the included angle. That would contradict the definition of $L$. Hence $180^\circ-\alpha_i\le60^\circ$, equivalently $\alpha_i\le120^\circ$.

The second delicate step is excluding pentagons. From the angle sum $540^\circ$, some angle satisfies $\alpha_i\ge108^\circ$. The corresponding angle in triangle $A_{i-1}A_iA_{i+1}$ is at most $72^\circ$. Since $72^\circ>60^\circ$, the side opposite it is strictly larger than $L$. This side is a diagonal, producing a contradiction. A careless argument that only used $\alpha_i\le120^\circ$ would not be strong enough to rule out $n=5$.

The third delicate step is excluding hexagons. Equality in the inequality

$$\sum\alpha_i\le6\cdot120^\circ$$

forces every angle to equal $120^\circ$. Then each diagonal joining vertices at distance two equals $L$, so every consecutive triple of vertices forms an equilateral triangle. That structure implies a regular hexagon, whose longest diagonal has length $2L$. Ignoring the equality condition would leave open the possibility of a nonregular hexagon.

## Alternative Approaches

After obtaining the bound $\alpha_i\le120^\circ$, one may proceed more directly. For $n=5$, $6$, any angle sum argument shows that some interior angle is at least $108^\circ$ or all are exactly $120^\circ$. In either situation the diagonal joining the neighboring vertices of that angle has length at least $L$, with strict inequality for the pentagon case and equality for every such diagonal in the hexagon case. The hexagon then decomposes into a chain of equilateral triangles, yielding a regular hexagon and the contradiction $2L>L$.

Another approach uses vectors. Since every side has length $L$, write the edge vectors as $L\mathbf u_i$ with $|\mathbf u_i|=1$. The diagonal joining every second vertex equals $L(\mathbf u_i+\mathbf u_{i+1})$. The condition that every such diagonal have length at most $L$ implies $|\mathbf u_i+\mathbf u_{i+1}|\le1$, hence the angle between consecutive edge vectors is at least $120^\circ$. Translating this into interior-angle bounds leads again to $n\le6$, after which the remaining cases are eliminated as above.
