## Problem

Let $x_1$, $x_2$, $\ldots$, $x_n$ be real numbers such that $x_1^2+x_2^2+\ldots+x_n^2=1$. Prove that the sum of the absolute values of the $2^n$ numbers $$\pm x_1 \pm x_2 \pm \ldots \pm x_n$$ (with all possible combinations of the signs “$+$” and “$-$”) does not exceed $2^n$.

Ya. Kasakovskikh

## Exploration

For each choice of signs $\varepsilon_1,\dots,\varepsilon_n\in{-1,1}$, write

$$S(\varepsilon)=\varepsilon_1x_1+\cdots+\varepsilon_nx_n.$$

The quantity to be estimated is

$$\sum_{\varepsilon}|S(\varepsilon)|.$$

The condition $x_1^2+\cdots+x_n^2=1$ suggests using the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality. Since the sum involves absolute values, a natural idea is to compare the $\ell_1$ norm of the family ${S(\varepsilon)}$ with its $\ell_2$ norm.

Testing small cases:

For $n=1$, the two numbers are $\pm x_1$, and their absolute values sum to $2|x_1|=2$.

For $n=2$ with $x_1=1$, $x_2=0$, the four numbers are $\pm1,\pm1$, whose absolute values sum to $4=2^2$.

For $n=2$ with $x_1=x_2=\frac1{\sqrt2}$, the four numbers are $\sqrt2,0,0,-\sqrt2$, and the sum of absolute values is $2\sqrt2<4$.

Thus equality is possible, and it occurs when all coordinates except one vanish.

To use Cauchy-Schwarz, one needs the sum of squares

$$\sum_{\varepsilon}S(\varepsilon)^2.$$

Expanding,

$$S(\varepsilon)^2 =\sum_i x_i^2+2\sum_{i<j}\varepsilon_i\varepsilon_jx_ix_j.$$

When summed over all sign choices, every mixed term should disappear because $\varepsilon_i\varepsilon_j$ takes the values $\pm1$ equally often. Then

$$\sum_{\varepsilon}S(\varepsilon)^2 =2^n\sum_i x_i^2 =2^n.$$

Applying Cauchy-Schwarz to the $2^n$ numbers $|S(\varepsilon)|$ gives

$$\left(\sum_{\varepsilon}|S(\varepsilon)|\right)^2 \le 2^n\sum_{\varepsilon}S(\varepsilon)^2 = 2^{2n},$$

which yields the desired bound.

The step most likely to hide an error is the cancellation of the mixed terms in the sum of squares. That cancellation must be justified carefully.

## Problem Understanding

We are given real numbers $x_1,\dots,x_n$ satisfying

$$x_1^2+\cdots+x_n^2=1.$$

For all $2^n$ choices of signs $\varepsilon_i\in{-1,1}$, we form the numbers

$$\varepsilon_1x_1+\cdots+\varepsilon_nx_n.$$

The task is to prove that the sum of their absolute values does not exceed $2^n$.

This is a Type B problem, a pure proof.

The core difficulty is to evaluate or estimate simultaneously all $2^n$ sign combinations. The natural route is to compute the second moment of these quantities and then convert it into an estimate for the first moment by Cauchy-Schwarz.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma states that

$$\sum_{\varepsilon} \left(\varepsilon_1x_1+\cdots+\varepsilon_nx_n\right)^2 = 2^n.$$

Its proof comes from expanding the square and showing that all mixed terms cancel when summed over all sign choices.

The second lemma states that for any collection of $N$ real numbers $a_1,\dots,a_N$,

$$\left(\sum_{k=1}^N|a_k|\right)^2 \le N\sum_{k=1}^N a_k^2.$$

This is the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality.

The main argument applies the second lemma to the $2^n$ numbers $S(\varepsilon)$ and then uses the first lemma.

The most delicate point is the proof that the mixed terms indeed sum to zero over all sign patterns.

## Solution

For each sign vector

$$\varepsilon=(\varepsilon_1,\dots,\varepsilon_n), \qquad \varepsilon_i\in\{-1,1\},$$

define

$$S(\varepsilon)=\varepsilon_1x_1+\cdots+\varepsilon_nx_n.$$

We must prove that

$$\sum_{\varepsilon}|S(\varepsilon)|\le 2^n.$$

Consider the sum of squares of all these numbers:

$$\Sigma=\sum_{\varepsilon}S(\varepsilon)^2.$$

Expanding the square gives

$$S(\varepsilon)^2 = \sum_{i=1}^n x_i^2 + 2\sum_{1\le i<j\le n}\varepsilon_i\varepsilon_jx_ix_j.$$

Hence

$$\Sigma = \sum_{\varepsilon}\sum_{i=1}^n x_i^2 + 2\sum_{1\le i<j\le n}x_ix_j \sum_{\varepsilon}\varepsilon_i\varepsilon_j.$$

For fixed $i\ne j$, among the $2^n$ sign vectors, exactly half satisfy

$\varepsilon_i\varepsilon_j=1$ and exactly half satisfy

$\varepsilon_i\varepsilon_j=-1$. Therefore

$$\sum_{\varepsilon}\varepsilon_i\varepsilon_j=0.$$

All mixed terms vanish, and we obtain

$$\Sigma = \sum_{\varepsilon}\sum_{i=1}^n x_i^2 = 2^n\sum_{i=1}^n x_i^2.$$

Using the hypothesis $x_1^2+\cdots+x_n^2=1$,

$$\Sigma=2^n.$$

Now apply the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality to the $2^n$ nonnegative numbers

$|S(\varepsilon)|$:

$$\left(\sum_{\varepsilon}|S(\varepsilon)|\right)^2 \le \left(\sum_{\varepsilon}1^2\right) \left(\sum_{\varepsilon}|S(\varepsilon)|^2\right).$$

Since there are $2^n$ sign vectors and $|S(\varepsilon)|^2=S(\varepsilon)^2$,

$$\left(\sum_{\varepsilon}|S(\varepsilon)|\right)^2 \le 2^n\Sigma = 2^n\cdot 2^n = 2^{2n}.$$

Taking square roots yields

$$\sum_{\varepsilon}|S(\varepsilon)| \le 2^n.$$

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The cancellation of mixed terms requires counting sign patterns correctly. Fix $i\ne j$. For every sign vector $\varepsilon$, change only the sign of $\varepsilon_i$. This operation pairs sign vectors bijectively. Within each pair, the values of $\varepsilon_i\varepsilon_j$ are opposite, so their contributions cancel. Hence the total sum of $\varepsilon_i\varepsilon_j$ over all sign vectors is zero.

The computation of $\Sigma$ must not overlook the factor $2^n$. After the mixed terms disappear, the remaining quantity is

$$\sum_{\varepsilon}\sum_{i=1}^n x_i^2.$$

The inner sum is independent of $\varepsilon$, so it is repeated once for each of the $2^n$ sign vectors, giving

$$2^n\sum_{i=1}^n x_i^2.$$

In the Cauchy-Schwarz step, the number of terms is exactly $2^n$. Using $N=2^n$ in

$$\left(\sum |a_k|\right)^2\le N\sum a_k^2$$

produces

$$\left(\sum_{\varepsilon}|S(\varepsilon)|\right)^2 \le 2^n\Sigma,$$

and substituting $\Sigma=2^n$ yields the sharp bound $2^{2n}$.

## Alternative Approaches

A probabilistic formulation gives the same argument in a different language. Let $\varepsilon_1,\dots,\varepsilon_n$ be independent random variables taking the values $\pm1$ with probability $1/2$. Then

$$X=\varepsilon_1x_1+\cdots+\varepsilon_nx_n$$

assumes exactly the $2^n$ values appearing in the problem, each with probability $2^{-n}$. The required inequality becomes

$$\mathbb E|X|\le 1.$$

Applying Cauchy-Schwarz in the form

$$(\mathbb E|X|)^2\le \mathbb E(X^2),$$

it remains to compute

$$\mathbb E(X^2) = \sum_{i=1}^n x_i^2 + 2\sum_{i<j}x_ix_j\,\mathbb E(\varepsilon_i\varepsilon_j).$$

Independence and symmetry give $\mathbb E(\varepsilon_i\varepsilon_j)=0$, hence

$$\mathbb E(X^2)=x_1^2+\cdots+x_n^2=1.$$

Thus $\mathbb E|X|\le1$, and multiplying by $2^n$ yields the desired inequality. The direct combinatorial proof is preferable here because it stays entirely within finite sums and avoids introducing probabilistic terminology.
