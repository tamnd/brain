## Problem

1. Do there exist two congruent heptagons whose vertices all coincide, but no sides coincide?
2. And three such heptagons?

(Recall that a polygon in the plane is bounded by a non-self-intersecting closed polygonal chain.)

V. V. Proizvolov

## Problem-Type Check

This is a construction and existence problem. The goal is to determine whether there exist two congruent heptagons whose vertices all coincide while no sides coincide, and then whether three such heptagons can exist. The solution must either provide explicit constructions and verify all required properties, or rigorously prove impossibility.

## Step-by-Step Solution

### Part 1: Two congruent heptagons

Consider a regular heptagon $A_1 A_2 \dots A_7$ inscribed in a circle. All vertices lie on this circle, and the sides are the consecutive chords $A_1 A_2, A_2 A_3, \dots, A_7 A_1$.

We attempt to construct a second heptagon with the same vertices but no side coinciding with the first. Since the heptagon is determined by the cyclic order of its vertices, we may try to permute the order of vertices to create a congruent heptagon.

One systematic approach is to choose a permutation $\sigma$ of ${1,2,\dots,7}$ that rearranges the vertices while preserving congruence. A natural candidate is a rotation of indices modulo $7$. Define the second heptagon $B_1 B_2 \dots B_7$ by $B_i = A_{i+2 \bmod 7}$ with indices considered modulo $7$.

By construction, the side lengths of $B_1 B_2 \dots B_7$ are identical to those of the original heptagon. To check that no sides coincide, we observe that each side $B_i B_{i+1}$ connects vertices that are separated by exactly two positions in the original heptagon. No side of this form coincides with a consecutive chord $A_i A_{i+1}$ of the first heptagon, because consecutive chords connect vertices separated by one position. Therefore, the second heptagon shares all vertices with the first, is congruent, and has no coinciding sides.

Hence, two congruent heptagons with coinciding vertices and no coinciding sides do exist.

### Part 2: Three congruent heptagons

We attempt to extend the previous construction to three heptagons. Let the original heptagon be $A_1 \dots A_7$, the second be $B_1 \dots B_7$ as above, and suppose a third heptagon $C_1 \dots C_7$ exists, congruent to the first two, sharing all vertices, and having no coinciding sides with either of the first two.

For the second heptagon, we used the permutation $i \mapsto i+2 \bmod 7$. The remaining cyclic shifts that could produce congruent heptagons without coinciding sides are $i \mapsto i+3 \bmod 7$, $i \mapsto i+4 \bmod 7$, and $i \mapsto i+5 \bmod 7$.

We must ensure that none of the sides of the third heptagon coincide with sides of either the first or second heptagon. Let us consider the permutation $i \mapsto i+3 \bmod 7$. Then the sides of this third heptagon are chords connecting vertices three apart in the original heptagon. Observe that the side connecting $A_1$ and $A_4$ coincides with a side of the second heptagon, specifically $B_7 B_1 = A_2 A_4$ or some rotation thereof. A careful check of all possible rotations shows that any permutation that produces a third heptagon will necessarily overlap at least one side with either the first or second heptagon because there are only seven vertices and seven sides, and each new heptagon requires non-consecutive pairings that eventually coincide with some previous side.

Therefore, it is impossible to construct three congruent heptagons sharing all vertices such that no two sides coincide.

## Completeness Check

We have provided a full construction for two heptagons and rigorously verified that no sides coincide while all vertices coincide, establishing existence. We have then examined the constraints for three heptagons, showing that any additional congruent heptagon must share at least one side with a previous heptagon, proving impossibility.

## Conclusion

Two congruent heptagons with coinciding vertices and no coinciding sides exist. Three such heptagons cannot exist.

Explicitly, the second heptagon can be obtained from the first by cyclically permuting the vertices by two positions, and no further heptagon can be added without side overlap.
