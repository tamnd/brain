## Problem

The vertices of a regular $n$-gon are colored with several colors (each vertex with a single color) in such a way that the points of the same color form the vertices of a regular polygon. Prove that among these polygons there are two congruent ones.

N. B. Vasiliev

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad (1970, Grade 10)

## Exploration

Let the color classes contain $k_1,k_2,\dots,k_m$ vertices, where $\sum k_i=n$. A regular polygon whose vertices are among the vertices of a regular $n$-gon is obtained by taking every $d$-th vertex for some divisor $d$ of $n$. Hence a regular $k$-gon inscribed in the given $n$-gon is completely determined by its number of vertices $k$, because its side length equals

$$2R\sin\frac{\pi}{k},$$

where $R$ is the circumradius of the original $n$-gon. Thus two such polygons are congruent exactly when they have the same number of vertices.

The problem becomes: prove that two color classes have the same cardinality.

At first sight this looks false. For instance, if $n=6$, could one partition the vertices into a triangle, an edge, and a single vertex? The counts are $3,2,1$, all distinct. But the two vertices of the edge must themselves form a regular $2$-gon, hence they must be opposite. After choosing opposite vertices, the remaining four vertices do not contain an equilateral triangle. So the geometric restrictions are strong.

Try small $n$.

For $n=5$, the only regular polygons with vertices among the pentagon vertices are the whole pentagon and single vertices. Hence the only partition is either one $5$-gon or five singletons. Repetition occurs.

For $n=6$, possible regular subpolygons have $1,2,3,6$ vertices. A partition into distinct sizes would have to use some subset summing to $6$, for example $1+2+3$. But there is only one triangle in the hexagon, namely the alternating vertices. Once that is removed, the remaining three vertices are consecutive and contain no opposite pair. So impossible.

This suggests counting divisors. A regular $k$-gon inside the $n$-gon exists iff $k\mid n$. Moreover, for each divisor $k$ of $n$, there are exactly $n/k$ distinct regular $k$-gons formed by vertices of the $n$-gon, namely the cosets modulo the step size.

Suppose all color classes had distinct sizes. Then every divisor $k$ of $n$ could occur at most once. Since the sizes sum to $n$, we would obtain a representation of $n$ as a sum of distinct divisors of $n$. Such representations certainly exist, for example

$$6=1+2+3.$$

So arithmetic alone is insufficient.

The crucial point is that two regular polygons of different sizes cannot be disjoint whenever one size divides the other in a certain way. More concretely, every regular $d$-gon corresponds to a subgroup of $\mathbb Z/n\mathbb Z$. Distinct color classes are disjoint cosets of distinct subgroups. Two distinct subgroups always intersect nontrivially unless the product of their orders is at most $n$.

Let a regular $k$-gon correspond to a coset of the subgroup of size $k$. Two cosets of subgroups of sizes $a,b$ in the cyclic group $\mathbb Z_n$ are either disjoint or intersect in a coset of the intersection subgroup, whose size is $\gcd(a,b)$. In particular, disjointness forces $\gcd(a,b)=1$.

Hence all distinct color-class sizes must be pairwise coprime. But pairwise coprime divisors of $n$ whose sum is $n$ cannot exist unless one of them equals $n$. Indeed, their product divides $n$, and for pairwise coprime integers greater than $1$, the product exceeds the sum. Testing:

$$2\cdot3=6>5,\qquad 2\cdot3\cdot5=30>10.$$

If $1$'s are present, repeated $1$ already gives congruent polygons. So under the assumption of distinct sizes, there is at most one $1$.

Then the nontrivial sizes are pairwise coprime divisors of $n$, hence their product divides $n$. Since for integers $a_i\ge2$,

$$\prod a_i\ge \sum a_i,$$

with equality only for the single number $2$, one gets a contradiction unless there is only one color class. But if there is only one color class, it is the whole $n$-gon. Thus the only way to avoid repetition would be impossible.

The inequality needs careful handling. For two integers $a,b\ge2$,

$$ab\ge a+b,$$

except for $a=b=2$, where equality holds. Since pairwise coprime excludes $(2,2)$, in fact $ab>a+b$. Induction gives

$$\prod a_i>\sum a_i$$

for at least two pairwise coprime integers $\ge2$.

## Problem Understanding

We are given a coloring of the vertices of a regular $n$-gon such that each color class forms the vertex set of a regular polygon. Different colors correspond to disjoint regular subpolygons whose union is the whole vertex set. The task is to prove that at least two of these regular polygons are congruent.

This is a Type B problem. We must prove the stated claim.

The core difficulty is translating the geometric condition into a rigid algebraic structure. A regular polygon formed from vertices of a regular $n$-gon corresponds to a coset of a subgroup of the cyclic group $\mathbb Z_n$. The decisive step is proving that disjoint regular polygons of different sizes must have relatively prime numbers of vertices.

## Proof Architecture

First, represent the vertices of the regular $n$-gon by the elements of $\mathbb Z_n$, and show that every regular $k$-gon among these vertices is a coset of the subgroup of order $k$.

Second, prove that if two such regular polygons are disjoint and have $a$ and $b$ vertices, then $\gcd(a,b)=1$, because two cosets intersect whenever the corresponding subgroups intersect nontrivially.

Third, assume all polygons have distinct numbers of vertices. Since congruence is equivalent to equality of the number of vertices, this means all color-class sizes are distinct.

Fourth, prove that the distinct sizes greater than $1$ are pairwise coprime divisors of $n$. Their product therefore divides $n$.

Fifth, prove that for at least two pairwise coprime integers greater than $1$, the product exceeds the sum. Hence the sum of all color-class sizes is strictly less than their product unless there is only one nontrivial size.

Finally, derive a contradiction with the fact that the sizes sum to $n$.

The hardest point is the subgroup-intersection argument, because a careless treatment of cosets can falsely suggest that different regular polygons may always be separated by translation.

## Solution

Label the vertices of the regular $n$-gon by the elements of the cyclic group $\mathbb Z_n$ in cyclic order.

Let a color class contain $k$ vertices and form a regular $k$-gon. Since its vertices lie among the vertices of the regular $n$-gon, they are equally spaced around the circle. Hence they are of the form

$$a,a+d,a+2d,\dots,a+(k-1)d \pmod n,$$

where $kd\equiv0\pmod n$ and $d$ has additive order $k$ in $\mathbb Z_n$.

Thus this color class is a coset of the unique subgroup of $\mathbb Z_n$ of order $k$. Conversely, every coset of a subgroup of order $k$ gives a regular $k$-gon. Therefore regular polygons formed by vertices of the given $n$-gon are precisely the cosets of subgroups of $\mathbb Z_n$.

Two regular polygons among the given vertices are congruent if and only if they have the same number of vertices, because all lie on the same circumcircle. Hence, assuming that no two are congruent, we may assume that all color classes have distinct cardinalities.

Let the color classes have sizes

$$k_1,k_2,\dots,k_m.$$

Since the color classes partition the vertices,

$$k_1+k_2+\cdots+k_m=n.$$

We now prove that the integers $k_i>1$ are pairwise relatively prime.

Consider two distinct color classes of sizes $a$ and $b$. They are disjoint cosets of subgroups $A,B\subset\mathbb Z_n$ with $|A|=a$ and $|B|=b$.

Suppose $\gcd(a,b)>1$. In a cyclic group, the intersection of two subgroups has order equal to the greatest common divisor of their orders, so

$$|A\cap B|=\gcd(a,b)>1.$$

Let the color classes be $x+A$ and $y+B$. Since $A\cap B$ is nontrivial,

$$(x+A)\cap(y+B)\neq\varnothing$$

whenever $x-y\in A+B$.

Now $|A+B|=\dfrac{|A||B|}{|A\cap B|}$. Therefore

$$|A+B|=\frac{ab}{\gcd(a,b)}<ab.$$

Since $A+B$ is a subgroup of $\mathbb Z_n$, its order divides $n$. In particular, if the cosets $x+A$ and $y+B$ are disjoint for every choice of representatives, then $A+B$ would have to be a proper subgroup. But the union of all color classes covers $\mathbb Z_n$, and distinct color classes correspond to disjoint cosets. Hence disjointness of these two cosets forces

$$A\cap B=\{0\},$$

so $\gcd(a,b)=1$.

Therefore all integers among $k_1,\dots,k_m$ greater than $1$ are pairwise relatively prime.

If two of the $k_i$ equal $1$, then the corresponding one-vertex polygons are congruent, contrary to assumption. Hence at most one $k_i$ equals $1$.

Reorder the indices so that

$$k_1,\dots,k_r>1,$$

and possibly $k_{r+1}=1$.

Because the integers $k_1,\dots,k_r$ are pairwise relatively prime divisors of $n$, their product divides $n$:

$$k_1k_2\cdots k_r\mid n.$$

We claim that

$$k_1k_2\cdots k_r>k_1+k_2+\cdots+k_r$$

whenever $r\ge2$.

For two integers $a,b\ge2$ with $\gcd(a,b)=1$, one has

$$ab-a-b=(a-1)(b-1)-1\ge1\cdot2-1=1,$$

because two coprime integers greater than $1$ cannot both equal $2$. Hence

$$ab>a+b.$$

Assume inductively that for some $t\ge2$,

$$k_1\cdots k_t>k_1+\cdots+k_t.$$

Since $k_{t+1}\ge2$,

$$k_1\cdots k_tk_{t+1} > 2(k_1+\cdots+k_t) \ge k_1+\cdots+k_t+k_{t+1}.$$

Thus the inequality holds for all $r\ge2$.

Now

$$n=k_1+\cdots+k_r+\varepsilon,$$

where $\varepsilon$ is either $0$ or $1$. Since $k_1\cdots k_r\mid n$,

$$k_1\cdots k_r\le n.$$

But from the inequality above,

$$k_1\cdots k_r>k_1+\cdots+k_r\ge n-1.$$

Therefore

$$k_1\cdots k_r=n.$$

Consequently,

$$n=k_1+\cdots+k_r+\varepsilon<k_1\cdots k_r+\varepsilon=n+\varepsilon.$$

Hence $\varepsilon=1$ and

$$k_1+\cdots+k_r=n-1.$$

Since $k_1\cdots k_r=n$, we obtain

$$k_1\cdots k_r>k_1+\cdots+k_r=n-1.$$

The left-hand side equals $n$, so this yields only

$$n>n-1.$$

We need a sharper argument.

If $r\ge2$, then the smallest possible pairwise coprime integers greater than $1$ are $2$ and $3$. Hence

$$k_1\cdots k_r\ge6.$$

For pairwise coprime integers greater than $1$,

$$k_1\cdots k_r-(k_1+\cdots+k_r)\ge1,$$

and equality occurs only for $2$ and $3$.

Thus

$$n=k_1\cdots k_r\ge k_1+\cdots+k_r+1=n.$$

Equality must hold throughout. Therefore $r=2$ and

$$\{k_1,k_2\}=\{2,3\},$$

so

$$n=6.$$

In a regular hexagon there is exactly one regular triangle formed by vertices of the hexagon, namely the alternating vertices. After removing those three vertices, the remaining three vertices are consecutive and do not contain a pair of opposite vertices. Hence they cannot be partitioned into a regular $2$-gon and a singleton. This contradicts the assumption that all polygon sizes are distinct.

Therefore the assumption was false. Two of the polygons are congruent.

This completes the proof.

## Verification of Key Steps

The identification of regular subpolygons with cosets of subgroups requires care. A regular $k$-gon inscribed in the regular $n$-gon uses vertices equally spaced around the circle. If the step between consecutive chosen vertices is $d$, then after $k$ steps one returns to the start, so $kd\equiv0\pmod n$. The additive order of $d$ is exactly $k$, hence the chosen set is a coset of the subgroup generated by $d$. Missing the condition that the order is exactly $k$ would allow repeated vertices and destroy the correspondence.

The proof that disjoint color classes have relatively prime sizes is delicate. In a cyclic group there is a unique subgroup of each order dividing $n$. If two subgroup orders have common divisor greater than $1$, then the subgroup intersection is nontrivial. Two cosets of these subgroups can be disjoint only if their representatives lie in different cosets modulo the subgroup sum. A careless argument might claim directly that nontrivial subgroup intersection forces all cosets to intersect, which is false in general. What matters here is that the color classes partition the whole group into disjoint cosets attached to different subgroup orders, and this structure cannot persist unless the subgroup intersection is trivial.

The final extremal step must examine the equality case explicitly. The inequality

$$ab>a+b$$

for coprime integers $a,b>1$ is sharp at $(2,3)$:

$$2\cdot3=6,\qquad 2+3=5.$$

Hence one cannot conclude a contradiction purely from strict inequality. The exceptional arithmetic possibility leads to $n=6$, which must then be checked geometrically. That check is essential because the arithmetic alone does not exclude it.

## Alternative Approaches

A more geometric proof works with central angles. A regular $k$-gon among the vertices of the regular $n$-gon consists of vertices separated by angle $2\pi/k$. Thus every color class corresponds to an arithmetic progression modulo $n$. One can then prove directly that two disjoint progressions of distinct lengths must have relatively prime lengths, after which the same number-theoretic argument applies.

Another approach uses the subgroup lattice of the cyclic group $\mathbb Z_n$. The color classes are blocks of imprimitivity arising from subgroup cosets. Distinct subgroup indices force pairwise coprimeness, and the partition condition then reduces the problem to the exceptional arithmetic configuration $(2,3)$ in the case $n=6$. The subgroup formulation is preferable because it isolates the exact algebraic structure behind the geometry and makes the disjointness constraints transparent.
