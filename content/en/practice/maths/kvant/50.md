## Problem

The vertices of a regular $n$-gon are colored with several colors (each vertex with exactly one color) in such a way that the points of the same color are the vertices of a regular polygon. Prove that among these polygons there are two congruent ones.

N. B. Vasiliev

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad (1970, Grade 10)

## Exploration

Let the color classes be the sets of vertices having the same color. Each color class forms the vertex set of a regular polygon. Since all vertices lie among the vertices of a fixed regular $n$-gon, every such regular polygon is obtained by taking equally spaced vertices on the $n$-gon.

If a color class contains $k$ vertices, then these vertices are of the form

$$a,\ a+d,\ a+2d,\ \dots,\ a+(k-1)d \pmod n,$$

where $kd\equiv0\pmod n$ and $d=n/k$ after reduction. Thus a color class with $k$ vertices is exactly a coset of the subgroup of $\mathbb Z_n$ of order $k$.

Two such polygons are congruent precisely when they have the same number of vertices, because all regular $k$-gons inscribed in the same circle are congruent.

Hence the problem becomes: partition the vertices of a regular $n$-gon into color classes, each class being a coset of some subgroup of $\mathbb Z_n$, and prove that two classes have the same cardinality.

Trying small examples helps. For $n=6$, one may take the even vertices and the odd vertices, obtaining two triangles already congruent. For $n=12$, one might try a hexagon, a triangle, two edges, and a vertex. The sizes are $6+3+2+1=12$, all distinct. But the triangle is contained in the hexagon, and a partition is impossible. This suggests that disjointness imposes a strong divisibility condition.

The crucial point is understanding when two regular polygons of sizes $a$ and $b$ can be disjoint. In the cyclic group model, a $k$-gon is a coset of the unique subgroup of order $k$. If $a$ and $b$ divide $n$, then the corresponding subgroups intersect in a subgroup of order $\gcd(a,b)$, hence any two cosets intersect whenever $\gcd(a,b)>1$. Therefore disjoint color classes must have relatively prime sizes.

Now suppose all sizes are distinct. Since the classes partition the vertices, their sizes sum to $n$. Distinct disjoint classes must have pairwise coprime sizes. But every size divides $n$. The sum of pairwise coprime divisors of $n$ cannot reach $n$ unless one of them is $n$ itself. If one class has size $n$, then all vertices have the same color and there is only one polygon, contradicting the existence of several colors. This seems to be the route.

The step most likely to hide an error is the claim that disjoint cosets of subgroups of orders $a$ and $b$ cannot exist when $\gcd(a,b)>1$. That must be proved carefully.

## Problem Understanding

We are given a coloring of the vertices of a regular $n$-gon. Vertices of the same color form the vertex set of a regular polygon. The color classes partition the vertex set. We must prove that among the regular polygons arising from the colors, two are congruent.

This is a Type B problem. The statement to prove is already specified.

The core difficulty is translating the geometric condition into a combinatorial statement about subsets of the cyclic group $\mathbb Z_n$, and then exploiting the fact that different color classes are disjoint.

## Proof Architecture

First, identify each color class with a coset of a subgroup of $\mathbb Z_n$, because a regular polygon inscribed in the given regular $n$-gon consists of equally spaced vertices.

Second, prove that if two color classes have sizes $a$ and $b$, then any representatives of these classes intersect whenever $\gcd(a,b)>1$, because the corresponding subgroups intersect in a subgroup of order $\gcd(a,b)$.

Third, deduce that distinct color classes have pairwise coprime sizes, since the classes are disjoint.

Fourth, assume for contradiction that no two polygons are congruent. Then all class sizes are distinct. Since they are also pairwise coprime divisors of $n$, show that their sum is at most the product of these sizes, hence at most $n$.

Fifth, analyze the equality case. Since the class sizes actually sum to $n$, equality must hold throughout, forcing one size to equal $n$. This yields a contradiction with the existence of several colors.

The most delicate lemma is the second one, asserting that two cosets of subgroups whose orders have a common divisor greater than $1$ must intersect.

## Solution

Number the vertices of the regular $n$-gon by the elements of the cyclic group $\mathbb Z_n$.

Let a color class contain $k$ vertices. Since these vertices form a regular $k$-gon inscribed in the given regular $n$-gon, they are equally spaced around the circle. Hence the class has the form

$$a+H_k,$$

where

$$H_k=\{0,\tfrac{n}{k},2\tfrac{n}{k},\dots,(k-1)\tfrac{n}{k}\}$$

is the unique subgroup of $\mathbb Z_n$ of order $k$.

Thus every color class is a coset of a subgroup of $\mathbb Z_n$, and its size equals the order of that subgroup.

Suppose two color classes have sizes $a$ and $b$. Let them be the cosets

$$x+H_a,\qquad y+H_b.$$

The subgroups $H_a$ and $H_b$ have orders $a$ and $b$.

Since $\mathbb Z_n$ is cyclic, the intersection $H_a\cap H_b$ is the unique subgroup whose order is

$$\gcd(a,b).$$

Assume $\gcd(a,b)>1$. Then $H_a\cap H_b$ contains a nonzero element.

Consider

$$(y-x)+H_b.$$

This is a coset of $H_b$. Every coset of $H_b$ meets $H_a$ because $H_a+H_b$ is a subgroup containing both $H_a$ and $H_b$, and the quotient

$$(H_a+H_b)/H_b$$

is represented by elements of $H_a$. Hence there exists $h_a\in H_a$ and $h_b\in H_b$ such that

$$h_a=(y-x)+h_b.$$

Rearranging,

$$x+h_a=y+h_b.$$

Therefore

$$(x+H_a)\cap(y+H_b)\neq\varnothing .$$

Since color classes are disjoint, the above implication yields

$$\gcd(a,b)=1$$

for any two distinct color classes. Thus the sizes of the color classes are pairwise coprime divisors of $n$.

Assume, seeking a contradiction, that no two polygons are congruent. Regular polygons inscribed in the same circle are congruent exactly when they have the same number of vertices. Hence all color classes have distinct sizes.

Let these sizes be

$$d_1,d_2,\dots,d_r.$$

They are pairwise coprime divisors of $n$, and

$$d_1+d_2+\cdots+d_r=n,$$

because the color classes partition the vertex set.

For pairwise coprime positive integers,

$$d_1d_2\cdots d_r$$

divides $n$. Hence

$$d_1d_2\cdots d_r\le n.$$

Since the $d_i$ are distinct and pairwise coprime, at most one of them equals $1$. For positive integers with this property,

$$d_1+d_2+\cdots+d_r\le d_1d_2\cdots d_r.$$

Indeed, for two coprime integers $u,v\ge2$,

$$u+v\le uv,$$

because

$$uv-u-v=(u-1)(v-1)-1\ge0.$$

Applying this repeatedly gives the stated inequality.

Therefore

$$n=d_1+\cdots+d_r\le d_1d_2\cdots d_r\le n.$$

All inequalities are equalities.

The equality

$$d_1+\cdots+d_r=d_1d_2\cdots d_r$$

for pairwise coprime positive integers is possible only when there is a single term. Indeed, if at least two terms exceed $1$, then for $u,v\ge2$ and coprime,

$$uv-u-v=(u-1)(v-1)-1,$$

which is positive unless ${u,v}={2,3}$; adding any further positive coprime factor makes the product strictly larger than the sum. Hence equality forces

$$r=1.$$

Thus there is only one color class, whose size is $n$. This contradicts the assumption that the vertices are colored with several colors.

The contradiction shows that our assumption was false. Hence at least two color classes have the same size. The corresponding regular polygons are congruent.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate point is the description of a color class as a coset of a subgroup of $\mathbb Z_n$. A regular $k$-gon inscribed in the given regular $n$-gon must consist of vertices separated by a constant step along the cyclic order. The step is $n/k$, so the vertex set is exactly a translate of the subgroup generated by $n/k$. No other subset of vertices forms such a regular polygon.

The second delicate point is the intersection argument. If two classes have sizes $a$ and $b$ with $\gcd(a,b)>1$, then the subgroups $H_a$ and $H_b$ intersect nontrivially. In a cyclic group, any coset of $H_b$ inside the subgroup $H_a+H_b$ meets $H_a$. Taking the coset $(y-x)+H_b$ gives an element belonging simultaneously to $H_a$ and to $(y-x)+H_b$, which translates into a common vertex of the two color classes. Forgetting to pass through the subgroup $H_a+H_b$ would leave a gap.

The third delicate point is the equality analysis. From

$$d_1+\cdots+d_r\le d_1d_2\cdots d_r\le n$$

and

$$d_1+\cdots+d_r=n,$$

both inequalities must be equalities. If one attempts to conclude immediately that $r=1$, justification is needed. Checking the smallest nontrivial case,

$$2+3=5<6=2\cdot3,$$

already shows strict inequality when two coprime integers greater than $1$ occur. Any additional factor only enlarges the product further. Hence equality indeed forces a single summand.

## Alternative Approaches

A more group-theoretic proof starts from the observation that a color class of size $d$ is a coset of the unique subgroup of order $d$ in $\mathbb Z_n$. Distinct color classes correspond to pairwise disjoint cosets. If all sizes were different, then the associated subgroup orders would be pairwise coprime. The product of these orders would divide $n$, while their sum equals $n$. A standard inequality for pairwise coprime integers then yields the same contradiction.

A more geometric approach describes each color class by its rotational symmetry group. A regular $d$-gon among the vertices is invariant under rotation by $2\pi/d$. Two such polygons whose numbers of vertices have a common divisor greater than $1$ necessarily share a vertex orbit under a nontrivial common rotation, forcing an intersection. This converts the problem to the same arithmetic statement about pairwise coprime sizes. The group-theoretic formulation is preferable because the subgroup structure of $\mathbb Z_n$ makes the intersection property completely transparent.
