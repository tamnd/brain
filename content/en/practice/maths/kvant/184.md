## Problem

Prove the identity $$\dfrac{C^0_n}{x} - \dfrac{C^1_n}{x + 1} + ... + (-1)^n\dfrac{C^n_n}{x + n} = \dfrac{n!}{x(x+1)(x+2)...(x+n)}.$$

G. E. Esipenko

## Exploration

The identity has the structure of a partial fraction decomposition of a rational function with equally spaced linear factors in the denominator. The right-hand side suggests the product $x(x+1)\cdots(x+n)$ plays the central role, since its reciprocal appears, multiplied by $n!$.

A natural approach is to clear denominators. Multiplying both sides by $x(x+1)\cdots(x+n)$ transforms the identity into a statement about a polynomial expression of degree at most $n-1$. Such expressions are often determined uniquely by their values at sufficiently many points.

The key obstruction is controlling the alternating binomial sum after clearing denominators and understanding how the product behaves when evaluated at negative integers, where almost all terms vanish.

The most fragile point is verifying the exact value of the surviving product at those special points, including its sign.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem, requiring a proof of a binomial partial fraction identity.

We must show

$$\sum_{k=0}^n (-1)^k \frac{\binom{n}{k}}{x+k} = \frac{n!}{x(x+1)\cdots(x+n)}.$$

The core difficulty is to convert the rational identity into a polynomial identity and then exploit special values of $x$ where the expression collapses dramatically.

## Proof Architecture

Define

$$P(x)=x(x+1)\cdots(x+n).$$

Multiply the claimed identity by $P(x)$ to obtain a polynomial expression

$$F(x)=\sum_{k=0}^n (-1)^k \binom{n}{k}\frac{P(x)}{x+k}.$$

Each term $\frac{P(x)}{x+k}$ is a polynomial of degree $n$, but the alternating combination forces cancellation of the highest-degree terms, leaving a polynomial of degree at most $n-1$.

We will prove that $F(x)$ is constant by showing it takes the same value at $n+1$ distinct points $x=0,-1,\dots,-n$.

At each such point, only one term in the sum survives, allowing an explicit computation.

The key lemma is the evaluation of

$$\prod_{j\ne k}(j-k)$$

and its sign.

The hardest step is the correct handling of this product and its cancellation into $n!$.

## Solution

Let

$$P(x)=\prod_{j=0}^n (x+j)=x(x+1)\cdots(x+n).$$

Multiplying the desired identity by $P(x)$ gives the equivalent statement

$$F(x)=\sum_{k=0}^n (-1)^k \binom{n}{k}\frac{P(x)}{x+k}=n!.$$

Fix $k$. Then

$$\frac{P(x)}{x+k}=\prod_{\substack{j=0 \\ j\ne k}}^n (x+j),$$

which is a polynomial of degree $n$.

Hence $F(x)$ is a polynomial in $x$ of degree at most $n$.

We now evaluate $F(x)$ at the points $x=-m$ for $m=0,1,\dots,n$.

Fix such $m$. In the sum defining $F(-m)$, every term with $k\ne m$ contains the factor $(-m+k)$ inside the product $\prod_{j\ne k}(x+j)$ with $j=m$, hence vanishes. Only the term $k=m$ remains.

Thus

$$F(-m)=(-1)^m \binom{n}{m}\prod_{\substack{j=0 \\ j\ne m}}^n (j-m).$$

We compute the product. Split indices:

For $j<m$, write $j-m=-(m-j)$. For $j>m$, keep $j-m$ positive. Hence

$$\prod_{\substack{j=0 \\ j\ne m}}^n (j-m) = \left(\prod_{j=0}^{m-1} (j-m)\right)\left(\prod_{j=m+1}^{n} (j-m)\right).$$

The first factor equals

$$\prod_{j=0}^{m-1} (-(m-j)) = (-1)^m \prod_{j=0}^{m-1} (m-j)=(-1)^m m!.$$

The second factor equals

$$\prod_{j=m+1}^n (j-m)=\prod_{t=1}^{n-m} t=(n-m)!.$$

Therefore

$$\prod_{\substack{j=0 \\ j\ne m}}^n (j-m)=(-1)^m m!(n-m)!.$$

Substituting into $F(-m)$ yields

$$F(-m)=(-1)^m \binom{n}{m} (-1)^m m!(n-m)!.$$

The signs cancel, giving

$$F(-m)=\binom{n}{m} m!(n-m)!.$$

Using $\binom{n}{m}=\frac{n!}{m!(n-m)!}$, we obtain

$$F(-m)=n!.$$

Thus $F(x)$ takes the same value $n!$ at all points $x=0,-1,-2,\dots,-n$, which are $n+1$ distinct values.

Since $F(x)$ is a polynomial of degree at most $n$, a polynomial agreeing with a constant at $n+1$ distinct points must itself be that constant. Hence

$$F(x)=n!.$$

Dividing by $P(x)$ yields

$$\sum_{k=0}^n (-1)^k \frac{\binom{n}{k}}{x+k} = \frac{n!}{x(x+1)\cdots(x+n)}.$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The critical computation is the product

$$\prod_{j\ne m}(j-m).$$

Splitting into $j<m$ and $j>m$ ensures every factor is accounted for exactly once. The sign contribution arises only from the $m$ negative factors in the first block, producing $(-1)^m$. The remaining magnitudes form factorials $m!$ and $(n-m)!$, giving a complete factorization without hidden cancellations.

Another delicate point is the uniqueness argument: a polynomial of degree at most $n$ is uniquely determined by its values at $n+1$ distinct points, which justifies concluding constancy from the computed evaluations.

## Alternative Approaches

A different method uses partial fraction decomposition of the rational function $\frac{n!}{x(x+1)\cdots(x+n)}$ by induction on $n$, isolating one linear factor at a time and comparing residues at $x=-k$.

Another approach uses finite differences applied to the function $f(x)=\frac{1}{x}$, where the alternating binomial sum appears as an $n$-th forward difference evaluated at $x$, producing the same rational expression after telescoping.
