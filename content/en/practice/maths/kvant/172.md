## Problem

Prove that for any prime $p$ the number $$\underbrace{11 \ldots 1}_{p}\underbrace{22 \ldots 2}_{p}\underbrace{33 \ldots 3}_{p} \ldots \underbrace{99 \ldots 9}_{p} - 123456789$$ is divisible by $p$.

M. L. Gerver

## Exploration

Let the number be denoted by $A_p$, formed by concatenating the blocks

$$\underbrace{11\ldots1}_{p},\ \underbrace{22\ldots2}_{p},\ \ldots,\ \underbrace{99\ldots9}_{p},$$

so that $A_p$ has $9p$ digits. The comparison number is

$$B = 123456789 = \sum_{k=1}^{9} k \cdot 10^{9-k}.$$

The structure suggests rewriting each block as a geometric sum:

$$\underbrace{kk\ldots k}_{p} = k(10^{p-1} + 10^{p-2} + \cdots + 1),$$

so the whole number factors into a common block sum multiplied by powers of $10^p$.

The key idea is to compare $A_p$ with $B$ modulo $p$, using that for primes $p \neq 2,5$, Fermat’s theorem gives $10^p \equiv 10 \pmod p$, and for $p=2,3,5$ the structure degenerates and must be checked separately.

The most delicate point is ensuring that the geometric sum factor behaves correctly modulo $p$, especially when $p=3$ where $9$ is not invertible.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem: prove that for every prime $p$, the integer

$$\underbrace{11\ldots1}_{p}\underbrace{22\ldots2}_{p}\cdots\underbrace{99\ldots9}_{p} - 123456789$$

is divisible by $p$.

The structure of the number strongly suggests grouping digits into blocks of length $p$ and exploiting modular arithmetic with powers of $10$. The expected mechanism is reduction of each block via geometric series and application of Fermat’s theorem for primes not dividing $10$.

## Proof Architecture

The proof proceeds by introducing the block sum

$$S_p = 1 + 10 + 10^2 + \cdots + 10^{p-1},$$

and rewriting $A_p$ in terms of $S_p$.

The first lemma expresses $A_p$ as

$$A_p = S_p \sum_{k=1}^9 k \cdot 10^{(9-k)p}.$$

The second lemma analyzes $S_p \bmod p$ for different primes $p$, distinguishing the cases $p \neq 3$ and $p=3$.

The third lemma uses $10^p \equiv 10 \pmod p$ when $p \neq 2,5$ to reduce $10^{(9-k)p}$ to $10^{9-k}$ modulo $p$.

The final step matches the resulting expression with $123456789$.

The most fragile point is the interaction between $S_p$ and modular inversion of $9$, especially at $p=3$.

## Solution

Let

$$S_p = 1 + 10 + 10^2 + \cdots + 10^{p-1}.$$

Each block of $p$ identical digits $k$ equals

$$k \cdot S_p.$$

Since the blocks are placed from most significant to least significant, we obtain

$$A_p = \sum_{k=1}^9 k \cdot S_p \cdot 10^{(9-k)p},$$

hence

$$A_p = S_p \sum_{k=1}^9 k \cdot 10^{(9-k)p}.$$

We first consider primes $p \neq 2,3,5$.

For such primes, $\gcd(p,10)=1$, hence Fermat’s theorem gives

$$10^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod p,$$

so

$$10^p \equiv 10 \pmod p.$$

Iterating,

$$10^{(9-k)p} \equiv 10^{9-k} \pmod p.$$

We also evaluate $S_p$ modulo $p$. Since $10^p \equiv 10 \pmod p$ and $10 \not\equiv 1 \pmod p$ for $p \neq 3$, we compute the geometric sum:

$$S_p = \frac{10^p - 1}{9}.$$

Reducing modulo $p$,

$$10^p - 1 \equiv 10 - 1 = 9 \pmod p,$$

hence

$$S_p \equiv \frac{9}{9} \equiv 1 \pmod p.$$

Therefore,

$$A_p \equiv \sum_{k=1}^9 k \cdot 10^{9-k} \pmod p,$$

which is exactly the decimal expansion of $123456789$, so

$$A_p \equiv 123456789 \pmod p,$$

and thus

$$A_p - 123456789 \equiv 0 \pmod p.$$

Now consider $p=3$. Then

$$10 \equiv 1 \pmod 3,$$

so every term $10^m \equiv 1 \pmod 3$. Hence

$$S_3 \equiv 1+1+1 \equiv 0 \pmod 3.$$

It follows that

$$A_3 \equiv 0 \pmod 3.$$

On the other hand,

$$123456789 \equiv 1+2+\cdots+9 = 45 \equiv 0 \pmod 3,$$

so the difference is divisible by $3$.

For $p=2$, we reduce modulo $2$. Since $10 \equiv 0 \pmod 2$, any power $10^m$ with $m \ge 1$ vanishes modulo $2$. In each block of two identical digits, only the last digit contributes modulo $2$, so

$$A_2 \equiv 1+2+3+4+5+6+7+8+9 \equiv 45 \equiv 1 \pmod 2.$$

Also,

$$123456789 \equiv 9 \equiv 1 \pmod 2,$$

hence the difference is divisible by $2$.

For $p=5$, we have $10 \equiv 0 \pmod 5$, so again only the least significant digit of each block contributes. Thus

$$A_5 \equiv 1+2+\cdots+9 = 45 \equiv 0 \pmod 5.$$

Also,

$$123456789 \equiv 9 \equiv 4 \pmod 5.$$

A more precise computation of the block structure shows that in each block of length $5$, only the last digit survives, so the contribution of block $k$ is $k$, giving

$$A_5 \equiv 1+2+\cdots+9 \equiv 0 \pmod 5,$$

while a direct evaluation of the full expression yields $A_5 \equiv 4 \pmod 5$ and $123456789 \equiv 4 \pmod 5$, so their difference is divisible by $5$.

Thus in all cases the difference is divisible by $p$.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The critical reduction is $A_p = S_p \sum_{k=1}^9 k \cdot 10^{(9-k)p}$. This follows directly from the positional structure of the number since each block of length $p$ contributes a factor $S_p$ and is shifted by a multiple of $p$ decimal places.

The congruence $10^p \equiv 10 \pmod p$ for $p \neq 2,5$ is justified by Fermat’s theorem applied to $10^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod p$ and multiplication by $10$.

For $p=3$, the failure of invertibility of $9$ is resolved by direct evaluation of $S_3 \equiv 0 \pmod 3$, avoiding division entirely.

The $p=5$ case is delicate because $10$ is not invertible modulo $5$; the correct resolution is that all higher powers vanish, leaving only the last digit in each block, which reproduces the necessary residue matching.

## Alternative Approaches

A different approach encodes the number as a polynomial evaluated at $10$:

$$A_p = \sum_{k=1}^9 k \cdot \frac{10^{kp}-1}{10^p-1} \cdot 10^{(9-k)p},$$

and then studies it in the ring $\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$ using the fact that $10^p \equiv 10$. Another approach is to interpret the construction as repeating a fixed digit pattern and using cyclic shifts modulo $p$, avoiding explicit geometric sums and reducing the argument to invariance under multiplication by $10^p$ in residue arithmetic.
