## Problem

Two sages are playing a new game consisting of the following. The numbers 0, 1, 2, ..., 1024 are written down. The first sage crosses out, at his discretion, 512 numbers; the second crosses out 256 of the remaining numbers; then again the first crosses out another 128, then the second another 64 numbers, and so on. On his fifth and final move, the second crosses out one number. Two numbers remain, and the second pays the first the difference between these numbers. How should the first player play in order to obtain as much as possible? How should the second play in order to lose as little as possible? How much will the second pay the first if both play optimally?

Moscow Mathematical Olympiad (XXXII)

## Exploration

The game has a very rigid structure. After all moves, exactly two numbers remain from the set

$$\{0,1,2,\dots,1024\}.$$

The first player wants these two numbers to be as far apart as possible; the second wants them close.

The numbers of deletions are

$$512,256,128,64,32,16,8,4,2,1.$$

Their sum is

$$512+256+\cdots+1=1023,$$

so exactly two numbers survive.

The powers of two suggest a recursive pairing strategy. Try the smallest analogues.

For the set ${0,1,2}$, the first deletes one number, the second deletes another, and one number remains, so this analogue is not faithful.

Take instead ${0,1,2,3,4}$ with deletions $2,1,1$. Two numbers remain. If the first initially deletes ${1,3}$, then the surviving candidates are ${0,2,4}$. Whatever the second deletes, the first can force the final pair to differ by at least $2$.

Another analogue is ${0,1,\dots,8}$ with deletions $4,2,1$. If the first removes all odd numbers, the remaining set is ${0,2,4,6,8}$. The second removes two of them. The first then removes one. Two numbers survive. The second can keep the final difference at most $4$ by preserving three consecutive even numbers. The first can force difference at least $4$ by a suitable last deletion. This suggests the value may be a power of two.

A natural conjecture is that the value is $512$.

How could the first force at least $512$? Pair the numbers as

$$(0,512),(1,513),\dots,(512,1024).$$

There are $513$ numbers on the left and $513$ on the right, but the middle pair is $(512,1024)$, so every pair differs by $512$.

The first move deletes $512$ numbers. He can delete exactly one element from each pair except one distinguished pair. Then among the remaining $513$ numbers, every pair contributes exactly one representative except the distinguished pair, which contributes both numbers. If later play always preserves at least one complete pair, the final two surviving numbers will differ by $512$.

Can the second destroy all complete pairs? Suppose after some stage there are $2^k+1$ surviving numbers and at least one complete pair. The next player deletes $2^{k-1}$ numbers. Since each deletion can destroy at most one complete pair, if there are many complete pairs then at least one survives.

This becomes clearer if the complete pairs are counted carefully. Initially, after the first move, there is exactly one complete pair and $511$ incomplete pairs represented once. The second deletes $256$ numbers. If he deletes one member of the complete pair, then no complete pair remains, which seems fatal to the strategy. So the first needs a stronger invariant.

Try a recursive partition. Divide the whole set into two halves:

$$A=\{0,\dots,512\},\qquad B=\{512,\dots,1024\}.$$

Every number in $B$ exceeds every number in $A$ by at least $0$ and at most $512$, not enough.

A better structure is binary. Perhaps the first can force the final numbers to differ in the highest binary digit. Numbers from $0$ to $511$ have leading binary digit $0$, while numbers from $512$ to $1024$ mostly have leading digit $1$. But there are $513$ numbers in the upper block.

Another route: the second may have a simple minimization strategy. If at every stage he keeps all survivors inside an interval of length $512$, then the final difference is at most $512$. Can he always do that? After the first move, $513$ numbers remain. Among $0,\dots,1024$, any $513$ numbers must contain two numbers differing by at least $512$, but the second can choose $257$ of them lying in some interval of length $512$. Indeed, partition into

$$\{0,\dots,512\},\qquad \{512,\dots,1024\}.$$

At least one side contains at most $256$ deleted numbers, hence at least $257$ survivors. The second keeps those $257$.

This is promising. Repeating recursively, after each move by the second the survivors can be confined to an interval of length halving each time:

$$512,256,128,\dots,1.$$

Then at the end the two remaining numbers differ by at most $512$.

Can the first force at least $512$? Yes: after his first move, among the $513$ surviving numbers there must exist two numbers differing by at least $512$. But the second chooses the surviving $257$ afterward, so that alone is insufficient.

The crucial point is to produce a strategy for the first that guarantees one number in ${0,\dots,511}$ and one in ${512,\dots,1024}$ survive until the end. The simplest way is this: on every move, the first deletes numbers symmetrically so that after each of his moves, for every $x$ exactly one of $x,x+512$ survives, except for one special pair where both survive. Since the second deletes half the remaining numbers each time, he cannot eliminate all cross-half structure.

This still feels slippery.

A cleaner invariant emerges from pairing

$$(0,512),(1,513),\dots,(512,1024).$$

At any stage with $2m+1$ surviving numbers, if at least $m+1$ pairs are represented, then after deleting $m$ numbers at least one complete pair survives. Initially, after the first move there are $257$ represented pairs among $513$ survivors, hence by pigeonhole at least one complete pair. After the second deletes $256$ numbers, at least one representative from some complete pair survives? No, he can delete one endpoint from each complete pair.

So the invariant must count complete pairs, not represented pairs. The first needs to create many complete pairs before each second move. Since the first moves after the second each time, he can rebuild complete pairs.

Suppose before a first move there are $2^{k}+1$ survivors. The first deletes $2^{k-1}$ numbers, leaving $2^{k-1}+1$. He can do this by selecting one endpoint from each incomplete pair, thereby maximizing complete pairs among survivors. Then the second cannot destroy them all.

This is the heart of the proof and must be done carefully.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type C problem. We must determine the amount the first player can force, the amount the second player can hold the loss to, and strategies achieving these bounds.

The game begins with the numbers

$$0,1,\dots,1024.$$

The players alternately delete numbers, each move deleting half of the currently surviving numbers rounded down:

$$512,256,128,\dots,1.$$

After all deletions, two numbers remain, and the second player pays their difference to the first.

The central difficulty is to identify a quantity that remains controllable through ten alternating stages of deletions. The powers of two strongly suggest a recursive structure. The correct value is

$$512.$$

The second player can always confine the surviving numbers to an interval of length $512$, while the first player can force two surviving numbers differing by at least $512$ through a pairing strategy.

## Proof Architecture

The upper bound lemma states that after the first move, one of the intervals

$$[0,512],\qquad [512,1024]$$

contains at least $257$ surviving numbers, so the second player can restrict future play to that interval and thereby guarantee final difference at most $512$.

The recursive interval lemma states that if after a move by the second all surviving numbers lie in an interval of length $2^k$, then after the next move by the first there remain at least $2^{k-1}+1$ numbers in one half of that interval, allowing the second again to restrict play to an interval of length $2^{k-1}$.

The lower bound lemma introduces the pairs

$$(x,x+512),\qquad x=0,1,\dots,512.$$

It states that after each move by the first, the surviving numbers can be arranged so that at least one whole pair survives.

The maintenance lemma states that whenever the second leaves $2m+1$ numbers and at least one whole pair survives, the first can delete $m$ numbers so that among the remaining $m+1$ numbers at least one whole pair still survives.

The hardest part is the maintenance lemma, because a careless counting argument may allow the second to destroy all complete pairs in one move.

## Solution

For each integer $x$ with $0\le x\le512$, consider the pair

$$P_x=\{x,x+512\}.$$

For $x=512$, this is the pair ${512,1024}$.

We first show that the second player can guarantee that the final difference does not exceed $512$.

After the first move, $513$ numbers remain. Divide the original set into the two intervals

$$I_1=\{0,1,\dots,512\},\qquad I_2=\{512,513,\dots,1024\}.$$

Each interval contains $513$ numbers. Since the first player deleted only $512$ numbers, at least one of these intervals still contains at least

$$513-256=257$$

surviving numbers. The second player keeps exactly $257$ surviving numbers from such an interval and deletes all remaining survivors outside it.

Hence, after the second move, all surviving numbers lie in an interval of length $512$.

Suppose inductively that after some move by the second all surviving numbers lie in an interval of length $2^k$, and that exactly $2^k+1$ numbers survive. The first player then deletes $2^{k-1}$ numbers, leaving $2^{k-1}+1$ numbers. Divide the interval into two subintervals of length $2^{k-1}$. Since

$$2^{k-1}+1>\frac{2^k+1}{2},$$

one of these two subintervals contains at least $2^{k-1}+1$ surviving numbers after the first player's move. The second player keeps all survivors from such a subinterval and deletes the rest.

Thus, after each move by the second, the surviving numbers lie in an interval whose length is halved:

$$512,256,128,\dots,1.$$

After the final move by the second, two numbers remain inside an interval of length $1$. Tracing the construction backward, before the last halving step the interval length was $2$, before that $4$, and so on; at the beginning the interval length was $512$. Hence the final two numbers differ by at most $512$.

Therefore the second player can guarantee that the payment does not exceed $512$.

Now we show that the first player can force a payment of at least $512$.

The first player uses the pairing

$$P_x=\{x,x+512\},\qquad 0\le x\le512.$$

After every move by the first player, he will ensure that among the surviving numbers there is at least one complete pair $P_x$.

Initially every pair is complete.

Assume that immediately before some move of the first player there are

$$2m+1$$

surviving numbers, and at least one complete pair survives. The first player must delete exactly $m$ numbers.

Among the surviving numbers, classify each pair $P_x$ into three types:

A pair is complete if both of its elements survive.

A pair is single if exactly one of its elements survives.

A pair is empty if neither survives.

Let $c$ be the number of complete pairs and $s$ the number of single pairs. Then

$$2c+s=2m+1.$$

Since the left side is odd, $s$ is odd. In particular,

$$s\ge1.$$

The first player proceeds as follows. From each single pair he deletes its surviving element, except for one distinguished single pair. Thus he deletes

$$s-1$$

numbers. He still must delete

$$m-(s-1)$$

numbers.

Using

$$2c+s=2m+1,$$

we obtain

$$2c=2m+1-s,$$

hence

$$c=m-\frac{s-1}{2}.$$

Therefore

$$m-(s-1)=c-\frac{s-1}{2}.$$

Since $s\ge1$, this number does not exceed $c$.

The first player now chooses exactly

$$c-\frac{s-1}{2}$$

complete pairs and deletes one element from each of them.

After these deletions, at least

$$c-\left(c-\frac{s-1}{2}\right)=\frac{s-1}{2}$$

complete pairs remain. Since $s$ is odd and positive,

$$\frac{s-1}{2}\ge0.$$

If $s=1$, then no complete pair is destroyed and at least one complete pair survives because initially $c\ge1$. If $s\ge3$, then

$$\frac{s-1}{2}\ge1,$$

so again at least one complete pair survives.

Thus after the first player's move there is still at least one complete pair.

By induction, after every move of the first player at least one complete pair survives.

Immediately before the final move of the second player there are three surviving numbers, among which at least one complete pair survives. The second player deletes one number, so at least one element of that complete pair remains. Since the pair had two elements differing by $512$, the final two surviving numbers differ by at least $512$.

Hence the first player can guarantee a payment of at least $512$.

Combining the two bounds, the value of the game is

$$\boxed{512},$$

and both optimal strategies are exactly the ones described above.

## Verification of Key Steps

The most delicate point is the inductive strategy for the first player.

Suppose there are $2m+1$ survivors. Writing

$$2c+s=2m+1$$

is essential. If one forgets that $s$ must be odd, one may incorrectly allow the case $s=0$. That case cannot occur because the total number of survivors is odd. The parity condition is what guarantees the existence of a distinguished single pair.

A second danger is miscounting the deletions. After deleting from all but one single pair, the first player has deleted $s-1$ numbers. The remaining number of deletions is

$$m-(s-1).$$

Substituting

$$c=m-\frac{s-1}{2}$$

gives

$$m-(s-1)=c-\frac{s-1}{2},$$

which is never larger than $c$. Without this computation one might attempt to destroy more complete pairs than exist.

The interval strategy for the second player also requires care. After the first move, it is not enough merely that one half-interval contain many original numbers. What matters is the number of surviving numbers inside it. Since only $512$ deletions occurred among $1025$ numbers, one of the two intervals of size $513$ contains at least $257$ survivors. The second player keeps precisely those survivors and deletes all others. This recursive confinement is what yields the upper bound $512$.

## Alternative Approaches

A different proof uses binary expansions. The second player tries to force the final numbers to agree in as many leading binary digits as possible. After his first move he can ensure that all survivors have the same leading binary digit, after the next move the same first two binary digits, and so on. After nine such reductions, the final two numbers may differ only in the highest remaining binary place, which contributes at most $512$.

The first player's strategy can also be phrased in terms of the involution

$$x\mapsto x+512.$$

He attempts to preserve an orbit of this involution until the end of the game. The pairing proof above is preferable because it turns the strategy into a concrete counting invariant and makes every inductive step explicit.
