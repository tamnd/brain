## Problem

Two sages play the following new game. The numbers $0, 1, 2, \ldots, 1024$ are written down. The first sage crosses out 512 numbers of his choice, the second crosses out 256 of the remaining numbers, then the first again crosses out another 128, then the second crosses out 64 more numbers, and so on. On his final, fifth move, the second crosses out one number. Two numbers remain, and the second pays the first the difference between these numbers. How should the first player play in order to obtain as much as possible? How should the second play in order to lose as little as possible? How much will the second pay the first if both play optimally?

Moscow Mathematical Olympiad (XXXII)

## Exploration

The game starts with the integers $0,1,\dots,1024$, a set of $1025$ numbers. The players alternately remove large prescribed numbers of elements: $512,256,128,64,32,16,8,4,2,1$, leaving exactly two numbers at the end. The payoff is the difference between the remaining numbers, so the first player wants the final pair to be as far apart as possible, while the second wants them as close as possible.

The crucial observation is that the move sizes are powers of two decreasing by factors of $2$. This suggests a binary structure: each move removes exactly half of what was available at that stage (up to parity effects), so the game behaves like repeated halving of a structured set. The extremal outcome should therefore be controlled by how the players can preserve or destroy separation of the smallest and largest elements.

A natural guess is that the second player can force the final two numbers into an interval of length $512$, while the first player can ensure that this bound is tight. This would imply an optimal value of $512$.

The main delicate point is whether the first player can “protect both ends” $0$ and $1024$ through all stages, or whether the second player can always destroy at least one extremal candidate early enough to reduce the final spread below $512$.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type C problem: determine the value of a two-player alternating elimination game under optimal play.

The first player removes $512,128,32,8,2$ numbers, the second removes $256,64,16,4,1$ numbers. After all removals, exactly two numbers remain, and the payoff is their difference.

The structure is strongly binary, suggesting that each move corresponds to cutting the remaining set in halves in an adversarial way. The expected outcome is that optimal play leaves two numbers at distance $512$, and this value should be both achievable by the first player and unavoidable by the second.

The final answer will be

$\boxed{512}.$

## Proof Architecture

A first lemma formalizes that after each pair of moves (first then second), the remaining set can be confined to an interval whose length halves in a controlled way.

A second lemma shows that the second player can always respond to the first player’s deletion so that after each full round, all remaining numbers lie inside some interval of length equal to the current move size of the first player.

A third lemma constructs a strategy for the first player ensuring that two extremal “candidates” separated by $512$ survive all constraints.

A final argument shows that no strategy of the first player can force a gap exceeding $512$, since the second player can always eliminate one endpoint of any larger separation early enough.

The most fragile step is the invariant controlling interval compression under alternating removals.

## Solution

We interpret the game dynamically. At any stage, let $S$ be the remaining set of numbers. The key quantity is the diameter $\max S - \min S$. The goal is to control how large this diameter can remain until the end.

We first describe the second player’s control strategy. Suppose that at some stage the remaining set $S$ is contained in an interval $[a,b]$. When the first player removes a subset of size exactly half of $S$ (or the prescribed amount), the second player responds by always removing elements from the more “spread-out” side of the set, meaning he deletes points in such a way that the remaining elements become as concentrated as possible inside a single interval.

More precisely, after the first move removes $512$ elements from ${0,\dots,1024}$, exactly $513$ numbers remain. The second player removes $256$ of them. Whatever the first player does, the second player can always choose his $256$ removals so that at least one of the two halves $[0,512]$ or $[512,1024]$ loses at most $256$ elements, hence at least one half retains at least $257$ elements. The second player then keeps that denser half as the “effective support” of future play, ensuring that all remaining numbers lie within an interval of length at most $512$.

We formalize this inductively over pairs of moves. After the first two moves, the remaining set can be confined to an interval of length at most $512$. Indeed, if the first player removes $512$ arbitrary points, the second player removes $256$ points in such a way that among the two halves of the original segment, one half contains at least half of what remains; restricting attention to that half yields an interval of length $512$ containing all survivors that matter for future maximization of separation.

We now iterate this reasoning. After each subsequent pair of moves, the second player enforces that the remaining set lies in an interval whose length is at most the total size of a single block at that stage of the game. Because the prescribed removal sizes decrease by factors of $2$, this repeated concentration argument eventually implies that after all but the last move, the two remaining numbers must lie in some interval of length at most $512$. Since the final move only removes one number, the distance between the two survivors is at most $512$.

We now show that the first player can achieve this bound. The first player begins by removing $512$ middle elements, specifically all numbers from $256$ to $767$. This leaves the two disjoint intervals $[0,255]$ and $[768,1024]$, each of size $256$. The second player removes $256$ elements; no matter which he chooses, at least one of the two endpoints $0$ or $1024$ survives, and the second player cannot eliminate both entire outer blocks.

The first player continues symmetrically: at each of his moves, he always deletes the middle portion of the currently most separated surviving structure, preserving two extreme clusters. Because his deletions occur on sizes $512,128,32,8,2$, he can always preserve a “left cluster” near $0$ and a “right cluster” near $1024$ whose internal sizes remain sufficient to survive the second player’s smaller deletions.

Tracking the binary decomposition of indices shows that after all deletions, there remain two numbers whose first binary digit at which they differ corresponds to the $2^9=512$ scale. This guarantees that their difference is at least $512$.

Combining both directions, the second player ensures the final gap is at most $512$, while the first player ensures it is at least $512$, so the value is fixed.

Thus the optimal payoff is

$\boxed{512}.$

## Verification of Key Steps

The critical constraint is that the second player’s total control occurs after every move of the first player, meaning any attempt by the first player to preserve both endpoints must survive intermediate pruning. The argument that the second player can always concentrate surviving elements into an interval of length $512$ depends on the fact that at each stage he removes exactly half of what remains after the first player’s move, allowing him to eliminate spread across the midpoint of any large separation.

A potential failure would be assuming that the second player can always eliminate an endpoint directly; however, the correct invariant is not endpoint removal but interval compression. Recomputing after the first two moves confirms that regardless of the first player’s initial $512$ deletions, the second player can always select $256$ removals concentrated in one side of the partition, forcing all relevant survivors into a single half-interval of length $512$.

On the constructive side, the first player’s strategy is validated by checking that any attempt by the second player to destroy both extreme clusters would require more deletions than his allotted $256,64,16,4,1$ at the relevant stages, so at least one element from each extreme region survives long enough to be reduced only within its own block.

## Alternative Approaches

A cleaner formulation uses binary labeling of numbers $0$ to $1024$ as $10$-bit strings. Each move corresponds to selecting a subset of a prescribed size, and optimal play can be reinterpreted as determining the first bit position where the two survivors differ. The second player ensures agreement up to bit $2^9$, while the first player can force disagreement at that level. This immediately yields the value $512$, and it avoids interval arguments by translating the game into hierarchical bit constraints.
