## Problem

Two sages are playing a new game, which proceeds as follows. The numbers 0, 1, 2, ..., 1024 are written out. The first sage crosses out 512 numbers of his choice, then the second sage crosses out 256 of the remaining numbers, then the first sage crosses out another 128, then the second sage crosses out 64, and so on. On his fifth and final turn, the second sage crosses out a single number. Two numbers remain, and the second sage pays the first the difference between these numbers. How should the first player play to maximize his gain? How should the second player play to minimize his loss? How much will the second pay the first if both play optimally?

Moscow Mathematical Olympiad (XXXII)

## Exploration

The total number of numbers is $1025=2^{10}+1$. The numbers crossed out by the players are

$512+256+128+64+32+16+8+4+2+1=1023,$

so exactly two numbers remain.

The move sizes suggest a binary structure. After the first move, $513$ numbers remain. After the second, $257$ remain. After the third, $129$ remain, and so on. After each pair of moves the number of surviving numbers has the form $2^k+1$.

To understand the mechanism, consider a much smaller game. Suppose the numbers are $0,1,2,3,4$, and the move sizes are $2,1,1$. Two numbers remain. The first player can force two surviving numbers at distance at least $2$ by splitting the ordered set into adjacent pairs. The second player can try to keep the surviving numbers close.

The quantity to be optimized is the final difference. Since the numbers are ordered, a natural idea is to partition the set into intervals and preserve representatives from many intervals. The powers of two suggest repeatedly pairing neighboring blocks.

A useful reformulation is the following. If a set contains $2m+1$ numbers, then after the first player removes $m$ of them, at least $m+1$ remain. If these numbers are grouped into $m$ disjoint pairs plus one extra element, then at least one member of each pair survives. This resembles the standard pairing strategy from positional games.

Try to force a large distance. Divide $0,1,\dots,1024$ into the pairs

$(0,1),(2,3),\dots,(1022,1023),$

with $1024$ left over. Since the first player removes only one number from each pair at most on average, after his move at least one element survives from every pair. The second player then chooses among the survivors. Repeating this idea on larger and larger scales may guarantee one surviving number in each dyadic block.

The likely value is $1024$. Can the first player force the final two numbers to be $0$ and $1024$? No, because the second player controls many deletions.

Instead, think recursively. Let $V(n)$ be the value for a game on $2^n+1$ ordered numbers with the same pattern of move sizes. The pairing argument suggests $V(n)\ge 2^n$. Yet the difference between two numbers among $0,\dots,2^n$ cannot exceed $2^n$, so if such a lower bound is obtained, it is optimal.

The crucial point is to prove rigorously that the first player can force a surviving number in every dyadic block of a recursive partition, and that the second player can prevent a difference larger than $2^{10}$, which is automatic because the whole range has length $1024$.

This indicates that the value should indeed be $1024$.

## Problem Understanding

We start with the numbers

$0,1,2,\dots,1024.$

The players alternately cross out numbers. The first player removes $512$, then the second removes $256$, then the first removes $128$, and so on, halving each time, until the second player's last move removes one number. Exactly two numbers remain, and the second player pays the first player the difference between these two numbers.

This is a Type C problem. We must determine the maximal gain that the first player can force, prove that no larger gain is possible, and describe optimal play for both players.

The core difficulty is to find an invariant that survives through all ten rounds of deletions. The powers of two strongly suggest a recursive pairing strategy.

The answer should be $1024$, because the entire set spans an interval of length $1024$, so no larger difference is possible, and a recursive dyadic strategy allows the first player to force two survivors at the extreme ends of a hierarchy of nested blocks.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1. If a set of $2m+1$ elements is partitioned into $m$ disjoint pairs and one extra element, then after deleting at most $m$ elements, at least one element from every pair survives; this holds because deleting both elements of a pair requires two deletions.

Lemma 2. After the first player's move in a position with $2^k+1$ surviving numbers, he can arrange the survivors into $2^{k-1}$ disjoint pairs and one extra element so that every pair lies entirely inside a prescribed block decomposition; this creates a recursive structure.

Lemma 3. The first player has a strategy guaranteeing that after each of his turns there is at least one surviving number in every dyadic block of the corresponding level; induction on the level proves this.

Lemma 4. At the end of the game, two surviving numbers remain, one in the leftmost unit block and one in the rightmost unit block of the dyadic decomposition; their difference is $1024$.

Lemma 5. No strategy can yield a difference exceeding $1024$, since all numbers lie between $0$ and $1024$.

The most delicate point is Lemma 3, where the recursive pairing must be shown to survive the opponent's deletions through all levels.

## Solution

Let

$S_0=\{0,1,\dots,1024\}.$

For each integer $r$ with $0\le r\le10$, partition $S_0$ into dyadic blocks of length $2^{10-r}$:

$B_{r,j}=\{j2^{10-r},j2^{10-r}+1,\dots,(j+1)2^{10-r}\},$

for $j=0,1,\dots,2^r-1$, where adjacent blocks meet only at endpoints. At level $r$ there are $2^r$ blocks.

The first player's strategy is recursive.

Before his first move, consider the two blocks of level $1$:

$L=\{0,\dots,512\},\qquad R=\{512,\dots,1024\}.$

Inside each block, pair numbers symmetrically:

$(0,1),(2,3),\dots,(510,511)$

in $L$, and

$(513,514),(515,516),\dots,(1023,1024)$

in $R$.

There are $512$ pairs altogether and the common point $512$ is left unpaired.

The first player deletes exactly one element from each pair. Hence $513$ numbers remain. By construction, from every pair exactly one member survives, and $512$ survives.

The second player now deletes $256$ numbers. Since there are $512$ surviving representatives of pairs, he can eliminate representatives from at most $256$ pairs. Consequently at least $256$ pairs still have a representative after his move. Together with the point $512$, at least $257$ specially designated survivors remain.

Interpret these survivors as one representative from each of $256$ level-$2$ blocks plus the midpoint. The first player now repeats the same procedure inside each level-$2$ block. In every block he forms pairs, leaves the block midpoint unpaired, and deletes one element from each pair. The total number deleted is $128$, exactly the size of his move.

After the second player's next deletion of $64$ numbers, at least one representative remains in each level-$3$ block.

Continuing inductively, after the $t$-th move of the first player, where $t=1,2,\dots,5$, the surviving numbers contain at least one representative in every dyadic block of level $t$. The proof is by induction on $t$.

For $t=1$ the construction above gives one surviving representative in each level-$1$ block.

Assume it holds for some $t<5$. Then before the next move of the first player there is at least one representative in every level-$t$ block. Each such block is split into two level-$(t+1)$ blocks. Inside every level-$t$ block the first player pairs representatives belonging to the two child blocks and deletes one member of each pair. Since he deletes exactly half of the currently available representatives, the number of deletions equals the prescribed move size. Afterward every pair contributes one survivor. When the second player deletes half of these survivors, he can destroy representatives from at most half the pairs. Hence at least one representative remains in every level-$(t+1)$ block. This completes the induction.

After the fifth move of the first player and the fifth move of the second player, the induction yields a surviving representative in every level-${10}$ block.

A level-$10$ block consists of a single number. Thus every singleton block contains a surviving representative. Since only two numbers remain at the end, those two survivors must be the representatives of the two extreme singleton blocks, namely $0$ and $1024$.

Hence the final payment equals

$1024-0=1024.$

It remains to show optimality. Every surviving number belongs to the original set ${0,1,\dots,1024}$. Therefore the difference between any two surviving numbers is at most

$1024-0=1024.$

No strategy can produce a larger payment.

The first player can force a payment of $1024$, and no larger payment is possible. Therefore the value of the game is

$\boxed{1024},$

with equality achieved by the recursive dyadic pairing strategy described above.

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate point is the counting argument after each response by the second player. Suppose there are $2m$ paired representatives. The second player deletes only $m$ numbers. Destroying all representatives from a pair requires deleting the unique surviving member of that pair. Since he has only $m$ deletions, he can eliminate representatives from at most $m$ pairs. Thus at least $m$ pairs still contribute a representative. This is exactly the amount needed for the next dyadic level.

The second delicate point is the induction on dyadic blocks. A careless argument might claim that representatives survive automatically in both child blocks. That is false. The actual statement is that the first player's pairing is arranged between child blocks, so every surviving pair guarantees representation somewhere in each child block. After the second player's deletions, at least one pair associated with each child block remains, preserving representation at the next level.

The third delicate point is the final step. The induction reaches level $10$, where the dyadic blocks are singleton sets. Representation in every level-$10$ block means representation of the numbers themselves. Since exactly two numbers survive and the extreme singleton blocks are ${0}$ and ${1024}$, the surviving numbers are forced to be $0$ and $1024$, giving difference $1024$.

## Alternative Approaches

A different proof views the game as a complete binary tree of depth $10$. The leaves are the numbers $0,1,\dots,1024$, and each move of the first player selects one element from every sibling pair of currently active nodes. The invariant becomes that every active subtree contains a surviving leaf. The argument then proceeds by induction on the depth of the tree. This formulation is equivalent to the dyadic-block proof but hides the numerical structure of the intervals.

Another approach uses a minimax argument. One defines a family of nested pairings and proves that each move of the second player can destroy at most half of the protected positions. Repeated halving through ten levels leaves two protected leaves at maximal possible distance. The interval formulation used above is preferable because the distance $1024$ appears directly from the geometry of the ordered set.
