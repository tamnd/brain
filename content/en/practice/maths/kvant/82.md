## Problem

On a circular road there are several identical cars parked. It is known that if all the gasoline in these cars were poured into one of them, that car would be able to travel around the entire circular road and return to its starting position. Prove that at least one of the cars standing on the road can go around the whole circle, taking gasoline from the other cars along the way.

## Exploration

Let the circle contain $n$ cars indexed in order along the road. Let $a_i$ denote the amount of fuel in the $i$-th car and let $b_i$ be the amount of fuel needed to drive from car $i$ to car $i+1$ (with indices taken modulo $n$). The condition of the problem states that if all fuel is collected into a single car, that car can complete the circuit, which translates to

$$\sum_{i=1}^n a_i \ge \sum_{i=1}^n b_i.$$

Define $s_i = a_i - b_i$. Then $\sum_{i=1}^n s_i \ge 0$.

The goal is to find a starting index $k$ such that the cumulative sums

$$s_k,\quad s_k + s_{k+1},\quad \dots,\quad s_k + \cdots + s_{k+n-1}$$

are all nonnegative. This would mean that a car starting at $k$ never runs out of fuel while collecting fuel from cars it passes.

The key issue is that although total surplus is nonnegative, it may be distributed unevenly, so some prefixes are negative. The correct strategy is to locate a point where the cumulative sum attains its minimum and start immediately after it.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem. One must prove that under the condition of nonnegative total fuel surplus, there exists a starting car such that moving around the circle and collecting fuel allows completion of the full loop without running out of fuel, and also that no other possibility is required beyond this argument.

The core difficulty is to convert a global inequality on total fuel into a local condition guaranteeing a valid starting position for a cyclic process. The intuitive expectation is that the “lowest fuel deficit point” should serve as a reset point after which accumulated surplus never becomes negative.

The expected answer is that such a starting car always exists, justified by choosing the position of minimal partial sum and starting immediately after it.

## Proof Architecture

Let $s_i = a_i - b_i$. The first lemma asserts that $\sum_{i=1}^n s_i \ge 0$, which follows directly from the condition that one car with all fuel can complete the circle.

The second lemma states that if $S_k = s_1 + \cdots + s_k$ attains its minimum at some index $m$, then starting from $m+1$ yields all cyclic partial sums nonnegative, because any segment starting after a minimum cannot dip below that minimum.

The third lemma formalizes that cyclic reindexing preserves total sums and allows interpretation of the route starting from any chosen car.

The hardest direction is the second lemma, since it converts a global extremal property of prefix sums into a uniform nonnegativity condition over all cyclic intervals.

## Solution

Let $a_i$ be the fuel in the $i$-th car and $b_i$ the fuel required to move from car $i$ to car $i+1$, with indices taken modulo $n$. Define $s_i = a_i - b_i$. The condition of the problem implies

$$\sum_{i=1}^n a_i \ge \sum_{i=1}^n b_i,$$

hence

$$\sum_{i=1}^n s_i \ge 0.$$

Define prefix sums $S_0 = 0$ and

$$S_k = \sum_{i=1}^k s_i \quad \text{for } 1 \le k \le n.$$

Let $m$ be an index such that $S_m \le S_k$ for all $k \in {0,1,\dots,n}$.

Consider starting at car $m+1$. For any $t \in {1,\dots,n}$, the fuel balance after visiting $t$ cars is

$$\sum_{i=m+1}^{m+t} s_i,$$

where indices are taken cyclically. This expression can be rewritten using prefix sums as

$$(S_{m+t} - S_m),$$

where indices exceeding $n$ are interpreted using $S_n = \sum_{i=1}^n s_i$ and periodic continuation.

If $m+t \le n$, then $S_{m+t} \ge S_m$ by minimality of $S_m$, so the difference is nonnegative. If $m+t > n$, then

$$S_{m+t} = S_n + S_{m+t-n},$$

and therefore

$$S_{m+t} - S_m = (S_n - S_m) + S_{m+t-n}.$$

Both terms are nonnegative since $S_m$ is minimal and $S_n \ge S_m$, and also $S_{m+t-n} \ge S_m$ implies $S_{m+t-n} - S_m \ge 0$, which yields nonnegativity of the whole expression.

Thus every partial sum along the journey starting from car $m+1$ is nonnegative, so the car starting at $m+1$ never runs out of fuel while collecting fuel from visited cars and completes the full circle.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The central step is the use of the minimal prefix sum index $m$. The validity depends on confirming that every cyclic segment sum can be expressed as a difference of two prefix sums, which holds because each segment corresponds either to a standard prefix interval or to a wrapped interval decomposed into a full sum plus a prefix.

The second delicate point is ensuring that wrapping around the index $n$ does not break the inequality. Writing $S_{m+t} = S_n + S_{m+t-n}$ reduces the wrapped case to two standard prefix expressions, both compared against the same minimal value $S_m$, so no hidden negative contribution appears.

The third point is that minimality of $S_m$ among all $S_k$ including $S_0=0$ ensures that comparisons remain valid even when the minimum occurs at the origin.

## Alternative Approaches

A different proof uses a greedy simulation: starting from an arbitrary car, one attempts to traverse the circle while tracking fuel, and whenever failure occurs, the next car becomes a new candidate start. This process eliminates at least one candidate per failure and eventually yields a valid starting position.

Another approach interprets the problem as finding a rotation of a sequence with nonnegative partial sums, which can be shown via cyclic shifting and a discrete optimization argument over all $n$ rotations.
