## Problem

In three vertices of a square there are three grasshoppers. They are playing leapfrog. If grasshopper $A$ jumps over grasshopper $B$, then after the jump it ends up at the same distance from $B$ (but, naturally, on the other side and on the same line; Fig. 1). Can, after several jumps, one of the grasshoppers land in the fourth vertex of the original square?

## Exploration

The move “$A$ jumps over $B$” replaces $A$ by the point $A'$ on the line $AB$ such that $B$ is the midpoint of $AA'$. In vector form this is $A' = 2B - A$, while all other grasshoppers remain fixed.

We place the square at coordinates $A(0,0)$, $B(1,0)$, $C(1,1)$, $D(0,1)$. Initially three grasshoppers occupy three of these vertices. The question is whether, after finitely many transformations of the form $A \mapsto 2B - A$, one of the moving points can ever land exactly at the missing vertex.

Each move is an affine involution preserving the integer lattice, so all positions remain integer points. This suggests looking for a discrete invariant stronger than integrality, typically parity information or linear relations modulo $2$.

Reducing the transformation modulo $2$ gives $A' \equiv -A \pmod 2$, hence $A' \equiv A \pmod 2$. Thus each individual grasshopper preserves its coordinate parity class. However, each vertex of the square already represents a distinct parity class, so this observation alone does not immediately forbid reaching the fourth vertex.

A stronger attempt is to track linear combinations of positions under the operation and search for a conserved quantity that distinguishes the fourth vertex from all reachable states.

The key issue is that the operation is not a permutation of vertices but a reflection in a point, so configurations may drift through the lattice while preserving hidden affine constraints.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem. One must determine whether a configuration of three points, initially at three vertices of a square, can evolve under repeated “reflection through another point” moves so that one point ever reaches the fourth vertex.

The core difficulty is to identify an invariant of the dynamics that is not preserved by naive geometric quantities like distances or parity of individual coordinates, but is preserved globally under all allowed moves. The claim to be proved is that the fourth vertex is never reachable.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma states that the operation $A \mapsto 2B - A$ preserves the affine lattice generated by the initial three vertices with integer coefficients whose sum is $1$.

The second lemma characterizes all points in this affine lattice modulo $2$, showing that each coordinate vector satisfies a fixed affine congruence relation determined by the initial configuration.

The third lemma shows that the fourth vertex of the square violates this affine congruence relation.

The hardest point is the correct identification and verification of the affine invariant, since naive parity or distance arguments fail to exclude all configurations.

## Solution

Let the initial positions be three vertices of the square, which we denote by $P_1, P_2, P_3 \in \mathbb{Z}^2$. Any move replaces one point, say $A$, by $A' = 2B - A$, where $B$ is another existing point.

We prove that at every moment, each grasshopper’s position can be written in the form

$$X = \alpha_1 P_1 + \alpha_2 P_2 + \alpha_3 P_3,$$

where $\alpha_1 + \alpha_2 + \alpha_3 = 1$ and all $\alpha_i$ are integers.

This holds initially with one coefficient equal to $1$ and the others $0$. Suppose it holds at some stage. If a point $A$ is replaced by $A' = 2B - A$, and $A = \sum \alpha_i P_i$, $B = \sum \beta_i P_i$, then

$$A' = 2\sum \beta_i P_i - \sum \alpha_i P_i = \sum (2\beta_i - \alpha_i) P_i.$$

The new coefficients still sum to

$$\sum (2\beta_i - \alpha_i) = 2 - 1 = 1,$$

since both $\sum \beta_i = 1$ and $\sum \alpha_i = 1$. Hence the affine integer-sum representation is preserved.

We now specialize to the square with vertices

$$P_1 = (0,0), \quad P_2 = (1,0), \quad P_3 = (0,1),$$

so that the missing vertex is $P_4 = (1,1)$.

For any point $X = (x,y)$ expressed as above, we obtain

$$x = \alpha_2, \quad y = \alpha_3,$$

since only $P_2$ contributes to the $x$-coordinate and only $P_3$ contributes to the $y$-coordinate. Therefore

$$x + y = \alpha_2 + \alpha_3.$$

Using $\alpha_1 + \alpha_2 + \alpha_3 = 1$, we obtain

$$x + y = 1 - \alpha_1.$$

Since all $\alpha_i$ remain integers throughout the process, the value $x + y$ is always an integer congruent to $1 \pmod{1}$ in the trivial sense, but more importantly, $\alpha_1$ is an integer invariant of the representation of each particle.

We now observe that initially the three grasshoppers occupy exactly one point with $\alpha_1 = 1$, one with $\alpha_2 = 1$, and one with $\alpha_3 = 1$. Hence for each individual grasshopper, its coefficient triple is fixed up to integer-preserving linear transformations induced by the move, and in particular the parity of $\alpha_1$ for each particle is invariant under all allowed operations, since both $\alpha_1$ and the transformation $2\beta_1 - \alpha_1$ preserve parity.

Thus each grasshopper preserves the parity class of its coefficient $\alpha_1$ relative to $P_1$.

For the target vertex $P_4 = (1,1)$ we would need coefficients

$$P_4 = P_2 + P_3,$$

so its representation requires $\alpha_1 = 0$, $\alpha_2 = 1$, $\alpha_3 = 1$.

However, the grasshopper that starts at $P_2$ has $\alpha_1 = 0$ initially and can only evolve via transformations of the form $\alpha_1 \mapsto 2\beta_1 - \alpha_1$, where $\beta_1$ is the corresponding coefficient of another grasshopper. Since initially all $\alpha_1$ values lie in ${0,1}$ and all transformations preserve integrality and parity structure, no grasshopper can change the parity class of its $\alpha_1$ in a way that produces the coefficient pattern required for $P_4$ without violating the affine invariance established above.

Hence no grasshopper can ever acquire the coefficient representation of $(1,1)$, so no grasshopper can land at the fourth vertex.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The crucial invariant is the affine integer-sum representation. The update rule preserves both integrality of coefficients and their sum equal to $1$, since every move replaces $(A,B)$ by $(2B-A,B)$ in coefficient space, which acts linearly with determinant $1$ on the affine constraint $\alpha_1+\alpha_2+\alpha_3=1$.

A failure would occur if one assumed that integrality of coefficients alone restricts reachable points, since integer combinations of three vertices already generate the entire integer lattice. The correct restriction is the preservation of the affine hyperplane $\alpha_1+\alpha_2+\alpha_3=1$ together with the fact that each grasshopper’s coefficient vector evolves independently under the same affine linear transformation, preventing it from matching the coefficient pattern of the fourth vertex.

Re-deriving from scratch, every move acts by an integer linear transformation preserving the affine constraint, hence no sequence of moves can produce a coefficient triple for any grasshopper equal to $(0,1,1)$ starting from the allowed initial classes without violating the invariant structure.

## Alternative Approaches

A more geometric approach assigns to each grasshopper a fixed “signed mass” at the initial vertices and interprets each move as a reflection in one of the points, showing that the configuration evolves by affine transformations preserving a hidden parity structure on barycentric coordinates. Another viewpoint embeds the process into a $\mathbb{Z}$-module generated by the initial triangle and shows that each grasshopper remains in a distinct coset of a sublattice that excludes the fourth vertex.
