## Problem

Prove that every convex polyhedron has two faces with the same number of sides.

A. Grünthal

## Exploration

A convex polyhedron has a finite number of faces, edges, and vertices. Each face is a polygon with at least three sides, and each edge belongs to exactly two faces. The key global relations are Euler’s formula $V - E + F = 2$ and the double counting identity $\sum k f_k = 2E$, where $f_k$ denotes the number of faces with $k$ sides.

The statement concerns repetition among face sizes. A natural attempt to avoid repetition is to assume all faces have distinct numbers of sides, so the face sizes form a strictly increasing sequence of integers starting from $3$. This restriction forces the sum of face degrees to grow rapidly with the number of faces, while Euler’s formula and vertex degree constraints limit how large the number of edges can be.

The most delicate point is to compare a lower bound on $E$ obtained from forcing all face sizes to be distinct with an upper bound on $E$ coming from vertex degree constraints. A contradiction should arise when these bounds are incompatible.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem: we must prove that in every convex polyhedron there exist two faces with the same number of sides.

The core difficulty is to translate a combinatorial statement about face sizes into global inequalities involving $V$, $E$, and $F$, and then force a contradiction under the assumption that all face sizes are distinct.

The expected mechanism is a double counting argument combined with Euler’s formula and degree constraints at vertices.

The claim is that repetition of face sizes is unavoidable in any convex polyhedron.

## Proof Architecture

The proof assumes all faces have pairwise distinct numbers of sides and derives a contradiction.

The first lemma expresses $2E$ as the sum of face degrees $\sum k f_k$.

The second lemma uses Euler’s formula together with the fact that every vertex has degree at least $3$ to obtain $E \le 3F - 6$.

The third lemma minimizes $\sum k f_k$ under the constraint that all face sizes are distinct positive integers at least $3$, producing a lower bound on $E$.

The final step compares the upper and lower bounds for $E$ and shows they are incompatible.

The most delicate point is the minimization of the sum of distinct face sizes and ensuring that this extremal configuration is correctly identified.

## Solution

Let $f_k$ denote the number of faces with $k$ sides. Each edge is incident to exactly two faces, hence

$$\sum_{k \ge 3} k f_k = 2E.$$

Assume that all faces have distinct numbers of sides. Then each $f_k$ is either $0$ or $1$, and there are exactly $F$ values of $k$ appearing. Since every face has at least $3$ sides, the face sizes are $k_1 < k_2 < \dots < k_F$ with $k_i \ge 3$.

For a fixed number of faces $F$, the quantity $\sum k_i$ is minimized when $k_i = 3 + (i-1)$ for all $i$, since replacing any gap by a smaller integer strictly decreases the sum while preserving distinctness and the lower bound $k_i \ge 3$. Hence

$$2E \ge \sum_{i=1}^F (3+i-1).$$

The right-hand side is an arithmetic progression:

$$\sum_{i=1}^F (i+2) = \sum_{i=1}^F i + 2F = \frac{F(F+1)}{2} + 2F = \frac{F(F+5)}{2}.$$

Thus

$$2E \ge \frac{F(F+5)}{2}, \quad E \ge \frac{F(F+5)}{4}.$$

Next we derive an upper bound for $E$. Every vertex has degree at least $3$, so counting edge-vertex incidences gives $2E \ge 3V$. Euler’s formula $V - E + F = 2$ implies $V = E - F + 2$. Substituting yields

$$2E \ge 3(E - F + 2).$$

Expanding,

$$2E \ge 3E - 3F + 6,$$

so

$$E \le 3F - 6.$$

Combining the bounds,

$$\frac{F(F+5)}{4} \le 3F - 6.$$

Multiplying by $4$ gives

$$F(F+5) \le 12F - 24,$$

so

$$F^2 - 7F + 24 \le 0.$$

The quadratic polynomial $F^2 - 7F + 24$ has discriminant $49 - 96 = -47$, hence it is strictly positive for all real $F$. This contradicts the inequality.

Therefore the assumption that all faces have distinct numbers of sides is impossible, so there exist two faces with the same number of sides.

$$\boxed{\text{Every convex polyhedron has two faces with the same number of sides.}}$$

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The most delicate step is the minimization of $\sum k_i$ under the constraint that the $k_i$ are distinct integers at least $3$. Any deviation from consecutive integers increases the sum because replacing a larger gap by a smaller missing integer preserves distinctness and strictly decreases the total.

Another sensitive point is the derivation of $E \le 3F - 6$. This depends on the inequality $2E \ge 3V$, which holds because each vertex in a convex polyhedron is incident to at least three edges; substituting Euler’s formula gives a linear relation between $E$ and $F$ without hidden assumptions.

The final contradiction step relies on verifying that the quadratic $F^2 - 7F + 24$ is always positive, confirmed via its negative discriminant.

## Alternative Approaches

A different approach replaces global inequalities with a purely graph-theoretic argument on the planar dual graph. In the dual, faces become vertices and face degrees become vertex degrees, and the problem becomes showing that a planar graph cannot have all vertex degrees distinct. That method shifts the argument to degree sequences in planar graphs but ultimately relies on the same tension between Euler’s formula and degree constraints.

The direct inequality approach is preferable because it isolates the contradiction in a single scalar inequality involving $F$, avoiding additional structural translation steps.
