## Problem

In a set consisting of $n$ elements, $2^{n-1}$ subsets are chosen, each three of which have a common element. Prove that all these subsets have a common element.

Moscow Mathematical Olympiad (XXX)

## Exploration

Let the chosen family of subsets be $\mathcal F$, with $|\mathcal F|=2^{n-1}$. The condition says that every three members of $\mathcal F$ have a common element.

The statement resembles a Helly-type phenomenon for a very large family. The number $2^{n-1}$ is exactly half of all subsets of an $n$-element set. The natural way to seek a contradiction is to assume that no element belongs to all members of $\mathcal F$.

Take the ground set $X=\{1,\dots,n\}$. For each $i\in X$, if $i$ is not common to all members of $\mathcal F$, there exists a set $A_i\in\mathcal F$ with $i\notin A_i$.

Because every three chosen sets have a common element, any three of the sets $A_i$ must intersect. But the intersection of all $A_i$ is empty, since for each element $i$, the set $A_i$ omits $i$. Hence the family $\{A_i\}$ has empty total intersection while every three members intersect.

Try to understand the structure of $\{A_i\}$. If $A_i\cap A_j\cap A_k\neq\varnothing$ for every triple, then for each element $x$, the number of sets among the $A_i$ that omit $x$ can be at most $2$; otherwise three of them would all omit $x$, making their intersection miss $x$. Since $A_i$ omits $i$, there are at least $n$ omissions altogether. Having at most $2$ omissions per element gives at most $2n$ omissions, which is too weak.

A better route is to count subsets outside $\mathcal F$. Since $A_i\in\mathcal F$, no member of $\mathcal F$ can be contained in the complement $B_i=X\setminus A_i$, because then that member together with two copies of $A_i$ would have empty intersection. More directly, if $C\subseteq B_i$, then $C\cap A_i=\varnothing$, so $C$ cannot belong to $\mathcal F$, for otherwise $A_i,A_i,C$ would violate the triple-intersection condition.

Thus every subset of $B_i$ lies outside $\mathcal F$. Let $b_i=|B_i|$. Then at least $2^{b_i}$ subsets are excluded because of $A_i$.

Since $\bigcup_i B_i=X$, the complements $B_i$ cover the ground set. The sum $\sum 2^{b_i}$ should then exceed $2^{n-1}$ unless one of the $B_i$ equals $X$. We need a clean argument. The covering condition implies $\sum b_i\ge n$. By convexity of $2^x$,

$$
\sum_{i=1}^n 2^{b_i}\ge n\cdot 2^{(\sum b_i)/n}\ge 2n.
$$

This is far from enough.

A more promising idea is to use the union of all downward-closed families $\mathcal P(B_i)$. We need to show that the union contains at least $2^{n-1}$ subsets. Since the $B_i$ cover $X$, every subset $S$ whose complement has size at most $1$ must contain some $B_i$? That does not look right.

Consider complements. A subset $T$ belongs to $\mathcal P(B_i)$ iff $T\subseteq B_i$, equivalently $X\setminus T\supseteq A_i$. Thus all supersets of every $A_i$ correspond to excluded subsets under complement. The family of supersets of $A_i$ has size $2^{b_i}$.

The key observation is that the sets $A_i$ have empty intersection. Hence for every subset $Y\subseteq X$, either $Y$ contains some $A_i$ or $X\setminus Y$ contains some $A_i$. Indeed, if neither side contains any $A_i$, then for every $i$ the set $A_i$ meets both $Y$ and $X\setminus Y$; choosing $x_i\in A_i\cap Y$ and $y_i\in A_i\cap(X\setminus Y)$ does not immediately yield a contradiction.
