## Problem

In the trapezoid $ABCD$ with bases $AB=a$ and $CD=b$, the segment $A_1B_1$ joining the midpoints of the diagonals is drawn. In the resulting trapezoid $A_1B_1CD$, the segment $A_2B_2$ joining the midpoints of the diagonals is again drawn, and so on (Fig. 1). Can some number occur twice in the sequence of lengths of the segments $AB$, $A_1B_1$, $A_2B_2$, ...? Will this sequence be monotonic (increasing or decreasing)? Does it tend to some limit?

## Exploration

Let $x_n$ denote the length of the upper base of the $n$-th trapezoid in the construction, and let the lower base remain $b$. Thus $x_0=a$, $x_1=A_1B_1$, $x_2=A_2B_2$, and so on.

The first task is to find the length of the segment joining the midpoints of the diagonals of a trapezoid with bases of lengths $u$ and $v$.

Choose coordinates

$$A=(0,0),\qquad B=(u,0),\qquad D=(p,h),\qquad C=(p+v,h).$$

The midpoint of diagonal $AC$ is

$$M=\left(\frac{p+v}{2},\frac h2\right),$$

and the midpoint of diagonal $BD$ is

$$N=\left(\frac{u+p}{2},\frac h2\right).$$

Hence

$$MN=\frac{|u-v|}{2}.$$

Thus every step replaces the upper base length $x_n$ by

$$x_{n+1}=\frac{|x_n-b|}{2}.$$

The entire problem is therefore reduced to the dynamics of this recurrence.

Trying a few examples:

If $a=10$, $b=2$, then

$$10,\ 4,\ 1,\ \frac12,\ \frac34,\ \frac58,\dots$$

The sequence first decreases, then increases.

If $a=1$, $b=5$, then

$$1,\ 2,\ \frac32,\ \frac74,\ \frac{13}{8},\dots$$

The sequence first increases, then decreases.

Both examples suggest convergence to a fixed point. Solving

$$x=\frac{|x-b|}{2}$$

gives two possibilities. If $x\ge b$, then $2x=x-b$, impossible for $b>0$. If $x<b$, then

$$2x=b-x,$$

hence

$$x=\frac b3.$$

The crucial point is to prove convergence and determine whether repeated values can occur. Writing

$$y_n=x_n-\frac b3,$$

one finds

$$y_{n+1}= \begin{cases} -\dfrac12\,y_n,& x_n<b,\\[2mm] \dfrac12\,y_n-\dfrac b2,& x_n\ge b. \end{cases}$$

The second case can occur at most once, because then

$$x_{n+1}=\frac{x_n-b}{2}<x_n,$$

and in fact $x_{n+1}<b$. After that,

$$y_{n+1}=-\frac12\,y_n.$$

Hence the distance from $b/3$ is halved at every step, proving convergence.

For repeated values, if $x_{n+1}=x_n$, then $x_n=b/3$. Thus once the sequence reaches $b/3$, every subsequent term equals $b/3$. It remains to determine when $b/3$ is actually attained. For $x_n<b$,

$$x_{n+1}=\frac{b-x_n}{2}.$$

The equation $x_{n+1}=b/3$ yields $x_n=b/3$. Therefore the only way to hit the fixed point is to start from it, or to arrive there after the unique possible step from $x_n=b$.

Indeed, if some term equals $b$, the next equals $0$, and then the next equals $b/2$, then $b/4$, etc.; $b/3$ is not reached. Thus repeated values occur precisely when some term already equals $b/3$.

## Problem Understanding

We repeatedly apply the following operation to a trapezoid: join the midpoints of its diagonals, obtaining a new segment parallel to the bases. Together with the lower base it forms a new trapezoid, and the process is repeated.

The problem asks three things. Can two terms of the sequence

$$AB,\ A_1B_1,\ A_2B_2,\dots$$

be equal? Is the sequence necessarily monotonic? Does it converge?

This is a Type A problem. We must classify when repeated values occur and determine completely the behavior of the sequence.

The core difficulty is to express the length of the segment joining the midpoints of the diagonals in terms of the two bases of a trapezoid. Once that formula is found, the geometry becomes a one-dimensional recurrence relation.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma states that in a trapezoid with bases of lengths $u$ and $v$, the segment joining the midpoints of the diagonals has length $\frac{|u-v|}{2}$.

The second lemma states that if $x_n$ denotes the $n$-th segment length in the construction, then

$$x_{n+1}=\frac{|x_n-b|}{2}.$$

The third lemma states that after at most one step all terms satisfy $x_n<b$.

The fourth lemma states that for every $n$ with $x_n<b$,

$$x_{n+1}-\frac b3=-\frac12\left(x_n-\frac b3\right).$$

The fifth lemma states that

$$\left|x_{n+1}-\frac b3\right| =\frac12\left|x_n-\frac b3\right|$$

for all sufficiently large $n$, hence $x_n\to b/3$.

The hardest point is showing that the recurrence eventually becomes linear around the fixed point $b/3$ and that this yields complete information about monotonicity and repeated values.

## Solution

Let $x_0=a$, and for $n\ge0$ let $x_n$ denote the length of the upper base of the trapezoid obtained after $n$ steps. Thus $x_1=A_1B_1$, $x_2=A_2B_2$, and so forth. The lower base is always $CD=b$.

Consider an arbitrary trapezoid with bases of lengths $u$ and $v$. Choose coordinates

$$A=(0,0),\qquad B=(u,0),\qquad D=(p,h),\qquad C=(p+v,h).$$

Let $M$ and $N$ be the midpoints of diagonals $AC$ and $BD$.

Then

$$M=\left(\frac{p+v}{2},\frac h2\right), \qquad N=\left(\frac{u+p}{2},\frac h2\right).$$

Hence $MN$ is parallel to the bases and

$$MN=\left|\frac{u+p}{2}-\frac{p+v}{2}\right| =\frac{|u-v|}{2}.$$

Applying this to the trapezoid whose bases are $x_n$ and $b$, we obtain

$$x_{n+1}=\frac{|x_n-b|}{2}.$$

Suppose first that $x_n\ge b$. Then

$$x_{n+1}=\frac{x_n-b}{2}<x_n.$$

Moreover,

$$x_{n+1}<b.$$

Thus after at most one step every subsequent term satisfies $x_n<b$.

For all indices with $x_n<b$,

$$x_{n+1}=\frac{b-x_n}{2}.$$

Subtracting $b/3$ gives

$$x_{n+1}-\frac b3 = \frac{b-x_n}{2}-\frac b3 = -\frac12\left(x_n-\frac b3\right).$$

Therefore

$$\left|x_{n+1}-\frac b3\right| = \frac12\left|x_n-\frac b3\right|.$$

Since the distance from $b/3$ is multiplied by $1/2$ at each step, it tends to $0$. Consequently,

$$\lim_{n\to\infty}x_n=\frac b3.$$

We next study monotonicity.

If $x_n<b/3$, then

$$x_{n+1} = \frac{b-x_n}{2} > \frac{b-b/3}{2} = \frac b3,$$

and

$$x_{n+1}-x_n = \frac{b-3x_n}{2} >0.$$

If $b/3<x_n<b$, then

$$x_{n+1} < \frac b3,$$

and

$$x_{n+1}-x_n = \frac{b-3x_n}{2} <0.$$

Thus every term below $b/3$ is followed by a larger term above $b/3$, and every term above $b/3$ is followed by a smaller term below $b/3$. Unless a term equals $b/3$, the sequence alternates around $b/3$ and cannot be monotonic.

Finally, we determine when a value can occur twice.

Assume $x_n=x_{n+1}$. Then

$$x_n=\frac{|x_n-b|}{2}.$$

If $x_n\ge b$, this gives

$$2x_n=x_n-b,$$

which is impossible because $b>0$.

If $x_n<b$, this gives

$$2x_n=b-x_n,$$

hence

$$x_n=\frac b3.$$

Therefore equal consecutive terms occur only at the fixed point $b/3$.

Now suppose some value occurs twice, say $x_m=x_n$ with $m<n$. From the relation

$$x_{k+1}-\frac b3 = -\frac12\left(x_k-\frac b3\right)$$

valid for all sufficiently large $k$, the quantity

$$\left|x_k-\frac b3\right|$$

strictly decreases unless it is $0$. Hence two distinct terms can be equal only when both equal $b/3$.

Conversely, if some term equals $b/3$, then every subsequent term also equals $b/3$.

Thus a number occurs twice in the sequence if and only if the value $b/3$ appears; in that case it appears infinitely many times.

The complete answer is

$$\boxed{\begin{array}{c} \text{The sequence satisfies }x_{n+1}=\dfrac{|x_n-b|}{2}.\\[2mm] \text{It converges to } \dfrac b3.\\[2mm] \text{Except when a term equals } \dfrac b3,\text{ it is not monotonic;}\\ \text{its terms alternate about } \dfrac b3.\\[2mm] \text{A value occurs twice iff } \dfrac b3 \text{ occurs,}\\ \text{and then it occurs infinitely many times.} \end{array}}$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The geometric formula is the foundation of the entire argument. Using coordinates,

$$M=\left(\frac{p+v}{2},\frac h2\right),\qquad N=\left(\frac{u+p}{2},\frac h2\right),$$

so

$$MN=\frac{|u-v|}{2}.$$

A careless computation that omits the absolute value would produce negative lengths when $u<v$ and would invalidate the recurrence.

The convergence proof depends on showing that the case $x_n\ge b$ disappears permanently. If $x_n\ge b$, then

$$x_{n+1}=\frac{x_n-b}{2}<b.$$

Hence every later term is governed by the linear relation

$$x_{n+1}=\frac{b-x_n}{2}.$$

Without proving this reduction, the contraction argument around $b/3$ would not be justified.

For repeated values, one must exclude the possibility of a nontrivial cycle. When $x_n<b$,

$$\left|x_{n+1}-\frac b3\right| = \frac12\left|x_n-\frac b3\right|.$$

The distance to $b/3$ decreases strictly unless it is already $0$. Hence no periodic orbit of length greater than $1$ can exist. This is the exact point where an informal argument could overlook a two-cycle.

## Alternative Approaches

Instead of coordinates, one may prove the lemma geometrically. Let the diagonals intersect at $P$. In a trapezoid, the triangles cut off by the diagonals are similar, which gives a fixed ratio on each diagonal. Expressing the positions of the midpoints along the diagonals then yields the length of the segment joining them as half the difference of the bases.

After obtaining

$$x_{n+1}=\frac{|x_n-b|}{2},$$

another approach is to introduce

$$y_n=3x_n-b.$$

Once $x_n<b$, the recurrence becomes

$$y_{n+1}=-\frac12\,y_n.$$

The entire behavior follows immediately: alternating signs give oscillation about $b/3$, the factor $1/2$ gives convergence, and the only fixed point is $y=0$, corresponding to $x=b/3$. The coordinate derivation of the geometric lemma is preferable because it reaches the recurrence with the least amount of auxiliary machinery.
