## Problem

In a trapezoid $ABCD$ with bases $AB=a$ and $CD=b$, a segment $A_1B_1$ is drawn connecting the midpoints of the diagonals. In the resulting trapezoid $A_1B_1CD$, a segment $A_2B_2$ is again drawn connecting the midpoints of the diagonals, and so on (Fig. 1). Can any number occur twice in the sequence of lengths of the segments $AB$, $A_1B_1$, $A_2B_2$, ...? Is this sequence monotonic (increasing or decreasing)? Does it converge to some limit?

## Exploration

Let the lengths of the successive segments be $x_0=a$, $x_1$, $x_2$, and so on. The construction is repeated in a trapezoid whose bases are always the previous segment and the fixed base $CD$ of length $b$. The central geometric fact needed is the length of the segment joining the midpoints of the diagonals of a trapezoid.

In a trapezoid with bases of lengths $p$ and $q$, that midpoint segment is parallel to the bases and has length $\frac{|p-q|}{2}$. Applying this to the first trapezoid gives $x_1=\frac{|a-b|}{2}$, and inductively

$$x_{n+1}=\frac{|x_n-b|}{2}.$$

This reduces the geometric problem to a one-dimensional dynamical system with a piecewise linear map. Fixed points satisfy $x=\frac{|x-b|}{2}$, giving candidates $x=b$ and $x=\frac{b}{3}$. The behavior should be determined by whether the iteration can reach $3b$, since that would map directly to $b$.

A potential subtlety is whether oscillations can create repeated values without settling, or whether monotonicity fails due to switching of the absolute value branch.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem.

A trapezoid is given, and a segment is repeatedly constructed by joining midpoints of diagonals. Each step produces a new trapezoid, hence a new segment. The task is to analyze whether values in the resulting sequence of lengths can repeat, whether the sequence is monotone, and whether it converges.

The geometric process reduces to a nonlinear recurrence, and the main difficulty is to control its long-term behavior and possible cycles.

The final structure will show that the sequence is governed by the iteration $x_{n+1}=\frac{|x_n-b|}{2}$, that nontrivial repetition does not occur, that the sequence is not monotone in general, and that it converges to $\frac{b}{3}$ except in a degenerate case where it becomes constant at $b$.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma identifies the length of the midpoint segment in a trapezoid with given bases $p$ and $q$, yielding $\frac{|p-q|}{2}$.

The second lemma derives the recurrence $x_{n+1}=\frac{|x_n-b|}{2}$.

The third lemma determines all fixed points of the recurrence, giving exactly $x=b$ and $x=\frac{b}{3}$.

The fourth lemma shows that the only possible repetition of a value in the sequence occurs when the sequence becomes eventually constant, which can happen only at fixed points.

The fifth lemma analyzes convergence of the iteration and identifies $\frac{b}{3}$ as the global attractor except for a single special initial condition leading to $b$.

The most delicate step is excluding nontrivial cycles, since the absolute value introduces a branch switch that could in principle create periodic behavior.

## Solution

Let $ABCD$ be a trapezoid with $AB \parallel CD$, $|AB|=a$, $|CD|=b$. Let $A_0B_0=AB$ and define inductively $A_{n+1}B_{n+1}$ as the segment joining the midpoints of the diagonals of trapezoid $A_nB_nCD$, with length $x_n=|A_nB_n|$.

Consider a trapezoid with bases of lengths $p$ and $q$. Let $M$ and $N$ be the midpoints of the diagonals. In vector form, placing the trapezoid so that the bases are horizontal, one computes that $M$ and $N$ both have coordinates obtained by averaging endpoints of opposite vertices. The horizontal displacement between $M$ and $N$ equals half the difference of the projections of the diagonals onto the base direction, which depends only on the base lengths. This yields that $MN$ is parallel to the bases and has length $\frac{|p-q|}{2}$.

Applying this to $A_nB_nCD$ gives

$$x_{n+1}=\frac{|x_n-b|}{2}, \qquad x_0=a.$$

A number $x$ is a fixed point if and only if

$$x=\frac{|x-b|}{2}.$$

If $x\ge b$, then $|x-b|=x-b$ and $2x=x-b$, hence $x=b$. If $x<b$, then $|x-b|=b-x$ and $2x=b-x$, hence $3x=b$, so $x=\frac{b}{3}$. Both values satisfy their respective inequalities, so the set of fixed points is exactly $\left{b,\frac{b}{3}\right}$.

Suppose that some value appears twice in the sequence, so $x_i=x_j$ for some $i<j$. Then the iteration from $x_i$ reproduces the same future trajectory as from $x_j$, so the sequence becomes periodic from index $i$. Hence a repetition implies eventual periodicity.

We show that no periodic orbit of period greater than $1$ exists. If a cycle existed, it would lie entirely within one of the two regions $x\ge b$ or $x<b$ or switch between them. In the region $x\ge b$, the map is $x\mapsto \frac{x-b}{2}$, which is strictly decreasing and maps $[b,\infty)$ into itself only at the single point $b$, since otherwise it strictly decreases magnitude. Thus no nontrivial cycle exists there. In the region $x<b$, the map is $x\mapsto \frac{b-x}{2}$, which is also strictly contracting and linear, hence any cycle would force equality of all iterates and therefore a fixed point. If switching between regions occurs, applying the map twice yields a linear contraction with factor $\frac{1}{4}$, so any two-step cycle would force equality of all values as well. Hence all eventual periodic sequences are constant, and repetition occurs only after reaching a fixed point.

Now we determine convergence. If at some step $x_n=3b$, then $x_{n+1}=b$, and then $x_{n+k}=b$ for all $k\ge 1$. This occurs exactly when $x_{n-1}=3b$, which is possible for $x_0=7b$ since then $x_1=\frac{|7b-b|}{2}=3b$. Thus $a=7b$ produces eventual constant value $b$.

Assume now that the sequence never hits $b$. Then it never hits $3b$ either. Consider the map $f(x)=\frac{|x-b|}{2}$. For $x>b$, we have $f(x)=\frac{x-b}{2}<x$, so the sequence decreases while remaining nonnegative. For $x<b$, we have $f(x)=\frac{b-x}{2}$, and applying the map twice yields

$$f(f(x))=\frac{\left|\frac{b-x}{2}-b\right|}{2}=\frac{x+b}{4}.$$

This expression lies between $x$ and $\frac{b}{3}$ and shows contraction toward $\frac{b}{3}$. Hence all trajectories that never hit $b$ converge to $\frac{b}{3}$.

Thus the sequence either becomes constant at $b$ (only when $a=7b$), or converges to $\frac{b}{3}$ in all other cases.

The sequence is not monotone in general, since transitions between the regions $x<b$ and $x>b$ may produce increases and decreases, as seen when $a>b$ large gives $x_1<x_0$ but later $x_n$ may increase after crossing below $b$. Hence no global monotonicity holds.

## Verification of Key Steps

The critical structural point is exclusion of nontrivial cycles. If a cycle of length two existed, then for some $x$ we would have $x=\frac{|,\frac{|x-b|}{2}-b,|}{2}$. In both branches of the absolute value, expanding leads to linear equations whose only consistent solutions reduce to fixed points $b$ and $\frac{b}{3}$. Any attempt to maintain alternation between $x>b$ and $x<b$ forces a strict contraction factor $\frac{1}{4}$ over two steps, contradicting periodicity unless equality holds at every step.

The second delicate point is the exceptional case $a=7b$. Direct computation yields $x_1=3b$, then $x_2=b$, and the recurrence remains constant. No other initial value produces $3b$ at any stage because solving $|x-b|/2=3b$ gives $x=7b$ uniquely, and backward iteration is single-valued.

The third point concerns convergence to $\frac{b}{3}$. In the region $x<b$, iterating twice gives $f(f(x))=\frac{x+b}{4}$, and solving $x=\frac{x+b}{4}$ yields $x=\frac{b}{3}$. Any deviation from this value is strictly reduced under two iterations, excluding alternative limits.

## Alternative Approaches

One alternative is to treat the map $x\mapsto \frac{|x-b|}{2}$ as a dynamical system and study its graph, viewing intersections with the diagonal to identify fixed points and using invariant intervals to show global attraction. Another approach is to perform a full case analysis by encoding the sign sequence of $x_n-b$, reducing the problem to symbolic dynamics on two symbols. Both approaches ultimately reduce to the same contraction mechanism, while the present direct algebraic iteration avoids additional combinatorial structure.
