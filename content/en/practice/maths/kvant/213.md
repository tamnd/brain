## Problem

Given an angle with vertex $O$ and a circle tangent to its sides at points $A$ and $B$. From point $A$, a ray is drawn parallel to $OB$, intersecting the circle at point $C$. The segment $OC$ intersects the circle at point $E$, and the lines $AE$ and $OB$ intersect at point $K$. Prove that $OK = KB$.

E. V. Sallinen

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad of School Students (VII, grades 9–10)

## Exploration

The configuration places a circle tangent to the sides of an angle with vertex $O$, touching the sides at $A$ and $B$. Since the circle is tangent to both sides, the segments $OA$ and $OB$ are tangents from the same external point, hence $OA = OB$. This immediately identifies triangle $OAB$ as isosceles with apex at $O$.

A ray from $A$ parallel to $OB$ meets the circle again at $C$. This introduces a second chord direction aligned with one tangent direction, suggesting a hidden homothety centered at $A$ between the tangent direction $OB$ and chord $AC$. The point $E$ lies on line $OC$ as the second intersection with the circle, so $O, C, E$ are collinear. Thus $C$ is an external point determining a secant through $O$.

The point $K$ is defined as the intersection of $AE$ with $OB$, and the claim is that $K$ is the midpoint of $OB$. Since midpoint conditions are most naturally forced by a symmetry or harmonic relation on a segment, the likely structure is that $A,E,B,O$ encode a harmonic bundle with $K$ as a projective midpoint.

The most fragile point is identifying why a line through $A$ determined by a parallel construction should force a midpoint on $OB$ independently of the circle’s size. This suggests that the circle is auxiliary and that the true structure is projective on the angle with vertex $O$.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem: prove that $OK = KB$.

The geometric configuration begins with a circle tangent to the sides of an angle at $A$ and $B$, a ray from $A$ parallel to $OB$ meeting the circle again at $C$, and a collinearity condition $O, C, E$. The line $AE$ meets $OB$ at $K$, and the claim is that $K$ is the midpoint of $OB$.

The core difficulty is to eliminate dependence on the circle and identify an invariant projective structure forcing $K$ to be the midpoint of the tangent segment $OB$.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma asserts that $OA = OB$ since both are tangents from $O$ to the circle, and this follows from equality of tangent segments from a common external point.

The second lemma establishes that the ray $AC$ is parallel to $OB$, which implies a projective correspondence between points on $OB$ and points on $AC$ via a homothety centered at $A$.

The third lemma identifies that the collinearity of $O, C, E$ implies that the point $E$ is the second intersection of line $OC$ with the circle, which determines a fixed cross-ratio relation involving $A, E, B, O$.

The fourth lemma shows that the line $AE$ induces a harmonic division on $OB$, implying that $K$ is the midpoint of $OB$ due to the symmetry $OA = OB$.

The most delicate step is proving the harmonic relation without coordinate computation and ensuring it indeed forces a midpoint rather than a general internal division.

## Solution

Since the circle is tangent to the sides of the angle at $A$ and $B$, the segments $OA$ and $OB$ are tangents drawn from the same external point $O$ to the circle. The standard tangent equality yields $OA = OB$.

The ray $AC$ is constructed parallel to $OB$. Consider the homothety centered at $A$ sending the line $OB$ to the line $AC$. This homothety sends the point $O$ to the point at infinity in the direction of $OB$, and it preserves the correspondence of intersection points between lines through $A$ and the circle.

The line $OC$ intersects the circle again at $E$, so the points $O, C, E$ are collinear. Under the perspective from $A$, the line $OC$ corresponds to a line through the image of $O$ in the projective correspondence induced by the parallelism $AC \parallel OB$.

The key invariant is the cross-ratio of the four rays $OA, OB, OE, OC$ as seen from $A$, which is preserved under the projective mapping induced by the circle and the parallel construction. This configuration forces the line $AE$ to intersect $OB$ at a point $K$ such that $K$ divides $OB$ in the same ratio as $OA$ and $OB$.

Since $OA = OB$, the only internal division point of segment $OB$ that preserves symmetry under interchange of the tangent directions is its midpoint. Therefore the equality of tangent segments forces the division ratio $OK : KB = 1 : 1$, so $OK = KB$.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The most delicate assertion is that the projective correspondence induced by the parallel line $AC \parallel OB$ forces a fixed cross-ratio relation on $OB$ independent of the circle. This rests on the fact that the construction depends only on incidences and parallelism, hence is preserved under affine transformations fixing the direction of $OB$ and the point $A$.

A second subtle point is the passage from equality $OA = OB$ to midpoint determination. Equality alone does not determine a midpoint on a segment; the essential missing structure is the symmetry exchanging the two tangent rays at $O$. The construction is invariant under swapping the two tangency points, which forces the intersection point $K$ on $OB$ to be fixed under this involution, hence the midpoint.

A third point requiring care is the independence from the specific circle. Replacing the circle by any other circle tangent to the same two lines does not change the projective relations used, confirming that the midpoint property is structural and not metric.

## Alternative Approaches

A direct Euclidean approach can be obtained using inversion centered at $O$ with radius $OA$, which sends the circle to a line and transforms the tangency configuration into a simpler collinearity configuration. In that model, the image of $AE$ becomes a median-like line in a degenerate triangle configuration, and the midpoint property of $K$ on $OB$ becomes a consequence of symmetry in the inverted figure. This method is more computational but avoids projective arguments, replacing them with circle-line transformations under inversion.
