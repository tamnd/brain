## Problem

Consider all positive integers whose decimal representation contains only the digits 1 and 0. Partition these numbers into two groups in such a way that the sum of any two distinct numbers from the same group contains at least two 1s in its decimal representation.

E. B. Dynkin, S. A. Molchanov, A. L. Rosenthal, A. K. Tolpygo

Mathematical Problems. 3rd ed. – Moscow: Nauka, 1971.

## Exploration

The numbers under consideration are exactly those positive integers whose decimal expansion consists only of the digits $0$ and $1$, with the leading digit necessarily equal to $1$.

The condition concerns sums of two distinct numbers from the same group. The sum must contain at least two digits equal to $1$.

To understand which sums fail, begin with examples. We have

$1+10=11,$

which is acceptable. Also

$1+100=101,$

acceptable. Likewise

$10+100=110.$

These all contain at least two $1$'s.

A bad sum would have exactly one digit $1$. Such a number is a power of $10$, namely

$10^k=100\ldots0.$

Hence the only forbidden situation is that two distinct numbers from the same group sum to a power of $10$.

The problem becomes: color all $0$-$1$ numbers with two colors so that whenever

$a+b=10^k,$

the numbers $a$ and $b$ receive different colors.

It is useful to understand all pairs satisfying $a+b=10^k$. Write

$10^k=111\ldots1+888\ldots9,$

with $k$ digits. If $a$ is a $0$-$1$ number below $10^k$, then subtracting it from $10^k$ produces another $0$-$1$ number: every digit $1$ of $a$ becomes $0$, every digit $0$ becomes $1$. For example,

$100101+899=101000.$

Thus the complement operation inside $k$ digits pairs $0$-$1$ numbers whose sum is $10^k$.

Represent a number by the set of positions of its digit $1$'s. Then taking the complement inside ${0,1,\ldots,k-1}$ corresponds exactly to forming the partner whose sum with the original number is $10^k$.

The graph whose vertices are all finite subsets of nonnegative integers and whose edges join a set to its complement inside some finite initial segment looks bipartite. The parity of the number of $1$'s changes by

$|S|\mapsto k-|S|,$

so

$|S|+(k-|S|)=k.$

Across every edge the parity changes iff $k$ is odd. For even $k$ this observation alone is insufficient.

A more robust invariant is the largest position occupied by a $1$. If $S$ corresponds to a number having highest $1$ at position $m$, then in the complement inside ${0,\dots,k-1}$ the highest occupied position is strictly less than $m$ when $k=m+1$, but can become $k-1$ when $k>m+1$. A better encoding is needed.

Let $s(n)$ be the number of $1$ digits in $n$. If $n'$ is the complement of $n$ inside $k$ digits, then

$s(n')=k-s(n).$

Hence

$s(n)+s(n')=k.$

Modulo $2$,

$s(n')\equiv s(n)+k \pmod 2.$

Across an edge corresponding to $10^k$, parity changes exactly when $k$ is odd, so parity of digit count alone does not color all edges properly.

Trying small chains suggests another invariant: parity of

$s(n)+\text{(length of }n).$

If the length is $m+1$, and the complement is taken inside $k$ digits, then the partner has length $k$ and digit count $k-s(n)$. The parity becomes

$(k-s(n))+k\equiv s(n)\pmod2,$

while the original parity is

$s(n)+(m+1).$

These are not automatically opposite.

A different viewpoint helps. Let $S$ be the set of positions of $1$'s. Define

$\chi(S)=|S|\pmod2.$

An edge joins $S$ with $[0,k-1]\setminus S$. Their colors differ by

$k\pmod2.$

So odd $k$ edges are handled, even $k$ edges are not.

For even $k$, the complement preserves parity. We need another bit that flips exactly when $k$ is even. The parity of the sum of positions works:

$\sigma(S)=\sum_{i\in S} i \pmod2.$
