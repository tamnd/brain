## Problem

Consider all natural numbers whose decimal representation consists only of the digits 1 and 0. Partition these numbers into two groups so that the sum of any two distinct numbers from the same group contains at least two 1s in its decimal representation.

E. B. Dynkin, S. A. Molchanov, A. L. Rosenthal, A. K. Tolpygo

Mathematical Problems. 3rd ed. – Moscow: Nauka, 1971.

## Exploration

The numbers under consideration are precisely the positive integers whose decimal expansion contains only the digits $0$ and $1$. Let us call such numbers binary-decimal numbers.

The condition concerns the sum of two distinct numbers from the same group. We need that this sum contain at least two digits equal to $1$.

The first task is to understand when the sum of two distinct binary-decimal numbers can fail the condition. The sum certainly cannot consist entirely of zeros. Thus the only dangerous possibility is that the sum has exactly one digit equal to $1$.

A positive integer with exactly one digit $1$ is a power of $10$, say $10^k$. Can two distinct binary-decimal numbers add to $10^k$? Trying small examples,

$$1+9=10,$$

but $9$ is not allowed.

For binary-decimal numbers,

$$1+10=11,\qquad 1+100=101,\qquad 10+100=110.$$

Each of these sums already has at least two $1$s.

Suppose $a+b=10^k$. Since $10^k$ has digit $0$ in every position below $k$, every lower decimal position must contribute either $0$ or $10$. Because the digits of $a$ and $b$ are only $0$ and $1$, a carry into a position can arise only from a pair $1+1$. Working from the units digit upward, if any carry ever occurs, the next position receives at least $1$, making it difficult to end with a single isolated $1$.

Trying to solve $a+b=1000$ directly, the units digit forces $0+0$, the tens digit forces $0+0$, the hundreds digit forces $0+0$, and then the thousands digit would have to be $1+0$ or $0+1$, giving one summand equal to $1000$ and the other equal to $0$. Since $0$ is not natural, no solution exists. The same argument works for every $10^k$.

Hence the only sums violating the requirement are impossible for distinct natural binary-decimal numbers. This suggests that the condition is automatically satisfied for every pair of distinct such numbers.

If that is true, any partition works. The strongest possibility is to place all numbers into a single group and leave the second group empty.

The step most likely to hide an error is proving rigorously that the sum of two distinct binary-decimal numbers cannot have exactly one digit $1$.

## Problem Understanding

We consider the set of all natural numbers whose decimal representation uses only the digits $0$ and $1$. We must divide this set into two groups so that whenever two distinct numbers belong to the same group, their sum has at least two digits equal to $1$ in its decimal representation.

This is a Type D problem. We must exhibit a partition and verify that it satisfies the required property.

The core difficulty is determining whether there exist pairs of distinct binary-decimal numbers whose sum contains only one digit $1$. If no such pair exists, then no separation is needed at all.

The answer is to place every number into one group and leave the other group empty. This should work because the sum of two distinct binary-decimal numbers can never be a power of $10$, and a positive decimal number with fewer than two digits $1$ must be exactly a power of $10$.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma states that a positive integer whose decimal representation contains fewer than two digits equal to $1$ and whose sum with another positive integer is positive must be a power of $10$; this is because a positive number containing exactly one digit $1$ and no other nonzero digits has the form $10^k$.

The second lemma states that no two distinct natural binary-decimal numbers have sum $10^k$ for any $k\ge0$; examining the decimal digits from right to left forces one summand to be $10^k$ and the other to be $0$.

From these lemmas it follows that the sum of any two distinct natural binary-decimal numbers contains at least two digits equal to $1$.

The hardest direction is the proof of the second lemma, since one must exclude all possible carry patterns rigorously.

## Solution

Let $S$ be the set of all natural numbers whose decimal representation consists only of the digits $0$ and $1$.

We claim that every element of $S$ may be placed into a single group. The second group may be empty. It remains to prove that for any two distinct numbers $a,b\in S$, the decimal representation of $a+b$ contains at least two digits equal to $1$.

Assume the contrary. Then for some distinct $a,b\in S$, the number $a+b$ contains fewer than two digits equal to $1$.

Since $a$ and $b$ are positive, $a+b>0$. A positive decimal number cannot contain zero digits equal to $1$ and still have all other digits equal to $0$. Hence $a+b$ contains exactly one digit equal to $1$.

Therefore

$$a+b=10^k$$

for some integer $k\ge0$.

We show that this is impossible.

Write

$$a=\sum_{i=0}^{m} a_i10^i,\qquad b=\sum_{i=0}^{m} b_i10^i,$$

where each $a_i,b_i\in{0,1}$.

Since $a+b=10^k$, every digit below the $k$-th position is $0$.

Consider the units digit. The sum digit is $0$, and there is no incoming carry. Because $a_0,b_0\in{0,1}$, the equality

$$a_0+b_0=0$$

follows. Hence

$$a_0=b_0=0.$$

No carry is produced.

Now proceed inductively through positions $1,2,\dots,k-1$. At position $i$, there is no incoming carry. The digit of $10^k$ in that position is $0$. Thus

$$a_i+b_i=0,$$

so

$$a_i=b_i=0.$$

Again no carry is produced.

Consequently every digit of both $a$ and $b$ below position $k$ is $0$, and no carry reaches position $k$.

At position $k$, the digit of $10^k$ is $1$. Since there is no incoming carry,

$$a_k+b_k=1.$$

Because $a_k,b_k\in{0,1}$, one of them equals $1$ and the other equals $0$.

For every position above $k$, the digit of $10^k$ is $0$. There is no carry from position $k$, so the same argument gives

$$a_i=b_i=0 \qquad (i>k).$$

Thus one of the numbers $a,b$ equals $10^k$ and the other equals $0$.

This contradicts the fact that both $a$ and $b$ are natural numbers.

Hence no two distinct elements of $S$ can have sum equal to $10^k$. Therefore the sum of any two distinct elements of $S$ cannot contain fewer than two digits equal to $1$.

So placing all numbers of $S$ into one group satisfies the required condition. The second group may be empty.

The required partition is

$$\boxed{\text{Group 1}=S,\qquad \text{Group 2}=\varnothing.}$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate point is the passage from “fewer than two digits equal to $1$” to “the number is $10^k$”. Since $a+b>0$, the sum cannot have zero digits equal to $1$ and all remaining digits equal to $0$. Thus exactly one digit is equal to $1$, and every other digit is $0$, which is precisely the decimal form of $10^k$.

The second delicate point is the exclusion of carries. At the units position, obtaining digit $0$ in the sum forces $a_0=b_0=0$. Hence no carry enters the tens position. Repeating the same argument at each subsequent position below $k$ shows that every such digit in both summands is $0$, and still no carry appears. This induction eliminates every possible carry chain.

A careless argument might say merely that “a carry would create extra nonzero digits”. That statement is not sufficient. The proof must show position by position that every lower digit is forced to be $0$, which prevents carries altogether.

## Alternative Approaches

Instead of working digit by digit, one may argue modulo powers of $10$. If $a+b=10^k$, then

$$a+b\equiv0\pmod{10}.$$

Since the last digits of $a$ and $b$ are each $0$ or $1$, divisibility by $10$ forces both last digits to be $0$. Dividing by $10$ and repeating the argument shows that both numbers are divisible by $10^k$. Hence $a=10^kx$ and $b=10^ky$ with $x,y\in{0,1}$. From $x+y=1$ one obtains ${a,b}={10^k,0}$, a contradiction.

The digit-by-digit proof is preferable because it mirrors the decimal addition process directly and makes the impossibility of carries completely transparent.
