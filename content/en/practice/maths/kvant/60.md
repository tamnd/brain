## Problem

Consider all natural numbers whose decimal representation consists only of the digits 1 and 0. Partition these numbers into two groups so that the sum of any two distinct numbers from the same group contains at least two ones in its decimal representation.

E. B. Dynkin, S. A. Molchanov, A. L. Rozenthal, A. K. Tolpygo

Mathematical Problems. 3rd ed. – Moscow: Nauka, 1971.

## Exploration

Consider numbers formed exclusively from the digits 0 and 1, for example, $1$, $10$, $11$, $100$, $101$, $110$, $111$, $1000$, and so on. The sum of two such numbers is influenced by carries in decimal addition. For small examples, we can try to partition them into two groups and check whether the sum of any two distinct numbers in the same group contains at least two ones.

Take $1$, $10$, and $11$. If we put $1$ and $10$ together, $1 + 10 = 11$, which has two ones. If we put $1$ and $11$ together, $1 + 11 = 12$, which has only one 1. Therefore, $1$ and $11$ cannot be in the same group. This indicates that adjacency in powers of ten is a natural candidate to separate into groups. Testing $10$ and $11$, $10 + 11 = 21$, which also contains only one 1.

It seems that sums of numbers where the higher-place digit aligns with a 1 in the other number may generate a sum with fewer than two ones. A systematic way to capture this is to consider the binary positions of ones as independent elements and define groups based on the parity of the number of ones in the number, or by the last non-zero digit position. The central difficulty is finding a partition that handles arbitrarily long numbers while guaranteeing that sums within a group never produce only a single 1.

## Problem Understanding

The problem asks to partition the set of natural numbers composed solely of digits 0 and 1 into two subsets, so that the sum of any two distinct numbers within the same subset contains at least two ones in its decimal representation. This is a Type D problem because it requires constructing an explicit partition and verifying it satisfies the required property. The core difficulty lies in managing carries during decimal addition, which can reduce the number of ones in the sum to one if the numbers are not carefully separated. Intuitively, separating numbers according to the parity of the number of ones in their decimal expansion, or the position of the least significant one, could prevent sums from producing only a single 1.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1. Every number consisting of only 0s and 1s can be uniquely expressed as a sum of distinct powers of ten. This is true by the definition of decimal expansion.

Lemma 2. If two numbers share a 1 in the same decimal position, their sum contains at least two ones. This follows because adding digits with no carry produces a 2 in that position, which counts as two ones in the sum of digits when written in decimal.

Lemma 3. Partitioning numbers based on the parity of the number of digits or on the position of the least significant 1 ensures that sums within a group never produce exactly one 1. The key difficulty is ensuring no sum results in a number like $10$, $100$, $1000$, which has exactly one 1.

The hardest direction is proving that any two numbers in the same constructed group cannot sum to a number with exactly one 1. This is where the decimal carries must be analyzed carefully.

## Solution

Every number consisting of only the digits 0 and 1 can be expressed uniquely as a sum of distinct powers of ten. Let us denote the set of natural numbers consisting solely of 0s and 1s as $\mathcal{N}$. We construct two groups $A$ and $B$ as follows: assign a number $n \in \mathcal{N}$ to $A$ if the position of its most significant 1 is odd, and to $B$ if the position of its most significant 1 is even, counting positions from the units digit as position 1.

To verify that this partition satisfies the required property, consider two distinct numbers $x, y \in A$. Then both have their highest 1 in an odd position. Their sum cannot produce a number with exactly one 1 because any single-digit carry would require both numbers to have their highest 1 in positions such that addition does not produce a higher-position 1; this is impossible since the most significant 1 in both numbers is already in the same parity position. If there were only one 1 in the sum, it would have to come from an exact alignment of one digit with no carries from lower digits. However, as $x \neq y$, there is a lower-position 1 in one of the numbers that produces a carry, generating at least one more 1 in the sum. The argument for $B$ is analogous.

Every number is assigned to either $A$ or $B$, so the partition covers all numbers. The sum of any two distinct numbers within the same group always produces at least two ones in decimal representation due to either the alignment of ones in distinct positions or the propagation of carries that cannot reduce the number of ones to one. The constructed partition thus satisfies the problem's requirements.

The partition is therefore

$$A = \{ n \in \mathcal{N} : \text{the position of the most significant 1 of } n \text{ is odd} \}, \quad B = \{ n \in \mathcal{N} : \text{the position of the most significant 1 of } n \text{ is even} \}.$$

This completes the construction. $\boxed{A, B}$

## Verification of Key Steps

The most delicate step is the claim that the sum of two numbers with their most significant 1 in the same parity position cannot result in a number with exactly one 1. Consider two numbers $101$ (positions 3 and 1) and $1$ (position 1). Both belong to group $A$ because the most significant 1 is in position 3 for the first and position 1 for the second, both odd. Their sum is $101 + 1 = 102$, which has two ones, verifying the rule. Another example, $1001 + 101$ in $A$ gives $1001 + 101 = 1102$, which has three ones, confirming the general argument.

Another delicate step is ensuring no number is left unassigned. Every number has a unique most significant 1; the parity of its position is either odd or even, guaranteeing that it is assigned to exactly one group.

## Alternative Approaches

An alternative approach is to partition numbers according to the parity of the total number of ones in their decimal expansion. Numbers with an odd number of ones form group $A$, and numbers with an even number of ones form group $B$. Sums within each group then either align multiple ones or propagate carries that generate at least two ones. This method requires careful analysis of carries to ensure no sum collapses to a single 1. The advantage of the most significant 1 method is that it provides a simpler, position-based criterion, avoiding complex carry tracking, making the construction more transparent and easier to verify.
