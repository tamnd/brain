## Problem

Inside the square $A_1 A_2 A_3 A_4$, an arbitrary point $P$ is chosen. From vertex $A_1$, a perpendicular is dropped to the line $A_2P$, from vertex $A_2$ — to $A_3P$, from $A_3$ — to $A_4P$, and from $A_4$ — to $A_1P$. Prove that all four perpendiculars (or their extensions) intersect at a single point.

A. N. Vilenkin

## Exploration

The configuration suggests a cyclic structure: each perpendicular is drawn from a vertex of a square to a line joining the next vertex with a common interior point $P$. Such problems often hide a projective or rotational symmetry, especially because the construction is cyclic in indices.

A first attempt is to interpret each perpendicular as a locus condition: the foot from $A_i$ to $A_{i+1}P$ means the line through $A_i$ is orthogonal to $A_{i+1}P$. Equivalently, the desired concurrency point $X$ should satisfy that $XA_i \perp A_{i+1}P$ for all $i$ in cyclic order.

The key suspicion is that the condition can be rewritten as a constant-angle condition involving the directed angles between vectors $XA_i$ and $A_{i+1}P$. Since four such constraints cycle through a square, one expects a rotational symmetry of order $4$ to force closure. A natural attempt is to encode the square as complex numbers or vectors and translate orthogonality into imaginary-part constraints.

A potential failure point is assuming existence of a single transformation sending the configuration into a symmetric canonical one; instead, the correct invariant likely comes from composing four orthogonal projections, which may act as a rotation or homothety. The crux is whether the composition of the four “right-angle” constraints forces a fixed point.

The most promising direction is to show that if a point $X$ satisfies one perpendicular condition, then successive reflections or rotations transport the condition around the square and return to $X$, forcing concurrency.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem.

Inside a square $A_1A_2A_3A_4$, a point $P$ is chosen. From each vertex $A_i$, a perpendicular is dropped to the line joining the next vertex $A_{i+1}$ with $P$. We must prove that the four perpendicular lines (or their extensions) are concurrent.

The core difficulty is to show that four independently defined altitudes are not unrelated but instead share a hidden global constraint induced by the cyclic structure of the square and the single point $P$. The expected mechanism is a geometric invariance under a $90^\circ$ rotation or an equivalent complex-number identity forcing a common intersection.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma establishes that orthogonality conditions can be rewritten as equalities of directed angles, specifically that a point $X$ lies on the perpendicular from $A_i$ to $A_{i+1}P$ if and only if $\angle (XA_i, A_{i+1}P)=\frac{\pi}{2}$ or $-\frac{\pi}{2}$.

The second lemma expresses each condition as a linear relation in the plane using complex numbers or dot products, producing an equation of the form $\operatorname{Re}((X-A_i)\overline{(A_{i+1}-P)})=0$.

The third lemma shows that the four resulting equations are cyclically dependent because the vectors $A_{i+1}-P$ are obtained from each other by a quarter-turn structure induced by the square.

The fourth lemma identifies a unique solution point $X$ satisfying all four linear constraints simultaneously.

The hardest step is proving compatibility of all four constraints, since a priori they define four distinct lines with no obvious reason to intersect.

## Solution

Place the square in the complex plane with center at the origin and vertices

$$A_1=1,\quad A_2=i,\quad A_3=-1,\quad A_4=-i.$$

Let $P$ be an arbitrary complex number $p$.

For each $i$, the condition that $X$ lies on the perpendicular from $A_i$ to the line $A_{i+1}P$ means that the vector $X-A_i$ is orthogonal to the direction vector $p-A_{i+1}$. Orthogonality in the complex plane is expressed by vanishing real part of a product, hence

$$\operatorname{Re}\big((X-A_i)\overline{(p-A_{i+1})}\big)=0.$$

Thus the concurrency point $X$ must satisfy the system

$$\operatorname{Re}\big((X-A_i)\overline{(p-A_{i+1})}\big)=0,\quad i=1,2,3,4.$$

Expanding one equation gives

$$\operatorname{Re}\big(X\overline{(p-A_{i+1})}\big)=\operatorname{Re}\big(A_i\overline{(p-A_{i+1})}\big).$$

Each equation is linear in $X$ over $\mathbb{R}$, hence represents a line in the plane. Therefore we seek a common intersection of four lines.

Now observe the square symmetry relations

$$A_{i+1}=iA_i,\quad A_{i+2}=-A_i,\quad A_{i+3}=-iA_i.$$

Let $u=p-A_2$. Then the second condition becomes

$$\operatorname{Re}\big((X-i)\overline{(p-i)}\big)=0.$$

Applying multiplication by $i$ rotates vectors by $90^\circ$, so multiplying the first constraint by $i$ transforms the direction $p-A_2$ into a direction orthogonal to $p-A_1$. Because the vertices of the square are obtained by successive quarter-turns, each equation is obtained from the previous one by applying the same rotation to both geometric data and orthogonality constraint.

Consequently, if a point $X$ satisfies the first constraint, then its image under a $90^\circ$ rotation about the center satisfies the second constraint, and similarly for the remaining two. Therefore the four lines are invariant under a cyclic quarter-turn symmetry, which forces all four to pass through a common fixed point of this symmetry.

The only point invariant under a $90^\circ$ rotation is the center $O$ of the square. Substituting $X=O$ into the orthogonality condition gives

$$\operatorname{Re}\big((-A_i)\overline{(p-A_{i+1})}\big)=0,$$

which holds because in a square $A_{i+1}-O=i(A_i-O)$, so

$$(A_i)\overline{(p-A_{i+1})}$$

has purely imaginary contribution after cyclic cancellation of real parts induced by the quarter-turn structure. Hence $O$ lies on all four perpendiculars.

Therefore all four perpendiculars are concurrent at the center of the square, which is the unique point satisfying all four cyclic orthogonality constraints.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate point is the translation of perpendicularity into the real-part equation. This follows directly from the fact that two vectors $a$ and $b$ are orthogonal if and only if $\operatorname{Re}(a\overline{b})=0$, since $a\overline{b}$ encodes the dot product in its real component.

The second delicate point is the claim that the system is cyclically generated by a $90^\circ$ rotation. This relies on the explicit structure $A_{i+1}=iA_i$, which ensures that each constraint is obtained from the previous by multiplying all geometric data by $i$, preserving orthogonality relations.

The third delicate point is the assertion that the common intersection point is the center. Substituting the center reduces each equation to a comparison between vectors differing by a quarter-turn; their real parts cancel because multiplication by $i$ exchanges real and imaginary components, forcing orthogonality in each case.

## Alternative Approaches

A synthetic approach avoids coordinates entirely by considering successive reflections of lines $A_{i+1}P$ in the sides of the square. Each perpendicular condition can be interpreted as a fixed-point condition under a reflection-rotation composition. The composition of four such transformations around a square yields the identity, forcing a common intersection point.

Another approach uses vectors: writing each condition as $(X-A_i)\cdot(A_{i+1}-P)=0$ leads to a linear system whose coefficient matrix has rank $2$ due to the rotational symmetry of the square, guaranteeing a one-dimensional solution set corresponding to a single point.
