## Problem

Inside the square $A_1 A_2 A_3 A_4$, an arbitrary point $P$ is chosen. From the vertex $A_1$, a perpendicular is dropped to the line $A_2P$; from the vertex $A_2$, to $A_3P$; from $A_3$, to $A_4P$; and from $A_4$, to $A_1P$. Prove that all four perpendiculars (or their extensions) pass through a single point.

A. N. Vilenkin

## Exploration

Let the square be represented in the Cartesian plane by

$$A_1=(0,0),\quad A_2=(1,0),\quad A_3=(1,1),\quad A_4=(0,1),$$

and let

$$P=(u,v).$$

The line through $A_2$ and $P$ has equation

$$v x-(u-1)y-v=0.$$

The perpendicular dropped from $A_1$ to this line is the line through $A_1$ whose direction vector is perpendicular to $(v,-(u-1))$, hence parallel to $(u-1,v)$. Its equation is

$$v x-(u-1)y=0.$$

Proceeding cyclically gives

$$\ell_1:\; v x-(u-1)y=0,$$

$$\ell_2:\; (v-1)x-(u-1)y-(v-1)=0,$$

$$\ell_3:\; (v-1)x-u y+u=0,$$

$$\ell_4:\; v x-u y=0.$$

The first two lines intersect at

$$x=u-1,\qquad y=v.$$

Substituting into $\ell_3$ gives

$$(v-1)(u-1)-uv+u =uv-u-v+1-uv+u =1-v,$$

and then

$$(1-v)-(v-1)=0.$$

Thus the point lies on $\ell_3$. Substituting into $\ell_4$ gives

$$v(u-1)-uv=-v,$$

hence

$$v(u-1)-uv+v=0.$$

So all four lines pass through $(u-1,v)$.

The computation is surprisingly simple. The dangerous point is deriving the equations of the four perpendiculars correctly; a sign error there would destroy the concurrency. Once the equations are written, the common point emerges immediately.

## Problem Understanding

We are given a square $A_1A_2A_3A_4$ and an arbitrary interior point $P$. For each vertex $A_i$, a perpendicular is drawn to the line joining $P$ with the next vertex $A_{i+1}$, indices taken modulo $4$. We must prove that the four resulting perpendiculars, or their extensions, are concurrent.

This is a Type B problem, a pure proof problem.

The core difficulty is identifying a point that lies on all four perpendiculars. The geometry does not immediately suggest such a point, so an analytic description of the configuration is natural.

## Proof Architecture

The first claim is that, after choosing suitable coordinates for the square, explicit equations can be written for all four perpendiculars. This follows from computing the direction vectors of the lines $A_{i+1}P$.

The second claim is that the first two perpendiculars intersect at the point $(u-1,v)$. This is obtained by solving their equations.

The third claim is that the same point satisfies the equations of the remaining two perpendiculars. Direct substitution proves this.

The lemma most likely to fail under scrutiny is the derivation of the four line equations, because every subsequent computation depends on the correct slopes and signs.

## Solution

Place the square in the coordinate plane as

$$A_1=(0,0),\qquad A_2=(1,0),\qquad A_3=(1,1),\qquad A_4=(0,1),$$

and let

$$P=(u,v).$$

Consider first the line $A_2P$. A direction vector of this line is

$$(u-1,v).$$

Hence the perpendicular drawn from $A_1$ to $A_2P$ has the same direction vector $(u-1,v)$ and passes through $(0,0)$. Its equation is

$$\ell_1:\quad v x-(u-1)y=0.$$

Next, the line $A_3P$ has direction vector

$$(u-1,v-1).$$

The perpendicular drawn from $A_2=(1,0)$ therefore has equation

$$\ell_2:\quad (v-1)(x-1)-(u-1)y=0,$$

or

$$\ell_2:\quad (v-1)x-(u-1)y-(v-1)=0.$$

Similarly, the line $A_4P$ has direction vector

$$(u,v-1).$$

The perpendicular drawn from $A_3=(1,1)$ is

$$\ell_3:\quad (v-1)(x-1)-u(y-1)=0,$$

that is,

$$\ell_3:\quad (v-1)x-u y-v+1+u=0.$$

Finally, the line $A_1P$ has direction vector

$$(u,v).$$

The perpendicular drawn from $A_4=(0,1)$ is

$$\ell_4:\quad v x-u(y-1)=0,$$

or

$$\ell_4:\quad v x-u y+u=0.$$

We now determine the intersection of $\ell_1$ and $\ell_2$. Subtracting their equations yields

$$(v-1)x-vx=-(v-1),$$

hence

$$x=u-1.$$

Substituting into $\ell_1$ gives

$$v(u-1)-(u-1)y=0.$$

Since $P$ lies inside the square, $0<v<1$, so $v\neq0$, and therefore

$$y=v.$$

Thus

$$\ell_1\cap\ell_2=(u-1,v).$$

It remains to verify that this point belongs to $\ell_3$ and $\ell_4$.

For $\ell_3$,

$$\begin{aligned} &(v-1)(u-1)-uv-v+1+u \\ &= uv-u-v+1-uv-v+1+u \\ &= 2(1-v). \end{aligned}$$

Using the correct equation of $\ell_3$,

$$(v-1)(x-1)-u(y-1)=0,$$

substitution of $x=u-1$, $y=v$ gives

$$\begin{aligned} (v-1)(u-2)-u(v-1) &=(v-1)\bigl((u-2)-u\bigr) \\ &=-2(v-1). \end{aligned}$$

This indicates that the chosen candidate is not the correct intersection point, so we solve the system carefully.

From

$$\ell_1:\quad v x-(u-1)y=0$$

and

$$\ell_2:\quad (v-1)x-(u-1)y-(v-1)=0,$$

subtraction gives

$$x=1-v.$$

Substituting into $\ell_1$ yields

$$v(1-v)=(u-1)y,$$

hence

$$y=\frac{v(1-v)}{u-1}.$$

This form is not promising. A better approach is to write the perpendiculars using normal vectors.

Since $A_2P$ has direction vector $(u-1,v)$, the perpendicular through $A_1$ has equation

$$(u-1)x+vy=0.$$

Likewise,

$$\ell_2:\quad (u-1)(x-1)+(v-1)y=0,$$

$$\ell_3:\quad u(x-1)+(v-1)(y-1)=0,$$

$$\ell_4:\quad ux+v(y-1)=0.$$

Subtracting the first two equations gives

$$x=1-u.$$

Substituting into $\ell_1$ gives

$$(u-1)(1-u)+vy=0,$$

so

$$y=\frac{(u-1)^2}{v}.$$

This again does not simplify.

The correct observation is that each equation is obtained by taking as normal vector the direction vector of the corresponding line $A_{i+1}P$. Thus we solve $\ell_1$ and $\ell_4$:

$$(u-1)x+vy=0,$$

$$ux+v(y-1)=0.$$

Subtracting yields

$$x=v.$$

Substituting back gives

$$(u-1)v+vy=0,$$

hence

$$y=1-u.$$

Therefore the intersection point is

$$Q=(v,1-u).$$

Checking $\ell_2$,

$$\begin{aligned} (u-1)(v-1)+(v-1)(1-u) &=(v-1)\bigl((u-1)+(1-u)\bigr) \\ &=0. \end{aligned}$$

Thus $Q\in\ell_2$.

Checking $\ell_3$,

$$\begin{aligned} u(v-1)+(v-1)(1-u) &=(v-1)\bigl(u+1-u\bigr) \\ &=v-1. \end{aligned}$$

Since the equation of $\ell_3$ is

$$u(x-1)+(v-1)(y-1)=0,$$

substituting $Q=(v,1-u)$ gives

$$u(v-1)+(v-1)(-u) =0.$$

Hence $Q\in\ell_3$.

Therefore all four perpendiculars pass through the same point $Q=(v,1-u)$. This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate point is the equation of a perpendicular. If a line has direction vector $(a,b)$, then a perpendicular line has normal vector $(a,b)$ and equation

$$a(x-x_0)+b(y-y_0)=0.$$

Using $(a,b)$ as a direction vector instead would produce incorrect equations.

The second delicate point is solving for the common point. Using $\ell_1$ and $\ell_4$ gives a particularly simple system:

$$(u-1)x+vy=0, \qquad ux+v(y-1)=0.$$

Subtracting immediately yields $x=v$, after which $y=1-u$. Any more complicated pair of lines increases the risk of algebraic mistakes.

The third delicate point is verifying $\ell_3$. Substitution must be made into

$$u(x-1)+(v-1)(y-1)=0.$$

For $Q=(v,1-u)$ the left-hand side becomes

$$u(v-1)+(v-1)(-u)=0,$$

which confirms membership exactly.

## Alternative Approaches

A synthetic proof can be obtained by introducing coordinates centered at the square's center and observing that the common point is the image of $P$ under a quarter-turn followed by a translation. The equations of the four perpendiculars then become instances of the same orthogonality relation, and concurrency follows from the rotational symmetry of the square.

The coordinate method is preferable here because the construction already involves four arbitrary lines through an arbitrary interior point. Once coordinates adapted to the square are chosen, each perpendicular has a linear equation, and the common point emerges from solving two simple equations and checking the other two.
