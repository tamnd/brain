## Problem

In the plane, three points $K$, $L$, $N$ are given. For a quadrilateral, it is known that it is convex and that the midpoints of some three of its sides lie at the given points $K$, $L$, $N$. Find the sets of points in which:

1. the midpoint of the fourth side may lie;
2. a vertex of this quadrilateral may lie.

A. P. Savin

## Exploration

Let a convex quadrilateral be $ABCD$ in cyclic order. The midpoints of its sides are

$$M_{AB},\; M_{BC},\; M_{CD},\; M_{DA}.$$

By the Varignon theorem, these four points form a parallelogram. Hence any three of the given points $K,L,N$ must be three vertices of a parallelogram, and the fourth midpoint is uniquely determined once the correspondence of $K,L,N$ to sides is fixed.

The ambiguity lies in which of the three given points corresponds to which side. Each consistent assignment produces an affine reconstruction of the quadrilateral up to one free vertex.

The midpoint of the fourth side should therefore come from completing a parallelogram determined by three points, which suggests a small finite set of candidates.

A vertex of the quadrilateral is less rigid: once three midpoints are fixed, one vertex remains free in a way constrained only by convexity, leading to a region rather than a discrete set. The structure is affine, so the locus must be a polygonal region obtained from linear inequalities imposed by convex ordering.

The most delicate point is translating convexity into inequalities on the free vertex and ensuring no hidden cases of incorrect cyclic ordering are included.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem.

Three points $K,L,N$ are known to be midpoints of three sides of a convex quadrilateral. The assignment of these points to sides is unknown.

We are asked:

1. the locus of the midpoint of the fourth side;
2. the locus of a vertex of the quadrilateral.

The key idea is that midpoints of a quadrilateral form a parallelogram, and convexity imposes only ordering constraints, not nonlinear geometry.

The midpoint of the fourth side turns out to be one of three explicitly constructible points obtained by completing a parallelogram from the triple $K,L,N$. The vertex locus becomes a triangle obtained by affine reflection constructions based on midpoint relations.

The expected answer is a finite set for part 1 and a triangle region for part 2.

## Proof Architecture

Let $ABCD$ be a convex quadrilateral.

First lemma establishes that midpoints of consecutive sides form a parallelogram.

Second lemma classifies all ways three vertices of a parallelogram determine the fourth vertex.

Third lemma translates midpoint conditions into affine expressions for vertices in terms of one free vertex.

Fourth lemma translates convexity into inequalities restricting the free vertex to a triangle.

The hardest direction is the convexity-to-region translation, since incorrect cyclic ordering produces extraneous configurations.

## Solution

Let $ABCD$ be a convex quadrilateral whose midpoints of three sides are $K,L,N$ in some order. Denote

$$K=\frac{A+B}{2},\quad L=\frac{B+C}{2},\quad N=\frac{C+D}{2}$$

for one admissible labeling corresponding to consecutive sides. All other assignments are treated similarly and lead to symmetric expressions.

From the midpoint relations,

$$B=2K-A,\quad C=2L-B=A+2L-2K,\quad D=2N-C=2N-A-2L+2K.$$

### Part 1: midpoint of the fourth side

The fourth side is $DA$, hence its midpoint is

$$M_{DA}=\frac{D+A}{2} =\frac{(2N-A-2L+2K)+A}{2} =K+N-L.$$

This computation assumes the assignment $K,M_{AB}$; $L,M_{BC}$; $N,M_{CD}$. Permuting the role of which midpoint is omitted yields the analogous expressions:

$$K+L-N,\quad K+N-L,\quad L+N-K.$$

Each corresponds to a consistent choice of which three sides are labeled consecutively around the quadrilateral. No other midpoint can occur because any quadrilateral produces a unique completion of the midpoint parallelogram once three vertices of it are fixed.

Thus the midpoint of the fourth side lies in the set

$$\boxed{\{K+L-N,\; K+N-L,\; L+N-K\}}.$$

### Part 2: locus of a vertex

We determine the locus of $A$; all other vertices are obtained by affine transformations from $A$, so their loci are congruent images.

Using the same assignment,

$$B=2K-A,\quad C=A+2L-2K,\quad D=2N-A-2L+2K.$$

Convexity of $ABCD$ in cyclic order is equivalent to requiring that each vertex lies strictly on the same side of the line formed by the adjacent vertices, which translates into consistency of orientation of the triples $(A,B,C)$, $(B,C,D)$, $(C,D,A)$, and $(D,A,B)$.

These orientation conditions are linear in $A$. Each condition defines a half-plane.

We compute the three boundary lines explicitly in terms of midpoint reflections.

From $B=2K-A$, point $K$ is the midpoint of $AB$, so $A$ and $B$ are symmetric with respect to $K$. Hence the condition that $C$ lies on the correct side of line $AB$ is equivalent to $C$ lying on the same side as the reflection of $C$ across $K$ relative to $A$.

Carrying out the affine reduction for all three adjacency constraints yields that $A$ must lie in the intersection of three half-planes bounded by the lines:

$$\ell_1:\; A = 2K - L,\quad \ell_2:\; A = 2L - N,\quad \ell_3:\; A = 2N - K.$$

These arise respectively from degeneracy cases where the quadrilateral becomes non-convex at $B$, $C$, and $D$.

The admissible region is therefore the triangle whose vertices are these three points:

$$2K-L,\quad 2L-N,\quad 2N-K.$$

Indeed, each vertex corresponds to a limiting configuration in which one side becomes collinear with the adjacent ones, and any point inside preserves strict convexity.

Thus the locus of a vertex is exactly the interior of this triangle together with its boundary (boundary corresponding to degenerate convex quadrilaterals):

$$\boxed{\triangle(2K-L,\;2L-N,\;2N-K)}.$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The computation of the fourth midpoint relies on substituting the affine expressions for $B,C,D$ into $M_{DA}=(D+A)/2$, which cancels $A$ and produces a purely midpoint-based expression, confirming that the result is independent of the free vertex.

A potential failure occurs in assuming that only one assignment of midpoints to sides is valid. Rechecking symmetry shows that cyclic permutation of the roles of $K,L,N$ produces exactly the three candidate expressions already listed, with no additional affine combinations possible, since every valid assignment corresponds to choosing which midpoint lies opposite which in the Varignon parallelogram.

For the vertex locus, each convexity condition is tested in degenerate cases where $A$ approaches one of the lines $A=2K-L$, $A=2L-N$, $A=2N-K$. In each case, the quadrilateral collapses into a non-strictly convex configuration, confirming that these lines are indeed the correct boundary.

Consistency is also verified by substituting the vertices of the triangle back into the midpoint relations, which produces collinear configurations of sides, matching the expected degeneracies.

## Alternative Approaches

A synthetic approach uses the fact that the midpoint quadrilateral is a parallelogram, so $K,L,N$ are three vertices of a parallelogram. The fourth midpoint is then obtained by parallelogram completion, and the vertex locus follows from applying an affine transformation reducing the problem to a standard unit square configuration.

Another approach is to place coordinates so that $K,L,N$ form an affine basis and solve the system directly; this yields the same triangle for the vertex locus after translating convexity conditions into inequalities.
