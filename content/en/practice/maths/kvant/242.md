## Problem

Let $A_iH_i$ be the altitude and $A_iM_i$ be the median drawn from vertex $A_i$ of an acute triangle $A_1A_2A_3$ ($i=1$, 2, 3). Prove that one of the three products $|H_1M_1|\cdot|A_2A_3|$, $|H_2M_2|\cdot|A_3A_1|$, $|H_3M_3|\cdot|A_1A_2|$ is equal to the sum of the other two. Is this statement true for a right triangle and for an obtuse triangle?

S. Salnikov, 10th grade student (Mary)

## Exploration

Place triangle $A_1A_2A_3$ with $A_2A_3=a$, $A_3A_1=b$, $A_1A_2=c$. For each vertex $A_i$, the point $H_i$ is the foot of the altitude to the opposite side and $M_i$ is the midpoint of that side, so $H_iM_i$ is a segment on a fixed side.

A first reduction is to express each $|H_iM_i|$ in terms of the side lengths only. Using projections and the law of cosines suggests that the foot of the altitude divides a side in a way determined by squared side lengths, so differences such as $c^2-b^2$ are likely to appear. This hints that the three quantities become absolute differences of $a^2,b^2,c^2$ up to scaling.

If that reduction is correct, the statement becomes a purely algebraic assertion about three real numbers: among $|a^2-b^2|$, $|b^2-c^2|$, $|c^2-a^2|$, one equals the sum of the other two. This would reduce the geometric problem to ordering real numbers, where a telescoping identity appears after sorting.

The key risk is an incorrect expression for $H_iM_i$, since the midpoint shift must be handled carefully and signs must be tracked consistently.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem.

We must show that among the three quantities $|H_1M_1|\cdot |A_2A_3|$, $|H_2M_2|\cdot |A_3A_1|$, $|H_3M_3|\cdot |A_1A_2|$, one equals the sum of the other two, and then determine whether the same holds for right and obtuse triangles.

The core difficulty is to express $|H_iM_i|$ in a symmetric algebraic form and then recognize a hidden ordering identity.

The final conclusion will show that the statement is not restricted to acute triangles and in fact holds for all nondegenerate triangles.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma expresses $H_iM_i$ as a signed difference of squared side lengths divided by twice the opposite side length, obtained via projection and the law of cosines.

The second lemma converts the three products $|H_iM_i|\cdot |A_jA_k|$ into $\frac12|x-y|$ where $x,y$ are squares of side lengths.

The third lemma proves that for any three real numbers, among their pairwise absolute differences, the largest equals the sum of the other two after ordering.

The final step applies this ordering to $a^2,b^2,c^2$.

The most delicate point is the exact computation of $H_iM_i$, since any sign mistake would destroy the algebraic reduction.

## Solution

Let $A_2A_3=a$, $A_3A_1=b$, $A_1A_2=c$. Consider vertex $A_1$ and side $A_2A_3$.

Let $H_1$ be the foot of the altitude from $A_1$ onto $A_2A_3$. By projection onto the line $A_2A_3$, the segment $A_2H_1$ equals the projection of $A_2A_1$ onto $A_2A_3$, hence

$A_2H_1 = A_1A_2\cos \angle A_2 = c\cos A_2.$

By the law of cosines,

$\cos A_2 = \frac{a^2 + c^2 - b^2}{2ac},$

hence

$A_2H_1 = c\cdot \frac{a^2 + c^2 - b^2}{2ac} = \frac{a^2 + c^2 - b^2}{2a}.$

The midpoint $M_1$ of $A_2A_3$ satisfies $A_2M_1=\frac a2$. Therefore the signed displacement along $A_2A_3$ is

$H_1M_1 = A_2H_1 - A_2M_1 = \frac{a^2 + c^2 - b^2}{2a} - \frac a2 = \frac{c^2 - b^2}{2a}.$

Taking absolute values and multiplying by $a$ gives

$|H_1M_1|\cdot a = \frac12 |c^2 - b^2|.$

Cyclically repeating the same computation yields

$|H_2M_2|\cdot b = \frac12 |a^2 - c^2|,\qquad |H_3M_3|\cdot c = \frac12 |b^2 - a^2|.$

Set $x=a^2$, $y=b^2$, $z=c^2$. The three quantities become

$\frac12|y-z|,\qquad \frac12|z-x|,\qquad \frac12|x-y|.$

Multiplying the desired statement by $2$, it is equivalent to showing that among $|x-y|$, $|y-z|$, $|z-x|$, one equals the sum of the other two.

Assume without loss of generality that $x\le y\le z$. Then

$|x-y|=y-x,\qquad |y-z|=z-y,\qquad |x-z|=z-x.$

Direct computation gives

$|x-z| = z-x = (z-y) + (y-x) = |y-z| + |x-y|.$

Thus the largest of the three absolute differences equals the sum of the other two. Since the labeling of $x,y,z$ corresponds to an ordering of $a^2,b^2,c^2$, exactly one of the three original expressions equals the sum of the other two. This proves the claim for all nondegenerate triangles, including acute, right, and obtuse cases.

Therefore the statement is true in an acute triangle, and it is also true in right and obtuse triangles without restriction. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The central computation is $H_1M_1=\frac{c^2-b^2}{2a}$. This depends on two independent ingredients, the projection formula $A_2H_1=c\cos A_2$ and the law of cosines expression for $\cos A_2$. Both combine linearly and the cancellation of $a^2$ after subtracting $\frac a2$ is exact and unavoidable, leaving only $c^2-b^2$ in the numerator.

The second delicate point is the passage from geometric expressions to absolute differences of $a^2,b^2,c^2$. This step is stable because each product $|H_iM_i|\cdot |A_jA_k|$ removes the denominator corresponding to the opposite side, leaving a symmetric expression independent of angles.

The final potential failure would be assuming a special ordering of $a^2,b^2,c^2$ without justification. The argument instead uses a full case split via sorting, which covers all configurations.

## Alternative Approaches

A vector approach places the triangle in Euclidean space and interprets $H_i$ as orthogonal projections and $M_i$ as affine combinations of vertices, leading to barycentric expressions for $H_iM_i$ in terms of squared side lengths.

Another approach uses directed segments on each side and interprets the identity as a statement about telescoping differences after ordering $a^2,b^2,c^2$, bypassing trigonometry entirely. This method is conceptually shorter but less direct in justifying the geometric projection step, which is more transparent in the trigonometric derivation above.
