## Problem

$a$, $b$, $c$ are the sides of a triangle. Prove that

$$\frac{a}{b+c-a} + \frac{b}{c+a-b} + \frac{c}{a+b-c} \geq 3.$$

S. T. Berkolayko

## Exploration

Since $a,b,c$ are the sides of a triangle, the quantities

$$b+c-a,\qquad c+a-b,\qquad a+b-c$$

are positive.

A natural substitution is

$$x=\frac{b+c-a}{2},\qquad y=\frac{c+a-b}{2},\qquad z=\frac{a+b-c}{2}.$$

Then $x,y,z>0$ and

$$a=y+z,\qquad b=z+x,\qquad c=x+y.$$

The inequality becomes

$$\frac{y+z}{2x}+\frac{z+x}{2y}+\frac{x+y}{2z}\ge 3,$$

or

$$\frac12\left(\frac yx+\frac zx+\frac zy+\frac xy+\frac xz+\frac yz\right)\ge 3.$$

For $x=y=z$, corresponding to an equilateral triangle, the left-hand side equals $3$, so equality is possible.

The transformed inequality resembles the classical inequality

$$\frac{x}{y}+\frac{y}{x}\ge 2.$$

Grouping the six fractions into three reciprocal pairs gives

$$\left(\frac{x}{y}+\frac{y}{x}\right) +\left(\frac{y}{z}+\frac{z}{y}\right) +\left(\frac{z}{x}+\frac{x}{z}\right)\ge 6.$$

After multiplying by $\tfrac12$, the desired lower bound $3$ follows immediately.

The only step requiring care is verifying that the substitution indeed parametrizes all triangles and transforms the denominators correctly.

## Problem Understanding

We are given a triangle with side lengths $a,b,c$ and must prove

$$\frac{a}{b+c-a}+\frac{b}{c+a-b}+\frac{c}{a+b-c}\ge 3.$$

This is a Type B problem, a pure proof.

The core difficulty is to exploit the triangle inequalities. The expressions $b+c-a$, $c+a-b$, and $a+b-c$ suggest introducing the standard variables corresponding to the distances from the semiperimeter:

$$x=\frac{b+c-a}{2},\quad y=\frac{c+a-b}{2},\quad z=\frac{a+b-c}{2}.$$

After this substitution the inequality reduces to a sum of reciprocal pairs, where the elementary inequality

$$t+\frac1t\ge2$$

can be applied.

## Proof Architecture

Define

$$x=\frac{b+c-a}{2},\quad y=\frac{c+a-b}{2},\quad z=\frac{a+b-c}{2}.$$

Since $a,b,c$ are sides of a triangle, $x,y,z>0$.

Show that

$$a=y+z,\qquad b=z+x,\qquad c=x+y.$$

This follows by solving the linear system determined by the definitions of $x,y,z$.

Rewrite the given expression in terms of $x,y,z$ as

$$\frac12\left(\frac yx+\frac zx+\frac zy+\frac xy+\frac xz+\frac yz\right).$$

This is a direct substitution.

Group the six fractions into three reciprocal pairs and use

$$\frac uv+\frac vu\ge2$$

for positive $u,v$.

Adding the three inequalities yields a lower bound of $6$ for the parenthesized expression.

Multiply by $\tfrac12$ to obtain the desired lower bound $3$.

The most delicate point is the substitution $a=y+z$, $b=z+x$, $c=x+y$, because every later step depends on its correctness.

## Solution

Let

$$x=\frac{b+c-a}{2},\qquad y=\frac{c+a-b}{2},\qquad z=\frac{a+b-c}{2}.$$

Since $a,b,c$ are the sides of a triangle,

$$b+c-a>0,\qquad c+a-b>0,\qquad a+b-c>0,$$

hence

$$x,y,z>0.$$

Adding the definitions of $y$ and $z$ gives

$$y+z =\frac{c+a-b+a+b-c}{2} =\frac{2a}{2} =a.$$

Similarly,

$$z+x=b,\qquad x+y=c.$$

Therefore

$$a=y+z,\qquad b=z+x,\qquad c=x+y,$$

and

$$b+c-a=2x,\qquad c+a-b=2y,\qquad a+b-c=2z.$$

Substituting into the left-hand side,

$$\begin{aligned} \frac{a}{b+c-a} +\frac{b}{c+a-b} +\frac{c}{a+b-c} &= \frac{y+z}{2x} +\frac{z+x}{2y} +\frac{x+y}{2z}\\ &= \frac12\left( \frac yx+\frac zx+\frac zy+\frac xy+\frac xz+\frac yz \right). \end{aligned}$$

Now

$$\frac{x}{y}+\frac{y}{x}\ge2,$$

because

$$\frac{x}{y}+\frac{y}{x}-2 =\frac{(x-y)^2}{xy}\ge0.$$

Applying the same argument to the pairs $(y,z)$ and $(z,x)$ yields

$$\frac{x}{y}+\frac{y}{x} +\frac{y}{z}+\frac{z}{y} +\frac{z}{x}+\frac{x}{z} \ge6.$$

Hence

$$\frac12\left( \frac yx+\frac zx+\frac zy+\frac xy+\frac xz+\frac yz \right) \ge \frac12\cdot6 =3.$$

Thus

$$\frac{a}{b+c-a} +\frac{b}{c+a-b} +\frac{c}{a+b-c} \ge3.$$

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the substitution. Starting from

$$x=\frac{b+c-a}{2},\qquad y=\frac{c+a-b}{2},\qquad z=\frac{a+b-c}{2},$$

we compute

$$y+z =\frac{c+a-b+a+b-c}{2} =a,$$

and similarly obtain $b=z+x$ and $c=x+y$. A sign error in any definition would destroy these identities and invalidate the remainder of the proof.

The second delicate step is the transformation of the denominators. From the definition of $x$,

$$b+c-a=2x,$$

and analogously

$$c+a-b=2y,\qquad a+b-c=2z.$$

Without checking these identities explicitly, the factor $\tfrac12$ in the rewritten expression could easily be lost.

The final step uses

$$\frac{x}{y}+\frac{y}{x}\ge2.$$

The proof

$$\frac{x}{y}+\frac{y}{x}-2 =\frac{(x-y)^2}{xy}$$

requires $x,y>0$. Positivity is guaranteed by the triangle inequalities, which is why establishing $x,y,z>0$ at the beginning is essential.

## Alternative Approaches

Using the same substitution

$$a=y+z,\qquad b=z+x,\qquad c=x+y,$$

one may apply the arithmetic mean-geometric mean inequality directly:

$$\frac{y+z}{2x}\ge\sqrt{\frac{yz}{x^2}},$$

and similarly for the other two terms. Multiplying the three resulting inequalities gives

$$\prod_{cyc}\frac{y+z}{2x}\ge1.$$

The arithmetic mean-geometric mean inequality then yields

$$\frac13\sum_{cyc}\frac{y+z}{2x} \ge \sqrt[3]{\prod_{cyc}\frac{y+z}{2x}} \ge1,$$

which implies the desired inequality.

The main proof is preferable because it reduces the problem immediately to three instances of the elementary inequality

$$\frac{u}{v}+\frac{v}{u}\ge2,$$

making the equality case and the source of the constant $3$ completely transparent.
