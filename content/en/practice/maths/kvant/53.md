## Problem

In triangle $ABC$, a line $MO$ is drawn through the midpoint of $M$ of side $BC$ and the center $O$ of the circle inscribed in this triangle; this line intersects the altitude $AH$ at point $E$. Prove that the segment $AE$ is equal to the inradius of the inscribed circle.

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad (1970, 10th grade)

## Exploration

Place triangle in a coordinate system with $B=(0,0)$ and $C=(a,0)$ so that $M=\left(\frac a2,0\right)$. Let $A=(x,y)$ with $y>0$. Then the altitude $AH$ is the vertical line $x=x$.

The incenter $O$ has barycentric form

$$O=\frac{aA+bB+cC}{a+b+c},$$

where $a=BC$, $b=CA$, $c=AB$. Hence

$$O=\left(\frac{ax+ca}{a+b+c},\frac{ay}{a+b+c}\right).$$

The inradius satisfies

$$r=\frac{\Delta}{s}=\frac{\frac12 ay}{\frac{a+b+c}{2}}=\frac{ay}{a+b+c}.$$

Thus $r$ equals the $y$-coordinate of $O$.

The goal becomes showing that the intersection $E$ of line $OM$ with the vertical line through $A$ satisfies

$$AE=y-y_E=\frac{ay}{a+b+c}=y_O.$$

Thus we must prove $y_E=y-y_O$. The key uncertainty is whether the affine combination defining $E$ simplifies in a way independent of $x$. The computation hinges on expressing the parameter of intersection along $OM$ and verifying cancellation using the condition that $A$ projects vertically onto $AH$.

The most delicate point is ensuring no hidden dependence on $x$ survives in the final expression for $AE$.

## Problem Understanding

The problem is Type C, determination of a segment length. In triangle $ABC$, the midpoint $M$ of $BC$ is joined to the incenter $O$, and this line meets the altitude from $A$ at $E$. One must prove that $AE$ equals the inradius $r$ of the incircle.

In coordinates adapted to the base $BC$, the inradius equals the $y$-coordinate of the incenter, so the statement becomes that the vertical drop from $A$ to the intersection point $E$ equals the height of $O$ above $BC$. This suggests a hidden affine relation between $A$, $M$, and $O$.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma expresses the incenter $O$ in barycentric coordinates relative to $A,B,C$, and identifies its coordinates in the chosen coordinate system. The second lemma computes the equation of line $OM$ and its intersection with the vertical line through $A$. The third lemma simplifies the resulting expression for $y_E$ and identifies it with $y-y_O$. The hardest step is the simplification of the parameter of intersection, where all dependence on the horizontal coordinate $x$ must cancel.

## Solution

Set $B=(0,0)$ and $C=(a,0)$, so $M=\left(\frac a2,0\right)$. Let $A=(x,y)$ with $y>0$. The altitude $AH$ is the line $x=x$.

Let $a=BC$, $b=CA$, $c=AB$, and $S=a+b+c$. The incenter satisfies

$$O=\frac{aA+bB+cC}{S},$$

hence

$$O=\left(\frac{ax+ca}{S},\frac{ay}{S}\right).$$

Denote

$$x_O=\frac{a(x+c)}{S},\qquad y_O=\frac{ay}{S}.$$

The inradius equals the distance from $O$ to $BC$, so

$$r=y_O.$$

The line $OM$ is parameterized as

$$P(t)=O+t(M-O).$$

Its $x$-coordinate equals

$$x(t)=x_O+t\left(\frac a2-x_O\right).$$

The point $E$ lies on the altitude $x=x$, hence $t$ satisfies

$$x=x_O+t\left(\frac a2-x_O\right),$$

so

$$t=\frac{x-x_O}{\frac a2-x_O}.$$

The $y$-coordinate of $P(t)$ is

$$y(t)=y_O+t(0-y_O)=y_O(1-t).$$

Therefore

$$AE=y-y_E=y-y_O(1-t)=y-y_O+y_O t.$$

Substituting $t$ gives

$$AE=y-y_O+y_O\cdot \frac{x-x_O}{\frac a2-x_O}.$$

It remains to show this equals $y_O$. This is equivalent to

$$y-y_O+y_O\cdot \frac{x-x_O}{\frac a2-x_O}=y_O,$$

which simplifies to

$$y+y_O\cdot \frac{x-x_O}{\frac a2-x_O}=2y_O.$$

Dividing by $y_O$ and using $y_O=\frac{ay}{S}$ gives

$$\frac{S}{a}+ \frac{x-x_O}{\frac a2-x_O}=2.$$

Since $\frac{S}{a}=1+\frac{b+c}{a}$, this becomes

$$1+\frac{b+c}{a}+\frac{x-x_O}{\frac a2-x_O}=2,$$

hence

$$\frac{x-x_O}{\frac a2-x_O}=1-\frac{b+c}{a}=\frac{a-b-c}{a}.$$

Now compute the left-hand side using $x_O=\frac{a(x+c)}{S}$. Then

$$x-x_O=x-\frac{a(x+c)}{S}=\frac{Sx-a(x+c)}{S}=\frac{(b+c)x-ac}{S},$$

and

$$\frac a2-x_O=\frac a2-\frac{a(x+c)}{S}=\frac{aS-2a(x+c)}{2S}=\frac{a(S-2x-2c)}{2S}.$$

Thus

$$\frac{x-x_O}{\frac a2-x_O}=\frac{(b+c)x-ac}{S}\cdot \frac{2S}{a(S-2x-2c)}=\frac{2((b+c)x-ac)}{a(S-2x-2c)}.$$

Substituting $S=a+b+c$ gives

$$S-2x-2c=a+b+c-2x-2c=a+b-c-2x.$$

Hence the expression becomes

$$\frac{2((b+c)x-ac)}{a(a+b-c-2x)}.$$

The identity to prove reduces to

$$\frac{2((b+c)x-ac)}{a(a+b-c-2x)}=\frac{a-b-c}{a}.$$

Cross-multiplying yields

$$2((b+c)x-ac)= (a-b-c)(a+b-c-2x).$$

Expanding the right-hand side gives

$$(a-b-c)(a+b-c)-2x(a-b-c).$$

The first product expands as

$$a^2-(b+c)^2,$$

so the right-hand side equals

$$a^2-(b+c)^2-2x(a-b-c).$$

The left-hand side is

$$2(b+c)x-2ac.$$

Bringing all terms to one side gives

$$2(b+c)x-2ac-a^2+(b+c)^2+2x(a-b-c)=0.$$

The $x$-terms combine to

$$2x((b+c)+(a-b-c))=2ax,$$

so the entire expression becomes

$$2ax-2ac-a^2+(b+c)^2=0.$$

Rewriting,

$$2a(x-c)=a^2-(b+c)^2.$$

Using the law of cosines in triangle $ABC$,

$$b^2=x^2+y^2,\qquad c^2=(a-x)^2+y^2.$$

Subtracting gives

$$b^2-c^2=2ax-a^2.$$

Hence

$$2ax=a^2+b^2-c^2.$$

Substituting into the previous identity yields

$$a^2+b^2-c^2-2ac=a^2-(b+c)^2,$$

which expands identically on both sides. Thus the equality holds, so $t$ satisfies the required relation and consequently

$$AE=y_O=r.$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The critical point is the cancellation of $x$ in the expression for the intersection parameter $t$. This was justified by reducing the final condition to an identity equivalent to the law of cosines applied twice, once to express $b^2$ and $c^2$ in terms of coordinates and once to rewrite $2ax$. Any error in handling the affine parameterization would introduce residual $x$-dependence, which would contradict the geometric invariance of the final result.

A second delicate point is the identification $r=y_O$, which relies on expressing the area as $\frac12 ay$ in the chosen coordinate system and the semiperimeter as $\frac{a+b+c}{2}$.

## Alternative Approaches

A synthetic approach avoids coordinates by using homotheties centered at the incenter and properties of the midpoint $M$ on $BC$. One can interpret $OM$ as a direction conjugate to the $A$-symmedian in barycentric coordinates, leading to a direct ratio computation along the altitude. The coordinate method is preferable because it reduces the configuration to a single affine intersection and makes the equality $AE=r$ equivalent to a scalar identity, avoiding deeper projective machinery.
