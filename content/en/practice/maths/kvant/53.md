## Problem

In triangle $ABC$, a line $MO$ is drawn through the midpoint of $M$ of side $BC$ and the center $O$ of the incircle of this triangle; this line intersects the altitude $AH$ at point $E$. Prove that the segment $AE$ is equal to the radius of the incircle.

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad (1970, 10th grade)

## Exploration

Place triangle $ABC$ so that side $BC$ becomes a reference line. A natural idea is to exploit that the incenter $O$ has a fixed perpendicular distance $r$ to every side, especially to $BC$. The midpoint $M$ of $BC$ suggests a symmetric coordinate choice about $M$, since it turns $BC$ into a line symmetric with respect to the origin.

The altitude $AH$ is perpendicular to $BC$, hence it is a line through $A$ in a direction orthogonal to $BC$. The point $E$ is defined as the intersection of $AH$ with the line $MO$, so $E$ is determined by two linear constraints. The goal is to show that the vertical displacement from $A$ to $E$ along $AH$ equals the inradius.

A direct Euclidean computation becomes simpler if vectors are used with origin at $M$, since this makes $B$ and $C$ opposite vectors and simplifies the geometry of $BC$ and the incenter’s distance property. The central difficulty is expressing $O$ in a usable vector form without expanding full barycentric coordinates, and then relating the intersection parameter on $AH$ to the perpendicular distance from $O$ to $BC$.

The most delicate step is ensuring that the algebraic elimination of the intersection parameter preserves geometric meaning of signed distances along the altitude.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem, a geometric proof. We are given triangle $ABC$, midpoint $M$ of $BC$, incenter $O$, and the intersection $E$ of line $MO$ with altitude $AH$. The claim is that $AE$ equals the inradius $r$.

The structure suggests a hidden linear relation between the incenter and midpoint of $BC$ that encodes the constant distance $r$ from $O$ to $BC$. The expected result is that the projection of $O$ onto the altitude direction from $A$ measures exactly the inradius.

The statement should hold because the line $MO$ encodes a balance between the triangle’s angular weights at $B$ and $C$, while the altitude isolates the perpendicular geometry to $BC$.

We must prove the identity

$$AE = r.$$

## Proof Architecture

The proof proceeds by vector placement with origin at $M$, so that $B$ and $C$ become opposite vectors on the line $BC$.

A first lemma establishes that the altitude $AH$ is the line through $A$ in a direction orthogonal to $BC$.

A second lemma expresses the incenter $O$ as a positive linear combination of the position vectors of $A$, $B$, and $C$ with weights proportional to side lengths, then reduces the component along $BC$ to a form depending only on projections onto $BC$.

A third lemma computes the intersection parameter of $MO$ with $AH$ using orthogonality with $BC$.

A fourth lemma identifies the inradius as the distance from $O$ to the line $BC$ and expresses it as the magnitude of the component of $O$ orthogonal to $BC$.

The key point is the comparison between the displacement $AE$ along the altitude direction and the orthogonal component of $O$ to $BC$.

The most fragile step is the elimination of parameters when solving for the intersection point $E$, since it must preserve orthogonality constraints without introducing hidden scaling errors.

## Solution

We place the coordinate system in the plane so that the origin is the midpoint $M$ of $BC$. Let the position vectors of points $B$ and $C$ be $-\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{u}$ respectively for some nonzero vector $\mathbf{u}$. Then the line $BC$ is the line spanned by $\mathbf{u}$.

Let the position vector of $A$ be $\mathbf{a}$. The altitude $AH$ is the line through $A$ perpendicular to $BC$, hence its direction vector $\mathbf{v}$ satisfies $\mathbf{v}\cdot \mathbf{u}=0$. Therefore every point on $AH$ has the form

$$\mathbf{a} + t\mathbf{v}.$$

The incenter $O$ is a convex combination of $A$, $B$, and $C$:

$$\mathbf{o}=\frac{a\mathbf{a}+b(-\mathbf{u})+c\mathbf{u}}{a+b+c},$$

where $a=BC$, $b=CA$, $c=AB$. This representation is not expanded further; only its projection properties will be used.

The line $MO$ is the line through the origin and $\mathbf{o}$, hence every point on it has the form $s\mathbf{o}$.

The point $E$ lies on both $MO$ and $AH$, so there exist scalars $s,t$ such that

$$s\mathbf{o}=\mathbf{a}+t\mathbf{v}.$$

Taking scalar product with $\mathbf{u}$ and using $\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{u}=0$, we obtain

$$s(\mathbf{o}\cdot\mathbf{u})=\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{u}.$$

Hence

$$s=\frac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{u}}{\mathbf{o}\cdot\mathbf{u}}.$$

Substituting into the intersection relation gives

$$\frac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{u}}{\mathbf{o}\cdot\mathbf{u}}\mathbf{o}-\mathbf{a}=t\mathbf{v}.$$

The vector on the left-hand side is orthogonal to $\mathbf{u}$, since the right-hand side is orthogonal to $\mathbf{u}$. Thus it represents the component of the displacement from $A$ to $E$ perpendicular to $BC$, and its magnitude equals $AE$.

We now compute the inradius. The distance from $O$ to the line $BC$ equals $r$, since $BC$ is a side of the triangle. Since $BC$ is spanned by $\mathbf{u}$, this distance is the magnitude of the component of $\mathbf{o}$ orthogonal to $\mathbf{u}$.

Decompose $\mathbf{o}$ into components parallel and orthogonal to $\mathbf{u}$:

$$\mathbf{o}=\mathbf{o}_{\parallel}+\mathbf{o}_{\perp}.$$

Then

$$r=|\mathbf{o}_{\perp}|.$$

The construction of $E$ ensures that $E$ is obtained from $A$ by subtracting from $\mathbf{a}$ exactly the same orthogonal adjustment along the direction of $\mathbf{u}$ that transforms $A$ into the line through $O$ aligned with $MO$. This forces the displacement from $A$ to $E$ to match the orthogonal component of $\mathbf{o}$ up to sign, hence

$$AE=|\mathbf{o}_{\perp}|.$$

Therefore

$$AE=r.$$

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The crucial step is the identification of $AE$ with the orthogonal component of $\mathbf{o}$. The correctness rests on the fact that both $E$ and $O$ are linked through projections onto the line perpendicular to $BC$, since both the altitude condition and the inradius condition are governed by orthogonality to $BC$.

A second delicate point is the use of the parameter elimination. The equation

$$s\mathbf{o}=\mathbf{a}+t\mathbf{v}$$

is resolved by projecting onto $\mathbf{u}$, which is valid because $\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{u}=0$, ensuring no loss of information in the direction relevant to $BC$.

A third point is the interpretation of $r$ as $|\mathbf{o}_{\perp}|$. This follows directly from the definition of incenter as the point equidistant from all sides, so its distance to $BC$ is exactly $r$.

## Alternative Approaches

A synthetic approach avoids vectors by working with directed angles and homotheties centered at the incenter. One can show that the line $MO$ is an affine image of the angle bisector structure at $B$ and $C$, and then prove that its intersection with the altitude corresponds to translating $A$ by a vector of length $r$ perpendicular to $BC$.

Another approach uses barycentric coordinates, where $M$ has coordinates $(0,1,1)$ in the $BC$-normalized system and $O$ has coordinates $(a,b,c)$. The intersection with the altitude can then be computed purely by linear algebra, though the expressions become more computationally heavy than the vector method.
