## Problem

In triangle $ABC$, a line $MO$ is drawn through the midpoint $M$ of side $BC$ and the center $O$ of the circle inscribed in this triangle, intersecting the altitude $AH$ at point $E$. Prove that the segment $AE$ is equal to the radius of the inscribed circle.

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad (1970, 10th grade)

## Exploration

Let $a=BC$, $b=CA$, $c=AB$, and let $r$ be the inradius. Since $AH$ is an altitude, it is natural to place coordinates so that $BC$ is horizontal. Take

$$B\left(-\frac a2,0\right),\qquad C\left(\frac a2,0\right),$$

hence

$$M=(0,0).$$

Let

$$A=(x,h),$$

where $h=AH$.

The incenter has coordinates

$$O=\left(\frac{a x}{a+b+c},\frac{a h}{a+b+c}\right),$$

because barycentric weights are proportional to side lengths. Since the distance from $O$ to $BC$ equals the inradius,

$$r=\frac{a h}{a+b+c}.$$

The line $MO$ passes through the origin, so its equation is

$$Y=\frac{h}{x}X$$

provided $x\neq0$. The altitude $AH$ is the vertical line

$$X=x.$$

Their intersection is therefore

$$E=(x,h).$$

This cannot be right, because it gives $E=A$. The mistake comes from using the same parameter $h$ for both $A$ and $O$ after cancellation. Recomputing carefully,

$$O=\left(\frac{a x}{s'},\frac{a h}{s'}\right), \qquad s'=a+b+c.$$

Hence the slope of $MO$ is

$$\frac{a h/s'}{a x/s'}=\frac hx.$$

So indeed the line $MO$ passes through $A$. This means the coordinate choice hides the geometry incorrectly, because the incenter formula must use total weight $a+b+c$, but coordinates should be

$$O=\frac{aA+bB+cC}{a+b+c}.$$

Substituting gives

$$O=\left(\frac{ax+\frac a2(c-b)}{a+b+c},\frac{ah}{a+b+c}\right).$$

Now the cancellation disappears.

The intersection with $X=x$ yields

$$y_E = h\cdot \frac{x}{x+\frac{c-b}{2}}.$$

The target is

$$AE=h-y_E=r.$$

This expression looks unpleasant. A cleaner coordinate system may help.

Take instead

$$B=(0,0),\qquad C=(a,0),\qquad A=(u,h).$$

Then

$$M=\left(\frac a2,0\right),$$

and

$$O= \left( \frac{au+ac}{a+b+c}, \frac{ah}{a+b+c} \right).$$

The line through $M$ and $O$ intersects $x=u$ at some point $E$. Compute its parameter form:

$$(x,y)=\left(\frac a2,0\right) +t\left( \frac{a(u+c)}{a+b+c}-\frac a2, \frac{ah}{a+b+c} \right).$$

Setting $x=u$ gives

$$t= \frac{u-\frac a2}{ \frac{a(u+c)}{a+b+c}-\frac a2 }.$$

This still looks messy. There should be a simplification using

$$c^2=u^2+h^2,\qquad b^2=(u-a)^2+h^2.$$

The crucial point is likely an identity such as

$$2u-a=c-b.$$

Indeed,

$$c^2-b^2=u^2-(u-a)^2=2au-a^2,$$

hence

$$(c-b)(c+b)=a(2u-a).$$

Since

$$c+b\neq a$$

in general, this does not simplify directly.

A synthetic approach may be cleaner. Since $O$ is the incenter,

$$d(O,BC)=r.$$

The line through $M$ and $O$ meets the altitude $AH$ at $E$. The claim $AE=r$ suggests that $E$ is obtained from $A$ by descending exactly one inradius along the altitude.

Perhaps the ratio on $AH$ is the key. Let

$$AH=h.$$

Then

$$\frac{AE}{AH}=\frac rh=\frac{a}{a+b+c}.$$

So it suffices to prove

$$\frac{AE}{AH}=\frac{a}{a+b+c}.$$

Because $E$ lies on line $MO$, Menelaus or mass points may produce exactly this ratio.

The hardest step is establishing the precise division ratio on the altitude without drowning in coordinates.

## Problem Understanding

We are given a triangle $ABC$, its incenter $O$, the midpoint $M$ of side $BC$, and the altitude $AH$ from $A$ to $BC$. The line through $M$ and $O$ intersects the altitude at a point $E$. The task is to prove that

$$AE=r,$$

where $r$ is the inradius of triangle $ABC$.

This is a Type B problem, a pure proof problem.

The core difficulty is relating the affine condition that $E$ lies on the line $MO$ to the metric quantity $r$. The natural invariant is the ratio

$$\frac{AE}{AH},$$

because

$$r=\frac{[ABC]}{s} = \frac{\frac12 a\cdot AH}{\frac12(a+b+c)} = \frac{a\cdot AH}{a+b+c}.$$

Thus the target identity becomes

$$\frac{AE}{AH}=\frac{a}{a+b+c}.$$

## Proof Architecture

First, introduce coordinates adapted to the altitude and midpoint structure:

$$B=(0,0),\quad C=(a,0),\quad A=(u,h).$$

Then $AH$ is the vertical line $x=u$, and

$$M=\left(\frac a2,0\right).$$

Next, compute the coordinates of the incenter using side-length weights:

$$O=\frac{aA+bB+cC}{a+b+c}.$$

Then parametrize the line $MO$ and determine its intersection with the line $x=u$ defining the altitude.

After simplifying the resulting expression, prove that the $y$-coordinate of $E$ equals

$$h-r.$$

This implies

$$AE=r.$$

The lemma most likely to fail under careless manipulation is the algebraic simplification converting the coordinate expression for $E$ into the inradius formula. Every cancellation must be checked explicitly.

## Solution

Let

$$BC=a,\qquad CA=b,\qquad AB=c,$$

and let the altitude from $A$ to $BC$ have length

$$AH=h.$$

Choose coordinates

$$B=(0,0),\qquad C=(a,0),\qquad A=(u,h).$$

Then

$$M=\left(\frac a2,0\right),$$

and the altitude $AH$ is the vertical line

$$x=u.$$

The incenter of a triangle with vertices $A,B,C$ has coordinates

$$O=\frac{aA+bB+cC}{a+b+c}.$$

Hence

$$O= \left( \frac{au+ac}{a+b+c}, \frac{ah}{a+b+c} \right).$$

Write the line through $M$ and $O$ in parametric form:

$$(x,y)= \left(\frac a2,0\right) +t\left( \frac{au+ac}{a+b+c}-\frac a2, \frac{ah}{a+b+c} \right).$$

This line meets the altitude $x=u$ at the point $E$. Therefore

$$u= \frac a2 +t\left( \frac{au+ac}{a+b+c}-\frac a2 \right).$$

Solving for $t$ gives

$$t= \frac{u-\frac a2}{ \frac{au+ac}{a+b+c}-\frac a2 }.$$

Simplify the denominator:

$$\frac{au+ac}{a+b+c}-\frac a2 = \frac{2au+2ac-a(a+b+c)}{2(a+b+c)}.$$

Thus

$$t= \frac{(2u-a)(a+b+c)} {2au+2ac-a(a+b+c)}.$$

Now compute the $y$-coordinate of $E$:

$$y_E= t\cdot\frac{ah}{a+b+c} = \frac{ah(2u-a)} {2au+2ac-a(a+b+c)}.$$

We next simplify the denominator. Since

$$b^2=(u-a)^2+h^2, \qquad c^2=u^2+h^2,$$

their difference is

$$c^2-b^2 = u^2-(u-a)^2 = 2au-a^2.$$

Hence

$$(c-b)(c+b)=a(2u-a).$$

Because

$$c+b>0,$$

we obtain

$$2u-a=\frac{(c-b)(b+c)}a.$$

Substitute this into the denominator:

$$2au+2ac-a(a+b+c) = a(2u-a)+a(c-b).$$

Using the previous identity,

$$a(2u-a)=(c-b)(b+c),$$

so

$$2au+2ac-a(a+b+c) = (c-b)(b+c)+a(c-b).$$

Factor:

$$= (c-b)(a+b+c).$$

Therefore

$$y_E = \frac{ah}{a+b+c}.$$

The point $A$ has $y$-coordinate $h$, so

$$AE=h-y_E = h-\frac{ah}{a+b+c}.$$

Since

$$2[ABC]=ah,$$

the inradius satisfies

$$r=\frac{[ABC]}{\frac12(a+b+c)} = \frac{ah}{a+b+c}.$$

Hence

$$AE = h-r.$$

This contradicts the desired statement, so one algebraic step must be rechecked.

Return to the expression

$$y_E= \frac{ah(2u-a)} {2au+2ac-a(a+b+c)}.$$

The denominator equals

$$2au-a^2+2ac-ab-ac = a(2u-a)+a(c-b).$$

Using

$$a(2u-a)=(c-b)(b+c),$$

we get

$$(c-b)(b+c)+a(c-b) = (c-b)(a+b+c).$$

Therefore

$$y_E = \frac{ah}{a+b+c}\cdot \frac{2u-a}{c-b}.$$

Now

$$\frac{2u-a}{c-b} = \frac{b+c}{a},$$

again by

$$(c-b)(b+c)=a(2u-a).$$

Consequently

$$y_E= \frac{h(b+c)}{a+b+c}.$$

Thus

$$AE = h-y_E = h\left(1-\frac{b+c}{a+b+c}\right) = \frac{ah}{a+b+c}.$$

Finally,

$$r=\frac{[ABC]}{s} = \frac{\frac12 ah}{\frac12(a+b+c)} = \frac{ah}{a+b+c}.$$

Hence

$$AE=r.$$

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the coordinate formula for the incenter:

$$O=\frac{aA+bB+cC}{a+b+c}.$$

The weights opposite the vertices must be used. Interchanging $b$ and $c$ changes the final line equation and produces an incorrect ratio.

The second delicate step is the identity

$$(c-b)(b+c)=a(2u-a).$$

Starting from

$$c^2=u^2+h^2,\qquad b^2=(u-a)^2+h^2,$$

we compute

$$c^2-b^2 = u^2-(u^2-2au+a^2) = 2au-a^2.$$

Since

$$c^2-b^2=(c-b)(c+b),$$

the identity follows exactly.

The third delicate step is simplifying

$$\frac{2u-a}{c-b}.$$

Using the previous identity,

$$\frac{2u-a}{c-b} = \frac{b+c}{a}.$$

Replacing it incorrectly by

$$\frac{a}{b+c}$$

would reverse the final ratio and give

$$AE=h-r,$$

which is false.

## Alternative Approaches

A synthetic proof can be obtained through affine geometry. Since $M$ is the midpoint of $BC$, the line through $M$ parallel to the angle bisector at $A$ induces a fixed ratio on the altitude. Using the fact that the incenter divides the segment joining $A$ to the touchpoint structure in the ratio

$$r:AH=\frac{a}{a+b+c},$$

one can derive directly that the intersection point on the altitude satisfies

$$\frac{AE}{AH}=\frac{a}{a+b+c}.$$

The proof becomes shorter, but the ratio computations are less transparent.

Another approach uses vectors. Writing

$$O=\frac{aA+bB+cC}{a+b+c}$$

and

$$M=\frac{B+C}{2},$$

one expresses the condition that $E$ lies simultaneously on $AH$ and on $MO$. Solving the resulting vector equation gives

$$\overrightarrow{AE} = -\frac{a}{a+b+c}\,\overrightarrow{AH},$$

from which

$$AE=r$$

follows immediately because

$$r=\frac{a}{a+b+c}\,AH.$$

The coordinate method used above is preferable here because every quantity has a direct geometric meaning and the necessary algebra remains elementary.
