## Problem

Solve the equation $$\sqrt[3]{1-x}+\sqrt[3]{1+x}=3,$$ where $p$ is an arbitrary real number.

Yu. I. Ionin

## Exploration

Let

$$a=\sqrt[3]{1-x}, \quad b=\sqrt[3]{1+x}.$$

Then the equation becomes $a+b=3$, while also $a^3=1-x$ and $b^3=1+x$. Adding these identities eliminates $x$, giving

$$a^3+b^3=2.$$

Using the algebraic identity for the sum of cubes,

$$a^3+b^3=(a+b)^3-3ab(a+b),$$

and substituting $a+b=3$, one obtains a relation between $a$ and $b$. This reduces the problem to a system in two real numbers with fixed sum and product. The consistency of that system is the decisive point.

## Problem Understanding

The task is to determine all real solutions of the equation

$$\sqrt[3]{1-x}+\sqrt[3]{1+x}=3.$$

This is a Type A problem, requiring classification of all real $x$ satisfying the equation.

The core difficulty is that the cube roots introduce nonlinear coupling, and the correct strategy is to convert the equation into an algebraic system in auxiliary variables and check whether it is realizable over the reals.

## Proof Architecture

Introduce $a=\sqrt[3]{1-x}$ and $b=\sqrt[3]{1+x}$, reducing the problem to the system $a+b=3$ and $a^3+b^3=2$.

Express $a^3+b^3$ using $(a+b)^3-3ab(a+b)$, which yields a constraint on $ab$.

Solve for $ab$ and then examine whether real numbers $a,b$ with the obtained sum and product exist by checking the discriminant of the associated quadratic equation.

The key step is verifying whether the resulting algebraic system admits real solutions; failure of real realizability resolves the problem.

## Solution

Let

$$a=\sqrt[3]{1-x}, \quad b=\sqrt[3]{1+x}.$$

Then the given equation becomes

$$a+b=3.$$

Cubing the definitions yields

$$a^3=1-x, \quad b^3=1+x,$$

and adding gives

$$a^3+b^3=2.$$

Using the identity

$$a^3+b^3=(a+b)^3-3ab(a+b),$$

and substituting $a+b=3$, we obtain

$$2=27-9ab.$$

Hence

$$9ab=25, \quad ab=\frac{25}{9}.$$

Thus $a$ and $b$ must satisfy

$$a+b=3, \quad ab=\frac{25}{9}.$$

They would then be roots of the quadratic equation

$$t^2-3t+\frac{25}{9}=0.$$

Its discriminant is

$$\Delta=9-\frac{100}{9}=\frac{81-100}{9}=-\frac{19}{9}.$$

Since $\Delta<0$, this quadratic has no real roots, so there do not exist real numbers $a$ and $b$ satisfying both conditions.

Because $a$ and $b$ are defined as real cube roots of real numbers, they must themselves be real. This contradiction shows that no real $x$ can satisfy the original equation.

Hence the equation has no real solutions.

$$\boxed{\varnothing}$$

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The transformation from the original equation to variables $a$ and $b$ preserves equivalence because the real cube root function is bijective on $\mathbb{R}$, so no extraneous branches are introduced.

The identity for $a^3+b^3$ is applied exactly with the substitution $a+b=3$, yielding a unique value for $ab$, so no hidden assumptions enter.

The final step relies on the discriminant criterion for real solvability of a quadratic with real coefficients; since the discriminant is strictly negative, no real pair $(a,b)$ can realize the required sum and product, completing the contradiction without ambiguity.

## Alternative Approaches

One alternative approach is to observe symmetry and set $x=\frac{1}{2}(t^3-1)$ or similar substitutions, converting the equation into a single-variable polynomial condition in $t=\sqrt[3]{1-x}$. This leads again to an inconsistency after eliminating $x$.

Another approach uses convexity considerations of the cube root function to bound $\sqrt[3]{1-x}+\sqrt[3]{1+x}$, showing that its maximum is strictly less than $3$, which also implies nonexistence of solutions.
