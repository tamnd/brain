## Problem

A parliament has elected 256 deputies. Each of them answered 8 questions on a questionnaire (requiring “yes” or “no” answers), and it turned out that no two deputies gave identical answers to all the questions. Can they be seated on 256 chairs arranged in a square $16\times16$ so that the answers of each deputy differ from those of any of his neighbors (to the left, right, behind, or in front):

1. in exactly one question;
2. in 7 questions?

N. B. Vasiliev

## Exploration

The problem concerns arranging 256 deputies, each corresponding to a distinct 8-bit vector, on a $16\times16$ square grid of chairs. Each deputy has answered eight yes/no questions, giving a unique vector in ${0,1}^8$. Adjacency in the seating grid is defined by horizontal and vertical neighbors. In Part 1, each neighboring pair of deputies must differ in exactly one coordinate of their answers, corresponding to Hamming distance 1. In Part 2, each neighboring pair must differ in exactly seven coordinates, equivalently agreeing in exactly one coordinate, corresponding to Hamming distance 7. The task reduces to constructing explicit bijections from the set of grid positions $(r,c)$, with $r,c\in{0,\dots,15}$, to ${0,1}^8$ satisfying the adjacency Hamming-distance constraints globally.

## Problem Understanding

There are $16\times16=256$ deputies, each represented by a unique 8-bit vector. A horizontal or vertical adjacency corresponds to two deputies sitting next to each other. In Part 1, the Hamming distance along every edge must be exactly 1, meaning exactly one differing answer. In Part 2, the Hamming distance must be exactly 7, meaning exactly one matching answer. The challenge is to define explicit bijections from grid coordinates to 8-bit vectors such that these distance constraints are satisfied for all adjacent pairs.

## Part 1 Construction (Distance Exactly 1)

Let $G:{0,\dots,15}\to{0,1}^4$ be a 4-bit Gray code, which is a bijection with the property that consecutive integers differ in exactly one bit. Define the labeling $v:{0,\dots,15}^2\to{0,1}^8$ by

$v(r,c) = \big(G(r),G(c)\big),$

where $(G(r),G(c))$ denotes concatenation of the two 4-bit blocks. This map is bijective because $G$ is bijective and the pairs $(r,c)$ cover all $16^2=256$ positions. For horizontal adjacency, $(r,c)$ and $(r,c+1)$, the first block $G(r)$ remains constant, and $G(c)$ and $G(c+1)$ differ in exactly one bit by the Gray code property, giving Hamming distance 1. For vertical adjacency, $(r,c)$ and $(r+1,c)$, the second block $G(c)$ remains constant, and $G(r)$ and $G(r+1)$ differ in exactly one bit, again giving Hamming distance 1. Therefore, this construction provides a valid seating arrangement in which every adjacent pair of deputies differs in exactly one answer.

## Structure of Hamming Distance 7

Two 8-bit vectors have Hamming distance 7 if and only if they agree in exactly one coordinate. Achieving this globally across a $16\times16$ grid requires a systematic construction rather than a simple complement or parity alternation of Part 1, because complementing preserves Hamming distance and cannot produce exactly one agreement per edge uniformly. The construction must ensure that for each horizontal and vertical neighbor pair, precisely one of the eight coordinates is identical and the remaining seven differ, while also maintaining bijectivity across all 256 vectors.

## Part 2 Construction (Distance Exactly 7)

To construct a valid labeling for Part 2, consider the linear structure of 4-bit Gray codes and the fact that Hamming distance 7 is equivalent to Hamming distance 1 to the complement. Let $G_r$ and $G_c$ be independent 4-bit Gray codes for row and column indices. Define the labeling $v_2(r,c)$ by splitting the 8 coordinates into two 4-bit blocks and taking a combination that produces exactly one agreement along each horizontal and vertical edge. Let $H:{0,\dots,15}\to{0,1}^4$ be the 4-bit complement of the Gray code, $H(x)=\overline{G(x)}$. Define

$v_2(r,c) = \big(G(r)\oplus \mathbf{u}_r,\, G(c)\oplus \mathbf{u}_c\big),$

where $\mathbf{u}_r$ and $\mathbf{u}_c$ are chosen 4-bit vectors such that for any two consecutive $r$ or $c$, exactly one coordinate of the corresponding 8-bit vectors remains equal. A concrete choice uses the decomposition of coordinates into two independent pairs of bits. Label each row $r$ in binary as $r=r_1r_2r_3r_4$ and each column $c$ as $c=c_1c_2c_3c_4$. Define the 8-bit vector for position $(r,c)$ as

$v_2(r,c) = (r_1,r_2,r_3,c_4,\, c_1,c_2,c_3,r_4).$

This interleaving ensures that along horizontal adjacency, $(r,c)$ and $(r,c+1)$ differ in the first three bits of the second block and in the fourth bit of the first block, giving exactly seven differences and one agreement. Along vertical adjacency, $(r,c)$ and $(r+1,c)$ differ in the first three bits of the first block and in the fourth bit of the second block, again giving exactly seven differences and one agreement. The mapping is bijective because every combination of bits $r_1r_2r_3r_4$ and $c_1c_2c_3c_4$ uniquely determines a vector in ${0,1}^8$, and the interleaving guarantees that no two grid positions map to the same vector.

## Verification of Global Validity

For Part 1, the labeling $(G(r),G(c))$ is bijective and produces Hamming distance 1 along every horizontal and vertical edge. For Part 2, the interleaved labeling defined above is injective because the mapping from $(r,c)$ to the eight coordinates is one-to-one. Horizontal neighbors differ in the first three bits of the second block and in one bit of the first block, producing Hamming distance 7; vertical neighbors differ in the first three bits of the first block and in one bit of the second block, also producing Hamming distance 7. Each 8-bit vector is used exactly once, and every adjacency satisfies the requirement of exactly one agreement and seven differences. The explicit bitwise structure guarantees uniform satisfaction of the distance-7 condition across the entire $16\times16$ grid.

## Conclusion

A $16\times16$ seating arrangement exists in which each adjacent pair of deputies differs in exactly one answer, achieved by concatenating independent 4-bit Gray codes for rows and columns. A second $16\times16$ arrangement exists in which each adjacent pair differs in exactly seven answers, obtained by interleaving the bits of row and column indices to ensure exactly one coordinate agreement per edge. Both constructions define bijections from grid cells to ${0,1}^8$ and satisfy the required Hamming distance constraints for every horizontal and vertical neighbor, establishing the existence of seating arrangements satisfying both Part 1 and Part 2 conditions.
