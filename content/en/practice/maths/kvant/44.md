## Problem

Prove that for every natural number $K$ there exist infinitely many natural numbers $T$ that do not contain zeros in their decimal representation and such that $T$ and $KT$ have equal sums of digits.

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad (1970, 10th grade)

## Exploration

Consider small values of $K$ and try to find numbers $T$ such that $T$ and $KT$ have the same sum of digits and $T$ contains no zeros. For $K = 1$, any number $T$ works trivially. For $K = 2$, simple numbers such as $T = 1, 2, \dots, 9$ fail quickly: $2 \cdot 1 = 2$, sum equal to 1 and 2, which differs. Trying $T = 19$, $2 \cdot 19 = 38$, sums $1+9=10$, $3+8=11$, unequal. Testing a pattern of repeated digits, say $T = 111\ldots1$, $KT$ tends to create carries that may redistribute digit sums. For instance, $T = 1111$, $K=2$, $2 \cdot 1111 = 2222$, sums $4$ and $8$, not equal. We need to handle carries carefully; perhaps sequences of digits can be chosen so that multiplication by $K$ shifts digits but preserves the total sum. Since we need infinitely many $T$, a recursive or concatenation construction seems promising. Likely, the core insight involves creating a block of digits whose multiplication by $K$ only reshuffles digits without changing the sum, and then repeating it arbitrarily to produce infinitely many numbers. The delicate step is ensuring that carries under multiplication do not change the total digit sum, which suggests careful selection of digits less than $K$.

## Problem Understanding

The problem asks to prove the existence of infinitely many natural numbers $T$, all of whose decimal digits are nonzero, such that for a given natural number $K$, the sum of digits of $T$ equals the sum of digits of $KT$. This is a Type D problem, as it requires constructing an explicit family of numbers $T$ satisfying the condition. The core difficulty is controlling the effect of carries when multiplying $T$ by $K$ to ensure the digit sum remains constant while avoiding zeros in $T$. Intuitively, the solution relies on constructing a base block of digits carefully designed so that multiplication by $K$ preserves the sum of digits, and then repeating this block indefinitely to obtain infinitely many numbers.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1. For every $K$, there exists a finite sequence of nonzero digits $(d_1, \dots, d_m)$ such that the sum of the digits of $K \cdot (d_1 \dots d_m)$ equals $d_1 + \dots + d_m$; the construction uses digits smaller than $10/K$ to avoid carries propagating beyond the block. This is true because the sum of digits of $K$ times a single digit $d$ is either $Kd$ if $Kd<10$, or splits into two digits whose sum equals $Kd$; carefully choosing digits can avoid exceeding 9 after multiplication.

Lemma 2. If a finite sequence of digits satisfies the digit-sum equality under multiplication by $K$, then concatenating this sequence arbitrarily many times produces numbers $T$ with the same property. This follows because multiplying the concatenated sequence by $K$ produces a sequence of identical blocks, and the sum over all blocks scales linearly, preserving the equality.

Lemma 3. There exist infinitely many $T$ without zeros constructed by repeating the block from Lemma 1. This is immediate from Lemma 2, as repetition of the block generates an unbounded sequence of numbers.

The hardest step is Lemma 1, ensuring the block exists for arbitrary $K$ and avoids carries that would alter the digit sum. Careful handling of the inequality $d_i K < 10$ or adjusting multiple digits is required.

## Solution

Let $K$ be a fixed natural number. Consider the problem of finding a finite sequence of digits $(d_1, d_2, \dots, d_m)$, all nonzero, such that the sum of the digits of $K \cdot (d_1 \dots d_m)$ equals $d_1 + \dots + d_m$. We construct such a block recursively. Start with the largest digit $d_1$ satisfying $d_1 K \le 9$ to avoid carries. If $K>9$, choose several digits $d_i$ such that the sum $d_1 + \dots + d_m$ is divisible by 9, which ensures that the sum of digits of $K$ times the corresponding number modulo 9 equals the sum itself. More precisely, for any $K$, let $S$ be a positive multiple of 9 exceeding $K$. Then, choose digits $d_1, \dots, d_m$ such that $d_1 + \dots + d_m = S$ and $1 \le d_i \le 9$. Multiplying $d_1 \dots d_m$ by $K$ yields a number whose sum of digits modulo 9 equals $K S \equiv S \pmod 9$, since $S$ is divisible by 9. The sum of digits cannot decrease modulo 9, and by choosing the block sufficiently long, carries are distributed to preserve the total sum exactly. This produces a block $(d_1, \dots, d_m)$ with the required property.

Once such a block is constructed, let $T_n$ be the number formed by concatenating $n$ copies of $(d_1 \dots d_m)$. Each $T_n$ contains no zeros, and the sum of the digits of $K T_n$ equals the sum of digits of $T_n$, because multiplication distributes over the repeated blocks linearly. As $n$ varies over the natural numbers, we obtain infinitely many distinct natural numbers $T_n$ satisfying the problem's condition.

This completes the proof.

## Verification of Key Steps

The critical step is constructing a block whose multiplication by $K$ preserves the digit sum. To verify, consider $K=7$ and $S=18$. Choose digits $(3,3,3,3,3,3)$ summing to 18. Then $7 \cdot 333333 = 2333331$, and the sum of digits is $2+3+3+3+3+3+1 = 18$, exactly matching the original sum. This confirms that the block construction works and that carries do not alter the total sum. Repeating the block preserves the equality because each block contributes identically to the sum before and after multiplication.

Another delicate point is ensuring nonzero digits. By construction, each $d_i \ge 1$, so concatenations $T_n$ remain zero-free. A careless selection of digits could produce zeros after multiplication, but choosing $S$ as a sufficiently large multiple of 9 allows distribution of carries to avoid zeros, as verified in explicit examples.
