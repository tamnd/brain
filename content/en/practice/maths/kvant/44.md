## Problem

Prove that for every natural number $K$ there exist infinitely many natural numbers $T$ containing no zeros in their decimal representation and such that $T$ and $KT$ have the same sum of digits.

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad (1970, Grade 10)

## Exploration

Let $s(n)$ denote the sum of the decimal digits of $n$.

The condition is $s(T)=s(KT)$. Since multiplication by $K$ usually changes the digit sum, some special structure is needed.

A standard fact is that for every integer $n$,

$$s(n)\equiv n \pmod 9.$$

Hence

$$s(KT)-s(T)\equiv (K-1)T \pmod 9.$$

If we want equality, then certainly the difference is $0$, so it is natural to force $(K-1)T$ to be divisible by $9$.

Try small examples. Let $K=2$. Taking

$$T=45,$$

gives $KT=90$, and $s(T)=9=s(KT)$. The appearance of a trailing zero in $KT$ is harmless; only $T$ must contain no zeros.

The relation between digit sum and carries is useful. When adding decimal numbers, every carry decreases the digit sum by $9$. For multiplication by powers of $10$, no carries occur and the digit sum is unchanged.

Suppose $9\mid (K-1)T$. Then

$$KT=T+(K-1)T.$$

If the addition on the right can be arranged to occur without carries, then

$$s(KT)=s(T)+s((K-1)T).$$

Since $(K-1)T$ is divisible by $9$, its digit sum is a multiple of $9$. Equality $s(KT)=s(T)$ would then require

$$s((K-1)T)=0,$$

which is impossible unless $(K-1)T=0$.

So the no-carry route is wrong. Instead, we should make the addition produce exactly enough carries to cancel the digit sum of $(K-1)T$.

A more promising idea is to choose $T$ so that $(K-1)T$ is a number consisting of a single nonzero digit followed by many zeros. Then its digit sum is small and easy to control.

Let

$$m=K-1.$$

Choose $r$ so that $m\mid 10^r$. This is impossible when $m$ has factors other than $2$ and $5$, so a different construction is needed.

The Olympiad nature of the problem suggests using repunits. Let

$$R_n=\underbrace{11\cdots 1}_{n\text{ digits}}=\frac{10^n-1}{9}.$$

If $n$ is chosen so that

$$10^n\equiv 1\pmod{9K},$$

then

$$K R_n=\frac{K(10^n-1)}9$$

is an integer whose decimal expansion can be controlled because $10^n-1$ is a string of $n$ nines.

Let

$$M=\frac{K(10^n-1)}9.$$

Then

$$R_n+M=KR_n.$$

Since $10^n-1$ is divisible by $9K$, we have

$$M=10^n-1 \quad\text{times an integer}.$$

This still does not immediately give equality of digit sums.

A more direct idea is better. Let $d$ be a multiple of $9$ with $d\ge K$. Consider

$$T=d\,R_n.$$

Its decimal representation consists of $n$ copies of the digit $d$, hence contains no zeros. Also

$$KT=(Kd)R_n.$$

If $Kd<10$, then digit sums are easy, but this cannot hold for arbitrary $K$.

The crucial observation is to choose $d$ so that $Kd$ is a multiple of $9$. Then $s(KT)$ can be computed via the representation of $KT$ as repeated blocks. The most flexible choice is to take $d=9$. Then

$$T=9R_n=10^n-1.$$

Unfortunately this contains only nines and works beautifully:

$$s(T)=9n.$$

Now

$$KT=K(10^n-1)=K10^n-K.$$

If $10^n>K$, then

$$K10^n-K=(K-1)10^n+(10^n-K).$$

The second summand has digit sum

$$s(10^n-K)=9n-s(K-1),$$

a standard complement formula. Therefore

$$s(KT)=s(K-1)+9n-s(K-1)=9n=s(T).$$

This gives infinitely many $n$ with $10^n>K$.

That appears to solve the problem completely.

## Problem Understanding

We must prove that for every natural number $K$ there exist infinitely many natural numbers $T$ whose decimal representation contains no digit $0$ and for which the sum of the digits of $T$ equals the sum of the digits of $KT$.

This is a Type D problem. We must explicitly construct infinitely many such numbers $T$ for an arbitrary fixed $K$ and verify the required property.

The core difficulty is finding a family of numbers whose digit sums remain unchanged after multiplication by an arbitrary fixed integer $K$.

The natural candidate is

$$T=10^n-1,$$

whose decimal representation consists entirely of nines. Such numbers contain no zeros, and their digit sum is immediately known.

## Proof Architecture

Let $s(N)$ denote the sum of the decimal digits of $N$.

Lemma 1. For every integer $A$ with $0<A<10^n$,

$$s(10^n-A)=9n-s(A-1).$$

Sketch: write $10^n-A=(10^n-1)-(A-1)$ and subtract digitwise from a string of $n$ nines.

Lemma 2. For every integer $K$ and every $n$ with $10^n>K$,

$$s\!\left(K(10^n-1)\right)=9n.$$

Sketch: rewrite $K(10^n-1)$ as $(K-1)10^n+(10^n-K)$ and apply Lemma 1.

Main construction. Take

$$T=10^n-1$$

for any $n$ satisfying $10^n>K$.

The step most likely to fail under scrutiny is the computation of $s(10^n-K)$ via Lemma 1.

## Solution

Let $s(N)$ denote the sum of the decimal digits of the positive integer $N$.

Fix an arbitrary natural number $K$.

For every integer $n$ such that $10^n>K$, define

$$T=10^n-1.$$

The decimal representation of $T$ consists of $n$ digits, all equal to $9$. Hence $T$ contains no zeros and

$$s(T)=9n.$$

We shall prove that

$$s(KT)=9n$$

as well.

First establish the following identity. If $0<A<10^n$, then

$$10^n-A=(10^n-1)-(A-1).$$

The number $10^n-1$ consists of $n$ digits equal to $9$. Subtracting $A-1$ from this string of nines is performed without borrowing, digit by digit. Consequently each decimal digit of $10^n-A$ is the complement to $9$ of the corresponding digit of $A-1$. Therefore

$$s(10^n-A)=9n-s(A-1).$$

Apply this with $A=K$. Since $10^n>K$,

$$s(10^n-K)=9n-s(K-1).$$

Now compute $KT$:

$$KT=K(10^n-1)=K10^n-K.$$

Since $10^n>K$,

$$K10^n-K=(K-1)10^n+(10^n-K).$$

The number $(K-1)10^n$ occupies positions strictly to the left of the digits of $10^n-K$, so no carrying occurs in this sum. Hence

$$s(KT)=s(K-1)+s(10^n-K).$$

Using the formula obtained above,

$$s(KT)=s(K-1)+\bigl(9n-s(K-1)\bigr)=9n.$$

Since $s(T)=9n$, we obtain

$$s(KT)=s(T).$$

There are infinitely many integers $n$ satisfying $10^n>K$. For each such $n$, the number

$$T=10^n-1$$

contains no zeros and satisfies

$$s(T)=s(KT).$$

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The identity

$$s(10^n-A)=9n-s(A-1)$$

depends on the absence of borrowing. Writing

$$10^n-A=(10^n-1)-(A-1),$$

the minuend has every digit equal to $9$. If a decimal digit of $A-1$ is $a_i$, the corresponding digit of the difference is $9-a_i$. Summing over all $n$ positions gives exactly

$$9n-s(A-1).$$

A careless argument using $9n-s(A)$ instead would be false. For example,

$$10^3-1=999,$$

whose digit sum is $27$, while $27-s(1)=26$.

The decomposition

$$K10^n-K=(K-1)10^n+(10^n-K)$$

is valid because

$$(K-1)10^n+(10^n-K)=K10^n-K.$$

The condition $10^n>K$ ensures that $10^n-K$ is a positive integer with at most $n$ digits. Hence its digits lie entirely to the right of the digits of $(K-1)10^n$, so no carry can occur when the two numbers are added.

Finally,

$$s(KT)=s(K-1)+s(10^n-K)$$

would fail if the digit blocks overlapped. The inequality $10^n>K$ prevents this overlap and is exactly the hypothesis needed for the digit-sum additivity.

## Alternative Approaches

The same family $T=10^n-1$ can be viewed through the congruence

$$s(N)\equiv N\pmod 9.$$

One computes

$$KT=(K-1)10^n+(10^n-K),$$

and the second summand is the $9$-complement of $K-1$ in an $n$-digit block. Its digit sum is therefore $9n-s(K-1)$. Adding the digit sum contributed by $(K-1)10^n$ yields $9n$, which equals $s(10^n-1)$.

Another approach is to write the decimal expansion of $K-1$ and construct $KT$ directly as a concatenation of the digits of $K-1$ and their complements to $9$. The resulting digit sum is automatically $9n$. This is essentially the same mechanism as the main proof, but expressed in terms of complementary digit blocks rather than the identity for $10^n-K$.
