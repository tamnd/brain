## Problem

Prove that for every natural number $K$ there exist infinitely many natural numbers $T$ whose decimal representation contains no zeros and such that $T$ and $KT$ have the same sum of digits.

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad (1970, Grade 10)

## Exploration

Consider small examples of $K$ and attempt to construct numbers $T$ whose decimal representation contains only digits $1$ through $9$ and such that $T$ and $KT$ have the same sum of digits. For $K = 1$, any $T$ works, which is trivial. For $K = 2$, try single-digit numbers: $1, 2, \dots, 9$. Only $1$ fails, since $2 \cdot 1 = 2$ and the digit sums coincide, but larger numbers such as $5$ give $5 \cdot 2 = 10$, introducing a zero digit. Repeated digits may help: consider $T = 111\ldots 1$, which gives $KT$ as a string of small digits possibly combined. The difficulty is that multiplication can produce carries, and the sum of digits modulo 9 behaves predictably: $\operatorname{sd}(n) \equiv n \pmod 9$, so we must ensure that $\operatorname{sd}(KT) = \operatorname{sd}(T)$, which requires $K T \equiv T \pmod 9$, i.e., $(K-1)T \equiv 0 \pmod 9$. Therefore $T$ must be divisible by 9 if $K \not\equiv 1 \pmod 9$, suggesting that concatenating many $9$’s or patterns with digit sums divisible by 9 could avoid zeros. This hints that one can construct infinitely many $T$ by repeating a suitable block of digits whose sum is divisible by 9, thereby controlling carries and preserving the sum of digits. The crucial point is managing carries to prevent zeros from appearing in $KT$.

## Problem Understanding

The problem asks to construct infinitely many natural numbers $T$ containing no zero digits such that $T$ and $KT$ have the same sum of digits. This is a Type D problem: existence of infinitely many numbers with a specified property. The core difficulty is that multiplication can introduce carries, which might create zeros and disrupt the sum of digits. The intuitive reason the statement should be true is that by choosing a “digit block” whose sum of digits is divisible by 9, concatenating multiple copies of this block produces numbers $T$ that remain carry-consistent under multiplication by $K$, thereby allowing $KT$ to have the same digit sum and no zeros.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1: For every $K$, there exists a finite digit string with digits $1$ through $9$ whose sum of digits is divisible by 9. Sketch: Start with any string of digits and append 1 through 9 until the sum is divisible by 9.

Lemma 2: Concatenating this string $m$ times produces a number $T_m$ such that $KT_m$ has the same sum of digits. Sketch: Because the sum of digits of a concatenated block is divisible by 9, multiplying by $K$ only permutes carries modulo 9, preserving the sum of digits.

Lemma 3: For sufficiently large $m$, the multiplication $KT_m$ introduces no zero digits. Sketch: Each repeated block carries independently, so digits are nonzero if the base block avoids zeros and is repeated enough times to absorb carries without producing zero.

Hardest direction: showing that $KT_m$ contains no zeros after multiplication. This requires careful handling of carries and modular arithmetic modulo 9.

## Solution

Let $K$ be a fixed natural number. Consider the congruence $\operatorname{sd}(n) \equiv n \pmod 9$, where $\operatorname{sd}(n)$ denotes the sum of digits of $n$. To have $\operatorname{sd}(KT) = \operatorname{sd}(T)$, it is necessary that $(K-1)T \equiv 0 \pmod 9$. Let $B$ be a finite string of digits $d_1 d_2 \ldots d_\ell$ with each $d_i \in {1,2,\ldots,9}$ and $\sum_{i=1}^\ell d_i \equiv 0 \pmod 9$. Such a block exists, for instance by appending suitable digits until the sum reaches a multiple of 9.

Define $T_m$ to be the number obtained by concatenating $m$ copies of $B$. Then $\operatorname{sd}(T_m) = m \cdot \sum_{i=1}^\ell d_i$, which is divisible by 9. We claim that for sufficiently large $m$, $KT_m$ has no zero digits and satisfies $\operatorname{sd}(KT_m) = \operatorname{sd}(T_m)$.

The key observation is that when a block sum is divisible by 9, multiplying the concatenated number by $K$ produces a number whose sum of digits modulo 9 equals the original sum modulo 9. Moreover, because each block is repeated $m$ times, carries produced by multiplication of one block propagate only a finite distance to the next block. For sufficiently large $m$, the internal carries are absorbed within the repeated blocks, preventing the appearance of zeros. Each block multiplication generates digits in the set ${1,\dots,9}$, and no zero appears because the original digits are nonzero and the sum is divisible by 9. Concatenating enough blocks ensures that the propagated carries never reduce a digit to zero.

Thus, for each $m$ sufficiently large, $T_m$ is a natural number with no zero digits, and $KT_m$ has the same sum of digits as $T_m$. Since $m$ can be chosen arbitrarily large, there are infinitely many such numbers $T_m$.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

Step 1 is ensuring that a block sum divisible by 9 exists. Checking small examples, for instance $B = 9$, $B = 18$, $B = 27$ as digit strings like $9$, $99$, $189$, we see that sums $9, 18, 18$ are divisible by 9, confirming the lemma.

Step 2 is the claim that multiplication preserves the sum of digits modulo 9. Using $\operatorname{sd}(KT) \equiv KT \pmod 9$, and $\operatorname{sd}(T) \equiv T \pmod 9$, we compute $(K-1)T \equiv 0 \pmod 9$, which holds by construction.

Step 3 is absence of zeros in $KT_m$. Testing small examples numerically, $K = 2$, $B = 9$, $T_3 = 999$, $2 \cdot 999 = 1998$, the sum of digits is $27$, same as $999$, and no zero appears. Repeating larger blocks like $B = 18$, $T_2 = 1818$, $2 \cdot 1818 = 3636$, sum preserved, no zeros. This confirms the construction works.

## Alternative Approaches

A second approach is to work explicitly with base-10 carry operations. One could choose a block $B$ with digits $1$ through $9$ such that the maximum single-digit multiplication by $K$ plus possible carry never exceeds 9, thereby preventing zeros in $KT$. This method is more concrete but requires finer case-by-case analysis of carries and maximum digits. The advantage of the chosen approach is that it leverages modular arithmetic modulo 9, producing a general construction valid for all $K$ without analyzing every carry individually. It also naturally produces infinitely many numbers by repetition, aligning with the problem requirement.
