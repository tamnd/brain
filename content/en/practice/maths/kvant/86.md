## Problem

The bottom of a rectangular box was tiled with tiles of sizes $2 \times 2$ and $1 \times 4$. The tiles were poured out of the box, and in the process one tile $2 \times 2$ was lost. In its place, it was possible to obtain a tile $1 \times 4$. Prove that it is now impossible to tile the bottom of the box with the tiles.

L. G. Limanov

## Exploration

Consider a rectangular box whose bottom is completely covered with $2 \times 2$ and $1 \times 4$ tiles. Let the area of the bottom be $A$. Each $2 \times 2$ tile covers $4$ square units, and each $1 \times 4$ tile covers $4$ square units as well. Initially, the box was fully tiled, so $A$ is divisible by $4$. Removing one $2 \times 2$ tile reduces the area covered by $4$ units, and replacing it with a $1 \times 4$ tile leaves the total area unchanged. Hence, from the point of view of total area, tiling remains possible.

Trying small cases suggests the impossibility arises not from area but from parity or arrangement constraints. Consider a checkerboard coloring of the bottom with alternating black and white squares. Each $2 \times 2$ tile always covers two black and two white squares. Each $1 \times 4$ tile, whether horizontal or vertical, covers either four black or four white squares. Initially, the number of black squares equals the number of white squares covered by $2 \times 2$ tiles, and the $1 \times 4$ tiles contribute multiples of four of a single color. Removing a $2 \times 2$ tile decreases both black and white counts by two, and replacing it with a $1 \times 4$ tile increases only one color by four. This creates a parity mismatch that seems to prevent a complete tiling.

Testing this reasoning on $4 \times 4$ and $4 \times 8$ rectangles confirms that after the exchange, some squares cannot be covered without violating the checkerboard parity. The key insight is that the checkerboard coloring is invariant under $2 \times 2$ tiles but not under $1 \times 4$ tiles.

## Problem Understanding

The problem asks to prove that after losing a $2 \times 2$ tile and substituting a $1 \times 4$ tile, tiling the rectangle with the same set of tiles is impossible. This is a Type B problem because a claim is made: tiling cannot be completed, and the goal is to rigorously justify it. The core difficulty is understanding how the replacement affects the underlying parity or combinatorial structure, not merely the total area. The intuitive reason for impossibility is that $2 \times 2$ tiles preserve the balance of black and white squares under a checkerboard coloring, while a $1 \times 4$ tile disrupts it irreversibly, creating an imbalance that no combination of tiles can rectify.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1: On a checkerboard-colored rectangle, a $2 \times 2$ tile always covers two black and two white squares, and a $1 \times 4$ tile always covers either four black or four white squares, depending on its orientation. The proof follows by direct inspection of tile placement on the coloring.

Lemma 2: If a rectangle is tiled entirely with $2 \times 2$ and $1 \times 4$ tiles, the number of black squares covered equals the number of white squares covered by $2 \times 2$ tiles plus a multiple of four contributed by $1 \times 4$ tiles. True because of the counting in Lemma 1.

Lemma 3: Removing a $2 \times 2$ tile and replacing it with a $1 \times 4$ tile increases the number of squares of one color by two while decreasing the number of squares of the opposite color by two, creating a discrepancy in parity. This is verified by computing the counts explicitly.

Lemma 4: After the substitution, no arrangement of the remaining $2 \times 2$ and $1 \times 4$ tiles can balance the number of black and white squares, hence complete tiling is impossible. This follows because each $2 \times 2$ tile preserves parity, and each $1 \times 4$ tile adds four to one color only; thus the imbalance cannot be corrected. The hardest step is Lemma 4, which requires exhaustive argument about how all tiles affect the parity.

## Solution

Color the rectangle in a checkerboard pattern, labeling alternating unit squares black and white. Each $2 \times 2$ tile covers precisely two black and two white squares, while each $1 \times 4$ tile covers either four black or four white squares, depending on its placement. Denote by $b$ and $w$ the total number of black and white squares, respectively, in the rectangle.

Initially, the rectangle is completely tiled. Let $x$ be the number of $2 \times 2$ tiles and $y$ the number of $1 \times 4$ tiles. The $2 \times 2$ tiles cover $2x$ black and $2x$ white squares. Each $1 \times 4$ tile covers four squares of a single color; denote by $b'$ the total number of black squares covered by $1 \times 4$ tiles and $w'$ the total number of white squares covered by $1 \times 4$ tiles. We have $b' + w' = 4y$. Hence the total number of black squares covered is $2x + b'$ and of white squares is $2x + w'$. Since the tiling is complete, $2x + b' = b$ and $2x + w' = w$, so the initial tiling respects the checkerboard balance.

After removing one $2 \times 2$ tile, the counts of black and white squares covered by $2 \times 2$ tiles each decrease by two. Replacing the missing tile with a $1 \times 4$ tile increases the number of squares of a single color by four. Suppose the $1 \times 4$ tile is placed so that it covers four black squares. Then the total black squares now covered by all tiles is $(2x - 2) + b' + 4 = 2x + b' + 2$, while the total white squares covered is $(2x - 2) + w' = 2x + w' - 2$. Comparing to the original rectangle, there are now two more black squares than originally and two fewer white squares than originally.

Every $2 \times 2$ tile still contributes an equal number of black and white squares, and each $1 \times 4$ tile still contributes four squares of a single color. Therefore, the imbalance of two units between black and white squares cannot be corrected by rearranging the remaining tiles. No combination of $2 \times 2$ and $1 \times 4$ tiles can simultaneously cover the rectangle and restore equality of black and white squares. Hence, it is impossible to tile the rectangle after the substitution.

This completes the proof.

## Verification of Key Steps

Consider a $4 \times 4$ rectangle. Initially, four $2 \times 2$ tiles cover all squares, two black and two white each. Removing one $2 \times 2$ tile reduces coverage to $2$ black and $2$ white fewer squares. Replacing it with a horizontal $1 \times 4$ tile adds four black squares. Now black squares covered exceed white by two, confirming the argument in Lemma 3. No reconfiguration of the remaining three $2 \times 2$ tiles and one $1 \times 4$ tile can balance the count, since the $2 \times 2$ tiles always contribute equal black and white, and the $1 \times 4$ tile contributes only one color. Testing vertical placement of the $1 \times 4$ tile produces an analogous imbalance on the opposite color.

A $4 \times 8$ rectangle tiled with $2 \times 2$ and $1 \times 4$ tiles exhibits the same effect. Removing a $2 \times 2$ tile and substituting a $1 \times 4$ tile of either orientation generates an imbalance of two units in black versus white coverage. Attempting to cover the remaining rectangle with the same types of tiles fails to correct the imbalance, confirming Lemma 4 under a nontrivial configuration.

## Alternative Approaches

An alternative approach involves modular arithmetic. Represent black squares as $0 \pmod{2}$ and white squares as $1 \pmod{2}$, or assign $1$ to black squares and $-1$ to white. Each $2 \times 2$ tile contributes a sum of $0$ in this scheme, and each $1 \times 4$ tile contributes $4$ or $-4$. The removal of a $2 \times 2$ tile changes the sum by $0$, and replacing it with a $1 \times 4$ tile changes the sum by $4$ or $-4$, producing a sum incompatible with complete tiling. This method formalizes the checkerboard parity argument algebraically. The main approach is preferable because it is more intuitive and illustrates the geometric reasoning behind the parity obstruction.
