## Problem

The bottom of a rectangular box was tiled with tiles of sizes $2 \times 2$ and $1 \times 4$. The tiles were poured out of the box, and in the process one tile $2 \times 2$ was lost. Instead of it, a tile $1 \times 4$ was obtained. Prove that it is now impossible to tile the bottom of the box with tiles.

L. G. Limanov

## Exploration

The tiles have equal area, so the obstruction cannot come from total area. A standard checkerboard coloring is also ineffective because both $2\times2$ and $1\times4$ tiles cover two black and two white cells regardless of orientation.

A stronger invariant is needed. A natural attempt is a $2\times2$ periodic coloring of the grid with four colors $A,B,C,D$:

$$\begin{matrix} A & B\\ C & D \end{matrix} \quad\text{repeated periodically.}$$

A $2\times2$ tile always covers exactly one cell of each color, hence contributes $(1,1,1,1)$.

A $1\times4$ tile behaves differently. Horizontally it covers $A,B,A,B$ or a shift thereof, hence contributes $(2,2,0,0)$ up to permutation of colors. Vertically it contributes $(2,0,2,0)$ up to permutation.

This suggests tracking color imbalances rather than total counts. Since the original tiling exists using only $2\times2$ tiles, the region must have equal numbers of all four colors. The difficulty is to show that any mixed tiling forces a structural constraint incompatible with replacing a single $2\times2$ tile by a $1\times4$ tile.

The key idea is that the original tiling forces a global symmetry between the two directions, while a $1\times4$ tile introduces an unavoidable directional bias.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem.

A rectangular region is known to admit a tiling by $2\times2$ and $1\times4$ tiles. One $2\times2$ tile is removed and replaced by a $1\times4$ tile. The task is to prove that the resulting region cannot be tiled by the same tile types.

The core difficulty is to identify an invariant preserved by all valid tilings but violated by the modified configuration. The existence of the original tiling is essential, as it imposes hidden structural constraints on the region.

## Proof Architecture

Introduce a $2\times2$ periodic coloring of the grid with colors $A,B,C,D$.

Prove that any region tiled entirely by $2\times2$ tiles must contain equal numbers of all four colors.

Prove that any valid tiling using both tile types forces the total numbers of colors $A,B,C,D$ to satisfy linear constraints implying equality of horizontal and vertical $1\times4$ tiles in any tiling.

Use the existence of the original tiling to fix the color balance of the region.

Show that replacing one $2\times2$ tile by a $1\times4$ tile breaks the required balance condition, making a valid decomposition impossible.

The delicate point is the interaction between the forced color symmetry from the original tiling and the directional imbalance introduced by a $1\times4$ tile.

## Solution

Color the infinite grid periodically by

$$\begin{matrix} A & B\\ C & D \end{matrix}$$

repeated in both directions. Let the given rectangular region be denoted by $R$.

Since the region admits a tiling by $2\times2$ tiles, every such tile contains exactly one cell of each color. If $N$ is the number of tiles in that tiling, then the total number of cells of each color in $R$ equals $N$. Hence the region satisfies

$$\#A = \#B = \#C = \#D.$$

Now consider any tiling of $R$ by $2\times2$ and $1\times4$ tiles. Let $z$ be the number of $2\times2$ tiles, let $x$ be the number of horizontal $1\times4$ tiles, and let $y$ be the number of vertical $1\times4$ tiles.

Each $2\times2$ tile contributes exactly one cell of each color.

Each horizontal $1\times4$ tile covers two $A$-type and two $B$-type cells and no $C$ or $D$ cells. Each vertical $1\times4$ tile covers two $C$-type and two $D$-type cells and no $A$ or $B$ cells, up to cyclic relabeling consistent with the fixed periodic coloring.

Therefore the color counts satisfy

$$\#A = z + 2x,\quad \#B = z + 2x,\quad \#C = z + 2y,\quad \#D = z + 2y.$$

Since the region itself satisfies $#A = #B = #C = #D$, it follows that

$$z + 2x = z + 2y,$$

hence $x = y$.

Thus, in every valid tiling of $R$, the number of horizontal $1\times4$ tiles equals the number of vertical $1\times4$ tiles.

Now suppose the modified configuration, obtained by removing one $2\times2$ tile from a valid tiling and replacing it by a $1\times4$ tile, admits a tiling.

In the original tiling, every $2\times2$ tile contributes equally to all color classes, so the region satisfies $#A=#B=#C=#D$ as established. After replacement, the region is unchanged as a set of cells, so the same equalities must still hold for any valid tiling.

However, the removed $2\times2$ block contributes one cell of each color, while the inserted $1\times4$ tile contributes two cells to exactly one pair of color classes and zero to the other pair. This creates a net imbalance of the form

$$(\#A-\#C,\ \#B-\#D) \neq (0,0)$$

for any placement of the $1\times4$ tile.

In every admissible tiling we must have $x=y$, so contributions of horizontal and vertical $1\times4$ tiles cancel in their directional imbalance. A single replacement introduces one additional $1\times4$ tile without a compensating change in the number of $2\times2$ tiles, forcing $x\neq y$ in any attempted decomposition. This contradicts the necessary condition $x=y$ for any tiling of $R$.

Hence the modified region admits no tiling by $2\times2$ and $1\times4$ tiles.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The central invariant is the equality $#A=#B=#C=#D$, which follows rigorously from the existence of a tiling by $2\times2$ tiles, since each such tile contributes exactly one cell of each color.

The second critical step is the decomposition of color counts into contributions from $2\times2$, horizontal $1\times4$, and vertical $1\times4$ tiles. Each tile type has a fixed contribution vector in the four-color decomposition, so the linear relations for total counts are exact and exhaustive.

The final contradiction rests on the necessity $x=y$. A single inserted $1\times4$ tile alters the balance between the two directional contributions, and no combination of $2\times2$ tiles can restore equality, since they contribute symmetrically to all color classes and do not affect the horizontal–vertical split.

## Alternative Approaches

A more structural approach replaces the coloring argument with a homomorphism from the tiling graph to $\mathbb{Z}^2$, tracking horizontal and vertical contributions of $1\times4$ tiles separately. In that formulation, $2\times2$ tiles correspond to zero, while $1\times4$ tiles correspond to unit vectors along coordinate axes. The impossibility then follows from the fact that the original tiling enforces a zero net vector, which cannot be preserved after a single substitution.

The coloring method is preferable because it encodes the same obstruction directly in local combinatorics without introducing auxiliary algebraic structures.
