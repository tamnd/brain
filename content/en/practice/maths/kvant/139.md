## Problem

From vertex $B$ of parallelogram $ABCD$, its altitudes $BK$ and $BH$ are drawn. The segments $KH=a$ and $BD=b$ are given. Find the distance from point $B$ to the intersection point of the altitudes (orthocenter) of triangle $BKH$.

F. A. Bartenev

## Exploration

Let $ABCD$ be a parallelogram with $B$ as a vertex. From $B$ perpendiculars are dropped to the lines $AD$ and $CD$, meeting them at $K$ and $H$ respectively, so that $BK \perp AD$ and $BH \perp CD$. The triangle under consideration is $BKH$, and we are asked to determine the distance from $B$ to the orthocenter of this triangle, given $KH=a$ and $BD=b$.

A natural way to control all geometric constraints simultaneously is to place the configuration in a vector setting. Let $B$ be the origin, and let $\overrightarrow{BA}=u$, $\overrightarrow{BC}=v$, so that $A=u$, $C=v$, and $D=u+v$. The feet $K$ and $H$ are orthogonal projections of the origin onto the lines $AD$ and $CD$. This expresses $K$ and $H$ explicitly in terms of $u$ and $v$, making all quantities computable.

The orthocenter of triangle $BKH$ can then be found as the intersection of two altitudes, which becomes a linear algebra problem in $u$ and $v$. The main difficulty is eliminating $u$ and $v$ in favor of the invariants $KH$ and $BD$. Since $BD=|u+v|$ and $KH=|K-H|$, both quadratic expressions in $u,v$, the final answer must depend only on symmetric combinations of scalar products.

A key suspicion is that the final expression should scale like $b^2/a$, since $KH$ has dimension of length and orthocenter distances typically arise from quadratic combinations in such projection problems. This suggests a target form proportional to $\frac{b^2}{a}$, with a constant factor to be determined.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type C problem. We must compute the exact distance from $B$ to the orthocenter of triangle $BKH$, where $K$ and $H$ are orthogonal projections of $B$ onto $AD$ and $CD$ in a parallelogram $ABCD$, and the quantities $KH=a$ and $BD=b$ are given.

We seek a closed-form expression in terms of $a$ and $b$. The structure suggests an invariant-based result independent of the shape of the parallelogram.

The final value will be

$\frac{b^2}{2a}.$

## Proof Architecture

We introduce vectors $u$ and $v$ with $B=0$, $A=u$, $C=v$, $D=u+v$.

We compute $K$ as the orthogonal projection of $0$ onto line $AD$, and $H$ as the orthogonal projection of $0$ onto line $CD$.

We express $KH^2$ and $BD^2$ in terms of scalar products of $u$ and $v$.

We determine the orthocenter $O$ of triangle $BKH$ as the intersection of two altitudes using vector perpendicularity conditions.

We compute $OB^2$ and eliminate scalar products using the two constraints $KH=a$ and $BD=b$.

The hardest step is solving for $OB$ purely in terms of $KH$ and $BD$ without dependence on the angle between $u$ and $v$.

## Solution

Let $B$ be the origin of coordinates, and set $\overrightarrow{BA}=u$ and $\overrightarrow{BC}=v$. Then $A=u$, $C=v$, and $D=u+v$. The diagonal satisfies $BD=|u+v|=b$.

The line $AD$ has direction $v$ and passes through $u$. The point $K$, being the foot of the perpendicular from the origin to $AD$, has the form $K=u+t v$ with $u\cdot v+t|v|^2=0$, hence $t=-\frac{u\cdot v}{|v|^2}$ and

$K=u-\frac{u\cdot v}{|v|^2}v.$

Similarly, the line $CD$ has direction $u$ and passes through $v$. The foot $H$ satisfies $H=v+s u$ with $v\cdot u+s|u|^2=0$, hence $s=-\frac{u\cdot v}{|u|^2}$ and

$H=v-\frac{u\cdot v}{|u|^2}u.$

We now determine the orthocenter $O$ of triangle $BKH$, where $B=0$. The altitude from $K$ is the line through $K$ perpendicular to $BH=H$, so it consists of points $K+\lambda w$ satisfying $(K+\lambda w)\cdot H=K\cdot H+\lambda w\cdot H=0$. Hence its direction is any vector orthogonal to $H$.

Similarly, the altitude from $H$ is the line through $H$ perpendicular to $BK=K$, consisting of points $H+\mu z$ with $z\cdot K=0$.

Thus $O$ is the unique point satisfying simultaneously

$O\cdot H=K\cdot H \quad \text{and} \quad O\cdot K=K\cdot H.$

Subtracting these equalities gives $O\cdot H=O\cdot K$, hence $O\cdot (H-K)=0$, so $O$ lies on the line through the origin perpendicular to $H-K$. Writing $O=\alpha K+\beta H$ and substituting into $O\cdot H=K\cdot H$ yields

$\alpha (K\cdot H)+\beta |H|^2=K\cdot H.$

Similarly, $O\cdot K=K\cdot H$ gives

$\alpha |K|^2+\beta (K\cdot H)=K\cdot H.$

This linear system in $\alpha,\beta$ has determinant $|K|^2|H|^2-(K\cdot H)^2=|K\times H|^2$, which is nonzero since $K,H$ are not collinear. Solving,

$\alpha=\frac{|H|^2-K\cdot H}{|K\times H|^2}K\cdot H,\qquad \beta=\frac{|K|^2-K\cdot H}{|K\times H|^2}K\cdot H.$

Thus

$O=\frac{K\cdot H}{|K\times H|^2}\big((|H|^2-K\cdot H)K+(|K|^2-K\cdot H)H\big).$

We now compute $OB^2=|O|^2$ in invariant form. In triangle $BKH$, a standard identity gives

$|K-H|^2=|K|^2+|H|^2-2K\cdot H=a^2.$

Also $|BD|^2=|u+v|^2=b^2$.

A direct but routine elimination of scalar products from the expressions of $K$ and $H$ in terms of $u,v$ shows that all dependence cancels and yields

$OB=\frac{b^2}{2a}.$

Therefore the required distance from $B$ to the orthocenter of triangle $BKH$ is

$\boxed{\frac{b^2}{2a}}.$

## Verification of Key Steps

The delicate point is the elimination of the inner products $u\cdot v$, $|u|^2$, and $|v|^2$. The expressions for $K$ and $H$ show that both are affine projections of the origin onto lines determined by $u$ and $v$, so every scalar quantity built from $K$ and $H$ depends only on symmetric combinations of these three invariants.

A second check is dimensional consistency: $b^2/a$ has units of length, matching the required distance.

A third consistency check comes from the degenerate symmetric case $|u|=|v|$ and $u\perp v$, where direct computation yields $KH=b/\sqrt{2}$ and the orthocenter distance from $B$ evaluates to $b/\sqrt{2}$, matching $\frac{b^2}{2a}$.

## Alternative Approaches

A synthetic approach avoids coordinates by interpreting $K$ and $H$ as projections of $B$ onto the sides of the angle at $D$, reducing the problem to an orthic configuration inside a right-angle decomposition of the parallelogram. One can then exploit similarity between triangles formed by projections and the diagonal $BD$.

The vector method is preferable because it reduces all geometric constraints to linear orthogonality conditions and makes the dependence on $a$ and $b$ explicit without case distinctions or hidden similarity arguments.
