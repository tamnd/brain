## Problem

What is the locus of points occupied by the centroids of triangles whose three vertices lie respectively on the three sides $AB$, $BC$ and $AC$ of the given triangle $ABC$?

L. G. Makarov

## Exploration

Let points $X \in AB$, $Y \in BC$, $Z \in AC$ be written in affine form

$$X = A + t(B-A), \quad Y = B + u(C-B), \quad Z = C + v(A-C),$$

where $t,u,v \in [0,1]$. The centroid is

$$G = \frac{X+Y+Z}{3}.$$

Expanding gives a linear dependence of $G$ on the parameters $t,u,v$, hence the locus is the image of the unit cube under an affine map into the plane of $ABC$. Such an image is a convex polygon whose vertices occur at extreme values $t,u,v \in {0,1}$.

Computing these extreme images yields six distinct points, suggesting a hexagon. Each vertex corresponds to a choice where two variables are $1$ and one is $0$, or permutations thereof, producing barycentric coordinates of the form $\frac{2A+B}{3}$ and its cyclic variants.

The key step is verifying that no additional boundary arises beyond these six points and that the entire locus is exactly the convex hull of them.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem. We are to determine the locus of the centroid $G$ of a triangle whose vertices move independently along the sides of a fixed triangle $ABC$.

The structure suggests an affine image of a cube, hence a convex polygon in the plane. The expected answer is a hexagon whose vertices divide the sides of $ABC$ in the ratio $2:1$.

## Proof Architecture

First, we express $X,Y,Z$ in barycentric form relative to $ABC$ and derive a barycentric expression for $G$.

Second, we determine the image of the parameter cube $[0,1]^3$ under the affine map $(t,u,v) \mapsto G$, showing that it is convex.

Third, we identify all extreme points by analyzing when $(t,u,v)$ lies in ${0,1}^3$ and show that exactly six distinct images arise.

Fourth, we prove that these six points are the vertices of the locus and that every admissible centroid lies inside their convex hull.

The most delicate point is proving that no additional extremal configurations exist beyond the boundary values of $t,u,v$.

## Solution

Let $X \in AB$, $Y \in BC$, $Z \in AC$ be parameterized as

$$X = (1-t)A + tB, \quad Y = (1-u)B + uC, \quad Z = (1-v)C + vA,$$

where $t,u,v \in [0,1]$.

Then

$$X+Y+Z = (1-t)A + tB + (1-u)B + uC + (1-v)C + vA.$$

Grouping coefficients gives

$$X+Y+Z = (1-t+v)A + (t+1-u)B + (u+1-v)C.$$

Hence the centroid $G$ satisfies

$$G = \frac{1}{3}\Big((1-t+v)A + (t+1-u)B + (u+1-v)C\Big).$$

Thus $G$ has barycentric coordinates

$$\alpha = \frac{1-t+v}{3}, \quad \beta = \frac{t+1-u}{3}, \quad \gamma = \frac{u+1-v}{3},$$

with $\alpha+\beta+\gamma=1$.

The mapping $(t,u,v) \mapsto G$ is affine, so the image of the cube $[0,1]^3$ is a convex polytope in the plane. Its vertices occur among images of the vertices of the cube, i.e. among points with $t,u,v \in {0,1}$.

We compute these images.

For $(t,u,v)=(0,0,0)$ and $(1,1,1)$ we obtain

$$G = \frac{A+B+C}{3}.$$

For $(1,0,0)$,

$$(\alpha,\beta,\gamma)=\left(0,\frac{2}{3},\frac{1}{3}\right), \quad G=\frac{2B+C}{3}.$$

For $(0,1,0)$,

$$G=\frac{A+2C}{3}.$$

For $(0,0,1)$,

$$G=\frac{2A+B}{3}.$$

For $(1,1,0)$,

$$G=\frac{B+2C}{3}.$$

For $(1,0,1)$,

$$G=\frac{A+2B}{3}.$$

For $(0,1,1)$,

$$G=\frac{2A+C}{3}.$$

These six points are distinct and lie on the sides of the triangle $ABC$-related affine structure: each is obtained by taking one vertex with weight $\frac{2}{3}$ and another with weight $\frac{1}{3}$.

Since the image of a cube under an affine map is convex and its extreme points are contained among images of cube vertices, the locus is exactly the convex hull of these six points.

Therefore the locus is a convex hexagon with vertices

$$\frac{2A+B}{3}, \quad \frac{A+2B}{3}, \quad \frac{2B+C}{3}, \quad \frac{B+2C}{3}, \quad \frac{2C+A}{3}, \quad \frac{C+2A}{3}.$$

Hence the locus is the hexagon formed by points dividing the sides of $ABC$ in the ratio $2:1$ taken cyclically.

$$\boxed{\text{A convex hexagon with vertices } \frac{2A+B}{3}, \frac{A+2B}{3}, \frac{2B+C}{3}, \frac{B+2C}{3}, \frac{2C+A}{3}, \frac{C+2A}{3}}$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The crucial step is the claim that no new boundary points arise from interior values of $t,u,v$. This follows because $G$ is an affine function of $(t,u,v)$, hence its image of the convex cube $[0,1]^3$ is convex and its extreme points occur among images of extreme points of the cube. Any interior point of the cube is a convex combination of vertices, so its image is a convex combination of the corresponding images, preventing it from lying on the boundary outside the hull of vertex images.

A second delicate point is the distinctness of the six non-central images. Each corresponds to a barycentric triple with exactly one zero coordinate and two nonzero coordinates, which cannot coincide with any other such permutation since the zero coordinate determines the vertex opposite to the side where the centroid lies.

## Alternative Approaches

A synthetic approach avoids coordinates by interpreting $G$ as the image of the map sending $(X,Y,Z)$ to their barycentric average. Each coordinate varies independently along a segment, and the centroid map preserves affine structure, implying the locus is the Minkowski sum of three segments scaled by $\frac{1}{3}$. This directly yields a hexagon as the sum of three directed segments in the plane. The affine-coordinate method used above is preferable because it makes the extremal structure explicit and eliminates ambiguity about the boundary.
