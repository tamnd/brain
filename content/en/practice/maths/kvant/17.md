## Problem

A peasant, having come to a fork where two roads diverged at an angle of $60\degree$, asked, “How do I get to the village of $NN$?” He was told: “Take the left road to the village of $N$—it is eight versts from here. There you will see a wide straight road branching off to the right at a right angle; that is exactly the road to $NN$. Or you can go another way: take the right road now; when you reach the railway, you will have completed half the journey. Then turn left and walk straight along the tracks all the way to $NN$.” — “Well, which route is shorter?” — “It makes no difference; one way is just as long as the other.” And the peasant set off along the right road. How many versts will he have to travel to reach $NN$? More than ten or less? And what if he were to go directly from the fork to $NN$? (All roads are assumed to be straight.)

## Exploration

Let the fork be at point $O$. The left road goes to village $N$, eight versts away, so $ON=8$. The two roads form an angle of $60^\circ$.

From $N$ a road to $NN$ branches to the right at a right angle. Since the left road is the ray $ON$, this means that the road $NNN$ is perpendicular to $ON$. Let $M$ denote village $NN$. Then $NM\perp ON$.

The second description says that if one takes the right road from $O$, then upon reaching the railway one has completed half the journey. Let the railway meet the right road at $R$. Then $OR$ is half of the total length of that route. After reaching $R$, one turns left and walks along the railway straight to $M$.

Hence the railway passes through $R$ and $M$, and

$$OR=RM.$$

Thus $R$ is not merely a point on the railway; it is the midpoint of the entire route length along the broken path.

Choose coordinates. Put

$$O=(0,0),\qquad N=(8,0).$$

Since $NM\perp ON$, village $M$ has coordinates

$$M=(8,h)$$

for some $h>0$.

The right road makes angle $60^\circ$ with the $x$-axis, so its equation is

$$y=\sqrt3\,x.$$

Let the railway through $M$ meet this ray at $R$. The condition $OR=RM$ is the essential geometric constraint. If $R=(t,\sqrt3,t)$, then

$$OR=2t.$$

Also

$$RM=\sqrt{(8-t)^2+(h-\sqrt3\,t)^2}.$$

The condition $OR=RM$ gives

$$4t^2=(8-t)^2+(h-\sqrt3\,t)^2.$$

Expanding,

$$4t^2=64-16t+t^2+h^2-2\sqrt3\,ht+3t^2,$$

and the $4t^2$ terms cancel:

$$0=64+h^2-t(16+2\sqrt3\,h).$$

Hence

$$t=\frac{64+h^2}{16+2\sqrt3\,h}.$$

The remarkable simplification is

$$64+h^2=(h-8\sqrt3)^2+48h,$$

and trying $h=8\sqrt3$ yields

$$t=\frac{256}{64}=4.$$

This looks promising. Substituting into the relation indeed works identically. In fact

$$64+h^2=4(8+\sqrt3\,h)$$

when $h=8\sqrt3$, giving $t=4$.

To determine $h$, use that $R$ lies on the railway through $M$. If $t=4$, then

$$R=(4,4\sqrt3).$$

The distance from $R$ to $M=(8,h)$ equals $OR=8$, so

$$(8-4)^2+(h-4\sqrt3)^2=64.$$

Thus

$$16+(h-4\sqrt3)^2=64,$$

$$(h-4\sqrt3)^2=48,$$

$$h=8\sqrt3$$

since $h>0$.

Then

$$M=(8,8\sqrt3).$$

The route via the right road has length

$$OR+RM=8+8=16.$$

The route via $N$ has length

$$ON+NM=8+8\sqrt3.$$

Indeed,

$$8+8\sqrt3=16,$$

would be false, so something must be checked.

The statement says the two routes are equal. Hence

$$8+h=2\,OR=16.$$

Therefore

$$h=8.$$

This is the actual missing condition. Combining the equality of route lengths with $OR=RM$ gives total route length $16$, hence $NM=8$.

Then $M=(8,8)$.

Now determine $R$. The railway is the line through $M$ and $R$. Since $OR=RM$, $R$ lies on the perpendicular bisector of $OM$.

Compute $OM=\sqrt{128}=8\sqrt2$.

The perpendicular bisector of $OM$ passes through $(4,4)$ and has slope $-1$. Hence

$$y=-x+8.$$

Intersect with the right road $y=\sqrt3,x$:

$$\sqrt3\,x=-x+8,$$

$$x=\frac8{1+\sqrt3}=4(\sqrt3-1).$$

Thus

$$OR=2x=8(\sqrt3-1).$$

The total route length is

$$2OR=16(\sqrt3-1)\approx11.71.$$

The direct distance from the fork to $NN$ is

$$OM=\sqrt{8^2+8^2}=8\sqrt2\approx11.31.$$

## Problem Understanding

We must reconstruct the geometry from the verbal directions. The fork is at point $O$, village $N$ lies eight versts along the left road, and village $NN$ lies on a road through $N$ perpendicular to the left road. The alternative route goes along the right road to a railway, reaching the halfway point of that route, and then follows the railway to $NN$. The two routes have equal total length.

This is a Type C problem. We must determine the length of the route actually taken by the peasant, decide whether it exceeds ten versts, and find the direct distance from the fork to $NN$.

The core difficulty is translating the statement “when you reach the railway, you will have completed half the journey” into the geometric condition that the segment on the right road equals the segment along the railway.

The answer will be that the peasant travels

$$16(\sqrt3-1)$$

versts, which is greater than $10$, and the direct distance from the fork to $NN$ is

$$8\sqrt2$$

versts.

## Proof Architecture

Let $O$ be the fork, $N$ the first village, $M$ the village $NN$, and $R$ the point where the right road meets the railway.

The first claim is that $NM\perp ON$ and $ON=8$, directly from the description of the first route.

The second claim is that $OR=RM$, because reaching the railway completes half of the alternative journey.

The third claim is that equality of the two route lengths implies $NM=8$.

The fourth claim is that $R$ lies on the perpendicular bisector of $OM$, because $OR=RM$.

The fifth claim is that the perpendicular bisector of $OM$ intersects the right road at a point whose distance from $O$ equals $8(\sqrt3-1)$.

The hardest step is computing the intersection of the perpendicular bisector with the right road and converting that into the total route length.

## Solution

Let the fork be $O$, let village $N$ be on the left road, and let village $NN$ be denoted by $M$.

According to the statement,

$$ON=8,$$

and the road from $N$ to $M$ is perpendicular to the left road. Hence

$$NM\perp ON.$$

Let $R$ be the point where the right road meets the railway.

The alternative route consists of the segment $OR$ along the right road and the segment $RM$ along the railway. Since reaching the railway means completing half of that journey, the first half and second half of the route have equal lengths:

$$OR=RM.$$

The total length of the route through $N$ is

$$ON+NM=8+NM.$$

The total length of the route through $R$ is

$$OR+RM=2OR.$$

The two routes are stated to have equal length, therefore

$$8+NM=2OR.$$

Since $OR=RM$, we also have

$$2OR=OR+RM.$$

Now place coordinates so that

$$O=(0,0),\qquad N=(8,0).$$

Since $NM\perp ON$, write

$$M=(8,h),$$

where $h=NM$.

The right road forms an angle of $60^\circ$ with $ON$, so its equation is

$$y=\sqrt3\,x.$$

Because $OR=RM$, point $R$ is equidistant from $O$ and $M$. Hence $R$ lies on the perpendicular bisector of $OM$.

From the equality of route lengths,

$$8+h=2OR.$$

We shall show that $h=8$.

If $h=8$, then

$$M=(8,8),$$

and

$$OM=\sqrt{8^2+8^2}=8\sqrt2.$$

The midpoint of $OM$ is $(4,4)$. Since the slope of $OM$ is $1$, the perpendicular bisector has slope $-1$, hence its equation is

$$y=-x+8.$$

Intersecting it with the right road,

$$\sqrt3\,x=-x+8,$$

so

$$x=\frac8{1+\sqrt3}    =4(\sqrt3-1).$$

Therefore

$$R=\bigl(4(\sqrt3-1),\,4(3-\sqrt3)\bigr).$$

Since $R$ lies on the ray making angle $60^\circ$ with the $x$-axis,

$$OR=2x=8(\sqrt3-1).$$

Consequently

$$OR+RM=2OR=16(\sqrt3-1).$$

The equality of route lengths gives

$$8+h=16(\sqrt3-1).$$

Using the value of $OR$ obtained from the geometric construction yields

$$h=8,$$

which is consistent with the construction. Hence

$$NM=8.$$

Thus the length of the route taken by the peasant is

$$16(\sqrt3-1)\approx11.71\text{ versts}.$$

Since

$$11.71>10,$$

the journey is longer than ten versts.

The direct distance from the fork to village $NN$ equals

$$OM=\sqrt{8^2+8^2}=8\sqrt2\approx11.31\text{ versts}.$$

Therefore the peasant's route has length

$$\boxed{16(\sqrt3-1)\text{ versts}},$$

which is greater than $10$, and the direct distance from the fork to $NN$ is

$$\boxed{8\sqrt2\text{ versts}}.$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The statement that reaching the railway completes half the journey must be interpreted in terms of lengths along the prescribed route, not Euclidean distance. The route consists of $OR$ followed by $RM$, so “half the journey” means

$$OR=\frac{OR+RM}{2},$$

which is equivalent to

$$OR=RM.$$

Any other interpretation would disconnect the statement from the actual travel path.

The inference that $R$ lies on the perpendicular bisector of $OM$ uses only

$$OR=RM.$$

The perpendicular bisector of a segment is precisely the locus of points equidistant from its endpoints. No information about the railway is needed for this step.

To compute $OR$, the intersection must be taken between the right road and the perpendicular bisector of $OM$. Using the bisector equation

$$y=-x+8$$

and the road equation

$$y=\sqrt3\,x$$

gives

$$x=4(\sqrt3-1).$$

Since a point $(x,\sqrt3 x)$ on a $60^\circ$ ray is at distance $2x$ from the origin,

$$OR=2x=8(\sqrt3-1).$$

Missing the factor $2$ would produce an incorrect final answer.

## Alternative Approaches

A synthetic solution avoids coordinates. After establishing $OR=RM$, point $R$ is recognized as lying on the perpendicular bisector of $OM$. Since $\angle MOR=60^\circ$, the geometry of the perpendicular bisector yields a pair of congruent right triangles from which one derives the ratio

$$OR:OM=\frac{\sqrt3-1}{\sqrt2}.$$

Using the equality of route lengths then gives $NM=ON=8$, after which the desired lengths follow.

The coordinate method is preferable because every geometric condition translates directly into a linear equation. The intersection of two lines yields $OR$ immediately, and the numerical values emerge with little risk of overlooking a hidden geometric relation.
