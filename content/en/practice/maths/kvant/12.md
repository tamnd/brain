## Problem

Which quadrilaterals can be cut by a straight line into two quadrilaterals similar to each other?

## Exploration

A line cuts a quadrilateral into two quadrilaterals only if it meets two opposite sides. If it met adjacent sides, one of the pieces would be a triangle. Thus the cutting line joins a point of one side to a point of the opposite side.

Suppose the two resulting quadrilaterals are similar. Each of them has one side lying on the cutting line. In the two pieces, these sides are subsegments of the same straight line. Under a similarity between the pieces, a side must correspond to a side. Since both pieces possess a distinguished side whose adjacent vertices lie on the original boundary while the side itself lies entirely in the interior, these two sides must correspond to one another.

Let the cutting segment be $EF$. If the similarity ratio is $k$, then the side $EF$ of the first quadrilateral corresponds to the side $EF$ of the second. Hence

$$EF=k\,EF.$$

Since $EF\ne0$, we obtain $k=1$. Thus the two quadrilaterals are not merely similar but congruent.

This strongly suggests that the original quadrilateral must possess a symmetry exchanging the two pieces. A natural candidate is a centrally symmetric quadrilateral, that is, a parallelogram. Indeed, any line through the center of a parallelogram joining opposite sides cuts it into two congruent quadrililaterals.

The crucial point is to prove the converse: if a quadrilateral is cut into two similar quadrilaterals, then it must be a parallelogram.

## Problem Understanding

We must determine all quadrilaterals for which there exists a straight line cutting the quadrilateral into two quadrilaterals similar to each other.

This is a Type A problem. We must classify all such quadrilaterals and prove both necessity and sufficiency.

The main difficulty is showing that similarity already forces the two pieces to be congruent, and that the existence of such a congruent decomposition implies that the original quadrilateral is a parallelogram.

The expected answer is that the quadrilateral must be a parallelogram. Intuitively, the cutting segment appears in both pieces and has the same length in each; a similarity sending one piece to the other therefore has scale factor $1$, giving congruent pieces. Congruent pieces on opposite sides of the same segment suggest a half-turn symmetry, which is exactly the symmetry of a parallelogram.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1. A straight line that cuts a quadrilateral into two quadrilaterals must intersect two opposite sides of the quadrilateral.

Sketch. Intersecting adjacent sides would produce a triangular piece.

Lemma 2. If a quadrilateral is cut into two similar quadrilaterals by a segment $EF$, then the similarity ratio equals $1$, so the two quadrilaterals are congruent.

Sketch. The side $EF$ of one piece corresponds to the side $EF$ of the other, forcing equality of scale.

Lemma 3. Let a quadrilateral $ABCD$ be cut by a segment $EF$, with $E\in AB$ and $F\in CD$, into two congruent quadrilaterals. Then $AE=CF$ and $BE=DF$.

Sketch. In the congruent quadrilaterals, the common side $EF$ corresponds to itself; comparing the remaining boundary sides yields the stated equalities.

Lemma 4. The equalities $AE=CF$ and $BE=DF$ imply $AB=CD$, and similarly the congruence gives $AD=BC$.

Sketch. Add the equal lengths on each side.

Lemma 5. A quadrilateral with $AB=CD$ and $BC=AD$ is a parallelogram.

Sketch. Consider diagonal $AC$; triangles $ABC$ and $CDA$ are congruent by SSS, yielding parallel opposite sides.

The hardest direction is proving necessity. The most delicate point is Lemma 2, where one must justify that the common cutting segment corresponds to itself under the similarity.

## Solution

Let $ABCD$ be a quadrilateral that can be cut by a straight line into two similar quadrilaterals.

By Lemma 1, the cutting line intersects two opposite sides of $ABCD$. After relabeling if necessary, let the line meet sides $AB$ and $CD$ at points $E$ and $F$, respectively. The quadrilateral is thus divided into the quadrilaterals

$$Q_1=AEDF,\qquad Q_2=EBCF.$$

Since $EF$ lies in the interior of the original quadrilateral, each of $Q_1$ and $Q_2$ has exactly one side that is not contained in the boundary of $ABCD$, namely the side $EF$.

Let a similarity map $Q_1$ onto $Q_2$. The image of the unique interior side of $Q_1$ must be the unique interior side of $Q_2$. Hence the side $EF$ corresponds to the side $EF$.

If the similarity ratio is $k$, corresponding sides satisfy

$$EF=k\,EF.$$

Because $EF>0$, we obtain $k=1$. Therefore the similarity is an isometry, and $Q_1$ and $Q_2$ are congruent.

Now compare the sides of the congruent quadrilaterals. Their common side $EF$ corresponds to itself. Consequently the sides adjacent to $E$ and $F$ correspond pairwise, giving

$$AE=CF,\qquad DF=BE.$$

Adding these equalities yields

$$AB=AE+BE=CF+DF=CD.$$

The remaining pair of corresponding outer sides gives

$$AD=BC.$$

Thus the original quadrilateral satisfies

$$AB=CD,\qquad BC=AD.$$

Consider triangles $ABC$ and $CDA$. We have

$$AB=CD,\qquad BC=DA,\qquad AC=AC.$$

Hence the triangles are congruent by SSS.

From the congruence,

$$\angle BAC=\angle DCA, \qquad \angle BCA=\angle DAC.$$

These equalities imply

$$AB\parallel CD, \qquad BC\parallel AD.$$

Therefore $ABCD$ is a parallelogram.

This proves necessity.

Conversely, let $ABCD$ be a parallelogram, and let $O$ be the intersection point of its diagonals. Choose any line through $O$ meeting sides $AB$ and $CD$ at points $E$ and $F$. The segment $EF$ cuts the parallelogram into two quadrilaterals.

A half-turn about $O$ sends the parallelogram to itself. Since $O$ is the midpoint of both diagonals and also the midpoint of $EF$, this half-turn exchanges the two quadrilaterals determined by $EF$. Hence those quadrilaterals are congruent, and therefore similar.

Thus every parallelogram admits such a cut.

We have shown that a quadrilateral can be cut by a straight line into two similar quadrilaterals if and only if it is a parallelogram.

$$\boxed{\text{All and only parallelograms}}$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate point is the claim that the common side $EF$ must correspond to itself under the similarity. Each resulting quadrilateral has exactly one side not belonging to the boundary of the original quadrilateral. That side is $EF$. Any similarity between the pieces preserves the incidence structure of the boundary, so the unique interior side of one piece must map to the unique interior side of the other. Hence $EF$ corresponds to $EF$.

The second delicate point is deriving congruence from similarity. Once $EF$ corresponds to $EF$, the similarity ratio $k$ satisfies $EF=kEF$. Since $EF\neq0$, division by $EF$ gives $k=1$. No nontrivial scaling is possible.

The third delicate point is the deduction that $AB=CD$ and $AD=BC$ force a parallelogram. From these equalities, triangles $ABC$ and $CDA$ have three corresponding equal sides. Their SSS congruence yields equal alternate interior angles along diagonal $AC$, namely $\angle BAC=\angle DCA$ and $\angle BCA=\angle DAC$. These angle equalities imply both pairs of opposite sides are parallel, which is exactly the definition of a parallelogram.

## Alternative Approaches

After proving that the two pieces are congruent, one may construct the isometry carrying one piece onto the other. Because the common side $EF$ is fixed and the pieces lie on opposite sides of it, the isometry cannot be a translation or a reflection preserving each side separately. It is a half-turn exchanging the endpoints of the corresponding boundary chains. The midpoint of $EF$ becomes a center of symmetry of the entire quadrilateral. A quadrilateral possessing a center of symmetry is a parallelogram.

The presented solution is preferable because it avoids classifying plane isometries. After obtaining congruence, it uses only elementary side equalities and a standard SSS argument, keeping the proof entirely within classical Euclidean geometry.
