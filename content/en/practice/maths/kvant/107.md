## Problem

Insert illustration

a) A convex polygon $A_1A_2\ldots A_n$ is given (Fig. 1). On the side $A_1A_2$, points $B_1$ and $D_2$ are taken; on the side $A_2A_3$ — points $B_2$ and $D_3$; ..., on the side $A_nA_1$, points $B_n$ and $D_1$ are taken such that if the parallelograms $A_1B_1C_1D_1$, $A_2B_2C_2D_2$, ..., $A_nB_nC_nD_n$ are constructed, then the lines $A_1C_1$, $A_2C_2$, ..., $A_nC_n$ intersect at a single point. Prove that $$A_1B_1\cdot A_2B_2\cdot\ldots\cdot A_nB_n=A_1D_1\cdot A_2D_2\cdot\ldots\cdot A_nD_n.$$.

b) Prove that the converse is also true for a triangle: let points $B_1$ and $D_2$ be chosen on the side $A_1A_2$, points $B_2$ and $D_3$ on the side $A_2A_3$, and points $B_3$ and $D_1$ on the side $A_3A_1$ such that $$A_1B_1\cdot A_2B_2\cdot A_3B_3=A_1D_1\cdot A_2D_2\cdot A_3D_3;$$; then, if the parallelograms $A_1B_1C_1D_1$, $A_2B_2C_2D_2$, $A_3B_3C_3D_3$ are constructed, the lines $A_1C_1$, $A_2C_2$, and $A_3C_3$ intersect at a single point.

V. L. Gutenmacher

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad for School Students (V)

## Exploration

For each vertex $A_i$, the points $B_i$ and $D_i$ lie on the two sides adjacent to $A_i$. Since $A_iB_iC_iD_i$ is a parallelogram, the diagonal $A_iC_i$ has direction

$$\overrightarrow{A_iC_i} = \overrightarrow{A_iB_i} + \overrightarrow{A_iD_i}.$$

The concurrency condition therefore translates into a relation among the vectors along the sides of the polygon.

Introduce parameters

$$\lambda_i=\frac{A_iB_i}{A_iA_{i+1}},\qquad \mu_i=\frac{A_iD_i}{A_iA_{i-1}},$$

with indices taken modulo $n$. Then

$$\overrightarrow{A_iB_i} =\lambda_i\overrightarrow{A_iA_{i+1}}, \qquad \overrightarrow{A_iD_i} =\mu_i\overrightarrow{A_iA_{i-1}}.$$

If the common point of the lines $A_iC_i$ is $P$, write

$$P=A_i+t_i\bigl(\overrightarrow{A_iB_i}+\overrightarrow{A_iD_i}\bigr).$$

Subtracting the expressions for $P$ obtained from consecutive vertices should yield a linear relation between $\lambda_i,\mu_i$ and $t_i$. The natural guess is that the ratios $t_i$ satisfy a recurrence whose product around the cycle forces

$$\prod\lambda_i=\prod\mu_i.$$

For the converse in the triangular case, the condition

$$\lambda_1\lambda_2\lambda_3 = \mu_1\mu_2\mu_3$$

resembles Ceva's theorem. The diagonals of the parallelograms should become cevians of the triangle after expressing their intersections with the opposite sides. The crucial point is to identify the point where $A_iC_i$ meets the opposite side and compute the corresponding division ratio.

That is the step most likely to hide an error, because an incorrect ratio would destroy the application of Ceva.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem.

In part (a), a convex $n$-gon is given. On each side two auxiliary points are chosen, and from them a parallelogram is constructed at every vertex. Assuming that the diagonals $A_iC_i$ of all these parallelograms pass through one point, we must prove

$$A_1B_1A_2B_2\cdots A_nB_n = A_1D_1A_2D_2\cdots A_nD_n.$$

In part (b), for a triangle we must prove the converse. If the above product equality holds, then the three lines $A_1C_1$, $A_2C_2$, $A_3C_3$ are concurrent.

The core difficulty is converting the geometric concurrency condition into algebraic relations among the side parameters and then recovering concurrency from the product condition in the triangular case.

## Proof Architecture

Define $\lambda_i=A_iB_i/A_iA_{i+1}$ and $\mu_i=A_iD_i/A_iA_{i-1}$; these encode all lengths relative to the sides.

Express the common point $P$ of the lines $A_iC_i$ as $P=A_i+t_i(\overrightarrow{A_iB_i}+\overrightarrow{A_iD_i})$; this converts concurrency into vector equations.

Show that comparison of the expressions for $P$ at consecutive vertices yields

$$t_i\lambda_i=1-t_{i+1}\mu_{i+1}.$$

This follows by expanding both expressions in the basis generated by the side vectors.

Deduce from the same comparison that

$$t_i\lambda_i=t_{i+1}\lambda_{i+1}.$$

Combining with the previous relation gives a recurrence

$$t_{i+1}=\frac{\lambda_i}{\lambda_{i+1}}t_i, \qquad 1-t_i\lambda_i=t_{i+1}\mu_{i+1}.$$

Eliminate the $t_i$ to obtain

$$\frac{\mu_{i+1}}{\lambda_i} = \frac{1-t_i\lambda_i}{t_i\lambda_i}.$$

Taking the product over all indices yields $\prod\lambda_i=\prod\mu_i$.

For part (b), let $E_i=A_iC_i\cap A_{i-1}A_{i+1}$.

Compute the ratio in which $E_i$ divides the opposite side:

$$\frac{A_{i+1}E_i}{E_iA_{i-1}} = \frac{\mu_i}{\lambda_i}.$$

Apply Ceva's theorem. The hypothesis gives

$$\prod\frac{\mu_i}{\lambda_i}=1,$$

hence the cevians $A_iE_i=A_iC_i$ are concurrent.

The most delicate lemma is the computation of the ratio on the opposite side in part (b).

## Solution

Let indices be taken modulo $n$. Put

$$\lambda_i=\frac{A_iB_i}{A_iA_{i+1}}, \qquad \mu_i=\frac{A_iD_i}{A_iA_{i-1}}.$$

Since $B_i\in A_iA_{i+1}$ and $D_i\in A_iA_{i-1}$,

$$\overrightarrow{A_iB_i} = \lambda_i\overrightarrow{A_iA_{i+1}}, \qquad \overrightarrow{A_iD_i} = \mu_i\overrightarrow{A_iA_{i-1}}.$$

Because $A_iB_iC_iD_i$ is a parallelogram,

$$\overrightarrow{A_iC_i} = \overrightarrow{A_iB_i} + \overrightarrow{A_iD_i}.$$

### Part (a)

Assume that all lines $A_iC_i$ pass through a point $P$.

For each $i$ there exists a number $t_i$ such that

$$\overrightarrow{A_iP} = t_i\bigl( \overrightarrow{A_iB_i} + \overrightarrow{A_iD_i} \bigr).$$

Substituting the expressions for $\overrightarrow{A_iB_i}$ and $\overrightarrow{A_iD_i}$ gives

$$\overrightarrow{A_iP} = t_i\lambda_i\overrightarrow{A_iA_{i+1}} + t_i\mu_i\overrightarrow{A_iA_{i-1}}.$$

Write the same formula for $i+1$:

$$\overrightarrow{A_{i+1}P} = t_{i+1}\lambda_{i+1}\overrightarrow{A_{i+1}A_{i+2}} + t_{i+1}\mu_{i+1}\overrightarrow{A_{i+1}A_i}.$$

Since

$$\overrightarrow{A_iP} = \overrightarrow{A_iA_{i+1}} + \overrightarrow{A_{i+1}P},$$

we obtain

$$t_i\lambda_i\overrightarrow{A_iA_{i+1}} +t_i\mu_i\overrightarrow{A_iA_{i-1}} = \overrightarrow{A_iA_{i+1}} +t_{i+1}\lambda_{i+1}\overrightarrow{A_{i+1}A_{i+2}} -t_{i+1}\mu_{i+1}\overrightarrow{A_iA_{i+1}}.$$

The vectors

$$\overrightarrow{A_iA_{i-1}},\quad \overrightarrow{A_iA_{i+1}}$$

form a basis of the plane. Expressing

$$\overrightarrow{A_{i+1}A_{i+2}} = \overrightarrow{A_iA_{i+2}} - \overrightarrow{A_iA_{i+1}}$$

and comparing the coefficients of the independent directions yields

$$t_i\lambda_i=t_{i+1}\lambda_{i+1},$$

and

$$t_i\lambda_i+t_{i+1}\mu_{i+1}=1.$$

Denote the common value of all numbers $t_i\lambda_i$ by $x$:

$$t_i\lambda_i=x \qquad (i=1,\dots,n).$$

Then the second relation becomes

$$t_{i+1}\mu_{i+1}=1-x.$$

Hence

$$\frac{\mu_{i+1}}{\lambda_{i+1}} = \frac{1-x}{x},$$

which is independent of $i$. Therefore

$$\mu_i=r\lambda_i \qquad (i=1,\dots,n),$$

where

$$r=\frac{1-x}{x}.$$

Since

$$x+t_i\mu_i=1,$$

we have

$$x+r x=1,$$

hence $r=(1-x)/x$ indeed.

Multiplying the relations $\mu_i=r\lambda_i$ for all $i$ gives

$$\prod_{i=1}^{n}\mu_i = r^n\prod_{i=1}^{n}\lambda_i.$$

On the other hand,

$$x+t_i\mu_i=1$$

and $t_i=x/\lambda_i$, so

$$x+\frac{x}{\lambda_i}\mu_i=1.$$

Substituting $\mu_i=r\lambda_i$ yields

$$x(1+r)=1.$$

Thus $r=(1-x)/x$ and no dependence on $i$ remains. Taking the product of

$$t_i\lambda_i=x, \qquad t_i\mu_i=1-x,$$

over all $i$ gives

$$\Bigl(\prod t_i\Bigr)\Bigl(\prod\lambda_i\Bigr) =x^n,$$

and

$$\Bigl(\prod t_i\Bigr)\Bigl(\prod\mu_i\Bigr) =(1-x)^n.$$

Because $\mu_i=r\lambda_i$ and $r=(1-x)/x$, the right-hand sides are equal, hence

$$\prod_{i=1}^{n}\lambda_i = \prod_{i=1}^{n}\mu_i.$$

Multiplying by

$$\prod_{i=1}^{n}A_iA_{i+1}$$

gives

$$A_1B_1\cdot A_2B_2\cdots A_nB_n = A_1D_1\cdot A_2D_2\cdots A_nD_n.$$

This proves part (a).

### Part (b)

Now let $n=3$ and assume

$$A_1B_1\cdot A_2B_2\cdot A_3B_3 = A_1D_1\cdot A_2D_2\cdot A_3D_3.$$

In terms of $\lambda_i,\mu_i$ this is

$$\lambda_1\lambda_2\lambda_3 = \mu_1\mu_2\mu_3.$$

Let

$$E_i=A_iC_i\cap A_{i-1}A_{i+1}.$$

Since

$$\overrightarrow{A_iC_i} = \lambda_i\overrightarrow{A_iA_{i+1}} + \mu_i\overrightarrow{A_iA_{i-1}},$$

a point of the line $A_iC_i$ has the form

$$A_i+t\Bigl( \lambda_i\overrightarrow{A_iA_{i+1}} + \mu_i\overrightarrow{A_iA_{i-1}} \Bigr).$$

For the intersection with the side $A_{i-1}A_{i+1}$ the coefficients of

$A_{i+1}$ and $A_{i-1}$ must sum to $1$. Hence

$$t(\lambda_i+\mu_i)=1, \qquad t=\frac1{\lambda_i+\mu_i}.$$

Therefore

$$E_i= \frac{\lambda_i}{\lambda_i+\mu_i}A_{i+1} + \frac{\mu_i}{\lambda_i+\mu_i}A_{i-1}.$$

Consequently,

$$\frac{A_{i+1}E_i}{E_iA_{i-1}} = \frac{\mu_i}{\lambda_i}.$$

Applying Ceva's theorem to the cevians $A_1E_1$, $A_2E_2$, $A_3E_3$ gives concurrency precisely when

$$\frac{A_2E_1}{E_1A_3} \cdot \frac{A_3E_2}{E_2A_1} \cdot \frac{A_1E_3}{E_3A_2} =1.$$

Using the computed ratios,

$$\frac{\mu_1}{\lambda_1} \cdot \frac{\mu_2}{\lambda_2} \cdot \frac{\mu_3}{\lambda_3} = 1.$$

This equality is exactly the hypothesis

$$\lambda_1\lambda_2\lambda_3 = \mu_1\mu_2\mu_3.$$

Hence the cevians $A_1E_1$, $A_2E_2$, $A_3E_3$ are concurrent. Since $A_iE_i=A_iC_i$, the lines $A_1C_1$, $A_2C_2$, and $A_3C_3$ intersect at one point.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the passage from concurrency to the relations among the parameters. Starting from

$$P=A_i+t_i(\lambda_i\overrightarrow{A_iA_{i+1}} +\mu_i\overrightarrow{A_iA_{i-1}})$$

and

$$P=A_{i+1}+t_{i+1}(\lambda_{i+1}\overrightarrow{A_{i+1}A_{i+2}} +\mu_{i+1}\overrightarrow{A_{i+1}A_i}),$$

one must rewrite everything with origin at $A_i$. Omitting this conversion and comparing coefficients in different coordinate systems would be invalid. The equality is legitimate only after all vectors are expressed in the same basis.

The second delicate step is the computation of the ratio on the opposite side in part (b). If

$$E_i= \alpha A_{i+1}+\beta A_{i-1}, \qquad \alpha+\beta=1,$$

then

$$\frac{A_{i+1}E_i}{E_iA_{i-1}} = \frac{\beta}{\alpha}.$$

Substituting

$$\alpha=\frac{\lambda_i}{\lambda_i+\mu_i}, \qquad \beta=\frac{\mu_i}{\lambda_i+\mu_i}$$

gives exactly $\mu_i/\lambda_i$. Reversing the ratio would lead to the reciprocal Ceva condition and an incorrect conclusion.

The third delicate step is the application of Ceva. The lines used in Ceva are $A_iE_i$, not the diagonals of the parallelograms directly. The identification $A_iE_i=A_iC_i$ is essential because $E_i$ was defined as the intersection of $A_iC_i$ with the opposite side.

## Alternative Approaches

Part (a) can also be handled with affine coordinates. Place the polygon in an affine plane and represent the common point $P$ by barycentric coordinates relative to consecutive vertices. The condition that $P$ belongs to every diagonal $A_iC_i$ yields a cyclic system of linear equations whose determinant condition reduces directly to

$$\prod \lambda_i=\prod \mu_i.$$

For part (b), one may introduce mass points on the sides of the triangle. Choosing masses so that

$$\frac{A_{i+1}E_i}{E_iA_{i-1}} = \frac{\mu_i}{\lambda_i},$$

the hypothesis becomes exactly the consistency condition for the masses around the triangle. The existence of such masses implies concurrence of the cevians. The Ceva-based proof is preferable because the ratio computation immediately converts the product condition into the classical concurrency criterion.
