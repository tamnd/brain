## Problem

Prove that if the product of three positive numbers equals 1, and the sum of these numbers is strictly greater than the sum of their reciprocals, then exactly one of these numbers is greater than 1.

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad (1970, 8th grade)

## Exploration

Let the positive numbers be $a,b,c$ with $abc=1$. The inequality

$a+b+c>\frac1a+\frac1b+\frac1c$

can be rewritten using $abc=1$ as

$a+b+c>bc+ca+ab.$

The problem asks to determine how many of the numbers can exceed $1$ under this condition.

If all three numbers are at most $1$, then each reciprocal is at least $1$, and comparing termwise suggests the right-hand side should dominate, contradicting the strict inequality. Hence at least one number must exceed $1$.

If two numbers exceed $1$, then one number is below $1$ because the product is $1$. The difficulty is to show that in this configuration the inequality cannot hold. The expression is symmetric, so it is natural to seek a transformation that linearizes the multiplicative constraint $abc=1$. Logarithms convert products into sums, suggesting a convexity argument in terms of exponential or hyperbolic functions. The key difficulty is to control the competition between two positive “contributions” and one negative one under a fixed sum constraint.

The most fragile step is ruling out the case of exactly two numbers greater than $1$, since naive termwise comparisons do not separate the two sides cleanly.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem. We must prove that under $abc=1$ and

$a+b+c>\frac1a+\frac1b+\frac1c,$

exactly one of $a,b,c$ exceeds $1$.

The natural intuition is that the inequality measures an imbalance between numbers above and below $1$, and the condition $abc=1$ forces a compensating structure. The expected conclusion is that the only possible way to make the left-hand side larger is to have precisely one variable above $1$.

## Proof Architecture

Introduce logarithmic variables $a=e^x$, $b=e^y$, $c=e^z$ with $x+y+z=0$. Reformulate the inequality in terms of hyperbolic sine.

Prove that if at least two of $x,y,z$ are nonnegative, then a key convexity inequality between $\sinh(x+y)$ and $\sinh x+\sinh y$ forces a contradiction.

Show first that at least one of $a,b,c$ exceeds $1$.

Show then that not all three can exceed $1$.

Show finally that two cannot exceed $1$.

The most delicate lemma is the comparison $\sinh(x+y)\ge \sinh x+\sinh y$ for $x,y\ge 0$, since it is the decisive step excluding two positive logarithms.

## Solution

Let $a,b,c$ be positive real numbers such that $abc=1$. Write

$a=e^x,\quad b=e^y,\quad c=e^z,$

where $x,y,z\in\mathbb{R}$ and

$x+y+z=0.$

The given inequality becomes

$e^x+e^y+e^z>e^{-x}+e^{-y}+e^{-z}.$

Rewriting each difference as $e^t-e^{-t}=2\sinh t$, the inequality is equivalent to

$\sinh x+\sinh y+\sinh z>0.$

Since $z=-(x+y)$, this becomes

$\sinh x+\sinh y-\sinh(x+y)>0.$

First suppose all of $x,y,z$ are nonpositive. Then each of $a,b,c$ is at most $1$, so each reciprocal is at least $1$, and termwise comparison yields

$a+b+c\le \frac1a+\frac1b+\frac1c,$

contradicting the given strict inequality. Hence at least one of $x,y,z$ is positive, so at least one of $a,b,c$ exceeds $1$.

Assume now that $x\ge 0$ and $y\ge 0$, which corresponds to two of $a,b,c$ being at least $1$. Using the identity

$\sinh(x+y)=\sinh x\cosh y+\cosh x\sinh y,$

and the facts that $\cosh t\ge 1$ for all real $t$, we obtain

$\sinh(x+y)\ge \sinh x+\sinh y.$

Substituting this estimate into

$\sinh x+\sinh y-\sinh(x+y)$

gives

$\sinh x+\sinh y-\sinh(x+y)\le 0.$

This contradicts the required strict positivity. Hence it is impossible that two of $x,y,z$ are nonnegative, so at most one of $a,b,c$ is at least $1$.

Since at least one of $a,b,c$ exceeds $1$ and at most one can be at least $1$, exactly one of the three numbers is greater than $1$.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The critical step is the inequality

$\sinh(x+y)\ge \sinh x+\sinh y \quad \text{for } x,y\ge 0.$

From the identity

$\sinh(x+y)=\sinh x\cosh y+\cosh x\sinh y,$

we compare term by term. For $y\ge 0$, $\cosh y\ge 1$, hence $\sinh x\cosh y\ge \sinh x$. Similarly, for $x\ge 0$, $\cosh x\ge 1$, hence $\cosh x\sinh y\ge \sinh y$. Adding these inequalities yields

$\sinh(x+y)\ge \sinh x+\sinh y.$

A second delicate point is the reformulation of the original inequality. Using $a=e^x$ gives $\frac1a=e^{-x}$, so every term is accounted for exactly once in the transformation into hyperbolic sine form.

Finally, the implication that at least one variable exceeds $1$ follows from the observation that if all three were at most $1$, then all reciprocals are at least $1$, forcing the reverse inequality termwise and contradicting strictness.

## Alternative Approaches

A different method avoids logarithms and instead works directly with the symmetric form

$a+b+c>ab+bc+ca,$

together with $abc=1$, and proceeds by substituting $c=\frac1{ab}$ and analyzing the resulting inequality in two variables. This leads to a non-symmetric polynomial inequality that can be handled by case analysis depending on whether $a$ and $b$ are above or below $1$, but the expressions become more cumbersome.

The logarithmic and hyperbolic sine approach is preferable because it converts the product constraint into a linear condition and turns the inequality into a convexity statement, allowing a clean exclusion of the two-above-one-below configuration in a single step.
