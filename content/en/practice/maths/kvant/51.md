## Problem

Prove that if the product of three positive numbers equals 1, and the sum of these numbers is strictly greater than the sum of their reciprocals, then exactly one of these numbers is greater than 1.

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad (1970, 8th grade)

## Exploration

Consider three positive numbers $a$, $b$, $c$ such that $abc=1$. The inequality $a+b+c > \frac{1}{a} + \frac{1}{b} + \frac{1}{c}$ can be rewritten using the relation $1/a = bc$, $1/b = ac$, $1/c = ab$, giving $a+b+c > bc + ac + ab$. Small numerical experiments suggest that if all three numbers are less than 1, their sum is smaller than the sum of pairwise products. If two numbers exceed 1, their pairwise products dominate the sum of numbers, leading to a violation of the inequality. Testing examples such as $(2,1,1/2)$, $(1/2,1/2,4)$, and $(1,1,1)$ shows that the inequality holds exactly when one number exceeds 1 and the other two are less than 1. This hints at a structural necessity: the inequality forces the configuration of one number larger than 1 and the remaining two smaller than 1. The main challenge is to exclude all other possibilities rigorously.

## Problem Understanding

The problem requires proving a conditional statement about three positive numbers $a$, $b$, $c$ with $abc=1$, namely, that if their sum exceeds the sum of their reciprocals, then exactly one number is greater than 1. This is a Type B problem: the statement is given, and we must provide a rigorous proof. The core difficulty is to analyze the inequality $a+b+c > \frac{1}{a} + \frac{1}{b} + \frac{1}{c}$ under the constraint $abc=1$ and to show that only the configuration with exactly one number greater than 1 satisfies it, excluding the other configurations. Intuitively, if all numbers are equal to 1, the inequality fails, and if two or three numbers are greater than 1, the reciprocal sum grows faster than the direct sum, violating the inequality.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1. If all three numbers are equal to 1, then the inequality $a+b+c > \frac{1}{a} + \frac{1}{b} + \frac{1}{c}$ fails. This is true because both sides equal 3.

Lemma 2. If all three numbers are greater than 1, the inequality fails. This is true because $1/a$, $1/b$, $1/c$ are all less than 1, making $a+b+c > \frac{1}{a} + \frac{1}{b} + \frac{1}{c}$ impossible given $abc>1$.

Lemma 3. If two numbers are greater than 1 and the third is less than 1, the inequality fails. This is seen by expressing $1/a + 1/b + 1/c$ in terms of products $bc + ac + ab$ and comparing to $a+b+c$.

Lemma 4. If exactly one number is greater than 1, the inequality holds. This is shown by assigning the numbers $a>1$, $b<1$, $c<1$ and observing that the sum of reciprocals is smaller than the sum of numbers.

The hardest direction is Lemma 3, which eliminates the possibility of two numbers exceeding 1. The key subtlety is that swapping numbers does not change $abc=1$ but affects the sum comparison.
