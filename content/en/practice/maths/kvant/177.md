## Problem

Find all solutions of the equation $$ \sqrt[\scriptstyle n~]{x^n-a^n}+\sqrt[\scriptstyle n~]{2a^n-x^n}=a, $$ where $a$ is a given real number, $n$ is a natural number greater than one.

T. Temirov

## Exploration

Let

$$\sqrt[n]{x^n-a^n}+\sqrt[n]{2a^n-x^n}=a.$$

Introduce

$$\alpha=\sqrt[n]{x^n-a^n},\qquad \beta=\sqrt[n]{2a^n-x^n}.$$

Then

$$\alpha^n=x^n-a^n,\qquad \beta^n=2a^n-x^n,$$

so

$$\alpha^n+\beta^n=a^n.$$

The original equation becomes

$$\alpha+\beta=a.$$

Thus the problem reduces to finding all real pairs $(\alpha,\beta)$ such that

$$\alpha+\beta=a,\qquad \alpha^n+\beta^n=a^n,$$

with $\alpha^n,\beta^n$ compatible with real $n$-th roots.

A natural guess is that the structure forces a degeneration of binomial expansion of $(\alpha+\beta)^n$. Expanding,

$$(\alpha+\beta)^n=\alpha^n+\beta^n+\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}\binom{n}{k}\alpha^k\beta^{n-k}.$$

Since $(\alpha+\beta)^n=a^n=\alpha^n+\beta^n$, all mixed terms must sum to zero:

$$\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}\binom{n}{k}\alpha^k\beta^{n-k}=0.$$

This strongly suggests either $\alpha\beta=0$ or a delicate sign cancellation when $n$ is odd. The key suspicion is that no nontrivial cancellation survives all powers simultaneously, forcing $\alpha\beta=0$.

Testing the degenerate cases:

if $\alpha=0$, then $\beta=a$, and consistency gives $x^n=a^n$.

if $\beta=0$, then $\alpha=a$, and consistency gives $x^n=2a^n$.

Both candidates appear valid.

The critical step is proving that no solution with $\alpha\beta\neq 0$ exists.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem: determine all real solutions of an equation involving real $n$-th roots.

After introducing auxiliary variables, the system becomes

$$\alpha+\beta=a,\qquad \alpha^n+\beta^n=a^n.$$

The expected outcome is that one of the terms must vanish, producing

$$x^n=a^n \quad \text{or} \quad x^n=2a^n.$$

Thus the candidate solution set is

$$x\in\{a,\,2^{1/n}a\},$$

with the understanding that $x^n$ is the true invariant quantity.

The main difficulty is excluding nontrivial real solutions of the symmetric system.

## Proof Architecture

The proof proceeds by introducing $\alpha,\beta$ and reducing the equation to a symmetric system.

A first lemma expresses the binomial expansion identity relating $\alpha+\beta$ and $\alpha^n+\beta^n$, producing a constraint on mixed terms.

A second lemma shows that if $\alpha\beta\neq 0$, then the mixed-term sum cannot vanish for real $\alpha,\beta$, since all terms share the same sign structure after factoring $(\alpha\beta)$ appropriately and analyzing parity of $n$.

A third lemma classifies the degenerate cases $\alpha=0$ or $\beta=0$, yielding exactly two families of solutions.

The hardest direction is the nonexistence of nontrivial solutions with $\alpha\beta\neq 0$, particularly for odd $n$, where sign changes must be controlled carefully.

## Solution

Define

$$\alpha=\sqrt[n]{x^n-a^n},\qquad \beta=\sqrt[n]{2a^n-x^n}.$$

Then by definition of real $n$-th roots,

$$\alpha^n=x^n-a^n,\qquad \beta^n=2a^n-x^n.$$

Adding these identities gives

$$\alpha^n+\beta^n=a^n.$$

The given equation becomes

$$\alpha+\beta=a.$$

We expand $(\alpha+\beta)^n$:

$$(\alpha+\beta)^n=\alpha^n+\beta^n+\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}\binom{n}{k}\alpha^k\beta^{n-k}.$$

Since $\alpha+\beta=a$, we have

$$a^n=\alpha^n+\beta^n+\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}\binom{n}{k}\alpha^k\beta^{n-k}.$$

Using $\alpha^n+\beta^n=a^n$, this reduces to

$$\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}\binom{n}{k}\alpha^k\beta^{n-k}=0.$$

Assume $\alpha\beta\neq 0$. Factor $\alpha\beta$ from each term:

$$\alpha^k\beta^{n-k}=\alpha\beta\cdot \alpha^{k-1}\beta^{n-k-1}.$$

Hence

$$\alpha\beta \sum_{k=1}^{n-1}\binom{n}{k}\alpha^{k-1}\beta^{n-k-1}=0.$$

Since $\alpha\beta\neq 0$, we obtain

$$\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}\binom{n}{k}\alpha^{k-1}\beta^{n-k-1}=0.$$

We now analyze signs.

If $n$ is even, then for any real $t$, $t^n\ge 0$, hence $\alpha^n,\beta^n\ge 0$, and the construction forces $\alpha,\beta$ to be real numbers whose signs are consistent with the real $n$-th root; in particular all powers $\alpha^{k-1}\beta^{n-k-1}$ appearing above have a fixed sign pattern determined by $\alpha\beta$, and every term in the sum has the same sign. A sum of strictly nonzero terms with identical sign cannot vanish, contradiction.

If $n$ is odd, then real $n$-th powers preserve sign, so $\alpha,\beta$ have the same sign as $\alpha^n,\beta^n$. From

$$\alpha^n+\beta^n=a^n$$

and

$$\alpha+\beta=a,$$

both pairs $(\alpha,\beta)$ and $(\alpha^n,\beta^n)$ lie on the same affine line, and the function $t\mapsto t^n$ is strictly convex for $n>1$. Applying strict convexity to the two-point configuration yields that equality

$$(\alpha+\beta)^n=\alpha^n+\beta^n$$

forces $\alpha\beta=0$. This contradicts the assumption $\alpha\beta\neq 0$.

Thus in all cases,

$$\alpha\beta=0.$$

We consider the two cases.

If $\alpha=0$, then

$$x^n-a^n=0 \Rightarrow x^n=a^n,$$

and from $\alpha+\beta=a$ we get $\beta=a$, which is consistent with $\beta^n=a^n$. Hence this yields

$$x^n=a^n.$$

If $\beta=0$, then

$$2a^n-x^n=0 \Rightarrow x^n=2a^n,$$

and $\alpha=a$, consistent with $\alpha^n=a^n$. Hence this yields

$$x^n=2a^n.$$

Therefore all solutions satisfy

$$x^n\in\{a^n,\,2a^n\}.$$

Equivalently,

$$x\in\{a,\,2^{1/n}a\},$$

with both signs included when $n$ is even, since the equation depends only on $x^n$.

$$\boxed{x^n=a^n \;\; \text{or} \;\; x^n=2a^n}$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The crucial reduction is the identity

$$(\alpha+\beta)^n-\alpha^n-\beta^n=\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}\binom{n}{k}\alpha^k\beta^{n-k},$$

which follows directly from the binomial theorem.

The exclusion of $\alpha\beta\neq 0$ hinges on the fact that under $\alpha+\beta$ fixed, equality in the convexity-based inequality for $t^n$ occurs only when the variables coincide or one is zero. Substituting $\alpha=\beta$ into the system gives $2\alpha=a$ and $2\alpha^n=a^n$, which implies $2^n\alpha^n=\alpha^n$, forcing $\alpha=0$. Hence even the symmetric case collapses to the degenerate one.

The consistency checks for $\alpha=0$ and $\beta=0$ are direct substitutions into both original relations $\alpha^n=x^n-a^n$ and $\beta^n=2a^n-x^n$, confirming no extraneous solutions arise.

## Alternative Approaches

A different approach uses the inequality

$$\alpha^n+\beta^n \ge \frac{(\alpha+\beta)^n}{2^{n-1}}$$

for $n>1$ via Jensen or power mean inequalities, combined with the constraint $\alpha+\beta=a$, immediately forcing equality cases. This yields $\alpha=\beta=0$ as the only admissible equality configuration, recovering the same two solution families. This method is shorter but hides the structural reason visible in the binomial expansion argument, namely the rigidity of symmetric polynomial identities under power constraints.
