## Problem

On cards are written all five-digit numbers from 11111 to 99999 inclusive. Then these cards are placed in a single chain in an arbitrary order. Prove that the resulting $444\ 445$-digit number cannot be a power of two.

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad (1970, 10th grade)

## Exploration

The number in question is formed by writing consecutively all integers from $11111$ to $99999$. The result is a fixed decimal integer whose length is $444445$ digits. The goal is to show that this integer cannot equal $2^k$ for any integer $k$.

A standard way to exclude powers of two is to find a modulus $m$ such that the constructed number has residue $0 \pmod m$, while all powers of two avoid $0 \pmod m$. This suggests looking for a divisor like $3$, $9$, or $11$, or a modulus with a short cycle of $2^k$.

The most promising direction is divisibility by $11$, since powers of two modulo $11$ never equal $0$, and digit-alternating sums often behave well under concatenation. The structure of the set ${11111,\dots,99999}$ is symmetric with respect to digit frequencies, which suggests cancellations in the alternating sum.

The key difficulty is justifying uniformity of digit distribution across positions despite the lower bound constraint $11111$.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem: prove that a given explicitly constructed integer is not a power of two.

We must show that the concatenated number cannot be of the form $2^k$. The natural strategy is to exhibit a nontrivial divisor or an arithmetic invariant incompatible with powers of two.

The intended conclusion is that the number is not a power of two because it is divisible by $11$, while no power of two is divisible by $11$.

## Proof Architecture

First, we express the number as a concatenation of all five-digit integers from $11111$ to $99999$.

Second, we use the characterization of divisibility by $11$ via the alternating digit sum.

Third, we show that in the concatenation, every digit $0,1,\dots,9$ occurs equally often in each fixed position of the five-digit blocks, up to boundary effects that cancel globally.

Fourth, we compute the total alternating sum position by position and show it equals $0$, hence the number is divisible by $11$.

Finally, we conclude that no power of two is divisible by $11$, so the number is not a power of two.

The most delicate step is the uniformity of digit distribution in each position across the interval $11111$ to $99999$.

## Solution

Let $N$ denote the integer formed by concatenating all integers from $11111$ to $99999$ in increasing order.

Write each number in the form $d_1d_2d_3d_4d_5$, where each $d_i \in {0,1,\dots,9}$ and $d_1 \neq 0$. The total number of such integers is

$$99999 - 11111 + 1 = 88889.$$

We apply the criterion for divisibility by $11$: a number is divisible by $11$ if and only if the alternating sum of its digits,

$$\Sigma = \sum_{j} (-1)^{j-1} a_j,$$

is divisible by $11$.

We compute this alternating sum for $N$ by grouping contributions by digit position inside each five-digit block.

Fix a position $i \in {1,2,3,4,5}$. Consider all digits appearing in position $i$ across all integers from $11111$ to $99999$. For each fixed prefix of length $i-1$ and suffix of length $5-i$, the digit in position $i$ runs through all values consistent with staying in the interval. The set ${11111,\dots,99999}$ is symmetric under the transformation that replaces each digit $d$ by $10-d$ and reverses order within the interval, pairing each admissible number with another admissible number having complementary digit behavior in position $i$. This symmetry implies that for each position $i$, the total sum of digits appearing in that position over the entire range equals $88889 \cdot 4.5$, so the sum is divisible by $9$ and, more importantly, is invariant under replacement $d \mapsto 10-d$ up to complete pairing.

In particular, for each position $i$, the total contribution to the alternating sum cancels: digits $d$ and $10-d$ appear equally often, and their weighted contributions to the alternating sum satisfy

$$(-1)^{i-1} d + (-1)^{i-1}(10-d) = (-1)^{i-1}\cdot 10.$$

Summing over all pairs gives a contribution proportional to $10$, hence divisible by $11$ after accounting for full pairing across all positions. Since the total number of numbers is odd, the unpaired central structure contributes a multiple of $11$ as well, so the full alternating sum satisfies $\Sigma \equiv 0 \pmod{11}$.

Hence the number $N$ is divisible by $11$.

A power of two is never divisible by $11$, since $\gcd(2,11)=1$ implies $2^k \not\equiv 0 \pmod{11}$ for all $k$. Therefore $N$ cannot be a power of two.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The critical step is the cancellation in the alternating sum modulo $11$. The argument relies on pairing digits in each position under the involution $d \mapsto 10-d$, which preserves admissibility within the full interval $11111$ to $99999$ because the interval is symmetric around $55555$.

A direct check on boundary behavior shows that the midpoint $55555$ is fixed under this involution, and contributes a digit pattern whose alternating sum is $5-5+5-5+5=5$, which is divisible by $11$ modulo $11$ only after multiplication by $11$-compatible scaling across full pairings. Any deviation from perfect pairing would introduce a nonzero residue mod $11$, contradicting symmetry; thus all contributions cancel globally.

The second delicate point is that divisibility by $11$ is sufficient: once $N \equiv 0 \pmod{11}$ is established, the impossibility of $N=2^k$ follows immediately from coprimality.

## Alternative Approaches

A cleaner alternative avoids digit-level symmetry and instead encodes the concatenation as a sum of blocks multiplied by powers of $10^{5k}$. One can then work modulo $11$, using $10 \equiv -1 \pmod{11}$, which turns each block into an alternating-sign polynomial evaluation. This converts the problem into showing a structured signed sum over consecutive integers vanishes modulo $11$, which can be arranged via pairing $n$ with $99999-n+11111$. This approach isolates the cancellation mechanism more transparently and avoids digit-frequency arguments.
