## Problem

All five-digit numbers from 11111 to 99999 inclusive are written on cards. Then these cards are arranged in a single chain in an arbitrary order. Prove that the resulting $444\ 445$-digit number cannot be a power of two.

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad (1970, 10th grade)

## Exploration

Each card corresponds to a five-digit number whose digits range from $1$ to $9$. There are exactly $9^5$ such numbers, so the final concatenated number has $5\cdot 9^5 = 444,445$ digits.

A power of $2$ is never divisible by $3$, so it suffices to show that the constructed number is divisible by $3$, or even by $9$. The divisibility by $9$ can be tested via the sum of digits, so the task reduces to computing the total digit sum of all five-digit numbers with digits in ${1,\dots,9}$ and showing it is a multiple of $9$.

The key structural feature is that in the full set of all such numbers, each position behaves uniformly with respect to digits $1$ through $9$.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem: prove that a certain constructed number cannot be a power of $2$.

The constructed number is formed by concatenating all five-digit numbers using digits $1$ through $9$. The core difficulty is to extract a global arithmetic invariant, specifically a divisibility property, that is independent of the ordering of the cards.

The intended obstruction is that the digit sum of the entire construction is highly structured and forces divisibility by $9$, whereas powers of $2$ are never divisible by $3$.

## Proof Architecture

The proof proceeds by analyzing the digit distribution across all five-digit numbers with digits from $1$ to $9$. The first lemma establishes uniform frequency of each digit in each fixed position. The second lemma computes the total digit sum using this uniformity. The third lemma deduces divisibility by $9$ and excludes powers of $2$.

The most delicate point is the uniform distribution claim, since it must be justified without appealing to informal symmetry.

## Solution

Each card corresponds to a number $(d_1d_2d_3d_4d_5)$ where each $d_i \in {1,2,\dots,9}$. There are $9^5$ such numbers in total.

Fix a position $i \in {1,2,3,4,5}$. For any fixed digit $a \in {1,\dots,9}$, the number of five-digit strings in which the $i$-th digit equals $a$ is $9^4$, since the remaining four positions may be filled arbitrarily with digits from $1$ to $9$. Hence, in position $i$, each digit $1$ through $9$ occurs exactly $9^4$ times among all cards.

The contribution of position $i$ to the total digit sum of all cards is therefore

$$9^4(1+2+\cdots+9).$$

Since

$$1+2+\cdots+9 = 45,$$

the total contribution of one position equals $45\cdot 9^4$. Summing over the five positions gives total digit sum

$$S = 5 \cdot 45 \cdot 9^4.$$

The number $45$ equals $5\cdot 9$, hence

$$S = 5 \cdot 5 \cdot 9 \cdot 9^4 = 25 \cdot 9^5.$$

This shows that $S$ is divisible by $9$.

The sum of digits of a number determines its residue modulo $9$, hence the concatenated number is divisible by $9$. Any power of $2$ is coprime to $3$, since no power of $2$ is divisible by $3$. Therefore no power of $2$ can be divisible by $9$.

The constructed number cannot be a power of $2$.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate point is the uniformity claim for digit distribution. For a fixed position, fixing that digit leaves exactly four independent positions, each with $9$ choices, producing exactly $9^4$ completions. This count does not depend on which digit is fixed, so every digit occurs equally often.

The second point is the digit sum computation. Since each digit contributes independently by position and all occurrences are counted exactly once, no overcounting or omission occurs.

The third point is the transition from divisibility by $9$ to exclusion of powers of $2$. Every power of $2$ is not divisible by $3$, since its prime factorization contains only the prime $2$, so it cannot be divisible by $9=3^2$.

## Alternative Approaches

An alternative approach uses modular arithmetic directly on the concatenated structure, observing that each block of five digits contributes a fixed residue modulo $9$, independent of ordering. One can also argue modulo $3$ without computing the full digit sum, since $1+2+\cdots+9$ is divisible by $3$, already forcing each positional contribution to vanish modulo $3$.

The digit-sum method is preferable because it reduces the entire problem to a single clean symmetry count and avoids handling the structure of concatenation modulo arithmetic directly.
