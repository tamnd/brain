## Problem

1. Prove that for any point $M$ on the circumcircle of the equilateral triangle $ABC$, one of the three segments $MA$, $MB$, $MC$ is equal to the sum of the other two.
2. Three congruent circles $\gamma_1$, $\gamma_2$, $\gamma_3$ are pairwise tangent to each other, and a circle $\gamma$ is circumscribed about them, touching all three: $\gamma_1$, $\gamma_2$, and $\gamma_3$. Prove that for any point $M$ on the circle $\gamma$, the tangent drawn from point $M$ to one of the three circles $\gamma_1$, $\gamma_2$, $\gamma_3$ is equal to the sum of the tangents drawn from point $M$ to the other two circles.

## Exploration

The first statement concerns an equilateral triangle and a point on its circumcircle. A natural tool is Ptolemy's theorem, because four points on a circle immediately suggest a cyclic quadrilateral. If the vertices are ordered suitably on the circumcircle, Ptolemy gives a linear relation among $MA$, $MB$, and $MC$.

Let the circumcircle be traversed in the order $A,M,B,C$. Then Ptolemy applied to quadrilateral $AMBC$ yields

$$AB\cdot MC+BC\cdot AM=AC\cdot MB.$$

Since $ABC$ is equilateral,

$$AB=BC=CA,$$

hence

$$MC+AM=MB.$$

This is exactly the desired conclusion. The only issue is that the position of $M$ changes. Depending on the arc containing $M$, a different one of $MA,MB,MC$ becomes the sum of the other two. Thus the statement is essentially a direct consequence of Ptolemy.

For the second statement, the three congruent mutually tangent circles inside a larger circle have centers at the vertices of an equilateral triangle. The outer circle touching all three is centered at the same point as the circumcenter of that equilateral triangle. Hence the points of tangency of $\gamma$ with $\gamma_1,\gamma_2,\gamma_3$ form an equilateral triangle on $\gamma$.

The tangent length from a point $M$ on $\gamma$ to one of the inner circles should be expressible through the distance from $M$ to the corresponding tangency point on $\gamma$. Let $A$ be the tangency point of $\gamma$ and $\gamma_1$. If the common radius of the small circles is $r$ and the radius of $\gamma$ is $R=3r$, then for $M\in\gamma$,

$$MT_1^2=\operatorname{Pow}_{\gamma_1}(M)       =MO_1^2-r^2.$$

Writing $O_1$ on the radius through $A$, one finds

$$MO_1^2-r^2=2r\cdot MA.$$

Thus

$$MT_1=\sqrt{2r\,MA}.$$

The crucial point is then to connect $MA,MB,MC$ by part 1. Since tangent lengths are square roots of these quantities, a direct substitution does not immediately give a linear relation. The formula must be checked carefully.

Let the radius of $\gamma$ be $3r$, with center $O$. Put $O=(0,0)$ and $A=(3r,0)$, $O_1=(2r,0)$. For $M=(3r\cos\theta,3r\sin\theta)$,

$$MA^2=18r^2(1-\cos\theta).$$

Also

$$MO_1^2-r^2 =13r^2-12r^2\cos\theta-r^2 =12r^2(1-\cos\theta).$$

Hence

$$MO_1^2-r^2=\frac23\,MA^2,$$

so

$$MT_1=\sqrt{\frac23}\,MA.$$

This is much better: tangent length is proportional to the chord from $M$ to the corresponding tangency point. Then part 2 follows immediately from part 1 applied to the equilateral triangle formed by the three tangency points.

The step most likely to hide an error is the derivation of

$$MT_i=\sqrt{\frac23}\,MP_i,$$

where $P_i$ is the tangency point of $\gamma$ with $\gamma_i$.

## Problem Understanding

The first task asks for a metric relation among the distances from a point $M$ on the circumcircle of an equilateral triangle to its three vertices. One of these distances must equal the sum of the other two.

The second task concerns three equal pairwise tangent circles inside a larger circle tangent to all of them. For a point $M$ on the outer circle, let the tangent lengths from $M$ to the three inner circles be $t_1,t_2,t_3$. The claim is that one of these tangent lengths equals the sum of the other two.

This is a Type B problem. The goal is to prove the stated relations.

The core difficulty in part 1 is identifying the correct cyclic quadrilateral to which Ptolemy's theorem should be applied. The core difficulty in part 2 is expressing tangent lengths to the inner circles in a form that reduces the problem to part 1.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1. If $M$ lies on the circumcircle of equilateral triangle $ABC$, then according to the arc containing $M$, one of the equalities $MB=MA+MC$, $MC=MA+MB$, or $MA=MB+MC$ holds; this follows from Ptolemy's theorem applied to a suitable cyclic quadrilateral.

Lemma 2. In the configuration of three congruent pairwise tangent circles inside $\gamma$, the three points where $\gamma$ touches the small circles form an equilateral triangle; the centers of the small circles form an equilateral triangle, and the tangency points lie on the same radii.

Lemma 3. If $A$ is the tangency point of $\gamma$ and $\gamma_1$, then for every $M\in\gamma$ the tangent length $t_1$ from $M$ to $\gamma_1$ satisfies

$$t_1=\sqrt{\frac23}\,MA;$$

this follows from the power of $M$ with respect to $\gamma_1$ and an explicit computation.

Lemma 4. The analogous formulas hold for the other two circles.

The hardest step is Lemma 3, because an incorrect relation between tangent length and chord length would invalidate the reduction to part 1.

## Solution

### Part 1

Let $M$ be a point on the circumcircle of the equilateral triangle $ABC$.

The points $A,B,C,M$ lie on one circle. Suppose first that along the circle they occur in the order

$$A,M,B,C.$$

Applying Ptolemy's theorem to the cyclic quadrilateral $AMBC$, we obtain

$$AB\cdot MC+BC\cdot AM=AC\cdot MB.$$

Since $ABC$ is equilateral,

$$AB=BC=CA.$$

Dividing by this common side length gives

$$MC+AM=MB.$$

Thus $MB$ equals the sum of the other two distances.

If $M$ lies on a different arc of the circumcircle, the cyclic order of the four points changes. Applying Ptolemy's theorem to the corresponding cyclic quadrilateral yields one of the two remaining equalities

$$MC=MA+MB,$$

or

$$MA=MB+MC.$$

The three arcs determined by $A,B,C$ cover the whole circumcircle, so one of these three relations always holds. Hence one of the segments $MA$, $MB$, $MC$ equals the sum of the other two.

### Part 2

Let $A,B,C$ be the points where the outer circle $\gamma$ touches $\gamma_1,\gamma_2,\gamma_3$, respectively.

Let $O_1,O_2,O_3$ be the centers of the three congruent circles, each of radius $r$.

Because the circles are pairwise tangent,

$$O_1O_2=O_2O_3=O_3O_1=2r.$$

Hence $O_1O_2O_3$ is an equilateral triangle.

Let $O$ be the center of $\gamma$. Since $\gamma$ is tangent internally to each small circle, the distances $OO_i$ are all equal. Therefore $O$ is the circumcenter of the equilateral triangle $O_1O_2O_3$.

The tangency point $A$ lies on the line $OO_1$, and similarly for $B$ and $C$. Consequently $A,B,C$ are obtained from $O_1,O_2,O_3$ by a homothety centered at $O$. Hence $ABC$ is also an equilateral triangle.

Let the radius of $\gamma$ be $R$. Since $O$ is the circumcenter of the equilateral triangle $O_1O_2O_3$ of side $2r$,

$$OO_1=\frac{2r}{\sqrt3}.$$

Because $\gamma$ is internally tangent to $\gamma_1$,

$$R=OO_1+r=\frac{2r}{\sqrt3}+r.$$

The exact value of $R$ will not be needed.

Fix a point $M\in\gamma$. Let $t_1$ denote the tangent length from $M$ to $\gamma_1$.

By the power of a point,

$$t_1^2=MO_1^2-r^2.$$

Choose coordinates with

$$O=(0,0),\qquad A=(R,0),\qquad O_1=(R-r,0).$$

Write

$$M=(R\cos\theta,R\sin\theta).$$

Then

$$MA^2=(R\cos\theta-R)^2+R^2\sin^2\theta      =2R^2(1-\cos\theta).$$

Also,

$$\begin{aligned} MO_1^2-r^2 &=(R\cos\theta-(R-r))^2+R^2\sin^2\theta-r^2\\ &=2R(R-r)(1-\cos\theta). \end{aligned}$$

Hence

$$t_1^2 =\frac{R-r}{R}\,MA^2.$$

Since

$$R-r=OO_1=\frac{2r}{\sqrt3}, \qquad R=\frac{2r}{\sqrt3}+r,$$

we get

$$\frac{R-r}{R} = \frac{\frac{2}{\sqrt3}}{\frac{2}{\sqrt3}+1},$$

a positive constant independent of $M$. Therefore

$$t_1=k\,MA$$

for a fixed constant

$$k=\sqrt{\frac{R-r}{R}}.$$

The same computation gives

$$t_2=k\,MB, \qquad t_3=k\,MC.$$

The triangle $ABC$ is equilateral and $M$ lies on its circumcircle $\gamma$. By part 1, one of the three relations

$$MA=MB+MC, \qquad MB=MA+MC, \qquad MC=MA+MB$$

holds.

Multiplying by the positive constant $k$ yields respectively

$$t_1=t_2+t_3, \qquad t_2=t_1+t_3, \qquad t_3=t_1+t_2.$$

Thus one of the tangent lengths from $M$ to the circles $\gamma_1,\gamma_2,\gamma_3$ equals the sum of the other two.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the application of Ptolemy's theorem. For the cyclic order $A,M,B,C$,

$$AB\cdot MC+BC\cdot AM=AC\cdot MB.$$

Since all three sides of the equilateral triangle are equal, the common factor cancels and gives

$$MC+AM=MB.$$

If the cyclic order is changed, the role of the longest distance changes accordingly. The three arcs between consecutive vertices exhaust the circumcircle, so no position of $M$ is omitted.

The second delicate step is the computation of the tangent length. Starting from

$$t_1^2=MO_1^2-r^2,$$

one must not replace $MO_1^2-r^2$ by a quantity merely proportional to $MA$. Direct coordinate calculation yields

$$MO_1^2-r^2=2R(R-r)(1-\cos\theta),$$

while

$$MA^2=2R^2(1-\cos\theta).$$

The proportionality is with $MA^2$, not with $MA$. Consequently

$$t_1^2=\frac{R-r}{R}MA^2,$$

and hence $t_1=kMA$.

A careless argument at this point could incorrectly derive $t_1\propto\sqrt{MA}$, which would make the final linear relation impossible.

The third delicate step is verifying that the same constant $k$ works for all three circles. The computation depends only on the common radius $r$ and the common outer radius $R$, not on the specific circle. Congruence of the three configurations guarantees

$$t_i=k\,MP_i$$

for each tangency point $P_i\in{A,B,C}$.

## Alternative Approaches

For the first part, one may avoid invoking Ptolemy explicitly. Let $M$ lie on the arc opposite $B$. Since $ABC$ is equilateral, the inscribed angles subtending the corresponding arcs show that

$$\angle AMC=120^\circ.$$

Applying the law of cosines in triangle $AMC$ and exploiting the special $120^\circ$ angle leads to the identity

$$MB=MA+MC.$$

The verification is longer than the Ptolemy argument and requires additional trigonometric computation.

For the second part, one may use inversion centered at $M$. The tangent length to a circle is naturally expressed through the power of $M$, and the images of the three congruent circles acquire radii proportional to those tangent lengths. After inversion, the problem becomes a metric statement about three circles associated with an equilateral configuration. Although elegant, this approach introduces machinery that is unnecessary once the direct proportionality $t_i=k,MP_i$ has been established. The reduction to part 1 is shorter and more transparent.
