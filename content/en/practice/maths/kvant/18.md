## Problem

1. Prove that for any point $M$ on the circumcircle of the equilateral triangle $ABC$, one of the three segments $MA$, $MB$, $MC$ is equal to the sum of the other two.
2. Three congruent circles $\gamma_1$, $\gamma_2$, $\gamma_3$ are pairwise tangent to each other, and a circle $\gamma$ is circumscribed around them, tangent to all three: $\gamma_1$, $\gamma_2$, and $\gamma_3$. Prove that for any point $M$ on the circle $\gamma$, the tangent segment drawn from the point $M$ to one of the three circles $\gamma_1$, $\gamma_2$, $\gamma_3$ is equal to the sum of the tangent segments drawn from the point $M$ to the other two circles.

## Exploration

For the first part, the vertices of an equilateral triangle divide the circumcircle into three equal arcs of measure $120^\circ$. If $M$ lies on the circumcircle, then $M$ belongs to one of these three arcs. Suppose $M$ lies on the arc $BC$ not containing $A$.

The statement resembles Ptolemy's theorem. Since $A,B,C,M$ are concyclic, Ptolemy gives

$$AB\cdot MC+AC\cdot MB=BC\cdot MA.$$

Because $ABC$ is equilateral,

$$AB=BC=CA,$$

hence

$$MC+MB=MA.$$

This already proves the claim for points on the arc $BC$. The other arcs should give the analogous relations by symmetry.

For the second part, let the common radius of $\gamma_1,\gamma_2,\gamma_3$ be $r$, and let their centers be $A,B,C$. Since the circles are pairwise tangent, $AB=BC=CA=2r$, so $ABC$ is equilateral. The circle $\gamma$ tangent internally to all three circles has center at the circumcenter of $ABC$. Since $ABC$ is equilateral, this circumcenter is also the center of the circumcircle of $ABC$.

If $M$ lies on $\gamma$, the power of $M$ with respect to $\gamma_i$ equals the square of the tangent length from $M$ to $\gamma_i$. Writing $t_A,t_B,t_C$ for these tangent lengths, we obtain

$$t_A^2=MA^2-r^2, \qquad t_B^2=MB^2-r^2, \qquad t_C^2=MC^2-r^2.$$

The crucial step is to relate these quantities linearly. Assume, as in part 1, that

$$MA=MB+MC.$$

Then

$$\begin{aligned} t_A^2 &=(MB+MC)^2-r^2 \\ &=(MB^2-r^2)+(MC^2-r^2)+2MB\cdot MC+r^2 \\ &=t_B^2+t_C^2+2MB\cdot MC+r^2. \end{aligned}$$

To obtain $t_A=t_B+t_C$, it suffices that

$$(MB^2-r^2)(MC^2-r^2) =(MB\cdot MC+\tfrac{r^2}{2})^2,$$

or an equivalent identity. A direct computation seems unpleasant.

A better idea is to express $MA,MB,MC$ through angles on the circumcircle. Let $R$ be the radius of $\gamma$. Since the centers form an equilateral triangle of side $2r$,

$$R=2r+\frac{2r}{\sqrt3}.$$

If $M$ lies on arc $BC$, write

$$\angle BOM=2x,\qquad \angle MOC=2y,$$

with $x+y=60^\circ$. Then

$$MB=2R\sin x,\qquad MC=2R\sin y,$$

and

$$MA=2R\sin(x+y)=2R\sin60^\circ.$$

Using $R=r!\left(2+\frac2{\sqrt3}\right)$ gives

$$2R\sin60^\circ=r(\sqrt3+1).$$

Hence

$$MA=r(\sqrt3+1),$$

a constant. Then

$$t_A^2=MA^2-r^2 =r^2(3+2\sqrt3+1)-r^2 =r^2(3+2\sqrt3),$$

so

$$t_A=r(1+\sqrt3),$$

also constant.

Now

$$t_B^2=4R^2\sin^2x-r^2.$$

Since $R=r(2+\frac2{\sqrt3})$, one finds

$$4R^2=r^2\frac{16( \sqrt3+1)^2}{3}.$$

Trying $t_B=r\bigl((\sqrt3+1)\sin x+\cos x\bigr)$ gives

$$\begin{aligned} t_B^2 &=r^2\Bigl((\sqrt3+1)^2\sin^2x+\cos^2x +2(\sqrt3+1)\sin x\cos x\Bigr), \end{aligned}$$

which simplifies to

$$r^2\bigl((3+2\sqrt3)\sin^2x+\cos^2x +(\sqrt3+1)\sin2x\bigr).$$

Using $x+y=60^\circ$, a trigonometric factorization may produce

$$t_B=r\bigl(\sin x+(\sqrt3+1)\cos x\bigr) =2r\cos(x-75^\circ)\cos15^\circ.$$

There should be a cleaner route. Since tangent length squared equals power, perhaps

$$t_B=2\sqrt{Rr}\cos x.$$

Indeed,

$$4Rr\cos^2x =4Rr-4Rr\sin^2x.$$

Comparing with $4R^2\sin^2x-r^2$ requires

$$4Rr=(4R^2+4Rr)\sin^2x-r^2,$$

which is impossible identically.
