## Problem

When the hockey tournament (round-robin) ended, it turned out that for any group of teams one can find a team (possibly from the same group) that scored an odd number of points in games against the teams of this group. Prove that the number of teams in the tournament was even. (Loss — 0 points, draw — 1 point, win — 2 points.)

G. A. Kasparov

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad of School Students (1972, 10th grade)

## Exploration

Let $n$ be the number of teams. After the tournament, every pair of teams has exactly one result: win, loss, or draw. Assign to each game the score contribution in points, but reduce everything modulo $2$. A win gives $2 \equiv 0 \pmod 2$, a loss gives $0$, and a draw gives $1$. Hence only draws matter for parity.

Define a simple graph on the set of teams by connecting two teams exactly when they drew their match. For a team $v$ and a subset $S$ of teams, the parity of points that $v$ scored against $S$ equals the number of neighbors of $v$ inside $S$ in this graph, taken modulo $2$.

The condition becomes: for every subset $S$ of vertices, there exists some vertex $v$ (possibly outside $S$) such that the number of neighbors of $v$ inside $S$ is odd.

This is a strong nondegeneracy condition on the adjacency structure of the draw graph. It suggests that the adjacency matrix over $\mathbb{F}_2$ has trivial kernel, hence is invertible. The main obstruction is then to show that a symmetric $0$-diagonal matrix over $\mathbb{F}_2$ can only be invertible in even dimension.

The key point is therefore to translate the combinatorial condition into linear algebra over $\mathbb{F}_2$ and then use the structure theory of alternating bilinear forms.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem: prove that the number of teams is even.

The core difficulty is converting a parity condition on all subsets of vertices into a structural constraint on a matrix over $\mathbb{F}_2$, and then recognizing that this matrix is a symmetric zero-diagonal matrix, hence defines an alternating bilinear form. The conclusion follows from the fact that nondegenerate alternating forms exist only in even dimension.

The expected result is that the adjacency matrix of the “draw graph” is invertible over $\mathbb{F}_2$, which forces $n$ to be even.

## Proof Architecture

We introduce the draw graph $G$ and its adjacency matrix $A$ over $\mathbb{F}_2$.

We prove that the tournament condition implies that for every nonzero vector $x \in \mathbb{F}_2^n$, we have $Ax \neq 0$, hence $A$ is injective and therefore invertible.

We then interpret $A$ as the matrix of an alternating bilinear form $B(x,y)=x^T Ay$ over $\mathbb{F}_2$.

We prove that any nondegenerate alternating bilinear form exists only on even-dimensional spaces by showing that its determinant must vanish in odd dimension.

The crucial step is the equivalence between the tournament condition and injectivity of $A$.
