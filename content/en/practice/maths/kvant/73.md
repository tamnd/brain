## Problem

On a lottery ticket, 8 cells out of 64 must be marked. What is the probability that after the draw, in which 8 cells are also selected from the 64 (and all such possibilities are considered equally likely), exactly 4 of the marked cells are guessed correctly? 5 cells? ... all 8 cells?

## Exploration

Two independent samples of size $8$ are taken from a set of $64$ cells, one sample representing the marked cells on the ticket and the other representing the drawn cells. The event of interest depends only on how many elements the two subsets share.

For a fixed number $k$ of совпcides, the structure is determined by choosing $k$ correctly matched cells from the $8$ marked ones, and simultaneously choosing the remaining $8-k$ drawn cells from the $56$ unmarked ones. This suggests a hypergeometric distribution with a symmetry between the two subsets.

A potential failure point is double counting or misidentifying independence. The correct model is uniform choice of an $8$-subset from a $64$-set, conditioned against a fixed $8$-subset.

## Problem Understanding

The problem asks for the probability that two randomly chosen $8$-element subsets of a $64$-element set intersect in exactly $k$ elements, for $k=4,5,6,7,8$.

This is a Type A classification problem. The core difficulty is counting intersections correctly without overcounting configurations.

The probability for each $k$ is expected to have the hypergeometric form

$$\mathbb{P}(X=k)=\frac{\binom{8}{k}\binom{56}{8-k}}{\binom{64}{8}}.$$

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma identifies that fixing the ticket reduces the problem to counting subsets of size $8$ with prescribed intersection size.

The second lemma counts the number of draws with exactly $k$ correct matches by choosing $k$ from the marked cells and $8-k$ from the unmarked cells.

The third lemma verifies that these cases partition all possible outcomes for each fixed $k$.

The final assembly converts counts into probabilities by dividing by the total number of $8$-subsets of a $64$-element set.

The most delicate step is the combinatorial count of mixed selections, ensuring no overlap or omission between marked and unmarked parts.

## Solution

Fix the set $A$ of marked cells, $|A|=8$, and let $B$ be the randomly drawn set, also with $|B|=8$, chosen uniformly among all $8$-subsets of a $64$-element set $U$.

The event $|A\cap B|=k$ is realized by first selecting exactly $k$ elements from $A$ to belong to $B$, which can be done in $\binom{8}{k}$ ways. After this choice, the remaining $8-k$ elements of $B$ must be chosen from $U\setminus A$, which has $56$ elements, and this can be done in $\binom{56}{8-k}$ ways. Every subset $B$ with exactly $k$ matches arises uniquely from such a decomposition, since $A\cap B$ and $B\setminus A$ are uniquely determined by $B$.

Hence the number of favorable subsets $B$ is $\binom{8}{k}\binom{56}{8-k}$. The total number of possible draws is $\binom{64}{8}$. Therefore,

$$\mathbb{P}(|A\cap B|=k)=\frac{\binom{8}{k}\binom{56}{8-k}}{\binom{64}{8}}.$$

For the required values:

For $k=4$,

$$\mathbb{P}(4)=\frac{\binom{8}{4}\binom{56}{4}}{\binom{64}{8}}.$$

For $k=5$,

$$\mathbb{P}(5)=\frac{\binom{8}{5}\binom{56}{3}}{\binom{64}{8}}.$$

For $k=6$,

$$\mathbb{P}(6)=\frac{\binom{8}{6}\binom{56}{2}}{\binom{64}{8}}.$$

For $k=7$,

$$\mathbb{P}(7)=\frac{\binom{8}{7}\binom{56}{1}}{\binom{64}{8}}.$$

For $k=8$,

$$\mathbb{P}(8)=\frac{\binom{8}{8}\binom{56}{0}}{\binom{64}{8}}=\frac{1}{\binom{64}{8}}.$$

Thus the full collection of probabilities is

$$\boxed{ \left( \frac{\binom{8}{4}\binom{56}{4}}{\binom{64}{8}}, \frac{\binom{8}{5}\binom{56}{3}}{\binom{64}{8}}, \frac{\binom{8}{6}\binom{56}{2}}{\binom{64}{8}}, \frac{\binom{8}{7}\binom{56}{1}}{\binom{64}{8}}, \frac{1}{\binom{64}{8}} \right) }.$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The decomposition of a set $B$ with $|A\cap B|=k$ is uniquely determined by splitting $B$ into $(A\cap B)$ and $(U\setminus A)\cap B$. Any choice of $k$ elements from $A$ and $8-k$ from $U\setminus A$ produces a distinct $B$, since these two parts are disjoint and their union is $B$, eliminating any possibility of double counting.

The total count $\binom{64}{8}$ covers all possible draws because every $8$-subset of $U$ is equally likely under the problem statement. The disjoint union over $k$ of the sets ${B:|A\cap B|=k}$ partitions all $8$-subsets, since every subset has a well-defined intersection size with $A$.

The boundary case $k=8$ reduces correctly to selecting all elements of $A$, leaving no freedom in selection, producing probability $1/\binom{64}{8}$, consistent with the general formula.

## Alternative Approaches

A symmetric probabilistic interpretation treats each of the $8$ drawn cells as sequential hypergeometric sampling without replacement, leading to a binomial-type convolution conditioned on depletion of the marked pool. Another approach uses indicator random variables for each marked cell and computes the distribution of their sum under sampling without replacement, yielding the same hypergeometric law. The combinatorial method remains preferable due to its direct partition of the sample space and minimal probabilistic machinery.
