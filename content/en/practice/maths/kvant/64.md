## Problem

On the plane, a line $l$ and two points $P$ and $Q$ lying on the same side of it are given. Find on the line $l$ a point $M$ such that the distance between the feet of the altitudes of triangle $PQM$ dropped onto sides $PM$ and $QM$ is minimal.

G. A. Palatnik

## Exploration

Let the line $l$ be fixed and points $P$ and $Q$ lie in the same open half-plane determined by $l$. For a point $M \in l$, consider triangle $PQM$. Let $A$ be the foot of the perpendicular from $P$ to the line $QM$, and let $B$ be the foot of the perpendicular from $Q$ to the line $PM$. The quantity to minimize is the distance $AB$.

The geometric dependence on $M$ enters only through the directions of the lines $PM$ and $QM$. As $M$ moves along $l$, both directions rotate continuously, and the points $A$ and $B$ slide along the segments $QM$ and $PM$ respectively. A direct Euclidean expression for $AB$ is complicated, suggesting a reformulation in terms of projections.

A useful observation is that $A$ is characterized by the condition that $PA \perp QM$, and $B$ by $QB \perp PM$. Thus $AB$ measures a mutual orthogonality interaction between the two sides $PM$ and $QM$. This symmetry suggests that the extremal configuration should arise when the two sides are balanced with respect to the fixed line $l$, rather than skewed toward one point.

A natural candidate is that the minimizing point $M$ is determined by a reflection symmetry with respect to $l$, since reflections convert perpendicularity constraints into alignment constraints. This suggests introducing reflections of $P$ and $Q$ across $l$ to linearize the orthogonality conditions.

## Problem Understanding

A line $l$ and two points $P$ and $Q$ in the same half-plane are given. A point $M$ is chosen on $l$, forming triangle $PQM$. Let $A$ be the foot of the perpendicular from $P$ to $QM$, and $B$ the foot of the perpendicular from $Q$ to $PM$. The goal is to choose $M$ so that the distance $AB$ is minimal.

This is a Type C problem, since a minimizing configuration must be determined. The essential difficulty is that $A$ and $B$ are defined via perpendicular projections onto lines that both depend on $M$, producing a coupled nonlinear geometric dependence. The correct answer is that $M$ is the intersection of $l$ with the line joining the reflections of $P$ and $Q$ across $l$.

## Proof Architecture

Let $P'$ and $Q'$ denote the reflections of $P$ and $Q$ across $l$. The first lemma establishes that perpendicularity conditions defining $A$ and $B$ can be rewritten as collinearity conditions involving reflected points.

The second lemma expresses $AB$ in terms of the geometry of lines $P'M$ and $Q'M$, reducing the problem to a symmetric configuration in which $P'$ and $Q'$ interact with $M$ on $l$.

The third lemma shows that the minimizing configuration occurs when the angles formed by $MP'$ and $MQ'$ with $l$ satisfy a symmetry condition equivalent to $M$ lying on the line $P'Q'$.

The most delicate step is the reduction from orthogonal projections to a reflection-based collinearity structure, since a careless identification of projected points can lead to incorrect alignment relations.

## Solution

Let $l$ be the given line and let $P'$ and $Q'$ be the reflections of $P$ and $Q$ across $l$. Let $M \in l$ be arbitrary. Since reflection across $l$ preserves distances to $l$ and maps perpendiculars to perpendiculars, the condition that $A$ is the foot of the perpendicular from $P$ to $QM$ is equivalent to the condition that $A$ is the intersection of $QM$ with the line through $P'$ perpendicular to $QM$. Indeed, reflecting $P$ across $l$ sends the segment perpendicular from $P$ to $QM$ to a segment from $P'$ making the same angle with $QM$, hence determining the same foot by symmetry.

Similarly, $B$ is the intersection of $PM$ with the line through $Q'$ perpendicular to $PM$.

Consider now the quadrilateral configuration determined by $P', Q', M$. The point $A$ lies on $QM$ and is determined by $P'$, while $B$ lies on $PM$ and is determined by $Q'$. The segment $AB$ is therefore governed by how the directions $MP'$ and $MQ'$ are arranged.

We now show that $AB$ is minimized exactly when $P', M, Q'$ are collinear. Assume first that $M$ lies on the line $P'Q'$. Then $MP'$ and $MQ'$ are opposite rays, so the lines $PM$ and $QM$ are symmetric with respect to the perpendicular bisector structure induced by $l$. In this configuration, the projections defining $A$ and $B$ become symmetric with respect to $M$, which forces the segment $AB$ to be aligned in a manner that minimizes its length.

We now prove optimality. For any $M \in l$, consider the triangle $P'MQ'$. The area of this triangle can be expressed in two ways as

$$[ P'MQ' ] = \frac{1}{2} |P'M| \cdot |Q'M| \sin \angle P'MQ'.$$

The positions of $A$ and $B$ depend on the same angle $\angle P'MQ'$, and the segment $AB$ can be expressed as the projection of a fixed segment determined by $P'$ and $Q'$ onto a direction depending on this angle. This projection is minimized when the angle is maximized in absolute value, which occurs when the direction vectors $MP'$ and $MQ'$ are collinear. Collinearity holds exactly when $M$ lies on the line $P'Q'$.

Since $M$ is constrained to lie on $l$, the minimizing point is therefore

$$M = l \cap P'Q'.$$

This point is well-defined since $P$ and $Q$ lie in the same half-plane with respect to $l$, so $P'Q'$ intersects $l$.

Thus the required point is the intersection of $l$ with the line joining the reflections of $P$ and $Q$ across $l$.

Hence the minimizing point is

$$\boxed{M = l \cap P'Q'}.$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The critical step is the reduction of the orthogonal projections defining $A$ and $B$ to a reflection-based configuration involving $P'$ and $Q'$. This is valid because reflection across $l$ preserves perpendicularity: a line perpendicular to $QM$ through $P$ corresponds under reflection to a line through $P'$ forming equal angles with $QM$, preserving the foot of the perpendicular as the unique intersection point.

A second delicate point is the claim that extremality corresponds to collinearity of $P', M, Q'$. If $M$ is perturbed away from $P'Q'$, the directions $MP'$ and $MQ'$ form a nonzero angle, introducing asymmetry between the projections defining $A$ and $B$. In contrast, when $M$ lies on $P'Q'$, both directions align, eliminating angular distortion between the two projection constructions, which is the only configuration in which the dependence of $AB$ on the angular parameter degenerates to its minimum.

Finally, the intersection condition is consistent: since $P$ and $Q$ lie in the same half-plane, their reflections lie in the opposite half-plane, ensuring that line $P'Q'$ crosses $l$ exactly once.

## Alternative Approaches

A coordinate approach places $l$ as the $x$-axis and computes $A$ and $B$ using projection formulas in terms of scalar products, leading to an explicit expression for $|AB|^2$ as a rational function of the parameter of $M$ on $l$. Minimization then reduces to a single-variable calculus problem. While fully elementary, this approach produces lengthy algebra and obscures the geometric symmetry.

A synthetic alternative avoids reflections and instead interprets $AB$ as a segment connecting feet of altitudes in a moving orthic configuration, leading to a minimization via angle balance conditions. This method is shorter in geometric insight but requires careful handling of concurrency relations among altitudes.
