## Problem

In an acute triangle $ABC$, the angle bisector $AD$, the median $BM$, and the altitude $CH$ intersect at a single point. Prove that the angle $BAC$ is greater than $45^\circ$.

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad (1970, 9th grade)

## Exploration

Assign coordinates to the triangle to visualize relationships. For simplicity, place $C$ at the origin $(0,0)$ and $B$ on the x-axis at $(1,0)$. Let $A=(x,y)$ with $y>0$ to ensure an acute triangle. The altitude from $C$ is vertical if $H$ lies on $AB$, the median $BM$ passes through the midpoint of $AC$, and the angle bisector $AD$ divides $BC$ in the ratio $AB:AC$. Attempting explicit coordinate calculations suggests that the concurrency imposes a strong restriction on the shape of triangle $ABC$. This suggests that the required inequality $\angle BAC>45^\circ$ is necessary. The most delicate point is to translate the concurrency condition into a concrete inequality for the triangle’s angles without over-reliance on coordinates.

## Problem Understanding

The problem asks to show that if in an acute triangle $ABC$ the internal angle bisector from $A$, the median from $B$, and the altitude from $C$ meet at a single point, then $\angle BAC>45^\circ$. This is a Type B problem, as it requires proving a statement about an arbitrary acute triangle satisfying a geometric concurrency. The core difficulty is connecting the concurrency condition of three different cevians to a lower bound on one of the triangle's angles. The intuition is that the intersection of such distinct cevians restricts the shape of the triangle, forcing $A$ to be relatively large compared to $B$ and $C$, yielding the inequality $\angle BAC>45^\circ$.

## Proof Architecture

The proof proceeds via the following lemmas and sub-claims.

Lemma 1. If three cevians $AD$, $BM$, and $CH$ in an acute triangle meet at a single point, the triangle can be scaled so that $BC=1$ and the concurrency point has coordinates expressible in terms of the angles. This is justified by standard affine transformations and coordinate normalization.

Lemma 2. The coordinates of the concurrency point expressed in two ways—once along the angle bisector and once along the median—lead to a relation between side lengths and the cosine of $\angle BAC$. This follows from the formula for the intersection of cevians in coordinates.

Lemma 3. The concurrency condition implies a specific inequality on the sides $AB$ and $AC$, which in turn implies $\angle BAC>\pi/4$. This is the hardest step; it requires careful manipulation of ratios arising from the angle bisector theorem and the slope equality for the altitude.

These lemmas assemble into the main argument: converting concurrency into an explicit trigonometric inequality and showing that this inequality forces $\angle BAC>45^\circ$. The critical step is Lemma 3; a miscalculation in ratios would produce an incorrect bound.

## Solution

Let $ABC$ be an acute triangle with the stated concurrency. Normalize by placing $C$ at the origin $(0,0)$ and $B$ at $(1,0)$. Let $A=(a,h)$ with $h>0$. Denote $M$ as the midpoint of $AC$, $D$ as the point on $BC$ where the angle bisector from $A$ meets, and $H$ as the foot of the altitude from $C$.

By the angle bisector theorem, $D$ divides $BC$ in the ratio $AB:AC$, giving coordinates

$D = \frac{AC}{AB+AC}B + \frac{AB}{AB+AC}C = \left(\frac{AC}{AB+AC},0\right).$

The median from $B$ passes through $M=\left(\frac{a+0}{2},\frac{h+0}{2}\right)=\left(\frac{a}{2},\frac{h}{2}\right)$. Its equation is

$y - 0 = \frac{\frac{h}{2}-0}{\frac{a}{2}-1}(x-1) = \frac{h}{a-2}(x-1).$

The altitude from $C$ has equation $y= \frac{h-0}{a-0} (x-0) = \frac{h}{a}x$.

$\frac{h}{a-2}(x_0-1) = \frac{h}{a} x_0.$
