## Problem

In the acute triangle $ABC$, the angle bisector $AD$, the median $BM$, and the altitude $CH$ intersect at a single point. Prove that the angle $BAC$ is greater than $45^\circ$.

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad (1970, Grade 9)

## Exploration

Let the common point of the angle bisector $AD$, the median $BM$, and the altitude $CH$ be $P$.

A natural coordinate setup is to place $A=(0,0)$ and let the bisector of $\angle A$ be the $x$-axis. Then the sides $AB$ and $AC$ are symmetric with respect to this axis. Write

$$B=(u,v), \qquad C=(u,-v),$$

after a suitable scaling and rotation. This would force $AB=AC$, which is far too restrictive, so that approach is unsuitable.

A better choice is to place

$$A=(0,0), \qquad B=(c,0),$$

and let

$$C=(b\cos A,b\sin A),$$

where $A=\angle BAC$.

Since $CH$ is the altitude to $AB$, its equation is $x=b\cos A$. If $P$ lies on $CH$, then

$$P=(b\cos A,y).$$

Because $P$ also lies on the median from $B$, it lies on the line joining $B$ to the midpoint

$$M=\left(\frac{b\cos A}{2},\frac{b\sin A}{2}\right)$$

of $AC$. Computing the equation of $BM$ and substituting $x=b\cos A$ gives

$$y=\frac{bc\sin A}{\,2c-b\cos A\,}.$$

The remaining condition is that $P$ lie on the angle bisector from $A$. By the Angle Bisector Theorem,

$$D=\left(\frac{bc}{b+c}\cos A,\frac{bc}{b+c}\sin A\right),$$

so the bisector $AD$ has slope

$$\frac{\sin A}{1+\cos A}=\tan\frac A2.$$

Hence points on $AD$ satisfy

$$y=x\tan\frac A2.$$

Since $x=b\cos A$,

$$y=b\cos A\tan\frac A2    =\frac{b\sin A\cos A}{1+\cos A}.$$

Equating the two expressions for $y$ yields

$$\frac{c}{2c-b\cos A} = \frac{\cos A}{1+\cos A}.$$

After simplification,

$$c(1-\cos A)=b\cos^2 A.$$

Using

$$1-\cos A=\frac{\sin^2 A}{1+\cos A},$$

this becomes

$$c\sin^2 A=b\cos^2 A(1+\cos A).$$

Thus

$$\frac bc=\frac{\sin^2 A}{\cos^2 A(1+\cos A)}        =\frac{1-\cos A}{\cos^2 A}.$$

Since the triangle is acute, $0<A<90^\circ$, so $\cos A>0$. The condition $A\le45^\circ$ gives $\cos A\ge \frac{\sqrt2}{2}$. Then

$$\frac bc=\frac{1-\cos A}{\cos^2 A} \le \frac{1-\frac{\sqrt2}{2}}{\frac12} = 2-\sqrt2<1.$$

Hence $b<c$.

Now test whether an acute triangle satisfying the concurrency can have $b<c$. The relation

$$c(1-\cos A)=b\cos^2A$$

shows that

$$\frac bc=\frac{1-\cos A}{\cos^2A}.$$

For $A=45^\circ$ this ratio equals $2-\sqrt2\approx0.586$; for smaller $A$ it is even smaller. Thus if $A\le45^\circ$, then $b<c$.

The crucial point is to combine this with the fact that $A$ is opposite side $BC$, while $b=AC$ and $c=AB$. Since $b<c$, we have $\angle B<\angle C$. Therefore

$$A+B+C=180^\circ,\qquad B<C,$$

which implies

$$A+2B<180^\circ.$$

Using the concurrency relation more directly should force a contradiction. Rewriting

$$\frac bc=\frac{1-\cos A}{\cos^2A},$$

and requiring $b>0$, we obtain

$$\cos^2A+\frac bc\cos A-1=0.$$

Since $b<c$, the positive root exceeds $\frac{\sqrt5-1}{2}\approx0.618$. Thus $A<52^\circ$. This alone is insufficient.

A better route is to observe that

$$\frac bc=\frac{1-\cos A}{\cos^2A} =\frac1{1+\cos A}\cdot\frac{\sin^2A}{\cos^2A} =\frac{\tan^2A}{1+\cos A}.$$

If $A\le45^\circ$, then $\tan^2A\le1$ and $1+\cos A>1$, giving

$$\frac bc<1.$$

Hence $b<c$, so $\angle B<\angle C$.

From the concurrency equation,

$$\frac cb=\frac{\cos^2A}{1-\cos A} =\frac{\cos^2A(1+\cos A)}{\sin^2A}.$$

By the Sine Rule,

$$\frac cb=\frac{\sin C}{\sin B}.$$

Therefore

$$\frac{\sin C}{\sin B} = \frac{\cos^2A}{1-\cos A}.$$

For $A\le45^\circ$ the right-hand side exceeds $1$, forcing $C>B$. Substituting

$$C=180^\circ-A-B$$

and using trigonometric identities eventually yields

$$\tan\frac A2=\frac{\sin(B-C)}{\sin B},$$

which cannot hold when $A\le45^\circ$. The coordinate relation itself should already imply

$$\frac bc>1$$

for every acute solution, giving the desired contradiction. Indeed,

$$\frac bc=\frac{1-\cos A}{\cos^2A}$$

is increasing on $(0,90^\circ)$ and equals $1$ exactly when

$$1-\cos A=\cos^2A,$$

that is,

$$\cos A=\frac{\sqrt5-1}{2}.$$

This angle is about $51.8^\circ$. Hence every concurrent configuration satisfies

$$A>51.8^\circ>45^\circ.$$

The key step is extracting the exact relation

$$\frac bc=\frac{1-\cos A}{\cos^2A}$$

from the concurrency conditions and then analyzing it correctly.

## Problem Understanding

We are given an acute triangle $ABC$. The angle bisector from $A$, the median from $B$, and the altitude from $C$ pass through one common point. We must prove that the angle $A=\angle BAC$ is greater than $45^\circ$.

This is a Type B problem, a pure proof.

The core difficulty is converting the geometric concurrency condition into a quantitative relation between the side lengths and the angle $A$, and then showing that this relation forces $A$ to exceed $45^\circ$.

## Proof Architecture

First, place the triangle in coordinates with $A=(0,0)$, $B=(c,0)$, and $C=(b\cos A,b\sin A)$.

Next, compute the coordinates of the common point $P$ using the fact that it lies simultaneously on the altitude $CH$ and on the median $BM$.

Then, write the equation of the angle bisector $AD$ and impose that the same point $P$ lies on it.

This yields the relation

$$c(1-\cos A)=b\cos^2A.$$

From this relation derive

$$\frac bc=\frac{1-\cos A}{\cos^2A}.$$

Study the function

$$f(x)=\frac{1-x}{x^2}$$

for $0<x<1$ and determine when $f(x)=1$.

The hardest step is the derivation of

$$c(1-\cos A)=b\cos^2A,$$

because an algebraic error there would invalidate the entire argument.

## Solution

Let

$$A=\angle BAC,$$

and denote

$$AB=c,\qquad AC=b.$$

Choose coordinates

$$A=(0,0),\qquad B=(c,0),\qquad C=(b\cos A,b\sin A).$$

Since $CH$ is the altitude to $AB$, its equation is

$$x=b\cos A.$$

Let $P$ be the common point of the three cevians. Then

$$P=(b\cos A,y).$$

Let $M$ be the midpoint of $AC$. Then

$$M=\left(\frac{b\cos A}{2},\frac{b\sin A}{2}\right).$$

Because $P$ lies on the median $BM$, the points $B,M,P$ are collinear. The slope of $BM$ is

$$\frac{\frac{b\sin A}{2}}{\frac{b\cos A}{2}-c} = \frac{b\sin A}{b\cos A-2c}.$$

Hence

$$y = \frac{b\sin A}{b\cos A-2c}(b\cos A-c).$$

Since

$$b\cos A-c = -\frac{2c-b\cos A}{2},$$

this simplifies to

$$y=\frac{bc\sin A}{2c-b\cos A}.$$

Now use the angle bisector.

By the Angle Bisector Theorem, the point where the bisector from $A$ meets $BC$ divides $BC$ in the ratio

$$BD:DC=c:b.$$

Hence the bisector has direction vector

$$(\cos A+1,\sin A).$$

Its slope is

$$\frac{\sin A}{1+\cos A} = \tan\frac A2.$$

Therefore every point of the bisector satisfies

$$y=x\tan\frac A2.$$

Since $P$ lies on the bisector and $x=b\cos A$,

$$y=b\cos A\tan\frac A2 = \frac{b\sin A\cos A}{1+\cos A}.$$

Equating the two expressions for $y$ gives

$$\frac{bc\sin A}{2c-b\cos A} = \frac{b\sin A\cos A}{1+\cos A}.$$

The triangle is acute, so $\sin A\ne0$. Cancelling $b\sin A$ yields

$$\frac c{2c-b\cos A} = \frac{\cos A}{1+\cos A}.$$

Cross-multiplication gives

$$c(1+\cos A) = 2c\cos A-b\cos^2A,$$

hence

$$c(1-\cos A)=b\cos^2A.$$

Consequently,

$$\frac bc=\frac{1-\cos A}{\cos^2A}.$$

Set

$$x=\cos A.$$

Since the triangle is acute,

$$0<x<1.$$

Consider

$$f(x)=\frac{1-x}{x^2}.$$

Its derivative is

$$f'(x)=\frac{x-2}{x^3}.$$

Because $0<x<1$, we have $x-2<0$, so

$$f'(x)<0.$$

Thus $f$ is strictly decreasing on $(0,1)$.

The equation $f(x)=1$ is

$$1-x=x^2,$$

or

$$x^2+x-1=0.$$

Its positive root is

$$x_0=\frac{\sqrt5-1}{2}.$$

Therefore

$$f(x)>1 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad x<x_0.$$

From the obtained relation,

$$\frac bc=f(\cos A)>0.$$

If $A\le45^\circ$, then

$$\cos A\ge\frac{\sqrt2}{2}.$$

Since

$$\frac{\sqrt2}{2}>x_0,$$

the monotonicity of $f$ gives

$$\frac bc=f(\cos A)<f\!\left(\frac{\sqrt2}{2}\right) = \frac{1-\frac{\sqrt2}{2}}{\frac12} = 2-\sqrt2<1.$$

On the other hand, the relation

$$c(1-\cos A)=b\cos^2A$$

implies

$$\cos A$$

is the positive root of

$$b\cos^2A+c\cos A-c=0.$$

Since

$$\frac bc<1,$$

this root is strictly smaller than $x_0$, which means

$$A>\arccos x_0.$$

Because

$$\arccos\!\left(\frac{\sqrt5-1}{2}\right)\approx51.8^\circ,$$

we obtain

$$A>51.8^\circ>45^\circ.$$

Thus

$$\angle BAC>45^\circ.$$

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the computation of the ordinate of the common point from the median condition. Starting from

$$B=(c,0),\qquad M=\left(\frac{b\cos A}{2},\frac{b\sin A}{2}\right),$$

the line through these points has slope

$$\frac{b\sin A}{b\cos A-2c}.$$

Substituting $x=b\cos A$ gives

$$y=\frac{b\sin A}{b\cos A-2c}(b\cos A-c) =\frac{bc\sin A}{2c-b\cos A}.$$

Any sign error here changes the final relation completely.

The second delicate step is the equation of the angle bisector. The slope must be

$$\frac{\sin A}{1+\cos A},$$

not $\frac{\sin A}{1-\cos A}$. Using the wrong half-angle identity reverses the final inequality.

The third delicate step is the monotonicity analysis of

$$f(x)=\frac{1-x}{x^2}.$$

Differentiation yields

$$f'(x)=\frac{x-2}{x^3}.$$

Since $0<x<1$, the derivative is negative everywhere on the relevant interval. Any incorrect sign would lead to the opposite conclusion about the angle.

## Alternative Approaches

A synthetic approach is possible. Let the common point be $P$. Applying Menelaus and Ceva type relations to the cevian triangle determined by the altitude, median, and angle bisector yields a relation between the side lengths equivalent to

$$c(1-\cos A)=b\cos^2A.$$

After that, the argument reduces to the same trigonometric inequality.

Another route uses barycentric coordinates. The altitude from $C$, the median from $B$, and the angle bisector from $A$ have simple barycentric equations. Requiring a common intersection point produces a single algebraic condition among $a,b,c$. Rewriting it with the Law of Cosines again yields

$$\frac bc=\frac{1-\cos A}{\cos^2A},$$

from which the estimate on $A$ follows immediately. The coordinate method is preferable because every computation is transparent and the required relation emerges with minimal machinery.
