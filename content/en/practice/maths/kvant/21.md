## Problem

Inside a square of side length 1, several circles are placed whose total circumference is 10. Prove that there exists a line that intersects at least four of these circles.

Evening Mathematics School at Moscow State University (MSU)

## Exploration

A line intersects a circle if the distance from the center of the circle to the line does not exceed the radius. Let the circles have radii $r_1,\dots,r_n$. Their total circumference is $10$, hence

$$2\pi\sum_{i=1}^n r_i=10,$$

so

$$\sum_{i=1}^n r_i=\frac{5}{\pi}.$$

The statement asks for a line meeting at least four circles. A natural way to proceed is to average over all lines of a fixed direction.

Take vertical lines $x=t$, $0\le t\le1$. For a circle of radius $r$ and center $(a,b)$, the vertical line $x=t$ meets the circle exactly when

$$|t-a|\le r.$$

Among the parameter values $t\in[0,1]$, the set of such $t$ has length at least $2r$. If the center is close to a side of the square, part of the interval $(a-r,a+r)$ may lie outside $[0,1]$, but then the circle itself must be tangent to or contained in the square, which forces $a\ge r$ and $a\le1-r$. Hence $(a-r,a+r)\subset[0,1]$, and the length is exactly $2r$.

Therefore, if $N(t)$ denotes the number of circles intersected by the line $x=t$,

$$\int_0^1 N(t)\,dt=\sum_{i=1}^n 2r_i =2\sum_{i=1}^n r_i =\frac{10}{\pi}.$$

Since $10/\pi>3$, the average value of $N(t)$ exceeds $3$. Hence some vertical line intersects at least four circles.

The only point requiring care is the claim that a circle inside the unit square satisfies $r\le a\le1-r$. That guarantees the interval of intersecting vertical lines has length exactly $2r$.

## Problem Understanding

Several circles lie entirely inside a unit square. The sum of their circumferences equals $10$. We must prove that some line intersects at least four of the circles.

This is a Type B problem. The claim is given and must be proved.

The core difficulty is to convert the information about total circumference into information about intersections with a line. The natural bridge is an averaging argument: each circle contributes a certain amount to the family of vertical lines that meet it.

## Proof Architecture

First lemma: if a circle of radius $r$ lies inside the unit square and has center $(a,b)$, then $r\le a\le1-r$.

Reason: the distance from the center to each vertical side of the square must be at least the radius.

Second lemma: for such a circle, the set of vertical lines $x=t$ that intersect the circle has parameter length exactly $2r$.

Reason: the circle is intersected precisely when $|t-a|\le r$, and by the first lemma the interval $(a-r,a+r)$ lies inside $[0,1]$.

Third lemma: if $N(t)$ denotes the number of circles intersected by $x=t$, then

$$\int_0^1N(t)\,dt=2\sum r_i.$$

Reason: sum the contributions of all circles and use the second lemma.

The hardest step is the second lemma, because an incorrect treatment of circles near the boundary could change the contribution from $2r$ to a smaller value.

## Solution

Let the circles have radii $r_1,\dots,r_n$ and centers $(a_1,b_1),\dots,(a_n,b_n)$.

Since the total circumference is $10$,

$$2\pi\sum_{i=1}^n r_i=10,$$

hence

$$\sum_{i=1}^n r_i=\frac{5}{\pi}.$$

For each $t\in[0,1]$, let $N(t)$ be the number of circles intersected by the vertical line $x=t$.

Consider one circle, with radius $r$ and center $(a,b)$.

Because the circle lies inside the unit square, its center is at distance at least $r$ from each vertical side. Thus

$$r\le a\le1-r.$$

A vertical line $x=t$ intersects this circle exactly when the distance from the center to the line does not exceed the radius:

$$|t-a|\le r.$$

Therefore the set of parameters $t$ for which the line $x=t$ intersects the circle is the interval

$$[a-r,a+r].$$

Since $r\le a\le1-r$, this interval is contained in $[0,1]$ and has length

$$(a+r)-(a-r)=2r.$$

Now integrate $N(t)$ over $[0,1]$. Each circle contributes $2r_i$ to the integral, because it is counted for exactly a set of parameter values of length $2r_i$. Summing over all circles,

$$\int_0^1 N(t)\,dt = \sum_{i=1}^n 2r_i = 2\sum_{i=1}^n r_i = \frac{10}{\pi}.$$

Since

$$\frac{10}{\pi}>3,$$

the average value of $N(t)$ on the interval $[0,1]$ exceeds $3$.

Assume that every vertical line intersected at most three circles. Then $N(t)\le3$ for all $t$, and therefore

$$\int_0^1N(t)\,dt\le3,$$

which contradicts

$$\int_0^1N(t)\,dt=\frac{10}{\pi}>3.$$

Hence there exists some $t\in[0,1]$ such that

$$N(t)\ge4.$$

The corresponding vertical line $x=t$ intersects at least four circles.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate point is the boundary condition. If a circle of radius $r$ is contained in the unit square and has center $(a,b)$, then the leftmost point of the circle has $x$-coordinate $a-r$. Containment in the square gives $a-r\ge0$, hence $a\ge r$. Similarly, the rightmost point has coordinate $a+r\le1$, hence $a\le1-r$. No further assumptions are needed.

The second delicate point is the computation of the contribution $2r$. A vertical line $x=t$ intersects the circle precisely when its distance from the center equals $|t-a|$ and satisfies $|t-a|\le r$. The admissible parameters form the interval $[a-r,a+r]$. Because this interval lies entirely inside $[0,1]$, its length is exactly $2r$, not merely at most $2r$.

The third delicate point is the averaging argument. The identity

$$\int_0^1N(t)\,dt=\sum_i2r_i$$

counts the same set of incidences in two ways. For each circle, the measure of the parameter set of intersecting vertical lines is $2r_i$. Summing these measures gives the integral of the counting function $N(t)$.

## Alternative Approaches

Instead of vertical lines, one may choose a uniformly random vertical line $x=t$ with $t\in[0,1]$. For a circle of radius $r_i$, the probability that the chosen line intersects it is $2r_i$. The expected number of circles intersected is therefore

$$\sum_i 2r_i=\frac{10}{\pi}>3.$$

If every line met at most three circles, the expectation could not exceed $3$. Hence some line meets at least four circles. This is the probabilistic version of the same averaging argument.

Another approach is to project each circle onto the bottom side of the square. The projection is an interval of length $2r_i$. The total length of all projections is

$$2\sum r_i=\frac{10}{\pi}>3.$$

A point of the side covered by at least four projection intervals must exist, because otherwise the total multiplicity of coverage would be at most $3$. The vertical line through that point intersects at least four circles. The proof above is preferable because it expresses the multiplicity directly through the function $N(t)$ and avoids introducing a separate projection construction.
