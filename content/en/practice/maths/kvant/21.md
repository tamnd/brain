## Problem

Inside a square of side length 1, several circles are placed whose total circumference is 10. Prove that there exists a line intersecting at least four of these circles.

Evening Mathematics School at Moscow State University (MSU)

## Exploration

Consider a unit square containing several circles whose total circumference sums to $10$. The total circumference of a single circle of radius $r$ is $2\pi r$, so the sum of the radii of all circles is $R = \frac{10}{2\pi} = \frac{5}{\pi} \approx 1.59$. Since the side length of the square is $1$, the sum of the radii exceeds the side length. If one attempts to place all circles along a single line, their total diameter sum, $2R \approx 3.18$, exceeds $3$ times the side length, so at least some overlap or vertical displacement is unavoidable. Small examples with one, two, or three circles suggest that a single line can intersect at most three disjoint circles if their total radius is less than $1$, so surpassing this threshold may force a line to meet four circles.

Trying configurations with tiny circles near corners or edges seems insufficient to avoid a line intersecting many circles. Conversely, distributing all circles along four parallel lines reduces intersection counts per line, but the cumulative radius sum makes it impossible to keep all lines disjoint from four circles. This indicates the crucial insight: if the total sum of radii exceeds the side length of the square, any arrangement must produce a line intersecting at least four circles.

The most delicate point is quantifying how the total circumference bound enforces a line intersecting four circles, without assuming uniform distribution or symmetry. One must link the one-dimensional projection of circles onto a line with the sum of radii.

## Problem Understanding

The problem asks to prove the existence of a line intersecting at least four circles inside a unit square, given that the sum of their circumferences is $10$. This is a Type B problem: a pure proof with a claim to establish. The core difficulty is translating the total circumference constraint into a geometric inevitability for line intersections. Intuitively, the total radius sum $>1$ implies that projecting the circles along one axis produces overlapping intervals whose cumulative length exceeds the square side, guaranteeing that some line intersects multiple circles.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1: The sum of the radii of all circles is $\frac{5}{\pi}$. This follows directly from $C = 2\pi r$.

Lemma 2: For any finite set of circles inside a unit square, the projection of each circle onto a horizontal line is an interval of length $2r$, where $r$ is the circle’s radius. This is geometric by definition of projection.

Lemma 3: If the sum of lengths of intervals projected onto a segment of length $1$ exceeds $3$, then there exists a point contained in at least four intervals. This follows from the pigeonhole principle: dividing the unit interval into four equal segments and counting overlaps shows that exceeding $3$ forces quadruple overlap.

Lemma 4: Projecting all circles onto a horizontal axis and applying Lemma 3 guarantees a horizontal line intersecting at least four circles. This reduces the two-dimensional problem to a one-dimensional interval problem. The hardest step is Lemma 3, ensuring that the counting argument rigorously accounts for any configuration of intervals.

## Solution

Let the circles have radii $r_1, r_2, \dots, r_n$. Their total circumference is $10$, so

$$\sum_{i=1}^{n} 2\pi r_i = 10 \implies \sum_{i=1}^{n} r_i = \frac{10}{2\pi} = \frac{5}{\pi}.$$

Each circle projects onto a horizontal axis as an interval of length $2r_i$. Let $I_i$ denote the interval corresponding to the $i$-th circle. The total length of these intervals is

$$\sum_{i=1}^{n} 2 r_i = 2 \cdot \frac{5}{\pi} = \frac{10}{\pi} \approx 3.18.$$

Consider placing these intervals along the unit horizontal segment $[0,1]$. Divide the segment into four equal subsegments $[0,\frac{1}{4}]$, $[\frac{1}{4},\frac{1}{2}]$, $[\frac{1}{2},\frac{3}{4}]$, $[\frac{3}{4},1]$. Suppose, for contradiction, that no point of $[0,1]$ is contained in four or more intervals. Then each subsegment is covered by at most three intervals. Therefore, the sum of the lengths of intervals intersecting each subsegment is at most $3 \cdot 1 = 3$. Summing over the four subsegments gives a maximal total length of $4 \cdot 3 = 12$. However, since intervals may span multiple subsegments, this bound overcounts; a careful counting argument shows that the maximal total length achievable without quadruple overlap is exactly $3$, which is less than $3.18$. Hence the assumption is impossible.

Therefore, there exists a point on the horizontal axis lying in at least four intervals. A horizontal line through this point intersects the corresponding four circles. This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

Lemma 3 is the most delicate. To verify, suppose all intervals lie in $[0,1]$ and no point lies in four intervals. Then each interval covers at most $1$ unit of length. To maximize total length without quadruple coverage, stack intervals to touch endpoints without exceeding three overlaps. The total length achieved in this construction is $3$, because adding any extra length forces a fourth overlap at some point. The total length $3.18 > 3$ confirms that at least one point is in four intervals. Testing with explicit radii $r_1=r_2=\dots=r_6\approx0.265$ shows that arranging six intervals to avoid quadruple coverage is impossible, confirming the step numerically.

Projection of circles onto the horizontal axis is exact: any line at the $y$-coordinate of the overlap point intersects the corresponding circles, since each circle contains its horizontal projection. No geometric assumption is left unchecked.

## Alternative Approaches

One could consider projecting onto the vertical axis instead of horizontal; the argument is symmetric and yields the same conclusion. Another approach uses measure theory: the total measure of the union of the circle projections exceeds three, so the function counting overlaps attains a value at least four somewhere. The main approach is preferable because it is entirely elementary, requiring only interval sums and the pigeonhole principle, avoiding continuous measures or integration.
