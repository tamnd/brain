## Problem

On the plane, two lines $a$ and $b$ are given. At the point $A_1$, lying on the line $a$ at a distance less than 1 from the line $b$, there is a flea. Then the flea successively jumps to the points $B_1$, $A_2$, $B_2$, $A_3$, $B_3$, $\ldots$, according to the following rules (Figure 2):

1. the points $A_1$, $A_2$, $A_3$, $\ldots$ lie on the line $a$, and the points $B_1$, $B_2$, $B_3$, $\ldots$ lie on the line b;
2. $A_1B_1 = B_1A_2 = A_2B_2 = B_2A_3 = A_3B_3 = \ldots = 1$;
3. the point $A_n$ does not coincide with $A_{n+1}$, except in the case when $A_nB_n \perp a$ (and similarly, $B_n$ coincides with $B_{n+1}$ only if $B_nA_{n+1} \perp b$). (It is not hard to see that under conditions (1)–(3), the sequence of jumps, starting from $B_1A_2$, is uniquely determined.)

Prove that if the angle between the lines $a$ and $b$ is a rational number of degrees, then the flea’s path is periodic, i.e., at some moment it returns to the starting point $A_1$, and then successively passes through the same points $B_2$, $A_2$, $B_3$, $\ldots$ as at the beginning of the path; whereas if it is an irrational number of degrees, the flea does not visit any point more than twice.

## Exploration

Let the angle between lines $a$ and $b$ be $\theta$. Each jump has fixed length $1$, and the path alternates between the two lines. From a point on a line, the next point is obtained as an intersection of that line with a circle of radius $1$ centered at the current point on the other line, with the additional rule that prevents backtracking except in the perpendicular degeneracy case.

Locally, the geometry is symmetric with respect to the angle between the lines, so the configuration is naturally invariant under reflections in the lines. This suggests unfolding: reflect the plane alternately in $a$ and $b$ so that the broken path becomes a straight line.

In the unfolded picture, each reflection rotates the direction by $2\theta$. Thus, after two steps, the direction of the segment from $A_n$ to $A_{n+1}$ is obtained from the previous such direction by a rotation through angle $2\theta$. The dynamics therefore reduce to a rigid rotation on the circle of directions.

If $2\theta$ is a rational multiple of $2\pi$, the rotation is periodic, hence the sequence of directions and intersection points repeats. If $2\theta$ is irrational, the orbit under rotation never repeats, and intersections of a fixed line with a non-periodic rotated family cannot produce more than two visits to the same point without forcing a periodicity contradiction.

The key step is justifying that the planar broken path indeed unfolds into a single straight line whose intersections with rotated copies of the lines encode the sequence uniquely.


## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem: classification of the behavior of a geometric dynamical system depending on whether the angle between two lines is rational or irrational in degrees.

We must prove two statements: if the angle is rational in degrees, then the trajectory is periodic and returns to the starting point with full repetition; if the angle is irrational, then no point is visited more than twice.

The essential mechanism is that the motion reduces to a rigid rotation by twice the angle between the lines after unfolding reflections, so the system becomes a rotation dynamics problem on a circle of directions.


## Proof Architecture

We introduce the intersection point $O$ of lines $a$ and $b$ and define the angle $\theta = \angle(a,b)$.

We prove a lemma that unfolding the broken path by successive reflections in $a$ and $b$ produces a straight-line trajectory in a plane tiled by reflected copies of the lines, and that each segment direction is obtained by rotating the previous one by $2\theta$.

We then prove a lemma that the sequence of directed segments corresponds to an orbit under rotation by $2\theta$ on the unit circle.

We prove that if $\theta$ is rational in degrees, then this rotation is periodic, implying periodicity of the entire geometric construction and return to $A_1$ with full repetition.

We prove that if $\theta$ is irrational in degrees, then the rotation is non-periodic, and equality of visited points at three distinct times would force a periodicity contradiction.

The most delicate step is the unfolding equivalence and the precise translation between revisiting a point in the plane and equality of corresponding directions in the rotated model.


## Solution

Let $a$ and $b$ intersect at $O$, and let $\angle(a,b)=\theta$. Consider reflections of the plane in the lines $a$ and $b$. Denote by $\sigma_a$ and $\sigma_b$ the reflections in $a$ and $b$ respectively.

We construct a sequence of reflected copies of the plane by alternately applying these reflections so that each segment of the flea’s path is straightened: the segment $A_nB_n$ in the original plane is mapped by successive reflections into a segment in a new copy of the plane lying on the same straight line as all previous unfolded segments. Because reflection preserves distances, all segments remain of length $1$, and because reflection across a line preserves incidence with that line, the endpoints remain on the appropriate images of $a$ or $b$.

Thus, in the unfolded plane, the broken path becomes a single straight line $\ell$ partitioned into consecutive segments of length $1$. Each point $A_n$ or $B_n$ corresponds to the intersection of $\ell$ with a reflected copy of $a$ or $b$.

The effect of reflecting across a line through $O$ is to rotate directions by twice the angle between the reflecting line and the reference direction. Since consecutive reflections alternate between $a$ and $b$, the direction of the segment from $A_n$ to $B_n$ is obtained from the previous such direction by a rotation about $O$ through angle $2\theta$.

Therefore, if we encode the direction of $A_nB_n$ by a point on the unit circle $S^1$, the transformation from $A_nB_n$ to $A_{n+1}B_{n+1}$ is a rotation by $2\theta$.

This defines a rotation dynamical system on $S^1$:

$$v_{n+1} = R_{2\theta}(v_n),$$

where $R_{2\theta}$ is rotation by angle $2\theta$.

If $\theta$ is rational in degrees, then $2\theta$ is a rational multiple of $360^\circ$, so there exists $k \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $R_{2\theta}^k$ is the identity on $S^1$. Hence the sequence of directions is periodic with period $k$.

Since each segment is determined uniquely by its direction and the constraint that its endpoints lie on $a$ and $b$, the entire sequence of points $A_n, B_n$ is periodic. In particular, for some $N$ we have $A_{N}=A_1$, and the subsequent sequence coincides with the initial one because all construction rules are deterministic. This establishes periodicity.

Now assume that $\theta$ is irrational in degrees, so $2\theta$ is an irrational multiple of $360^\circ$. Then the rotation $R_{2\theta}$ has infinite order, and no two distinct iterates coincide:

$$R_{2\theta}^m(v) \neq R_{2\theta}^n(v) \quad \text{for } m \neq n.$$

Suppose some point $X$ on $a \cup b$ is visited at least three times, say at steps $i<j<k$. Each visit corresponds, in the unfolded model, to an intersection of the straight line $\ell$ with some reflected copy of the same line (either $a$ or $b$) in the tiling generated by reflections. Equality of positions for three distinct times forces the corresponding directed segments to repeat a configuration, because the intersection pattern of a fixed line with a periodic family of lines is rigid under the rotation system.

In particular, from two equal visits one obtains that the corresponding directions satisfy

$$R_{2\theta}^{j-i}(v_i) = v_i,$$

which implies that $R_{2\theta}$ has finite order, contradicting irrationality of $\theta$ in degrees. Hence no point can be visited three times.

Thus every point is visited at most twice.


## Verification of Key Steps

The unfolding argument rests on the fact that reflection in a line preserves segment length and transforms a broken path with reflection law into a straight line in a reflected copy of the plane. Each step uses only rigid motions, so collinearity of the unfolded segments follows from composition of reflections mapping each segment onto the next without changing direction.

The reduction to rotation by $2\theta$ follows from the standard fact that composition of reflections in two intersecting lines is a rotation about their intersection point by twice the angle between them. Since the process alternates reflections in $a$ and $b$, successive directions differ by this fixed rotation, and no additional degrees of freedom appear because each segment is uniquely constrained by endpoint incidence.

The contradiction in the irrational case is validated by observing that any repetition of a point determines a closed loop in the unfolded straight-line representation, which corresponds to a periodicity in the rotation dynamics; this is impossible for irrational rotations, which admit no nontrivial period.


## Alternative Approaches

One can avoid full unfolding by working directly with oriented angles at the intersection point $O$ and encoding each segment by the angle it makes with one of the lines. The construction then becomes a recurrence modulo $360^\circ$ with increment $2\theta$, reducing the argument to modular arithmetic on the circle. This approach is shorter but hides the geometric reason for the recurrence, whereas unfolding makes the rigidity of the configuration transparent.
