## Problem

Grid of lines shown on the cover of this issue of the journal consists of concentric circles of radii 1, 2, 3, 4, … centered at the point $O$, a straight line $l$ passing through the point $O$, and all possible tangents to the circles parallel to $l$. The entire plane is partitioned by these lines into cells, which are colored in a checkerboard pattern. In the chain of red points shown in the figure, every two adjacent points are opposite vertices of a blue cell. Prove that all points of such an infinite chain lie on a single parabola (hence the entire figure appears to be woven from white and blue parabolas).

## Exploration

Choose a coordinate system with the center $O$ of the circles as the origin and the line $l$ as the $x$-axis. Then the circles in the grid are given by equations $x^2 + y^2 = n^2$ for integers $n \ge 1$, while the tangents to these circles parallel to $l$ are horizontal lines $y = m$ for integers $m \in \mathbb{Z}$. The partition is therefore formed by intersections of the annuli between consecutive circles with horizontal strips between consecutive integer heights.

Each cell is bounded by arcs of two consecutive circles $r = n$ and $r = n+1$ together with segments of the lines $y = m$ and $y = m+1$. Thus each cell corresponds naturally to a pair of integers $(n,m)$.

Opposite vertices of such a cell correspond to opposite corners in this curvilinear rectangle, hence to the intersection points corresponding to $(n,m)$ and $(n+1,m+1)$, or symmetrically $(n,m+1)$ and $(n+1,m)$ depending on the checkerboard coloring. The chain condition forces a consistent choice of diagonal, hence a monotone evolution in both indices.

This suggests that along the chain, the quantity $n - m$ remains constant. Translating this into geometric coordinates yields a relation between $r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$ and $y$, which should reduce to a quadratic equation in $x$ and $y$, hence a conic. The structure strongly indicates a parabola after eliminating the square root.

The key point is to confirm that the combinatorial “opposite vertex” rule forces a linear relation between the discrete indices and then convert it into a Cartesian equation without ambiguity in sign choices for the circle intersections.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem: determine all possible infinite chains of red points satisfying the condition that consecutive points are opposite vertices of a blue cell in the given partition.

The goal is to prove that every such chain lies on a single parabola. The key difficulty is translating the combinatorial adjacency condition in a mixed polar Cartesian grid into a rigid analytic constraint on coordinates. The expected outcome is a one parameter family of parabolas arising from a constant difference between radial level and horizontal level indices.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma establishes a coordinate model in which the grid consists of circles $x^2 + y^2 = n^2$ and horizontal lines $y = m$.

The second lemma identifies each cell with a pair of integers $(n,m)$ and shows that opposite vertices correspond to simultaneous shifts $(n,m) \mapsto (n+1,m+1)$ or $(n,m) \mapsto (n+1,m-1)$ depending on fixed coloring orientation.

The third lemma proves that along any admissible chain the quantity $n - m$ is constant.

The fourth lemma converts the constraint $n - m = c$ into the analytic relation $\sqrt{x^2 + y^2} = y + c$ after fixing the correct branch of the circle coordinate determined by the chain orientation.

The fifth lemma eliminates the square root and shows that all points satisfy $x^2 = 2cy + c^2$, which is a parabola.

The most delicate step is the global consistency of sign choices when passing from discrete indices to the equation involving $\sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$, since a wrong branch would produce extraneous curves.

## Solution

Place the origin at $O$ and choose coordinates so that the line $l$ is the $x$-axis. The circles of the construction are then given by equations $x^2 + y^2 = n^2$ for integers $n \ge 1$, and the tangents to these circles parallel to $l$ are exactly the horizontal lines $y = m$ for integers $m \in \mathbb{Z}$. These two families of curves partition the plane into regions indexed by integer pairs $(n,m)$, where $n$ records the annulus between circles $r=n$ and $r=n+1$ and $m$ records the strip between $y=m$ and $y=m+1$.

Each cell of the partition is bounded by arcs of the circles $r=n$ and $r=n+1$ together with segments of the horizontal lines $y=m$ and $y=m+1$. Hence each cell has four vertices, which are intersection points of the form given by simultaneous solutions of $x^2 + y^2 = n^2$ or $x^2 + y^2 = (n+1)^2$ with $y=m$ or $y=m+1$.

Fix a cell corresponding to $(n,m)$. Its vertices are naturally ordered so that opposite vertices are obtained by changing both indices simultaneously, from $(n,m)$ to $(n+1,m+1)$ or from $(n,m+1)$ to $(n+1,m)$. The checkerboard coloring selects one of the two diagonals as “blue adjacency”, and the hypothesis states that consecutive red points are exactly opposite vertices of blue cells, hence correspond to a consistent diagonal choice throughout the chain.

Assign to each red point in the chain the unique pair $(n_k,m_k)$ determined by the cell structure in which it serves as a vertex. The opposite vertex condition implies that successive points satisfy either $(n_{k+1},m_{k+1}) = (n_k+1,m_k+1)$ for all $k$ or the symmetric alternative $(n_{k+1},m_{k+1}) = (n_k+1,m_k-1)$ for all $k$. Reversing orientation of the $y$-axis if necessary reduces to the first case, so one obtains $n_k - m_k = c$ for a constant integer $c$ independent of $k$.

For each point in the chain we therefore have simultaneously $x_k^2 + y_k^2 = n_k^2$ and $y_k \in (m_k, m_k+1)$ with $n_k - m_k = c$. The monotonic structure of the chain ensures that each point lies in the upper half-plane where the branch $n_k = \sqrt{x_k^2 + y_k^2}$ is compatible with the relation $n_k = y_k + c$, since $m_k$ and $y_k$ differ by less than $1$ while $n_k$ and $m_k$ differ by exactly $c$.

Hence for every point in the chain the relation $\sqrt{x_k^2 + y_k^2} = y_k + c$ holds. Squaring both sides yields

$$x_k^2 + y_k^2 = y_k^2 + 2cy_k + c^2,$$

which simplifies to

$$x_k^2 = 2cy_k + c^2.$$

This is the equation of a parabola with axis parallel to the $y$-axis when $c \neq 0$, and a degenerate limiting case when $c=0$, in which the equation forces $x_k = 0$ for all $k$, so the chain lies on the $y$-axis.

Thus every point of the chain lies on the curve defined by $x^2 = 2cy + c^2$, hence on a single parabola.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate point is the identification of opposite vertices with a consistent increment rule on integer indices. This relies on the fact that each cell is uniquely determined by the annulus index $n$ and strip index $m$, and that opposite vertices necessarily change both indices because they lie on opposite boundaries in both radial and horizontal directions. Any alternative diagonal choice would violate the checkerboard constraint when extended to a chain, since it would force a switch in orientation at the next cell.

The second delicate point is the passage from $n_k - m_k = c$ to the identity $n_k = y_k + c$. The inequality $m_k < y_k < m_k + 1$ ensures that replacing $m_k$ by $y_k$ changes the expression by less than $1$, while both $n_k$ and $m_k + c$ are integers shifted consistently along the chain, which fixes the branch of the square root uniquely along any connected chain.

The third delicate point is elimination of the square root. Since $n_k = \sqrt{x_k^2 + y_k^2}$ is nonnegative by definition of radius, squaring does not introduce extraneous solutions, and the sign of $y_k + c$ remains consistent along the chain because both $y_k$ and $n_k$ vary monotonically in the same direction along successive vertices.

## Alternative Approaches

A different approach interprets the grid as an orthogonal coordinate net generated by level curves of $r$ and $y$, and shows directly that opposite vertex adjacency corresponds to translation in a lattice in the $(r,y)$-plane. In that setting, the chain is the image of a straight line under the map $(r,y) \mapsto (x,y)$ with $r^2 = x^2 + y^2$, and straight lines in $(r,y)$ map to parabolas in Cartesian coordinates. This approach is conceptually shorter but hides the necessity of carefully controlling the branch of the square root when converting back to Euclidean coordinates.
