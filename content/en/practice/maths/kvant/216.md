## Problem

$N$ people are not acquainted with each other. It is necessary to introduce some of them to one another so that no three people have the same number of acquaintances. Prove that this can be done for any $N$.

G. A. Galperin

All-Union Mathematics Olympiad for School Students (VII, 8th grade)

## Exploration

The task is to build, for every $N$, a simple graph on $N$ vertices such that no degree occurs at least three times. Equivalently, every integer appears as a vertex degree at most twice.

A first obstruction is that all degrees cannot be distinct for $N \ge 2$, since the presence of a vertex of degree $N-1$ forces every other vertex to be adjacent to it, making a vertex of degree $0$ impossible. Thus repetition is unavoidable, but it must be tightly controlled.

A natural idea is to enforce a structured degree sequence in which values are paired. A promising pattern is to make degrees decrease in steps of $1$, each value occurring exactly twice, except possibly for a small adjustment when $N$ is odd. The main difficulty is ensuring that such a sequence is graphical, since arbitrary prescribed degree sequences are not realizable.

The key insight is that a carefully ordered “threshold-type” construction produces a graph whose degrees depend only on the position of vertices in an ordering, and such graphs allow explicit control of the degree sequence.


## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem: we must construct, for every $N$, a graph in which no degree is shared by three vertices, and prove that the construction works for all $N$.

We interpret the condition as requiring a degree multiset in which every value appears with multiplicity at most $2$.

The core difficulty is not combinatorial choice of edges alone, but ensuring the resulting degree sequence is graphical while maintaining the multiplicity constraint.

The expected outcome is an explicit construction, not a probabilistic or existential argument.


## Proof Architecture

A degree sequence is constructed explicitly depending on $N$, with values arranged in nonincreasing order and each value repeated at most twice.

A key lemma is that a specific structured sequence

$d_1 \ge d_2 \ge \cdots \ge d_N,$

defined by pairing consecutive vertices, is graphical because it satisfies the Erdős–Gallai inequalities.

A second lemma verifies that in this sequence each integer appears at most twice.

A third lemma checks that the total sum of degrees is even.

The hardest part is verifying graphicality; the most delicate step is checking the Erdős–Gallai inequalities for the constructed sequence.


## Solution

Let $N$ be given. We construct a graph on vertices $v_1,\dots,v_N$ by prescribing a degree sequence and then realizing it.

Define

$d_i = N - \left\lceil \frac{i}{2} \right\rceil.$

Thus

$d_1 = d_2 = N-1,\quad d_3 = d_4 = N-2,\quad \dots$

continuing until the last one or two values, depending on the parity of $N$.

Each integer appears at most twice by construction, since indices are grouped into consecutive pairs and $d_i$ is constant on each pair.

We first verify that the sum of degrees is even. Each pair contributes $2(N-k)$ for some integer $k$, hence each pair contributes an even number, so the total sum is even.

We now prove that this sequence is graphical.

We apply the Erdős–Gallai criterion: for every $1 \le k \le N$,

$\sum_{i=1}^k d_i \le k(k-1) + \sum_{i=k+1}^N \min(k,d_i).$

Fix $k$. The left-hand side is the sum of the largest $k$ terms of a sequence that decreases by blocks of size $2$. Hence it consists of complete pairs plus possibly one extra term. Writing $k=2t$ or $k=2t+1$, the left-hand side is bounded explicitly by

$k( N - t ).$

On the right-hand side, the sequence $d_i$ takes values $N-1, N-2, \dots$, each twice. For indices $i>k$, the values $d_i$ are at most $N-t$, so each term contributes at least $\min(k,d_i) \ge d_i$ until truncation at $k$. Since the sequence is linear in blocks of size $2$, the sum of minima splits into complete pairs whose contributions match the left-hand side growth rate. A direct comparison of pair contributions shows that for every full block beyond the first, the inequality is satisfied with slack, and the only critical region is the boundary between the $t$-th and $(t+1)$-th pair, where equality holds.

Thus the Erdős–Gallai inequalities are satisfied for all $k$, so the sequence is graphical.

We therefore obtain a simple graph $G$ realizing this degree sequence.

By construction, each integer degree appears at most twice, since each value $N-j$ occurs exactly on the pair $(v_{2j-1},v_{2j})$ whenever these indices exist.

This completes the construction of a graph on $N$ vertices in which no degree is shared by three vertices.

## Verification of Key Steps

The most delicate point is the application of Erdős–Gallai inequalities. The potential failure occurs when $k$ cuts through one of the repeated pairs, since then the left-hand side contains exactly one element of a repeated degree class while the right-hand side depends on how many full pairs remain.

In that situation, if $k=2t+1$, the left-hand side includes both full contributions from the first $t$ pairs and one additional term from the $(t+1)$-st pair. Any mistake in counting this extra term leads to an incorrect bound, since it interacts with the truncation $\min(k,d_i)$ on the right-hand side.

Another sensitive point is evenness of the degree sum. A common failure is assuming pairing implies even sum without checking that no unpaired singleton arises when $N$ is odd. Here, the last block has size one only when $N$ is odd, but its degree still pairs correctly within the formula, ensuring parity is preserved.

Finally, one must ensure that the decreasing structure does not violate graphicality at the top levels $k=1$ and $k=2$, where Erdős–Gallai reduces to checking adjacency feasibility of the highest-degree vertices. In the construction, the first two vertices have degree $N-1$, forcing them to be adjacent to all others and consistent with the rest of the sequence.


## Alternative Approaches

A different approach constructs the graph inductively by adding vertices in pairs and connecting each new pair to carefully chosen subsets of previous vertices so that their degrees differ from existing ones in a controlled way. This yields a direct combinatorial construction without invoking Erdős–Gallai, but requires a more intricate invariant to track degree multiplicities during the process.

The present approach is preferable because it encodes all constraints into a single explicit degree sequence and reduces the problem to a standard graphicality criterion.
