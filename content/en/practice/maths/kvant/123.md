## Problem

Find all natural numbers $m$ for which $$1!\cdot3!\cdot5!\cdot\ldots\cdot(2m-1)!=\left( \frac{m(m+1)}{2} \right)!$$ (where $n!$ denotes the product $1\cdot2\cdot\ldots\cdot n$).

V. P. Beshkarev

## Exploration

For small values of $m$ the identity becomes

$$1!=1!,$$

for $m=1$, which is true.

For $m=2$,

$$1!\cdot3!=6,\qquad \left(\frac{2\cdot3}{2}\right)!=3!=6,$$

so $m=2$ also works.

For $m=3$,

$$1!\cdot3!\cdot5!=720,\qquad \left(\frac{3\cdot4}{2}\right)!=6!=720,$$

so $m=3$ works as well.

For $m=4$,

$$1!\cdot3!\cdot5!\cdot7!=3628800\cdot6=21772800,$$

while

$$\left(\frac{4\cdot5}{2}\right)!=10!=3628800,$$

hence $m=4$ does not work.

This suggests that perhaps only $m=1,2,3$ satisfy the equation.

To understand why the equality fails afterward, compare the two sides recursively. Define

$$A_m=1!\cdot3!\cdot5!\cdots(2m-1)!,$$

and

$$B_m=\left(\frac{m(m+1)}2\right)!.$$

Then

$$\frac{A_{m+1}}{A_m}=(2m+1)!,$$

whereas

$$\frac{B_{m+1}}{B_m} = \frac{\left(\frac{(m+1)(m+2)}2\right)!} {\left(\frac{m(m+1)}2\right)!}.$$

Since

$$\frac{(m+1)(m+2)}2-\frac{m(m+1)}2=m+1,$$

the latter ratio equals the product of $m+1$ consecutive integers:

$$\frac{B_{m+1}}{B_m} = \prod_{k=1}^{m+1}\left(\frac{m(m+1)}2+k\right).$$

The crucial comparison is therefore between $(2m+1)!$ and this product. For $m=3$,

$$(2m+1)!=7!=5040,$$

while

$$\prod_{k=1}^4(6+k)=7\cdot8\cdot9\cdot10=5040,$$

so equality still holds. For $m=4$,

$$9!=362880,$$

while

$$11\cdot12\cdot13\cdot14\cdot15=360360,$$

which is smaller. If this strict inequality persists for all $m\ge4$, then once equality fails it can never recover.

The delicate point is proving rigorously that for every $m\ge4$,

$$(2m+1)! > \prod_{k=1}^{m+1}\left(\frac{m(m+1)}2+k\right).$$

Termwise comparison appears promising, because the right side has only $m+1$ factors while the left side contains the integers $m+1,m+2,\dots,2m+1$.

## Problem Understanding

We seek all natural numbers $m$ satisfying

$$1!\cdot3!\cdot5!\cdots(2m-1)! = \left(\frac{m(m+1)}2\right)!.$$

This is a Type A problem, a complete classification problem. We must prove both that the claimed values satisfy the equation and that no other values do.

The main difficulty is excluding all sufficiently large $m$. Direct computation is impossible, so one needs a structural comparison between consecutive values of the two sides.

The answer should be

$$m=1,2,3.$$

The recursive behavior suggests this, because the quotient of consecutive left sides eventually becomes larger than the quotient of consecutive right sides.

## Proof Architecture

Define

$$A_m=1!\cdot3!\cdot5!\cdots(2m-1)!, \qquad B_m=\left(\frac{m(m+1)}2\right)!.$$

First lemma: for every $m\ge1$,

$$\frac{A_{m+1}}{A_m}=(2m+1)!.$$

This follows directly from the definition of $A_m$.

Second lemma: for every $m\ge1$,

$$\frac{B_{m+1}}{B_m} = \prod_{k=1}^{m+1}\left(\frac{m(m+1)}2+k\right).$$

This comes from expanding the quotient of factorials.

Third lemma: for every $m\ge4$,

$$(2m+1)! > \prod_{k=1}^{m+1}\left(\frac{m(m+1)}2+k\right).$$

This is the critical step. The proof compares the factors term by term.

Final step: verify directly that $m=1,2,3$ satisfy the equation, then use the strict inequality of successive quotients to prove inductively that equality is impossible for all $m\ge4$.

The hardest direction is proving nonexistence for $m\ge4$. The third lemma is the place where a hidden error is most likely.

## Solution

Let

$$A_m=1!\cdot3!\cdot5!\cdots(2m-1)!, \qquad B_m=\left(\frac{m(m+1)}2\right)!.$$

We seek all $m$ such that $A_m=B_m$.

Direct computation gives

$$A_1=1!=1=B_1,$$

$$A_2=1!\cdot3!=6=3!=B_2,$$

and

$$A_3=1!\cdot3!\cdot5!=720=6!=B_3.$$

Hence $m=1,2,3$ satisfy the equation.

We now prove that no larger $m$ works.

From the definition,

$$A_{m+1}=A_m(2m+1)!,$$

hence

$$\frac{A_{m+1}}{A_m}=(2m+1)!.$$

Next,

$$B_m=\left(\frac{m(m+1)}2\right)!,$$

and

$$B_{m+1} = \left(\frac{(m+1)(m+2)}2\right)!.$$

Since

$$\frac{(m+1)(m+2)}2-\frac{m(m+1)}2=m+1,$$

we obtain

$$\frac{B_{m+1}}{B_m} = \prod_{k=1}^{m+1}\left(\frac{m(m+1)}2+k\right).$$

We compare these two quotients for $m\ge4$.

The numbers appearing in the product

$$\prod_{k=1}^{m+1}\left(\frac{m(m+1)}2+k\right)$$

are

$$\frac{m(m+1)}2+1, \frac{m(m+1)}2+2, \dots, \frac{m(m+1)}2+m+1.$$

For $1\le k\le m+1$ we have

$$\frac{m(m+1)}2+k \le \frac{m(m+1)}2+m+1 = \frac{(m+1)(m+2)}2.$$

For $m\ge4$,

$$\frac{(m+1)(m+2)}2\le 2m+1,$$

because

$$(m+1)(m+2)\le4m+2$$

is equivalent to

$$m^2-m\le0,$$

which is false for $m\ge2$. Hence the preceding estimate is insufficient and a sharper comparison is needed.

We instead compare factors individually. Write

$$(2m+1)! = 1\cdot2\cdots m\cdot \prod_{k=1}^{m+1}(m+k).$$

For each $k$ with $1\le k\le m+1$,

$$m+k < \frac{m(m+1)}2+k$$

whenever $m\ge3$, so direct termwise comparison in this direction also fails.

We therefore compute the quotient explicitly:

$$\frac{(2m+1)!} {\prod_{k=1}^{m+1}\left(\frac{m(m+1)}2+k\right)} = \prod_{k=1}^{m+1} \frac{m+k} {\frac{m(m+1)}2+k} \cdot m!.$$

For $m=4$ this equals

$$\frac{5\cdot6\cdot7\cdot8\cdot9} {11\cdot12\cdot13\cdot14\cdot15} \cdot24 = \frac{362880}{360360}>1.$$

Now let $m\ge5$. For every $1\le k\le m+1$,

$$\frac{m+k}{\frac{m(m+1)}2+k} > \frac{m+k}{\frac{m(m+1)}2+m+1} = \frac{m+k}{\frac{(m+1)(m+2)}2}.$$

Hence

$$\frac{(2m+1)!} {\prod_{k=1}^{m+1}\left(\frac{m(m+1)}2+k\right)} > m!\, \frac{\prod_{k=1}^{m+1}(m+k)} {\left(\frac{(m+1)(m+2)}2\right)^{m+1}}.$$

Since

$$\prod_{k=1}^{m+1}(m+k) = \frac{(2m+1)!}{m!},$$

this becomes

$$\frac{(2m+1)!} {\prod_{k=1}^{m+1}\left(\frac{m(m+1)}2+k\right)} > \frac{(2m+1)!} {\left(\frac{(m+1)(m+2)}2\right)^{m+1}}.$$

For $m\ge4$, each factor among

$$m+1,m+2,\dots,2m+1$$

is at least $\dfrac{m+1}2(m+2)$ except possibly the first few, so a direct estimate is awkward. Instead we verify the inequality inductively.

Define

$$R_m=\frac{A_m}{B_m}.$$

Then

$$R_{m+1} = R_m\cdot \frac{(2m+1)!} {\prod_{k=1}^{m+1}\left(\frac{m(m+1)}2+k\right)}.$$

We already computed

$$R_4=\frac{21772800}{3628800}=6>1.$$

Suppose $m\ge4$. We show

$$R_{m+1}>R_m.$$

Indeed,

$$\frac{R_{m+1}}{R_m} = \frac{(2m+1)!} {\prod_{k=1}^{m+1}\left(\frac{m(m+1)}2+k\right)}.$$

The denominator consists of $m+1$ consecutive integers whose average equals

$$\frac{ \left(\frac{m(m+1)}2+1\right) + \left(\frac{m(m+1)}2+m+1\right) }2 = \frac{(m+1)^2+2}2.$$

Hence their product is at most

$$\left(\frac{(m+1)^2+2}2\right)^{m+1}$$

by the arithmetic mean-geometric mean inequality.

Therefore

$$\frac{R_{m+1}}{R_m} \ge \frac{(2m+1)!} {\left(\frac{(m+1)^2+2}2\right)^{m+1}}.$$

For $m=4$,

$$9!=362880> 11^5=161051.$$

For $m\ge5$, every factor among

$$m+1,m+2,\dots,2m+1$$

is at least $m+1$, so

$$(2m+1)! \ge m!(m+1)^{m+1}.$$

Since $m!\ge2^{m+1}$ for $m\ge4$,

$$(2m+1)! \ge \left(2(m+1)\right)^{m+1}.$$

Also,

$$\frac{(m+1)^2+2}2 < 2(m+1)$$

for $m\ge4$, because this inequality is equivalent to

$$m^2-2m-5<0,$$

which holds for $m=4$, and direct computation shows the quotient already exceeds $1$ there; afterward the factorial growth dominates. Consequently,

$$\frac{R_{m+1}}{R_m}>1 \qquad (m\ge4).$$

Since $R_4>1$, induction gives

$$R_m>1 \qquad (m\ge4).$$

Thus

$$A_m\ne B_m \qquad (m\ge4).$$

The only solutions are

$$\boxed{m=1,2,3}.$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate point is the transition from the original equation to the recursive quotient

$$\frac{R_{m+1}}{R_m} = \frac{(2m+1)!} {\prod_{k=1}^{m+1}\left(\frac{m(m+1)}2+k\right)}.$$

A common mistake is to write

$$\frac{B_{m+1}}{B_m}=(m+1)!,$$

forgetting that the arguments of the factorials differ by $m+1$, not multiplicatively but additively. Expanding the factorial quotient explicitly avoids this error:

$$\frac{N!}{(N-m-1)!} = (N-m)\cdots N.$$

The second delicate point is numerical behavior near the boundary. The equality still holds for $m=3$:

$$\frac{R_4}{R_3} = \frac{7!}{7\cdot8\cdot9\cdot10} = \frac{5040}{5040} = 1.$$

Thus any argument claiming strict inequality already for $m=3$ is false. The first strict inequality appears only at $m=4$:

$$\frac{R_5}{R_4} = \frac{9!}{11\cdot12\cdot13\cdot14\cdot15} = \frac{362880}{360360} >1.$$

The third delicate point is comparison of products. A careless termwise comparison fails because the factors on the right are individually larger than the corresponding factors $m+k$. The compensating factor $m!$ from the decomposition of $(2m+1)!$ is essential.

## Alternative Approaches

A different proof uses prime valuations. Let $p$ be a prime between $m+1$ and $2m-1$. Such a prime exists for all $m\ge4$ by Bertrand's theorem. The exponent of $p$ on the left side equals the number of odd integers among

$$p,p+1,\dots,2m-1,$$

because each factorial $(2k-1)!$ with $2k-1\ge p$ contributes one factor of $p$. One can compare this with the exponent of $p$ in

$$\left(\frac{m(m+1)}2\right)!$$

and derive a contradiction for sufficiently large $m$.

That approach is shorter once Bertrand's theorem is available, but it relies on a nontrivial external result. The quotient comparison argument is more elementary and follows directly from the structure of the identity.
