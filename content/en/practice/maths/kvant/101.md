## Problem

In a colony consisting of $n$ bacteria, a single virus enters. In the first minute it destroys one bacterium, then divides into two new viruses, and simultaneously each of the remaining bacteria also divides into two new ones. In the next minute the resulting two viruses destroy two bacteria, and then both viruses and all remaining bacteria again divide, and so on. Will this colony live indefinitely, or will it eventually die out?

R. M. Kovtun

## Exploration

Let $V_k$ and $B_k$ denote the number of viruses and bacteria after the $k$-th minute. Initially,

$V_0 = 1, \quad B_0 = n.$

During each minute, every virus attempts to destroy one bacterium, after which all viruses split into two and all surviving bacteria also split into two. Thus, the number of viruses doubles each step, while the number of bacteria is reduced by the number of viruses before doubling and then doubled.

A key tension appears between exponential growth of viruses, $V_k = 2^k$, and the evolution of bacteria, which is reduced by $V_k$ each step. The decisive question is whether bacteria can remain ahead of the exponentially growing viral population indefinitely.

A natural quantity to track is the ratio of bacteria to viruses, since both are affected by powers of $2$.

The most delicate point is whether bacteria can ever “recover” after repeated doublings or whether the linear subtraction by $2^k$ eventually forces extinction.


## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem (“determine whether the colony survives or dies”).

We must determine whether the bacteria population ever reaches zero under the given deterministic process.

The key difficulty is the interaction of exponential growth (doubling each minute) with a linear but growing depletion term (each virus removes one bacterium per minute).

We will show that bacteria are eliminated in finite time for every initial $n$, so the colony does not survive indefinitely.


## Proof Architecture

We define the system $(V_k, B_k)$ and derive explicit recurrences.

We prove that $V_k = 2^k$ for all $k$.

We introduce the normalized quantity $x_k = \frac{B_k}{2^k}$ and derive a linear recurrence for $x_k$ while bacteria remain nonzero.

We show that as long as $B_k > 0$, we have $x_k = n - k$.

We identify the first moment when $x_k \le 1$, which forces complete annihilation of bacteria in the next step.

The most delicate point is justifying that once $V_k \ge B_k$, all bacteria are destroyed in that step and extinction is immediate.


## Solution

At the beginning of the $k$-th minute, there are $V_k$ viruses and $B_k$ bacteria. Each virus destroys exactly one bacterium if available, so the number of bacteria destroyed is $\min(V_k, B_k)$. After this destruction, all remaining organisms split into two.

Thus the system evolves according to

$V_{k+1} = 2V_k, \quad B_{k+1} = 2(B_k - \min(V_k, B_k)).$

Since $V_0 = 1$, we immediately obtain by induction that

$V_k = 2^k.$

We now analyze bacteria. As long as $B_k \ge V_k$, we have $\min(V_k, B_k) = V_k$, so

$B_{k+1} = 2(B_k - V_k) = 2B_k - 2^{k+1}.$

Define the normalized quantity

$x_k = \frac{B_k}{2^k}.$

Dividing the recurrence by $2^{k+1}$ yields

$x_{k+1} = \frac{2B_k - 2^{k+1}}{2^{k+1}} = \frac{B_k}{2^k} - 1 = x_k - 1.$

Initially,

$x_0 = \frac{B_0}{1} = n.$

As long as $B_k \ge V_k$, equivalently $x_k \ge 1$, the recurrence holds, and therefore

$x_k = n - k.$

Hence this regime persists exactly until the first index $k$ such that $n - k < 1$, that is, until $k = n-1$.

At $k = n-1$, we have

$x_{n-1} = 1,$

so

$B_{n-1} = 2^{n-1} = V_{n-1}.$

During the next minute, every virus destroys one bacterium, and since their numbers are equal, all bacteria are destroyed:

$B_{n-1} - \min(V_{n-1}, B_{n-1}) = 0.$

After the splitting step,

$B_n = 2 \cdot 0 = 0.$

Once $B_k = 0$, it remains zero for all subsequent steps. Thus extinction occurs at time $n$.

Therefore, for every initial $n$, the bacterial colony dies out in finite time.

$\boxed{\text{The colony does not live indefinitely; it becomes extinct after exactly } n \text{ minutes.}}$

∎


## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate point is the recurrence for $B_{k+1}$. The process explicitly states that each virus destroys one bacterium if available, so the total number removed is $\min(V_k, B_k)$. No other interaction affects bacteria before splitting, so the formula $B_{k+1} = 2(B_k - \min(V_k, B_k))$ accounts for all changes.

The second point is the evolution of the ratio $x_k$. Substituting $V_k = 2^k$ into the recurrence gives a strict linear decrement $x_{k+1} = x_k - 1$ in the regime $x_k \ge 1$, with no hidden dependence on $B_k$ beyond the ratio itself.

The third point is the transition at $x_{n-1} = 1$. At that moment $V_{n-1} = B_{n-1}$, so every bacterium is matched with a virus, leaving no survivors before division. Hence the subsequent population is exactly zero, not merely reduced.


## Alternative Approaches

One can interpret the system as a discrete-time balance equation comparing exponential growth of viruses with linear depletion of bacteria in logarithmic scale. In that view, the key observation is that bacteria normalized by $2^k$ decrease by exactly one unit per step, forcing extinction when this normalized quantity reaches zero.

Another approach is to unroll the recurrence explicitly for $B_k$ while tracking cumulative viral pressure $\sum 2^i$, which also leads to the same finite extinction time.
