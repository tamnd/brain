## Problem

From the numbers 1, 2, $\ldots$, $k$, all possible $(a_1,a_2,\ldots,a_n)$-tuples of length $n$ are formed (it is easy to see that their number is $k^n$). Two subsets $P$ and $Q$ of such collections are chosen (the same collection may belong to both $P$ and $Q$). It is known that if we take an arbitrary collection $(p_1,p_2,\ldots,p_n)$ from $P$ and an arbitrary collection $(q_1,q_2,\ldots,q_n)$ from $Q$, then they coincide in at least one position (i.e. $p_i=q_i$ for some $i$). Then either in $P$ or in $Q$ there are at most $k^{n-1}$ collections.

Prove this statement

1. for $k=2$ and any $n$;
2. for $n=1$, 2, З and any $k\ge2$;
3. for arbitrary $k\ge2$ and $n\ge1$.

Try to find other restrictions on the number of elements in the subsets $P$ and $Q$ related to this condition.

V. B. Alekseev

## Exploration

We interpret the condition as follows: for every $p \in P$ and $q \in Q$, there exists an index $i$ such that $p_i = q_i$. Equivalently, no pair $(p,q)$ is completely different in all coordinates.

It is natural to view tuples as points in the grid ${1,2,\ldots,k}^n$. The forbidden configuration is that all coordinates differ, i.e. $p_i \neq q_i$ for every $i$. The number of such completely disjoint pairs is $\bigl(k(k-1)\bigr)^n$, since each coordinate admits $k$ choices for $p_i$ and $k-1$ choices for $q_i$ avoiding equality.

The statement claims a strong structural consequence: two families avoiding this “full mismatch” interaction cannot both be large; one must have size at most $k^{n-1}$, the size of a full coordinate fiber.

Small cases suggest a rigidity phenomenon. For $n=1$, the condition forces every pair to agree, so at least one set has size $1 = k^{0}$. For general $n$, fixing a coordinate produces natural extremal examples of size $k^{n-1}$, so these are plausible extremizers.

The main difficulty is that the condition is global across coordinates, while the bound is local to a single coordinate fiber. The key idea is to slice the space by the first coordinate and analyze cross-interactions between slices of $P$ and $Q$.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem.

We are given two subsets $P,Q \subseteq {1,\dots,k}^n$ such that every $p \in P$ and $q \in Q$ coincide in at least one coordinate. We must prove that one of the sets has size at most $k^{n-1}$.

The extremal configuration is when one family consists of all tuples with a fixed coordinate equal to a fixed value, giving size $k^{n-1}$. The theorem asserts that no pair of families satisfying the condition can both exceed this size.

## Proof Architecture

We proceed by induction on $n$.

For the base case $n=1$, the condition forces complete equality between every element of $P$ and every element of $Q$, which implies that one of the sets has size at most $1$.

For the inductive step, we partition both $P$ and $Q$ according to the first coordinate, writing $P_a$ and $Q_a$ for elements whose first coordinate equals $a$. We analyze pairs of slices $P_a$ and $Q_b$ with $a \neq b$. Any such pair inherits the same condition in dimension $n-1$, so the inductive hypothesis applies.

The key lemma is that if many slices of $P$ and $Q$ are simultaneously large, then there must exist some pair of slices violating the inductive bound, forcing a contradiction.

The hardest part is ensuring that the interaction between different coordinate slices is controlled tightly enough to guarantee a global bound of $k^{n-1}$.

## Solution

We prove the statement by induction on $n$.

For $n=1$, every element of $P$ and every element of $Q$ is a single number from ${1,\dots,k}$. The condition says that for all $p \in P$ and $q \in Q$ we have $p=q$. If $P$ contains two distinct elements $x \neq y$, then choosing $q=x$ from $Q$ forces $y=x$, which is impossible. Hence $P$ contains at most one element or $Q$ contains at most one element. Since $k^{1-1}=1$, the claim holds.

Assume now that the statement holds for dimension $n-1$, and consider dimension $n$.

For each $a \in {1,\dots,k}$, define $P_a = {p \in P : p_1 = a}$ and $Q_a = {q \in Q : q_1 = a}$. For distinct $a \neq b$, consider $p \in P_a$ and $q \in Q_b$. If $p$ and $q$ agree in coordinate $1$, this is impossible since $p_1=a \neq b=q_1$. Hence any agreement must occur in one of the remaining coordinates $2,\dots,n$. Therefore the truncated $(n-1)$-tuples obtained by deleting the first coordinate still satisfy the original condition between $P_a$ and $Q_b$.

By the inductive hypothesis applied in dimension $n-1$, for every pair $a \neq b$ we have either $|P_a| \le k^{n-2}$ or $|Q_b| \le k^{n-2}$.

We now assume that both $|P| > k^{n-1}$ and $|Q| > k^{n-1}$ and derive a contradiction. Since $|P| = \sum_a |P_a|$, there exists at least one index $a_0$ such that $|P_{a_0}| > k^{n-2}$. Similarly, there exists $b_0$ such that $|Q_{b_0}| > k^{n-2}$.

If $a_0 \neq b_0$, then applying the inductive conclusion to the pair $(a_0,b_0)$ forces either $|P_{a_0}| \le k^{n-2}$ or $|Q_{b_0}| \le k^{n-2}$, contradicting the choice of $a_0$ and $b_0$. Hence we must have $a_0 = b_0$.

Fix this index $a_0$. Then for every $b \neq a_0$, the inductive statement applied to $(a_0,b)$ implies $|Q_b| \le k^{n-2}$. Summing over all $b \neq a_0$ gives

$$\sum_{b \neq a_0} |Q_b| \le (k-1)k^{n-2}.$$

Therefore

$$|Q| = |Q_{a_0}| + \sum_{b \neq a_0} |Q_b| \le |Q_{a_0}| + (k-1)k^{n-2}.$$

Since $|Q| > k^{n-1} = k \cdot k^{n-2}$, we obtain

$$|Q_{a_0}| > k^{n-1} - (k-1)k^{n-2} = k^{n-2}.$$

Thus both $|P_{a_0}| > k^{n-2}$ and $|Q_{a_0}| > k^{n-2}$.

Now restrict attention to the slice $a_0$. Within this slice, consider the $(n-1)$-dimensional families obtained by deleting the first coordinate. The original condition remains valid between $P_{a_0}$ and $Q_{a_0}$. By the inductive hypothesis applied in dimension $n-1$, we obtain that either $|P_{a_0}| \le k^{n-2}$ or $|Q_{a_0}| \le k^{n-2}$, contradicting the inequalities above.

This contradiction shows that it is impossible for both $|P|$ and $|Q|$ to exceed $k^{n-1}$. Hence either $|P| \le k^{n-1}$ or $|Q| \le k^{n-1}$.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The crucial reduction is the inheritance of the condition after fixing the first coordinate. If $p \in P_a$ and $q \in Q_b$ with $a \neq b$, equality cannot occur in coordinate $1$, so any valid agreement must lie in the remaining coordinates. This justifies applying the inductive hypothesis in dimension $n-1$ without loss.

The second delicate point is the summation bound on the slices of $Q$. The estimate $(k-1)k^{n-2}$ comes from applying the inductive bound to each of the $k-1$ slices separately; no overlap issues arise because we are summing disjoint sets $Q_b$.

The final contradiction depends on restricting both families to a single coordinate slice where both exceed $k^{n-2}$, allowing a direct application of the induction hypothesis in reduced dimension.

## Alternative Approaches

A different approach interprets the condition as forbidding pairs at Hamming distance $n$, turning the problem into an extremal question in the Hamming association scheme. One can then apply eigenvalue bounds for the corresponding graph, leading to the same threshold $k^{n-1}$ via spectral extremal graph theory.

Another approach uses compression techniques: repeatedly “shifting” elements of $P$ and $Q$ toward a fixed coordinate value does not destroy the condition and eventually forces one family into a single coordinate fiber, immediately yielding the bound $k^{n-1}$.
