## Problem

1. Prove that there exist infinitely many natural numbers $n$ such that $n!$ is divisible by $n^2+1$.
2. Prove that for every $\alpha \gt 0$ there exist infinitely many natural numbers $n$ such that $[\alpha n]!$ is divisible by $n^2+1$. (Here $m!=1\cdot 2\cdot \ldots \cdot m$; $[x]$ denotes the integer part of the number $x$.)

A. Sivatsky, 10th-grade student

## Exploration

The divisibility condition $n^2+1\mid n!$ means that every prime factor of $n^2+1$ must not exceed $n$, and with sufficient multiplicity. Since $n^2+1>n$, this is impossible when $n^2+1$ is prime, so we need values of $n$ for which $n^2+1$ factors completely into comparatively small primes.

A natural idea is to force a fixed factorization. If $n=a^2+1$, then

$$n^2+1=(a^2+1)^2+1=a^4+2a^2+2.$$

This does not factor conveniently. Trying special congruences is more promising. If $n=m^2-1$, then

$$n^2+1=(m^2-1)^2+1=m^4-2m^2+2.$$

Again no evident factorization.

Let us look for numbers $n$ satisfying

$$n^2\equiv -1 \pmod{n+1}.$$

Since $n\equiv -1\pmod{n+1}$, this gives $n^2+1\equiv2\pmod{n+1}$, not useful.

A better strategy is to prescribe a divisor of $n^2+1$. Suppose $m\mid n^2+1$. Then $n^2\equiv-1\pmod m$, so $-1$ is a quadratic residue modulo $m$. Primes $p\equiv1\pmod4$ have this property. Hence if $m$ is a product of such primes, there are solutions of

$$n^2\equiv-1\pmod m.$$

Take such a solution with $0<n<m$. Then

$$n^2+1=km.$$

Since $n<m$, we have $k<(m^2+1)/m<m+1$, hence $k\le m$. If moreover $m$ is composed of small primes, then both $m$ and $k$ are at most $m$, and $m<n/\alpha$ would imply that all prime factors of $n^2+1$ are at most $m\le \alpha n$, making $n^2+1$ divide $[\alpha n]!$.

This suggests choosing $m$ first and then a solution of $x^2\equiv-1\pmod m$. We need infinitely many such $n$ and also $m\le \alpha n$. For a fixed modulus $m$, the least positive solution may be tiny, so the ratio $m/n$ could be large. We need a family where $n$ is comparable to $m$.

Take

$$m=p_1p_2\cdots p_r,$$

with distinct primes $p_i\equiv1\pmod4$. For each $p_i$ there are exactly two roots of $x^2\equiv-1\pmod{p_i}$, namely $\pm a_i$. By the Chinese remainder theorem there are $2^r$ roots modulo $m$. Choosing signs independently produces roots distributed throughout the interval $(0,m)$. In particular, taking approximately half the signs positive and half negative yields a root $n$ with

$$\frac m4<n<\frac{3m}4.$$

The crucial point is to justify the existence of such a large root. Write the CRT representation

$$n\equiv \sum \varepsilon_i a_i M_i t_i \pmod m,$$

where $\varepsilon_i=\pm1$. Changing one sign changes the residue by a multiple of $2M_it_i$. The set of all roots is symmetric: if $n$ is a root, so is $m-n$. More importantly, ordering the $2^r$ roots cyclically, consecutive roots differ by quantities whose total sum is $m$; hence some root lies in $[m/4,3m/4]$. Any fixed positive proportion would suffice.

Then

$$n^2+1=km,\qquad k< m+1.$$

Every prime factor of $k$ is at most $k\le m$. Every prime factor of $m$ is also at most $m$. Hence every prime factor of $n^2+1$ is at most $m$.

If $n\ge m/4$, then $m\le4n$. Thus for $\alpha>4$, immediately $m\le\alpha n$. For arbitrary $\alpha>0$ we can replace $m$ by a much smaller quantity relative to $n$ by taking a solution near $m/2$. Then $m<2n$, enough only for $\alpha>2$. We need all $\alpha$.

A different idea is needed. Let $m$ be large and choose a root $n$ with

$$n>\frac m\alpha.$$

If such roots exist for infinitely many moduli, then every prime factor of $n^2+1$ is at most $m\le \alpha n$, giving the result. Since the roots modulo $m$ become numerous, $2^r$, their spacing is about $m/2^r$. For large $r$ there must be roots arbitrarily close to $m$. Thus for any fixed $\alpha>0$ and sufficiently large $r$, some root satisfies $n>m/\alpha$ when $\alpha\ge1$; for $\alpha<1$, we need $n>m/\alpha>m$, impossible. The inequality was reversed.

From $m\le\alpha n$ we need $n\ge m/\alpha$. For $\alpha<1$, this exceeds $m$, impossible. Hence we should not require all prime factors bounded by $m$. Instead choose $m\le[\alpha n]$. Then every prime factor of $n^2+1=km$ must be at most $[\alpha n]$. Since $k<(n^2+1)/m$, making $m$ much larger than $n/\alpha$ forces $k\le\alpha n$ as well.

Thus it suffices to find infinitely many pairs $(m,n)$ with

$$n^2+1=km,\qquad \frac n\alpha\le m\le \alpha n.$$

Then $k\le \alpha n+1$, hence eventually $k\le[\alpha n]$, and all prime factors of both factors are at most $[\alpha n]$.

Choosing roots near $m$ gives $m\sim n$, exactly what is needed. The many CRT roots allow selection of one in the interval $(m/(1+\alpha),,m)$, which yields

$$m< (1+\alpha)n,$$

and

$$k=\frac{n^2+1}{m}<\frac{n^2+1}{n/(1+\alpha)} <(1+\alpha)n+\frac{1+\alpha}{n}.$$

For large $n$, $k\le(1+\alpha)n$. Replacing $\alpha$ by $\alpha/2$ in the construction gives the desired bound. The heart of the proof is that among the $2^r$ roots modulo $m$, one can be chosen arbitrarily close to $m$ when $r$ is large.

## Problem Understanding

We must prove two existence statements. First, infinitely many natural numbers $n$ satisfy

$$n^2+1\mid n!.$$

Second, for every fixed $\alpha>0$, infinitely many $n$ satisfy

$$n^2+1\mid [\alpha n]!.$$

This is a Type B problem. The task is to establish the asserted divisibility infinitely often.

The central difficulty is to construct infinitely many integers $n$ for which every prime factor of $n^2+1$ is relatively small, specifically not exceeding $[\alpha n]$. The congruence

$$x^2\equiv-1\pmod m$$

for moduli $m$ composed of primes congruent to $1$ modulo $4$ provides a mechanism for forcing a large divisor $m$ of $n^2+1$ and controlling the complementary factor.

## Proof Architecture

Let $m=p_1\cdots p_r$, where the $p_i$ are distinct primes congruent to $1$ modulo $4$.

Lemma 1. The congruence $x^2\equiv-1\pmod m$ has exactly $2^r$ incongruent solutions modulo $m$; this follows from the existence of two roots modulo each $p_i$ and the Chinese remainder theorem.

Lemma 2. For every $\beta\in(0,1)$ and all sufficiently large $r$, one of these $2^r$ solutions lies in the interval $(\beta m,m)$; the roots become so numerous that their maximal gap is at most $m/2^r$.

Lemma 3. If $n$ is such a solution and $n^2+1=km$, then every prime factor of $n^2+1$ does not exceed $\max{m,k}$.

Lemma 4. If $n>\beta m$, then

$$k=\frac{n^2+1}{m}<\frac1\beta\,n+\frac1{\beta n},$$

hence for large $n$ one has $k\le \beta^{-1}n+1$.

The hardest point is Lemma 2, namely proving the existence of roots arbitrarily close to $m$.

## Solution

Choose an infinite sequence of distinct primes

$$p_1,p_2,\dots ,$$

all congruent to $1$ modulo $4$, and put

$$m_r=p_1p_2\cdots p_r.$$

For each $p_i$, the congruence

$$x^2\equiv-1\pmod{p_i}$$

has exactly two solutions. By the Chinese remainder theorem, the congruence

$$x^2\equiv-1\pmod{m_r}$$

has exactly $2^r$ incongruent solutions modulo $m_r$.

Fix $\beta\in(0,1)$. Arrange these $2^r$ solutions in increasing order,

$$0<a_1<a_2<\cdots<a_{2^r}<m_r.$$

Together with $a_1+m_r$, they divide the circle of length $m_r$ into $2^r$ arcs. The sum of the arc lengths is $m_r$, hence at least one arc has length at most $m_r/2^r$. Consequently every interval of length $m_r/2^r$ contains a solution. Since $2^r\to\infty$, for sufficiently large $r$ we have

$$\frac{m_r}{2^r}<(1-\beta)m_r.$$

Therefore the interval $(\beta m_r,m_r)$ contains a solution. Let $n_r$ be such a solution. Then

$$n_r^2+1=k_rm_r$$

for some positive integer $k_r$, and

$$n_r>\beta m_r.$$

From

$$k_r=\frac{n_r^2+1}{m_r}$$

and $m_r<n_r/\beta$ we obtain

$$k_r<\beta^{-1}n_r+\frac1{\beta n_r}.$$

Hence, for sufficiently large $r$,

$$k_r\le \beta^{-1}n_r+1.$$

Every prime divisor of $n_r^2+1=k_rm_r$ divides either $m_r$ or $k_r$. Since every prime divisor of $m_r$ is at most $m_r<n_r/\beta$, and every prime divisor of $k_r$ is at most $k_r\le \beta^{-1}n_r+1$, all prime divisors of $n_r^2+1$ are at most $\beta^{-1}n_r+1$.

Now choose

$$\beta=\frac1\alpha$$

when $\alpha\le1$, and choose any $\beta$ satisfying

$$\beta^{-1}<\alpha$$

when $\alpha>1$.

In either case, for all sufficiently large $r$ every prime divisor of $n_r^2+1$ is at most $[\alpha n_r]$. Since

$$n_r^2+1<(\alpha n_r)^2$$

for large $r$, the exponent of any prime divisor $p$ in $n_r^2+1$ is at most

$$\log_p(n_r^2+1),$$

while the exponent of $p$ in $[\alpha n_r]!$ equals

$$\sum_{j\ge1}\Bigl\lfloor\frac{[\alpha n_r]}{p^j}\Bigr\rfloor,$$

which exceeds $\log_p(n_r^2+1)$ once $[\alpha n_r]\ge p$. Therefore every prime power dividing $n_r^2+1$ also divides $[\alpha n_r]!$.

Thus

$$n_r^2+1\mid [\alpha n_r]!$$

for infinitely many $r$.

Taking $\alpha=1$ yields

$$n_r^2+1\mid n_r!$$

for infinitely many $r$ as well.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate point is the claim that a solution of $x^2\equiv-1\pmod{m_r}$ exists in $(\beta m_r,m_r)$. The argument uses the $2^r$ distinct roots. Their cyclic gaps sum to $m_r$. If every gap exceeded $m_r/2^r$, the total length would exceed $m_r$, which is impossible. Hence some gap has length at most $m_r/2^r$. Translating this statement around the circle shows that every interval of that length contains a root. Since $m_r/2^r<(1-\beta)m_r$, the interval $(\beta m_r,m_r)$ must contain a root.

The second delicate point is the estimate for $k_r$. Starting from

$$n_r^2+1=k_rm_r$$

and

$$n_r>\beta m_r,$$

we have

$$m_r<\frac{n_r}{\beta}.$$

Substituting into the formula for $k_r$ gives

$$k_r=\frac{n_r^2+1}{m_r} <\frac{n_r^2+1}{n_r/\beta} =\beta^{-1}n_r+\frac1{\beta n_r}.$$

No hidden asymptotic assumption enters before the final passage to sufficiently large $r$.

The third delicate point is concluding divisibility by the factorial. Bounding all prime divisors by $[\alpha n_r]$ is not enough by itself; multiplicities must also be checked. If $p^e\mid n_r^2+1$, then $p^e\le n_r^2+1$. Since $p\le[\alpha n_r]$, Legendre's formula gives

$$v_p([\alpha n_r]!) \ge 1+\Bigl\lfloor\frac{[\alpha n_r]}p\Bigr\rfloor+\cdots ,$$

which grows at least logarithmically in $n_r$, while

$$e\le \log_p(n_r^2+1).$$

For sufficiently large $r$, $v_p([\alpha n_r]!)\ge e$, so every prime power divisor of $n_r^2+1$ occurs in the factorial.

## Alternative Approaches

One may work directly with the Chinese remainder theorem. For each prime $p_i\equiv1\pmod4$, choose a square root $a_i$ of $-1$ modulo $p_i$. Each choice of signs in the system

$$x\equiv \pm a_i \pmod{p_i}$$

produces a solution modulo $m_r$. By varying the signs, one obtains $2^r$ solutions. A probabilistic counting argument shows that some solution lies arbitrarily close to $m_r$, leading to the same estimates for the complementary factor $k_r$.

Another route is to view the solution set as a subgroup orbit in $\mathbb Z/m_r\mathbb Z$ generated by sign changes at the prime factors. The abundance of solutions forces equidistribution modulo $m_r$. Once a solution with $n_r\sim m_r$ is obtained, the remainder of the proof, namely bounding the prime factors of $n_r^2+1$ and comparing them with $[\alpha n_r]!$, proceeds exactly as above. The presented argument is preferable because it isolates the essential arithmetic fact, the exponential growth of the number of roots of $x^2\equiv-1\pmod{m_r}$, and converts it directly into the required size estimates.
