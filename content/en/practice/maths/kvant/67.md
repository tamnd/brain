## Problem

A jeweler has been commissioned to make a gold ring of width $h$, having the form of a solid bounded by the surface of a sphere with center $O$ and the surface of a cylinder of radius $r$, whose axis passes through point $O$ (Fig. 1). The craftsman made such a ring, but chose $r$ to be too small. How much gold will he need to add if $r$ must be increased by $k$ times, while keeping the width $h$ unchanged?

## Exploration

Let the axis of the cylinder be the $z$-axis and let the center of the sphere be the origin $O$. The region described is the set of points simultaneously inside the sphere $x^2+y^2+z^2 \le R^2$ and inside the cylinder $x^2+y^2 \le r^2$. The unknown geometric parameter of the sphere is determined by the fixed “width” $h$, which must correspond to the full height of the cylindrical part of the intersection, namely the interval of $z$ for which the cylinder completely fits inside the sphere cross-section.

For a fixed $z$, the cross-section of the region is a disk of radius $\min{r,\sqrt{R^2-z^2}}$. The transition occurs when $r=\sqrt{R^2-z^2}$, that is when $z^2=R^2-r^2$. Hence the cylindrical regime has height $2\sqrt{R^2-r^2}$. This must equal $h$, so $R^2=r^2+\left(\frac h2\right)^2$.

The volume therefore splits into a central cylindrical part and two spherical caps. A direct integration suggests strong cancellation, and it is natural to expect a simplification depending only on $R^3$ and $\left(\frac h2\right)^3$.

The key point is to compute the volume in a form stable under the change $r \mapsto kr$ while keeping $h$ fixed, which forces $R$ to change accordingly.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type C problem.

A solid is formed by intersecting a sphere of radius $R$ with a cylinder of radius $r$ whose axis passes through the sphere’s center. The “width” $h$ is the height of the central cylindrical region inside the sphere, equal to $2\sqrt{R^2-r^2}$. The initial radius $r$ is increased to $kr$, while $h$ remains fixed, which changes $R$.

The task is to compute the increase in volume of the intersection region.

The final answer will be expressed as the difference of two explicit volume expressions:

$$\boxed{\frac{4\pi}{3}\left(\left(k^2r^2+\frac{h^2}{4}\right)^{3/2}-\left(r^2+\frac{h^2}{4}\right)^{3/2}\right)}.$$

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma identifies the geometric meaning of $h$ as $2\sqrt{R^2-r^2}$ and derives $R^2=r^2+\frac{h^2}{4}$.

The second lemma expresses the volume of the intersection as a function of $R$ and $h$ alone by integrating cross-sections and simplifying.

The third lemma shows that this volume reduces to $\frac{4\pi}{3}(R^3-(h/2)^3)$.

The final step substitutes the dependence of $R$ on $r$ and evaluates the difference under the transformation $r \mapsto kr$.

The most delicate point is the simplification of the integral into a cubic expression in $R$ and $h$, where cancellation must be verified exactly.

## Solution

Let the sphere have radius $R$ and center $O$, and let the cylinder have radius $r$ with axis passing through $O$. Place the coordinate system so that the cylinder axis is the $z$-axis. Then the sphere is described by $x^2+y^2+z^2\le R^2$ and the cylinder by $x^2+y^2\le r^2$.

For a fixed $z$, the cross-section of the solid is the set of $(x,y)$ satisfying both $x^2+y^2\le r^2$ and $x^2+y^2\le R^2-z^2$. Hence the cross-sectional radius is $\min{r,\sqrt{R^2-z^2}}$.

The transition between regimes occurs when $r=\sqrt{R^2-z^2}$, which is equivalent to $z^2=R^2-r^2$. Therefore the cylindrical regime corresponds to $|z|\le \sqrt{R^2-r^2}$. By the definition of the width $h$, this central height equals

$$h=2\sqrt{R^2-r^2}, \qquad \text{so} \qquad R^2=r^2+\frac{h^2}{4}.$$

Let $a=\frac h2$, so $a^2=R^2-r^2$.

The volume is computed by splitting along $z$:

$$V=2\int_0^a \pi r^2\,dz + 2\int_a^R \pi (R^2-z^2)\,dz.$$

The first term equals

$$2\pi r^2 a.$$

For the second term, an antiderivative of $R^2-z^2$ is $R^2z-\frac{z^3}{3}$, hence

$$2\pi\left[ R^2z-\frac{z^3}{3}\right]_a^R =2\pi\left(R^3-\frac{R^3}{3}-R^2a+\frac{a^3}{3}\right).$$

Adding both contributions yields

$$V=2\pi r^2 a+2\pi\left(\frac{2}{3}R^3-R^2a+\frac{a^3}{3}\right).$$

Since $r^2=R^2-a^2$, substitution gives

$$2\pi r^2 a-2\pi R^2 a=2\pi a(R^2-a^2-R^2)=-2\pi a^3.$$

Therefore

$$V=\frac{4\pi}{3}R^3-\frac{4\pi}{3}a^3.$$

Replacing $a=\frac h2$ yields

$$V=\frac{4\pi}{3}\left(R^3-\left(\frac h2\right)^3\right).$$

Using $R^2=r^2+\frac{h^2}{4}$, we write

$$V(r)=\frac{4\pi}{3}\left(\left(r^2+\frac{h^2}{4}\right)^{3/2}-\left(\frac h2\right)^3\right).$$

After increasing the radius to $kr$, the new sphere radius $R'$ satisfies

$$R'^2=k^2r^2+\frac{h^2}{4},$$

so

$$V(kr)=\frac{4\pi}{3}\left(\left(k^2r^2+\frac{h^2}{4}\right)^{3/2}-\left(\frac h2\right)^3\right).$$

The added volume is

$$\Delta V=V(kr)-V(r)=\frac{4\pi}{3}\left(\left(k^2r^2+\frac{h^2}{4}\right)^{3/2}-\left(r^2+\frac{h^2}{4}\right)^{3/2}\right).$$

This completes the determination of the required additional amount of gold.

## Verification of Key Steps

The first critical point is the identification of the width $h$ with $2\sqrt{R^2-r^2}$. This follows from the condition defining the boundary between the cylindrical regime $r\le \sqrt{R^2-z^2}$ and the spherical restriction $\sqrt{R^2-z^2}\le r$, which occurs exactly when $z^2=R^2-r^2$, producing a total height $2\sqrt{R^2-r^2}$.

The second delicate step is the simplification of the volume integral. Writing $a=\frac h2$ and substituting $r^2=R^2-a^2$ transforms the mixed terms $r^2a$ and $R^2a$ into a pure cubic term in $a$, producing exact cancellation. Any sign error in this step would destroy the reduction to $\frac{4\pi}{3}(R^3-a^3)$.

The final verification concerns the dependence of $R$ on $r$. Since $h$ is fixed, the relation $R^2=r^2+\frac{h^2}{4}$ remains valid after scaling, ensuring that the only change in volume comes from replacing $r$ by $kr$ inside this expression.

## Alternative Approaches

A different approach begins directly in spherical coordinates, where the condition $x^2+y^2\le r^2$ becomes $\rho^2\sin^2\varphi\le r^2$. Integrating the characteristic function of the intersection over angular variables leads to the same dependence on $R^3-(h/2)^3$, though the angular integration is less transparent than the axial slicing used above. The slicing method is preferable because it isolates the geometry into a single one-dimensional integral with a clear transition point.
