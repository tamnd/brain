## Problem

Prove that if a natural number is divisible by $10\,101\,010\,101$, then in its decimal representation at least six digits are non-zero.

A. K. Tolpygo

Attempting to find a multiple with very few non-zero digits, such as numbers with only one or two non-zero digits, leads to examining numbers of the form $10^k$ or sums like $10^a + 10^b$. Testing small exponents modulo $10,101,010,101$ shows these sums do not yield zero, suggesting a low number of non-zero digits cannot divide this number. The crucial insight is that the combination of these factors forces the decimal expansion to have at least six non-zero digits.

## Problem Understanding

The problem asks to prove a property about the decimal representation of natural numbers divisible by $10,101,010,101$. This is a Type B problem since the statement is a claim to prove: "if a number is divisible by $10,101,010,101$, then it has at least six non-zero digits." The core difficulty lies in relating the factorization of the divisor to constraints on the decimal digits of multiples, especially understanding why numbers with fewer than six non-zero digits cannot satisfy divisibility simultaneously by the prime-like factors. The intuition is that the alternating one-zero pattern of $10,101,010,101$ and its factorization create unavoidable spacing that forces multiple non-zero digits.
