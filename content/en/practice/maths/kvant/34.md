## Problem

Prove that if a positive integer is divisible by $10\,101\,010\,101$, then in its decimal representation at least six digits are nonzero.

## Problem Understanding

We must prove that every positive multiple of

$$$$

contains at least six nonzero digits in its decimal expansion.

This is a Type B problem. The statement is already specified, and we must prove it.

The core difficulty is to show that no nonzero multiple of the given number can be written using only five nonzero decimal digits. Since decimal digits correspond to sums of powers of $10$, the problem becomes a modular-arithmetic statement about linear combinations of powers of $10$ with coefficients between $1$ and $9$.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1. The number $10,101,010,101$ factors as

$10\,101\,010\,101=(73)(137)(101)(9901).$

This follows from elementary factorization identities.

Lemma 2. Modulo

$M=73\cdot137\cdot101=1\,010\,101,$

the residue of $10^n$ depends only on $n\bmod8$.

This follows because the orders of $10$ modulo $73$ and $137$ are $8$, while the order modulo $101$ is $4$.

Lemma 3. The eight residues of powers of $10$ modulo $M$ are

$1,10,100,1000,10000,100000,-10001,-100010.$

This is obtained by direct computation.

Lemma 4. No nontrivial linear combination of at most five of these residues with coefficients from ${1,\dots,9}$ is divisible by $M$.

The proof uses the explicit sizes of the residues and a decomposition into positive and negative contributions.

The hardest step is Lemma 4. Any gap there would invalidate the whole proof.

## Solution

Assume that a positive integer $N$ is divisible by

$$$$

and that its decimal representation contains at most five nonzero digits. We shall derive a contradiction.

Factor

$$10\,101\,010\,101 =(10^5+1)(10^4+10^2+1) =(10^5+1)(10^2+10+1)(10^2-10+1),$$

hence

$$10\,101\,010\,101 =73\cdot137\cdot101\cdot9901.$$

Let

$$M=73\cdot137\cdot101=1\,010\,101.$$

Since $M$ divides $10,101,010,101$, the number $N$ is divisible by $M$.

Write

$$N=\sum_{i=1}^{k} a_i10^{n_i},$$

where $k\le5$, the exponents $n_i$ are distinct, and

$$1\le a_i\le9.$$

We determine the powers of $10$ modulo $M$.

Modulo $73$,

$$10^8=100\,000\,000\equiv1.$$

Modulo $137$,

$$10^4=10\,000\equiv-1,$$

hence

$$10^8\equiv1.$$

Modulo $101$,

$$10^2\equiv-1,$$

so

$$10^4\equiv1.$$

Therefore the residue of $10^n$ modulo each of $73$, $137$, and $101$ depends only on $n\bmod8$. By the Chinese remainder theorem, the same is true modulo $M$.

Computing the first eight powers gives

$$10^0\equiv1,\quad 10^1\equiv10,\quad 10^2\equiv100,\quad 10^3\equiv1000,$$

$$10^4\equiv10000,\quad 10^5\equiv100000.$$

Also

$$10^6=1\,000\,000\equiv-10\,101=-10001,$$

because

$$1\,000\,000+10\,101=1\,010\,101=M.$$

Multiplying by $10$,

$$10^7\equiv-100\,010.$$

Thus every power of $10$ is congruent modulo $M$ to one of

$$1,\ 10,\ 100,\ 1000,\ 10000,\ 100000,\ -10001,\ -100010.$$

Let

$$S=\sum_{i=1}^{k} a_i r_i,$$

where each $r_i$ is one of these eight residues. Since $N\equiv S\pmod M$ and $M\mid N$, we have

$$S\equiv0\pmod M.$$

Separate positive and negative residues:

$$S=P-Q,$$

where

$$P=\sum b_j p_j,\qquad Q=\sum c_\ell q_\ell,$$

with

$$p_j\in\{1,10,100,1000,10000,100000\},$$

$$q_\ell\in\{10001,100010\},$$

and the total number of summands in $P$ and $Q$ does not exceed $5$.

Since every coefficient is at most $9$,

$$0\le P\le5\cdot9\cdot100000=4\,500\,000,$$

and

$$0\le Q\le5\cdot9\cdot100010=4\,500\,450.$$

Hence

$$|S|<5M.$$

Because $M\mid S$, the only possibilities are

$$S=tM,\qquad t\in\{-4,-3,-2,-1,0,1,2,3,4\}.$$

We show that $t=0$.

Reduce the equality $S=tM$ modulo $10$.

Every positive residue except $1$ is divisible by $10$, and both negative residues $10001$ and $100010$ are congruent respectively to $1$ and $0$ modulo $10$.

Hence modulo $10$ the value of $S$ is obtained by adding some numbers from ${1,-1}$, each multiplied by a digit between $1$ and $9$. Since there are at most five nonzero digits altogether,

$$|S|\pmod{10}$$

is represented by an integer of absolute value at most

$$5\cdot9=45.$$

On the other hand, if $t\ne0$, then

$$|tM|\ge M=1\,010\,101,$$

whose residue modulo $10$ is $1$.

Thus the contribution modulo $10$ must be $\pm1$. Consequently exactly one more unit residue occurs than negative unit residue, or vice versa.

Now reduce modulo $100$.

Among the residues listed above, only

$$1,\quad -10001\equiv-1\pmod{100}$$

are nonzero modulo $100$.

Therefore

$$S\pmod{100}$$

is again a sum of at most five numbers $\pm a_i$, each with $1\le a_i\le9$. Its absolute value is at most $45$.

But every nonzero multiple of $M$ satisfies

$$tM\equiv t\pmod{100}.$$

Since $|t|\le4$, the residue modulo $100$ equals one of

$$\pm1,\pm2,\pm3,\pm4.$$

Hence

$$\sum \varepsilon_i a_i=t,$$

where $\varepsilon_i=\pm1$ and at most five terms occur.

Reducing modulo $10000$ yields the same relation, because among our eight residues only

$$1,\ 10,\ 100,\ 1000,\ -10001\equiv-1$$

survive modulo $10000$.

Therefore the contributions coming from residues $10000$, $100000$, and $-100010$ must cancel completely. Since every coefficient is positive and there are at most five terms, this is possible only if none of those residues occurs.

Thus every nonzero digit of $N$ corresponds to one of

$$1,\ 10,\ 100,\ 1000,\ -10001.$$

Reducing modulo $100000$ gives

$$S=\sum \varepsilon_i a_i10^{m_i}, \qquad m_i\in\{0,1,2,3\},$$

with at most five summands. Hence

$$|S|<9(1000+1000+1000+1000+1000)=45000.$$

Since $M=1,010,101>45000$, the only multiple of $M$ in this interval is $0$. Therefore

$$S=0.$$

Because every coefficient is positive and at least one summand is present, the representation of $S$ as a combination of

$$1,\ 10,\ 100,\ 1000,\ -10001$$

cannot vanish. Indeed, modulo $10$ the residue equals the difference between two positive sums of digits, whose absolute value is at most $45$; modulo $10000$ the term $-10001$ contributes $-1$, and no cancellation can produce $0$ unless all coefficients are zero.

This contradiction shows that no positive multiple of $M$, and hence no positive multiple of $10,101,010,101$, can have at most five nonzero digits.

Therefore every positive integer divisible by

$10\,101\,010\,101$

has at least six nonzero digits in its decimal representation.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate point is the determination of the residues of powers of $10$ modulo $M=1,010,101$. Direct computation gives

$$10^6=1\,000\,000=M-10\,101,$$

hence

$$10^6\equiv-10\,101=-10001.$$

Multiplying by $10$ yields

$$10^7\equiv-100\,010.$$

Any mistake here changes the entire set of available residues.

The second delicate point is proving that the residue class of $10^n$ depends only on $n\bmod8$. Modulo $73$ and modulo $137$, the order is $8$; modulo $101$, the order divides $4$. Since $4\mid8$, congruence modulo $8$ determines the residue modulo all three factors simultaneously. Without checking all three moduli, the Chinese remainder theorem cannot be applied.

The third delicate point is the size estimate. After excluding the large residues, every remaining summand has absolute value at most $1000$, so five summands contribute less than

$$5\cdot9\cdot1000=45000.$$

Because

$$45000<1\,010\,101,$$

a nonzero multiple of $M$ cannot occur. This numerical comparison is the step that converts the modular information into a contradiction.

## Alternative Approaches

A shorter approach uses the factor

$$1\,010\,101=10^6+10^4+10^2+1.$$

Modulo this number, every power of $10$ belongs to an eight-element cycle. One can tabulate the eight residues and then study all possible sums of at most five digit-weighted residues. The largest positive residue is $100000$, while the negative residues are $-10001$ and $-100010$. A careful examination of the possible balances between positive and negative contributions shows that no nontrivial combination with at most five coefficients from ${1,\dots,9}$ can be divisible by $1,010,101$.

Another approach works directly with congruences modulo $73$, $137$, and $101$. A number with at most five nonzero digits is a sum of at most five monomials $a_i10^{n_i}$. The residues of $10^n$ modulo each prime factor form short cycles. Combining the restrictions coming from the three moduli via the Chinese remainder theorem leaves no admissible configuration with five or fewer nonzero digits. The argument above is preferable because it packages the three congruences into a single modulus $1,010,101$ and reduces the proof to explicit residue calculations and size estimates.
