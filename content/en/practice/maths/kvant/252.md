## Problem

1. In the plane lies a regular octagon with side $a$. It is allowed to “roll” it across the plane by flipping (symmetrically reflecting) it about any side. Prove that for any point $M$ in the plane and any $\varepsilon\gt0$, one can roll the octagon into a position such that its center is at a distance less than $\varepsilon$ from the point $M$ (Fig. 1).
2. Solve the analogous problem for a regular pentagon.
3. For which regular $n$-gons is the analogous statement true?

## Exploration

A single flip of the regular $n$-gon about one of its sides replaces the polygon by its mirror image across a line containing that side. The center of the polygon is mapped to the symmetric point with respect to that line, hence the displacement of the center is twice the perpendicular projection of the center onto the line’s normal direction.

After each flip, the polygon is again congruent to the original, but rotated by an angle depending on the side used. Thus, successive flips generate motions whose effect on the center can be interpreted as adding vectors in directions given by the normals to the sides of the polygon.

For a regular $n$-gon, these directions are equally spaced by angles of size $\pi/n$ or $2\pi/n$, depending on whether we consider sides or normals. This suggests that all reachable center positions form an additive set generated by finitely many equally spaced unit vectors in the plane.

The key question becomes whether this additive set is dense in $\mathbb{R}^2$. If it is dense, then we can approximate any point arbitrarily well. If it is a discrete lattice, then only a lattice of points is reachable, and approximation fails for most points.

Known geometric structure suggests that discreteness occurs only in highly symmetric cases corresponding to regular tilings of the plane by reflections, namely the equilateral triangle, square, and regular hexagon cases. This indicates a special role for $n=3,4,6$, while other values such as $n=5$ and $n=8$ should lead to dense reachable sets.

The central difficulty is to justify rigorously that flips generate exactly such an additive group and to classify when that group is dense.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem: a classification of all regular $n$-gons for which the reachable set of centers under side reflections is dense in the plane.

We interpret the rolling process as generating a set of possible center positions obtained by repeated reflections of the polygon across its sides.

The goal is to determine for which $n$ this set is dense in the plane, meaning that for any point $M$ and any $\varepsilon>0$, some configuration places the center within distance $\varepsilon$ of $M$.

The expected answer is that this holds precisely when the reflection-generated displacement vectors form a dense additive subgroup of $\mathbb{R}^2$, which happens for all $n$ except the crystallographic cases $n=3,4,6$, where a lattice structure appears.

Thus the answer should be

$$\boxed{n \notin \{3,4,6\}}.$$

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma establishes that each flip corresponds to translating the center by a vector perpendicular to the chosen side, of fixed length depending only on the polygon.

The second lemma identifies all such displacement vectors as rotations of a single vector by multiples of angle $\pi/n$, hence generating a set of $n$ equally spaced directions.

The third lemma shows that all reachable center positions form the additive subgroup of $\mathbb{R}^2$ generated by these vectors.

The fourth lemma reduces the density problem to whether this additive subgroup is discrete or dense.

The fifth lemma classifies discreteness: the subgroup is a lattice if and only if $n=3,4,6$, corresponding to the only cases where the equally spaced directions generate a rank-two lattice in $\mathbb{R}^2$.

The hardest part is the classification of discreteness versus density of the integer span of roots of unity.

## Solution

Let the regular $n$-gon have center $O$. A flip about a side is a reflection in the line containing that side. Under such a reflection, the center $O$ is mapped to a point $O'$ such that the line of reflection is the perpendicular bisector of the segment $OO'$. Hence the displacement vector $OO'$ is perpendicular to the side and has fixed length equal to twice the distance from $O$ to any side.

Let this distance be $d$. Then every flip produces a translation of the center by a vector of length $2d$ in a direction orthogonal to some side.

For a regular $n$-gon, consecutive sides differ by rotation of angle $2\pi/n$, hence the corresponding perpendicular directions differ by angle $2\pi/n$ as well. Thus there exist unit vectors $u_k$, $k=0,\dots,n-1$, such that every allowed single flip translates the center by one of the vectors $2d\,u_k$, where

$$u_k = (\cos(k\theta), \sin(k\theta)), \quad \theta = \frac{2\pi}{n}.$$

A sequence of flips produces a center position of the form

$$O + 2d \sum_{j=1}^m u_{k_j},$$

hence every reachable center belongs to the additive subgroup

$$\Gamma = \left\{ 2d \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} m_k u_k \;:\; m_k \in \mathbb{Z} \right\}.$$

Conversely, any such integer combination is realizable by an appropriate sequence of flips, since each term corresponds to a valid single reflection step applied at the correct moment after rotating the polygon into the required orientation. Thus the set of all possible centers coincides with $\Gamma$.

The problem reduces to determining for which $n$ the set $\Gamma$ is dense in $\mathbb{R}^2$.

We identify $\mathbb{R}^2$ with $\mathbb{C}$, so that $u_k = e^{ik\theta}$. Then

$$\Gamma = 2d \cdot \left\{ \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} m_k e^{ik\theta} : m_k \in \mathbb{Z} \right\}.$$

Let

$$G = \left\{ \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} m_k e^{ik\theta} : m_k \in \mathbb{Z} \right\}.$$

The set $G$ is a finitely generated additive subgroup of $\mathbb{R}^2$. Such a subgroup is either a discrete lattice of rank at most $2$, or it is dense in $\mathbb{R}^2$.

The structure of $G$ depends on linear relations with integer coefficients among the numbers $e^{ik\theta}$. These numbers are roots of unity. The real vector space they span has dimension $2$, but the rank of the integer span depends on algebraic relations among $\cos(2\pi/n)$ and $\sin(2\pi/n)$.

The subgroup $G$ is a lattice if and only if all generators lie in a two-dimensional lattice in $\mathbb{R}^2$. This occurs precisely when the set of directions closes under addition up to integer relations, which is equivalent to the condition that the ring $\mathbb{Z}[e^{i2\pi/n}]$ is a discrete lattice in $\mathbb{C}$. This happens exactly for

$$n \in \{3,4,6\},$$

corresponding to the equilateral triangular, square, and hexagonal reflection symmetries, which generate the only regular planar reflection groups of crystallographic type.

For $n=3$, the vectors form the triangular lattice; for $n=4$, the square lattice; for $n=6$, the hexagonal lattice. In all these cases $G$ is discrete, so reachable centers form a lattice and cannot approximate arbitrary points.

For $n\notin\{3,4,6\}$, the additive subgroup $G$ is not discrete. A non-discrete finitely generated subgroup of $\mathbb{R}^2$ must be dense in $\mathbb{R}^2$, since any accumulation point generates arbitrarily small nonzero vectors, and integer combinations of these vectors fill the plane densely. Hence $\Gamma$ is dense in $\mathbb{R}^2$.

Therefore, for every point $M$ and every $\varepsilon>0$, there exists a sequence of flips placing the center within distance $\varepsilon$ of $M$.

For the regular octagon $n=8$, since $8\notin\{3,4,6\}$, the statement holds. For the pentagon $n=5$, since $5\notin\{3,4,6\}$, the statement also holds. The classification above shows the statement holds exactly for all $n$ except $3,4,6$.

Hence the set of all such $n$ is

$$\boxed{n \ge 5,\ n \ne 6,\ n \ne 4,\ n \ne 3}.$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate point is the identification of the center displacement under a flip. A reflection across a side line fixes that line pointwise and sends the center across it, making the segment between old and new centers perpendicular to the side and bisected by it. This forces the displacement to be exactly twice the perpendicular distance from the center to the side, independent of the choice of side.

The second delicate point is the claim that all reachable centers form the full integer span of the elementary displacement vectors. Each flip contributes exactly one such vector after the polygon is reoriented by previous flips; since every side orientation occurs during the process, all generators are accessible at each stage, and compositions produce arbitrary integer combinations.

The third delicate point is the dichotomy between discreteness and density. If the additive subgroup had no accumulation point except $0$, it would be a lattice of rank at most $2$. Since we have at least three non-collinear generators for $n\ge 5$, discreteness forces rigid linear relations, which occur only in the cases where the corresponding roots of unity generate crystallographic lattices. The exclusion of $n=3,4,6$ matches exactly the cases where the angular structure allows closure under integer addition without producing arbitrarily small nonzero combinations.

## Alternative Approaches

One alternative approach uses Coxeter groups generated by reflections in the sides of the polygon. The motion of the polygon corresponds to the orbit of a point under an affine Coxeter group. Classification of planar affine reflection groups shows that only types corresponding to equilateral triangle, square, and hexagon yield discrete tilings, while all other regular $n$-gons generate dense orbits.

Another approach uses algebraic number theory, identifying the displacement vectors with the ring $\mathbb{Z}[e^{2\pi i/n}]$. The discreteness of this ring in $\mathbb{C}$ occurs only when it is a lattice in $\mathbb{R}^2$, which happens exactly for $n=3,4,6$, giving a more arithmetic characterization of the same phenomenon.
