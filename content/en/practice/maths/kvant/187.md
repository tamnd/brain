## Problem

On the plane, two points $A$ and $B$ are given. Find the locus of the third vertices $C$ of triangle $ABC$ such that:

1. the altitude $AA'$ is equal to the side $BC$;
2. the median $AA_1$ is equal to the side $AC$;
3. the median $AA_1$ is equal to the side $BC$;
4. the altitude $CC'$ is equal to the median $BB_1$;
5. the altitude $BB'$ is equal to the median $CC_1$.

M. A. Kvantov

## Exploration

Fix points $A$ and $B$ and treat $C$ as a variable point. The conditions involve altitudes and medians, so each relation can be rewritten in terms of areas, dot products, or vector lengths. This suggests that placing coordinates with $A=(0,0)$ and $B=(1,0)$ removes unnecessary scaling and simplifies all expressions.

With this normalization, $C=(x,y)$, the altitude from a point to a line becomes a simple expression involving absolute values, and medians become distances to midpoints. This converts each geometric condition into an algebraic equation in $x$ and $y$.

The main risk is that altitude expressions involve absolute values, which split the locus into symmetric branches. Another potential failure point is squaring equations too early and introducing extraneous components, so each equivalence must be checked carefully.

Each of the five conditions is independent, so the task reduces to finding five separate loci.

## Problem Understanding

Two fixed points $A$ and $B$ are given, and for each of five independent geometric conditions we are to determine the locus of points $C$ in the plane satisfying that condition.

This is a Type A classification problem applied five times.

After normalization $A=(0,0)$ and $B=(1,0)$, each condition becomes an explicit algebraic curve or union of curves in coordinates $(x,y)$, hence the loci can be described exactly.

## Proof Architecture

For each of the five conditions, the argument proceeds by placing $A=(0,0)$ and $B=(1,0)$ and writing $C=(x,y)$.

For the first condition, the altitude from $A$ to $BC$ is expressed as $\frac{|(B-A)\times(C-A)|}{|BC|}$ and equated to $|BC|$, yielding a Cartesian equation whose sign split must be handled.

For the second condition, the median from $A$ is written as the distance from $A$ to the midpoint of $BC$, producing a circle after expansion.

For the third condition, both median and side lengths are expressed in vector form, producing another circle after algebraic simplification.

For the fourth condition, altitude from $C$ to $AB$ and median from $B$ to $AC$ are both linear in coordinates after squaring, yielding a pair of straight lines.

For the fifth condition, altitude from $B$ to $AC$ is written using a cross product and equated to a median length, producing a quartic curve defined implicitly.

The most delicate part is the first condition, where absolute values split the locus into two distinct circles.

## Solution

Place $A=(0,0)$ and $B=(1,0)$, and let $C=(x,y)$.

For all computations, $AB=1$, $AC^2=x^2+y^2$, and $BC^2=(x-1)^2+y^2$.

### 1. Condition $AA'=BC$

The altitude from $A$ to line $BC$ equals $\frac{|(B-A)\times(C-A)|}{|BC|}=\frac{|y|}{\sqrt{(x-1)^2+y^2}}$. The condition becomes

$\frac{|y|}{\sqrt{(x-1)^2+y^2}}=\sqrt{(x-1)^2+y^2},$

hence

$|y|=(x-1)^2+y^2.$

If $y\ge 0$, then

$y=(x-1)^2+y^2,$

so

$(x-1)^2+y^2-y=0,$

which rewrites as

$(x-1)^2+\left(y-\frac12\right)^2=\frac14.$

If $y\le 0$, then

$-y=(x-1)^2+y^2,$

so

$(x-1)^2+y^2+y=0,$

which rewrites as

$(x-1)^2+\left(y+\frac12\right)^2=\frac14.$

Thus the locus is the union of two circles with centers $(1,\frac12)$ and $(1,-\frac12)$, both of radius $\frac12$.

### 2. Condition $AA_1=AC$

The midpoint of $BC$ is $\left(\frac{x+1}{2},\frac{y}{2}\right)$, hence

$AA_1^2=\frac{(x+1)^2+y^2}{4}.$

The condition $AA_1=AC$ becomes

$\frac{(x+1)^2+y^2}{4}=x^2+y^2,$

which simplifies to

$(x+1)^2+y^2=4x^2+4y^2.$

Expanding gives

$x^2+2x+1+y^2=4x^2+4y^2,$

hence

$3x^2+3y^2-2x-1=0.$

Completing the square,

$x^2-\frac{2}{3}x+y^2=\frac{1}{3},$

so

$\left(x-\frac{1}{3}\right)^2+y^2=\frac{4}{9}.$

Thus the locus is a circle centered at $\left(\frac13,0\right)$ with radius $\frac{2}{3}$.

### 3. Condition $AA_1=BC$

We again use $AA_1^2=\frac{(x+1)^2+y^2}{4}$ and $BC^2=(x-1)^2+y^2$. The condition becomes

$\frac{(x+1)^2+y^2}{4}=(x-1)^2+y^2,$

so

$(x+1)^2+y^2=4(x-1)^2+4y^2.$

Expanding yields

$x^2+2x+1+y^2=4x^2-8x+4+4y^2,$

hence

$3x^2+3y^2-10x+3=0.$

Dividing by $3$ gives

$x^2+y^2-\frac{10}{3}x+1=0.$

Completing the square,

$\left(x-\frac{5}{3}\right)^2+y^2=\frac{16}{9}.$

Thus the locus is a circle centered at $\left(\frac{5}{3},0\right)$ with radius $\frac{4}{3}$.

### 4. Condition $CC'=BB_1$

The altitude from $C$ to $AB$ equals $|y|$. The midpoint of $AC$ is $\left(\frac{x}{2},\frac{y}{2}\right)$, so

$BB_1^2=\left(\frac{x}{2}-1\right)^2+\frac{y^2}{4}.$

The condition $|y|=BB_1$ becomes

$y^2=\left(\frac{x}{2}-1\right)^2+\frac{y^2}{4}.$

Multiplying by $4$ gives

$4y^2=(x-2)^2+y^2,$

hence

$3y^2=(x-2)^2.$

Thus

$|y|=\frac{|x-2|}{\sqrt{3}},$

which represents two straight lines

$y=\frac{x-2}{\sqrt{3}},\quad y=-\frac{x-2}{\sqrt{3}}.$

### 5. Condition $BB'=CC_1$

The altitude from $B$ to line $AC$ equals

$BB'=\frac{|(C-A)\times(B-A)|}{|AC|}=\frac{|y|}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}.$

The midpoint of $AB$ is $\left(\frac12,0\right)$, hence

$CC_1^2=\left(x-\frac12\right)^2+y^2.$

The condition becomes

$\frac{|y|}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}=\sqrt{\left(x-\frac12\right)^2+y^2}.$

Squaring yields

$\frac{y^2}{x^2+y^2}=\left(x-\frac12\right)^2+y^2,$

and multiplying by $x^2+y^2$ gives the locus equation

$y^2=(x^2+y^2)\left[\left(x-\frac12\right)^2+y^2\right].$

This is an implicit quartic curve defining the required locus.

## Verification of Key Steps

In the first condition, splitting by the sign of $y$ is necessary because the altitude is expressed using $|y|$, and failure to separate cases would incorrectly merge two distinct circles into one algebraic equation.

In the fourth condition, squaring preserves equivalence because both sides are nonnegative lengths, and the resulting factorization reduces exactly to two linear equations without extraneous branches.

In the fifth condition, every step preserves equivalence because both sides are nonnegative; after clearing denominators, no division by zero occurs except at $C=A$, which does not satisfy the original relation.

## Alternative Approaches

A synthetic approach replaces coordinates with vector geometry and interprets each condition as a relation between areas and side lengths, reducing several cases to Apollonius-type loci. However, the coordinate method isolates each locus into explicit algebraic curves without ambiguity, making verification of each condition more direct and mechanically checkable.
