## Problem

Prove that $3^{1974}+5^{1974}$ is divisible by 13.

S. I. Meyzus

## Exploration

Work is carried out modulo $13$. The goal is to understand the behaviour of powers of $3$ and $5$ under reduction modulo $13$ and to detect periodicity in their residues.

Direct computation of small powers gives $3^1=3$, $3^2=9$, and $3^3=27$, and reduction of $27$ modulo $13$ yields a remainder of $1$. This indicates that powers of $3$ repeat with period dividing $3$ modulo $13$.

For $5$, computation gives $5^2=25$, $5^3=125$, and $5^4=625$. Reduction of $625$ modulo $13$ gives remainder $1$, showing that powers of $5$ repeat with period dividing $4$ modulo $13$.

The exponent $1974$ is divisible by $3$ and leaves remainder $2$ upon division by $4$. This suggests that $3^{1974}$ reduces to $1$ modulo $13$ while $5^{1974}$ reduces to the same residue as $5^2$ modulo $13$, which equals $-1$. Their sum is therefore expected to vanish modulo $13$.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem, requiring classification of a divisibility statement. The task is to prove that $3^{1974}+5^{1974}$ is divisible by $13$, which is equivalent to showing that it is congruent to $0$ modulo $13$.

The core difficulty lies in identifying the multiplicative periods of $3$ and $5$ modulo $13$ and correctly reducing a large exponent using these periods.

The expected result is

$3^{1974}+5^{1974} \equiv 0 \pmod{13},$

so the expression is divisible by $13$.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma establishes that $3^3 \equiv 1 \pmod{13}$ by direct computation.

The second lemma shows that $5^4 \equiv 1 \pmod{13}$ by direct computation.

The third lemma reduces $3^{1974}$ using the fact that $1974$ is divisible by $3$, yielding $3^{1974} \equiv 1 \pmod{13}$.

The fourth lemma reduces $5^{1974}$ using the decomposition $1974=4\cdot 493+2$, yielding $5^{1974}\equiv 5^2 \pmod{13}$ and then computes $5^2 \equiv -1 \pmod{13}$.

The final step combines the two residues.

The most delicate point is ensuring correct reduction of large exponents using the established periods, since an incorrect decomposition of $1974$ modulo the periods would invalidate the conclusion.

## Solution

Computation gives

$3^2=9,\quad 3^3=27,$

and reduction of $27$ modulo $13$ yields

$27 = 13\cdot 2 + 1,$

so

$3^3 \equiv 1 \pmod{13}.$

Computation also gives

$5^2=25,\quad 5^4=625,$

and reduction of $625$ modulo $13$ yields

$625 = 13\cdot 48 + 1,$

so

$5^4 \equiv 1 \pmod{13}.$

Since $1974=3\cdot 658$, repeated multiplication by $3^3$ yields

$3^{1974} = (3^3)^{658}.$

Using $3^3 \equiv 1 \pmod{13}$ gives

$(3^3)^{658} \equiv 1^{658} \equiv 1 \pmod{13},$

hence

$3^{1974} \equiv 1 \pmod{13}.$

Since $1974=4\cdot 493+2$, we write

$5^{1974}=5^{4\cdot 493+2}=(5^4)^{493}\cdot 5^2.$

Using $5^4 \equiv 1 \pmod{13}$ gives

$(5^4)^{493}\cdot 5^2 \equiv 1^{493}\cdot 5^2 \equiv 5^2 \pmod{13}.$

Direct computation yields

$5^2=25=13\cdot 1+12,$

so

$5^2 \equiv 12 \equiv -1 \pmod{13}.$

Thus

$5^{1974} \equiv -1 \pmod{13}.$

Combining both congruences gives

$3^{1974}+5^{1974} \equiv 1 + (-1) \equiv 0 \pmod{13}.$

Hence $3^{1974}+5^{1974}$ is divisible by $13$, and the result follows.

$\boxed{13 \mid \left(3^{1974}+5^{1974}\right)}$

## Verification of Key Steps

The reduction $3^3 \equiv 1 \pmod{13}$ is confirmed by explicit computation $27=13\cdot 2+1$, and no intermediate assumption beyond modular equality is used in raising both sides to the power $658$.

The reduction $5^4 \equiv 1 \pmod{13}$ is confirmed by explicit computation $625=13\cdot 48+1$, and the decomposition $1974=4\cdot 493+2$ ensures that all higher powers of $5$ reduce to a power of $5^4$ multiplied by $5^2$ with no remainder ambiguity.

The computation $5^2 \equiv -1 \pmod{13}$ is verified directly from $25=13\cdot 1+12$, and the identification $12\equiv -1 \pmod{13}$ follows from $12+1=13$.

## Alternative Approaches

A different approach uses Euler’s theorem, since $13$ is prime and $\varphi(13)=12$. One obtains $3^{12}\equiv 1 \pmod{13}$ and $5^{12}\equiv 1 \pmod{13}$, then reduces $1974$ modulo $12$. This yields $1974=12\cdot 164+6$, so both terms reduce to sixth powers, after which direct computation of $3^6$ and $5^6$ modulo $13$ leads to the same cancellation. The present approach is shorter because it exploits smaller multiplicative orders $3$ and $4$ directly, avoiding reduction modulo $12$.
