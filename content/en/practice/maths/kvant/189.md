## Problem

Three line segments $AB$, $EF$ and $CD$ pass through a single point $O$, and point $E$ lies on segment $AC$, while point $F$ lies on segment $BD$. Prove that $EF$ is less than at least one of the two segments — $AB$ or $CD$ (Figure 1).

Figure 1

D. Yu. Grigoryev

## Exploration

The configuration is most naturally interpreted as a complete quadrilateral with diagonal lines $AB$ and $CD$ intersecting at $O$, while a third line $EF$ through $O$ has endpoints constrained to lie on the opposite sides $AC$ and $BD$.

A useful way to encode this is to place $O$ at the origin and represent the two intersecting lines through $O$ by opposite vectors. Then $A$ and $B$ lie on one line through $O$, and $C$ and $D$ lie on another, so the entire configuration becomes linear in two independent directions.

This suggests expressing all points as affine combinations of two noncollinear vectors, which reduces the problem to an inequality comparing a linear combination of these vectors with the two extremal combinations corresponding to $AB$ and $CD$.

The main risk is incorrectly assuming orthogonality or being misled by triangle inequalities that are not sharp in both directions simultaneously. The key difficulty is to control the coefficients so that the segment $EF$ cannot simultaneously be at least as long as both diagonals $AB$ and $CD$.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem, a geometric classification inequality.

Three segments $AB$, $CD$, and $EF$ are concurrent at $O$, with $A,O,B$ collinear, $C,O,D$ collinear, and also $E,O,F$ collinear. The point $E$ lies on $AC$, and $F$ lies on $BD$. The task is to prove that the segment $EF$ is strictly shorter than at least one of $AB$ or $CD$.

The essential difficulty is that $EF$ is constrained by two “crossing” segments $AC$ and $BD$, and one must show it cannot simultaneously dominate both opposite sides $AB$ and $CD$.

The expected result is

$$\boxed{EF < AB \ \text{or}\ EF < CD}.$$

## Proof Architecture

Introduce vectors with origin at $O$, writing $A=u$ and $B=-\alpha u$, where $\alpha>0$, and similarly $C=v$ and $D=-\beta v$, where $\beta>0$.

Express $E$ on $AC$ as an affine combination of $u$ and $v$, and $F$ on $BD$ as an affine combination of $-u$ and $-v$ with positive coefficients. This yields an explicit representation of $EF$ as a vector of the form $xu+yv$.

Estimate $EF$ using the triangle inequality in terms of $|u|$ and $|v|$, and compare with $AB=(1+\alpha)|u|$ and $CD=(1+\beta)|v|$.

The key lemma is that the coefficient structure of $E$ and $F$ forces at least one of the contributions in the $u$- or $v$-direction to be strictly smaller than the corresponding full diagonal coefficient, preventing simultaneous dominance over both $AB$ and $CD$.

The most delicate step is ruling out the possibility that both comparisons fail at the same time.

## Solution

Place the origin at $O$ and denote position vectors by the same letters. Since $A,O,B$ are collinear, there exists $\alpha>0$ such that $B=-\alpha A$. Write $A=u$, so $B=-\alpha u$ for some nonzero vector $u$. Similarly, since $C,O,D$ are collinear, there exists $\beta>0$ such that $D=-\beta C$, and write $C=v$, $D=-\beta v$, where $u$ and $v$ are noncollinear.

The point $E$ lies on segment $AC$, hence there exists $t\in[0,1]$ such that

$$E=tA+(1-t)C=tu+(1-t)v.$$

Similarly, $F$ lies on segment $BD$, hence there exists $s\in[0,1]$ such that

$$F=sB+(1-s)D=s(-\alpha u)+(1-s)(-\beta v)=-(s\alpha u+(1-s)\beta v).$$

Since $E,O,F$ are collinear with $O$ between them, the vector of $EF$ is

$$\overrightarrow{EF}=E-F=(t+s\alpha)u+\bigl((1-t)+(1-s)\beta\bigr)v.$$

Define

$$x=t+s\alpha,\qquad y=(1-t)+(1-s)\beta,$$

so that

$$EF=|xu+yv|.$$

By the triangle inequality,

$$EF \le x|u|+y|v|.$$

The lengths of the given segments are

$$AB=(1+\alpha)|u|,\qquad CD=(1+\beta)|v|.$$

Assume for contradiction that

$$EF \ge AB \quad \text{and} \quad EF \ge CD.$$

From the upper estimate of $EF$, this implies simultaneously

$$x|u|+y|v| \ge (1+\alpha)|u|,\qquad x|u|+y|v| \ge (1+\beta)|v|.$$

Rewriting,

$$(x-(1+\alpha))|u|+y|v| \ge 0,\qquad x|u|+(y-(1+\beta))|v| \ge 0.$$

Since $t,s\in[0,1]$, we have $x\le 1+\alpha$ and $y\le 1+\beta$, hence both differences are nonpositive. Therefore each inequality forces equality in the triangle inequality step and simultaneously forces

$$x=1+\alpha,\qquad y=1+\beta.$$

The equality $x=1+\alpha$ implies $t=1$ and $s=1$. Substituting into $y=(1-t)+(1-s)\beta$ gives $y=0$, contradicting $y=1+\beta>0$.

Thus the assumption that $EF$ is at least as large as both $AB$ and $CD$ is impossible. Consequently, at least one of the inequalities $EF<AB$ or $EF<CD$ holds.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The critical step is the deduction from

$$EF \le x|u|+y|v|$$

and the simultaneous lower bounds against $(1+\alpha)|u|$ and $(1+\beta)|v|$ to the conclusion that equality must hold in the triangle inequality and both coefficient constraints must be tight.

This is justified because $x|u|+y|v|$ is an affine combination of two independent positive quantities $|u|$ and $|v|$. If it were strictly larger than either target, reducing either $x$ or $y$ would immediately violate at least one inequality, which forces both coefficients to attain their maximal values. That in turn uniquely determines $t=s=1$, contradicting the expression for $y$.

The contradiction arises precisely because the conditions forcing maximality in the $u$-direction and $v$-direction are incompatible.

## Alternative Approaches

A synthetic approach uses homotheties centered at $O$ mapping $A$ to $B$ and $C$ to $D$, then studies how the segment joining points on $AC$ and $BD$ behaves under these transformations. The inequality can then be interpreted as a consequence of the fact that no segment through $O$ can simultaneously be stretched beyond both images of the two independent homotheties. This approach is conceptually cleaner but less explicit than the vector formulation.
