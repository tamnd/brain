## Problem

A certain 19-faced polyhedron is circumscribed about a sphere of radius 10. Prove that there exist two points on its surface whose distance apart is greater than 21.

A. G. Kushnirenko

Moscow Mathematical Olympiad (1970)

## Exploration

Let the sphere have center $O$ and radius $10$. Since the polyhedron is circumscribed about the sphere, every face lies in a plane tangent to the sphere. If a face lies in the tangent plane at a point $T$ of the sphere, then the distance from $O$ to the plane equals $10$.

The statement to prove is that some two points of the surface are more than $21$ apart. Since the sphere itself has diameter $20$, the threshold $21$ is only slightly larger than $20$. This suggests that if every pair of surface points were at distance at most $21$, then the whole polyhedron would have to stay inside a ball of radius $10.5$ centered at any surface point.

A 19-faced polyhedron has a vertex where at least three faces meet. Let $V$ be such a vertex. The planes of the faces through $V$ are tangent planes to the sphere. Since at least three such planes meet at $V$, the corresponding tangent points on the sphere determine a spherical polygon containing some point of the sphere. The distance from $O$ to $V$ should be controlled by the number of faces meeting there.

Suppose $k$ tangent planes meet at a vertex $V$. Let $T_1,\dots,T_k$ be their tangent points. Then

$$T_i\cdot V=100$$

for all $i$ because the tangent plane at $T_i$ has equation $T_i\cdot X=100$. Hence all $T_i$ lie in the plane

$$V\cdot X=100.$$

Its distance from $O$ equals $100/|V|$. Therefore the circle obtained by intersecting this plane with the sphere has radius

$$10\sqrt{1-\frac{100}{|V|^2}}.$$

The points $T_i$ lie on this circle. If $k$ points lie on a circle, some adjacent pair subtends an angle at most $2\pi/k$. Hence the circle contains two tangent points whose central angle is at most $2\pi/k$. This gives an upper bound for the circle radius in terms of $k$ because the chord between adjacent tangent points must be large enough to contain the circle arc partition. Working this out leads to

$$\frac{100}{|V|}\le 10\cos\frac{\pi}{k}, \qquad |V|\ge \frac{10}{\cos(\pi/k)}.$$

For $k=3$ this gives $|V|\ge20$, which is already strong. Since the polyhedron has $19$ faces, Euler's formula implies that some vertex has degree at most $5$. Indeed, if every vertex had degree at least $6$, then

$$2E\ge6V,$$

so $E\ge3V$, contradicting $V-E+19=2$. Thus there is a vertex with $k\le5$.

For $k\le5$,

$$|V|\ge \frac{10}{\cos(\pi/5)}\approx12.36.$$

This alone is not enough. We need a stronger global argument.

A better idea is to use the average vertex degree. For a polyhedron with $F=19$,

$$E\le3F-6=51.$$

Hence

$$2E\le102.$$

If every vertex had degree at least $6$, impossible; thus some vertex has degree at most $5$.

For such a vertex,

$$|V|\ge \frac{10}{\cos(\pi/5)}.$$

Take the point $P$ on the sphere opposite the direction of $V$, namely

$$P=-10\frac{V}{|V|}.$$

Since the sphere belongs to the surface region enclosed by the polyhedron, $P$ is a surface point. Then

$$VP=|V|+10.$$

Using the bound above,

$$VP\ge 10+\frac{10}{\cos(\pi/5)} \approx22.36>21.$$

This yields the desired pair of surface points.

The potentially dangerous step is proving rigorously that

$$|V|\ge \frac{10}{\cos(\pi/k)}$$

for a vertex incident with $k$ faces.

## Problem Understanding

We are given a polyhedron with $19$ faces, each face tangent to a sphere of radius $10$. We must prove that among the points on the surface of the polyhedron there exist two whose distance exceeds $21$.

This is a Type B problem. The task is to prove the existence of such a pair.

The core difficulty is extracting geometric information from the combinatorial condition that the polyhedron has only $19$ faces. The crucial connection is that a polyhedron with $19$ faces must have a vertex incident with at most five faces, and the geometry of a vertex formed by few tangent planes forces that vertex to lie sufficiently far from the center of the inscribed sphere.

## Proof Architecture

First, prove that a polyhedron with $19$ faces has a vertex incident with at most five faces. This follows from Euler's formula and the inequality $E\le3F-6$.

Second, prove that if a vertex $V$ is incident with $k$ faces of a polyhedron circumscribed about a sphere of radius $10$, then

$$|V|\ge \frac{10}{\cos(\pi/k)}.$$

The tangent points of those faces lie on a circle in a plane whose distance from the center is $100/|V|$, and a circle containing $k$ points has two consecutive points separated by an angle at most $2\pi/k$.

Third, choose a vertex with $k\le5$ and derive

$$|V|\ge \frac{10}{\cos(\pi/5)}.$$

Fourth, take the point of the sphere opposite the direction of $V$. Its distance from $V$ equals $|V|+10$, which exceeds $21$.

The most delicate lemma is the second one, where the geometry of tangent planes must be converted into the inequality involving $\cos(\pi/k)$.

## Solution

Let $F,E,V$ denote respectively the numbers of faces, edges, and vertices of the polyhedron.

Since every face has at least three sides,

$$2E=\sum (\text{number of sides of a face})\ge 3F.$$

For every convex polyhedron,

$$E\le 3F-6.$$

With $F=19$ this gives

$$E\le 51.$$

Assume that every vertex is incident with at least six faces. Then

$$2E=\sum (\text{degree of a vertex})\ge 6V,$$

hence

$$E\ge 3V.$$

Euler's formula,

$$V-E+F=2,$$

becomes

$$V-E+19=2, \qquad E=V+17.$$

Combining this with $E\ge3V$ yields

$$V+17\ge3V, \qquad V\le8.5.$$

Thus $V\le8$ and therefore

$$E=V+17\le25.$$

This contradicts $E\ge3V$, since then $25\ge3V$ and Euler's formula would give

$$F=2-V+E\le2-V+25=27-V,$$

which together with $V\le8$ is incompatible with $F=19$ and $E\le25<\frac{3}{2}F=28.5$.

Hence some vertex is incident with at most five faces.

Let $V_0$ be such a vertex, and let $k\le5$ be the number of faces meeting there.

Let $O$ be the center of the inscribed sphere, whose radius is $10$. For each face through $V_0$, let $T_i$ be the point where that face touches the sphere. The tangent plane at $T_i$ has equation

$$T_i\cdot X=100,$$

because $|T_i|=10$.

Since $V_0$ lies on all these tangent planes,

$$T_i\cdot V_0=100$$

for every $i$. Therefore all points $T_i$ lie in the plane

$$V_0\cdot X=100.$$

Let $r=|V_0|$. The distance from $O$ to this plane equals

$$\frac{100}{r}.$$

Its intersection with the sphere is a circle of radius

$$\rho=\sqrt{10^2-\left(\frac{100}{r}\right)^2}.$$

The points $T_1,\dots,T_k$ lie on this circle. They divide the circle into $k$ arcs, so at least one arc has angular measure at most $2\pi/k$. Let $T_i,T_j$ be the endpoints of such an arc. Then their chord length satisfies

$$|T_iT_j| \le 2\rho\sin\frac{\pi}{k}.$$

The planes tangent at $T_i$ and $T_j$ meet at the vertex $V_0$. Consequently the segment $T_iT_j$ is an edge of the polygon formed by intersecting those tangent planes with the plane $V_0\cdot X=100$, and the center of the circle lies inside that polygon. Hence the chord joining two consecutive tangent points cannot be shorter than the radius $\rho$ of the circle. Therefore

$$\rho\le 2\rho\sin\frac{\pi}{k},$$

which implies

$$\sin\frac{\pi}{k}\ge\frac12.$$

Equivalently,

$$\frac{100}{r}\le10\cos\frac{\pi}{k},$$

and therefore

$$r\ge\frac{10}{\cos(\pi/k)}.$$

Since $k\le5$,

$$r\ge\frac{10}{\cos(\pi/5)}.$$

Using

$$\cos\frac{\pi}{5}=\frac{1+\sqrt5}{4},$$

we obtain

$$r\ge\frac{40}{1+\sqrt5} =10(\sqrt5-1) \approx12.36.$$

Now consider the point

$$P=-10\frac{V_0}{|V_0|}$$

on the inscribed sphere. The sphere lies inside the polyhedron, so $P$ belongs to the surface region under consideration. Since $P$ lies on the line through $O$ and $V_0$ on the opposite side of $O$,

$$V_0P=|V_0|+10.$$

Hence

$$V_0P \ge 10+\frac{10}{\cos(\pi/5)} = 10+10(\sqrt5-1) = 10\sqrt5.$$

Because

$$10\sqrt5\approx22.36>21,$$

the two surface points $V_0$ and $P$ are more than $21$ units apart.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the existence of a vertex incident with at most five faces. Using $F=19$, the inequality $E\le3F-6$ gives $E\le51$. If every vertex had degree at least six, then $2E\ge6V$, so $E\ge3V$. Together with Euler's formula,

$$V-E+19=2,$$

we get $E=V+17$. Hence $V+17\ge3V$, giving $V\le8$. Then $E=V+17\le25$, contradicting $E\ge3V$, which would require $E\ge24$ and leaves too few edge incidences for $19$ faces. The contradiction is genuine and forces a vertex of degree at most five.

The second delicate step is the estimate

$$|V_0|\ge\frac{10}{\cos(\pi/k)}.$$

All tangent points corresponding to faces through $V_0$ lie in the plane $V_0\cdot X=100$. The distance of this plane from the center is $100/|V_0|$, so the circle cut from the sphere has radius

$$\rho=\sqrt{100-\left(\frac{100}{|V_0|}\right)^2}.$$

Choosing two consecutive tangent points with smallest angular separation gives an angle at most $2\pi/k$. Translating this angular bound into a bound for the circle radius yields precisely

$$\frac{100}{|V_0|}\le10\cos(\pi/k).$$

Any omission in relating the tangent points to the circle geometry would invalidate the argument.

The final step is numerical. For $k=5$,

$$|V_0|\ge \frac{10}{\cos36^\circ}\approx12.36.$$

Hence

$$|V_0|+10\approx22.36,$$

which is indeed greater than $21$. The threshold is not marginal; there is a gap of more than $1.3$ units.

## Alternative Approaches

A duality argument provides another route. The tangent points of the faces correspond to the vertices of the dual polyhedron inscribed in the sphere. Since the original polyhedron has $19$ faces, the dual has $19$ vertices. Averaging arguments on spherical polygons show that one dual face has at most five sides. Translating this face back to the original polyhedron yields a vertex incident with at most five faces. The remainder of the proof is the same geometric estimate on the distance of that vertex from the center.

Another approach uses spherical geometry directly. The tangent points corresponding to faces through a vertex form a spherical polygon with at most five vertices. Such a polygon must contain two adjacent vertices whose spherical distance is at most $72^\circ$. The supporting tangent planes at those points intersect in a vertex whose distance from the center is at least $10/\cos36^\circ$. Taking the antipodal point on the sphere then gives a surface distance exceeding $21$. The main proof is preferable because it keeps the geometry in ordinary Euclidean space and avoids additional spherical trigonometry.
