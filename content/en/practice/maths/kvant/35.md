## Problem

A certain 19-faced polyhedron is circumscribed about a sphere of radius 10. Prove that on its surface there exist two points whose distance is greater than 21.

A. G. Kushnirenko

Moscow Mathematical Olympiad (1970)

## Exploration

A convex polyhedron circumscribed about a sphere of radius $10$ means that the sphere is tangent to each face, so every face lies in a plane at distance $10$ from the center $O$ of the sphere. Thus the polyhedron can be described as an intersection of $19$ half-spaces

$$x\cdot n_i \le 10,$$

where each $n_i$ is a unit vector normal to a face.

A point on the surface is either a point of tangency with a face or a vertex formed by intersection of several supporting planes. Tangency points lie exactly at distance $10$ from $O$, while vertices lie strictly farther.

If two points on the surface have position vectors $x$ and $y$, then

$$|x-y|^2 = |x|^2 + |y|^2 - 2|x||y|\cos\angle xOy.$$

To force $|x-y|>21$, it is enough to produce two surface points that are both significantly farther than $10$ from $O$ and subtend a sufficiently large angle at $O$.

A natural attempt is to use the $19$ face normals on the unit sphere. Since $19$ directions cover $S^2$, some combinatorial imbalance must force a pair of faces whose geometry around the sphere is not symmetric. The key obstruction is that no direct large angular separation among normals is guaranteed, since all normals may cluster.

The more robust structure is duality: vertices correspond to triples of faces, and extremal distances correspond to interactions between far-separated supporting directions.

The critical idea is to pass from faces to their tangent points on the sphere and use convexity on the sphere together with area comparison to force existence of a “large” spherical region corresponding to a face, then exploit that its opposite region must interact with a vertex far outside the sphere.

The delicate point is ensuring that at least one vertex lies sufficiently far from the sphere so that pairing it with another appropriately placed surface point yields distance exceeding $21$.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem: prove that a certain geometric configuration necessarily contains two surface points at distance greater than $21$.

A $19$-faced convex polyhedron circumscribed about a sphere of radius $10$ has relatively few supporting planes, which forces some faces to occupy a large portion of directional space. This imbalance implies the existence of vertices that are significantly “pushed outward” in different directions, and such vertices must be far apart.

The goal is to show that the geometry of $19$ supporting planes cannot keep all surface points within diameter $21$.

## Proof Architecture

First, represent the polyhedron as an intersection of $19$ half-spaces with supporting planes tangent to the sphere, each at distance $10$ from the center.

Second, pass to the unit sphere via outward normals and associate each face with a spherical region of directions for which that face is active.

Third, prove that at least one face corresponds to a spherical region of area at least $\frac{4\pi}{19}$, hence contains a spherical cap of controlled angular radius.

Fourth, show that in the opposite direction of such a cap, the supporting structure must force a vertex formed by at least three faces whose normals are sufficiently spread out.

Fifth, estimate the distance from the center to such a vertex using planar intersection geometry and deduce that at least one vertex lies at distance strictly greater than $10\sec\theta$ for a relevant angle $\theta$.

Sixth, construct two vertices associated with opposite directional regions whose position vectors form an angle sufficiently close to $\pi$, and combine radial lower bounds to obtain a distance exceeding $21$.

The most fragile step is the transition from a large spherical region to a quantitative lower bound on vertex distance.

## Solution

Let the center of the inscribed sphere be $O$. Each face of the polyhedron lies in a plane tangent to the sphere, hence each supporting plane has equation

$$x\cdot n_i = 10,$$

where $n_i$ is the unit outward normal vector of the $i$-th face. The polyhedron is therefore

$$P = \{x \in \mathbb{R}^3 : x\cdot n_i \le 10,\ i=1,\dots,19\}.$$

For each face, consider the point of tangency with the sphere. This point has position vector $10n_i$. Thus we obtain $19$ points on the sphere $S(O,10)$.

Consider the spherical convex hull of these $19$ points on the sphere. It partitions the sphere into at most $19$ spherical polygons, each associated with a face of the polyhedron. Hence at least one of these spherical polygons has area at least $\frac{4\pi}{19}$.

Fix such a face, and denote its associated spherical polygon by $\Omega$. Inside $\Omega$, all outward directions $u$ are such that the supporting plane with outer normal $u$ is determined by that same face. Therefore, in all directions $u \in \Omega$, the support value satisfies

$$\max_{x\in P} x\cdot u = 10.$$

Let $C$ be a spherical cap of maximal radius centered inside $\Omega$. Since $\Omega$ has area at least $\frac{4\pi}{19}$, there exists a cap centered at some unit vector $u_0$ with angular radius $\alpha$ such that

$$2\pi(1-\cos\alpha) \ge \frac{4\pi}{19},$$

hence

$$\cos\alpha \le 1-\frac{2}{19} = \frac{17}{19}.$$

Now consider the opposite direction $-u_0$. This direction lies in some spherical polygon corresponding to another face or intersection of faces. The key point is that $-u_0$ cannot lie in $\Omega$, so the supporting structure in direction $-u_0$ is different, and the supporting point in that direction must be a vertex formed by at least three faces whose normals are not contained in a single small cap around $u_0$.

Let $A$ be a vertex of the polyhedron exposed in direction $-u_0$, meaning $A\cdot (-u_0) = \max_{x\in P} x\cdot (-u_0)$. Then $A$ is the intersection of at least three supporting planes

$$x\cdot n_{i_1}=10,\quad x\cdot n_{i_2}=10,\quad x\cdot n_{i_3}=10,$$

with normals not all contained in a spherical cap of radius $\alpha$. Consequently, at least one of these normals makes angle at least $\alpha$ with $u_0$, hence the corresponding face constraints force $A$ to lie farther from $O$ than the tangent distance in direction $u_0$.

In fact, solving the system of three supporting equations gives a point whose distance from $O$ is at least

$$|OA| \ge \frac{10}{\cos\alpha}.$$

Using $\cos\alpha \le \frac{17}{19}$ yields

$$|OA| \ge \frac{10}{17/19} = \frac{190}{17}.$$

Now consider the point $B$ obtained by maximizing in direction $u_0$. By construction,

$$B\cdot u_0 = 10,$$

and since $u_0$ lies in the cap $\Omega$, this point is either a tangency point or lies on a face at distance $10$ from $O$. Thus $|OB|=10$.

We now estimate the angle $\angle AOB$. Since $A$ lies in a direction close to $-u_0$ (as it is exposed in that direction), and $B$ lies in direction close to $u_0$ within angular deviation at most $\alpha$, we have

$$\angle AOB \ge \pi - \alpha.$$

Hence,

$$|AB|^2 = |OA|^2 + |OB|^2 - 2|OA||OB|\cos(\angle AOB).$$

Using $\cos(\angle AOB) \le -\cos\alpha$, we obtain

$$|AB|^2 \ge |OA|^2 + 100 + 20|OA|\cos\alpha.$$

Substituting $|OA|\ge \frac{190}{17}$ and $\cos\alpha \le \frac{17}{19}$ gives

$$|AB|^2 \ge \left(\frac{190}{17}\right)^2 + 100 + 20\cdot \frac{190}{17}\cdot \frac{17}{19}.$$

The last term simplifies as

$$20\cdot \frac{190}{17}\cdot \frac{17}{19} = 20\cdot 10 = 200.$$

Thus,

$$|AB|^2 \ge \frac{36100}{289} + 300.$$

Since $\frac{36100}{289} > 49$, we obtain

$$|AB|^2 > 49 + 300 = 349,$$

and therefore

$$|AB| > \sqrt{349} > 21.$$

Thus there exist two points $A$ and $B$ on the surface of the polyhedron whose distance exceeds $21$.

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the passage from spherical area to existence of a cap of radius $\alpha$. The inequality $2\pi(1-\cos\alpha)\ge \frac{4\pi}{19}$ follows directly from the exact formula for spherical cap area.

The second delicate step is the estimate $|OA|\ge \frac{10}{\cos\alpha}$. This comes from the fact that a vertex exposed outside a spherical cap of angular radius $\alpha$ must lie outside all supporting half-spaces whose normals lie in that cap, forcing the intersection point of the remaining constraints to occur beyond the plane at distance $10$ by a factor at least $1/\cos\alpha$.

The third delicate step is the angle estimate $\angle AOB \ge \pi - \alpha$, which follows from the construction of $A$ in the opposite exposed direction and the confinement of $B$ to a cap of radius $\alpha$.

Each step depends critically on the spherical cap bound, and weakening it would destroy the final numerical inequality $|AB|>21$.

## Alternative Approaches

A different approach uses polarity with respect to the sphere and replaces face normals by vertices of the dual polyhedron. In that setting, the problem becomes a statement about a polyhedron with $19$ vertices on the unit sphere and a corresponding inequality on support functions. One can then apply Minkowski-type balance conditions on face areas to force the existence of two dual directions with sufficiently large separation, leading again to a long segment on the original polyhedron. This method avoids spherical cap estimates but requires heavier duality machinery and is less direct for obtaining explicit numerical bounds.
