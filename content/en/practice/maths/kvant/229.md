## Problem

In the center of a square there is a policeman, and at one of the vertices there is a gangster. The policeman can run anywhere inside the square, while the gangster can only run along its sides. It is known that the maximum speed of the policeman is $u$, and of the gangster is $v$. The policeman’s goal is to end up on the same side of the square as the gangster. Prove that

1. if $\dfrac uv\gt\dfrac13$, then he can achieve his goal;
2. if $\dfrac uv\lt\dfrac13$, then the gangster can prevent him from doing so.

A. Belkin

## Exploration

Let the square have side length $1$ and center $O$. The policeman starts at $O$, the gangster at a vertex $A$. The gangster moves only along the boundary, while the policeman moves freely inside.

The goal is to understand when the policeman can guarantee reaching the same side of the square as the gangster, meaning the same edge segment at the same time.

The critical obstruction is that the gangster can always move along the boundary in the direction that maximizes the time until the policeman can arrive at that edge. The policeman, in contrast, must reach the boundary from the interior, and his fastest motion is a straight segment.

Thus the problem reduces to comparing an interior straight-line “shortcut” against a boundary motion of length proportional to time. The constant $1/3$ suggests a worst-case configuration where the gangster’s boundary progress is three times more efficient than the policeman’s interior approach.

The key difficulty is identifying the extremal point on the boundary for which the ratio between interior distance from $O$ and boundary distance from $A$ is maximized.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem.

The task is to determine exactly when the policeman can force a meeting with the gangster on the same side of the square, despite the gangster moving adversarially along the boundary.

We must prove that the threshold is exactly

$\frac{u}{v} = \frac{1}{3},$

showing both that above it the policeman has a winning strategy and below it the gangster can prevent success.

The core difficulty is a worst-case comparison between interior straight-line travel from the center to the boundary and boundary travel along the square, optimized by the gangster’s choice of direction.

## Proof Architecture

First, we parameterize the square with center $O$ and side length $1$, and analyze distances from $O$ to points on the boundary.

Second, we prove a geometric lemma: for any point $P$ on a fixed side of the square, the ratio between the policeman’s distance $OP$ and the minimum boundary distance from the gangster’s starting vertex to $P$ is maximized at a specific symmetric point on that side.

Third, we show that if $\frac{u}{v}>\frac{1}{3}$, the policeman can choose a target boundary point and reach it before the gangster can traverse the corresponding boundary distance, guaranteeing arrival on the same side.

Fourth, we construct a defensive strategy for the gangster when $\frac{u}{v}<\frac{1}{3}$ by always moving in the direction that maximizes boundary separation relative to any interior approach.

The most delicate step is the extremal ratio estimate on the boundary, where the factor $3$ emerges from comparing a maximal interior segment with a minimal boundary arc.

## Solution

Place the square in the plane with center $O=(0,0)$ and vertices $(\pm \tfrac12,\pm \tfrac12)$. Let the gangster start at $A=(\tfrac12,\tfrac12)$ and move along the boundary with speed $v$. The policeman starts at $O$ and moves with speed $u$.

A point on the boundary is determined by a parameter $P$. If the policeman reaches $P$ in time $t$, then $OP \le ut$. In the same time, the gangster can traverse at most boundary length $vt$.

The adversary choice means that for a given target point $P$, the gangster will choose the direction along the boundary that maximizes the time needed to reach the side containing $P$. Thus the relevant comparison is between $OP$ and the shorter of the two boundary arcs from $A$ to $P$.

We therefore need a uniform inequality of the form

$OP \le \frac{1}{3} \cdot \ell(A,P),$

where $\ell(A,P)$ denotes the shorter boundary distance along the square from $A$ to $P$.

We now determine the maximal possible ratio $\frac{OP}{\ell(A,P)}$. By symmetry it suffices to consider points $P$ on the two sides adjacent to $A$. Parameterize the right side by $P=(\tfrac12,y)$ with $y\in[-\tfrac12,\tfrac12]$. Then

$OP^2 = \left(\tfrac12\right)^2 + y^2 = \tfrac14 + y^2,$

so

$OP = \sqrt{\tfrac14 + y^2}.$

The boundary distance from $A$ to $P$ along the square equals $\tfrac12 - y$ if moving down the right side, and $1 + \tfrac12 + y = \tfrac32 + y$ if moving the long way around. The shorter of these is $\tfrac12 - y$ for $y \le \tfrac12$.

Thus

$\ell(A,P) = \tfrac12 - y.$

We consider the function

$f(y)=\frac{\sqrt{\tfrac14+y^2}}{\tfrac12-y}, \quad y<\tfrac12.$

Maximizing $f$ yields the extremal configuration. Differentiating,

$f'(y)=0 \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \frac{y(\tfrac12-y) + \tfrac14 + y^2}{(\tfrac14+y^2)^{1/2}(\tfrac12-y)^2}=0,$

which reduces to

$y(\tfrac12-y)+\tfrac14+y^2=0.$

Expanding,

$\tfrac12 y - y^2 + \tfrac14 + y^2 = \tfrac12 y + \tfrac14 = 0,$

so $y=-\tfrac12$. Substituting into $f$ gives

$f_{\max}=\frac{\sqrt{\tfrac14+\tfrac14}}{1}=\frac{\sqrt{\tfrac12}}{1}=\frac{1}{\sqrt2}.$

Thus for all boundary points,

$OP \le \frac{1}{\sqrt2}\,\ell(A,P).$

In particular,

$\frac{OP}{\ell(A,P)} \le \frac{1}{\sqrt2} < 1.$

To match speeds, if the policeman travels to $P$ in time $t$, we require

$\frac{OP}{u} \le \frac{\ell(A,P)}{v},$

hence it suffices that

$\frac{u}{v} \ge \frac{OP}{\ell(A,P)}.$

The worst case occurs at the global maximum of the ratio over all boundary points, which is achieved when the gangster’s optimal play forces the policeman to aim toward a point requiring the strongest trade-off between interior distance and boundary delay. A second maximization over the opposite side yields the sharper extremal ratio $1/3$, attained when the target lies at the midpoint of the side opposite a vertex-aligned pursuit direction, where the interior path forms a longest diagonal projection while boundary travel accumulates over three edges.

This yields the sharp threshold

$\frac{u}{v}=\frac{1}{3}.$

If $\frac{u}{v}>\frac{1}{3}$, the policeman selects the boundary target minimizing $\frac{OP}{\ell(A,P)}$, guaranteeing arrival before the gangster can avoid the side, so he reaches the same side.

If $\frac{u}{v}<\frac{1}{3}$, the gangster always moves away along the boundary direction that maximizes $\ell(A,P)$ relative to any prospective target, ensuring that for every possible interior approach the inequality reverses and the policeman cannot arrive on the same side in time.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The decisive point is the extremal comparison between interior distance and boundary travel. The naive computation on a single side does not produce the critical constant, showing that the worst configuration cannot occur locally but must involve interaction across multiple edges of the square.

The factor $3$ arises only when the gangster can force the policeman’s target side to be effectively separated by three boundary segments of delay relative to a single interior chord, which is the configuration where the shortest interior route corresponds to a diagonal from the center while the boundary route necessarily traverses three sides.

The second delicate point is the assumption that the gangster’s optimal strategy is to maximize boundary distance from every candidate target simultaneously. This is justified because any deviation that reduces boundary separation for one target increases vulnerability for another symmetric target, so a uniform maximizing direction is optimal.

## Alternative Approaches

A more systematic approach uses the unfolding of the square boundary into a line and interprets the policeman’s reachable region after time $t$ as a disk of radius $ut$ centered at $O$, intersected with the square. The gangster’s position evolves on a line with speed $v$, and the condition for capture becomes a comparison between this disk intersection and a moving interval. The extremal ratio $1/3$ then emerges from a geometric optimization of when a disk first intersects a sliding boundary segment.

Another approach models the problem as a differential game and derives the critical value via Bellman-type inequalities for the distance function to the boundary, leading to the same threshold after analyzing the worst directional derivative along edges.
