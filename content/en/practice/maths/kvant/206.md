## Problem

Given an infinite sequence of digits. Prove that for any natural number $n$ coprime with the number 10, one can find in the sequence a block of consecutive digits such that the number written by these digits is divisible by $n$.

V. A. Ufnarovsky

## Exploration

Let the digits of the infinite sequence be $a_1, a_2, a_3, \dots$. A natural object to attach to the first $k$ digits is the integer

$$S_k = a_1 10^{k-1} + a_2 10^{k-2} + \cdots + a_k.$$

A block from position $i+1$ to $j$ corresponds to the number

$$N_{i,j} = a_{i+1}10^{j-i-1} + \cdots + a_j.$$

This is related to prefixes by

$$S_j = S_i \cdot 10^{j-i} + N_{i,j}.$$

Hence

$$N_{i,j} = S_j - S_i \cdot 10^{j-i}.$$

We want $N_{i,j} \equiv 0 \pmod n$. Rearranging gives

$$S_j \equiv S_i \cdot 10^{j-i} \pmod n.$$

Because $\gcd(n,10)=1$, powers of $10$ are invertible modulo $n$. This suggests normalizing prefixes by multiplying by $10^{-k}$ modulo $n$, turning the recurrence into something where equality of normalized values forces a divisible block.

The key obstruction to check is whether this normalization is well-defined and whether it truly converts a substring condition into equality in a finite set, allowing a pigeonhole argument.

## Problem Understanding

The problem asks to show that in any infinite digit sequence and for any integer $n$ coprime with $10$, there exists a finite consecutive block of digits whose numeric value is divisible by $n$.

This is a Type A classification problem. One must prove existence for every $n$ and show that the constructed block indeed works.

The core difficulty is converting a condition on a substring, which involves both subtraction and shifting by powers of $10$, into a condition about equality in a finite modular system so that repetition forces a solution.

The expected mechanism is a pigeonhole argument in a finite residue space derived from prefix sums adjusted by powers of $10$.

## Proof Architecture

First, define prefix values $S_k$ for the digit sequence.

Second, prove the identity $N_{i,j} = S_j - S_i 10^{j-i}$ relating substrings to prefixes.

Third, introduce normalized residues $T_k \equiv S_k \cdot 10^{-k} \pmod n$, using the existence of modular inverses since $\gcd(n,10)=1$.

Fourth, prove that equality $T_i = T_j$ implies $N_{i,j} \equiv 0 \pmod n$.

Fifth, apply the pigeonhole principle to the infinite sequence $(T_k)$ taking values in a finite set of size $n$.

The most delicate step is the correctness of normalization and its conversion back into a substring divisibility statement.

## Solution

Let the digits of the sequence be $a_1, a_2, a_3, \dots$. For each $k \ge 1$, define

$$S_k = \sum_{t=1}^k a_t 10^{k-t}.$$

This is the integer formed by the first $k$ digits.

For indices $1 \le i < j$, the block of digits from $i+1$ to $j$ forms the number

$$N_{i,j} = \sum_{t=i+1}^j a_t 10^{j-t}.$$

Expanding $S_j$ and separating terms up to $i$ gives

$$S_j = \sum_{t=1}^i a_t 10^{j-t} + \sum_{t=i+1}^j a_t 10^{j-t}.$$

The first sum equals $S_i \cdot 10^{j-i}$, hence

$$S_j = S_i \cdot 10^{j-i} + N_{i,j},$$

and therefore

$$N_{i,j} = S_j - S_i \cdot 10^{j-i}.$$

Assume $\gcd(n,10)=1$. Then there exists an integer $10^{-1}$ such that $10 \cdot 10^{-1} \equiv 1 \pmod n$, and consequently $10^{-k}$ is defined modulo $n$ for all $k \ge 1$.

Define

$$T_k \equiv S_k \cdot 10^{-k} \pmod n.$$

Take indices $i<j$ such that $T_i \equiv T_j \pmod n$. Then

$$S_i \cdot 10^{-i} \equiv S_j \cdot 10^{-j} \pmod n.$$

Multiplying both sides by $10^j$ yields

$$S_i \cdot 10^{j-i} \equiv S_j \pmod n.$$

Substituting this into the identity for $N_{i,j}$ gives

$$N_{i,j} = S_j - S_i \cdot 10^{j-i} \equiv 0 \pmod n.$$

Thus the block from $i+1$ to $j$ forms a number divisible by $n$.

Each $T_k$ takes values in the finite set of residues modulo $n$, so among the infinite sequence $T_1, T_2, T_3, \dots$, two indices $i<j$ exist with $T_i \equiv T_j \pmod n$. This produces a required block.

Hence for every $n$ coprime with $10$, a consecutive block of digits divisible by $n$ exists.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate point is the relation between prefixes and substrings. Writing $S_j$ as a sum over digits and factoring out $10^{j-i}$ from the first $i$ terms gives $S_i 10^{j-i}$ exactly, with no hidden carry issues since the expression is purely algebraic in integers.

The second point is the use of modular inverses. The assumption $\gcd(n,10)=1$ guarantees existence of an inverse of $10$ modulo $n$, and exponentiation preserves invertibility, so $10^{-k}$ is well-defined in $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$.

The third point is the implication from equality of $T_i$ and $T_j$ to divisibility of $N_{i,j}$. Multiplying the congruence $S_i 10^{-i} \equiv S_j 10^{-j}$ by $10^j$ is valid in modular arithmetic and yields exactly the needed relation substituted into the identity for $N_{i,j}$.

Finally, the pigeonhole argument uses that the sequence $(T_k)$ lies in a set of size $n$, so two values must coincide.

## Alternative Approaches

An alternative method avoids normalization and instead considers pairs $(S_k, k)$ modulo $n$ with a weighted relation encoded in a finite group structure combining residues and powers of $10$. One can also work directly with differences $S_j 10^{-j} - S_i 10^{-i}$ interpreted in the multiplicative group $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$.

The normalization method is preferable because it reduces the problem to a direct application of the pigeonhole principle in a single finite residue class, minimizing structural overhead and making the substring extraction identity explicit.
