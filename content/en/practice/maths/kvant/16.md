## Problem

Prove that a polynomial $p(x)$ with integer coefficients that takes the value 1 at three distinct integer values of $x$ cannot have any integer root.

## Exploration

Let the three distinct integers at which the polynomial takes the value $1$ be $a$, $b$, and $c$. Then the polynomial

$$q(x)=p(x)-1$$

has integer coefficients and vanishes at $a$, $b$, and $c$. Hence

$$q(x)=(x-a)(x-b)(x-c)r(x)$$

for some polynomial $r(x)$ with integer coefficients.

Suppose, seeking a contradiction, that $p$ has an integer root $m$. Then

$$q(m)=p(m)-1=-1.$$

Substituting $x=m$ into the factorization gives

$$(m-a)(m-b)(m-c)\,r(m)=-1.$$

Every factor is an integer. Since the product equals $-1$, each factor must be $\pm1$.

The crucial point is whether three distinct integers $a,b,c$ can all lie at distance $1$ from the same integer $m$. They cannot. The only integers satisfying $|m-t|=1$ are $m-1$ and $m+1$, so there are at most two such integers. This contradiction should finish the proof.

The step most likely to hide an error is the deduction from the product being $-1$ to the conclusion that each factor is $\pm1$. This is valid because all factors are integers.

## Problem Understanding

We are given a polynomial $p(x)$ with integer coefficients. There exist three distinct integers $a$, $b$, and $c$ such that

$$p(a)=p(b)=p(c)=1.$$

We must prove that $p(x)$ cannot have an integer root.

This is a Type B problem, a pure proof.

The core difficulty is to combine the three values equal to $1$ with the assumption of an integer root and derive an arithmetic contradiction.

## Proof Architecture

First claim: If $p(a)=p(b)=p(c)=1$ for distinct integers $a,b,c$, then $p(x)-1$ is divisible by $(x-a)(x-b)(x-c)$. This follows from the factor theorem applied three times.

Second claim: If $m$ is an integer root of $p$, then

$$(m-a)(m-b)(m-c)r(m)=-1$$

for some integer $r(m)$. This comes from evaluating the factorization of $p(x)-1$ at $x=m$.

Third claim: If a product of integers equals $-1$, then every factor is $\pm1$. The only divisors of $-1$ are $\pm1$.

Fourth claim: There do not exist three distinct integers all satisfying $|m-t|=1$. The only such integers are $m-1$ and $m+1$.

The hardest step is the final contradiction, where the divisibility information forces all three differences $m-a$, $m-b$, and $m-c$ to be $\pm1$, which is impossible for three distinct integers.

## Solution

Let the three distinct integers for which $p(x)=1$ be $a$, $b$, and $c$.

Consider the polynomial

$$q(x)=p(x)-1.$$

Since $q(a)=q(b)=q(c)=0$, the factor theorem gives

$$q(x)=(x-a)(x-b)(x-c)r(x),$$

where $r(x)$ is a polynomial with integer coefficients.

Assume, for contradiction, that $p$ has an integer root $m$. Then

$$p(m)=0,$$

hence

$$q(m)=p(m)-1=-1.$$

Evaluating the factorization of $q$ at $x=m$, we obtain

$$(m-a)(m-b)(m-c)r(m)=-1.$$

Because $m,a,b,c$ are integers and $r(x)$ has integer coefficients, the number $r(m)$ is an integer. Thus the left-hand side is a product of integers equal to $-1$.

A product of integers can equal $-1$ only if every factor is equal to $1$ or $-1$. Consequently,

$$m-a=\pm1,\qquad m-b=\pm1,\qquad m-c=\pm1.$$

Therefore

$$a,b,c\in\{m-1,m+1\}.$$

The set ${m-1,m+1}$ contains only two integers, whereas $a$, $b$, and $c$ are distinct. This is impossible.

The contradiction shows that our assumption that $p$ has an integer root is false. Hence $p(x)$ cannot have any integer root.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The factorization step uses the factor theorem exactly. Since

$$q(a)=q(b)=q(c)=0$$

and $a,b,c$ are distinct, the linear factors $(x-a)$, $(x-b)$, and $(x-c)$ are distinct and their product divides $q(x)$. Because $q(x)$ has integer coefficients and the divisor is monic, the quotient $r(x)$ also has integer coefficients.

The equality

$$(m-a)(m-b)(m-c)r(m)=-1$$

comes from substituting $x=m$ into the factorization. A careless argument might forget to justify that $r(m)$ is an integer. This follows because $r(x)$ has integer coefficients and $m$ is an integer.

From

$$(m-a)(m-b)(m-c)r(m)=-1$$

one must conclude that each factor is $\pm1$. The reason is arithmetic: every factor is an integer, and the only integer divisors of $-1$ are $1$ and $-1$. If one skipped this justification, the contradiction would not be fully established.

## Alternative Approaches

Starting from

$$p(x)-1=(x-a)(x-b)(x-c)r(x),$$

assume $p(m)=0$. Then

$$(m-a)(m-b)(m-c)\mid p(m)-1=-1.$$

Hence

$$|(m-a)(m-b)(m-c)|=1.$$

Since the factors are integers, each of $m-a$, $m-b$, and $m-c$ must be $\pm1$. This again forces $a$, $b$, and $c$ to belong to the two-element set ${m-1,m+1}$, a contradiction.

This approach is essentially the same idea expressed through divisibility rather than through the full product equation. The main proof is preferable because it makes every integer factor explicit and leaves no hidden divisibility step.
