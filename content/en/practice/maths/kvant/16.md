## Problem

Prove that a polynomial $p(x)$ with integer coefficients that takes the value 1 at three distinct integer values of $x$ cannot have any integer roots.

## Exploration

Let the three distinct integers at which the polynomial takes the value $1$ be $a$, $b$, and $c$. Then the polynomial

$$q(x)=p(x)-1$$

has integer coefficients and satisfies

$$q(a)=q(b)=q(c)=0.$$

Hence

$$q(x)=(x-a)(x-b)(x-c)r(x)$$

for some polynomial $r(x)$ with integer coefficients.

Suppose, seeking a contradiction, that $p$ has an integer root $m$. Then

$$q(m)=p(m)-1=-1.$$

Substituting $x=m$ into the factorization gives

$$(m-a)(m-b)(m-c)r(m)=-1.$$

Since all factors are integers, their product is $-1$. The only integer divisors of $-1$ are $\pm1$. Thus each factor $(m-a)$, $(m-b)$, and $(m-c)$ must be $\pm1$.

The crucial point is whether three distinct integers can all lie at distance $1$ from a fixed integer $m$. The only integers at distance $1$ from $m$ are $m-1$ and $m+1$, so there are at most two such integers. Since $a$, $b$, and $c$ are distinct, this is impossible.

That contradiction appears to settle the problem. The step most likely to hide an error is the deduction that each factor must be $\pm1$ from the fact that their product times another integer equals $-1$. This must be justified using the fact that every factor is an integer and the only integer factorization of $-1$ consists entirely of $\pm1$ factors.

## Problem Understanding

We are given a polynomial $p(x)$ with integer coefficients. There exist three distinct integers $a$, $b$, and $c$ such that

$$p(a)=p(b)=p(c)=1.$$

The task is to prove that $p(x)$ cannot have any integer root.

This is a Type B problem. The statement is given, and we must prove it.

The core difficulty is to combine the information about the three values equal to $1$ with the hypothetical existence of an integer root. The natural object is $p(x)-1$, which vanishes at the three specified integers and therefore contains the factor $(x-a)(x-b)(x-c)$.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma is that if $p(a)=p(b)=p(c)=1$ for distinct integers $a,b,c$, then $p(x)-1$ is divisible by $(x-a)(x-b)(x-c)$. This follows from the factor theorem applied three times.

The second lemma is that if an integer product equals $-1$, then every factor in that product is equal to $\pm1$. This follows because the only integer divisors of $-1$ are $\pm1$.

The final step is to assume an integer root $m$ of $p$, substitute $x=m$ into the factorization of $p(x)-1$, obtain a product equal to $-1$, deduce that $m-a$, $m-b$, and $m-c$ are all $\pm1$, and derive a contradiction because there are only two integers at distance $1$ from $m$.

The lemma most likely to fail under scrutiny is the second one, since the contradiction depends on showing that each difference $m-a$, $m-b$, and $m-c$ must be exactly $\pm1$.

## Solution

Assume that $p(x)$ is a polynomial with integer coefficients and that

$$p(a)=p(b)=p(c)=1$$

for three distinct integers $a$, $b$, and $c$.

Define

$$q(x)=p(x)-1.$$

Then $q(x)$ also has integer coefficients, and

$$q(a)=q(b)=q(c)=0.$$

Since $a$, $b$, and $c$ are roots of $q(x)$, the factor theorem gives

$$q(x)=(x-a)(x-b)(x-c)r(x),$$

where $r(x)$ is a polynomial with integer coefficients.

Suppose, for contradiction, that $p(x)$ has an integer root $m$. Then

$$p(m)=0,$$

so

$$q(m)=p(m)-1=-1.$$

Substituting $x=m$ into the factorization of $q(x)$ yields

$$(m-a)(m-b)(m-c)r(m)=-1.$$

Because $m,a,b,c$ are integers and $r(x)$ has integer coefficients, the numbers

$$m-a,\quad m-b,\quad m-c,\quad r(m)$$

are all integers.

Their product equals $-1$. Every integer divisor of $-1$ is equal to $\pm1$. Consequently,

$$m-a,\quad m-b,\quad m-c$$

must each be equal to $\pm1$.

Thus each of $a$, $b$, and $c$ is one of the two integers

$$m-1 \quad \text{or} \quad m+1.$$

There are only two such integers, yet $a$, $b$, and $c$ are distinct. This contradiction shows that no integer root $m$ can exist.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the factorization of $q(x)$. Since $q(a)=0$, the factor theorem gives $(x-a)\mid q(x)$. Dividing by $(x-a)$ produces a polynomial with integer coefficients because $q(x)$ has integer coefficients and $a$ is an integer. The resulting polynomial still vanishes at $b$ and $c$, so the same argument gives factors $(x-b)$ and $(x-c)$. Hence $(x-a)(x-b)(x-c)$ divides $q(x)$.

The second delicate step is the deduction from

$$(m-a)(m-b)(m-c)r(m)=-1.$$

Every factor is an integer. If one factor had absolute value at least $2$, then the absolute value of the entire product would be at least $2$. Since the product has absolute value $1$, every factor must have absolute value $1$. Therefore each factor equals $\pm1$.

The final delicate step is the contradiction. From

$$m-a=\pm1,$$

we obtain

$$a=m-1 \quad \text{or} \quad a=m+1.$$

The same holds for $b$ and $c$. The set ${m-1,m+1}$ contains exactly two integers, so it cannot contain three distinct integers $a,b,c$.

## Alternative Approaches

A slightly different proof begins with the identity

$$p(x)-1=(x-a)(x-b)(x-c)r(x).$$

If $m$ were an integer root of $p$, then

$$-1=(m-a)(m-b)(m-c)r(m).$$

Reducing to absolute values gives

$$1=|m-a|\,|m-b|\,|m-c|\,|r(m)|.$$

Since all factors are nonnegative integers, each factor must equal $1$. Hence

$$|m-a|=|m-b|=|m-c|=1.$$

The contradiction is immediate because only two integers are at distance $1$ from $m$.

The main approach is preferable because it keeps the argument entirely in the ring of integers and isolates the decisive fact that a product of integers equal to $-1$ forces every factor to be $\pm1$.
