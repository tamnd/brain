## Problem

Find all solutions in natural numbers of the equation $$n^x+n^y=n^z.$$

R. Egoryan, a 9th-grade student

## Exploration

Assume $n^x + n^y = n^z$ with $x,y,z \in \mathbb{N}$ and $n \in \mathbb{N}$. If $n=1$, then the left-hand side equals $2$, while the right-hand side equals $1$, so no solutions exist.

Assume $n \ge 2$. Let $x \le y$. Then

$$n^x + n^y = n^x(1 + n^{y-x}).$$

For the expression to be a pure power of $n$, the factor $1 + n^{y-x}$ must itself be a power of $n$. Setting $k = y-x \ge 0$ and $1 + n^k = n^m$ reduces the problem to analyzing when a number of the form $1 + n^k$ is a power of $n$.

Testing small values suggests that $k=0$ may work, while $k \ge 1$ quickly produces numbers strictly between consecutive powers of $n$, except possibly for very small bases such as $n=2$.

The critical step is to rule out all solutions of $1 + n^k = n^m$ for $k \ge 1$ and $n \ge 2$.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem. The goal is to determine all natural number solutions of the exponential Diophantine equation

$$n^x + n^y = n^z.$$

After normalization by factoring out the smaller power, the structure forces a condition of the form $1 + n^k = n^m$. The key difficulty is proving that this equation has no solutions for $k \ge 1$ and $n \ge 2$, except in trivial boundary cases.

The solution set will consist of the family where $n=2$ and $x=y$, producing $z=x+1$.

## Proof Architecture

First, reorder variables so that $x \le y$ and reduce the equation to $1 + n^{y-x} = n^{z-x}$.

Second, analyze the auxiliary equation $1 + n^k = n^m$ for $k \ge 0$ and $m \ge 0$.

Third, treat separately the case $k=0$, which forces $n=2$ and determines all corresponding exponents.

Fourth, treat $k \ge 1$ and prove that no solutions exist by comparing the position of $1 + n^k$ relative to consecutive powers $n^k$ and $n^{k+1}$.

The most delicate step is excluding hidden solutions for small bases, particularly $n=2$, where spacing between powers is minimal.

## Solution

If $n=1$, then $n^x + n^y = 2$ while $n^z = 1$, which contradicts the equation, so no solutions arise.

Assume $n \ge 2$. Without loss of generality, let $x \le y$. Then

$$n^x + n^y = n^x(1 + n^{y-x}).$$

For this to equal $n^z$, we obtain

$$1 + n^{y-x} = n^{z-x}.$$

Set $k = y-x$ and $m = z-x$, giving

$$1 + n^k = n^m.$$

Consider first $k=0$. Then

$$1 + 1 = n^m,$$

so $2 = n^m$. Since $n \ge 2$, this forces $n=2$ and $m=1$. Hence $z-x=1$, so $z=x+1$, and $x=y$.

Now assume $k \ge 1$. Then

$$n^k < 1 + n^k < n^k + n^k = 2n^k.$$

For $n \ge 2$, one has $2n^k \le n^{k+1}$ if and only if $2 \le n$, which holds. Hence

$$n^k < 1 + n^k \le n^{k+1}.$$

If $1 + n^k = n^m$, then $m$ must satisfy $m \in {k, k+1}$ or lie strictly between consecutive powers, which is impossible.

The equality $m=k$ gives $n^k = 1 + n^k$, which is impossible. The equality $m=k+1$ gives

$$n^{k+1} = 1 + n^k,$$

which rewrites as

$$n^k(n-1) = 1.$$

Since $n \ge 2$ and $k \ge 1$, the left-hand side is at least $2$, contradicting the equation.

Thus no solutions exist for $k \ge 1$.

Combining both cases yields the full solution family:

$$n=2,\quad x=y,\quad z=x+1.$$

Hence the set of all solutions is

$$\boxed{(n,x,y,z) \text{ such that } n=2,\ x=y,\ z=x+1}.$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The reduction step assumes factoring $n^x$ from both terms, which is valid for all $n \ge 1$ and $x \le y$. The transformed equation preserves equivalence because no division by zero occurs and exponent rules hold in natural numbers.

In the case $k \ge 1$, the exclusion of $m=k+1$ relies on solving $n^{k+1} = 1 + n^k$. Rearranging gives $n^k(n-1)=1$, which forces both factors to be $1$. This contradicts $n \ge 2$ and $k \ge 1$, since $n^k \ge 2$.

Boundary checking for $n=2$ confirms no hidden solution exists for $k \ge 1$: explicit substitution gives $1+2^k$, which is odd and strictly between $2^k$ and $2^{k+1}$.

## Alternative Approaches

A different method uses $n$-adic valuation considerations. Writing the equation as $n^x(1+n^{y-x})=n^z$ shows that $1+n^{y-x}$ must be divisible by $n$, forcing $n \mid 1$, which is impossible unless $y=x$. This immediately reduces the problem to $2n^x=n^z$, giving $n=2$ and $z=x+1$ as before.
