## Problem

Find the necessary and sufficient conditions that the numbers $a$, $b$, $\alpha$, $\beta$ must satisfy so that the rectangle $a\times b$ can be cut into several rectangles $\alpha\times\beta$.

For example, is it possible to cut the rectangle $50\times 60$ into rectangles of sizes:

1. $20\times 15$;
2. $5\times 8$;
3. $6{,}25\times 15$;
4. $(2-\sqrt{2})\times (2+\sqrt{2})$.

A. T. Kolotov

## Exploration

The rectangles of size $\alpha\times\beta$ may be used in either orientation. Thus the problem is equivalent to tiling the rectangle $[0,a]\times[0,b]$ by congruent rectangles whose sides are $\alpha$ and $\beta$.

A necessary condition comes from area:

$ab=N\alpha\beta$

for some positive integer $N$.

This condition is far from sufficient. A $1\times 1$ square cannot be tiled by $1\times\sqrt2$ rectangles because the areas are incompatible, but even when areas are compatible there are obstructions. For instance, a $1\times 2$ rectangle has area $2$, equal to the area of a $(1+\sqrt2)\times(2-\sqrt2)$ rectangle, yet a tiling is impossible.

The geometry suggests looking at all vertical and horizontal cutting lines produced by the tiling. If $\alpha/\beta$ is rational, after multiplying all lengths by a common denominator we reduce the problem to tiling a rectangle with congruent integer-sided rectangles. Then the obvious divisibility conditions should be sufficient.

The interesting case is $\alpha/\beta$ irrational. Suppose a vertical side of a tile has length $\alpha$. A horizontal side has length $\beta$. Every horizontal coordinate occurring in the tiling is obtained by adding lengths $\alpha$ and $\beta$ repeatedly. Hence every such coordinate belongs to the additive group

$G=\{m\alpha+n\beta:m,n\in\mathbb Z\}.$

If $\alpha/\beta$ is irrational, $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are linearly independent over $\mathbb Q$. Then each element of $G$ has a unique representation $m\alpha+n\beta$.

A classical argument of Dehn assigns to $x=m\alpha+n\beta$ the coefficient $m$. This additive functional vanishes on lengths that are integer multiples of $\beta$ and equals $1$ on $\alpha$. Extending it bilinearly to rectangle areas gives an invariant. For a tile $\alpha\times\beta$ the invariant is $1\cdot0=0$. Hence the large rectangle must also have invariant $0$.

Writing

$a=m_1\alpha+n_1\beta,\qquad b=m_2\alpha+n_2\beta,$

the invariant of the large rectangle becomes $m_1m_2$. Thus $m_1m_2=0$. Interchanging the roles of $\alpha$ and $\beta$ yields $n_1n_2=0$.

Therefore one of $a,b$ is an integer combination involving only $\beta$, and one of $a,b$ is an integer combination involving only $\alpha$. Since lengths are positive, one side is an integer multiple of $\alpha$ and the other an integer multiple of $\beta$, or vice versa.

This looks both necessary and sufficient. The delicate point is proving rigorously that every side length of the large rectangle belongs to $G$ and that the Dehn-type invariant is additive under dissections.

## Problem Understanding

We must determine exactly for which positive numbers $a,b,\alpha,\beta$ a rectangle of dimensions $a\times b$ can be dissected into finitely many rectangles of dimensions $\alpha\times\beta$, where individual tiles may be rotated.

This is a Type A problem. We must classify all quadruples $(a,b,\alpha,\beta)$ for which such a dissection exists and prove both necessity and sufficiency.

The core difficulty lies in the irrational case. When $\alpha/\beta$ is rational, the problem reduces to an arithmetic divisibility question. When $\alpha/\beta$ is irrational, area considerations are inadequate, and an additional invariant is needed.

The answer is as follows.

If $\alpha/\beta$ is rational, a tiling exists if and only if

$\frac ad,\qquad \frac bd$

are positive integers for

$d=\gcd(\alpha,\beta),$

equivalently, $a$ and $b$ belong to the lattice generated by $\alpha$ and $\beta$, and

$ab=N\alpha\beta$

for some positive integer $N$.

If $\alpha/\beta$ is irrational, a tiling exists if and only if one of the pairs

$(a,b)=(m\alpha,n\beta),\qquad (a,b)=(m\beta,n\alpha)$

holds with positive integers $m,n$.

Intuitively, irrational side lengths create a rigid two-dimensional arithmetic structure. A Dehn-type invariant forces the sides of the large rectangle to align separately with the $\alpha$ and $\beta$ directions.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma states that every side length appearing in the subdivision belongs to the additive group $G={m\alpha+n\beta:m,n\in\mathbb Z}$; this follows because all coordinates are obtained from $0$ by successive additions of tile side lengths.

The second lemma states that when $\alpha/\beta$ is irrational, every element of $G$ has a unique representation $m\alpha+n\beta$; this follows from linear independence over $\mathbb Q$.

The third lemma constructs an additive functional $\varphi:G\to\mathbb Z$ by $\varphi(m\alpha+n\beta)=m$ and shows that the rectangle invariant $D(x,y)=\varphi(x)\varphi(y)$ is additive under dissections into rectangles with side lengths in $G$.

The fourth lemma states that every tile $\alpha\times\beta$ has invariant $0$.

The fifth lemma derives the necessary conditions in the irrational case by applying the invariant twice, once with $\varphi(\alpha)=1,\varphi(\beta)=0$ and once with the roles reversed.

The final step proves sufficiency. In the irrational case the required integer multiples yield an obvious grid tiling. In the rational case all lengths are integer multiples of a common unit, and the problem reduces to tiling an integer rectangle by congruent integer rectangles.

The hardest direction is necessity for irrational $\alpha/\beta$. The lemma most susceptible to error is the additivity of the invariant under arbitrary rectangular dissections.

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate point is the claim that $a,b\in G$. Every tile corner has coordinates obtained from the origin by traversing a path along tile edges. Each step changes one coordinate by $\pm\alpha$ or $\pm\beta$. Consequently every coordinate is an integer linear combination of $\alpha$ and $\beta$. The right side of the large rectangle has coordinate $a$, and the top side has coordinate $b$, so both belong to $G$. A careless argument might only establish this for tile corners and forget to check that the boundary coordinates of the large rectangle are among them.

The second delicate point is additivity of the invariant. For a single vertical cut, bilinearity of $\varphi$ gives the required identity directly. Every finite rectangular subdivision can be generated recursively by cuts because the dual graph of a rectangular partition always contains a boundary rectangle whose removal leaves a smaller rectangular partition. Repeated application of the one-cut identity yields full additivity. The error to avoid is assuming additivity for arbitrary dissections without reducing them to elementary cuts.

## Alternative Approaches

The irrational case can be formulated in the language of Hamel bases. Choose a $\mathbb Q$-linear functional sending $\alpha$ to $1$ and $\beta$ to $0$. Extending this functional bilinearly to rectangles produces a Dehn invariant. The proof then becomes a special case of the classical theory of equidissections. The present argument avoids introducing vector-space machinery beyond the two-dimensional space generated by $\alpha$ and $\beta$.

Another approach uses electrical-network style weights. Assign weights to vertical segments according to the coefficient of $\alpha$ in their length. Summing weighted areas over the subdivision yields the same invariant implicitly. The direct additive-functional method is preferable because the necessity and sufficiency conditions emerge transparently from the coefficients of $\alpha$ and $\beta$ in the side lengths.
