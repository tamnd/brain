## Problem

Prove that if for the numbers $p_1$, $p_2$, $q_1$, $q_2$ the inequality $$ (q_1-q_2)^2+(p_1-p_2)(p_1q_2-p_2q_1)\lt0, $$ holds, then the quadratic trinomials $$ x^2+p_1x+q_1\quad\text{и}\quad x^2+p_2x+q_2 $$ have real roots, and between the two roots of each of them there lies a root of the other.

I. F. Sharygin

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad for School Students (V)

## Exploration

Let

$f_1(x)=x^2+p_1x+q_1$, $f_2(x)=x^2+p_2x+q_2$.

The given inequality is symmetric in $(p_1,q_1)$ and $(p_2,q_2)$, suggesting a relation stronger than mere positivity of discriminants, likely an interlacing criterion.

A standard mechanism for interlacing of zeros is monotonicity of the ratio $f_1/f_2$. If this ratio has no critical points, then it is monotone on each interval where it is defined, and sign changes must alternate across poles (zeros of $f_2$). This naturally produces the “between roots” property.

The expression

$$(q_1-q_2)^2 + (p_1-p_2)(p_1q_2-p_2q_1)$$

resembles a discriminant. This suggests computing the derivative of $f_1/f_2$ and examining when its numerator has real roots.

The key step is therefore to compute $(f_1/f_2)'$ and interpret the given inequality as negativity of a discriminant, forcing strict monotonicity.


## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem.

We are given a condition on two quadratic polynomials $f_1$ and $f_2$. We must prove two facts: both quadratics have real roots, and their roots interlace, meaning between the two roots of each quadratic lies a root of the other.

The core difficulty is to convert the algebraic inequality in coefficients into a structural statement about the mutual arrangement of roots. The correct perspective is that the inequality controls the derivative of the ratio $f_1/f_2$, forcing strict monotonicity, which in turn enforces interlacing and existence of real roots.


## Proof Architecture

First, the derivative of the ratio $\frac{f_1}{f_2}$ is computed explicitly, yielding a rational function whose numerator is a quadratic polynomial $N(x)$.

Second, the discriminant of $N(x)$ is expressed exactly as

$$4\Bigl((q_1-q_2)^2 + (p_1-p_2)(p_1q_2-p_2q_1)\Bigr),$$

so the hypothesis implies $N(x)$ has no real roots and hence does not change sign.

Third, strict sign definiteness of $N(x)$ implies $\frac{f_1}{f_2}$ is strictly monotone on every interval where defined.

Fourth, strict monotonicity forces both $f_1$ and $f_2$ to have two real roots, since otherwise the ratio could not exhibit the necessary sign transitions across vertical asymptotes.

Fifth, monotonicity forces alternation of zeros: between consecutive roots of one polynomial, the other must change sign exactly once.

The most delicate point is the passage from monotonicity of $\frac{f_1}{f_2}$ to existence of real roots of both quadratics, since it requires careful control of sign behavior near asymptotes.


## Solution

Let

$$f_1(x)=x^2+p_1x+q_1,\qquad f_2(x)=x^2+p_2x+q_2.$$

Consider the rational function

$$\varphi(x)=\frac{f_1(x)}{f_2(x)}.$$

We compute its derivative:

$$\varphi'(x)=\frac{f_1'(x)f_2(x)-f_1(x)f_2'(x)}{f_2(x)^2}.$$

Since

$$f_1'(x)=2x+p_1,\qquad f_2'(x)=2x+p_2,$$

the numerator equals

$$N(x)=(2x+p_1)(x^2+p_2x+q_2)-(2x+p_2)(x^2+p_1x+q_1).$$

Expanding both products and cancelling cubic terms yields

$$N(x)=(p_2-p_1)x^2+2(q_2-q_1)x+(p_1q_2-p_2q_1).$$

The discriminant of this quadratic is

$$\Delta_N = 4(q_2-q_1)^2 - 4(p_2-p_1)(p_1q_2-p_2q_1),$$

hence

$$\Delta_N = 4\Bigl((q_1-q_2)^2 + (p_1-p_2)(p_1q_2-p_2q_1)\Bigr).$$

By the hypothesis,

$$\Delta_N<0.$$

Therefore $N(x)$ has no real roots and does not change sign on $\mathbb{R}$. Consequently $\varphi'(x)$ has constant sign on every interval where it is defined, so $\varphi$ is strictly monotone on each connected component of its domain.

The function $\varphi$ is undefined exactly at the zeros of $f_2$. On any interval not containing such a zero, $\varphi$ is continuous and strictly monotone.

We show that $f_2$ must have two real zeros. If $f_2$ had no real zero, then it would keep constant sign on $\mathbb{R}$, and $\varphi$ would be continuous and strictly monotone on all of $\mathbb{R}$. Since

$$\lim_{x\to\pm\infty}\varphi(x)=1,$$

a strictly monotone function on $\mathbb{R}$ cannot have equal limits at both infinities unless it is constant, which is impossible because the numerator of $\varphi'$ is not identically zero. Hence $f_2$ has at least one real zero.

If $f_2$ had only one real zero, then $\varphi$ would have a single vertical asymptote. On the two sides of this asymptote, strict monotonicity forces the limits of $\varphi$ to be either both $+\infty$ or both $-\infty$, contradicting the fact that $\lim_{x\to-\infty}\varphi(x)=\lim_{x\to+\infty}\varphi(x)=1$. Hence $f_2$ has two distinct real zeros.

The same argument with roles of $f_1$ and $f_2$ exchanged shows that $f_1$ also has two distinct real zeros.

Let the zeros of $f_2$ be $c<d$. On $(-\infty,c)$, $(c,d)$, and $(d,\infty)$, the function $\varphi$ is strictly monotone. Near $c$ and $d$, the sign of $\varphi$ must change between $+\infty$ and $-\infty$ on opposite sides of each asymptote, since the monotone branches cannot reconnect values without crossing zero.

Thus each interval $(-\infty,c)$, $(c,d)$, $(d,\infty)$ contains exactly one zero of $f_1$ or exactly one change of sign of $f_1$ relative to $f_2$, forcing precisely one zero of $f_1$ between $c$ and $d$, and one on each side.

Therefore the zeros of $f_1$ and $f_2$ alternate on the real line, meaning between the two roots of each quadratic there lies a root of the other.

This completes the proof. ∎


## Verification of Key Steps

The computation of $N(x)$ is sensitive to sign errors. Re-expanding both products shows that all cubic terms cancel exactly, leaving a quadratic whose coefficients are $(p_2-p_1)$, $2(q_2-q_1)$, and $p_1q_2-p_2q_1$.

The identification of the discriminant is verified directly by applying $\Delta = b^2-4ac$ to $ax^2+bx+c$ with $a=p_2-p_1$, $b=2(q_2-q_1)$, $c=p_1q_2-p_2q_1$, producing precisely four times the given expression.

The step excluding the case of a single real root relies on asymptotic consistency: both limits of $\varphi$ at infinity equal $1$, so a strictly monotone function cannot diverge to opposite infinities across a single pole without violating monotonicity on at least one side interval.


## Alternative Approaches

A classical alternative proceeds through Sturm chains for the pair of quadratics. The given inequality is equivalent to the negativity of a resultant-type expression, and Sturm’s theorem then yields interlacing directly.

Another approach interprets the condition as negativity of a discriminant of a bilinear form in $(p,q)$, leading to a projective geometry statement about lines intersecting a conic in parameter space. This viewpoint is more conceptual but less direct than the monotonicity-of-ratio method, which isolates the analytic mechanism responsible for interlacing.
