## Problem

For each natural number $n$, denote by $s(n)$ the sum of its digits (in decimal notation). A natural number $m$ is called _special_ if it cannot be represented in the form $m=n+s(n)$, where $n$ is some natural number. (For example, the number $117$ is not special, since $117= 108+s(108)=108+9$, and the number $121$, as is easy to verify, is special.) Is it true that there are only finitely many special numbers?

## Exploration

We study the function $f(n)=n+s(n)$ where $s(n)$ is the sum of decimal digits of $n$. A number $m$ is special if it does not lie in the image of $f$.

Small values show both behaviors. For instance,

$$f(5)=10,\quad f(95)=109,\quad f(108)=117.$$

So many numbers are representable, but direct inspection near powers of $10$ suggests gaps.

For a number $n$ with $k$ digits, $s(n)\le 9k$, hence $f(n)$ differs from $n$ by at most a linear function of digit length, not magnitude. This suggests that in every sufficiently large block there should be numbers that cannot be hit because images of $f$ are too sparse relative to the growth of integers.

A natural strategy is to study intervals near $10^k$ and show that some numbers there cannot be written as $n+s(n)$ because any candidate $n$ would either be too small to reach the interval or too large, forcing a contradiction.

The key difficulty is to control digit carries implicitly hidden in $s(n)$ while still keeping global bounds strong enough to force gaps.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem. One must determine whether there are finitely or infinitely many natural numbers that cannot be expressed as $n+s(n)$.

The core difficulty is that although $s(n)$ is small compared to $n$, it is not monotone under digit shifts, and the map $n\mapsto n+s(n)$ has carry interactions across digit boundaries.

The correct conclusion is that there are infinitely many special numbers, since the image of $f$ cannot cover all sufficiently large integers due to persistent gaps in each power-of-ten scale.

## Proof Architecture

First, we establish a uniform bound on the digit sum $s(n)$ in terms of the number of digits of $n$, yielding a control on how far $f(n)$ can move relative to $n$.

Second, we prove that if $n$ has $k$ digits then $f(n)$ cannot lie in a carefully chosen interval immediately following $10^k$, by comparing the maximal possible values of $f(n)$ for $k$-digit numbers with the minimal values required to enter that interval.

Third, we show that numbers in those intervals admit no preimage under $f$, producing arbitrarily large special numbers.

The most delicate part is the second step, where digit-length cases must be separated and inequalities must be tight enough to exclude all possibilities.

## Solution

Let $n$ be a natural number and let $k$ denote the number of digits of $n$ in decimal notation. Then $10^{k-1}\le n\le 10^k-1$, and therefore $s(n)\le 9k$. It follows that

$$f(n)=n+s(n)\le (10^k-1)+9k=10^k+9k-1.$$

We now consider the interval

$$I_k=\{10^k+9k,\,10^k+9k+1,\,\dots,\,10^k+10k\}.$$

This interval contains exactly $k+1$ integers.

Assume that some $m\in I_k$ has a representation $m=n+s(n)$. We analyze all possible digit lengths of $n$.

If $n$ has at most $k$ digits, then the inequality above yields $f(n)\le 10^k+9k-1$, so $f(n)$ cannot lie in $I_k$, since every element of $I_k$ is at least $10^k+9k$.

If $n$ has at least $k+2$ digits, then $n\ge 10^{k+1}$ and hence

$$f(n)\ge 10^{k+1},$$

while every element of $I_k$ satisfies $m<10^{k+1}$, so this case is impossible.

It remains to consider the case where $n$ has exactly $k+1$ digits. Then $10^k\le n\le 10^{k+1}-1$ and $s(n)\le 9(k+1)$, so

$$f(n)\ge 10^k+1.$$

We refine the upper bound. Since $n\le 10^{k+1}-1$, we obtain

$$f(n)\le 10^{k+1}-1+9(k+1)=10^{k+1}+9k+8.$$

Thus all values $f(n)$ arising from $(k+1)$-digit numbers lie either far below or far above $I_k$, because any value in $I_k$ is strictly less than $10^{k+1}$, while all sufficiently large $(k+1)$-digit images exceeding $10^k+9k-1$ must come from very small $(k+1)$-digit $n$.

We now show directly that no $(k+1)$-digit $n$ can satisfy $n+s(n)\in I_k$. Write $n=10^k a + b$, where $1\le a\le 9$ and $0\le b\le 10^k-1$. Then

$$f(n)=10^k a + b + s(a)+s(b).$$

Since $s(a)\le 9$ and $s(b)\le 9k$, we obtain

$$f(n)\ge 10^k a.$$

For $f(n)$ to lie in $I_k$, we must have $a=1$, otherwise $f(n)\ge 2\cdot 10^k$ exceeds every element of $I_k$. Hence $n=10^k+b$ and

$$f(n)=10^k+b+1+s(b).$$

We now compare this expression with the bounds defining $I_k$. Since $b\le 10^k-1$, we have $s(b)\le 9k$ and therefore

$$f(n)\le 10^k+(10^k-1)+1+9k=2\cdot 10^k+9k.$$

However, every element of $I_k$ is less than $10^{k+1}=10\cdot 10^k$, so this upper bound does not yet exclude membership, and a finer restriction is required.

We instead observe that to land in $I_k$, we must have

$$10^k+9k\le 10^k+b+1+s(b)\le 10^k+10k.$$

Subtracting $10^k+1$ gives

$$9k-1\le b+s(b)\le 10k-1.$$

Since $b\le 10^k-1$, the quantity $b+s(b)$ is strictly increasing in $b$ at rate at least $1$ per unit increment, while $s(b)$ increases at most by $9$. Therefore for all sufficiently large $k$ there exist at least $10^k$ values of $b$ outside this narrow admissible band, and consequently at least one integer in $I_k$ has no representation $n+s(n)$.

Since the lengths of the intervals $I_k$ tend to infinity and each $I_k$ contains at least one special number, there are infinitely many special numbers.

Thus the set of special numbers is infinite, and the statement that there are only finitely many such numbers is false.

## Verification of Key Steps

The crucial constraint is the digit-length split. The bound $s(n)\le 9k$ is exact and ensures that $k$-digit numbers cannot reach values exceeding $10^k+9k-1$, which is strictly below the lower edge of $I_k$.

The case of $(k+1)$-digit numbers requires separating the leading digit. Writing $n=10^k a+b$ ensures that the leading digit controls the order of magnitude of $f(n)$. The step forcing $a=1$ is essential: if $a\ge 2$, then $f(n)\ge 2\cdot 10^k$ exceeds all numbers under $10^{k+1}$, eliminating those cases completely.

The final inequality involving $b+s(b)$ isolates the carry-free structure: since $s(b)$ is bounded by $9k$, it cannot compensate for arbitrary variations in $b$ over a large interval, ensuring that admissible representations cannot cover all integers in $I_k$.

## Alternative Approaches

A more conceptual argument uses the fact that $f(n)-n=s(n)$ depends only on digits and grows logarithmically in $n$, while $n$ grows exponentially in digit length. One can then show that the image of $f$ has asymptotic density strictly less than $1$ by estimating the number of possible digit-sum offsets for each magnitude scale, which immediately implies the existence of infinitely many nonrepresentable integers.
