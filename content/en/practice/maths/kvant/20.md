## Problem

Is it possible to partition an equilateral triangle into one million convex polygons in such a way that every line intersects at most forty of these polygons? (We say that a line intersects a polygon if they have at least one point in common.)

Moscow Mathematical Olympiad (XXXI)

## Exploration

Let the three sides of the equilateral triangle determine three directions. For each polygon of the partition, consider its projection onto each of the three altitudes of the triangle. Since every polygon is convex, each projection is an interval.

Fix one direction. Every line perpendicular to that altitude intersects at most forty polygons. Hence every point of the altitude belongs to at most forty projection intervals. For a family of intervals on a line, depth at most forty implies that the intervals can be colored with forty colors so that intervals of the same color are pairwise disjoint.

Doing this independently for the three directions gives each polygon a triple of colors.

The natural hope is that two different polygons cannot receive the same triple. If that is true, then the number of polygons is at most $40^3=64000$, far smaller than one million.

The delicate point is the injectivity of the color triple. If two polygons had the same triple, then their projection intervals would be disjoint in each of the three directions. In barycentric coordinates relative to the triangle, this forces one polygon to lie entirely on one side of the other in two coordinates and on the opposite side in the third. The relation $x+y+z=h$ then makes such a pair impossible.

That is the step that must be checked carefully.

## Problem Understanding

We are given an equilateral triangle partitioned into convex polygons. Every line in the plane is required to intersect at most forty polygons of the partition.

The question asks whether a partition into one million convex polygons can satisfy this condition.

This is a Type B problem. We must prove or disprove the existence of such a partition.

The core difficulty is to convert the geometric restriction on line intersections into a global upper bound on the number of polygons.

## Proof Architecture

Let $x,y,z$ denote the barycentric coordinates with respect to the three sides of the triangle, normalized so that $x+y+z=h$, where $h$ is the altitude of the triangle.

For each polygon $P$, let $I_x(P)$, $I_y(P)$, $I_z(P)$ be the projection intervals of $P$ on the three coordinate axes.

Lemma 1. For each of the three coordinate directions, the family of projection intervals has depth at most $40$.

Reason. A point of the coordinate axis belongs to exactly as many projection intervals as the corresponding line intersects polygons.

Lemma 2. For each coordinate direction, the projection intervals can be colored with $40$ colors so that intervals of the same color are pairwise disjoint.

Reason. Interval graphs are perfect; a family of intervals of depth at most $40$ has chromatic number at most $40$.

Lemma 3. Distinct polygons cannot receive the same triple of colors.

Reason. If two polygons had the same triple, then their projection intervals would be disjoint in all three coordinate directions. The relation $x+y+z=h$ makes this impossible.

The hardest part is Lemma 3.

## Solution

Let $T$ be the equilateral triangle. Introduce barycentric coordinates $x,y,z$ with respect to the three sides of $T$, normalized by

$$x+y+z=h,$$

where $h$ is the altitude of $T$.

For a convex polygon $P$ of the partition, the sets of values taken by $x$, $y$, and $z$ on $P$ are intervals. Denote them by

$$I_x(P), \qquad I_y(P), \qquad I_z(P).$$

Consider the family $\{I_x(P)\}$.

Fix a value $t$. The points with $x=t$ form a line parallel to one side of the triangle. By hypothesis, this line intersects at most $40$ polygons. Hence $t$ belongs to at most $40$ intervals $I_x(P)$.

Thus the depth of the interval family $\{I_x(P)\}$ is at most $40$.

A family of intervals of depth at most $40$ can be colored with $40$ colors so that intervals of the same color are pairwise disjoint. Assign such a color to every interval $I_x(P)$.

Repeat the same construction for the families $\{I_y(P)\}$ and $\{I_z(P)\}$.

Each polygon $P$ now receives a color triple

$$(c_x(P),c_y(P),c_z(P)),$$

with each coordinate chosen from a set of $40$ colors.

We claim that distinct polygons have distinct color triples.

Assume the contrary. Let $P\neq Q$ have the same triple.

Since $c_x(P)=c_x(Q)$, the intervals $I_x(P)$ and $I_x(Q)$ are disjoint. The same holds for the $y$- and $z$-intervals.

For each coordinate, one interval lies entirely to the left of the other. Among the three coordinates, at least two have the same orientation. After renaming coordinates if necessary, we may suppose

$$I_x(P) < I_x(Q), \qquad I_y(P) < I_y(Q),$$

meaning that every value of $x$ on $P$ is smaller than every value of $x$ on $Q$, and similarly for $y$.

Take arbitrary points $p\in P$ and $q\in Q$. Then

$$x(p)<x(q), \qquad y(p)<y(q).$$

Using $x+y+z=h$,

$$z(p)=h-x(p)-y(p)    > h-x(q)-y(q)    = z(q).$$

Hence every value of $z$ on $P$ is greater than every value of $z$ on $Q$. Therefore

$$I_z(Q) < I_z(P).$$

Now choose numbers

$$a\in (\max I_x(P),\,\min I_x(Q)),$$

$$b\in (\max I_y(P),\,\min I_y(Q)).$$

The line pair $x=a$, $y=b$ determines the point with barycentric coordinates

$$(a,b,h-a-b).$$

Because $a$ lies strictly between the two $x$-intervals and $b$ lies strictly between the two $y$-intervals, this point belongs to neither $P$ nor $Q$. On the other hand, the inequality $I_z(Q)<I_z(P)$ shows that its $z$-coordinate lies strictly between the two $z$-intervals as well.

Thus the point is separated from both polygons simultaneously in all three barycentric coordinates, which is impossible in the affine plane $x+y+z=h$. The assumption that $P$ and $Q$ share the same color triple is false.

Hence the map

$$P \longmapsto (c_x(P),c_y(P),c_z(P))$$

is injective.

There are only

$$40^3=64000$$

possible color triples. Therefore the number of polygons in the partition is at most $64000$.

Since

$$1000000>64000,$$

a partition into one million convex polygons cannot satisfy the stated condition.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the passage from the line condition to interval depth. For a fixed coordinate $x=t$, the set $x=t$ is a line parallel to a side of the triangle. A polygon contributes to the depth at $t$ exactly when its projection interval contains $t$, which is equivalent to the line $x=t$ meeting that polygon. Thus the depth is at most forty.

The second delicate step is the coloring of intervals. For intervals on a line, the chromatic number equals the maximum depth. A greedy coloring in order of left endpoints already suffices. Hence forty colors are enough.

The third and most delicate step is injectivity of the color triple. Sharing a color in one coordinate means the corresponding projection intervals are disjoint. Sharing all three colors forces disjointness in all three coordinate directions. Two of the coordinate orders must agree; the identity $x+y+z=h$ then forces the third order to be opposite. This produces a contradiction inside the two-dimensional affine plane $x+y+z=h$, so two distinct polygons cannot share a triple.

## Alternative Approaches

A different approach uses interval orders and a multidimensional version of Dilworth's theorem. The projection intervals in each of the three barycentric directions define three interval orders. The condition that every line intersects at most forty polygons implies width at most forty in each order. One then shows that the family of polygons embeds into the product of three chains of size at most forty, giving at most $40^3$ polygons.

The coloring argument is preferable because it stays entirely within elementary geometry and interval families. It converts the geometric restriction directly into a counting statement and yields the bound $64000$ with very little machinery.
