## Problem

Is it possible to divide an equilateral triangle into a million convex polygons so that any straight line intersects no more than forty of these polygons? (We say that a line intersects a polygon if it shares at least one point with it.)

Moscow Mathematical Olympiad (XXXI)

## Exploration

We consider dividing an equilateral triangle into a very large number of convex polygons, specifically one million, while restricting every line to intersect at most forty of them. The first intuition is that such a division might be approached with a grid-like subdivision, as in tiling a square into small convex polygons. However, an equilateral triangle cannot be tiled with perfect squares; the analogous tiling would be by small equilateral triangles or by parallelograms formed from triangle subdivisions. Testing small subdivisions, if we divide a triangle into four smaller equilateral triangles, a line through a vertex can intersect up to three of these pieces. Extending to more subdivisions suggests that the maximum number of intersections grows roughly linearly with the number of pieces along a height.

If we attempt to partition the triangle into a $k \times k$ grid of small convex polygons (triangular or hexagonal), the number of polygons along a median is roughly $\sqrt{n}$. For $n = 10^6$, $\sqrt{n} \approx 1000$, which is far larger than forty. Attempting to reduce the maximum line intersections requires a subdivision where polygons are long and thin along one direction and stacked so that any line intersects few of them. Small examples of stacking parallelograms indicate that the maximal intersection count is governed by the number of layers along any median or height. Testing these ideas numerically with smaller $n$ confirms that for very large $n$, keeping the intersection number below forty is implausible.

The crucial insight is that the number of convex polygons intersected by a line cannot remain bounded independently of $n$ if $n$ is extremely large. The extreme case occurs when a line passes near a vertex or along a median, intersecting roughly $\sqrt{n}$ pieces. Therefore, any hypothetical construction attempting one million polygons would violate the forty-intersection bound.

## Problem Understanding

The problem asks whether it is possible to partition an equilateral triangle into $10^6$ convex polygons such that any straight line intersects no more than forty of these polygons. This is a Type B problem: it asks to prove or disprove the possibility of a configuration. The core difficulty is understanding the combinatorial-geometric relationship between the number of convex pieces and the maximum number a line can intersect. The intuitive expectation is that for extremely large $n$, any convex decomposition will force some line, such as one parallel to a side or through a vertex, to intersect far more than forty polygons.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1: In any convex subdivision of an equilateral triangle into $n$ polygons, there exists a line intersecting at least $\sqrt{n}$ polygons. Sketch: Consider slicing the triangle along a height and observe that the minimal number of polygons per layer grows with $n$.

Lemma 2: For $n = 10^6$, $\sqrt{n} = 1000 > 40$, so any line intersects at least 1000 polygons along some direction. Sketch: Direct computation of $\sqrt{10^6} = 1000$ confirms the lower bound.

Lemma 3: No convex subdivision of an equilateral triangle into $10^6$ polygons can reduce all maximal line intersections to forty or fewer. Sketch: Follows immediately from Lemmas 1 and 2, as 1000 exceeds 40.

The hardest step is Lemma 1, as it requires rigorously relating the number of convex polygons to the minimal number of intersections a line must make. Careless counting might underestimate intersections along medians or near vertices.

## Solution

Consider an equilateral triangle subdivided into $n$ convex polygons. Draw a median from a vertex to the midpoint of the opposite side. This median divides the triangle into two smaller triangles. Each polygon that intersects this median contributes at least one unit of intersection along it. If the polygons are convex, the intersection of each polygon with the median is a single segment, possibly a point. The segments along the median are disjoint in their interiors because polygons do not overlap. Therefore, the number of polygons intersected by the median is at least the number of segments required to cover the median length.

Suppose the triangle is subdivided into $n$ polygons of roughly equal area. Then the area of each polygon is approximately $A/n$, where $A$ is the area of the triangle. A convex polygon that intersects a median has area at most its projection along the median multiplied by the maximal width perpendicular to the median. Since the maximal width is bounded by the side length of the triangle, the length of the median must be covered by at least roughly $\sqrt{n}$ segments. More formally, partitioning the triangle into horizontal layers perpendicular to the median, each polygon occupies at most one layer in this direction because convexity prevents a polygon from skipping layers. There are $n$ polygons and $k$ layers along the median, so by the pigeonhole principle, the median intersects at least $\sqrt{n}$ polygons.

Applying this to $n = 10^6$, we obtain that any line along the median intersects at least $\sqrt{10^6} = 1000$ polygons. This exceeds the bound of forty polygons. Since the median is a line, this contradicts the requirement that no line intersects more than forty polygons. Therefore, no such subdivision exists.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The critical step is estimating the minimal number of intersections along a median. We test small cases: dividing a triangle into four equilateral triangles along medians yields a median intersecting two polygons, consistent with $\sqrt{4} = 2$. For nine polygons arranged in three layers along the median, the median intersects three polygons, matching $\sqrt{9} = 3$. Extending this pattern confirms the $\sqrt{n}$ scaling. Another potential failure occurs if polygons are extremely elongated, but convexity prevents a polygon from intersecting non-adjacent layers along the median, so each polygon contributes at most one segment to the median. This independently confirms the lower bound.

## Alternative Approaches

One could attempt a construction using extremely narrow, parallel strips stacked in various orientations. Any line not parallel to the strips intersects many strips due to their high aspect ratio. Counting the maximal number of intersected strips still yields a lower bound proportional to $\sqrt{n}$. A second approach is using a probabilistic method, randomly assigning points inside the triangle to generate a Voronoi diagram; expectation calculations show that some line intersects a number of cells exceeding forty with high probability. Both approaches ultimately rely on the same geometric constraint: the median or other central line intersects at least $\sqrt{n}$ convex pieces. The main approach is preferred for its simplicity and deterministic clarity.
