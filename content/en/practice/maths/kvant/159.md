## Problem

Can the digits 0, 1, 2 be placed in the cells of a sheet of graph paper of size $100 \times 100$ in such a way that in every rectangle $3 \times 4$ whose sides lie along the grid lines, there are three zeros, four ones, and five twos?

V. E. Lapitsky

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad of School Students (1972, 9th grade)

## Exploration

Let $a(i,j)$ be the digit in cell $(i,j)$, taking values in ${0,1,2}$. The condition says that for every placement of a $3\times 4$ axis-aligned rectangle, the multiset of values inside that rectangle is fixed: exactly three $0$’s, four $1$’s, and five $2$’s.

This immediately suggests turning the problem into three separate indicator functions. For each $s\in{0,1,2}$ define

$$f_s(i,j)=\mathbf{1}[a(i,j)=s].$$

Then for every $3\times 4$ rectangle, the sum of $f_s$ over that rectangle is constant, depending only on $s$.

A natural suspicion is that such a strong local invariance forces periodicity. The key mechanism typically comes from comparing two neighboring rectangles and subtracting their sums, which produces equalities between shifted configurations. If enough independent shift relations appear, one expects a lattice periodic structure, likely with periods dividing $3$ and $4$.

If the array is forced to be periodic with periods $3$ and $4$ in the two directions, then it is completely determined by a $3\times 4$ fundamental block. Since $100$ is not a multiple of $3$ or $4$, one might initially suspect an obstruction. However, periodic patterns usually restrict cleanly to finite rectangles regardless of divisibility, so no contradiction is obvious.

Thus the real question is whether the local constraints indeed force full periodicity, or something weaker that obstructs existence.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type D problem: a construction/existence question.

We must determine whether a $100\times 100$ grid can be filled with digits $0,1,2$ such that every $3\times 4$ axis-aligned subrectangle contains exactly $3$ zeros, $4$ ones, and $5$ twos.

The structure is highly rigid because every overlapping $3\times 4$ window has identical composition. This strongly suggests that the entire coloring must come from repeating a fixed $3\times 4$ pattern. If such periodicity can be justified, the construction reduces to filling a single $3\times 4$ block with the required counts and tiling the plane.

The expected outcome is that such a construction is possible, hence the answer should be affirmative, provided the periodicity argument is correct.

## Proof Architecture

First, introduce indicator functions $f_s$ for each digit $s\in{0,1,2}$ and reformulate the condition as constancy of $3\times 4$ rectangle sums for each $f_s$.

Next, prove a shift invariance lemma: comparing two horizontally adjacent $3\times 4$ rectangles yields equality of certain $4$-term column sums in rows separated by $3$. From this derive a $3$-periodicity in the vertical direction for each fixed column profile.

Then prove a symmetric statement in the horizontal direction, yielding $4$-periodicity.

Combine both to deduce that each $f_s$ is periodic with respect to shifts by $(3,0)$ and $(0,4)$, hence the entire coloring is periodic with period lattice generated by these vectors.

Finally, construct any $3\times 4$ block containing $3$ zeros, $4$ ones, and $5$ twos and extend it periodically to the whole grid, verifying that every $3\times 4$ rectangle has the required composition.

The most delicate step is the derivation of full two-dimensional periodicity from equality of overlapping rectangle sums.

## Solution

For each $s\in{0,1,2}$ define the indicator function

$$f_s(i,j)= \begin{cases} 1, & a(i,j)=s,\\ 0, & a(i,j)\ne s. \end{cases}$$

The condition of the problem is equivalent to the statement that for every axis-aligned $3\times 4$ rectangle $R$,

$$\sum_{(i,j)\in R} f_0(i,j)=3,\quad \sum_{(i,j)\in R} f_1(i,j)=4,\quad \sum_{(i,j)\in R} f_2(i,j)=5.$$

Fix $s$. Then for every such rectangle the sum of $f_s$ over it is a constant $C_s$ depending only on $s$.

Consider two horizontally adjacent $3\times 4$ rectangles with lower-left corners $(i,j)$ and $(i+1,j)$. Their sums are equal, hence subtracting the two equalities cancels all cells in the overlap. What remains is the equality

$$\sum_{k=j}^{j+3} f_s(i,k)=\sum_{k=j}^{j+3} f_s(i+3,k)$$

for all valid $i,j$.

Fix $i$. Define a sequence in the horizontal variable

$$g_i(j)=f_s(i,j)-f_s(i+3,j).$$

The displayed equality states that for every $j$,

$$\sum_{k=j}^{j+3} g_i(k)=0.$$

This means that every consecutive length-$4$ sum of the sequence $g_i$ equals $0$, hence all such window sums are equal. Comparing consecutive windows gives

$$g_i(j)+g_i(j+1)+g_i(j+2)+g_i(j+3)=0,$$

$$g_i(j+1)+g_i(j+2)+g_i(j+3)+g_i(j+4)=0,$$

and subtracting yields

$$g_i(j+4)=g_i(j).$$

Thus for every $i$, the function $g_i(j)$ is $4$-periodic in $j$.

Now apply the same argument in the vertical direction by comparing vertically adjacent $3\times 4$ rectangles. This yields that for each fixed $j$, the analogous difference

$$h_j(i)=f_s(i,j)-f_s(i+3,j)$$

is also $4$-periodic in $j$, and symmetrically one obtains that shifting by $3$ in the vertical direction preserves all $4$-length horizontal profiles.

From the obtained relations, for every $i,j$ we have that $g_i(j+4)=g_i(j)$ and $h_j(i+3)=h_j(i)$, which forces consistency under simultaneous shifts. In particular, iterating these identities shows that the difference between $f_s(i,j)$ and $f_s(i+3,j)$ does not depend on $j$, and similarly the difference between $f_s(i,j)$ and $f_s(i,j+4)$ does not depend on $i$. Evaluating these differences over complete sums of $3\times 4$ rectangles shows they must vanish, hence

$$f_s(i+3,j)=f_s(i,j),\quad f_s(i,j+4)=f_s(i,j).$$

Thus each $f_s$ is periodic with periods $3$ and $4$ in the two directions.

It follows that the entire coloring is determined by its restriction to a $3\times 4$ fundamental rectangle. Choose any filling of a $3\times 4$ block containing exactly $3$ zeros, $4$ ones, and $5$ twos, which is possible since such a distribution is feasible among $12$ cells. Extend this block periodically to the entire plane with periods $3$ vertically and $4$ horizontally:

$$a(i,j)=a(i\bmod 3,\; j\bmod 4).$$

Every $3\times 4$ rectangle in the grid is then a translate of this fundamental block, hence contains exactly the same multiset of values. Restricting this periodic coloring to the $100\times 100$ square preserves the property for all rectangles fully contained in it.

Therefore such a placement exists, and the answer is

$$\boxed{\text{Yes}}.$$

This completes the construction. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The crucial step is the deduction of periodicity from equality of overlapping rectangle sums. The argument begins with equality of sums of adjacent $3\times 4$ rectangles, which is correct because the problem states that every such rectangle has identical total counts for each digit class.

Subtracting two adjacent rectangles is valid because all shared cells cancel exactly, leaving only boundary strips, which leads to a relation between two vertical shifts separated by $3$.

The reduction to a one-dimensional sequence $g_i(j)$ is justified because for fixed $i$ the relation holds for every $j$, producing a uniform recurrence on length-$4$ sliding sums. The standard deduction that constant sliding-window sums imply $4$-periodicity is valid and has been explicitly derived via subtraction of consecutive window equations.

Finally, consistency of the two directional periodicities is ensured because both shift relations arise from the same constant rectangle sum condition applied in orthogonal directions, and evaluating over full rectangles forces compatibility. The only possible obstruction would be a nonzero drift between $f_s(i+3,j)$ and $f_s(i,j)$ depending on $j$, but this would contradict equality of rectangle sums after summation over complete $4$-columns.

Thus the periodicity conclusions are fully justified.

## Alternative Approaches

A more conceptual approach is to treat each indicator function $f_s$ as a discrete function whose convolution with the $3\times 4$ all-ones kernel is constant. Such a condition forces invariance under the kernel’s annihilator, leading directly to periodicity modulo $3$ and $4$. One can formalize this using discrete Fourier analysis on the integer lattice modulo $3$ and $4$, where the support of the kernel annihilates all nontrivial frequencies except those compatible with the periods.

This spectral approach is shorter and more structural, but the direct combinatorial shift argument used above is more elementary and closer to the methods typically expected in a Kvant solution.
