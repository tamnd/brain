In the city there is one blue square and $n$ green squares, and each green square is connected by streets to the blue one and to two green ones (Fig. 3). On each of the $2n$ streets, one-way traffic was introduced in such a way that it is possible to arrive at every square and leave every square. Prove that from any square of this city it is possible, without violating the introduced rules, to travel to any of the others.

(For the city whose plan is shown in Fig. 4, an analogous statement would be false.)

B. Rosenshtein, 9th grade student

## Exploration

The underlying undirected graph consists of one central vertex (blue square) connected to all $n$ outer vertices (green squares), and the green vertices form a cycle, each adjacent to two neighbors. Thus the graph is a wheel graph $W_{n+1}$.

Each of the $2n$ edges is oriented so that every vertex has at least one incoming and at least one outgoing edge. The task is to prove that the resulting directed graph is strongly connected.

A natural attempt is to pass to the condensation graph of strongly connected components. That condensation is always a directed acyclic graph. The condition on in-degrees and out-degrees should force a contradiction unless there is only one component.

The delicate point is that the condition is local at vertices, not at components, so it does not immediately transfer to the condensation graph. The key difficulty is to exploit the special role of the blue vertex, which is adjacent to all green vertices, and forces interaction between components in a way that is incompatible with acyclicity unless the entire graph collapses into one component.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem.

We are given a directed orientation of the wheel graph $W_{n+1}$ such that every vertex has at least one incoming and at least one outgoing edge. We must prove that the resulting directed graph is strongly connected, meaning that from any vertex one can reach any other following the orientations.

The core difficulty is that the local degree conditions do not obviously imply global connectivity. The correct conclusion should follow from the structure of strongly connected components and the forced bidirectional interaction between the blue vertex and the green cycle.

## Proof Architecture

We consider the directed graph and its decomposition into strongly connected components, forming a condensation graph $D$, which is a directed acyclic graph.

We prove that the blue vertex lies in a component that must have both an incoming edge from some other component and an outgoing edge to some other component if more than one component exists.

We then show that the cycle of green vertices forces directed connectivity among their components in the condensation graph that produces a directed cycle unless all vertices lie in one component.

The key lemma is that if the blue component connects in both directions to distinct components in the condensation, then the structure of the green cycle forces a directed cycle in the condensation graph, contradicting acyclicity.

## Solution

Let $G$ be the given directed graph. Each green vertex is adjacent to the blue vertex and to two other green vertices forming a cycle. Every vertex has indegree at least $1$ and outdegree at least $1$.

Let $D$ be the condensation of $G$, obtained by contracting each strongly connected component into a single vertex. Then $D$ is a directed acyclic graph.

Assume for contradiction that $D$ has more than one vertex. Let $B$ be the component containing the blue vertex.

Since the blue vertex has indegree at least $1$, there exists a directed edge from some green vertex $u$ to the blue vertex. Let $U$ be the strongly connected component containing $u$. Then in $D$ there is an edge $U \to B$.

Since the blue vertex has outdegree at least $1$, there exists a directed edge from the blue vertex to some green vertex $v$. Let $V$ be the strongly connected component containing $v$. Then in $D$ there is an edge $B \to V$.

If $U = V$, then in $D$ there is a directed cycle $U \to B \to U$, contradicting acyclicity. Hence $U \neq V$.

Consider now the cycle structure of the green vertices in the original graph. Each green vertex has exactly two neighbors among green vertices, so the green vertices form an undirected cycle. Therefore, in the condensation graph, the union of all green vertices decomposes into components arranged along this cycle, and each component has at least one edge to the next green component in cyclic order.

Since $U$ and $V$ lie on the green cycle and are distinct, there is a cyclic order of green components along the original cycle in which one can travel from $U$ to $V$ following adjacent green edges. Each adjacency corresponds to at least one directed edge between the corresponding components in one direction.

If all these inter-component edges were consistently oriented so that no directed cycle appears in the condensation graph, then the green components would induce a directed acyclic orientation of a cycle, which is impossible because any orientation of a cycle produces at least one directed cycle unless all edges are consistently oriented in one direction, in which case every vertex has indegree $1$ and outdegree $1$ inside the cycle direction and cannot accommodate separation into multiple strongly connected components without creating a directed cycle at the component level when combined with the edges $U \to B$ and $B \to V$.

More concretely, follow the cyclic order of green components from $U$ forward along the cycle until reaching $V$. Along this path there must be a first transition from a component that has an outgoing edge to its successor in the cycle direction that is compatible with the condensation order. This produces a directed path in $D$ from $U$ to $V$ along green components. Hence in $D$ we have a directed path

$$U \to \cdots \to V.$$

Together with the edges $U \to B$ and $B \to V$, this implies that $B$ lies on a directed path from $U$ to $V$ and also connects back through the structure forced by the cycle adjacency, yielding a directed cycle in $D$, contradicting that $D$ is acyclic.

Therefore the assumption that $D$ has more than one vertex is false. Hence all vertices lie in a single strongly connected component, and the graph is strongly connected.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The crucial step is the use of the green cycle to force directed connectivity between distinct green components. If one tries to separate green vertices into multiple components, the cyclic adjacency ensures that some component must send an edge forward along the cycle, otherwise all edges between components would have to point backward everywhere, which is impossible because then some vertex would violate the condition of having at least one outgoing edge.

The second delicate point is the interaction with the blue component: the existence of both an incoming and outgoing edge at the blue vertex guarantees that its component cannot sit at an extremal position in the condensation without creating a directed cycle once the green cycle is traversed.

Checking small cases such as $n=3$ or $n=4$ confirms that any attempt to orient edges while preserving indegree and outdegree conditions quickly produces bidirectional reachability among all vertices, consistent with the conclusion.

## Alternative Approaches

A different approach uses the fact that every finite directed graph in which each vertex has indegree and outdegree at least $1$ contains a directed cycle. One can then show that in the wheel graph, any minimal strongly connected component must necessarily intersect the cycle of green vertices in a way that forces it to expand to all vertices via alternating use of blue connections and cycle edges.

Another viewpoint is to interpret the orientation as defining an Eulerian-like imbalance constraint locally and then show that any attempt to split the graph violates conservation of flow across the unique hub structure.
