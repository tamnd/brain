## Problem

Find the sum $$1\cdot n + 2\cdot (n-1) + 3\cdot (n-2)+ \ldots + n\cdot 1.$$

Try to solve the following, more general problem: find the sum $$\begin{aligned} S_{n,k}={}&[1\cdot 2\cdot \ldots \cdot k] \times [n(n-1)\cdot\ldots\cdot (n-k+1)]+{}\\ {}+{}& [2\cdot 3\cdot \ldots \cdot (k+1)] \times [(n-1)(n-2)\cdot\ldots\cdot (n-k)]+{}\\ {}+{}& [3\cdot 4\cdot \ldots \cdot (k+2)] \times [(n-2)(n-1)\cdot\ldots\cdot (n-k-1)]+{}\\ {}+{}& {\ldots}\,{\ldots}\,{\ldots}\,{\ldots}\,{\ldots}\,{\ldots}\,{\ldots}\,{\ldots} +{}\\ {}+{}& [(n-k)(n-k+1)\cdot \ldots \cdot (n-1)] \times [(k+1)k \cdot\ldots\cdot 2]+{}\\ {}+{}& [(n-k+1)(n-k+2)\cdot \ldots \cdot n] \times [k(k-1) \cdot\ldots\cdot 1]. \end{aligned}$$

V. N. Berezin

## Exploration

For the first sum,

$$1\cdot n+2\cdot(n-1)+\cdots+n\cdot1,$$

it is natural to write the $i$-th term as $i(n+1-i)$. Then

$$\sum_{i=1}^{n} i(n+1-i) =(n+1)\sum_{i=1}^{n}i-\sum_{i=1}^{n}i^2.$$

This gives

$$(n+1)\frac{n(n+1)}2-\frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}6 =\frac{n(n+1)(n+2)}6.$$

The result equals $\binom{n+2}{3}$.

The general sum suggests a binomial-coefficient interpretation. Let the summation index be $i=1,\dots,n-k+1$. The $i$-th term is

$$[i(i+1)\cdots(i+k-1)] [(n-i+1)(n-i)\cdots(n-i-k+2)].$$

Since

$$i(i+1)\cdots(i+k-1)=k!\binom{i+k-1}{k},$$

and

$$(n-i+1)(n-i)\cdots(n-i-k+2) =k!\binom{n-i+1}{k},$$

the term becomes

$$(k!)^2 \binom{i+k-1}{k} \binom{n-i+1}{k}.$$

Thus

$$S_{n,k} =(k!)^2 \sum_{i=1}^{n-k+1} \binom{i+k-1}{k} \binom{n-i+1}{k}.$$

The remaining sum resembles Vandermonde's convolution. Setting

$$r=i+k-1,$$

gives

$$\sum_{r=k}^{n} \binom{r}{k}\binom{n+k-r}{k}.$$

Trying small values helps. For $k=1$,

$$\sum_{r=1}^{n}r(n+1-r)=\binom{n+2}{3}.$$

For $k=2$ and $n=3$,

$$(1\cdot2)(3\cdot2)+(2\cdot3)(2\cdot1)=24,$$

while

$$(2!)^2\binom{6}{5}=4\cdot6=24.$$

This suggests

$$S_{n,k}=(k!)^2\binom{n+k+1}{2k+1}.$$

The delicate step is proving

$$\sum_{r=k}^{n} \binom{r}{k}\binom{n+k-r}{k} = \binom{n+k+1}{2k+1}.$$

A combinatorial proof of Vandermonde's convolution avoids algebraic mistakes.

## Problem Understanding

We are asked first to evaluate

$$1\cdot n+2\cdot(n-1)+\cdots+n\cdot1,$$

and then to solve the more general problem of finding the value of $S_{n,k}$.

This is a Type C problem, determination of an explicit value.

The core difficulty is recognizing the products of consecutive integers as factorial multiples of binomial coefficients and then evaluating the resulting convolution. The expected answer is

$$S_{n,k}=(k!)^2\binom{n+k+1}{2k+1},$$

because each factor of consecutive integers naturally converts into a binomial coefficient, after which a Vandermonde-type identity appears.

## Proof Architecture

The first claim is that the $i$-th summand of $S_{n,k}$ equals

$$(k!)^2 \binom{i+k-1}{k} \binom{n-i+1}{k}.$$

This follows directly from the factorial definition of binomial coefficients.

The second claim is that

$$S_{n,k} =(k!)^2 \sum_{r=k}^{n} \binom{r}{k}\binom{n+k-r}{k}.$$

This is obtained by the substitution $r=i+k-1$.

The third claim is the convolution identity

$$\sum_{r=k}^{n} \binom{r}{k}\binom{n+k-r}{k} = \binom{n+k+1}{2k+1}.$$

This is proved by counting $(2k+1)$-element subsets of a set with $n+k+1$ elements according to their $(k+1)$-st smallest element.

Combining the previous claims yields the formula for $S_{n,k}$.

The lemma most likely to fail under scrutiny is the convolution identity, so it must be proved carefully.

## Solution

Let

$$T_i=[i(i+1)\cdots(i+k-1)] [(n-i+1)(n-i)\cdots(n-i-k+2)]$$

be the $i$-th summand of $S_{n,k}$, where

$$1\le i\le n-k+1.$$

Using

$$\binom{i+k-1}{k} = \frac{i(i+1)\cdots(i+k-1)}{k!},$$

and

$$\binom{n-i+1}{k} = \frac{(n-i+1)(n-i)\cdots(n-i-k+2)}{k!},$$

we obtain

$$T_i = (k!)^2 \binom{i+k-1}{k} \binom{n-i+1}{k}.$$

Therefore

$$S_{n,k} = (k!)^2 \sum_{i=1}^{n-k+1} \binom{i+k-1}{k} \binom{n-i+1}{k}.$$

Put

$$r=i+k-1.$$

As $i$ runs from $1$ to $n-k+1$, the variable $r$ runs from $k$ to $n$. Hence

$$S_{n,k} = (k!)^2 \sum_{r=k}^{n} \binom{r}{k} \binom{n+k-r}{k}.$$

It remains to evaluate

$$\Sigma= \sum_{r=k}^{n} \binom{r}{k} \binom{n+k-r}{k}.$$

Consider the set

$$\{1,2,\dots,n+k+1\}.$$

We count its $(2k+1)$-element subsets.

The total number of such subsets is

$$\binom{n+k+1}{2k+1}.$$

Now classify these subsets according to their $(k+1)$-st smallest element. Let that element be $r+1$, where $k\le r\le n$.

Among the numbers $1,2,\dots,r$, exactly $k$ must be chosen, because $r+1$ is the $(k+1)$-st selected element. This can be done in

$$\binom{r}{k}$$

ways.

After choosing $r+1$, we still need $k$ more elements. These must be chosen from

$$r+2,r+3,\dots,n+k+1,$$

a set containing

$$n+k-r$$

elements. This can be done in

$$\binom{n+k-r}{k}$$

ways.

Hence the number of subsets whose $(k+1)$-st smallest element equals $r+1$ is

$$\binom{r}{k}\binom{n+k-r}{k}.$$

Summing over all possible values of $r$ gives

$$\Sigma = \sum_{r=k}^{n} \binom{r}{k} \binom{n+k-r}{k} = \binom{n+k+1}{2k+1}.$$

Substituting this into the expression for $S_{n,k}$ yields

$$S_{n,k} = (k!)^2 \binom{n+k+1}{2k+1}.$$

Thus

$$\boxed{S_{n,k}=(k!)^2\binom{n+k+1}{2k+1}}.$$

For the original problem, $k=1$, so

$$1\cdot n+2\cdot(n-1)+\cdots+n\cdot1 = \binom{n+2}{3} = \frac{n(n+1)(n+2)}6.$$

Therefore

$$\boxed{1\cdot n+2\cdot(n-1)+\cdots+n\cdot1 =\frac{n(n+1)(n+2)}6}.$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the conversion of consecutive products into binomial coefficients. For example,

$$k!\binom{i+k-1}{k} = k!\frac{(i+k-1)!}{k!(i-1)!} = \frac{(i+k-1)!}{(i-1)!} = i(i+1)\cdots(i+k-1).$$

The same computation applies to the second factor. Missing one endpoint would shift the upper index of the binomial coefficient and produce an incorrect formula.

The second delicate step is the range after the substitution $r=i+k-1$. When $i=1$, we get $r=k$. When $i=n-k+1$, we get $r=n$. Any other range would omit terms or introduce nonexistent ones.

The third delicate step is the combinatorial proof of the convolution identity. Once the $(k+1)$-st smallest element is fixed as $r+1$, exactly $k$ chosen elements lie before it and exactly $k$ lie after it. These numbers are forced by the total size $2k+1$. Forgetting this condition would count subsets multiple times.

## Alternative Approaches

The convolution identity may also be derived algebraically from Vandermonde's convolution. Writing

$$\binom{r}{k}=[x^k](1+x)^r, \qquad \binom{n+k-r}{k}=[y^k](1+y)^{\,n+k-r},$$

and summing over $r$ leads to the coefficient extraction form of Vandermonde's identity, yielding

$$\sum_{r=k}^{n} \binom{r}{k}\binom{n+k-r}{k} = \binom{n+k+1}{2k+1}.$$

For the special case $k=1$, a direct computation is shorter:

$$\sum_{i=1}^{n} i(n+1-i) =(n+1)\sum_{i=1}^{n}i-\sum_{i=1}^{n}i^2,$$

which immediately gives

$$\frac{n(n+1)(n+2)}6.$$

The combinatorial approach is preferable because it handles all values of $k$ uniformly and reveals the structure behind the general formula.
