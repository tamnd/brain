## Problem

Find the sum $$1\cdot n + 2\cdot (n-1) + 3\cdot (n-2)+ \ldots + n\cdot 1.$$

Try to solve the following, more general problem: find the sum $$\begin{aligned} S_{n,k}={}&[1\cdot 2\cdot \ldots \cdot k] \times [n(n-1)\cdot\ldots\cdot (n-k+1)]+{}\\ {}+{}& [2\cdot 3\cdot \ldots \cdot (k+1)] \times [(n-1)(n-2)\cdot\ldots\cdot (n-k)]+{}\\ {}+{}& [3\cdot 4\cdot \ldots \cdot (k+2)] \times [(n-2)(n-1)\cdot\ldots\cdot (n-k-1)]+{}\\ {}+{}& {\ldots}\,{\ldots}\,{\ldots}\,{\ldots}\,{\ldots}\,{\ldots}\,{\ldots}\,{\ldots} +{}\\ {}+{}& [(n-k)(n-k+1)\cdot \ldots \cdot (n-1)] \times [(k+1)k \cdot\ldots\cdot 2]+{}\\ {}+{}& [(n-k+1)(n-k+2)\cdot \ldots \cdot n] \times [k(k-1) \cdot\ldots\cdot 1]. \end{aligned}$$

V. N. Berezin

## Exploration

For the first sum,

$$1\cdot n+2\cdot(n-1)+\cdots+n\cdot1,$$

the $i$-th term is

$$i(n+1-i).$$

Hence

$$\sum_{i=1}^{n}i(n+1-i) =(n+1)\sum_{i=1}^{n}i-\sum_{i=1}^{n}i^2.$$

This gives

$$\frac{n(n+1)^2}{2}-\frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6} =\frac{n(n+1)(n+2)}{6}.$$

The result is

$$\binom{n+2}{3}.$$

This suggests that the general sum may also be expressible through a single binomial coefficient.

Let the terms of $S_{n,k}$ be indexed by $r=0,1,\dots,n-k$:

$$[(r+1)(r+2)\cdots(r+k)] [(n-r)(n-r-1)\cdots(n-r-k+1)].$$

The first product equals

$$\frac{(r+k)!}{r!}=k!\binom{r+k}{k},$$

and the second equals

$$\frac{(n-r)!}{(n-r-k)!} =k!\binom{n-r}{k}.$$

Therefore

$$S_{n,k} =(k!)^2\sum_{r=0}^{n-k} \binom{r+k}{k}\binom{n-r}{k}.$$

The remaining sum resembles Vandermonde's convolution. Testing small values:

For $k=1$,

$$\sum_{r=0}^{n-1}\binom{r+1}{1}\binom{n-r}{1} =\binom{n+2}{3}.$$

For $k=2$, $n=4$,

$$1\cdot6+3\cdot3+6\cdot1=21,$$

while

$$\binom{6}{5}=6.$$

Since $21=6\cdot\frac{(2!)^2}{?}$, the factor should be $(2!)^2=4$, giving

$$4\binom{7}{5}=84,$$

which is too large. Recomputing,

$$\binom{2}{2}\binom{4}{2} +\binom{3}{2}\binom{3}{2} +\binom{4}{2}\binom{2}{2} =6+9+6=21.$$

The value $21$ equals

$$\binom{7}{5}.$$

Hence

$$\sum_{r=0}^{n-k} \binom{r+k}{k}\binom{n-r}{k} =\binom{n+k+1}{2k+1}.$$

The crucial point is proving this convolution rigorously.

## Problem Understanding

We must evaluate a family of sums. The original problem corresponds to $k=1$, and the general problem asks for a closed formula for $S_{n,k}$.

This is a Type C problem, since the task is to determine the exact value of a quantity.

The main difficulty is transforming the complicated products into a form where a known combinatorial identity applies. The expected answer is

$$S_{n,k}=(k!)^2\binom{n+k+1}{2k+1},$$

because each product can be rewritten through binomial coefficients, after which the sum becomes a Vandermonde-type convolution.

## Proof Architecture

First, rewrite each term of $S_{n,k}$ as $(k!)^2\binom{r+k}{k}\binom{n-r}{k}$, using the factorial definition of binomial coefficients.

Second, prove the identity

$$\sum_{r=0}^{n-k} \binom{r+k}{k}\binom{n-r}{k} = \binom{n+k+1}{2k+1}.$$

This is the Vandermonde convolution after the substitution

$$\binom{r+k}{k}=\binom{r+k}{r}.$$

Third, substitute this identity into the expression for $S_{n,k}$ and simplify.

The step most likely to fail under scrutiny is the derivation of the convolution identity, so it must be proved carefully.

## Solution

Let the summands of $S_{n,k}$ be indexed by $r=0,1,\ldots,n-k$. Then

$$S_{n,k} = \sum_{r=0}^{n-k} \Bigl[(r+1)(r+2)\cdots(r+k)\Bigr] \Bigl[(n-r)(n-r-1)\cdots(n-r-k+1)\Bigr].$$

For every $r$,

$$(r+1)(r+2)\cdots(r+k) = \frac{(r+k)!}{r!} = k!\binom{r+k}{k},$$

and

$$(n-r)(n-r-1)\cdots(n-r-k+1) = \frac{(n-r)!}{(n-r-k)!} = k!\binom{n-r}{k}.$$

Hence

$$S_{n,k} = (k!)^2 \sum_{r=0}^{n-k} \binom{r+k}{k}\binom{n-r}{k}.$$

Since

$$\binom{r+k}{k}=\binom{r+k}{r},$$

we obtain

$$S_{n,k} = (k!)^2 \sum_{r=0}^{n-k} \binom{r+k}{r}\binom{n-r}{k}.$$

We now apply Vandermonde's convolution. For nonnegative integers $A,B,m$,

$$\sum_{t=0}^{m} \binom{A+t}{t}\binom{B-t}{m-t} = \binom{A+B+1}{m}.$$

Taking

$$A=k,\qquad B=n,\qquad m=2k+1,$$

the summand becomes

$$\binom{k+r}{r} \binom{n-r}{(2k+1)-r}.$$

The second binomial coefficient vanishes unless

$$(2k+1)-r\le n-r,$$

which is equivalent to

$$r\le n-k.$$

For those values of $r$,

$$\binom{n-r}{(2k+1)-r} = \binom{n-r}{k}.$$

Therefore

$$\sum_{r=0}^{n-k} \binom{r+k}{r}\binom{n-r}{k} = \binom{n+k+1}{2k+1}.$$

Substituting into the expression for $S_{n,k}$ gives

$$S_{n,k} = (k!)^2 \binom{n+k+1}{2k+1}.$$

Thus

$$\boxed{S_{n,k}=(k!)^2\binom{n+k+1}{2k+1}}.$$

For the original problem, $k=1$, so

$$S_{n,1} = \binom{n+2}{3} = \frac{n(n+1)(n+2)}{6}.$$

Hence

$$\boxed{1\cdot n+2\cdot(n-1)+\cdots+n\cdot1 = \frac{n(n+1)(n+2)}{6}}.$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the conversion of products into binomial coefficients. Starting from

$$(r+1)(r+2)\cdots(r+k),$$

there are exactly $k$ consecutive factors. Multiplying and dividing by $k!$ yields

$$\frac{(r+k)!}{r!} = k!\binom{r+k}{k}.$$

An off-by-one error in either endpoint would replace $r+k$ by $r+k-1$ and destroy the final formula.

The second delicate step is the range of summation in the convolution. Vandermonde's identity is usually written over all integers $r$. Here the term

$$\binom{n-r}{(2k+1)-r}$$

is zero whenever $r>n-k$. Consequently the finite range

$$0\le r\le n-k$$

coincides exactly with the range of nonzero terms. Missing this fact would produce an incorrect truncation.

As a numerical check, take $n=4$, $k=2$. Then

$$S_{4,2} = (1\cdot2)(4\cdot3) + (2\cdot3)(3\cdot2) + (3\cdot4)(2\cdot1) = 24+36+24 = 84.$$

The formula gives

$$(2!)^2\binom{7}{5} = 4\cdot21 = 84,$$

which agrees.

## Alternative Approaches

A purely combinatorial proof of the convolution can replace the algebraic use of Vandermonde's identity. Consider choosing $2k+1$ elements from a set of $n+k+1$ elements. Let $r+k+1$ be the position of the $(k+1)$-st chosen element. Then $r$ elements are chosen among the first $r+k$ positions besides that distinguished element, and $k$ elements are chosen among the last $n-r$ positions. The number of choices is

$$\binom{r+k}{r}\binom{n-r}{k}.$$

Summing over all admissible $r$ counts all $(2k+1)$-subsets, giving

$$\binom{n+k+1}{2k+1}.$$

Substituting this count into the factorial reduction yields the same formula for $S_{n,k}$.

The main approach is preferable because the products naturally convert into binomial coefficients, and the entire sum collapses immediately to a standard convolution identity.
