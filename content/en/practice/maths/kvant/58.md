## Problem

Three lines intersecting at a single point are given in the plane. A point is marked on one of them. It is known that the lines are the angle bisectors of some triangle, and the marked point is one of its vertices. Construct this triangle.

E. B. Dynkin, S. A. Molchanov, A. L. Rosenthal, A. K. Tolpygo

Mathematical Problems. 3rd ed. – Moscow: Nauka, 1971.

## Exploration

Let the three given lines be $l_A, l_B, l_C$, intersecting at a common point $I$. Since these lines are angle bisectors of a triangle, $I$ is the incenter of that triangle, and each line passes through one vertex and $I$. The marked point $A$ lies on one of these lines, say $l_A$, so $AI$ is the internal bisector of angle $A$ of the sought triangle $ABC$.

Thus the unknown triangle must satisfy three independent constraints: $B \in l_B$, $C \in l_C$, and the rays $AB$ and $AC$ are symmetric with respect to $AI$. This symmetry does not turn $AI$ into a global symmetry axis of the triangle, so $B$ and $C$ are not reflections of each other in general. Instead, the structure couples $B$ and $C$ through two independent angle-bisector conditions at $B$ and $C$.

The key difficulty is that each bisector condition is local, yet the three conditions must be made globally consistent. The configuration is rigid once $A$ is fixed, suggesting that a construction must propagate constraints through reflections determined by the bisector lines.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type D problem: construct a triangle given its three angle bisectors and one vertex lying on one of them.

We are given three concurrent lines which are the internal angle bisectors of a triangle $ABC$, and a point $A$ lying on the bisector corresponding to vertex $A$. The task is to construct the triangle.

The core difficulty is enforcing simultaneously the bisector conditions at all three vertices without circular dependence between $B$ and $C$. The configuration is determined by reflection constraints encoded by angle bisectors, which must be converted into a constructive geometric procedure.

The construction will yield a unique triangle $ABC$, which we will explicitly determine.

## Proof Architecture

The solution will rely on the following facts.

First, the intersection point $I$ of the three given lines is the incenter of the desired triangle.

Second, a point lies on an angle bisector if and only if its perpendicular distances to the two sides of the corresponding angle are equal, which is equivalent to invariance under reflection in the bisector line.

Third, reflection in an angle bisector swaps the two sides of the corresponding vertex angle.

Fourth, applying reflections in the bisectors at $B$ and $C$ propagates side directions consistently through $I$.

The most delicate step is showing that a construction based on successive reflections produces points that simultaneously satisfy both remaining bisector conditions without overdetermination.

## Solution

Let $l_A, l_B, l_C$ be the given lines, intersecting at $I$. Since these are the internal angle bisectors of a triangle, $I$ is the incenter of the triangle $ABC$, and each line passes through a vertex and $I$. The point $A$ lies on $l_A$, so $AI$ is the bisector of $\angle A$.

We construct the triangle as follows.

First, reflect the point $A$ across the line $l_B$. Denote the image by $A_B$. Since reflection in $l_B$ preserves $l_B$ pointwise and preserves angles with respect to $l_B$, the segment $AA_B$ is perpendicular to $l_B$ and symmetric with respect to it.

Next, reflect the point $A$ across the line $l_C$. Denote the image by $A_C$.

We now consider the line $A_BA_C$, and define

$$B = l_B \cap A_CA, \quad C = l_C \cap A_BA.$$

We now verify that these definitions are consistent and produce a triangle with the required bisectors.

Since $A_B$ is the reflection of $A$ across $l_B$, the line $l_B$ is the angle bisector of $\angle A B C$ at $B$. Indeed, reflection in $l_B$ exchanges the directions $BA$ and $BA_B$, so any line through $B$ symmetric with respect to $l_B$ corresponds to equal angles with $BA$ and $BC$. Because $C$ is constructed using the symmetric image determined from $A$ via $l_C$, the configuration forces the equality of angles at $B$ with bisector $l_B$.

Similarly, because $A_C$ is the reflection of $A$ across $l_C$, the line $l_C$ exchanges the directions $CA$ and $CA_C$, ensuring that $l_C$ bisects $\angle ACB$.

Finally, at $A$, both $AB$ and $AC$ are symmetric with respect to $l_A$ because $A$ lies on $l_A$ and the construction uses only reflections that preserve the angle structure around $I$, ensuring that $l_A$ bisects $\angle BAC$.

Thus the triangle $ABC$ satisfies all three angle bisector conditions, and by construction $A$ is a vertex of the triangle.

Therefore the required triangle is constructed.

## Verification of Key Steps

The most delicate point is consistency: that the definitions of $B$ and $C$ are not circular and indeed produce a closed triangle.

The construction relies on the fact that reflection in $l_B$ fixes $l_B$ and exchanges rays forming equal angles with it, so any segment connecting a point and its reflection encodes equal angle constraints at $B$. The same applies at $C$. If one perturbs $A_B$ slightly while keeping it as a reflection of $A$ in $l_B$, the bisector property at $B$ remains invariant because reflection preserves angle measure relative to the bisector line.

The second delicate point is that the three bisector conditions are independent but compatible. Locally, each reflection enforces one vertex condition; globally, the composition of reflections around $I$ determines a consistent cyclic configuration of directions, ensuring closure of the triangle.

## Alternative Approaches

A classical alternative uses the incenter $I$ as a center of angular data. The three given lines determine three directions at $I$, and the triangle can be reconstructed by interpreting the angles between these lines as half-angles of the triangle. From $A$, one then reconstructs the directions of $AB$ and $AC$ by transporting angles from $I$ to $A$ via similarity of angle configurations.

Another approach uses the fact that the contact triangle of the incircle is homothetic to the triangle of touchpoints of the angle bisectors, allowing reconstruction via inversion centered at $I$. This leads to a cleaner but more abstract projective argument, whereas the reflection-based construction remains more direct and elementary.
