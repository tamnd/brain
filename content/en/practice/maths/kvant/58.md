## Problem

Three lines intersecting at a single point are given in the plane. A point is marked on one of them. It is known that the lines are the angle bisectors of some triangle, and the marked point is one of its vertices. Construct this triangle.

E. B. Dynkin, S. A. Molchanov, A. L. Rosenthal, A. K. Tolpygo

Mathematical Problems. 3rd ed. – Moscow: Nauka, 1971.

## Exploration

The three given concurrent lines are the angle bisectors of a triangle. Since the angle bisectors of a triangle are concurrent at the incenter, the common intersection point of the three lines is the incenter $I$ of the sought triangle.

A vertex of the triangle is marked on one of the bisectors. Let this marked point be $A$. Then the given bisector through $A$ is the bisector of angle $A$.

The remaining two bisectors must be the bisectors through the other vertices $B$ and $C$. Denote them by $\ell_B$ and $\ell_C$.

The standard relation for a triangle with incenter $I$ is

$$\angle BIC=90^\circ+\frac{\angle A}{2}.$$

Since $\ell_B$ and $\ell_C$ are given, the angle between them is known. Hence $\angle BIC$ is known, and therefore $\angle A$ is known. This already suggests that the sides $AB$ and $AC$ should be determined as the two lines through $A$ making equal angles $\frac{\angle A}{2}$ with the bisector $AI$.

After drawing these two candidate sides, the points $B$ and $C$ would be obtained as intersections with $\ell_B$ and $\ell_C$. The crucial point is to prove that the bisector at $B$ is indeed $\ell_B$ and the bisector at $C$ is indeed $\ell_C$.

Suppose $B=\ell_B\cap AB$. Since $I$ lies on $\ell_B$, the line $BI$ is exactly $\ell_B$. If $BI$ is to be the bisector at $B$, we need

$$\angle ABI=\angle IBC.$$

The first angle is determined by the construction. Since $AI$ bisects angle $A$,

$$\angle BAI=\frac A2.$$

In triangle $AIB$,

$$\angle ABI =180^\circ-\angle AIB-\frac A2.$$

Now

$$\angle AIB=180^\circ-\angle BIC =90^\circ-\frac A2,$$

hence

$$\angle ABI =90^\circ.$$

This computation is impossible, so something is wrong. The error comes from taking the supplementary angle incorrectly. Recomputing carefully,

$$\angle AIB =180^\circ-\left(90^\circ+\frac C2+\frac B2\right),$$

is not convenient.

A better approach is to use the well-known incenter relation

$$\angle AIB=90^\circ+\frac C2.$$

Then

$$\angle ABI =180^\circ-\left(90^\circ+\frac C2\right)-\frac A2 =\frac B2.$$

This is exactly what is needed, because in triangle $ABC$ the angle at $B$ equals $B$.

Thus the construction is likely correct. The step most likely to hide an error is the verification that $BI$ and $CI$ are angle bisectors, because it requires the correct use of the incenter angle formulas.

## Problem Understanding

Three concurrent lines are given. They are known to be the three internal angle bisectors of some triangle. A point $A$ marked on one of the lines is known to be a vertex of that triangle. The task is to construct the triangle.

This is a Type D problem.

The common intersection point of the three bisectors is the incenter $I$ of the sought triangle. Since the marked vertex lies on one of the bisectors, that line must be the bisector of angle $A$. The angle at $A$ can be recovered from the angle between the other two bisectors by the formula

$$\angle BIC=90^\circ+\frac A2.$$

After determining $\angle A$, the sides through $A$ can be drawn, and the remaining vertices are obtained as intersections with the corresponding bisectors.

The constructed triangle should be the unique one because the incenter and all three angle bisectors are prescribed.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1. If $I$ is the common point of the three given lines and the marked vertex is $A$, then the angle at $A$ is determined by

$$A=2(\angle BIC-90^\circ).$$

This follows from the standard incenter formula $\angle BIC=90^\circ+\frac A2$.

Lemma 2. The sides $AB$ and $AC$ are uniquely determined as the two rays through $A$ making angles $\frac A2$ with the bisector $AI$.

A bisector and the full angle uniquely determine its sides.

Lemma 3. If $B=\ell_B\cap AB$ and $C=\ell_C\cap AC$, then $BI$ bisects $\angle ABC$ and $CI$ bisects $\angle ACB$.

This is proved by angle computations using $\angle AIB=90^\circ+\frac C2$ and $\angle AIC=90^\circ+\frac B2$.

The hardest direction is Lemma 3, because it is the step that verifies that the constructed triangle actually has the prescribed bisectors.

## Solution

Let the three given concurrent lines meet at $I$. Since they are the angle bisectors of some triangle, $I$ is the incenter of that triangle.

Let $A$ be the marked point. The given line passing through $A$ must be the bisector of angle $A$. Denote this line by $\ell_A$. Let the other two given lines be $\ell_B$ and $\ell_C$.

Since $\ell_B$ and $\ell_C$ are the bisectors through the other two vertices, the angle between them equals $\angle BIC$. For every triangle,

$$\angle BIC=90^\circ+\frac A2.$$

Hence the angle

$$A=2(\angle BIC-90^\circ)$$

is constructible from the given data.

Now construct the two rays through $A$ which form angles $\frac A2$ with the line $\ell_A$, one on each side of $\ell_A$. Call these rays $AB$ and $AC$. By construction, $\ell_A$ bisects the angle between them.

Define

$$B=AB\cap\ell_B,\qquad C=AC\cap\ell_C.$$

This determines a triangle $ABC$.

It remains to verify that the three given lines are precisely its angle bisectors.

The line $\ell_A$ bisects angle $A$ by construction.

Since $B$ lies on $\ell_B$ and $I$ lies on $\ell_B$, the line $BI$ is exactly $\ell_B$.

Let the angles of triangle $ABC$ be $A,B,C$. Since $AI$ is the bisector at $A$,

$$\angle BAI=\frac A2.$$

For an incenter,

$$\angle AIB=90^\circ+\frac C2.$$

Applying the angle sum in triangle $AIB$ gives

$$\angle ABI =180^\circ-\left(90^\circ+\frac C2\right)-\frac A2$$
