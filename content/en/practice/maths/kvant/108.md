## Problem

a) Prove that a line that divides a given triangle into two polygons of equal area and equal perimeter passes through the incenter of the triangle.

b) Prove the analogous statement for an arbitrary polygon into which a circle can be inscribed.

Yu. I. Ionin

All-Union School Mathematics Olympiad (V)

## Exploration

For a triangle, let a line intersect the boundary at points $X$ and $Y$, dividing the triangle into two polygons. The segment $XY$ belongs to the boundary of both parts. If the perimeters of the two parts are equal, the contributions of $XY$ cancel. Hence the portions of the original boundary belonging to the two parts must have equal total length.

This observation is much stronger than it first appears. The line cuts the boundary of the triangle into two boundary arcs. Equal perimeters imply that these two arcs have equal length, each equal to half the perimeter of the triangle.

Suppose the line meets sides $AB$ and $AC$ at points $X$ and $Y$. Then one boundary arc is $BX+BY+BC$, while the other is $AX+AY$. Equality gives

$$BX+BY+BC=AX+AY.$$

Using $AB=AX+BX$ and $AC=AY+CY$,

$$AB+AC-2(AX+AY)=BC,$$

hence

$$AX+AY=\frac{AB+AC-BC}{2}.$$

The right-hand side equals $s-BC$, where $s$ is the semiperimeter.

The area condition should now be translated into distances from the incenter. Let $I$ be the incenter and $r$ the inradius. Since $I$ is equidistant from the sides $AB$ and $AC$, the area of quadrilateral $AXYI$ is

$$\frac r2(AX+AY).$$

Substituting the value above yields

$$[AXYI]=\frac r2(s-BC).$$

The area of triangle $AIC$ plus triangle $AIB$ is also

$$\frac r2(AB+AC).$$

Subtracting triangle $ABC$ of area $rs$ gives

$$[AIC]+[AIB]-[ABC] =\frac r2(AB+AC)-rs =\frac r2(s-BC).$$

Thus

$$[AXYI]=[AIC]+[AIB]-[ABC].$$

After simplifying this identity, one obtains

$$[IXY]=[ABC]-[AXY]-[BIC].$$

Since the line gives equal areas, $[AXY]=\frac12[ABC]$. Also

$$[BIC]=\frac r2(BC+s-BC)=\frac{rs}{2} =\frac12[ABC].$$

Hence $[IXY]=0$, which forces $I$ to lie on $XY$.

The crucial point is proving rigorously that the perimeter condition converts into the relation

$$AX+AY=s-BC,$$

and then expressing the resulting area identity entirely in terms of $r$ and side lengths.

For a tangential polygon, the same perimeter observation remains true. The line cuts the boundary into two arcs of equal length. Let the incircle have center $I$ and radius $r$. The area of a region bounded by a boundary arc of length $L$ and the two segments joining its endpoints to $I$ equals $\frac r2L$, because the region decomposes into triangles with altitude $r$ to the corresponding sides. Equal arc lengths then imply equal such areas. Combining this with the assumption that the two polygonal parts have equal areas should again force $I$ onto the cutting line.

## Problem Understanding

We are given a triangle in part (a), and more generally a polygon admitting an inscribed circle in part (b). A line cuts the figure into two polygons having equal area and equal perimeter. We must prove that the cutting line passes through the center of the inscribed circle.

This is a Type B problem. The statement is already specified and must be proved.

The core difficulty is to extract a geometric consequence from the equal perimeter condition. The decisive observation is that equal perimeters imply that the two boundary arcs of the original figure determined by the cutting line have equal lengths. For a tangential figure, lengths of boundary arcs are converted into areas by using the common distance $r$ from the incenter to every side.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma states that if a segment $XY$ cuts a polygon into two parts with equal perimeters, then the two boundary arcs of the original polygon joining $X$ and $Y$ have equal lengths. This follows because the common segment $XY$ contributes equally to both perimeters.

The second lemma states that in a polygon with incircle center $I$ and radius $r$, the area of the region bounded by a boundary arc of length $L$ together with the segments from its endpoints to $I$ equals $\frac r2L$. This follows by decomposing the region into triangles having altitude $r$.

The third lemma states that if the two boundary arcs determined by $X$ and $Y$ have equal lengths, then the corresponding regions constructed in the second lemma have equal areas.

The final step shows that the equal area condition for the two polygonal parts implies that the triangle $IXY$ has zero area, hence $I$ lies on the line $XY$.

The lemma most likely to fail under insufficient scrutiny is the second one, because it requires a careful decomposition into triangles and a correct accounting of areas.

## Solution

Let a line intersect the boundary of a tangential polygon $P$ at points $X$ and $Y$, dividing $P$ into two polygons $P_1$ and $P_2$. Let $I$ be the center of the inscribed circle and let $r$ be its radius.

Denote by $\Gamma_1$ and $\Gamma_2$ the two boundary arcs of $P$ joining $X$ and $Y$. Let their lengths be $L_1$ and $L_2$.

Since the perimeters of $P_1$ and $P_2$ are equal, and each contains the segment $XY$, we have

$$L_1+XY=L_2+XY.$$

Hence

$$L_1=L_2.$$

For each arc $\Gamma_k$, consider the region $R_k$ bounded by $\Gamma_k$ together with the segments $IX$ and $IY$.

Every side of $P$ is tangent to the incircle, so the distance from $I$ to every side equals $r$. Decompose $R_k$ into triangles having vertex $I$ and bases equal to the side segments contained in $\Gamma_k$. If the total length of $\Gamma_k$ is $L_k$, then the sum of the areas of these triangles is

$$[R_k] = \frac r2\,L_k.$$

Since $L_1=L_2$, it follows that

$$[R_1]=[R_2].$$

The regions $R_1$ and $R_2$ cover the whole polygon together with the triangle $IXY$. More precisely,

$$[R_1]+[R_2]=[P]+[IXY].$$

Indeed, every point of $P$ belongs to at least one of the regions, and their overlap is exactly the triangle $IXY$.

Let the two polygons into which the line $XY$ divides $P$ be $P_1$ and $P_2$. By hypothesis,

$$[P_1]=[P_2].$$

The region $R_1$ is obtained from $P_1$ by adjoining the triangle $IXY$ if $I$ lies on the side of $XY$ containing $P_2$, and by removing that triangle if $I$ lies on the side containing $P_1$. In either case,

$$[R_1]-[R_2] = [P_1]-[P_2]\pm 2[IXY].$$

Since $[P_1]=[P_2]$ and $[R_1]=[R_2]$, we obtain

$$2[IXY]=0.$$

Therefore

$$[IXY]=0.$$

The points $I$, $X$, and $Y$ are collinear. Hence the cutting line $XY$ passes through the center $I$ of the inscribed circle.

This proves part (b).

Part (a) is the special case in which $P$ is a triangle. Every triangle possesses an inscribed circle, and its center is the incenter. Applying part (b) to a triangle shows that any line dividing the triangle into two polygons of equal area and equal perimeter passes through the incenter.

This completes the proof.

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the deduction $L_1=L_2$. Let the perimeters of the two parts be $p_1$ and $p_2$. Since the segment $XY$ appears in the boundary of both parts,

$$p_1=L_1+XY,\qquad p_2=L_2+XY.$$

The equality $p_1=p_2$ gives $L_1=L_2$. Forgetting that $XY$ belongs to both perimeters would produce an incorrect relation.

The second delicate step is the formula

$$[R_k]=\frac r2L_k.$$

Suppose $\Gamma_k$ consists of side segments of lengths $\ell_1,\ldots,\ell_m$. Joining $I$ to the endpoints of those segments decomposes $R_k$ into triangles whose heights are all $r$. Their total area equals

$$\sum_{j=1}^{m}\frac{r\ell_j}{2} = \frac r2\sum_{j=1}^{m}\ell_j = \frac r2L_k.$$

The argument depends on the tangency condition; without a common distance $r$ the formula fails.

The final delicate step is identifying the overlap of $R_1$ and $R_2$. Each region contains the segments $IX$ and $IY$. Their common part is precisely the triangle $IXY$. Consequently

$$[R_1]+[R_2]=[P]+[IXY].$$

Any larger overlap would invalidate the area computation. The boundary arcs lie on opposite sides of the segment $XY$, so no additional overlap occurs.

## Alternative Approaches

For the triangle, one may work entirely with side lengths and the inradius. If the cutting line meets two sides at points $X$ and $Y$, the equality of perimeters yields an explicit linear relation among $AX$ and $AY$. Translating that relation into areas using the inradius gives an identity implying $[IXY]=0$. This proof is computational and specific to triangles.

The tangential polygon argument used above isolates the geometric mechanism behind the result. Equal perimeters become equal boundary arc lengths, and equal boundary arc lengths become equal areas through the formula $\frac r2L$. Once this correspondence is established, the conclusion follows from a short area comparison. The method applies uniformly to every polygon possessing an inscribed circle, which makes it the more natural proof.
