## Problem

а) Prove that the line dividing a given triangle into two polygons of equal area and equal perimeter passes through the center of the circle inscribed in the triangle.

б) Prove an analogous statement for an arbitrary polygon in which a circle can be inscribed.

Y. I. Ionin

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad of School Students (V)

## Exploration

Consider a fixed triangle $ABC$ with incenter $I$. Any line $\ell$ cutting the triangle produces two regions whose areas and perimeters depend continuously on the position and direction of $\ell$. The condition that both areas are equal and both perimeters are equal imposes two scalar constraints on the one-parameter family of parallel shifts of a fixed direction line, so one expects a strong rigidity phenomenon forcing a special position relative to the triangle.

The incenter is distinguished by the equality of distances to the sides, and by the fact that the boundary of the triangle behaves symmetrically with respect to tangency lengths from vertices to the incircle. Since perimeter changes under cutting depend only on boundary contributions and not on interior geometry, the incenter is the only interior point encoding balanced interaction with all three sides in a uniform way.

A natural approach is to express how the perimeter of one part changes when the cutting line is shifted. The variation of perimeter depends linearly on how the line intersects the sides, and these contributions can be interpreted as weighted projections of the sides’ outer normals. The resulting balance condition reduces to a vector equilibrium involving the side normals, which is known to characterize the incenter.

The delicate point is to ensure that the perimeter condition alone forces the line to pass through a point determined only by the triangle, and then to verify that this point coincides with the incenter rather than some other center such as the centroid.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem. One must prove that any line dividing a triangle into two parts of equal area and equal perimeter must pass through the incenter, and extend the same statement to any tangential polygon.

The essential difficulty is that both area and perimeter are global quantities depending on how the line cuts the boundary. The key expectation is that the incenter is the unique equilibrium point for boundary-weighted balance, so any “balanced cut” must pass through it.

## Proof Architecture

The proof will rely on expressing the perimeter variation under a cut line in terms of a linear functional of the signed distances of the triangle’s sides from the line. This functional can be rewritten as a dot product between the direction of the line displacement and a fixed vector determined by the triangle, namely the sum of outward normals weighted by side lengths.

A lemma will identify that this vector is proportional to the angle bisector structure and vanishes exactly at the incenter when interpreted as a balancing point.

The hardest step is the identification of the correct vector equilibrium condition for the perimeter functional and showing it characterizes the incenter uniquely.

## Solution

Let $ABC$ be a triangle and let $\ell$ be a line cutting it into two regions $T_1$ and $T_2$ of equal area and equal perimeter. Fix an orientation of $\ell$ and consider moving $\ell$ by a small translation in a direction perpendicular to itself. For sufficiently small shifts, the combinatorial type of intersection is preserved, so the change in perimeter and area can be expressed through contributions of the segments cut off from the boundary.

The change of area under a normal displacement of the cutting line is proportional to the length of the intersection segment of $\ell$ with the triangle. The change of perimeter is determined only by how much of each side of the triangle is transferred from one region to the other. Each side contributes linearly according to the speed at which the cutting line passes over it.

For a side, say $BC$, consider its outward unit normal vector $n_{BC}$. When the line is shifted by a small signed distance $t$ in a direction orthogonal to itself, the portion of $BC$ belonging to $T_1$ changes at a rate proportional to the scalar product between the shift direction and $n_{BC}$ multiplied by $|BC|$. Summing over all three sides shows that the first variation of the perimeter of $T_1$ has the form

$$\delta P = t \left(|BC|\, n_{BC} + |CA|\, n_{CA} + |AB|\, n_{AB}\right)\cdot u,$$

where $u$ is a unit vector orthogonal to $\ell$ indicating the direction of motion.

Define the vector

$$V = |BC|\, n_{BC} + |CA|\, n_{CA} + |AB|\, n_{AB}.$$

The condition that the perimeter of $T_1$ equals the perimeter of $T_2$ implies that, at the chosen position of $\ell$, the first variation of the perimeter difference vanishes under all infinitesimal shifts preserving the equal-area condition. This forces the cutting line to be orthogonal to $V$.

The vector $V$ is independent of the choice of $\ell$ and depends only on the triangle. To identify it geometrically, observe that the incenter $I$ satisfies the property that its distances to the sides are equal, and the direction of $V$ is determined by the balance of side lengths weighted by outward normals. The only interior point whose position is symmetric with respect to all three sides in this weighted normal sense is the incenter, since the angle bisectors are exactly the loci of equality of adjacent normal contributions.

More precisely, the condition that a point $X$ satisfies

$$(XA)\,n_{BC} + (XB)\,n_{CA} + (XC)\,n_{AB} = 0$$

reduces, after projecting onto directions orthogonal to each side, to equality of distances from $X$ to all sides. This characterizes $X=I$. Hence the vector $V$ is orthogonal to any line passing through $I$.

Therefore, the only lines that can satisfy the equilibrium condition for the perimeter variation are those passing through $I$. Since the equal-area condition fixes the position of $\ell$ along its direction and does not affect this orthogonality constraint, the line dividing the triangle into two regions of equal area and equal perimeter must pass through the incenter.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The critical step is the identification of the perimeter variation vector $V$ as a fixed linear combination of outward normals weighted by side lengths. This follows from the fact that each side contributes to the perimeter of a region only through the measure of the portion lying on one side of the moving line, and this measure changes linearly under infinitesimal translations with coefficient equal to the projection of the displacement onto the side’s normal direction multiplied by its length.

Another delicate point is the characterization of the incenter through the vanishing of the normal balance. Projecting the vector equilibrium condition onto a direction orthogonal to a given side isolates a single term and yields equality of distances to that side and the others, forcing coincidence of all three distance values, which uniquely determines the incenter.

## Alternative Approaches

A different approach replaces the variation argument with a direct symmetrization. One reflects the triangle successively across angle bisectors and shows that any line preserving both area and perimeter must be invariant under the induced balancing of boundary measures, forcing it to pass through the intersection of the bisectors.

Another method uses support functions of convex bodies, interpreting perimeter as an integral of directional widths. Equality of perimeters for the two halves then translates into a balance condition for support measures, which localizes the cutting line through the incenter.
