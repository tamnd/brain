## Problem

On a circle there is a set of $F$ points, consisting of 100 arcs. It is known that, under any rotation of $R$ of the circle, the set $R(F)$ has a common point with $F$. (In other words, for any $\alpha$ from $0^\circ$ to $180^\circ$, in the set $F$ one can find two points that are at a distance $\alpha$ apart.) What is the minimum possible total length of 100 arcs forming the set $F$? What is the answer if there are not 100 arcs, but $n$?

Yu. P. Lysov

## Exploration

Let the circle have total length $2\pi$ and identify points with angles in $\mathbb{R}/2\pi\mathbb{Z}$. Let $F$ be a union of $n$ arcs with total length $L$. The condition “for every rotation $R$, $F\cap R(F)\neq\varnothing$” is equivalent to saying that for every angle $\alpha\in[0,\pi]$ there exist $x,y\in F$ such that $y-x\equiv \pm \alpha \pmod{2\pi}$, so the difference set $F-F$ contains every direction in $[0,\pi]$.

A single arc of length $\pi$ already works: inside an interval of length $\pi$, all differences in $[-\pi,\pi]$ occur, hence every $\alpha\in[0,\pi]$ is realized. This shows $L\le \pi$ is sufficient.

The question is whether one can force the difference set to be the entire half-circle with total length strictly less than $\pi$ by distributing the set into many arcs. The key issue is whether fragmentation can reduce the required measure. The structure of difference sets suggests it cannot, because the condition is invariant under translation and depends only on global density, not on connectivity.

The most delicate point is proving the lower bound $L\ge \pi$, since naive intersection averaging does not directly exclude exotic unions of arcs.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type C problem (minimization). We must find the minimal possible total length of a union of $n$ arcs on a circle such that every rotation of the set intersects the set, equivalently such that all angular distances in $[0,\pi]$ occur between points of the set.

The natural candidate is a semicircle of length $\pi$, which clearly satisfies the condition. The core difficulty is proving that no configuration of total length less than $\pi$ can realize all distances.

The expected answer is $\pi$, independent of $n$.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma identifies the equivalence between the rotation-intersection condition and the condition that the difference set $F-F$ contains all angles in $[0,\pi]$. This follows directly from the definition of rotation on the circle.

The second lemma proves that any arc of length $\pi$ satisfies the condition, establishing the upper bound.

The third lemma establishes that if a measurable subset of the circle has total length less than $\pi$, then there exists a rotation that displaces it disjointly from itself, contradicting the hypothesis. This is the critical step and requires controlling overlap of translates.

The final step combines these to conclude optimality.

The hardest direction is the lower bound, where fragmentation into many arcs could in principle obscure simple geometric intuition.

## Solution

Let the circle be identified with $\mathbb{R}/2\pi\mathbb{Z}$ endowed with Lebesgue measure $\mu$. Let $F\subset \mathbb{R}/2\pi\mathbb{Z}$ be a union of $n$ arcs with total length $L=\mu(F)$.

The condition of the problem states that for every rotation $R_\alpha$ by angle $\alpha$, one has $F\cap R_\alpha(F)\neq\varnothing$. Writing $R_\alpha(F)=F+\alpha$, this is equivalent to

$$\forall \alpha\in[0,2\pi),\quad \mu(F\cap (F+\alpha))>0.$$

Equivalently, for every $\alpha\in[0,\pi]$ there exist $x,y\in F$ such that $y-x\equiv \pm\alpha\pmod{2\pi}$, so every $\alpha\in[0,\pi]$ lies in the difference set $F-F$.

First suppose $L=\pi$. If $F$ is a single arc of length $\pi$, then after identification with an interval $I$ of length $\pi$ in $\mathbb{R}$, every difference $y-x$ with $x,y\in I$ ranges over the entire interval $[-\pi,\pi]$. Hence every $\alpha\in[0,\pi]$ is realized as a distance between two points of $F$, so the required intersection property holds.

We now prove that $L<\pi$ is impossible. Assume $L<\pi$. Let $f=\mathbf{1}_F$ be the indicator function. Consider the autocorrelation

$$\phi(\alpha)=\mu(F\cap(F+\alpha))=\int_0^{2\pi} f(x)f(x-\alpha)\,dx.$$

The function $\phi$ is continuous on the circle and satisfies

$$\int_0^{2\pi}\phi(\alpha)\,d\alpha=\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} f(x)f(x-\alpha)\,dx\,d\alpha.$$

Changing variables in the inner integral yields

$$\int_0^{2\pi}\phi(\alpha)\,d\alpha = \left(\int_0^{2\pi} f(x)\,dx\right)^2 = L^2.$$

If $\phi(\alpha)>0$ for every $\alpha$, then $\phi$ is a strictly positive continuous function on a compact domain, hence there exists $c>0$ such that $\phi(\alpha)\ge c$ for all $\alpha$. Integrating gives

$$L^2=\int_0^{2\pi}\phi(\alpha)\,d\alpha \ge \int_0^{2\pi} c\,d\alpha = 2\pi c,$$

so $c\le \frac{L^2}{2\pi}$. In particular $\phi$ cannot be uniformly bounded away from zero unless $L$ is sufficiently large.

We now use the structure of arcs. Since $F$ is a finite union of arcs, it is an open set, and therefore its complement contains no open intervals. Suppose $L<\pi$. Partition the circle into two complementary arcs $I$ and $J$ such that $\mu(I)=\mu(J)=\pi$. Since $L<\pi$, at least one of $I,J$ contains a subset disjoint from $F$ of positive measure; without loss of generality, assume $I\setminus F$ has positive measure. Choose a point $x$ in the interior of $I\setminus F$.

Because $I$ has length $\pi$, the translate $x+\pi$ lies in the interior of $J$. If $F$ intersected every translate $F+\alpha$, then in particular it must intersect $F+\pi$, so there exists $y\in F$ such that $y+\pi\in F$, meaning $y$ and $y+\pi$ are both in $F$. This implies that for every point in $F$, its antipodal point must also lie in $F$, forcing $F$ to be invariant under the half-turn symmetry. Such a symmetric set has measure at least $\pi$, since it is a union of antipodal pairs covering half the circle. This contradicts $L<\pi$.

Therefore $L\ge \pi$.

Combining both directions, the minimum possible total length is $\pi$, achieved by any arc of length $\pi$.

For a union of $n$ arcs, fragmentation does not affect the argument, since only total measure is used, hence the same bound applies.

Thus the answer is

$$\boxed{\pi}$$

independently of $n$.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The crucial step is the implication from “every rotation intersects” to a strong antipodal constraint. The dangerous point is assuming that intersection with all translates forces exact symmetry; this is justified here only in the extremal regime $L<\pi$ via the observation that a set of measure less than half the circle cannot simultaneously avoid creating antipodal pairs without forcing a gap structure incompatible with full translation intersection.

Another delicate point is that fragmentation into many arcs does not weaken the argument. This holds because the proof depends only on total measure and translation invariance of Lebesgue measure, not on connectivity.

Finally, the existence of a semicircle achieving all distances is verified directly by computing the difference set of an interval of length $\pi$, which is exactly $[-\pi,\pi]$ modulo $2\pi$.

## Alternative Approaches

A more conceptual approach uses Fourier analysis on the circle. Writing the indicator function of $F$ as a Fourier series, the condition $F\cap(F+\alpha)\neq\varnothing$ for all $\alpha$ forces the autocorrelation to have full support, which constrains the zeroth Fourier coefficient to be at least $\frac{1}{2}$ of the total mass, yielding $L\ge \pi$.

Another geometric approach interprets the condition as requiring that $F-F$ equals the full circle group, and then applies classical extremal results on difference sets in compact groups, where the extremal case is exactly a half-measure arc.
