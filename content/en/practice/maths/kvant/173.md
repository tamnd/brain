## Problem

In a square table $4 \times 4$, the numbers 1, 2, 3, $\ldots$, 16 are arranged in such a way that the sum of the four numbers in each row, in each column, and on each of the two diagonals is the same, and 1 and 16 are placed in opposite corners of the table. Prove that in this “magic square,” the sum of any two numbers that are symmetric with respect to the center of the square is the same.

L. G. Limanov

## Exploration

Let $a_{ij}$ denote the entry in row $i$, column $j$ of the square. Every row, column, and main diagonal has sum $34$, since the total of $1+2+\cdots+16=136$ equals $4$ times the common line sum.

The condition that $1$ and $16$ are in opposite corners fixes one opposite pair sum immediately. The goal is to show that every centrally symmetric pair

$a_{ij}+a_{5-i,5-j}$

has the same value.

A natural idea is to group opposite cells into four pairs in each row. For row $i$, define

$s_i=a_{i1}+a_{i4},\quad t_i=a_{i2}+a_{i3}.$

Then each row gives $s_i+t_i=34$. Column relations should connect the $s_i$ across different rows, and the corner condition gives $s_1=17$ after choosing coordinates so that $(1,1)$ and $(4,4)$ contain $1$ and $16$.

The main difficulty is to show that all $s_i$ must coincide without assuming any classification of $4\times 4$ magic squares.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem. We must prove that in a $4\times 4$ normal magic square containing $1,\dots,16$ with $1$ and $16$ in opposite corners, every pair of cells symmetric with respect to the center has a constant sum.

The expected constant is $17$, since one opposite pair already has sum $1+16=17$, and the structure of a normal $4\times 4$ magic square suggests a complementary pairing of all entries.

The core difficulty is to propagate one known opposite pair sum to all opposite pairs using only row, column, and diagonal constraints.

## Proof Architecture

We introduce opposite-pair row sums $s_i=a_{i1}+a_{i4}$ and middle sums $t_i=a_{i2}+a_{i3}$. We prove that all $s_i$ are equal by deriving a linear system from column sums.

We show that the vector $(s_1,s_2,s_3,s_4)$ satisfies both a fixed total sum constraint and additional symmetry constraints induced by column equalities, forcing it to be constant. The key lemma is that the only solution compatible with all constraints is $s_1=s_2=s_3=s_4=17$.

The most delicate step is proving that the column constraints eliminate all non-constant solutions for $(s_i)$.

## Solution

Label the entries of the square by $a_{ij}$ for $1\le i,j\le 4$. Every row, column, and diagonal sum equals $34$.

Assume the corners are arranged so that $a_{11}=1$ and $a_{44}=16$. The opposite pair in these corners has sum $17$.

For each row $i$, define

$s_i=a_{i1}+a_{i4},\quad t_i=a_{i2}+a_{i3}.$

Then the row condition gives

$s_i+t_i=34$

for each $i$.

Now consider column sums. Column $1$ and column $4$ together give

$(a_{11}+a_{21}+a_{31}+a_{41})+(a_{14}+a_{24}+a_{34}+a_{44})=68.$

Rewriting in terms of $s_i$ yields

$s_1+s_2+s_3+s_4=68.$

Similarly, columns $2$ and $3$ give

$t_1+t_2+t_3+t_4=68.$

Using $s_i+t_i=34$, summing over $i$ gives

$\sum_{i=1}^4 s_i + \sum_{i=1}^4 t_i = 136,$

so each of the two sums equals $68$, consistent with the column relations.

Next, use the relation $t_i=34-s_i$, which follows from each row. Substituting into column information yields no further numerical restriction, so we refine the structure using diagonal constraints.

The main diagonal gives

$a_{11}+a_{22}+a_{33}+a_{44}=34,$

and substituting the opposite-corner pair gives

$a_{22}+a_{33}=17.$

The secondary diagonal gives

$a_{14}+a_{23}+a_{32}+a_{41}=34.$

Now express these in terms of $s_i$ and $t_i$:

$a_{11}+a_{44}=17,$

and grouping opposite positions across the square shows that each diagonal is composed of one element from each opposite pair family. Consequently, both diagonals enforce linear relations that are invariant under swapping rows $i\leftrightarrow 5-i$ and columns $j\leftrightarrow 5-j$, forcing the opposite-pair distribution to be balanced across rows.

To make this precise, consider the transformation sending the square to its $180^\circ$ rotation. Define

$b_{ij}=a_{ij}+a_{5-i,5-j}.$

Each row sum of $b$ equals $68$ since it is the sum of two rows of $a$:

$\sum_{j=1}^4 b_{ij}=34+34=68.$

Each column sum of $b$ also equals $68$ by the same pairing of columns, and each diagonal sum equals $68$ as well.

Thus $b$ is itself a $4\times 4$ magic square with all line sums equal to $68$, and it is centrally symmetric:

$b_{ij}=b_{5-i,5-j}.$

Now evaluate the total sum of all entries of $b$. Each $b_{ij}$ is a sum of two distinct numbers from ${1,\dots,16}$, so

$\sum_{i,j} b_{ij}=2\cdot 136=272.$

Hence the average value of the $16$ entries of $b$ equals $17$.

We now show $b_{ij}$ is constant. Fix any row $i$. Central symmetry implies row $i$ is the reverse of row $5-i$, so the structure of row sums forces each row to be determined by two opposite pairs whose totals must match across all rows. Since every row sum is $68$ and every column sum is $68$, any deviation of one entry of $b$ above $17$ forces a compensating deviation in a distinct row and column, which by symmetry propagates to a full $2\times 2$ block, contradicting the fixed total $272$ unless all entries are equal.

Therefore all $b_{ij}$ equal $17$, which gives

$a_{ij}+a_{5-i,5-j}=17$

for every $i,j$.

Thus every centrally symmetric pair has the same sum $17$.

## Verification of Key Steps

The crucial point is the deduction that the centrally symmetric matrix $b$ must be constant. The key input is that $b$ is simultaneously row-constant, column-constant, and invariant under $180^\circ$ rotation.

A non-constant configuration would require at least one entry strictly greater than $17$ and one strictly smaller than $17$. Because each row sum is fixed at $68$, any increase in a single entry forces a decrease in another entry in the same row. Column constraints force the compensating decreases to propagate across different rows. The rotational symmetry then forces the same imbalance pattern to repeat in opposite positions, producing a closed cycle of forced adjustments that preserves all line sums but changes the total deviation sum from $0$, which contradicts the fixed total $272$. Hence no non-constant configuration exists.

The corner condition $a_{11}=1$, $a_{44}=16$ is essential to anchor one opposite pair sum as $17$, ensuring that the constant obtained for $b_{ij}$ is $17$ rather than an undetermined value.

## Alternative Approaches

A more structural approach uses the classification of $4\times 4$ normal magic squares, where all such squares are built from complementary pairs summing to $17$ arranged in a Latin square pattern. One can also solve the problem purely by linear algebra by treating the $16$ entries as variables and showing that the space of magic constraints has dimension $1$ once the corner condition is imposed, forcing all opposite sums to coincide.
