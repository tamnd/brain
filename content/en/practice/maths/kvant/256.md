## Problem

A polygon is circumscribed about a circle. The points of tangency of its sides with the circle serve as the vertices of a second polygon inscribed in this circle. Prove that the product of the distances from an arbitrary point $M$ on the circle to the sides of one polygon is equal to the product of the distances from this point to the sides of the second polygon.

(By the distance from a point $M$ to a side we mean the distance to the line containing that side.)

A. N. Chernyshev

## Exploration

Let the circle be the unit circle with center $O$ and let each point on it be represented by a unit complex number $e^{i\theta}$. Write $M = e^{i\theta}$. Let the tangency points of the circumscribed polygon be $T_k = e^{i\alpha_k}$ in cyclic order.

Each side of the circumscribed polygon is tangent to the circle at $T_k$, hence it is the tangent line at $T_k$. The second polygon is formed by the points $T_k$, so its sides are the chords $T_kT_{k+1}$.

The problem compares products of distances from $M$ to two different families of lines: tangents at $T_k$ and chords $T_kT_{k+1}$. The natural strategy is to express both distances in trigonometric form in terms of the angles $\theta$, $\alpha_k$, and then compare products. The key expectation is that both products reduce to expressions involving $\sin\frac{\theta-\alpha_k}{2}$, with different but canceling denominators.

The most delicate point is ensuring that the product over chord denominators is independent of $M$, and that the remaining factors match exactly the tangent-line expression.

## Problem Understanding

The problem asks to prove an identity between two products of distances from a point $M$ on a circle to two families of lines derived from a tangential polygon and the inscribed polygon formed by its tangency points.

This is a Type B problem, since it asks to prove a statement.

The central difficulty is to convert geometric distances to a uniform analytic form on the circle and then show a nontrivial cancellation of terms in the product over all sides.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma expresses the distance from a unit-circle point $M=e^{i\theta}$ to the tangent at $T_k=e^{i\alpha_k}$ as $2\sin^2\frac{\theta-\alpha_k}{2}$.

The second lemma expresses the distance from $M$ to the chord $T_kT_{k+1}$ as a product $\dfrac{2\sin\frac{\theta-\alpha_k}{2}\sin\frac{\theta-\alpha_{k+1}}{2}}{\sin\frac{\alpha_{k+1}-\alpha_k}{2}}$.

The third lemma shows that the product over all chords has a denominator independent of $M$ and that each factor $\sin\frac{\theta-\alpha_k}{2}$ appears exactly twice in the numerator.

The fourth lemma compares the resulting expressions and establishes equality.

The most delicate point is the chord distance formula and its exact factorization into endpoint contributions.

## Solution

Place the circle in the complex plane with center $O=0$ and radius $1$. Every point on the circle has the form $e^{i\theta}$. Let the tangency points of the circumscribed polygon be $T_k=e^{i\alpha_k}$, ordered cyclically.

The side of the circumscribed polygon tangent at $T_k$ is the tangent line at $T_k$. Its equation is

$$\operatorname{Re}(\overline{T_k}z)=1.$$

For $M=e^{i\theta}$, the signed distance from $M$ to this line equals

$$\operatorname{Re}(\overline{T_k}M)-1.$$

Since $\overline{T_k}M=e^{i(\theta-\alpha_k)}$, we obtain

$$\operatorname{Re}(\overline{T_k}M)-1=\cos(\theta-\alpha_k)-1=-2\sin^2\frac{\theta-\alpha_k}{2}.$$

Hence the distance is

$$d(M, T_k\text{-tangent})=2\sin^2\frac{\theta-\alpha_k}{2}.$$

The product of distances from $M$ to all sides of the circumscribed polygon equals

$$\prod_k 2\sin^2\frac{\theta-\alpha_k}{2}.$$

We now compute the distance from $M$ to the chord $T_kT_{k+1}$. The line through two unit points $e^{i\alpha_k}$ and $e^{i\alpha_{k+1}}$ can be written in determinant form; the distance from $z$ to this line is

$$d(z, T_kT_{k+1}) =\frac{\left|\operatorname{Im}\big((T_{k+1}-T_k)\overline{(z-T_k)}\big)\right|}{|T_{k+1}-T_k|}.$$

Substituting $z=e^{i\theta}$, and using the identity $|e^{ix}-e^{iy}|=2\left|\sin\frac{x-y}{2}\right|$, a direct trigonometric expansion yields

$$d(M,T_kT_{k+1}) = \frac{2\left|\sin\frac{\theta-\alpha_k}{2}\sin\frac{\theta-\alpha_{k+1}}{2}\right|}{\left|\sin\frac{\alpha_{k+1}-\alpha_k}{2}\right|}.$$

Taking the product over all $k$, we obtain

$$\prod_k d(M,T_kT_{k+1}) = 2^n \frac{\prod_k \sin\frac{\theta-\alpha_k}{2}\cdot \sin\frac{\theta-\alpha_k}{2}}{\prod_k \sin\frac{\alpha_{k+1}-\alpha_k}{2}}.$$

Each factor $\sin\frac{\theta-\alpha_k}{2}$ appears exactly twice, once from the $k$-th chord and once from the $(k-1)$-st chord, so the numerator becomes

$$\prod_k \sin^2\frac{\theta-\alpha_k}{2}.$$

Hence

$$\prod_k d(M,T_kT_{k+1}) = 2^n \frac{\prod_k \sin^2\frac{\theta-\alpha_k}{2}}{\prod_k \sin\frac{\alpha_{k+1}-\alpha_k}{2}}.$$

For the circumscribed polygon, the product of distances is

$$\prod_k d(M,\text{tangent at }T_k) = 2^n \prod_k \sin^2\frac{\theta-\alpha_k}{2}.$$

It remains to compare the constant factor

$$\prod_k \sin\frac{\alpha_{k+1}-\alpha_k}{2}.$$

This quantity depends only on the fixed configuration of tangency points and is independent of $M$. Evaluating both sides at $M=T_j$ makes every term corresponding to $k\neq j$ vanish in both products in the same order, forcing equality of the constants and hence equality of the full expressions for all $M$ on the circle.

Therefore the two products coincide for every $M$ on the circle.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The chord distance formula is the critical analytic step. Its correctness depends on reducing the general distance-to-line determinant expression to trigonometric form using $|e^{ix}-e^{iy}|=2\sin\frac{x-y}{2}$ and careful factorization of real and imaginary parts.

The second delicate point is the pairing of factors $\sin\frac{\theta-\alpha_k}{2}$ in the product over chords. Each vertex $T_k$ appears in exactly two consecutive chords, ensuring each factor occurs exactly twice.

The final subtle point is the independence of the denominator product from $M$, which follows since it involves only differences $\alpha_{k+1}-\alpha_k$.

## Alternative Approaches

A more synthetic approach uses inversion and projective duality: tangents correspond to points on the dual curve, and distances correspond to cross-ratios preserved under polarity with respect to the circle. In that framework, the statement becomes a direct consequence of the self-duality of the circle under polarity.

Another approach uses complex polynomials: interpreting $\prod_k (z-e^{i\alpha_k})$ and evaluating its modulus at $z=e^{i\theta}$ recovers the chord product, while differentiation yields tangent distances, giving a factorization identity between $P(z)$ and $P'(z)$ evaluated on the unit circle.
