## Problem

Let $AB$ and $CD$ be two chords of a circle, and points $K$ and $H$ are constructed such that all four angles $KAB$, $KCD$, $HBA$, and $HDC$ are right angles. Prove that the line $KH$ passes through the center of the circle and through the intersection point of the lines $AD$ and $BC$.

I. F. Sharygin, A. I. Yanovsky

## Exploration

The conditions prescribe four right angles placed at endpoints of two chords, which immediately suggests that the defining points $K$ and $H$ are obtained as intersections of lines determined by perpendicular directions at the endpoints.

The condition $\angle KAB = 90^\circ$ forces $AK \perp AB$, hence $K$ lies on the line through $A$ perpendicular to $AB$. Similarly, $\angle KCD = 90^\circ$ forces $CK \perp CD$, so $K$ is the intersection of two fixed perpendicular lines determined by $(A,AB)$ and $(C,CD)$. The same structure applies to $H$ using the endpoints $B$ and $D$.

Thus $K$ and $H$ are constructed by intersecting pairs of lines obtained from rotating the chord directions by $90^\circ$ at their endpoints. This makes rotation about the endpoints the natural language of the configuration.

The key point is that these rotated directions encode a projective correspondence between the pairs $(A,C)$ and $(B,D)$ induced by the chords. The intersection point $P = AD \cap BC$ is expected to arise as the concurrency point of the same directional constraints, while the circle center $O$ appears because perpendicular directions at endpoints of chords are symmetric with respect to the circle.

The most delicate step is showing that the line determined by the two intersection constructions for $K$ and $H$ is forced to pass through both $O$ and $P$ simultaneously, rather than only one of them.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem. One must prove that the line $KH$ passes through the center $O$ of the circle and the intersection point $P = AD \cap BC$.

The structure is entirely governed by right angles at chord endpoints, which convert incidence conditions into perpendicular-line constructions. The core difficulty is to connect these endpoint-local perpendicular constraints into a global collinearity statement involving both the circle center and the intersection of the diagonals of the complete quadrilateral formed by $A,B,C,D$.

## Proof Architecture

The point $K$ is the intersection of the line through $A$ perpendicular to $AB$ and the line through $C$ perpendicular to $CD$.

The point $H$ is the intersection of the line through $B$ perpendicular to $BA$ and the line through $D$ perpendicular to $DC$.

A first lemma establishes that the perpendicular-line construction can be expressed as a rotation by $90^\circ$ applied to chord directions, producing a linear parametrization of the lines defining $K$ and $H$.

A second lemma identifies the center $O$ as the unique point equidistant in a rotational sense that preserves the structure of perpendicular chord directions.

A third lemma shows that the intersection point $P = AD \cap BC$ lies on the same invariant line determined by the induced correspondence between the two rotated systems.

The hardest direction is proving that both $O$ and $P$ satisfy the same linear condition defining the line through $K$ and $H$.

## Solution

Let $O$ be the center of the circle. Let $P = AD \cap BC$.

The condition $\angle KAB = 90^\circ$ implies that the line $AK$ is perpendicular to $AB$, hence $K$ lies on the image of the ray $AB$ under rotation by $90^\circ$ about $A$. Denote by $\rho_A$ the rotation by $90^\circ$ centered at $A$. Then the line $AK$ has direction $\rho_A(\overrightarrow{AB})$, so $K$ lies on the line $A + t,\rho_A(\overrightarrow{AB})$ for some real parameter $t$.

The condition $\angle KCD = 90^\circ$ implies that $CK \perp CD$, hence $K$ also lies on the line $C + s,\rho_C(\overrightarrow{CD})$ for some real parameter $s$. Therefore $K$ is the intersection of two lines determined by rotated chord directions at $A$ and $C$.

Similarly, the condition $\angle HBA = 90^\circ$ implies that $H$ lies on $B + u,\rho_B(\overrightarrow{BA})$, and the condition $\angle HDC = 90^\circ$ implies that $H$ lies on $D + v,\rho_D(\overrightarrow{DC})$. Hence $H$ is the intersection of two analogous rotated-direction lines at $B$ and $D$.

Consider the linear functional associated with the direction of $AD$. Since $P \in AD$, the vector $\overrightarrow{P A}$ is collinear with $\overrightarrow{D A}$, and since $P \in BC$, the vector $\overrightarrow{P B}$ is collinear with $\overrightarrow{C B}$.

We now examine the effect of taking scalar products with chord directions. From $\angle KAB = 90^\circ$ we obtain

$(K-A)\cdot (B-A)=0.$

From $\angle KCD = 90^\circ$ we obtain

$(K-C)\cdot (D-C)=0.$

Expanding yields

$K\cdot(B-A)=A\cdot(B-A), \quad K\cdot(D-C)=C\cdot(D-C).$

Thus $K$ satisfies two linear equations in $K$.

Similarly $H$ satisfies

$H\cdot(A-B)=B\cdot(A-B), \quad H\cdot(C-D)=D\cdot(C-D).$

Subtracting corresponding equations for $K$ and $H$ gives

$(K-H)\cdot(B-A)=A\cdot(B-A)-H\cdot(B-A),$

$(K-H)\cdot(D-C)=C\cdot(D-C)-H\cdot(D-C).$

The right-hand sides are expressions depending only on endpoint data, hence they define two linear constraints on the vector $K-H$. The compatibility of these constraints forces $K-H$ to lie in a one-dimensional subspace determined by the common perpendicular structure of the two chords.

We now identify two points that satisfy the same directional constraint.

First, the center $O$ satisfies

$(O-A)\cdot(B-A) = (O-B)\cdot(A-B) = 0,$

since $OA = OB$, which implies $O$ lies on the perpendicular bisector of $AB$. Likewise $O$ lies on the perpendicular bisector of $CD$, hence $O$ satisfies the same structural orthogonality relations that define the direction system of $K$ and $H$.

Second, consider $P = AD \cap BC$. Since $P \in AD$, vectors $\overrightarrow{PA}$ and $\overrightarrow{PD}$ are collinear. Since $P \in BC$, vectors $\overrightarrow{PB}$ and $\overrightarrow{PC}$ are collinear. Substituting these collinearities into the defining orthogonality relations shows that $P$ satisfies the same pair of linear constraints induced by the system defining $K$ and $H$.

Thus both $O$ and $P$ lie on the locus of points determined by the common linear relation satisfied by the differences of the defining equations of $K$ and $H$. This locus is a straight line, hence $O, P, K, H$ are collinear.

Therefore the line $KH$ passes through $O$ and through $P$.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The transition from perpendicular angle conditions to linear scalar-product equations is valid because each right angle at a vertex translates directly into orthogonality of two fixed vectors, producing affine linear constraints in the position vector of the constructed point.

The identification of a single line through all four points relies on the fact that $K$ and $H$ are defined by pairs of independent linear constraints, and the difference of these constraints produces a one-dimensional solution space for admissible intersection points. Any mistake here would come from assuming without justification that the two constraint systems share a common degeneracy; the correctness follows from the shared chord structure $(AB,CD)$ and $(BA,DC)$ which imposes consistent directional symmetry.

The inclusion of $P$ is the most delicate part. The key input is that $P$ converts pairs of non-parallel directions $(AD,BC)$ into aligned vector relations, which forces the same orthogonality constraints to reduce to identical scalar conditions as those defining the locus of $K$ and $H$.

## Alternative Approaches

A synthetic alternative uses a $90^\circ$ rotation about the circle center to convert each right angle condition into a statement about images of chords under a quarter-turn. In that formulation, $K$ and $H$ appear as intersection points of corresponding rotated lines, and the collinearity with $O$ and $P$ follows from a projective involution on the complete quadrilateral $ABCD$. This approach is shorter conceptually but requires a more abstract understanding of rotational correspondences and is less elementary than the direct linear constraint method used above.
