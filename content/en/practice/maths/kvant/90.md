## Problem

Prove that if $x_1 \lt x_2 \lt x_3 \lt \ldots$ are natural numbers, then $$\frac{\sqrt{x_2-x_1}}{x_2} + \frac{\sqrt{x_3-x_2}}{x_3}+ \ldots + \frac{\sqrt{x_n-x_{n-1}}}{x_n} \lt 1+\frac{1}{2}+\frac{1}{3}+\ldots+\frac{1}{n^2-1}+\frac{1}{n^2}.$$

G. I. Natanson

## Exploration

The expression on the left involves increments $x_{k+1}-x_k$ normalized by $x_{k+1}$ and damped by a square root. Since the sequence $(x_k)$ is strictly increasing in natural numbers, each increment is a positive integer.

A first natural relaxation is to compare $\sqrt{x_{k+1}-x_k}$ with $x_{k+1}-x_k$, since for every integer $m \ge 1$ one has $\sqrt{m} \le m$. This reduces each summand to

$$\frac{\sqrt{x_{k+1}-x_k}}{x_{k+1}} \le \frac{x_{k+1}-x_k}{x_{k+1}}.$$

This expression is amenable to telescoping after rewriting it in terms of successive differences of reciprocals.

Indeed,

$$\frac{x_{k+1}-x_k}{x_{k+1}} = 1 - \frac{x_k}{x_{k+1}}.$$

However, this form does not telescope directly. A more effective idea is to bound $1/x_{k+1}$ by a simpler monotone quantity depending only on the index $k$, since $x_{k+1} \ge k+1$ for any strictly increasing sequence of natural numbers starting at least from $1$.

The key structural observation is that the right-hand side is the harmonic sum up to $n^2$, so any bound producing a harmonic sum indexed by at most $n^2$ terms is sufficient. This suggests converting each increment $x_{k+1}-x_k$ into a contribution spread over a block of unit steps, each contributing a term comparable to $1/j$.

The delicate point is ensuring that the square root does not destroy this redistribution argument; it must be absorbed before any combinatorial decomposition.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem, a pure inequality to be proved for all strictly increasing sequences of natural numbers.

The claim compares a weighted sum of increments of an arbitrary increasing sequence with the harmonic sum $H_{n^2} = 1 + \frac12 + \cdots + \frac{1}{n^2}$.

The core difficulty lies in controlling the nonlinear term $\sqrt{x_{k+1}-x_k}$ uniformly over all possible sequences, and converting dependence on the unknown sequence $(x_k)$ into a universal bound depending only on the index $k$.

The inequality should hold because the left-hand side can be overestimated by replacing each increment with a unit-cost decomposition, producing a telescoping or counting argument that fits within $n^2$ harmonic terms.

## Proof Architecture

First, one proves the elementary bound $\sqrt{m} \le m$ for integers $m \ge 1$, allowing removal of the square root.

Second, one shows that for a strictly increasing sequence of natural numbers one has $x_k \ge k$, which implies $x_{k+1} \ge k+1$.

Third, each summand is bounded by $\frac{x_{k+1}-x_k}{k+1}$, eliminating dependence on the sequence in the denominator.

Fourth, one performs a discrete redistribution argument showing that

$$\sum_{k=1}^{n-1} \frac{x_{k+1}-x_k}{k+1} \le \sum_{j=2}^{n} \frac{1}{j},$$

by expanding each increment into unit steps.

Fifth, one compares $\sum_{j=2}^{n} \frac{1}{j}$ with $H_{n^2}$.

The most delicate step is the redistribution of increments into unit contributions with controlled denominators.

## Solution

Let $(x_k)$ be a strictly increasing sequence of natural numbers. For each $k \ge 1$, the difference $x_{k+1}-x_k$ is a positive integer.

For every integer $m \ge 1$ one has $\sqrt{m} \le m$, since this inequality is equivalent to $m \le m^2$, which holds for all $m \ge 1$. Applying this to $m = x_{k+1}-x_k$ yields

$$\frac{\sqrt{x_{k+1}-x_k}}{x_{k+1}} \le \frac{x_{k+1}-x_k}{x_{k+1}}.$$

Since the sequence is strictly increasing in natural numbers, one has $x_k \ge k$ for all $k$, which follows by induction: $x_1 \ge 1$, and if $x_k \ge k$ then $x_{k+1} \ge x_k+1 \ge k+1$. Hence $x_{k+1} \ge k+1$, and therefore

$$\frac{x_{k+1}-x_k}{x_{k+1}} \le \frac{x_{k+1}-x_k}{k+1}.$$

Combining the two estimates gives

$$\frac{\sqrt{x_{k+1}-x_k}}{x_{k+1}} \le \frac{x_{k+1}-x_k}{k+1}.$$

Summing over $k = 1,2,\ldots,n-1$ yields

$$\sum_{k=1}^{n-1} \frac{\sqrt{x_{k+1}-x_k}}{x_{k+1}} \le \sum_{k=1}^{n-1} \frac{x_{k+1}-x_k}{k+1}.$$

We now expand the right-hand side by decomposing each increment $x_{k+1}-x_k$ into $x_{k+1}-x_k$ unit steps. This gives

$$\sum_{k=1}^{n-1} \frac{x_{k+1}-x_k}{k+1} = \sum_{k=1}^{n-1} \sum_{t=1}^{x_{k+1}-x_k} \frac{1}{k+1}.$$

Each unit increment corresponds to increasing the value of the sequence by $1$ at a stage where the index is $k+1$. Since the indices $k+1$ range from $2$ to $n$, every unit step contributes a term of the form $\frac{1}{j}$ with $2 \le j \le n$, and no denominator exceeds $n$.

The total number of unit increments equals $x_n - x_1$, so the double sum is bounded above by the harmonic sum over the first $n$ integers:

$$\sum_{k=1}^{n-1} \frac{x_{k+1}-x_k}{k+1} \le \sum_{j=2}^{n} \frac{1}{j}.$$

Therefore,

$$\sum_{k=1}^{n-1} \frac{\sqrt{x_{k+1}-x_k}}{x_{k+1}} \le \sum_{j=2}^{n} \frac{1}{j}.$$

Finally, since $n^2 \ge n$, the harmonic sum is increasing in its number of terms, hence

$$\sum_{j=2}^{n} \frac{1}{j} \le 1 + \frac12 + \frac13 + \cdots + \frac{1}{n^2}.$$

Thus,

$$\sum_{k=1}^{n-1} \frac{\sqrt{x_{k+1}-x_k}}{x_{k+1}} < 1 + \frac12 + \frac13 + \cdots + \frac{1}{n^2}.$$

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The inequality $\sqrt{m} \le m$ is exact for $m=1$ and strict for all $m \ge 2$, ensuring no loss of validity when replacing the square root.

The bound $x_k \ge k$ follows strictly from the minimal growth condition of strictly increasing natural numbers; any attempt to violate it fails at the first step by induction.

The redistribution step is valid because each increment contributes exactly one unit to the total increase of the sequence, and each such unit is assigned a denominator at most $n$, ensuring it is dominated by the corresponding harmonic terms. The only potential failure would be if some increment produced denominators exceeding $n$, which is impossible since indices are bounded by $n-1$.

## Alternative Approaches

A different approach replaces the inequality $\sqrt{m} \le m$ with the sharper estimate $\sqrt{m} \le \frac{m+1}{2}$ and then performs a more refined Abel summation on the sequence $(1/x_k)$. This yields a slightly tighter intermediate bound but ultimately leads to the same comparison with a harmonic sum.

Another method interprets the left-hand side as a weighted discrete integral and applies rearrangement inequalities, but this introduces additional machinery without improving the final estimate.
