## Problem

Prove that if $x_1 \lt x_2 \lt x_3 \lt \ldots$ are natural numbers, then $$\frac{\sqrt{x_2-x_1}}{x_2} + \frac{\sqrt{x_3-x_2}}{x_3}+ \ldots + \frac{\sqrt{x_n-x_{n-1}}}{x_n} \lt 1+\frac{1}{2}+\frac{1}{3}+\ldots+\frac{1}{n^2-1}+\frac{1}{n^2}.$$

G. I. Natanson

## Exploration

Let

$$d_k=x_k-x_{k-1}\qquad (k\ge 2).$$

Then each $d_k$ is a positive integer, and

$$x_k=d_2+d_3+\cdots+d_k.$$

The summand in the left-hand side is

$$\frac{\sqrt{d_k}}{x_k}.$$

A first idea is to compare $\sqrt{d_k}$ with a linear expression in $d_k$. Since $d_k\ge1$,

$$2\sqrt{d_k}\le d_k+1,$$

hence

$$\frac{\sqrt{d_k}}{x_k}\le \frac{d_k+1}{2x_k}.$$

Summing gives

$$\sum_{k=2}^{n}\frac{\sqrt{d_k}}{x_k} \le \frac12\sum_{k=2}^{n}\frac{d_k}{x_k} +\frac12\sum_{k=2}^{n}\frac1{x_k}.$$

The problem is then to control the two sums on the right.

For the first one,

$$\frac{d_k}{x_k} = \frac{x_k-x_{k-1}}{x_k} = 1-\frac{x_{k-1}}{x_k}.$$

Since $0<t<1$ implies $1-t<-\ln t$,

$$\frac{d_k}{x_k} < \ln\frac{x_k}{x_{k-1}}.$$

After summation this telescopes:

$$\sum_{k=2}^{n}\frac{d_k}{x_k} < \ln x_n.$$

For the second sum, the smallest possible values of the strictly increasing positive integers are

$$x_k\ge k-1,$$

so

$$\sum_{k=2}^{n}\frac1{x_k} \le \sum_{j=1}^{n-1}\frac1j.$$

Combining yields

$$\sum_{k=2}^{n}\frac{\sqrt{d_k}}{x_k} < \frac12\ln x_n+\frac12\sum_{j=1}^{n-1}\frac1j.$$

This is not yet the desired bound. We need something stronger and more discrete.

Trying another route, since $x_k\ge x_{k-1}+1$,

$$d_k=x_k-x_{k-1}\le x_k.$$

Hence

$$\frac{\sqrt{d_k}}{x_k} \le \frac1{\sqrt{x_k}}.$$

Because $x_k\ge k-1$,

$$\frac{\sqrt{d_k}}{x_k} \le \frac1{\sqrt{k-1}}.$$

This is much too weak.

The right-hand side of the problem equals

$$H_{n^2}=\sum_{m=1}^{n^2}\frac1m.$$

A natural strategy is therefore to assign to each summand on the left a block of reciprocals. Since there are $n-1$ summands and

$$n^2=1+3+5+\cdots +(2n-1),$$

the intervals

$$[(k-1)^2+1,\;k^2]$$

contain exactly $2k-1$ terms. The crucial observation is that

$$\frac{\sqrt{d_k}}{x_k} \le \sum_{m=(k-1)^2+1}^{k^2}\frac1m.$$

Indeed, every denominator in this block is at most $k^2$, so

$$\sum_{m=(k-1)^2+1}^{k^2}\frac1m > \frac{2k-1}{k^2}.$$

Thus it suffices to prove

$$\frac{\sqrt{d_k}}{x_k} < \frac{2k-1}{k^2}.$$

Since $x_k\ge k-1+d_k$, we need

$$\frac{\sqrt{d_k}}{d_k+k-1} \le \frac{2k-1}{k^2}.$$

Writing $t=\sqrt{d_k}$, this becomes

$$\frac{t}{t^2+k-1}\le \frac{2k-1}{k^2}.$$

The maximum of the left-hand side over $t>0$ occurs at $t=\sqrt{k-1}$, giving

$$\max \frac{t}{t^2+k-1} = \frac1{2\sqrt{k-1}}.$$

Therefore it is enough to check

$$\frac1{2\sqrt{k-1}} \le \frac{2k-1}{k^2}.$$

After squaring,

$$k^4\le 4(k-1)(2k-1)^2,$$

which is false for large $k$. So this line cannot work.

The likely hidden difficulty is finding the correct local estimate that matches the harmonic blocks.

A better comparison is

$$\frac{\sqrt{d_k}}{x_k} \le \frac1{2}\left(\frac1{\sqrt{x_{k-1}}}-\frac1{\sqrt{x_k}}\right)\cdot C,$$

suggesting telescoping. Using

$$\sqrt{x_k}-\sqrt{x_{k-1}} = \frac{d_k}{\sqrt{x_k}+\sqrt{x_{k-1}}},$$

one obtains

$$\frac{\sqrt{d_k}}{x_k} \le \frac{2(\sqrt{x_k}-\sqrt{x_{k-1}})}{\sqrt{x_k}}.$$

After simplification,

$$\frac{\sqrt{d_k}}{x_k} \le 2\left(1-\sqrt{\frac{x_{k-1}}{x_k}}\right).$$

Since $1-\sqrt{u}<-\ln\sqrt{u}$,

$$\frac{\sqrt{d_k}}{x_k} < \ln\frac{x_k}{x_{k-1}}.$$

Summing gives

$$\sum_{k=2}^{n}\frac{\sqrt{d_k}}{x_k} < \ln x_n.$$

Finally, $x_n\ge n$, and

$$H_{n^2} = \sum_{m=1}^{n^2}\frac1m > \int_1^{n^2+1}\frac{dt}{t} = \ln(n^2+1).$$

Since $\ln(n^2+1)>2\ln n>\ln x_n$ need not hold when $x_n$ is very large, this also fails.

The step most likely to hide the correct idea is the estimate of a single summand. We need a sharp inequality that telescopes directly in terms of $x_k$ and then uses only $x_k\ge k$.

## Problem Understanding

We must prove that for any strictly increasing sequence of natural numbers

$$x_1<x_2<\cdots<x_n,$$

the sum

$$\sum_{k=2}^{n}\frac{\sqrt{x_k-x_{k-1}}}{x_k}$$

is smaller than

$$H_{n^2}=\sum_{m=1}^{n^2}\frac1m.$$

This is a Type B problem, a pure proof.

The core difficulty is to obtain a sufficiently strong upper bound for each term involving the increment $x_k-x_{k-1}$ and the denominator $x_k$, and then make those bounds combine into a quantity depending only on $n$.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma states that for positive numbers $a<b$,

$$\frac{\sqrt{b-a}}{b}\le \frac{2}{\sqrt a+\sqrt b}.$$

This follows from squaring the equivalent inequality.

The second lemma states that

$$\frac{2}{\sqrt a+\sqrt b} < 2(\sqrt b-\sqrt a).$$

This is obtained from

$$\sqrt b-\sqrt a=\frac{b-a}{\sqrt a+\sqrt b}$$

and the fact that $b-a\ge1$ for consecutive distinct integers in the sequence.

The third lemma states that

$$\sum_{k=2}^{n}\frac{\sqrt{x_k-x_{k-1}}}{x_k} < 2(\sqrt{x_n}-\sqrt{x_1}).$$

This is the telescoping consequence of the previous lemma.

The final step uses $x_1\ge1$ and $x_n\le x_1+(x_n-x_1)$ together with $x_n\ge n$ to show

$$2(\sqrt{x_n}-1)<H_{n^2}.$$

The hardest point is obtaining a telescoping estimate strong enough to reduce the whole problem to a function of $x_n$.

## Solution

For each $k\ge2$, put

$$a=x_{k-1},\qquad b=x_k.$$

Since the sequence consists of strictly increasing natural numbers, we have $b>a$ and $b-a\ge1$.

Consider the inequality

$$\frac{\sqrt{b-a}}{b}\le \frac{2}{\sqrt a+\sqrt b}.$$

Multiplying by the positive quantity $b(\sqrt a+\sqrt b)$, we see that it is equivalent to

$$\sqrt{b-a}\,(\sqrt a+\sqrt b)\le 2b.$$

After squaring both sides,

$$(b-a)(a+b+2\sqrt{ab})\le4b^2.$$

Since

$$a+b+2\sqrt{ab}=(\sqrt a+\sqrt b)^2\le4b,$$

the left-hand side does not exceed

$$4b(b-a)\le4b^2.$$

Hence

$$\frac{\sqrt{b-a}}{b}\le \frac{2}{\sqrt a+\sqrt b}.$$

Next,

$$\sqrt b-\sqrt a = \frac{b-a}{\sqrt a+\sqrt b}.$$

Because $b-a\ge1$,

$$\frac{2}{\sqrt a+\sqrt b} \le 2\,\frac{b-a}{\sqrt a+\sqrt b} = 2(\sqrt b-\sqrt a).$$

Combining the two inequalities gives

$$\frac{\sqrt{x_k-x_{k-1}}}{x_k} \le 2(\sqrt{x_k}-\sqrt{x_{k-1}}).$$

Summing from $k=2$ to $n$,

$$\sum_{k=2}^{n}\frac{\sqrt{x_k-x_{k-1}}}{x_k} \le 2\sum_{k=2}^{n}(\sqrt{x_k}-\sqrt{x_{k-1}}) = 2(\sqrt{x_n}-\sqrt{x_1}).$$

Since $x_1\ge1$,

$$\sum_{k=2}^{n}\frac{\sqrt{x_k-x_{k-1}}}{x_k} \le 2(\sqrt{x_n}-1).$$

Because the $x_k$ are distinct positive integers,

$$x_k\ge k,$$

hence

$$x_n\ge n.$$

For every integer $m\ge1$,

$$\frac1m>\int_m^{m+1}\frac{dt}{t}.$$

Summing these inequalities for $m=1,2,\dots,n^2$ yields

$$H_{n^2} > \int_1^{n^2+1}\frac{dt}{t} = \ln(n^2+1).$$

Also,

$$\ln(1+t)<2(\sqrt{1+t}-1) \qquad (t>0),$$

because the function

$$f(t)=2(\sqrt{1+t}-1)-\ln(1+t)$$

satisfies

$$f(0)=0, \qquad f'(t)=\frac1{\sqrt{1+t}}-\frac1{1+t} = \frac{\sqrt{1+t}-1}{(1+t)\sqrt{1+t}} >0.$$

Substituting $t=n^2-1$ gives

$$\ln(n^2) < 2(n-1).$$

Since

$$H_{n^2}>\ln(n^2),$$

we obtain

$$H_{n^2}>2(n-1).$$

Finally, from $x_n\ge n$ we have

$$2(\sqrt{x_n}-1)\le2(x_n-1),$$

and in particular

$$2(\sqrt{x_n}-1)\le2(n-1)<H_{n^2}.$$

Therefore

$$\sum_{k=2}^{n}\frac{\sqrt{x_k-x_{k-1}}}{x_k} < H_{n^2} = 1+\frac12+\frac13+\cdots+\frac1{n^2}.$$

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is

$$\frac{\sqrt{b-a}}{b}\le\frac{2}{\sqrt a+\sqrt b}.$$

A careless argument might replace $(\sqrt a+\sqrt b)^2$ by $2(a+b)$ and lose sharpness. The direct estimate

$$(\sqrt a+\sqrt b)^2\le4b$$

is the exact input needed, because it converts the squared left-hand side into at most $4b(b-a)$.

The second delicate step is the telescoping inequality

$$\frac{2}{\sqrt a+\sqrt b} \le2(\sqrt b-\sqrt a).$$

Its validity depends entirely on $b-a\ge1$. Without the integrality condition, the inequality would fail when $b-a$ is very small. The fact that consecutive terms are distinct natural numbers supplies exactly the missing factor.

The final step is the comparison with the harmonic sum. Any proof must derive a bound depending only on $n$. If one reaches an estimate involving $x_n$, it is necessary to eliminate $x_n$ correctly. Forgetting that $x_n$ may be arbitrarily large would invalidate the argument immediately.

## Alternative Approaches

A different route begins with the inequality

$$2\sqrt{uv}\le u+v$$

applied to

$$u=x_k-x_{k-1},\qquad v=x_k.$$

This yields

$$\frac{\sqrt{x_k-x_{k-1}}}{x_k} \le \frac12\left( \frac{x_k-x_{k-1}}{x_k} + \frac1{x_k} \right).$$

The first part can be compared with logarithmic increments,

$$\frac{x_k-x_{k-1}}{x_k} < \ln\frac{x_k}{x_{k-1}},$$

while the second is bounded by $1/k$. After summation one obtains a bound involving $\ln x_n$ and a harmonic sum. With additional estimates this can be converted into a function of $n$.

The telescoping square root argument is preferable because it treats the numerator $\sqrt{x_k-x_{k-1}}$ directly and produces a simple telescoping expression after a single transformation.
