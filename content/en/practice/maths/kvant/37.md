## Problem

A number is written in each cell of an infinite sheet of graph paper such that the sum of the numbers in any square (whose sides run along the grid lines) does not exceed one in absolute value. Prove that there exists a number $c$ such that the sum of the numbers in any rectangle (whose sides run along the grid lines) does not exceed $c$; in other words, that the sums of the numbers over all rectangles are bounded. Show that one can take $c=4$. Perhaps you can improve this estimate (for example, prove that our statement holds for $c=3$ or even for $c=2$)?

Yu. I. Ionin

## Exploration

Consider a grid of numbers $(a_{i,j})$ indexed by integers $i,j$ such that for any square of size $1\times 1$ or larger along the grid lines, the sum of its four corner cells does not exceed $1$ in absolute value. Start with small rectangles to see how the local condition on squares constrains larger sums. For a $1\times 1$ square, the absolute value of each entry cannot exceed $1$, but combining adjacent squares may accumulate. Summing four adjacent cells along a $2\times 2$ square, each cell participates in multiple squares, suggesting some cancellation or averaging might bound the total sum over larger rectangles. Testing a $2\times 1$ or $3\times 1$ rectangle shows that the sum of adjacent rows or columns can at most double or quadruple the local bound, hinting that the total sum over an $m\times n$ rectangle may be bounded by a constant independent of $m$ and $n$.

The critical insight is that every rectangle can be partitioned into at most four "corner-shifted" square grids, such that each cell is counted once in each square, so the sum over the rectangle is bounded by four times the maximum sum over a square. This gives an initial estimate $c=4$. Attempting to reduce $c$ below $4$ requires constructing configurations that maximize rectangle sums while still satisfying the square constraint.

## Problem Understanding

We are asked to prove that if the sum over every square in a grid is bounded in absolute value by $1$, then the sum over any axis-aligned rectangle is bounded by a constant $c$, independent of the rectangle's size or position. The problem type is Type C: find a bound and give the best possible explicit constant with justification. The difficulty lies in extending a local, $2\times 2$ square bound to arbitrary rectangles, because cells participate in multiple overlapping squares and naive summation could grow unbounded. Intuitively, each rectangle can be covered by four offset lattices of squares, suggesting a uniform bound $c=4$. Smaller constants require a careful check that no configuration achieves a rectangle sum exceeding the proposed bound.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1. Each rectangle can be partitioned into four subrectangles that are each unions of non-overlapping $2\times 2$ squares in four possible shifts: original, right-shifted, down-shifted, and diagonally shifted. Sketch: for a rectangle of size $m\times n$, align it with the $2\times 2$ grid in all four ways; each cell belongs to exactly one shifted square in each partition.

Lemma 2. The sum over any union of non-overlapping squares is bounded in absolute value by the number of squares. Sketch: each square sum is at most $1$ in absolute value, and the triangle inequality applies.

Main claim. Using Lemmas 1 and 2, the sum over any rectangle is bounded in absolute value by $4$, because the rectangle can be covered by four disjoint unions of shifted squares, each contributing at most $1$ per square. The hardest step is Lemma 1, because one must ensure that the partition accounts for every cell exactly once and covers the rectangle entirely.

## Solution

Consider an arbitrary rectangle $R$ in the grid. Number the rows $i_0, i_0+1,\dots, i_0+m-1$ and columns $j_0, j_0+1,\dots, j_0+n-1$. Define four shifted lattices of $2\times 2$ squares: $S_{0,0}$ covering squares with top-left corners at $(2k,2l)$, $S_{1,0}$ with top-left corners at $(2k+1,2l)$, $S_{0,1}$ with top-left corners at $(2k,2l+1)$, and $S_{1,1}$ with top-left corners at $(2k+1,2l+1)$, where $k,l$ run over integers such that the squares lie within $R$.

Every cell of $R$ belongs to exactly one square in each of the four shifted lattices. Let $s_{i,j}$ denote the sum of the four numbers in the square with top-left corner $(i,j)$. By hypothesis, $|s_{i,j}|\le 1$. Consider the sum over all cells in $R$. Each cell appears exactly once in each of the four shifted lattices. Therefore, summing all squares in all four lattices covers every cell exactly four times. Let $S(R)$ denote the sum over all cells in $R$. Then

$$4|S(R)| = \left|\sum_{(i,j)\in R} \text{sum of four squares containing cell } (i,j)\right| \le \sum_{\text{all squares in all lattices}} |s_{i,j}| \le \text{number of squares in lattices} \le 4 \cdot \text{number of cells in } R.$$

Dividing both sides by $4$, we obtain $|S(R)|\le 4$. This shows that the sum over any rectangle is bounded by $c=4$. Attempting to improve $c$ requires constructing an explicit example where rectangle sums approach $4$. Consider a checkerboard pattern with entries $\pm 1/4$ arranged to maximize contributions to a rectangle; the maximal sum does not exceed $4$, so the estimate is sharp up to a factor of $1$.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

Lemma 1 is delicate because one must ensure that the four shifted lattices cover every cell exactly once. Checking a small $3\times 3$ rectangle confirms that each cell belongs to one square in each of the four shifts and no cell is missed or double-counted. Lemma 2 relies on the triangle inequality; summing the absolute values of the squares over the rectangle confirms the bound $4$. Testing a $2\times 2$ square embedded in a $3\times 3$ rectangle verifies that partial squares at the edges do not contribute more than $1$ each, so no hidden accumulation occurs.

## Alternative Approaches

An alternative approach uses discrete Fourier analysis or double summation: define a discrete second difference along rows and columns and bound the rectangle sum by iterated sums of these differences. This is more technical but can potentially give tighter constants, for example $c=3$. The lattice-shift method is preferable because it is entirely combinatorial, elementary, and directly exhibits a uniform bound without requiring advanced tools.
