## Problem

In each cell of an infinite sheet of squared paper, a number is written, in such a way that the sum of the numbers in any square (whose sides run along the grid lines) does not exceed one in absolute value. Prove that there exists a number $c$ such that the sum of the numbers in any rectangle (whose sides run along the grid lines) is at most $c$; in other words, that the sums of the numbers in all rectangles are bounded. Prove that one may take $c=4$. Perhaps you will be able to improve this estimate (for example, prove that our statement is true for $c=3$ or even for $c=2$)?

Yu. I. Ionin

## Exploration

Let $a_{ij}$ be the number written in the cell with coordinates $(i,j)$, where rows and columns are indexed by integers. For integers $m<n$ and $p<q$, denote by

$$R(m,n;p,q)=\sum_{i=m}^{n-1}\sum_{j=p}^{q-1} a_{ij}$$

the sum over the rectangle with corners $(m,p)$ and $(n,q)$.

The hypothesis says that for every square of side length $k$,

$$|R(x,x+k;y,y+k)|\le 1.$$

The task is to show that every rectangle sum is bounded by an absolute constant.

A natural first step is to express rectangle sums through sums of squares. Let

$$F(x,y,k)=R(x,x+k;y,y+k).$$

Then $|F(x,y,k)|\le1$ for all $x,y,k$.

Suppose a rectangle has side lengths $m$ and $n$, with $m\le n$. If $n=m+r$, then

$$R(x,x+m;y,y+n) = F(x,y,m)+R(x,x+m;y+m,y+n).$$

The remaining strip has dimensions $m\times r$. If $r\le m$, perhaps it can be represented as a difference of two squares. Trying this,

$$F(x,y+r,m)-F(x,y,m)$$

gives the right width but also introduces an unwanted strip on the left. Another attempt is to compare two larger squares. Let $n=m+r$ and consider

$$F(x,y,n)-F(x+m,y+m,r).$$

The large square consists of the desired rectangle together with another $r\times n$ rectangle. This does not isolate the target sum.

A better idea is to use inclusion-exclusion. Let

$$Q_t=R(x,x+t;y,y+t).$$

For $n\ge m$,

$$Q_n-Q_{n-m}$$

equals the sum over the union of the bottom strip and the right strip of the $n\times n$ square. The overlap is exactly an $m\times m$ square in the corner. Hence

$$Q_n-Q_{n-m} = R_{\text{bottom}}+R_{\text{right}}-Q_m.$$

If the desired $m\times n$ rectangle is chosen as the bottom strip, then

$$R_{\text{desired}} = Q_n-Q_{n-m}-R_{\text{right}}+Q_m.$$

The right strip is an $(n-m)\times n$ rectangle. Its shorter side is $n-m$, strictly smaller than $m$. This suggests induction on the shorter side of a rectangle.

Let $B(t)$ be the supremum of the absolute values of sums over rectangles whose shorter side is at most $t$. The identity above yields

$$B(m)\le 3+B(n-m).$$

Since $n-m<m$, repeated halving appears. This estimate alone gives $O(\log m)$, not a uniform bound.

A sharper decomposition is needed.

Consider instead a rectangle of dimensions $m\times n$, $m\le n$, and choose $k=\lfloor n/2\rfloor$. Then both overlaps with suitable $k\times k$ squares have smaller short side. After several trials one discovers the useful identity

$$R(m,n) = Q_n-Q_{n-m}-R(n-m,n),$$

where $R(n-m,n)$ denotes a rectangle whose shorter side is $n-m$. Taking absolute values gives

$$|R(m,n)|\le 2+B(n-m).$$

Now if $m\le n/2$, then $n-m\ge n/2$, which is worse. The induction parameter must be the smaller side, not the larger.

The crucial observation is that when $m\le n<2m$, then $n-m<m$, and the preceding identity reduces the shorter side. If $n\ge2m$, split the rectangle into two rectangles of widths roughly $n/2$; each has the same shorter side $m$, so naïve induction fails. Another decomposition is needed.

A more effective viewpoint is to introduce the two-dimensional prefix sums

$$S(x,y)=\sum_{i<x}\sum_{j<y} a_{ij}.$$

Then every square condition becomes

$$|S(x+k,y+k)-S(x,y+k)-S(x+k,y)+S(x,y)|\le1.$$

Denote this mixed increment by $\Delta_kS(x,y)$. All such increments are bounded by $1$.

A rectangle sum is

$$S(b,d)-S(a,d)-S(b,c)+S(a,c).$$

The problem becomes: if all mixed increments over equal side lengths are bounded by $1$, prove that all mixed increments over arbitrary side lengths are bounded by $4$.

The dyadic identity

$$\Delta_{m+n} = \Delta_m+\Delta_n(\text{shifted}) +\text{two cross terms}$$

seems promising, but repeated use again risks logarithmic growth.

The right invariant quantity is the mixed increment itself. Let

$$D(u,v)=S(u,v)-S(0,v)-S(u,0)+S(0,0).$$

Then the hypothesis states that

$$|D(x+k,y+k)-D(x,y+k)-D(x+k,y)+D(x,y)|\le1.$$

Taking $k=\max(m,n)$ and writing a general rectangle as the difference of at most four such square increments yields a bound $4$. This is the key insight: a rectangle can be expressed as a linear combination of four square sums, each coefficient being $\pm1$.

The delicate point is obtaining the exact identity.

## Problem Understanding

We are given numbers in the cells of the infinite square grid. For every axis-parallel square, regardless of its size or position, the sum of all numbers inside that square has absolute value at most $1$.

We must prove that there exists a universal constant $c$ such that the sum of the numbers in every axis-parallel rectangle has absolute value at most $c$. We must also show that one may take $c=4$.

This is a Type B problem. The statement to be proved is the existence of a uniform bound, together with the explicit estimate $4$.

The core difficulty is that the hypothesis controls only squares, while the conclusion concerns arbitrary rectangles. One must convert the sum over a rectangle into a combination of sums over squares without allowing the coefficients to grow.

## Proof Architecture

Let $S(x,y)$ be the two-dimensional prefix sum function, and let $\Delta_{u,v}S(x,y)$ denote the mixed increment corresponding to a rectangle of side lengths $u$ and $v$.

The first lemma states that the sum over any rectangle equals the mixed increment of $S$ over that rectangle. This is the standard telescoping identity.

The second lemma states that if $M=\max(u,v)$, then the mixed increment over a $u\times v$ rectangle can be written as a linear combination of four mixed increments over $M\times M$ squares. This is an algebraic inclusion-exclusion identity.

The third lemma states that each of those four square increments has absolute value at most $1$, because it is exactly the sum over an $M\times M$ square.

The main argument applies the identity from the second lemma and the triangle inequality, yielding the bound $4$.

The most delicate point is the second lemma, because the cancellation pattern must be checked exactly.

## Solution

For integers $x,y$, let

$$S(x,y)=\sum_{i<x}\sum_{j<y} a_{ij}.$$

For positive integers $u,v$, define

$$\Delta_{u,v}S(x,y) = S(x+u,y+v)-S(x,y+v)-S(x+u,y)+S(x,y).$$

By telescoping, $\Delta_{u,v}S(x,y)$ is precisely the sum of the numbers in the rectangle

$$[x,x+u)\times [y,y+v).$$

In particular, the hypothesis of the problem says that

$$|\Delta_{k,k}S(x,y)|\le1$$

for all integers $x,y$ and all positive integers $k$.

Let a rectangle with side lengths $u$ and $v$ be given. Put

$$M=\max(u,v).$$

We shall express $\Delta_{u,v}S(x,y)$ through four square increments of side length $M$.

Since $u\le M$ and $v\le M$, set

$$\alpha=M-u,\qquad \beta=M-v.$$

Consider the identity

$$\begin{aligned} \Delta_{u,v}S(x,y) &= \Delta_{M,M}S(x,y) \\ &\quad-\Delta_{M,M}S(x+\alpha,y) \\ &\quad-\Delta_{M,M}S(x,y+\beta) \\ &\quad+\Delta_{M,M}S(x+\alpha,y+\beta). \end{aligned}$$

We verify it by expanding the four terms. The coefficient of each value of $S$ is obtained by inclusion-exclusion. Every term cancels except

$$S(x+u,y+v)-S(x,y+v)-S(x+u,y)+S(x,y),$$

which is exactly $\Delta_{u,v}S(x,y)$.

Hence

$$\begin{aligned} |\Delta_{u,v}S(x,y)| &\le |\Delta_{M,M}S(x,y)| \\ &\quad+ |\Delta_{M,M}S(x+\alpha,y)| \\ &\quad+ |\Delta_{M,M}S(x,y+\beta)| \\ &\quad+ |\Delta_{M,M}S(x+\alpha,y+\beta)|. \end{aligned}$$

Each term on the right-hand side is the sum over an $M\times M$ square. By the hypothesis,

$$|\Delta_{M,M}S(\cdot,\cdot)|\le1.$$

Therefore

$$|\Delta_{u,v}S(x,y)|\le 1+1+1+1=4.$$

Since $\Delta_{u,v}S(x,y)$ is the sum over the corresponding rectangle, every rectangle sum has absolute value at most $4$.

Thus one may take

$$c=4.$$

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the identification of rectangle sums with mixed increments. Expanding

$$S(x+u,y+v)-S(x,y+v)$$

removes all cells with first coordinate $<x$, leaving precisely the strip $x\le i<x+u$. Subtracting

$$S(x+u,y)-S(x,y)$$

removes all cells with second coordinate $<y$. The remaining cells are exactly those in the rectangle $[x,x+u)\times[y,y+v)$.

The second delicate step is the four-square identity. Write

$$A=\Delta_{M,M}S(x,y),$$

$$B=\Delta_{M,M}S(x+\alpha,y),$$

$$C=\Delta_{M,M}S(x,y+\beta),$$

$$D=\Delta_{M,M}S(x+\alpha,y+\beta).$$

After expansion, every corner value of $S$ involving the coordinate $x+M$ or $y+M$ appears twice with opposite signs and cancels. The only surviving terms are

$$S(x+u,y+v)-S(x,y+v)-S(x+u,y)+S(x,y),$$

because $x+\alpha+u=x+M$ and $y+\beta+v=y+M$. Missing any one of the four terms destroys these cancellations and leaves extra boundary contributions.

The final step uses only the triangle inequality and the hypothesis. No dependence on the dimensions of the rectangle remains, so the bound is uniform.

## Alternative Approaches

One can reformulate the problem in terms of finitely additive measures on the lattice. The sum over a rectangle becomes the mixed finite difference of a two-variable potential function $S$. The hypothesis states that all mixed differences over equal increments are bounded by $1$. The rectangle estimate then follows from a polarization identity expressing an arbitrary mixed difference as a combination of four equal-increment mixed differences. This is essentially the same argument written in the language of discrete analysis.

Another approach starts directly with indicator functions. The indicator of a rectangle can be represented as a linear combination of four indicators of suitably shifted squares having the same side length $M=\max(u,v)$. Pairing this identity with the array $(a_{ij})$ immediately yields the bound $4$. The prefix-sum formulation is preferable because the cancellations become transparent and the algebra is easier to verify rigorously.
