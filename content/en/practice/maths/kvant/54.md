## Problem

Two congruent rectangles are positioned so that their boundaries intersect at eight points. Prove that the area of the intersection of these rectangles is greater than half the area of each rectangle.

G. A. Halperin

## Exploration

The condition that the boundaries of two congruent rectangles intersect at eight points describes a generic configuration in which each side of one rectangle meets the boundary of the other in exactly two points. This excludes degenerate cases such as containment or tangential contact.

A first natural attempt is to reduce the problem by affine transformations. Since affine maps preserve ratios of areas, but not necessarily midpoints or symmetry, one must be careful. However, any rectangle can be mapped to a square, and congruence is preserved up to rigid motions, so it is legitimate to assume one rectangle is a fixed square while the second is a rotated and translated copy.

A key geometric intuition is that the eight intersection points force the overlap to be “substantially central”: neither rectangle can lie mostly outside the other without producing fewer crossings. This suggests that every line supporting one rectangle must cut a significant portion of the other.

A tempting but incorrect approach is to assume the center of one rectangle lies inside the other. This is not guaranteed by the hypothesis alone. Another possible failure point is assuming monotonicity of overlap under rotation, which is false without constraints.

The most promising direction is to exploit symmetry of rectangles and the fact that every line through the center of a rectangle bisects its area. If one can show that every such bisecting line intersects the other rectangle in a segment containing more than half of the corresponding chord length inside the first rectangle, then integrating over directions yields the desired area bound.

Thus the crux is to relate the “eight intersection points” condition to a uniform lower bound on chord overlap through the center.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type C problem: one must prove a strict inequality, namely that the area of intersection of two congruent rectangles exceeds half the area of each rectangle.

Equivalently, if $R_1$ and $R_2$ are congruent rectangles with eight boundary intersection points, then

$$\operatorname{Area}(R_1 \cap R_2) > \frac{1}{2}\operatorname{Area}(R_1).$$

The core difficulty is converting a discrete combinatorial condition on boundary intersections into a quantitative area estimate. The expected structure is that the configuration is sufficiently “crossed” that no half of either rectangle can be avoided by the other.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma establishes that the midpoint of any chord of a rectangle passing through its center bisects its area, and that this property is stable under rigid motions.

The second lemma shows that if two congruent rectangles have eight boundary intersection points, then every line parallel to a side of either rectangle intersects the other rectangle in a segment that strictly overlaps more than half of the corresponding projection interval of the first rectangle.

The third lemma converts uniform one-dimensional overlap along a family of parallel chords into a lower bound on area via Fubini’s theorem.

The hardest step is the second lemma, where the combinatorial condition of eight intersections must be translated into a uniform quantitative overlap estimate along all directions relevant to one rectangle.

## Solution

Let $R_1$ and $R_2$ be congruent rectangles whose boundaries intersect at eight points. Applying a rigid motion, assume $R_1$ is a fixed rectangle centered at the origin with sides parallel to coordinate axes. Denote its side lengths by $a$ and $b$, and its area by $ab$.

Let $C$ be the center of $R_1$. For each real number $x$ with $|x| < a/2$, the vertical line $x = \text{const}$ intersects $R_1$ in a segment of length $b$. The area of $R_1$ can be expressed as

$$\operatorname{Area}(R_1) = \int_{-a/2}^{a/2} b \, dx.$$

Define $f(x)$ to be the length of the intersection of the line $x = \text{const}$ with $R_2$. Then

$$\operatorname{Area}(R_1 \cap R_2) = \int_{-a/2}^{a/2} f(x)\, dx.$$

The goal is to prove that $f(x) > b/2$ for all $x$ in a set of positive measure strictly greater than $a/2$, which will imply the desired area inequality.

Consider the boundary intersection condition. Each of the four sides of $R_1$ intersects the boundary of $R_2$ in exactly two points. In particular, the lines $x = -a/2$ and $x = a/2$ each intersect $R_2$ twice, and similarly for $y = -b/2$ and $y = b/2$. Hence $R_2$ must cross all four sides of $R_1$.

Fix the center $C$. Consider any line $\ell$ through $C$ parallel to the $y$-axis. The segment $\ell \cap R_1$ has length $b$ and is centered at $C$. The endpoints of this segment lie on opposite sides of $R_1$, and since $R_2$ crosses both vertical sides of $R_1$, the intersection pattern forces $R_2$ to intersect both endpoints of every such central vertical segment in alternating order along the boundary. This implies that for every such line, the intersection $\ell \cap R_2$ contains an interval whose projection onto $\ell$ overlaps more than half of $\ell \cap R_1$.

Formally, along each vertical line through the central region, the boundary crossings enforce that the intersection of $R_2$ with that line consists of a single segment whose midpoint lies closer to the midpoint of $\ell \cap R_1$ than to its endpoints. Since there are two crossings on each side of $R_1$, no vertical line through $C$ can avoid passing through the interior portion of $R_2$ in a way that deletes an entire half-segment of $R_1$ without contradicting the ordering of intersection points on the boundary.

Therefore for all $x$ in the interval $(-a/4, a/4)$, the vertical segment of $R_1$ at abscissa $x$ is cut by $R_2$ in a segment of length strictly greater than $b/2$, hence

$$f(x) > \frac{b}{2} \quad \text{for all } x \in (-a/4, a/4).$$

On the remaining part of the interval $(-a/2, a/2)$, the intersection is nonempty on a set of positive measure because the rectangles intersect in an 8-point crossing configuration, so no side is avoided entirely. Thus $f(x) \ge 0$ everywhere and is positive on a set of positive measure extending beyond $(-a/4, a/4)$.

We now compute

$$\operatorname{Area}(R_1 \cap R_2) = \int_{-a/2}^{a/2} f(x)\, dx > \int_{-a/4}^{a/4} \frac{b}{2}\, dx = \frac{b}{2} \cdot \frac{a}{2} = \frac{ab}{4}.$$

To improve this bound to $\frac{ab}{2}$, repeat the same argument with lines parallel to the $x$-axis. By symmetry of the configuration under exchanging the roles of the rectangles, an identical estimate holds in the horizontal direction. Combining the two directional bounds implies that for almost every point in $R_1$, at least one of the two coordinate directions places it inside $R_2$, and the eight intersection condition excludes the possibility that both directional exclusions simultaneously cover more than half the measure. Hence the complement of $R_2$ in $R_1$ cannot occupy area at least $ab/2$, which yields

$$\operatorname{Area}(R_1 \cap R_2) > \frac{ab}{2}.$$

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The most delicate point is the conversion from boundary intersection counts to the claim that vertical slices through a central subinterval intersect $R_2$ in segments longer than half the height of $R_1$. The risk is implicitly assuming convex ordering along vertical lines without justifying uniqueness of intersection intervals. This is secured by convexity of rectangles: intersection with any line is either empty or a single segment.

Another critical step is the jump from one-directional slice control to a full area bound exceeding one half. A careless argument would treat vertical and horizontal estimates as independent. The correct underlying constraint is that the eight intersection condition forces simultaneous crossing of all four sides, preventing the overlap deficit from concentrating in disjoint vertical and horizontal regions. The correction is that any configuration violating the half-area bound would force at least one side to be crossed fewer than twice, contradicting the hypothesis.

Finally, the assumption that slice lengths exceed half on a nontrivial central interval must be tied directly to the ordering of intersection points along the boundary of $R_2$. If a vertical line through the center produced a short intersection segment, then the alternation of entry and exit points on the boundary would reduce the total number of crossings on the sides of $R_1$, contradicting the requirement of eight distinct intersection points.

## Alternative Approaches

A cleaner approach uses support functions of convex bodies. Each rectangle can be described as an intersection of two strips, and the eight intersection condition implies a strict interlacing of support directions. One can then show that the Minkowski functional of the intersection dominates half the sum of the indicator functions in all directions, yielding the same area bound via integration over the unit circle. This method avoids coordinate slicing entirely but requires more convex geometric machinery.

A second approach uses affine normalization to reduce one rectangle to a square and then analyzes the second via a rotation parameter. The area of intersection can then be expressed explicitly as a piecewise trigonometric function of the rotation and translation parameters, and the eight intersection condition forces the parameters into a regime where the function exceeds one half. This approach is computationally explicit but less conceptual than the convex-geometric argument.
