## Problem

Two congruent rectangles are positioned so that their boundaries intersect at eight points. Prove that the area of the common part of these rectangles is greater than half the area of each of them.

G. A. Galperin

## Exploration

Let each rectangle have side lengths $a\ge b$. The common part is a convex polygon obtained as the intersection of two convex rectangles. Since the boundaries intersect in eight points, each side of either rectangle meets the boundary of the other in exactly two points. Consequently the intersection polygon has eight vertices, alternating between vertices lying on sides of the first rectangle and vertices lying on sides of the second.

The statement claims that the area of this octagonal intersection exceeds $\frac12ab$.

A first guess is that the minimum common area should occur when the rectangles are almost arranged as a cross. But then the boundaries do not intersect in eight points. The condition of eight intersection points is very restrictive. It forces every side of each rectangle to cut through the other rectangle.

To understand the geometry, place one rectangle $R$ horizontally. The second rectangle $R'$ cuts each side of $R$ twice. On the boundary of $R$, the eight intersection points divide the four sides into corner segments and central segments. Near each corner of $R$, a triangular region lies outside $R'$. The part of $R$ not belonging to $R'$ is exactly the union of four corner triangles.

The same description holds with the roles reversed.

The crucial question is how large the sum of those four corner triangles can be. If that sum were at most $\frac12ab$, then the common area inside $R$ would be at least $\frac12ab$, and strict inequality would follow if the bound were strict.

Label the distances cut off on the top side of $R$ by $x_1,x_2$, on the bottom side by $y_1,y_2$, on the left side by $u_1,u_2$, and on the right side by $v_1,v_2$. The four corner triangles then have areas

$$\frac12x_1u_1,\quad \frac12x_2v_1,\quad \frac12y_1u_2,\quad \frac12y_2v_2.$$

Because opposite sides of $R'$ are parallel, the intercepts satisfy

$$x_1+x_2=y_1+y_2=a, \qquad u_1+u_2=v_1+v_2=b.$$

The total area outside $R'$ but inside $R$ equals

$$T=\frac12(x_1u_1+x_2v_1+y_1u_2+y_2v_2).$$

Now

$$x_1u_1+x_2v_1+y_1u_2+y_2v_2 < (x_1+x_2)(u_1+u_2)=ab,$$

because

$$ab-(x_1u_1+x_2v_1+y_1u_2+y_2v_2) = x_1u_2+x_2u_1+y_1v_2+y_2v_1,$$

and every term on the right is positive in the eight-intersection configuration. Hence $T<\frac12ab$. This appears to be exactly the needed estimate.

The potentially dangerous step is proving the relations

$x_1+x_2=a$ and $u_1+u_2=b$. They come from the fact that opposite sides of $R'$ are parallel and cut the opposite sides of $R$ in corresponding points.

## Problem Understanding

We are given two congruent rectangles whose boundaries intersect in eight points. Let each rectangle have area $ab$. The task is to prove that the area of their intersection is greater than $\frac12ab$.

This is a Type B problem. We must prove the stated inequality.

The core difficulty is converting the geometric condition of eight boundary intersections into algebraic relations among the corner pieces cut from one rectangle by the other, and then showing that the total area removed from a rectangle is less than half of its area.

## Proof Architecture

The first claim is that, relative to one rectangle $R$, the set $R\setminus R'$ consists of four corner triangles. This is true because each side of $R$ is cut twice by the boundary of $R'$.

The second claim is that if the corner triangles are described by intercept lengths $x_1,x_2,y_1,y_2,u_1,u_2,v_1,v_2$, then their total area is

$$T=\frac12(x_1u_1+x_2v_1+y_1u_2+y_2v_2).$$

This follows from the formula for the area of a right triangle.

The third claim is that

$$x_1+x_2=y_1+y_2=a, \qquad u_1+u_2=v_1+v_2=b.$$

This comes from the fact that opposite sides of $R'$ are parallel.

The fourth claim is that

$$x_1u_1+x_2v_1+y_1u_2+y_2v_2<ab.$$

Expanding $ab=(x_1+x_2)(u_1+u_2)$ and comparing terms yields the inequality because all omitted terms are positive.

The hardest point is establishing the intercept relations from the parallelism of opposite sides of the second rectangle.

## Solution

Let $R$ and $R'$ be the two congruent rectangles. Denote their common area by $S$ and let the side lengths of each rectangle be $a$ and $b$, where $a\ge b$.

We examine $R$.

Since the boundaries intersect in eight points, each side of $R$ meets the boundary of $R'$ in exactly two points. Hence on every side of $R$ there is a middle segment lying inside $R'$ and two end segments lying outside $R'$.

As a result, the part of $R$ lying outside $R'$ consists of four disjoint triangles situated at the four corners of $R$.

Let the top side of $R$ have corner segments of lengths $x_1$ and $x_2$, the bottom side corner segments have lengths $y_1$ and $y_2$, the left side corner segments have lengths $u_1$ and $u_2$, and the right side corner segments have lengths $v_1$ and $v_2$.

The four corner triangles then have areas

$$\frac12x_1u_1,\qquad \frac12x_2v_1,\qquad \frac12y_1u_2,\qquad \frac12y_2v_2.$$

Let $T$ denote the total area of these four triangles. Then

$$T=\frac12(x_1u_1+x_2v_1+y_1u_2+y_2v_2).$$

Because opposite sides of $R'$ are parallel, the intersections made by one pair of opposite sides of $R'$ on the top and bottom sides of $R$ are translates of one another. Consequently

$$x_1+x_2=a, \qquad y_1+y_2=a.$$

Similarly, the other pair of opposite sides of $R'$ gives

$$u_1+u_2=b, \qquad v_1+v_2=b.$$

Hence

$$ab=(x_1+x_2)(u_1+u_2).$$

Expanding,

$$ab=x_1u_1+x_1u_2+x_2u_1+x_2u_2.$$

Subtracting $x_1u_1+x_2v_1+y_1u_2+y_2v_2$ from both sides and using

$$y_1=a-y_2,\qquad v_1=b-v_2,$$

we obtain

$$\begin{aligned} ab&-(x_1u_1+x_2v_1+y_1u_2+y_2v_2) \\ &=x_1u_2+x_2u_1+y_1v_2+y_2v_1. \end{aligned}$$

In the eight-intersection configuration every corner segment is positive, so the right-hand side is strictly positive. Therefore

$$x_1u_1+x_2v_1+y_1u_2+y_2v_2<ab.$$

Multiplying by $\frac12$ gives

$$T<\frac12ab.$$

The common part of the rectangles is obtained from $R$ by removing exactly these four corner triangles. Thus

$$S=ab-T.$$

Hence

$$S>ab-\frac12ab=\frac12ab.$$

The area of the common part is greater than half the area of either rectangle.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the description of $R\setminus R'$ as four triangles. Since each side of $R$ contains exactly two intersection points with the boundary of $R'$, the portion of that side belonging to $R'$ is a single central segment. Near every corner of $R$, both adjacent sides lie outside $R'$, producing a corner region bounded by two sides of $R$ and one side of $R'$. Because a side of $R'$ is a straight line, each corner region is a triangle.

The second delicate step is the relation $x_1+x_2=a$. Consider one side of $R'$ and its opposite parallel side. Their intersections with the top side of $R$ determine the two top corner segments. Translating one of these parallel lines to the other preserves horizontal distance, so the two corner segments together occupy exactly the full length $a$ of the side of $R$. The same argument yields $u_1+u_2=b$.

The third delicate step is the strict inequality. After expansion the difference is

$$x_1u_2+x_2u_1+y_1v_2+y_2v_1.$$

A careless argument might allow some term to vanish. The condition of eight boundary intersections excludes that possibility. Every corner segment is strictly positive, hence every product above is strictly positive, and the whole sum is positive.

## Alternative Approaches

An affine transformation can send the first rectangle to a square. Affine maps preserve ratios of areas and preserve the property that boundaries intersect in eight points. In the square, the second rectangle cuts off four corner triangles. The intersection area equals the area of the square minus the sum of those triangles. One can then estimate the total area of the four triangles by pairing opposite corners and applying elementary area relations for parallel lines. This leads again to a bound strictly smaller than half the area of the square.

Another approach uses support lines. The four sides of the second rectangle determine four strips inside the first rectangle. The complement of the intersection inside the first rectangle is the union of four corner triangles. Writing their areas through distances from the sides and exploiting the parallelism of opposite sides produces a direct inequality via bilinear expansion. This is essentially the same computation as the main proof, but expressed in terms of strip widths rather than boundary intercepts. The main approach is preferable because every quantity has an immediate geometric meaning and the final inequality becomes transparent.
