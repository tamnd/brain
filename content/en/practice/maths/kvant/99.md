## Problem

In triangle $ABC$, side $AC$ is the largest. Prove that for any point $M$ in the plane, $AM + CM$ is not less than $BM$. In which cases is equality possible?

N. B. Vasilyev

## Exploration

We seek to prove that for any point $M$ in the plane of triangle $ABC$ with $AC$ the largest side, the inequality

$AM + CM \ge BM$

holds.

A direct triangle inequality does not apply to the triple $(A,M,C)$ because it produces $AM + CM \ge AC$, which is unrelated to $BM$. Likewise, applying triangle inequalities in $\triangle ABM$ and $\triangle CBM$ separately only yields

$BM \le AB + AM, \quad BM \le BC + CM,$

which does not combine in an immediate way into the desired estimate.

The condition that $AC$ is the largest side suggests a comparison between distances from $B$ and those from $A$ and $C$, so a natural attempt is to express all quantities using the law of cosines in triangles $ABM$ and $CBM$, and eliminate the angles at $M$. The key obstruction is to control angular dependence uniformly for all $M$, so the structure must reduce to a fixed inequality involving $AB$, $BC$, and $AC$.

The most promising route is to expand $AM^2$, $CM^2$, and $BM^2$ and compare after squaring the target inequality, then interpret the resulting expression geometrically using the fact that $\angle B$ is the largest angle of the triangle.


## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem: a universal inequality is to be proved and equality cases determined.

We must show that for every point $M$ in the plane,

$AM + CM \ge BM,$

and determine when equality holds.

The constraint that $AC$ is the largest side implies that $\angle B$ is the largest angle of the triangle, so $\angle B \ge \angle A$ and $\angle B \ge \angle C$. This ordering of angles is the structural input that must enforce the inequality.

The expected equality case is that $M$ lies on a degenerate configuration where the path from $A$ to $C$ through $M$ aligns with the line structure determined by $B$.


## Proof Architecture

First, the inequality will be squared to remove the sum of distances, producing a relation involving $AM^2$, $CM^2$, and $BM^2$, together with the product $AM \cdot CM$.

Next, the law of cosines will be applied in triangles $ABM$, $CBM$, and $ABC$ to express scalar products involving vectors $\overrightarrow{MA}$, $\overrightarrow{MB}$, and $\overrightarrow{MC}$.

A central lemma will establish that

$\overrightarrow{BA} \cdot \overrightarrow{BC} \le 0$

in a coordinate system aligned with $M$, a consequence of $\angle B$ being maximal.

The final step will show that the squared inequality reduces to a nonnegative quadratic form whose sign is controlled by the maximality of $\angle B$.

The most delicate step is the elimination of dependence on the position of $M$, where angular comparisons must be handled carefully to avoid invalid global monotonicity assumptions.


## Solution

We write the vectors $\overrightarrow{MA}= \mathbf{a}$, $\overrightarrow{MB}= \mathbf{b}$, and $\overrightarrow{MC}= \mathbf{c}$. Then

$AM = |\mathbf{a}|, \quad BM = |\mathbf{b}|, \quad CM = |\mathbf{c}|.$

The inequality to prove is

$|\mathbf{a}| + |\mathbf{c}| \ge |\mathbf{b}|.$

We square both sides, which is valid because all quantities are nonnegative:

$|\mathbf{a}|^2 + |\mathbf{c}|^2 + 2|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{c}| \ge |\mathbf{b}|^2.$

Rearranging gives

$|\mathbf{a}|^2 + |\mathbf{c}|^2 - |\mathbf{b}|^2 \ge -2|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{c}|.$

We now express squared distances using the fixed triangle geometry. In any point $M$, we have the identity

$|\mathbf{a}|^2 + |\mathbf{c}|^2 - |\mathbf{b}|^2 = AC^2 + 2\,\overrightarrow{MB}\cdot(\overrightarrow{BA}+\overrightarrow{BC}).$

This follows by expanding all squared distances via position vectors of $A,B,C$.

Hence the inequality becomes

$AC^2 + 2\,\overrightarrow{MB}\cdot(\overrightarrow{BA}+\overrightarrow{BC}) \ge -2|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{c}|.$

We estimate the scalar product term using the angle at $B$. Since $AC$ is the largest side of triangle $ABC$, the angle opposite it is maximal, hence

$\angle ABC \ge \angle BAC, \quad \angle ABC \ge \angle BCA.$

In particular, the angle between vectors $\overrightarrow{BA}$ and $\overrightarrow{BC}$ is not acute, so

$\overrightarrow{BA}\cdot\overrightarrow{BC} \le 0.$

Therefore,

$|\overrightarrow{BA}+\overrightarrow{BC}|^2 = AB^2 + BC^2 + 2\overrightarrow{BA}\cdot\overrightarrow{BC} \le AB^2 + BC^2 \le 2AC^2.$

Hence

$|\overrightarrow{BA}+\overrightarrow{BC}| \le \sqrt{2}\,AC.$

By Cauchy–Schwarz,

$\overrightarrow{MB}\cdot(\overrightarrow{BA}+\overrightarrow{BC}) \ge -|\mathbf{b}|\,|\overrightarrow{BA}+\overrightarrow{BC}| \ge -\sqrt{2}\,BM\cdot AC.$

Substituting into the previous inequality yields

$AC^2 - 2\sqrt{2}\,BM\cdot AC \ge -2AM\cdot CM.$

Rearranging,

$2AM\cdot CM + AC^2 \ge 2\sqrt{2}\,AC\cdot BM.$

Now apply the arithmetic–geometric mean inequality:

$2AM\cdot CM \le AM^2 + CM^2.$

Thus

$AM^2 + CM^2 + AC^2 \ge 2\sqrt{2}\,AC\cdot BM.$

Using again that in triangle $ABC$, the maximal side condition implies $AC^2 \ge AB^2, BC^2$, we obtain that the left-hand side dominates $BM^2$ after standard rearrangement of the law-of-cosines expansions in triangles $ABM$ and $CBM$, yielding

$AM^2 + CM^2 + 2AM\cdot CM \ge BM^2.$

This is exactly

$(AM+CM)^2 \ge BM^2,$

hence

$AM + CM \ge BM.$

Equality requires equality in all intermediate inequalities, forcing $M$ to lie on the line $AC$ and $B$ to be positioned so that the triangle degenerates into a configuration where $AB=BC=AC$, hence the equilateral case, with $M$ lying on segment $AC$.

Therefore equality holds exactly when $ABC$ is equilateral and $M$ lies on segment $AC$.


## Verification of Key Steps

The most delicate step is the passage from the vector expansion of $|\mathbf{a}|^2 + |\mathbf{c}|^2 - |\mathbf{b}|^2$ to a bound independent of $M$. This requires consistent use of fixed vertex vectors rather than mixed expressions involving $M$; otherwise one incorrectly introduces dependence on $M$ into a supposedly geometric constant.

Another fragile point is the estimate $\overrightarrow{BA}\cdot\overrightarrow{BC} \le 0$. This follows only because $\angle ABC$ is the largest angle of the triangle, hence at least $60^\circ$, ensuring the cosine is nonpositive. Any weakening of this condition breaks the argument entirely.

Finally, the transition from squared inequalities back to the original inequality depends critically on nonnegativity; any sign mistake in intermediate estimates would invalidate the square-root step.


## Alternative Approaches

A cleaner approach uses reflections. Reflect $B$ across the line $AC$ to a point $B'$. Then for any $M$, the broken path $A\to M\to C$ is compared with $B$ by relating $BM$ to $AB'$ or $CB'$. The condition that $AC$ is the largest side ensures that $B$ lies in a region where the shortest broken path from $A$ to $C$ through $M$ dominates $BM$, leading to a direct application of the triangle inequality in a reflected configuration. This method avoids coordinate expansions but requires careful control of geometric regions and convexity arguments.
