## Problem

Investigate how many solutions the system of equations $$ \left\{\begin{array}{l} x^2+y^2+xy=a,\\ x^2-y^2=b, \end{array}\right. $$ has, where $a$ and $b$ are real numbers.

E. A. Yasinovyy

## Exploration

Introduce the linear change of variables

$u = x + y, \qquad v = x - y.$

Then

$x = \frac{u+v}{2}, \qquad y = \frac{u-v}{2},$

so the system can be rewritten entirely in terms of $u$ and $v$.

A direct computation yields

$x^2 + y^2 + xy = \frac{3u^2 + v^2}{4}, \qquad x^2 - y^2 = uv.$

Hence the system becomes

$\frac{3u^2 + v^2}{4} = a, \qquad uv = b.$

Substituting $v = b/u$ (with $u \neq 0$) transforms the problem into a quartic equation in $u$, but it becomes quadratic in $t = u^2$. This reduction suggests that the number of solutions depends on how many nonnegative roots $t$ has, and on how each such root lifts to pairs $(u,v)$.

The key subtlety is the sign structure: $t = u^2$ must be nonnegative, and each admissible $u$ produces exactly one $v$, but $u$ itself has two signs. The classification therefore reduces to counting admissible values of $t$ and checking their positivity.


## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem. One must determine, for real parameters $a$ and $b$, how many real solutions the system

$x^2 + y^2 + xy = a, \qquad x^2 - y^2 = b$

has.

The system is symmetric enough that a linear change of variables should reduce it to an algebraic equation in a single variable. The main difficulty lies in correctly translating the number of real solutions of the reduced equation back to the number of ordered pairs $(x,y)$, including all sign and degeneracy cases.

The final answer is determined by the sign of $a$ and the discriminant condition $4a^2 \ge 3b^2$, with special behavior at the boundary.


## Proof Architecture

The first lemma establishes the identities

$x^2 + y^2 + xy = \frac{3u^2 + v^2}{4}, \qquad x^2 - y^2 = uv.$

The second lemma shows that the system is equivalent, for $u \neq 0$, to the equation

$3u^4 - 4au^2 + b^2 = 0.$

The third lemma proves that setting $t = u^2$ reduces the problem to a quadratic equation

$3t^2 - 4at + b^2 = 0,$

and characterizes when $t \ge 0$.

The fourth lemma classifies the sign of the roots using the relations

$t_1 + t_2 = \frac{4a}{3}, \qquad t_1 t_2 = \frac{b^2}{3}.$

The final lemma translates admissible $t$ values into the exact number of $(x,y)$ solutions, accounting for the two possible signs of $u$.

The most delicate step is the reconstruction of $(x,y)$ from $(u,v)$ and the handling of degenerate cases where $u = 0$.


## Solution

Introduce the variables

$u = x + y, \qquad v = x - y.$

Then

$x = \frac{u+v}{2}, \qquad y = \frac{u-v}{2}.$

A direct expansion gives

$x^2 + y^2 + xy = \frac{3u^2 + v^2}{4},$

$x^2 - y^2 = uv.$

Thus the system is equivalent to

$\frac{3u^2 + v^2}{4} = a, \qquad uv = b.$

From $uv = b$, if $u \neq 0$ then $v = b/u$. Substituting into the first equation yields

$\frac{3u^2 + \frac{b^2}{u^2}}{4} = a.$

Multiplying by $4u^2$ gives

$3u^4 - 4au^2 + b^2 = 0.$

Set $t = u^2 \ge 0$. Then

$3t^2 - 4at + b^2 = 0.$

Its discriminant is

$\Delta = 16a^2 - 12b^2.$

Thus real roots exist exactly when $4a^2 \ge 3b^2$.

The roots are

$t_{1,2} = \frac{2a \pm \sqrt{4a^2 - 3b^2}}{3}.$

From Vieta’s formulas,

$t_1 + t_2 = \frac{4a}{3}, \qquad t_1 t_2 = \frac{b^2}{3} \ge 0.$

If $4a^2 > 3b^2$ and $a < 0$, then $t_1 + t_2 < 0$ while $t_1 t_2 > 0$, so both roots are negative and hence inadmissible since $t = u^2 \ge 0$. Therefore there are no real solutions in this case.

If $4a^2 > 3b^2$ and $a > 0$, then $t_1 + t_2 > 0$ and $t_1 t_2 > 0$, so both $t_1, t_2 > 0$. Each admissible $t$ produces two values of $u$, namely $u = \pm \sqrt{t}$. For each such $u$, the value $v = b/u$ is uniquely determined, hence each $t$ contributes exactly two distinct pairs $(u,v)$ and therefore two distinct pairs $(x,y)$. Since there are two positive roots, the total number of solutions is $4$.

If $4a^2 < 3b^2$, there are no real values of $t$, hence no real solutions.

In the boundary case $4a^2 = 3b^2$, there is a double root

$t = \frac{2a}{3}.$

If $a < 0$, then $t < 0$, so no solutions exist. If $a > 0$, then $t > 0$, giving $u = \pm \sqrt{t}$ and hence two solutions. If $a = 0$, then necessarily $b = 0$ and the system reduces to

$x^2 + y^2 + xy = 0, \qquad x^2 = y^2,$

which forces $x = y = 0$, giving exactly one solution.

Thus the number of solutions is

\begin{cases} 0, & 4a^2 < 3b^2 \text{ or } (4a^2 > 3b^2,\, a < 0), \\ 1, & a = 0,\, b = 0, \\ 2, & 4a^2 = 3b^2,\, a > 0, \\ 4, & 4a^2 > 3b^2,\, a > 0. \end{cases} }$$--- ## Verification of Key Steps The transformation $(x,y) \mapsto (u,v)$ is bijective because the inverse relations $x = (u+v)/2$ and $y = (u-v)/2$ uniquely recover $(x,y)$ from $(u,v)$. The reduction to $t = u^2$ is valid whenever $u \neq 0$, and the case $u = 0$ corresponds to $b = uv = 0$, which is handled separately; direct substitution shows that this occurs only in the degenerate case $a = b = 0$. The sign classification of $t_1, t_2$ is forced by Vieta’s relations: if $t_1 t_2 > 0$, then the roots have the same sign, and that sign equals the sign of $t_1 + t_2 = 4a/3$, which determines whether both are positive or both negative. The lifting from $(t,u)$ to solutions is exact because for each $u \neq 0$, the equation $v = b/u$ determines a unique $v$, and distinct choices of $u$ yield distinct $(x,y)$ via the linear inverse transformation. --- ## Alternative Approaches One can solve the system by eliminating $y$ directly, leading to a quartic equation in $x$ whose discriminant analysis reproduces the same condition $4a^2 \ge 3b^2$. Another approach uses geometry: the equations represent a conic intersection, and the change to $(u,v)$ diagonalizes the quadratic forms, turning the problem into the intersection of a parabola in $u^2$ with sign constraints on $u$. The $(u,v)$ method is preferable because it reduces the system immediately to a quadratic in $u^2$, making both classification and counting transparent.
