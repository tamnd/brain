## Problem

Investigate how many solutions the system of equations $$\left\{\begin{array}{l} x^2+y^2+xy=a,\\ x^2-y^2=b, \end{array}\right.$$ has, where $a$ and $b$ are some real numbers.

E. A. Yasinovy̆ĭ

## Exploration

Let

$$u=x^2,\qquad v=y^2,\qquad t=xy.$$

Then the system becomes

$$u+v+t=a,\qquad u-v=b.$$

The variables $u,v,t$ are not independent, since $t^2=uv$.

From $u-v=b$ we obtain $u=v+b$. Substituting into the first equation gives

$$2v+t=a-b,$$

hence

$$t=a-b-2v.$$

Using $t^2=uv=v(v+b)$,

$$(a-b-2v)^2=v(v+b).$$

This is a quadratic equation in $v$. Expanding,

$$4v^2-4(a-b)v+(a-b)^2=v^2+bv,$$

so

$$3v^2-(4a-3b)v+(a-b)^2=0.$$

The number of real solutions $(x,y)$ should be determined by the number of admissible roots $v=y^2\ge0$. The discriminant of this quadratic is

$$(4a-3b)^2-12(a-b)^2 =4a^2-3b^2.$$

Thus the quantity

$$\Delta=4a^2-3b^2$$

must govern the answer.

Before drawing conclusions, it is useful to parametrize the solutions directly. Solving the linear system for $u$ and $v$,

$$v=\frac{4a-3b\pm\sqrt{\Delta}}6,\qquad u=\frac{4a+3b\pm\sqrt{\Delta}}6.$$

The same sign must be chosen in both formulas.

For each choice of sign we have

$$uv=\frac{(4a\pm\sqrt{\Delta})^2-(3b)^2}{36} =\frac{2a^2-ab-b^2\pm a\sqrt{\Delta}}3.$$

A simpler computation using $t=a-u-v$ yields

$$t=\frac{a\mp\sqrt{\Delta}}3.$$

Then

$$u,v\ge0,\qquad t^2=uv.$$

For a fixed admissible triple $(u,v,t)$, the equations

$$x^2=u,\qquad y^2=v,\qquad xy=t$$

determine the signs. If $u,v>0$ and $t\neq0$, there are exactly two solutions:

$$(x,y),\quad (-x,-y).$$

If $u=v=t=0$, there is one solution. If exactly one of $u,v$ is zero, then necessarily $t=0$, again giving one solution.

The delicate point is to determine, from $a$ and $b$, how many admissible sign choices occur and when one of them degenerates.

## Problem Understanding

We must determine, for arbitrary real parameters $a$ and $b$, the number of real solutions $(x,y)$ of

$$\begin{cases} x^2+y^2+xy=a,\\ x^2-y^2=b. \end{cases}$$

This is a Type A problem. We must classify all parameter pairs $(a,b)$ according to the number of solutions.

The core difficulty is that the system is quadratic. After introducing

$$u=x^2,\qquad v=y^2,\qquad t=xy,$$

the system becomes linear in $u,v,t$, and the nonlinear condition is reduced to $t^2=uv$. The resulting quadratic equation has discriminant $4a^2-3b^2$, which controls existence and multiplicity.

The answer will depend on whether $4a^2-3b^2$ is negative, zero, or positive, and on certain boundary cases where one of $u,v$ vanishes.

## Proof Architecture

First, derive explicit formulas for $u=x^2$, $v=y^2$, and $t=xy$ in terms of $a$, $b$, and $\sqrt{4a^2-3b^2}$.

Second, prove that real solutions can exist only when $4a^2-3b^2\ge0$, because otherwise the quadratic equation for $v$ has no real roots.

Third, prove that for each sign choice

$$\varepsilon=\pm1,$$

the corresponding triple is

$$u_\varepsilon=\frac{4a+3b+\varepsilon\sqrt{\Delta}}6,\quad v_\varepsilon=\frac{4a-3b+\varepsilon\sqrt{\Delta}}6,\quad t_\varepsilon=\frac{a-\varepsilon\sqrt{\Delta}}3,$$

where $\Delta=4a^2-3b^2$.

Fourth, prove that a sign choice produces real $(x,y)$ if and only if $u_\varepsilon\ge0$ and $v_\varepsilon\ge0$.

Fifth, count the solutions arising from each admissible sign choice. An admissible nondegenerate triple gives two solutions, while the triple $(0,0,0)$ gives one.

The most delicate step is determining exactly when both sign choices are admissible and when one of them degenerates.

## Solution

Put

$$u=x^2,\qquad v=y^2,\qquad t=xy.$$

Then

$$u+v+t=a,\qquad u-v=b,$$

and

$$t^2=uv.$$

From $u-v=b$ we obtain

$$u=v+b.$$

Substituting into $u+v+t=a$,

$$t=a-b-2v.$$

Using $t^2=uv=v(v+b)$,

$$(a-b-2v)^2=v(v+b).$$

After expansion,

$$3v^2-(4a-3b)v+(a-b)^2=0.$$

Its discriminant equals

$$\Delta=(4a-3b)^2-12(a-b)^2 =4a^2-3b^2.$$

Hence a necessary condition for existence of real solutions is

$$\Delta\ge0.$$

Assume $\Delta\ge0$. Solving the quadratic,

$$v_\varepsilon=\frac{4a-3b+\varepsilon\sqrt{\Delta}}6,$$

where $\varepsilon=\pm1$. Since $u=v+b$,

$$u_\varepsilon=\frac{4a+3b+\varepsilon\sqrt{\Delta}}6.$$

Finally,

$$t_\varepsilon=a-u_\varepsilon-v_\varepsilon =\frac{a-\varepsilon\sqrt{\Delta}}3.$$

For each $\varepsilon$,

$$t_\varepsilon^2=u_\varepsilon v_\varepsilon,$$

because the triple was obtained from the equation $t^2=uv$. Therefore real numbers $x,y$ exist precisely when

$$u_\varepsilon\ge0,\qquad v_\varepsilon\ge0.$$

Now

$$u_{+}=\frac{4a+3b+\sqrt{\Delta}}6,\qquad v_{+}=\frac{4a-3b+\sqrt{\Delta}}6.$$

Since $\sqrt{\Delta}\le2|a|$, the inequalities

$$4a\pm3b+\sqrt{\Delta}\ge0$$

are equivalent to $a\ge0$. Thus the sign $\varepsilon=+1$ is admissible exactly when $a\ge0$.

Similarly,

$$u_{-}=\frac{4a+3b-\sqrt{\Delta}}6,\qquad v_{-}=\frac{4a-3b-\sqrt{\Delta}}6.$$

The inequalities

$$4a\pm3b-\sqrt{\Delta}\ge0$$

are equivalent to

$$a\ge0,\qquad a^2\ge b^2.$$

Since $\Delta\ge0$, this reduces to

$$a\ge |b|.$$

Hence the sign $\varepsilon=-1$ is admissible exactly when $a\ge|b|$.

We now count solutions.

If $\Delta<0$, no admissible triple exists, hence there are no solutions.

Suppose $\Delta=0$. Then there is only one triple:

$$u=\frac{4a+3b}{6},\qquad v=\frac{4a-3b}{6},\qquad t=\frac a3.$$

Existence requires $a\ge0$. Since $\Delta=0$,

$$4a^2=3b^2.$$

If $a>0$, then $u>0$, $v>0$, $t\ne0$, and there are exactly two solutions,

$$(x,y),\quad (-x,-y).$$

If $a=b=0$, then $(x,y)=(0,0)$ is the unique solution.

Now let $\Delta>0$.

If $a<0$, neither sign is admissible, hence there are no solutions.

If

$$0\le a<|b|,$$

only the sign $\varepsilon=+1$ is admissible. The corresponding triple satisfies $u>0$, $v>0$, $t\ne0$, so it yields exactly two solutions.

If

$$a>|b|,$$

both signs are admissible. For each sign, $u>0$, $v>0$, $t\ne0$. Each admissible triple gives exactly two solutions, and the two triples are distinct because $\Delta>0$. Hence there are

$$2+2=4$$

solutions.

Finally, let

$$a=|b|>0.$$

Then $\Delta=a^2>0$.

For one sign choice we obtain a nondegenerate triple, yielding two solutions.

For the other sign choice,

$$(u,v,t)=(a,0,0)$$

when $b=a$, or

$$(u,v,t)=(0,a,0)$$

when $b=-a$.

This yields exactly one solution pair,

$$(\sqrt a,0),\,(-\sqrt a,0)$$

or

$$(0,\sqrt a),\,(0,-\sqrt a),$$

respectively. Thus this branch contributes two solutions.

The total number of solutions is again $4$.

Combining all cases, the number $N(a,b)$ of real solutions is

$$N(a,b)= \begin{cases} 0,&4a^2-3b^2<0,\\[2mm] 0,&4a^2-3b^2\ge0,\ a<0,\\[2mm] 1,&(a,b)=(0,0),\\[2mm] 2,&a\ge0,\ 4a^2-3b^2=0,\ (a,b)\ne(0,0),\\[2mm] 2,&4a^2-3b^2>0,\ 0\le a<|b|,\\[2mm] 4,&4a^2-3b^2>0,\ a\ge|b|,\ (a,b)\ne(0,0). \end{cases}$$

Therefore the complete classification is

$$\boxed{ N(a,b)= \begin{cases} 0,&a<0\ \text{or}\ 4a^2-3b^2<0,\\[1mm] 1,&(a,b)=(0,0),\\[1mm] 2,&a\ge0,\ 4a^2-3b^2=0,\ (a,b)\ne(0,0),\\[1mm] 2,&4a^2-3b^2>0,\ 0\le a<|b|,\\[1mm] 4,&4a^2-3b^2>0,\ a\ge|b|. \end{cases}}$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the reduction to the quadratic equation for $v$. Starting from

$$u=v+b,\qquad t=a-b-2v,$$

the identity $t^2=uv$ gives

$$(a-b-2v)^2=v(v+b).$$

Expanding both sides yields

$$a^2-2ab+b^2-4(a-b)v+4v^2=v^2+bv,$$

hence

$$3v^2-(4a-3b)v+(a-b)^2=0.$$

Any sign error in the coefficient of $v$ changes the discriminant and invalidates the classification.

The second delicate step is determining admissibility of the branch $\varepsilon=-1$. We need

$$4a\pm3b-\sqrt{\Delta}\ge0.$$

These are equivalent to

$$4a-3|b|\ge\sqrt{\Delta}.$$

Both sides are nonnegative only when $a\ge|b|$. Squaring then gives

$$(4a-3|b|)^2-(4a^2-3b^2) =12(a-|b|)^2\ge0,$$

so the inequality indeed holds. Without checking nonnegativity before squaring, one could obtain incorrect extra cases.

The third delicate step is counting solutions from a fixed admissible triple. When $u,v>0$, the equations

$$x^2=u,\qquad y^2=v$$

produce four sign combinations, but the condition $xy=t$ retains exactly two of them. If $t>0$, the surviving pairs are $(\sqrt u,\sqrt v)$ and $(-\sqrt u,-\sqrt v)$; if $t<0$, they are $(\sqrt u,-\sqrt v)$ and $(-\sqrt u,\sqrt v)$. Thus each nondegenerate branch contributes exactly two solutions.

## Alternative Approaches

Instead of introducing $u,v,t$, one may set

$$s=x+y,\qquad d=x-y.$$

Then

$$x^2+y^2+xy=\frac{3s^2+d^2}{4}, \qquad x^2-y^2=sd.$$

The system becomes

$$3s^2+d^2=4a,\qquad sd=b.$$

Eliminating $d=b/s$ leads to

$$3s^4-4as^2+b^2=0.$$

This is a quadratic equation in $s^2$ with discriminant $16(4a^2-3b^2)$. The same quantity $4a^2-3b^2$ appears naturally, and the counting reduces to determining how many nonnegative roots $s^2$ occur.

The approach using $u=x^2$, $v=y^2$, and $t=xy$ is preferable because the admissibility conditions are expressed directly in terms of squares and the final counting of solution pairs becomes completely transparent.
