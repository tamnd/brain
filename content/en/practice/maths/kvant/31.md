## Problem

A square sheet of paper is cut by a straight line into two pieces. One of the resulting pieces is then again cut into two pieces, and this process is repeated many times. What is the smallest number of cuts that must be made so that among the resulting pieces there are exactly one hundred 20-gons?

I. N. Bernstein

Moscow Mathematical Olympiad (1970)

## Exploration

We begin by experimenting with small examples. Consider a square as a polygon with four sides. A single cut across the square can produce two polygons; depending on the cut, these can be triangles, quadrilaterals, or other polygons with more sides. Each subsequent cut is applied to exactly one existing piece, and the number of sides of the resulting pieces is determined by the geometry of the cut. Observing the simplest cases, if we make one cut, the total number of pieces becomes two. Each new cut increases the total number of pieces by one, but crucially, it can also increase the number of sides of one piece substantially if the cut is chosen to pass through existing vertices.

We notice a pattern: a polygon with $n$ sides can be cut into two polygons whose total number of sides increases by two. That is, if a polygon has $s$ sides, after one cut, the sum of the sides of the two pieces becomes $s + 2$. Testing this on small polygons confirms the pattern: cutting a quadrilateral into two quadrilaterals increases the total sides from $4$ to $6$, and cutting a quadrilateral into a triangle and a pentagon increases the total sides from $4$ to $3 + 5 = 8$, but the minimum total sides achievable by a cut is $6 = 4 + 2$. Therefore, to minimize the number of cuts to reach a given number of 20-gons, we should aim to maximize the number of sides in each piece created by each cut.

The core difficulty lies in modeling the process combinatorially: each cut increases the total number of pieces by one and increases the sum of the sides by exactly two. We need to determine how many cuts are required so that exactly 100 pieces are 20-gons, starting from a 4-gon.

## Problem Understanding

The problem asks for the smallest number of cuts to produce exactly one hundred 20-gons from a square by repeatedly cutting one piece at a time. This is a Type C problem, as we are minimizing the number of cuts required to reach a target configuration. The challenge is to relate the number of sides created to the number of cuts, tracking both the number of pieces and the sum of their sides. Intuitively, each cut increases the total number of sides by two and the number of pieces by one, so we can set up a simple linear relationship to solve for the minimum number of cuts.

The expected answer is an integer. We anticipate that the minimal strategy is to cut each time in a way that produces as many 20-gons as possible with as few cuts as possible, by always increasing the number of sides of a piece efficiently.
