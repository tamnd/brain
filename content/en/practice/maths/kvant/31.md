## Problem

A square sheet of paper is cut along a straight line into two pieces. One of the resulting pieces is then cut again into two pieces, and this process is repeated many times. What is the smallest number of cuts that must be made so that among the resulting pieces there are exactly one hundred 20-gons?

I. N. Bernstein

Moscow Mathematical Olympiad (1970)

## Exploration

After each cut, exactly one existing piece is replaced by two new pieces. Hence after $n$ cuts there are $n+1$ pieces.

The quantity that matters is the number of sides of the pieces. Let $s(P)$ denote the number of sides of a polygonal piece $P$. Initially there is one square, so the total number of sides is $4$.

Suppose a piece with $k$ sides is cut by a straight segment joining two boundary points. Let the resulting pieces have $a$ and $b$ sides. Along the boundary of the original polygon, the cut endpoints divide the boundary into two chains. If these chains contain $r$ and $k-r$ sides of the original polygon, then each new piece consists of one chain together with the cut segment. Thus

$$a=r+2,\qquad b=(k-r)+2,$$

and therefore

$$a+b=k+4.$$

Replacing one $k$-gon by polygons with $a$ and $b$ sides increases the total number of sides by

$$(a+b)-k=4.$$

Hence every cut increases the total number of sides of all pieces by exactly $4$.

To obtain one hundred $20$-gons, the total number of sides among all pieces must be at least

$$100\cdot 20=2000.$$

Since after $n$ cuts the total number of sides equals

$$4+4n,$$

we need

$$4+4n\ge 2000,$$

so $n\ge 499$.

The lower bound is easy. The difficult point is whether it can actually be attained. Equality in the side count would force every piece to be a $20$-gon, because with $499$ cuts the total number of sides is exactly $2000$ and there are exactly $500$ pieces. Thus the average number of sides is $4$, which seems impossible. This indicates that the previous reasoning is incomplete.

Let us compute more carefully. After $499$ cuts there are $500$ pieces. If exactly $100$ of them are $20$-gons, the remaining $400$ pieces must contribute

$$2000-100\cdot20=0$$

sides if the total side count is exactly $2000$, which is impossible. Therefore $499$ cuts cannot suffice.

Let $n$ be the number of cuts. Then the total number of sides is $4n+4$. Besides the $100$ desired $20$-gons, there are

$$(n+1)-100=n-99$$

other pieces. Every polygonal piece has at least $3$ sides. Hence

$$4n+4\ge 100\cdot20+3(n-99).$$

This yields

$$4n+4\ge 2000+3n-297,$$

so

$$n\ge1699.$$

Now test whether equality is possible. If $n=1699$, then there are $1700$ pieces, namely $100$ twenty-gons and $1600$ others. The inequality becomes an equality, so all the remaining $1600$ pieces must be triangles.

Thus the target configuration is:

$$100\ \text{twenty-gons} \quad\text{and}\quad 1600\ \text{triangles}.$$

The remaining task is to show that such a configuration can actually be produced.

A cut of a $k$-gon can create a triangle and a $(k+1)$-gon by cutting off a corner. Repeating this operation on the same piece increases its number of sides by $1$ at each step while producing one new triangle.

Starting from a square, one corner cut yields a triangle and a pentagon. Repeating on the pentagon yields another triangle and a hexagon, and so on. After $16$ such corner cuts, the evolving polygon becomes a $20$-gon and $16$ triangles have been created. Thus one $20$-gon can be produced from a square using $16$ cuts, generating $16$ triangles.

To obtain $100$ twenty-gons, first create $100$ quadrilateral pieces. Since each cut increases the number of pieces by $1$, producing $100$ pieces from one square requires $99$ cuts. Then independently transform each quadrilateral into a $20$-gon by $16$ corner cuts, producing $16$ triangles each. This requires

$$99+100\cdot16=1699$$

cuts.

The numbers match the lower bound.

The crucial point is proving the inequality leading to $1699$ and constructing a configuration achieving equality.

## Problem Understanding

We start with a square. Each cut is a straight-line cut applied to exactly one existing piece, splitting that piece into two polygonal pieces. After many such cuts, among all resulting pieces there must be exactly one hundred $20$-gons.

This is a Type C problem. We must determine the smallest possible number of cuts and prove both that fewer cuts are impossible and that the claimed number can be achieved.

The core difficulty is to relate the combinatorics of polygon sides to the number of cuts. The answer should arise from counting the total number of sides of all pieces and exploiting the fact that every remaining piece has at least three sides.

The answer is

$$1699.$$

Intuitively, every desired $20$-gon requires many additional sides. The most economical way to supply them is for all non-$20$-gons to be triangles.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1. If a $k$-gon is cut into polygons with $a$ and $b$ sides, then $a+b=k+4$; this follows by tracing the two boundary arcs between the cut endpoints.

Lemma 2. After $n$ cuts, the total number of sides of all pieces equals $4n+4$; this follows from Lemma 1 because each cut increases the total side count by $4$.

Lemma 3. If after $n$ cuts there are exactly one hundred $20$-gons, then $n\ge1699$; compare the total number of sides from Lemma 2 with the minimum possible contribution of the remaining pieces, namely three sides each.

Lemma 4. A quadrilateral can be transformed into a $20$-gon by $16$ cuts, producing $16$ triangles; each cut removes a corner and increases the side count of the remaining polygon by $1$.

Lemma 5. One can obtain $100$ quadrilaterals from the original square using $99$ cuts; repeatedly cut a quadrilateral into two quadrilaterals.

The hardest direction is proving optimality. The lemma most likely to fail under scrutiny is Lemma 3, because it contains the key counting argument.

## Solution

Let $S$ denote the sum of the numbers of sides of all pieces.

Suppose a $k$-gon is cut by a straight segment whose endpoints lie on its boundary. The two endpoints divide the boundary of the $k$-gon into two boundary chains. If these chains contain $r$ and $k-r$ sides of the original polygon, then the two new polygons consist of these chains together with the cut segment. Consequently their numbers of sides are

$$r+2,\qquad (k-r)+2.$$

Hence

$$(r+2)+((k-r)+2)=k+4.$$

Thus replacing the original $k$-gon by the two new polygons increases the total number of sides by exactly $4$.

Initially there is one square, so $S=4$. After each cut, $S$ increases by $4$. Therefore after $n$ cuts,

$$S=4+4n.$$

Also, after $n$ cuts there are exactly $n+1$ pieces.

Assume that among these pieces there are exactly one hundred $20$-gons. The remaining number of pieces is

$$(n+1)-100=n-99.$$

Every polygon has at least three sides. Therefore

$$S\ge100\cdot20+3(n-99).$$

Substituting $S=4n+4$ gives

$$4n+4\ge2000+3n-297.$$

Hence

$$n\ge1699.$$

Thus at least $1699$ cuts are necessary.

It remains to show that $1699$ cuts are sufficient.

First produce $100$ quadrilaterals from the original square. Since each cut increases the number of pieces by one, this requires exactly $99$ cuts. It is possible because any quadrilateral can be cut by a segment joining two opposite sides so that both resulting pieces are quadrilaterals.

Now take one of these quadrilaterals. Cut off a corner by a segment joining two adjacent sides near a vertex. The removed piece is a triangle, while the remaining polygon gains one side. Thus a quadrilateral becomes a pentagon. Repeating the same operation on the larger polygon successively yields

$$4\to5\to6\to\cdots\to20.$$

The number of cuts required is

$$20-4=16.$$

At the end we have one $20$-gon and $16$ triangles.

Perform this procedure independently on each of the $100$ quadrilaterals. This uses

$$100\cdot16=1600$$

additional cuts and produces exactly $100$ twenty-gons.

The total number of cuts is

$$99+1600=1699.$$

Since $1699$ cuts are sufficient and no smaller number can suffice, the minimum number of cuts is

$$\boxed{1699}.$$

Equality is attained when the final pieces consist of $100$ twenty-gons and $1600$ triangles.

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the identity $a+b=k+4$. The cut segment appears as a side of each new polygon, contributing two sides in total. Every original side of the $k$-gon belongs to exactly one of the two boundary chains. Hence the original contribution $k$ is replaced by $k+4$, not by $k+2$ or $k+3$.

The second delicate step is the lower bound. After $n$ cuts there are $n+1$ pieces. Removing the one hundred desired $20$-gons leaves $n-99$ pieces. Each of these must have at least three sides. Replacing this minimum by $4$ would give a much weaker bound and would miss the optimum. The estimate

$$4n+4\ge2000+3(n-99)$$

is the exact place where the value $1699$ arises.

The third delicate step is the construction of a $20$-gon from a quadrilateral. Cutting off a corner removes a triangle and replaces the chosen vertex by two new vertices. The remaining polygon gains exactly one side. Repeating the operation sixteen times raises the side count from $4$ to $20$. A mistaken count of the side increase would invalidate the construction.

## Alternative Approaches

A graph-theoretic approach treats the final arrangement as a planar subdivision of the original square. Using Euler's formula, one can express the total number of sides of all pieces in terms of the number of cuts and derive the identity $S=4n+4$ without analyzing individual cuts. The remainder of the argument is the same counting estimate.

Another approach starts from the desired final configuration. If there are $100$ twenty-gons and $m$ other pieces, then the total side count is at least $2000+3m$. Since every cut contributes exactly four units to the total side count and increases the number of pieces by one, one obtains a system of inequalities whose unique extremal solution is $m=1600$ and $n=1699$. The constructive part then shows that this extremal configuration is realizable. The direct side-counting method used above is preferable because it makes the invariant $S=4n+4$ completely transparent.
