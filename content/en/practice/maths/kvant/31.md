## Problem

A square sheet of paper is cut along a straight line into two pieces. One of the resulting pieces is then cut into two again, and this process is repeated many times. What is the minimum number of cuts required so that among the resulting pieces there are exactly one hundred 20-sided polygons?

I. N. Bernstein

Moscow Mathematical Olympiad (1970)

## Exploration

We consider a square sheet of paper that is being cut repeatedly along straight lines. Each cut increases the total number of pieces by exactly one. Our target is to produce exactly one hundred 20-sided polygons. A square initially has four sides. Each cut adds at most one side to the newly created piece if done along an edge and can add more if it intersects the interior. To form a 20-sided polygon from a square, successive cuts must increase the number of sides from 4 to 20.

Testing small examples, we see that a square cut once along a straight line produces two pieces with at most 5 sides each if the cut is not aligned with an edge. Two successive cuts can produce a piece with at most 6 or 7 sides, depending on placement. Each cut adds exactly one new piece, so to reach 100 pieces in total, at least 99 cuts are required. The challenge is to ensure that among these pieces, exactly 100 are 20-gons.

The crucial observation is that the number of sides of a polygon increases by at most one with each new cut that intersects it. Therefore, to transform a square into a 20-gon requires exactly 16 cuts on that piece. The problem reduces to distributing cuts across pieces so that 100 of them reach 20 sides while minimizing the total number of cuts.

The delicate step is ensuring no piece exceeds 20 sides and that no extra 20-gons are produced inadvertently by a cut affecting multiple pieces. Understanding how cuts contribute to both the number of pieces and the number of sides is central to the solution.

## Problem Understanding

The problem asks for the minimum number of cuts required to obtain exactly 100 pieces, each with 20 sides, starting from a square and repeatedly cutting pieces along straight lines. This is a Type C problem, as we are asked to find the minimum number of cuts, not just to construct an example. The core difficulty lies in counting the number of sides added by each cut and ensuring that each 20-gon is formed efficiently without wasting cuts.

Intuitively, each 20-gon requires 16 additional sides from its original square, and each cut produces one new piece, so we must balance the creation of pieces with the growth of sides. The answer should be attainable by a strategy in which each cut contributes optimally to producing a 20-gon, likely by working on one piece at a time and splitting it so that side-count growth is maximized.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1: Each straight cut increases the total number of pieces by exactly one. This is true because a straight cut separates a polygon into two connected pieces.

Lemma 2: A cut can increase the number of sides of a polygon by at most one. If the cut intersects only two edges, the new polygon has one additional edge compared to the original. This is geometric: cutting across an existing polygon replaces part of its boundary by a new segment.

Lemma 3: To transform a square into a 20-gon requires exactly 16 cuts applied to that piece. Starting from 4 sides, each cut adds one side, and $4 + 16 = 20$.

Lemma 4: To obtain 100 twenty-sided polygons, each square must be processed individually into a 20-gon; cuts cannot be shared efficiently across multiple pieces without creating extra 20-gons. This is because any cut intersects only one polygon, producing one new piece; side growth is local.

Lemma 5: The minimum number of cuts is the sum of cuts required to produce 100 pieces and the additional cuts to increase sides to 20 for each. There are 100 twenty-gons, initially one square, so 99 cuts create 100 pieces, and each piece requires 16 side-increasing cuts, totaling $16 \cdot 100 = 1600$ side-increasing cuts. Therefore, total minimum cuts are $1600 + 99 = 1699$.

The hardest direction is ensuring that no fewer cuts can produce 100 twenty-sided polygons. Lemma 4 is most delicate, as it relies on the fact that side-increasing cuts cannot be shared efficiently among pieces.

## Solution

By Lemma 1, each straight cut increases the total number of pieces by exactly one. Starting with one square, we need to obtain 100 polygons, so at least 99 cuts are required to reach 100 pieces.

By Lemma 2, each cut increases the number of sides of a polygon by at most one. A square has 4 sides, so transforming it into a 20-gon requires $20 - 4 = 16$ side-increasing cuts, as stated in Lemma 3.

To obtain exactly 100 twenty-sided polygons, each of the 100 pieces must undergo 16 side-increasing cuts. These cuts are applied sequentially to each piece; no cut can efficiently contribute to side growth in multiple polygons without producing extra 20-gons prematurely, confirming Lemma 4.

Hence, the total number of cuts needed consists of the 99 cuts to create 100 pieces and the 1600 cuts needed to raise each piece to 20 sides. Summing these yields $1600 + 99 = 1699$ cuts. No arrangement can reduce this total because any attempt to reduce the number of side-increasing cuts would fail to reach 20 sides per polygon, and any attempt to reduce the number of cuts to create pieces would fail to reach 100 polygons.

Therefore, the minimum number of cuts required to obtain exactly 100 twenty-sided polygons is

$$\boxed{1699}.$$

## Verification of Key Steps

For Lemma 2, consider a square cut along a line intersecting two edges. The resulting polygons gain one extra side along the new segment, confirming the one-side-per-cut rule. If a cut intersects more than two edges, one polygon may gain more than one side, but the other polygon will have fewer sides, so the side-increasing count per polygon does not decrease.

For Lemma 4, suppose one attempts to share a cut to increase sides of multiple pieces simultaneously. If a cut intersects two polygons, it produces a new piece in each, but side growth is split, and the total number of sides added across the intended 20-gons cannot decrease, preserving the total count of 16 side-increasing cuts per polygon.

For the final total, we verify small cases. Two squares require $2 \cdot 16 = 32$ side-increasing cuts plus one cut to create the second square, totaling 33 cuts, matching the formula $16n + (n-1)$. Applying this for $n=100$ gives $1600 + 99 = 1699$.

## Alternative Approaches

One could attempt a recursive approach by always cutting the largest polygon available to produce a 20-gon while minimizing cuts. This reduces the problem to a combinatorial optimization, but the counting argument above is more direct and transparent. Another approach might involve creating intermediate polygons with more than 4 sides to reduce the number of side-increasing cuts, but any such approach fails to reduce the total number of required cuts because side growth is strictly bounded by Lemma 2. The primary approach is preferable for its simplicity, exact accounting, and clarity in proving minimality.
