## Problem

![](//www.kvant.digital/media/common/1970-06-028-01.png?_v=2)

$n$ identical coins lie on a table, forming a closed chain (see figure). How many revolutions will a coin $M$ of the same size make while rolling once around the outside of the entire chain, as shown in the figure (coin $M = 2~\text{коп.}$)? How will the answer change if coin $M$ has a radius differing by a factor of $k$ from the radius of each of the coins in the chain?

## Exploration

Let the coins in the chain all have radius $r$. First examine small values of $n$.

If $n=1$, a coin of the same radius rolling once around a fixed coin makes $2$ revolutions. This classical result already shows that the number of revolutions is not obtained merely by dividing the traveled distance by the circumference of the moving coin.

If $n=2$, the centers of the two fixed coins are separated by $2r$. The locus of the center of the moving coin consists of two straight segments of length $2r$ and two semicircles of radius $2r$. The total length is

$$L=4r+4\pi r.$$

Dividing by the circumference $2\pi r$ gives

$$\frac{L}{2\pi r}=\frac2\pi+2,$$

which is not an integer, so this cannot be the answer. An additional contribution must come from the turning of the direction of motion.

For a curve along which a disk rolls without slipping, the angle through which it rotates equals the traveled arc length divided by its radius, plus the total turning angle of the tangent. For a closed convex curve traversed once, the tangent turns through $2\pi$.

The chain of $n$ equal touching coins has centers at the vertices of a regular $n$-gon of side $2r$. The locus of the center of a rolling coin of the same radius is the outer parallel curve at distance $2r$ from this polygon. Its perimeter equals the perimeter of the polygon plus $2\pi(2r)$.

The polygon perimeter is

$$P=2nr.$$

Hence

$$L=P+4\pi r=2nr+4\pi r.$$

For equal radii, the number of revolutions should be

$$\frac{L}{2\pi r}+1 = \frac{2nr+4\pi r}{2\pi r}+1 = \frac n\pi+3.$$

Checking $n=1$ gives $1/\pi+3$, which is wrong because the derivation used a polygonal chain and does not apply to the degenerate case $n=1$. For $n\ge2$ it is plausible.

Now suppose the moving coin has radius $R=kr$. Then the center follows the outer parallel curve at distance $r+R=(1+k)r$ from the polygon. Its perimeter is

$$L=P+2\pi(r+R) = 2nr+2\pi(1+k)r.$$

The total tangent turning remains $2\pi$. Hence the number of revolutions is

$$N = \frac{L}{2\pi R}+1 = \frac{2nr+2\pi(1+k)r}{2\pi kr}+1 = \frac{n}{\pi k}+\frac{1+k}{k}+1 = \frac{n}{\pi k}+2+\frac1k.$$

For $k=1$ this becomes $\frac n\pi+3$.

The step most likely to conceal an error is the formula relating the rotation of a rolling disk to both the traveled length and the total turning of the path.

## Problem Understanding

The fixed coins, each of radius $r$, form a closed chain. Their centers are the vertices of a regular $n$-gon with side length $2r$. A coin $M$ rolls without slipping around the outside of the whole chain. We must determine how many complete revolutions it makes.

This is a Type C problem, because a numerical quantity is to be determined.

The core difficulty is that the rotation of a rolling coin is affected not only by the distance traveled by its center but also by the continual change in the direction of motion. The familiar fact that a coin rolling around an equal coin makes two revolutions instead of one comes from precisely this additional effect.

The answer for a moving coin of radius $R=kr$ will be

$$N=\frac{n}{\pi k}+2+\frac1k.$$

For equal radii, $k=1$, this becomes

$$N=\frac n\pi+3.$$

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma states that if a disk of radius $R$ rolls without slipping along a smooth curve, then its total angle of rotation equals the traveled arc length divided by $R$ plus the total turning angle of the tangent to the curve; this follows from comparing infinitesimal changes.

The second lemma states that the locus of the center of the rolling coin is the outer parallel curve at distance $r+R$ from the regular $n$-gon formed by the centers of the fixed coins; this is a direct geometric description of tangency.

The third lemma states that the perimeter of the outer parallel curve of a convex polygon at distance $d$ equals the polygon perimeter plus $2\pi d$; the added pieces are circular arcs whose total angle is $2\pi$.

The fourth lemma states that the tangent to a convex closed curve turns through $2\pi$ during one circuit; this is the total curvature theorem for a simple convex closed curve.

The hardest point is the first lemma, because it is where the extra rotation responsible for the classical coin paradox enters.
