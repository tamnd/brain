## Problem

In the country of Anchuria, ruled by President Miraflores, the time for new presidential elections has approached. The country has exactly 20 million voters, of whom only one percent (the regular army of Anchuria) support Miraflores. Naturally, Miraflores wants to be elected, but at the same time he wants the elections to appear democratic. By a “democratic vote,” Miraflores means the following: all voters are divided into several equal groups; then each group is again divided into some number of equal groups; then these latter groups are again divided into equal groups, and so on. In the smallest groups, a representative of the group, an elector, is chosen; then the electors choose representatives for voting in the next larger group, and so on. Finally, the representatives of the largest groups elect the president. Miraflores may divide the voters into groups however he wishes and instruct his supporters how to vote. Can he organize such “democratic elections” so that he is elected president? (In the event of a tie, the opposition wins.)

## Problem Understanding

We have $20,000,000$ voters, exactly $200,000$ of whom support Miraflores. Elections are organized through several hierarchical levels. At each level every group is split into equal subgroups. Representatives are elected upward level by level, and the final representatives elect the president. Miraflores may choose the entire subdivision scheme and may distribute his supporters arbitrarily.

The problem asks whether he can guarantee his own election despite having only $1%$ support.

This is a Type B problem. We must prove or disprove the possibility.

The core difficulty is determining the smallest proportion of supporters that can force victory in such a hierarchical majority system. One must find a universal lower bound on the number of supporters needed to win a group and compare it with $1%$.

## Proof Architecture

Let $f(G)$ denote the minimum fraction of voters in a group $G$ that must support Miraflores in order for that group ultimately to elect a Miraflores representative.

For a smallest group, prove that $f(G)>\frac12$, because a strict majority is required.

For a group split into $m$ equal subgroups, prove that

$$f(G)\ge \frac{m+1}{2m}\min_i f(G_i),$$

because more than half of the subgroup representatives must support Miraflores.

Show that every nontrivial odd branching number is a power of $5$, hence at least $5$.

Deduce that every time a level is traversed upward,

$$f(G)\ge \frac35\,\min_i f(G_i).$$

Show by induction through the hierarchy that the fraction needed at the top is at least

$$\frac12\left(\frac35\right)^r,$$

where $r$ is the number of nontrivial levels.

Prove that $r\le7$, because $20,000,000=2^8\cdot5^7$ and each nontrivial odd branching level consumes at least one factor $5$.

Compute the resulting lower bound

$$\frac12\left(\frac35\right)^7=\frac{2187}{156250}>0.01.$$

Hence $1%$ support is insufficient.

The most delicate lemma is the recursive lower bound for $f(G)$, because it must account simultaneously for majorities at different levels.

## Solution

Let $f(G)$ be the minimum fraction of voters in a group $G$ that must support Miraflores in order that the representative ultimately chosen for $G$ support Miraflores.

Consider first a smallest group, one that directly elects an elector. Since ties are won by the opposition, a Miraflores elector can be chosen only if strictly more than half of the voters in that group support him. Therefore

$$f(G)>\frac12$$

for every smallest group.

Now consider a group $G$ that is divided into $m$ equal subgroups

$$G_1,\dots,G_m.$$

Let

$$\alpha=\min_i f(G_i).$$

To make the representative of $G$ support Miraflores, more than half of the subgroup representatives must support him. Since ties lose, at least

$$\frac{m+1}{2}$$

subgroups must themselves elect Miraflores representatives.

Each such winning subgroup contains at least an $\alpha$ fraction of Miraflores supporters. Since all subgroups have equal size, the whole group $G$ must contain at least

$$\frac{m+1}{2}\cdot\frac1m\cdot\alpha = \frac{m+1}{2m}\alpha$$

of its voters supporting Miraflores. Hence

$$f(G)\ge \frac{m+1}{2m}\alpha.$$

This establishes the recursive inequality

$$f(G)\ge \frac{m+1}{2m}\min_i f(G_i).$$

Next we examine the possible values of $m$.

Since

$$20\,000\,000=2^8\cdot5^7,$$

every group size occurring in the process is of the form $2^a5^b$. Any branching number $m$ must divide the size of the group being subdivided; therefore every branching number is also of the form $2^u5^v$.

If $m$ is even, then exactly half of the subgroup representatives could support Miraflores, producing a tie that the opposition wins. Such a level cannot improve Miraflores's position. The only branching numbers relevant to a winning strategy are odd. Every odd divisor of $20,000,000$ is a power of $5$, hence every nontrivial odd branching number satisfies

$$m\ge5.$$

For odd $m$,

$$\frac{m+1}{2m} = \frac12+\frac1{2m}.$$

Since $m\ge5$,

$$\frac{m+1}{2m}\ge\frac35.$$

Consequently every nontrivial level satisfies

$$f(G)\ge\frac35\,\min_i f(G_i).$$

Let $r$ be the number of nontrivial levels in the hierarchy. Starting from the smallest groups, where $f>\frac12$, and applying the previous inequality successively through the $r$ levels, we obtain

$$f(\text{top}) > \frac12\left(\frac35\right)^r.$$

Each nontrivial level uses an odd branching factor at least $5$. Therefore the product of the branching factors over all nontrivial levels is at least $5^r$. This product must divide $20,000,000=2^8\cdot5^7$, so

$$5^r\mid 5^7,$$

which implies

$$r\le7.$$

Hence

$$f(\text{top}) > \frac12\left(\frac35\right)^7 = \frac{2187}{156250}.$$

A direct computation gives

$$\frac{2187}{156250}=0.0139968\ldots>0.01.$$

Thus any arrangement of the election requires more than $1%$ support in order to guarantee the election of a Miraflores representative at the top level.

Miraflores possesses exactly $1%$ of the voters. This is less than the minimum fraction required. Therefore no choice of subdivisions and no voting instructions can secure his election.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The recursive inequality deserves an independent check. Suppose a group is divided into $m$ equal subgroups. A Miraflores representative for the whole group requires at least $(m+1)/2$ winning subgroups. If each winning subgroup needs at least an $\alpha$ fraction of supporters, then the total support inside the whole group is at least

$$\frac{m+1}{2}\cdot\alpha\cdot\frac1m.$$

No cancellation or overlap is possible because the subgroups are disjoint and equal in size. This reproduces

$$f(G)\ge\frac{m+1}{2m}\alpha.$$

The claim that $m=5$ is the most favorable branching factor must also be checked numerically. For admissible odd branching numbers,

$$m=5,\quad \frac{m+1}{2m}=\frac35=0.6,$$

while

$$m=25,\quad \frac{m+1}{2m}=\frac{13}{25}=0.52,$$

and

$$m=125,\quad \frac{m+1}{2m}=\frac{63}{125}=0.504.$$

The quantity multiplying the previous level is smallest for larger $m$, but using $m=25$ consumes two factors of $5$ at once. Comparing equal expenditures of factors of $5$,

$$\left(\frac35\right)^2=0.36 \quad\text{and}\quad \frac{13}{25}=0.52.$$

Thus two successive $5$-splits require a smaller support fraction than one $25$-split. Repeating this comparison shows that using only branching factor $5$ minimizes the necessary support.

Finally, the arithmetic bound must be checked:

$$\frac12\left(\frac35\right)^7 = \frac{2187}{156250} = 0.0139968\ldots$$

which exceeds

$$0.01.$$

The gap is substantial, so no rounding issue can affect the conclusion.

## Alternative Approaches

One may assign to every group a weight equal to the minimum fraction of supporters needed to control that group. The recursive rule then becomes multiplication by $(m+1)/(2m)$ at each level. The problem reduces to minimizing the product of these factors subject to the constraint that the product of all branching numbers divides $5^7$. Comparing

$$\frac{5^t+1}{2\cdot5^t} \quad\text{with}\quad \left(\frac35\right)^t$$

shows that splitting a factor $5^t$ into $t$ successive factors $5$ always decreases the required support. Hence the optimum hierarchy uses seven levels of branching factor $5$, yielding the same lower bound as in the main solution.

Another approach is to work with absolute numbers instead of fractions. Starting from the top, every winning group must contain at least three winning subgroups out of five. Iterating seven times shows that at least $3^7$ smallest groups must be won. Each smallest group requires a strict majority. Counting voters in these groups again leads to the threshold

$$\frac12\left(\frac35\right)^7,$$

and hence to the impossibility of victory with only $1%$ support. The fractional formulation is preferable because the recursive structure becomes transparent and avoids tracking large integers.
