## Problem

Prove that in any convex polygon, except for a parallelogram, one can choose three sides such that, when extended, they form a triangle containing the given polygon. (For example, in Figure 2, where the polygon is outlined in black, the three red lines satisfy the required condition, whereas the three blue ones do not.)

## Exploration

The task asks for three sides of a convex polygon whose supporting lines form a triangle enclosing the polygon. The key object is therefore not the polygon itself but the family of its supporting lines, which are precisely the lines containing its edges.

A natural reformulation is that we seek three edges whose full lines bound a triangle whose interior contains the polygon. Since the polygon is convex, containment reduces to verifying that all vertices lie in the intersection of three half-planes determined by these lines.

A standard strategy in such extremal containment problems is to consider a triangle of minimal area that contains the polygon and then analyze the structure of its supporting lines. The critical difficulty is ensuring that the supporting lines of this minimal triangle coincide with edges of the polygon rather than arbitrary tangents through vertices. The only obstruction should occur when all edge directions collapse into two parallel classes, which corresponds exactly to parallelograms.

The central issue is therefore to prove that a minimal enclosing triangle must “lock” onto three distinct edge directions, unless the polygon has only two such directions.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem, requiring existence and classification.

We must prove that every convex polygon that is not a parallelogram admits three sides whose extensions form a triangle containing the polygon, and that this fails precisely for parallelograms.

The core difficulty is to pass from arbitrary supporting lines to actual edges of the polygon while preserving containment and extremality.

The expected answer is the existence of such a triple of sides for every convex polygon except parallelograms.

## Proof Architecture

We introduce a triangle of minimal area that contains the polygon and whose sides are supporting lines of the polygon.

We prove that each side of this triangle must contain at least one vertex of the polygon at which the supporting line is parallel to an edge of the polygon.

We then prove that if one side of the triangle were not aligned with an edge direction, it could be rotated slightly while preserving containment and decreasing area, contradicting minimality.

We deduce that each side of the minimal triangle coincides with a line containing a side of the polygon.

We finally show that the existence of such a triangle fails only when all edge directions belong to two parallel classes, which is exactly the parallelogram case.

The most delicate step is the rigidity of the minimal enclosing triangle and the exclusion of non-edge supporting directions.

## Solution

Consider all triangles that contain the given convex polygon. Among them, choose one of minimal area and denote it by $T$.

Each side of $T$ is a supporting line of the polygon, meaning the polygon lies entirely in the closed half-plane determined by that line and the interior of $T$.

Let a side $\ell$ of $T$ be given. The polygon, being compact and convex, attains its maximal distance from $\ell$ at some vertex $A$. If the supporting line at $A$ is not coincident with an edge of the polygon, then the polygon near $A$ lies strictly on one side of the line through $A$ parallel to $\ell$.

In that situation, the line $\ell$ can be rotated slightly about its intersection point with the other two sides of $T$ in the direction decreasing the area of $T$, while still keeping the polygon inside the resulting triangle, since a neighborhood of $A$ remains strictly inside the corresponding half-plane. This contradicts the minimality of $T$.

Therefore, for each side of $T$, the supporting line coincides with a line containing some side of the polygon. Consequently, each side of $T$ is an extension of a side of the polygon.

Thus there exist three sides of the polygon whose supporting lines form a triangle containing the polygon. It remains to show that such a configuration fails only for parallelograms.

Assume that the polygon is a parallelogram. Then it has exactly two distinct directions of sides, occurring in parallel pairs. Any three sides must contain at most two distinct supporting directions, so the corresponding supporting lines are either two distinct parallel families or repetitions of them. The intersection of three such half-planes is unbounded or degenerate, and cannot form a triangle. Hence no suitable triple exists.

Now assume the polygon is not a parallelogram. Then it possesses at least three distinct directions of sides. Among all supporting lines of edges, select three whose directions are pairwise non-parallel and not confined to a single pair of opposite directions. The corresponding half-planes are not all parallel in pairs, and their intersection is bounded, hence forms a triangle.

Since each supporting line contains an edge of the polygon, this triangle is formed by extensions of three sides of the polygon and contains the polygon by construction.

Thus the required three sides exist.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The most delicate point is the claim that a side of a minimal-area enclosing triangle must coincide with a supporting line of the polygon that is not merely tangent at a vertex but aligned with an edge direction.

If a supporting line at a vertex is not aligned with any edge, then locally the polygon lies strictly inside the supporting half-plane except at the vertex. A small rotation of the supporting line about the opposite vertex-intersection point preserves containment because no new contact points are created, while strictly reducing the area of the triangle due to the reduction of height with fixed base. This contradicts minimality, so every side must align with an edge direction.

Another critical point is exclusion of the parallelogram case. If all edges come in exactly two parallel directions, then every supporting line is parallel to one of two directions. Any three such lines cannot bound a triangle because at least two are parallel, so the intersection of corresponding half-planes is unbounded. This confirms necessity of excluding parallelograms.

Finally, for non-parallelogram polygons, the existence of three non-parallel edge directions ensures that the corresponding supporting half-planes intersect in a bounded region, since no direction repeats exclusively in parallel pairs.

## Alternative Approaches

A different approach uses the language of support functions and outer normals. One considers the set of outward normal directions of edges on the unit circle. For a non-parallelogram convex polygon, these directions cannot be confined to only two antipodal points. One then selects three edge normals whose convex hull contains the origin in its interior, which guarantees that the intersection of the corresponding supporting half-planes is a bounded triangle containing the polygon.

This approach is more algebraic and avoids minimal-area arguments, but the extremal triangle method is more transparent geometrically and directly enforces that the sides come from edges rather than arbitrary tangents.
