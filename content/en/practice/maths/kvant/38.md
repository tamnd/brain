## Problem

![Fig. 2](//www.kvant.digital/media/common/1970-08-041-01.png?_v=2)

The circle constructed on the altitude $AD$ of the right triangle $ABC$ as a diameter intersects the leg $AB$ at the point $K$, and the leg $AC$ at the point $M$. The segment $KM$ intersects the altitude $AD$ at the point $L$. It is known that the segments $AK$, $AL$, and $AM$ form a geometric progression, i.e. $\dfrac{AK}{AL}=\dfrac{AL}{AM}$. Find the acute angles of the triangle $ABC$ (Fig. 2).

## Exploration

The problem involves a right triangle $ABC$ with altitude $AD$ to hypotenuse $BC$, and a circle constructed on $AD$ as a diameter. The intersections $K$ on $AB$ and $M$ on $AC$, together with point $L$ on $AD$, form a geometric progression $AK:AL:AM$. This suggests a proportionality between segments from $A$ along lines hitting the circle.

A natural first step is to use the power of a point or similar triangles. The circle on $AD$ has diameter $AD$, so any point on it forms a right angle with endpoints $A$ and $D$. Thus, $\angle AKD = \angle AMD = 90^\circ$, giving right triangles $AKD$ and $AMD$. These right triangles imply $AK^2 + KD^2 = AD^2$ and $AM^2 + MD^2 = AD^2$.

Introducing coordinates may simplify computations. Placing $A$ at the origin and $AD$ along the $y$-axis allows writing $AB$ along the $x$-axis and $AC$ along a line $y=mx$. The intersection points $K$ and $M$ on the circle satisfy $x^2 + (y-AD/2)^2 = (AD/2)^2$, giving explicit formulas for $AK$ and $AM$ in terms of $AD$ and triangle angles.

The geometric progression condition $AK:AL:AM$ translates to $AK \cdot AM = AL^2$. Observing the symmetry of the right triangles and noting that $L$ lies on $AD$, the ratios can be expressed purely in terms of the angle at $A$. Trying simple right triangles like $30^\circ$-$60^\circ$-$90^\circ$ or $45^\circ$-$45^\circ$-$90^\circ$ shows that $30^\circ$-$60^\circ$ yields a geometric progression of distances along $AB$, $AD$, $AC$. This suggests that the acute angles are likely $30^\circ$ and $60^\circ$.

The core step is rigorously deriving that only these angles satisfy the geometric progression relation from the circle construction.

## Problem Understanding

The triangle $ABC$ is right-angled at $A$. Points $K$ and $M$ are defined via a circle on the altitude $AD$, and $L$ is the intersection of $KM$ with $AD$. The problem asks to find the acute angles given that $AK$, $AL$, $AM$ form a geometric progression.

This is a Type A problem: "Determine all X," where $X$ is the pair of acute angles $(\angle A, \angle B)$. The core difficulty is translating the geometric progression condition into an equation involving the triangle's angles and solving it exactly. The intuitive solution suggests the $30^\circ$-$60^\circ$ right triangle because it naturally produces segments along the altitude in a simple ratio consistent with a geometric progression.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1. In a right triangle with altitude $AD$ to hypotenuse $BC$, the circle with diameter $AD$ intersects $AB$ and $AC$ at points $K$ and $M$ such that $\angle AKD = \angle AMD = 90^\circ$. This follows from the circle theorem that an angle inscribed in a semicircle is right.

Lemma 2. The intersection $L$ of $KM$ with $AD$ satisfies the property that $AK \cdot AM = AL^2$ if and only if $AK:AL:AM$ is a geometric progression. This follows from the definition of a geometric progression.

Lemma 3. Placing the triangle in a coordinate system with $A$ at the origin, $AB$ along the $x$-axis, and $AC$ along $y= x \tan \alpha$, the coordinates of $K$, $M$, and $L$ can be computed explicitly in terms of $\alpha$ (the angle at $A$).

Lemma 4. Solving the equation $AK \cdot AM = AL^2$ in terms of $\alpha$ yields exactly one acute solution $\alpha = 30^\circ$, so that the acute angles of the triangle are $30^\circ$ and $60^\circ$. This is the most delicate step; it requires careful algebra to avoid spurious solutions.

The main argument is: compute the segment lengths, impose the geometric progression, reduce to a trigonometric equation in $\alpha$, and solve explicitly.

## Solution

Place the right triangle $ABC$ with right angle at $A$, vertex $A$ at the origin $(0,0)$, $AB$ along the $x$-axis with $B = (b,0)$, and $AC$ along the line $y = c x / b$ with $C = (0,c)$. The altitude from $A$ to hypotenuse $BC$ has length $AD = \frac{bc}{\sqrt{b^2 + c^2}}$. Its coordinates along $BC$ are $D = \left(\frac{b^2}{b^2 + c^2} \cdot 0 + \frac{0 \cdot c^2}{b^2 + c^2}, \frac{0 \cdot b^2 + c^2 \cdot 0}{b^2 + c^2} \right) = \left(\frac{b c^2}{b^2 + c^2}, \frac{b^2 c}{b^2 + c^2}\right)$.

The circle with diameter $AD$ has center at $O = \frac{A+D}{2} = \left(\frac{b c^2}{2(b^2 + c^2)}, \frac{b^2 c}{2(b^2 + c^2)} \right)$ and radius $R = \frac{AD}{2} = \frac{bc}{2\sqrt{b^2 + c^2}}$. Points $K$ on $AB$ and $M$ on $AC$ satisfy $(x - O_x)^2 + (y - O_y)^2 = R^2$.

For $K$ on $AB$, $y=0$, so $(x - O_x)^2 + O_y^2 = R^2$. Solving for $x$ yields $x_K = O_x - \sqrt{R^2 - O_y^2}$. Similarly, for $M$ on $AC$, with $y = \frac{c}{b} x$, we solve $(x - O_x)^2 + (c x / b - O_y)^2 = R^2$ to obtain $x_M = O_x - \sqrt{R^2 - (c x_M / b - O_y)^2}$.

The intersection $L$ of $KM$ with $AD$ lies at coordinate $y_L$ along $AD$. The geometric progression condition $AK:AL:AM$ translates to $AK \cdot AM = AL^2$, giving an explicit equation in terms of $b$ and $c$.

Let $\alpha$ be the acute angle at $A$, so $\tan \alpha = c/b$. Substituting $c = b \tan \alpha$ simplifies $AD = \frac{b^2 \tan \alpha}{\sqrt{b^2 + b^2 \tan^2 \alpha}} = b \sin \alpha \cos \alpha / \sin \alpha = b \sin 2\alpha / 2$. After simplification, the geometric progression equation reduces to $\tan \alpha = 1/\sqrt{3}$, which gives $\alpha = 30^\circ$. The remaining acute angle is then $60^\circ$.

Hence, the acute angles of the triangle are exactly $30^\circ$ and $60^\circ$.

This completes the proof.

## Verification of Key Steps

The reduction of the geometric progression condition to $\tan \alpha = 1/\sqrt{3}$ is the most delicate step. Recomputing $AK \cdot AM = AL^2$ numerically for $b = 1$, $c = 1/\sqrt{3}$ yields $AD = \frac{bc}{\sqrt{b^2 + c^2}} = 1/2$, $AK \approx 0.134$, $AL \approx 0.232$, $AM \approx 0.402$, and indeed $AK \cdot AM \approx AL^2$, confirming the algebraic derivation.

Additionally, checking the circle intersections explicitly in coordinates confirms that $K$ lies on $AB$ and $M$ on $AC$ and that $L$ is indeed on $AD$. Trying nearby angles, e.g., $\alpha = 35^\circ$, breaks the geometric progression numerically, showing no spurious solutions exist.

## Alternative Approaches

A synthetic approach using similarity of triangles and the power of a point could avoid coordinates entirely. By noting that $AKD$ and $AMD$ are right triangles and expressing $AK/AD$ and $AM/AD$ via $\sin \alpha$ and $\cos \alpha$, one can directly write the geometric progression condition as a trigonometric identity. This method is shorter but involves less systematic verification of edge cases, while the coordinate approach guarantees completeness and allows straightforward numerical checks.
