## Problem

![Fig. 2](//www.kvant.digital/media/common/1970-08-041-01.png?_v=2)

Fig. 2

A circle constructed on the altitude $AD$ of a right triangle $ABC$ as its diameter intersects the leg $AB$ at point $K$ and the leg $AC$ at point $M$. The segment $KM$ intersects the altitude $AD$ at point $L$. It is known that the segments $AK$, $AL$, and $AM$ form a geometric progression, i.e., $\dfrac{AK}{AL}=\dfrac{AL}{AM}$. Find the acute angles of triangle $ABC$ (Fig. 2).

L. M. Lopovok

## Exploration

Let $ABC$ be a right triangle with $\angle A = 90^\circ$, $AB = b$, $AC = c$. Place coordinates $A = (0,0)$, $B = (b,0)$, $C = (0,c)$. The altitude from $A$ to $BC$ is the perpendicular from $A$ to the line $BC$, and its foot is $D$.

A standard computation gives
$$
D=\left(\frac{bc^2}{b^2+c^2},\frac{b^2c}{b^2+c^2}\right).
$$
The segment $AD$ is the diameter of a circle, so its midpoint is the center, and points $K$ and $M$ are the second intersections of this circle with $AB$ and $AC$ respectively. This forces $K$ and $M$ to be symmetric in a precise metric sense relative to $AD$.

A key structural observation is that $K$ and $M$ are determined only by the circle with diameter $AD$, so their coordinates are rational expressions in $b$ and $c$. The condition $AK, AL, AM$ forming a geometric progression is equivalent to
$$
AL^2 = AK \cdot AM,
$$
which couples a length on $AD$ with two intercept lengths on the coordinate axes.

Direct computation in full generality is possible but algebraically heavy. A more effective strategy is to test whether a known special right triangle, such as $45^\circ\!-\!45^\circ\!-\!90^\circ$ or $30^\circ\!-\!60^\circ\!-\!90^\circ$, can satisfy the condition, then use the resulting structure to deduce uniqueness.

Testing $b=c$ gives $K$ and $M$ symmetric, but the midpoint $L$ of $AD$ is forced by symmetry, and the geometric progression condition fails numerically. Testing $b:c=\sqrt{3}:1$ or $1:\sqrt{3}$ also produces a consistent configuration where the intersection points become simple rational ratios along the sides, suggesting a trigonometric angle of $15^\circ$ may appear, since $15^\circ$ is the only acute angle producing both $\sqrt{3}$ and $1$ interactions through half-angle structure.

The decisive step is therefore to determine whether the condition enforces a tangent ratio leading to $\tan \angle B = 2 - \sqrt{3}$ or its reciprocal, which corresponds to $15^\circ$ or $75^\circ$.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem. One must determine all possible acute angles of a right triangle $ABC$ such that a circle with diameter equal to the altitude from $A$ intersects the legs in a configuration where the three segments $AK$, $AL$, $AM$ form a geometric progression.

The core difficulty is the interaction between a power-of-a-point construction (circle with diameter $AD$) and a metric condition along the altitude $AD$. The expected outcome is that the triangle is uniquely determined up to similarity, hence its acute angles are fixed.

The final result is expected to be a specific pair of acute angles, and symmetry considerations suggest they are complementary, so one expects $\{\angle B,\angle C\} = \{15^\circ,75^\circ\}$.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma establishes explicit coordinates of $D$ in terms of $b$ and $c$ using orthogonality conditions in a right triangle.

The second lemma determines the coordinates of $K$ and $M$ as intersections of the circle with diameter $AD$ with the axes $AB$ and $AC$.

The third lemma expresses $AK$ and $AM$ in closed form and shows they depend only on $b$ and $c$ via symmetric rational expressions.

The fourth lemma shows that $L$, as the intersection of $KM$ with $AD$, divides $AD$ in a ratio depending rationally on $b$ and $c$.

The final step transforms the condition $AL^2 = AK \cdot AM$ into an equation in the ratio $b/c$, whose solution yields a unique value corresponding to $\tan 15^\circ$.

The most delicate step is the computation of the intersection point $L$ and ensuring that no extraneous algebraic simplifications discard valid configurations.

## Solution

Place the right triangle so that $\angle A = 90^\circ$, $A = (0,0)$, $B = (b,0)$, and $C = (0,c)$ with $b,c>0$.

The line $BC$ has equation
$$
\frac{x}{b} + \frac{y}{c} = 1.
$$
The altitude from $A$ to $BC$ is perpendicular to $BC$, hence has slope $b/c$. Its intersection with $BC$ is the foot $D$. Solving the perpendicular intersection gives
$$
D=\left(\frac{bc^2}{b^2+c^2}, \frac{b^2c}{b^2+c^2}\right).
$$

Let $AD$ be a diameter of a circle. Its center is
$$
O=\left(\frac{bc^2}{2(b^2+c^2)}, \frac{b^2c}{2(b^2+c^2)}\right).
$$
The circle has radius $AD/2$, so its equation is
$$
\left(x-O_x\right)^2+\left(y-O_y\right)^2=\frac{AD^2}{4}.
$$

The intersection with $AB$ occurs at $y=0$. Substituting $y=0$ into the circle equation yields a quadratic in $x$ whose roots are $0$ and a second point $K$ on segment $AB$. Hence $K=(AK,0)$ where
$$
AK=\frac{bc^2}{b^2+c^2}.
$$

Similarly, intersecting with $AC$ gives $M=(0,AM)$ where
$$
AM=\frac{b^2c}{b^2+c^2}.
$$

Thus
$$
AK=\frac{bc^2}{b^2+c^2}, \qquad AM=\frac{b^2c}{b^2+c^2}.
$$

The line $KM$ has intercept form
$$
\frac{x}{AK}+\frac{y}{AM}=1.
$$
The altitude $AD$ is parametrized by $tD$. Substituting $(tx_D,ty_D)$ into the line equation gives the intersection parameter $t$:
$$
\frac{tx_D}{AK}+\frac{ty_D}{AM}=1,
$$
hence
$$
t=\frac{1}{\frac{x_D}{AK}+\frac{y_D}{AM}}.
$$
Therefore
$$
AL = t \cdot AD.
$$

Substituting the explicit expressions for $x_D,y_D,AK,AM$ yields
$$
\frac{x_D}{AK}=\frac{bc^2/(b^2+c^2)}{bc^2/(b^2+c^2)}=1,
\qquad
\frac{y_D}{AM}=\frac{b^2c/(b^2+c^2)}{b^2c/(b^2+c^2)}=1.
$$
Hence
$$
t=\frac{1}{2}, \qquad AL=\frac{AD}{2}.
$$

The geometric progression condition becomes
$$
\left(\frac{AD}{2}\right)^2 = AK \cdot AM.
$$
Substituting $AK$ and $AM$ gives
$$
\frac{AD^2}{4}=\frac{b^3c^3}{(b^2+c^2)^2}.
$$
Since in a right triangle
$$
AD=\frac{bc}{\sqrt{b^2+c^2}},
$$
we obtain
$$
\frac{b^2c^2}{4(b^2+c^2)}=\frac{b^3c^3}{(b^2+c^2)^2}.
$$
Dividing by $b^2c^2$ yields
$$
\frac{1}{4(b^2+c^2)}=\frac{bc}{(b^2+c^2)^2}.
$$
Multiplying gives
$$
b^2+c^2=4bc.
$$
Let $t=b/c$. Then
$$
t^2+1=4t,
$$
so
$$
t^2-4t+1=0,
\qquad
t=2\pm\sqrt{3}.
$$

Thus
$$
\frac{b}{c}=2+\sqrt{3} \quad \text{or} \quad 2-\sqrt{3}.
$$

Since $2+\sqrt{3}=\tan 75^\circ$ and $2-\sqrt{3}=\tan 15^\circ$, the acute angles of the triangle are $15^\circ$ and $75^\circ$.

$$
\boxed{\{\angle B,\angle C\}=\{15^\circ,75^\circ\}}
$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The most sensitive step is the computation of $t$ for the intersection point $L$. The cancellation in $\frac{x_D}{AK}$ and $\frac{y_D}{AM}$ depends on correctly matching corresponding expressions from the circle construction. A direct recomputation confirms that both ratios reduce to $1$, forcing $t=\frac12$, so $L$ is indeed the midpoint of $AD$ independently of $b$ and $c$.

The second delicate step is transforming the geometric progression condition into an algebraic equation in $b$ and $c$. Substituting $AD=\frac{bc}{\sqrt{b^2+c^2}}$ verifies that no sign or scaling inconsistencies occur, and the resulting quadratic in $b/c$ is consistent and symmetric under swapping $b$ and $c$.

Finally, identifying $2\pm\sqrt{3}$ with $\tan 75^\circ$ and $\tan 15^\circ$ follows from standard tangent addition formulas applied to $45^\circ\pm30^\circ$.

## Alternative Approaches

A synthetic approach avoids coordinates by using homothety centered at $A$ mapping the circle with diameter $AD$ to a unit configuration, then interpreting $K$ and $M$ via right-angle constraints in semicircles. In that framework, the condition $AK,AL,AM$ in geometric progression translates into a harmonic division property on $AD$ and leads directly to a trigonometric identity involving $\tan \angle B$.

The coordinate method is preferable because it makes the proportional structure of $AK$, $AM$, and $AD$ explicit and reduces the geometric condition to a single algebraic equation in $b/c$ without requiring auxiliary projective arguments.
