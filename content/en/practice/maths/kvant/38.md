## Problem

![Fig. 2](//www.kvant.digital/media/common/1970-08-041-01.png?_v=2)

Fig. 2

A circle constructed on the altitude $AD$ of the right triangle $ABC$ as a diameter intersects leg $AB$ at point $K$ and leg $AC$ at point $M$. The segment $KM$ intersects the altitude $AD$ at point $L$. It is known that the segments $AK$, $AL$, and $AM$ form a geometric progression, i.e., $\dfrac{AK}{AL}=\dfrac{AL}{AM}$. Find the acute angles of triangle $ABC$ (Fig. 2).

L. M. Lopovok

## Exploration

Let $\angle B=\beta$ and $\angle C=\gamma$, with $\beta+\gamma=90^\circ$.

Place the right triangle in coordinates:

$$A=(0,0),\qquad B=(b,0),\qquad C=(0,c).$$

Then the altitude from $A$ to the hypotenuse is the line through the origin perpendicular to $BC$, hence

$$AD:\ y=\frac bc\,x.$$

The circle with diameter $AD$ passes through $A$. Let $D=(x_D,y_D)$.

Since $D$ lies on $BC$ and on $y=\frac bc x$, solving gives

$$x_D=\frac{bc^2}{b^2+c^2},\qquad y_D=\frac{b^2c}{b^2+c^2}.$$

The circle with diameter $AD$ has equation

$$x^2+y^2-x_Dx-y_Dy=0.$$

Intersecting with $AB$ ($y=0$) gives

$$x(x-x_D)=0,$$

so besides $A$,

$$K=(x_D,0),$$

hence

$$AK=x_D=\frac{bc^2}{b^2+c^2}.$$

Similarly, intersecting with $AC$ ($x=0$) gives

$$M=(0,y_D),$$

hence

$$AM=y_D=\frac{b^2c}{b^2+c^2}.$$

The line through $K$ and $M$ has intercept form

$$\frac{x}{x_D}+\frac{y}{y_D}=1.$$

Intersecting with $AD$, namely $y=\frac bc x$, yields

$$x\!\left(\frac1{x_D}+\frac{b/c}{y_D}\right)=1.$$

Using the formulas for $x_D,y_D$,

$$\frac1{x_D}=\frac{b^2+c^2}{bc^2}, \qquad \frac{b/c}{y_D} =\frac{b^2+c^2}{bc^2},$$

so

$$x_L=\frac{x_D}{2}, \qquad y_L=\frac{y_D}{2}.$$

Thus $L$ is the midpoint of $AD$, and

$$AL=\frac12\,AD.$$

Now

$$AD=\frac{bc}{\sqrt{b^2+c^2}},$$

hence

$$AL=\frac{bc}{2\sqrt{b^2+c^2}}.$$

The condition

$$\frac{AK}{AL}=\frac{AL}{AM}$$

is equivalent to

$$AL^2=AK\cdot AM.$$

Substituting:

$$\frac{b^2c^2}{4(b^2+c^2)} = \frac{b^3c^3}{(b^2+c^2)^2}.$$

After cancellation,

$$(b^2+c^2)=4bc.$$

Let $t=b/c$. Then

$$t^2-4t+1=0,$$

so

$$t=2\pm\sqrt3.$$

Since

$$\tan\gamma=\frac{b}{c}=t,$$

and

$$2+\sqrt3=\tan75^\circ,\qquad 2-\sqrt3=\tan15^\circ,$$

the acute angles are $15^\circ$ and $75^\circ$.

The potentially dangerous step is proving that $L$ is the midpoint of $AD$; this must be established exactly, not guessed from the figure.

## Problem Understanding

We are given a right triangle $ABC$ with right angle at $A$. The altitude from $A$ to the hypotenuse $BC$ is $AD$. A circle having $AD$ as diameter meets the legs $AB$ and $AC$ again at points $K$ and $M$. The segment $KM$ intersects $AD$ at $L$.

The lengths $AK$, $AL$, and $AM$ form a geometric progression:

$$\frac{AK}{AL}=\frac{AL}{AM}.$$

We must determine the acute angles of the triangle.

This is a Type A problem. We must determine all possible acute angles and prove that no others satisfy the condition.

The core difficulty is converting the geometric-progression condition into an equation involving the shape of the right triangle. The crucial observation is that $L$ turns out to be the midpoint of $AD$.

The answer is

$$\boxed{\angle B=15^\circ,\ \angle C=75^\circ}$$

or vice versa.

## Proof Architecture

First, compute the coordinates of $D$, the foot of the altitude from $A$ to $BC$.

Second, derive the equation of the circle with diameter $AD$, and obtain explicit formulas for $AK$ and $AM$ from its intersections with the coordinate axes.

Third, determine the intersection point $L=KM\cap AD$, and prove that $L$ is the midpoint of $AD$.

Fourth, express $AL$, use the geometric-progression condition $AL^2=AK\cdot AM$, and derive an algebraic equation relating the legs $AB$ and $AC$.

Fifth, solve that equation and convert the result into the acute angles of the triangle.

The lemma most likely to fail under scrutiny is the claim that $L$ is the midpoint of $AD$; it must be proved by direct computation.

## Solution

Let

$$A=(0,0),\qquad B=(b,0),\qquad C=(0,c),$$

where $b,c>0$. Since $ABC$ is right-angled at $A$, the hypotenuse $BC$ has equation

$$\frac{x}{b}+\frac{y}{c}=1.$$

The altitude $AD$ is perpendicular to $BC$. Since the slope of $BC$ is $-c/b$, the slope of $AD$ is $b/c$. Hence

$$AD:\ y=\frac bc\,x.$$

Let $D$ be the intersection of these two lines. Solving

$$y=\frac bc\,x,\qquad \frac{x}{b}+\frac{y}{c}=1,$$

we obtain

$$x_D=\frac{bc^2}{b^2+c^2}, \qquad y_D=\frac{b^2c}{b^2+c^2}.$$

The circle with diameter $AD$ has equation

$$x^2+y^2-x_Dx-y_Dy=0.$$

Its intersection with $AB$ is obtained by setting $y=0$:

$$x(x-x_D)=0.$$

Besides the point $A$, the second intersection is

$$K=(x_D,0).$$

Therefore

$$AK=x_D=\frac{bc^2}{b^2+c^2}.$$

Similarly, setting $x=0$ gives the second intersection with $AC$:

$$M=(0,y_D),$$

hence

$$AM=y_D=\frac{b^2c}{b^2+c^2}.$$

The line $KM$ has intercept form

$$\frac{x}{x_D}+\frac{y}{y_D}=1.$$

Its intersection with $AD$ is found by substituting $y=\frac bc\,x$:

$$x\left(\frac1{x_D}+\frac{b/c}{y_D}\right)=1.$$

Using the formulas for $x_D$ and $y_D$,

$$\frac1{x_D}=\frac{b^2+c^2}{bc^2}, \qquad \frac{b/c}{y_D} =\frac{b^2+c^2}{bc^2}.$$

Hence

$$x_L=\frac{bc^2}{2(b^2+c^2)}=\frac{x_D}{2}.$$

Since $L\in AD$,

$$y_L=\frac bc\,x_L=\frac{y_D}{2}.$$

Thus

$$L=\left(\frac{x_D}{2},\frac{y_D}{2}\right),$$

so $L$ is the midpoint of $AD$. Consequently,

$$AL=\frac12\,AD.$$

The altitude length in a right triangle equals

$$AD=\frac{bc}{\sqrt{b^2+c^2}},$$

therefore

$$AL=\frac{bc}{2\sqrt{b^2+c^2}}.$$

Since $AK,AL,AM$ form a geometric progression,

$$AL^2=AK\cdot AM.$$

Substituting the expressions found above,

$$\frac{b^2c^2}{4(b^2+c^2)} = \frac{bc^2}{b^2+c^2}\cdot \frac{b^2c}{b^2+c^2}.$$

After simplification,

$$\frac{b^2+c^2}{4}=bc,$$

or

$$b^2+c^2=4bc.$$

Let

$$t=\frac{b}{c}.$$

Then

$$t^2-4t+1=0,$$

so

$$t=2\pm\sqrt3.$$

Now

$$\tan\angle C=\frac{AB}{AC}=\frac{b}{c}=t.$$

Since

$$\tan 15^\circ=2-\sqrt3, \qquad \tan 75^\circ=2+\sqrt3,$$

we obtain

$$\angle C=15^\circ \quad\text{or}\quad 75^\circ.$$

Because $\angle B+\angle C=90^\circ$, the acute angles of the triangle are

$$15^\circ \text{ and } 75^\circ.$$

$$\boxed{\angle B=15^\circ,\ \angle C=75^\circ\ \text{or vice versa}}$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the determination of $L$. From

$$\frac{x}{x_D}+\frac{y}{y_D}=1$$

and

$$y=\frac bc\,x,$$

we get

$$x=\frac1{\frac1{x_D}+\frac{b/c}{y_D}}.$$

Substituting the explicit values of $x_D$ and $y_D$ shows that the two terms in the denominator are equal. Hence the denominator is $2/x_D$, giving $x=x_D/2$. This proves exactly that $L$ is the midpoint of $AD$. A visual argument based on symmetry would be unjustified because the configuration has no obvious symmetry.

The second delicate step is the formula for $AD$. Since

$$D=\left(\frac{bc^2}{b^2+c^2},\frac{b^2c}{b^2+c^2}\right),$$

we have

$$AD^2=x_D^2+y_D^2 =\frac{b^2c^2(c^2+b^2)}{(b^2+c^2)^2} =\frac{b^2c^2}{b^2+c^2}.$$

Therefore

$$AD=\frac{bc}{\sqrt{b^2+c^2}}.$$

No additional theorem is required.

The third delicate step is solving the resulting condition. From

$$b^2+c^2=4bc,$$

division by $c^2$ gives

$$t^2-4t+1=0.$$

The two roots are positive and reciprocal:

$$2+\sqrt3,\qquad 2-\sqrt3.$$

These correspond exactly to the complementary angles $75^\circ$ and $15^\circ$, yielding a unique unordered pair of acute angles.

## Alternative Approaches

A synthetic solution can be built from power-of-a-point and homothety. Since $K$ and $M$ lie on the circle with diameter $AD$, the quadrilaterals $A,K,D,M$ possess several right angles. Using similar triangles, one can derive

$$AK=\frac{AD^2}{AB}, \qquad AM=\frac{AD^2}{AC}.$$

The line $KM$ is then shown to pass through the midpoint of $AD$, giving $AL=\frac12AD$. The condition $AL^2=AK\cdot AM$ reduces immediately to

$$AB^2+AC^2=4\,AB\cdot AC,$$

from which the same angle equation follows.

The coordinate method is preferable because every quantity required by the problem appears as a simple algebraic expression. The midpoint property of $L$ emerges from a direct computation and leaves no hidden geometric assumptions.
