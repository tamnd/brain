## Problem

Prove that there does not exist a polyhedron such that at least four edges are incident to each vertex and at least four edges are incident to each face.

L. G. Limanov

## Exploration

Let $V$, $E$, and $F$ denote the numbers of vertices, edges, and faces of the polyhedron.

The condition on the vertices says that every vertex is incident to at least four edges. Summing the degrees of all vertices gives

$$\sum_{v}\deg(v)=2E.$$

Since each degree is at least $4$,

$$2E\ge 4V,$$

hence

$$E\ge 2V.$$

The condition on the faces says that every face is bounded by at least four edges. If $l(f)$ denotes the number of edges on the boundary of a face, then

$$\sum_f l(f)=2E.$$

Each face contributes at least $4$, so

$$2E\ge 4F,$$

hence

$$E\ge 2F.$$

Combining these inequalities gives

$$V+F\le E.$$

For a polyhedron, Euler's formula holds:

$$V-E+F=2.$$

Rewriting,

$$V+F=E+2.$$

The two relations are incompatible, since they imply simultaneously

$$E+2=V+F\le E.$$

The contradiction appears immediate. The only place where a hidden mistake could occur is in the counting identities

$\sum \deg(v)=2E$ and $\sum l(f)=2E$. For a polyhedron, every edge has exactly two endpoints and is adjacent to exactly two faces, so both identities are valid.

## Problem Understanding

We are asked to prove that no polyhedron exists for which every vertex is incident to at least four edges and every face is incident to at least four edges.

This is a Type B problem, a pure proof.

The core difficulty is to combine the local lower bounds on vertex degrees and face boundary lengths with a global constraint on polyhedra. Euler's formula provides that global constraint. Double counting of edge incidences yields inequalities that contradict Euler's relation.

## Proof Architecture

First, prove that $E\ge 2V$ by counting vertex-edge incidences in two ways; the sum of vertex degrees equals $2E$, while every degree is at least $4$.

Second, prove that $E\ge 2F$ by counting face-edge incidences in two ways; the sum of face boundary lengths equals $2E$, while every face has at least four boundary edges.

Third, combine these inequalities to obtain $V+F\le E$.

Fourth, invoke Euler's formula $V-E+F=2$, which is equivalent to $V+F=E+2$.

Finally, show that $V+F\le E$ and $V+F=E+2$ cannot both hold.

The most delicate point is the justification that the sum of the face boundary lengths equals $2E$, because every edge must be counted exactly twice, once for each adjacent face.

## Solution

Assume, for contradiction, that such a polyhedron exists. Let $V$, $E$, and $F$ be its numbers of vertices, edges, and faces.

For each vertex $v$, let $\deg(v)$ be the number of edges incident to $v$. Every edge has exactly two endpoints, so counting vertex-edge incidences gives

$$\sum_v \deg(v)=2E.$$

By hypothesis, every vertex is incident to at least four edges. Hence

$$\sum_v \deg(v)\ge 4V.$$

Combining these relations yields

$$2E\ge 4V,$$

or

$$E\ge 2V.$$

Next, for each face $f$, let $l(f)$ denote the number of edges on its boundary. Every edge belongs to the boundaries of exactly two faces, so counting face-edge incidences gives

$$\sum_f l(f)=2E.$$

By hypothesis, every face is incident to at least four edges. Therefore

$$\sum_f l(f)\ge 4F.$$

Combining these relations yields

$$2E\ge 4F,$$

or

$$E\ge 2F.$$

Adding the inequalities

$$V\le \frac E2, \qquad F\le \frac E2,$$

we obtain

$$V+F\le E.$$

On the other hand, Euler's formula for a polyhedron states that

$$V-E+F=2.$$

Rearranging,

$$V+F=E+2.$$

The last two equations imply

$$E+2=V+F\le E,$$

which is impossible.

The assumption that such a polyhedron exists leads to a contradiction. Hence no polyhedron can have at least four edges incident to every vertex and at least four edges incident to every face.

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first counting step uses

$$\sum_v \deg(v)=2E.$$

Each edge contributes exactly $1$ to the degree of each of its two endpoints, hence contributes exactly $2$ to the total sum. Summing over all edges gives $2E$.

The second counting step uses

$$\sum_f l(f)=2E.$$

Each edge lies on the boundaries of exactly two faces of a polyhedron. Thus each edge contributes exactly $2$ to the total count of face-edge incidences. Summing over all edges gives $2E$. A careless argument could fail here if one forgot to justify that every edge is adjacent to two faces.

The contradiction step requires both inequalities. From $E\ge2V$ alone one gets only $V\le E/2$, which is compatible with Euler's formula. From $E\ge2F$ alone one gets only $F\le E/2$, also compatible with Euler's formula. Adding them is essential:

$$V+F\le E.$$

Euler gives

$$V+F=E+2.$$

The contradiction is exactly the difference of $2$ between these quantities.

## Alternative Approaches

A different proof uses average degrees. From Euler's formula one derives

$$\frac{2E}{V}<4 \quad\text{or}\quad \frac{2E}{F}<4,$$

which means that every polyhedron has either a vertex of degree at most $3$ or a face bounded by at most $3$ edges. This is a standard consequence of

$$V-E+F=2.$$

The given conditions exclude both possibilities, producing a contradiction.

The direct double-counting argument is preferable because it reaches the contradiction in a few lines and uses only the most basic incidence counts together with Euler's formula.
