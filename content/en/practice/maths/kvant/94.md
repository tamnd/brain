## Problem

Prove that there does not exist a polyhedron for which each vertex and each face is incident to at least four edges.

L. G. Limanov

## Exploration

A polyhedron with all vertices of degree at least $4$ forces a strong lower bound on the total vertex incidence with edges, namely that the sum of vertex degrees is at least $4V$. Since every edge contributes exactly $2$ to this sum, this yields $2E \ge 4V$, hence $E \ge 2V$.

Similarly, requiring that every face is incident to at least $4$ edges means every face has length at least $4$. Summing face degrees gives $2E \ge 4F$, hence $E \ge 2F$.

Combining both inequalities suggests that both $V$ and $F$ are at most $\frac{E}{2}$, which forces $V+F \le E$. However Euler’s formula for a convex polyhedron gives $V - E + F = 2$, which rearranges to $V+F = E+2$. This produces an immediate contradiction. The only potential subtlety is whether the standard identities for sums of vertex degrees and face degrees apply without hidden assumptions; they do for any polyhedron whose graph is a connected planar embedding.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem, a nonexistence proof. We must show that no polyhedron can satisfy the condition that every vertex is incident to at least four edges and every face is bounded by at least four edges.

The core difficulty is to translate local combinatorial constraints on vertex degrees and face sizes into global constraints via double counting, and then reconcile them with Euler’s formula. The expected contradiction arises from incompatible linear inequalities in $V$, $E$, and $F$.

## Proof Architecture

The proof proceeds by first establishing that the sum of all vertex degrees equals $2E$, and from the hypothesis that each vertex has degree at least $4$, deducing $2E \ge 4V$. This yields $V \le \frac{E}{2}$.

Next, the sum of all face boundary lengths equals $2E$, and from the hypothesis that each face has at least $4$ edges, deducing $2E \ge 4F$, hence $F \le \frac{E}{2}$.

These two inequalities combine into $V+F \le E$.

Finally, Euler’s formula $V - E + F = 2$ implies $V+F = E+2$, contradicting the previous inequality.

The most delicate point is the validity of the identity relating face boundary sums to $2E$, which depends on each edge being incident to exactly two faces in a planar polyhedral embedding.

## Solution

Let $V$, $E$, and $F$ denote the numbers of vertices, edges, and faces of the polyhedron. The polyhedron determines a connected planar graph embedded on the sphere, so Euler’s formula holds in the form $V - E + F = 2$.

Each edge has two endpoints, so when summing degrees over all vertices, each edge contributes exactly two to the total. This gives the identity

$$\sum_{v} \deg(v) = 2E.$$

By assumption, every vertex is incident to at least four edges, hence $\deg(v) \ge 4$ for all vertices $v$. Summing over all vertices yields

$$2E = \sum_{v} \deg(v) \ge 4V,$$

which implies $V \le \frac{E}{2}$.

Each edge is incident to exactly two faces in a polyhedral embedding, so when summing the number of edges bounding each face, every edge is counted twice. This gives

$$\sum_{f} \deg(f) = 2E,$$

where $\deg(f)$ denotes the number of edges in the boundary of face $f$ counted with multiplicity.

By assumption, every face is incident to at least four edges, so $\deg(f) \ge 4$ for all faces $f$. Summing over all faces yields

$$2E = \sum_{f} \deg(f) \ge 4F,$$

which implies $F \le \frac{E}{2}$.

Adding the two inequalities gives

$$V + F \le E.$$

On the other hand, Euler’s formula gives

$$V - E + F = 2,$$

which rearranges to

$$V + F = E + 2.$$

The two relations $V+F \le E$ and $V+F = E+2$ are incompatible, since they would imply $E+2 \le E$, a contradiction. Therefore, no such polyhedron exists. This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The identity $\sum_v \deg(v) = 2E$ follows because each edge has exactly two endpoints and contributes once to the degree of each endpoint, with no overcounting or omission.

The identity $\sum_f \deg(f) = 2E$ requires that each edge is incident to exactly two faces. In a polyhedral embedding on the sphere, every edge borders exactly two distinct faces, and each occurrence is counted once in the boundary length of each face, ensuring each edge contributes exactly two to the total face-degree sum.

The transformation from the inequalities $V \le \frac{E}{2}$ and $F \le \frac{E}{2}$ to $V+F \le E$ is obtained by direct addition, preserving validity without hidden assumptions.

## Alternative Approaches

An alternative approach is to start from the planar graph viewpoint and apply Euler’s formula together with the handshake lemmas for vertices and faces, deriving a general inequality for planar graphs of minimum vertex degree and minimum face size. In this framing, the contradiction appears as a violation of the general bound $E \le 2V + 2F - 4$ specialized under strong local degree constraints.

A different geometric viewpoint uses curvature: assigning each vertex and face a discrete angular defect shows that a polyhedron with all degrees at least $4$ would force nonpositive curvature everywhere, contradicting the total curvature $4\pi$ of the sphere. The combinatorial proof above is more direct and avoids geometric interpretation.
