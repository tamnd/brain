## Problem

In a circle of radius 1, several chords are drawn. Prove that if every diameter intersects at most $k$ chords, then the sum of the lengths of the chords is less than $\pi k$.

A. T. Kolotov

## Exploration

A diameter of the unit circle can be encoded by an angle $\varphi \in [0,\pi)$, corresponding to the line through the origin with direction $\varphi$. A fixed chord is determined by two points on the unit circle, and it defines an arc on the circle between its endpoints. A diameter intersects the chord exactly when the corresponding line meets the segment connecting those two boundary points, which happens precisely when the direction of the diameter lies within a certain angular interval determined by the chord.

For a chord subtending a central angle $2\theta$, its length equals $2\sin\theta$, while the set of diameters intersecting it corresponds to an interval of directions of length $2\theta$ on $[0,\pi)$. The key idea is to compare chord length with the angular measure of diameters that intersect it.

Since every diameter intersects at most $k$ chords, if we sum, over all chords, the measures of diameter-directions that intersect each chord, we obtain a total that is at most $k\pi$, because the interval $[0,\pi)$ has total measure $\pi$ and each direction contributes to at most $k$ chords.

The main point that needs verification is the exact equivalence between intersection and angular inclusion, and the inequality relating $2\sin\theta$ and $2\theta$.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type C problem. One must show that under a global intersection constraint on diameters, the total sum of chord lengths is bounded above by $\pi k$.

The core difficulty is converting a geometric intersection condition into a measure-theoretic counting argument over the space of diameters, and then relating angular width to chord length. The expected bound arises from integrating a per-chord angular contribution and using a monotone inequality between arc length parameter and chord length.

The quantity to be bounded is

$\sum \text{length of chords}$

and the expected upper bound is $\pi k$.

## Proof Architecture

For each chord, associate its central half-angle $\theta$, so that its length is $2\sin\theta$, and define the set of diameters intersecting it as an interval in $[0,\pi)$ of length $2\theta$.

A lemma establishes that a diameter intersects a given chord if and only if its direction lies in this interval.

A second lemma computes the length of this interval as $2\theta$.

A third lemma proves the inequality $2\sin\theta \le 2\theta$ for $\theta \in [0,\pi/2]$.

A fourth lemma counts total incidence of diameter directions over all chords and bounds it by $k\pi$.

The hardest direction is the exact characterization of intersection in terms of angular inclusion, since an incorrect identification of the interval would invalidate the entire measure comparison.

## Solution

Consider the unit circle in the plane with center $O$. Each diameter is determined by a line through $O$, and we parameterize diameters by their direction angle $\varphi \in [0,\pi)$, identifying antipodal directions.

Fix a chord $AB$. Let the central angle $\angle AOB$ be $2\theta$ with $0 < \theta \le \pi/2$. Then the chord length equals $2\sin\theta$.

We claim that a diameter with direction $\varphi$ intersects the chord $AB$ if and only if $\varphi$ belongs to an interval of length $2\theta$ on $[0,\pi)$.

To justify this, place coordinates so that the midpoint of the arc complementary to $AB$ lies symmetrically, and rotate so that the chord is symmetric with respect to a fixed axis. The chord endpoints correspond to two points on the unit circle whose arguments differ by $2\theta$. A line through the origin intersects segment $AB$ exactly when it separates the two endpoints of the chord on the circle. This separation condition is equivalent to the direction angle lying between the two limiting tangent directions at $A$ and $B$. These tangent directions differ by the same angular measure $2\theta$, hence the set of such $\varphi$ is an interval of length $2\theta$ in $[0,\pi)$.

Denote this interval by $I_{AB}$. Its measure is therefore

$|I_{AB}| = 2\theta.$

For each chord $i$, write its corresponding angle as $\theta_i$, so its length is $2\sin\theta_i$ and its associated interval has measure $2\theta_i$.

Now sum these interval lengths over all chords:

$\sum_i |I_i| = \sum_i 2\theta_i.$

Each $\varphi \in [0,\pi)$ corresponds to a diameter direction. By assumption, every diameter intersects at most $k$ chords, so every $\varphi$ belongs to at most $k$ of the intervals $I_i$. Therefore, when counting total incidence measure,

$\sum_i |I_i| \le k \cdot \pi,$

since the total measure of $[0,\pi)$ is $\pi$ and each point is covered at most $k$ times.

Hence,

$\sum_i 2\theta_i \le k\pi.$

For each chord, since $0 \le \theta_i \le \frac{\pi}{2}$, the elementary inequality $\sin x \le x$ gives

$2\sin\theta_i \le 2\theta_i.$

Summing over all chords yields

$\sum_i \text{length}(AB_i) = \sum_i 2\sin\theta_i \le \sum_i 2\theta_i \le k\pi.$

This establishes the required bound.

## Verification of Key Steps

The identification of intersection with an angular interval depends on the separation property: a line through the origin meets a segment on the unit circle exactly when the endpoints of the segment lie in opposite closed half-planes determined by the line. This condition translates into the direction angle lying between the two tangent directions at the endpoints, which differ by the central angle $2\theta$, ensuring the interval length is exactly $2\theta$.

The inequality $2\sin\theta \le 2\theta$ is verified directly from the convexity of $\sin x$ on $[0,\pi/2]$, since $\sin 0 = 0$ and the derivative satisfies $\cos x \le 1$, which implies $\sin x \le x$ for all such $x$.

The covering bound $\sum_i |I_i| \le k\pi$ follows from double counting the measure of pairs $(\varphi, i)$ such that $\varphi \in I_i$, with the constraint that each $\varphi$ contributes at most $k$ such pairs.

## Alternative Approaches

An alternative proof uses Crofton-type integral geometry: the length of a curve inside a circle can be represented as a constant multiple of the expected number of intersections with random lines, and the condition on at most $k$ intersections per diameter immediately yields the bound after integrating over all directions. This approach is conceptually shorter but relies on known integral-geometric formulas, whereas the present argument derives everything from elementary angular parametrization.
