## Problem

The polynomial $p$ has the following property: for some number $a$ $$p(x)=p(a-x).$$

Prove that $p(x)$ can be represented as a polynomial in $$\left(x-\frac{a}{2}\right)^2.$$

For example, if $p(x)=x^5+(1-x)^5$, then, obviously, $p(x)=p(1-x)$ and, as is easy to verify, $$p(x)=5y^2+\frac{5}{2}y+\frac{1}{16},$$ where $$y=\left(x-\frac{1}{2}\right)^2.$$

## Exploration

Let $p(x)$ be a polynomial satisfying $p(x)=p(a-x)$. Introducing the shifted variable $t=x-\frac{a}{2}$ rewrites the symmetry point $x=a-x$ as $t\mapsto -t$, since replacing $x$ by $a-x$ yields $t\mapsto (a-x)-\frac{a}{2}=\frac{a}{2}-x=-t$. Defining $q(t)=p!\left(t+\frac{a}{2}\right)$ transforms the condition into $q(t)=q(-t)$ for all $t$.

A polynomial invariant under $t\mapsto -t$ must have no odd powers in its expansion, since each monomial $t^k$ changes sign according to the parity of $k$. This suggests that $q(t)$ is a polynomial in $t^2$. The key point requiring justification is that the vanishing of all odd coefficients indeed follows rigorously from functional symmetry, and that any polynomial involving only even powers can be expressed uniquely as a polynomial in $t^2$.

The structure of the argument therefore reduces to proving that symmetry forces coefficient cancellation for odd degrees and that the remaining even part can be reindexed into a single polynomial expression in one variable $u=t^2$.

## Problem Understanding

The problem asks to prove that if a polynomial satisfies $p(x)=p(a-x)$ for some constant $a$, then it can be written as a polynomial in $\left(x-\frac{a}{2}\right)^2$.

This is a Type B problem, requiring a structural representation theorem rather than a computation or classification. The core difficulty is to convert a functional symmetry about $x=\frac{a}{2}$ into an algebraic restriction on polynomial coefficients and then to reformulate the resulting even polynomial as a polynomial in a single squared variable.

The expected conclusion is that there exists a polynomial $r$ such that

$$p(x)=r\!\left(\left(x-\frac{a}{2}\right)^2\right).$$

## Proof Architecture

Introduce the change of variables $t=x-\frac{a}{2}$ and define $q(t)=p!\left(t+\frac{a}{2}\right)$, establishing equivalence between $p(x)=p(a-x)$ and $q(t)=q(-t)$.

Prove that if a polynomial satisfies $q(t)=q(-t)$ for all $t$, then all coefficients of odd powers in its standard monomial expansion vanish.

Prove that any polynomial containing only even powers of $t$ can be written uniquely as a polynomial in $t^2$ by regrouping terms according to exponent halving.

Combine these statements to construct a polynomial $r$ such that $q(t)=r(t^2)$ and translate back to $p(x)$.

The most delicate step is the deduction that symmetry forces vanishing of all odd coefficients, since it depends on comparing polynomial identities rather than pointwise heuristic parity arguments.

## Solution

Define $t=x-\frac{a}{2}$ and introduce the polynomial $q(t)=p!\left(t+\frac{a}{2}\right)$. Substituting $x=a-x$ into the definition of $t$ yields

$$(a-x)-\frac{a}{2}=\frac{a}{2}-x=-\left(x-\frac{a}{2}\right)=-t.$$

The condition $p(x)=p(a-x)$ becomes $q(t)=q(-t)$ for all real $t$.

Write the polynomial $q(t)$ in the form

$$q(t)=\sum_{k=0}^{n} c_k t^k.$$

Then

$$q(-t)=\sum_{k=0}^{n} c_k (-t)^k=\sum_{k=0}^{n} c_k (-1)^k t^k.$$

The identity $q(t)=q(-t)$ implies

$$\sum_{k=0}^{n} c_k t^k=\sum_{k=0}^{n} c_k (-1)^k t^k.$$

Subtracting the right-hand side from the left-hand side gives the zero polynomial

$$\sum_{k=0}^{n} c_k \bigl(1-(-1)^k\bigr)t^k=0.$$

A polynomial is identically zero only when all its coefficients vanish, hence for each $k$ one has

$$c_k \bigl(1-(-1)^k\bigr)=0.$$

For even $k$, the factor $1-(-1)^k$ equals $0$, and for odd $k$ it equals $2$, which forces $c_k=0$. Therefore $c_k=0$ for all odd indices $k$, and the polynomial reduces to

$$q(t)=\sum_{j=0}^{m} c_{2j} t^{2j}.$$

Define a new polynomial $r$ by

$$r(u)=\sum_{j=0}^{m} c_{2j} u^j.$$

Substituting $u=t^2$ yields

$$r(t^2)=\sum_{j=0}^{m} c_{2j} (t^2)^j=\sum_{j=0}^{m} c_{2j} t^{2j}=q(t).$$

Returning to the original variable gives

$$p(x)=q\!\left(x-\frac{a}{2}\right)=r\!\left(\left(x-\frac{a}{2}\right)^2\right).$$

This establishes that $p(x)$ is a polynomial in $\left(x-\frac{a}{2}\right)^2$.

## Verification of Key Steps

The passage from $q(t)=q(-t)$ to vanishing of odd coefficients relies on subtracting the two polynomial expressions and obtaining a polynomial with all coefficients multiplied by $1-(-1)^k$. The validity of coefficientwise comparison follows from the fact that a polynomial identically equal to zero has all coefficients equal to zero, since otherwise evaluating at sufficiently many distinct points would contradict uniqueness of polynomial interpolation.

The construction of $r(u)$ from even coefficients is validated by direct substitution $u=t^2$, which preserves each term $t^{2j}$ as $u^j$ without introducing cross terms, ensuring no loss of information or ambiguity in representation.

The substitution back to $x$ preserves equality since $t$ was defined as an affine transformation of $x$, hence invertible over the underlying field.

## Alternative Approaches

A different approach uses the decomposition of any polynomial into even and odd parts with respect to the origin after shifting by $\frac{a}{2}$. Writing $q(t)=\frac{q(t)+q(-t)}{2}+\frac{q(t)-q(-t)}{2}$ shows directly that the odd part vanishes under the symmetry condition, leaving only an even polynomial. The remainder of the argument proceeds by expressing even polynomials as functions of $t^2$. This method emphasizes functional decomposition rather than coefficient comparison, but it ultimately encodes the same algebraic structure. This completes the proof. ∎
