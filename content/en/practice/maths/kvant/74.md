## Problem

The polynomial $p$ has the following property: for some number $a$ $$p(x)=p(a-x).$$

Prove that $p(x)$ can be expressed as a polynomial in $$\left(x-\frac{a}{2}\right)^2.$$

For example, if $p(x)=x^5+(1-x)^5$, then obviously $p(x)=p(1-x)$ and, as is not difficult to verify, $$p(x)=5y^2+\frac{5}{2}y+\frac{1}{16},$$ where $$y=\left(x-\frac{1}{2}\right)^2.$$

## Exploration

The condition

$$p(x)=p(a-x)$$

says that the polynomial is unchanged when $x$ is reflected about the point $a/2$. It is natural to shift the variable so that the center of symmetry becomes the origin. Let

$$t=x-\frac a2.$$

Then

$$a-x=\frac a2-t,$$

so the condition becomes

$$p\!\left(\frac a2+t\right)=p\!\left(\frac a2-t\right).$$

Define

$$q(t)=p\!\left(\frac a2+t\right).$$

Then

$$q(t)=q(-t).$$

Thus $q$ is an even polynomial.

Small examples suggest what should happen. If

$$q(t)=t^4+3t^2+1,$$

then it is already a polynomial in $t^2$. If

$$q(t)=t^6-2t^4+t^2,$$

the same is true. An even polynomial has only even powers:

$$q(t)=c_0+c_2t^2+c_4t^4+\cdots.$$

Replacing $t^2$ by a new variable $y$ gives

$$q(t)=c_0+c_2y+c_4y^2+\cdots.$$

Hence $q$ is a polynomial in $y=t^2$.

The only point that requires a careful proof is the statement that a polynomial satisfying $q(t)=q(-t)$ contains no odd powers. That is the step most likely to hide an unjustified assertion.

## Problem Understanding

We are given a polynomial $p$ such that

$$p(x)=p(a-x)$$

for all $x$, where $a$ is a fixed number. We must prove that $p$ can be written in the form

$$p(x)=F\!\left(\left(x-\frac a2\right)^2\right)$$

for some polynomial $F$.

This is a Type B problem. The statement itself is given and must be proved.

The core difficulty is to convert the symmetry about the point $a/2$ into an algebraic statement and then prove rigorously that the resulting polynomial contains only even powers.

## Proof Architecture

First, introduce the shifted polynomial

$$q(t)=p\!\left(\frac a2+t\right),$$

and show from the hypothesis that $q(t)=q(-t)$.

Second, prove that if a polynomial satisfies $q(t)=q(-t)$, then every coefficient of an odd power of $t$ is zero. This follows by comparing the coefficients of $q(t)$ and $q(-t)$.

Third, write

$$q(t)=c_0+c_2t^2+c_4t^4+\cdots,$$

and factor it through the variable $y=t^2$.

Finally, substitute

$$t=x-\frac a2$$

to obtain the required representation of $p$.

The most delicate lemma is the second one, because it is the step that converts symmetry into the vanishing of all odd coefficients.

## Solution

Define

$$q(t)=p\!\left(\frac a2+t\right).$$

For every $t$,

$$q(-t) = p\!\left(\frac a2-t\right).$$

Since

$$\frac a2-t = a-\left(\frac a2+t\right),$$

the given condition $p(x)=p(a-x)$ yields

$$p\!\left(\frac a2-t\right) = p\!\left(\frac a2+t\right).$$

Hence

$$q(-t)=q(t)$$

for all $t$.

Write

$$q(t)=c_0+c_1t+c_2t^2+\cdots+c_nt^n.$$

Then

$$q(-t)=c_0-c_1t+c_2t^2-\cdots+(-1)^nc_nt^n.$$

Because $q(t)=q(-t)$ identically, the coefficients of equal powers of $t$ must coincide. For each odd index $k$,

$$c_k=-c_k,$$

so

$$c_k=0.$$

Thus every odd-power coefficient vanishes, and

$$q(t)=c_0+c_2t^2+c_4t^4+\cdots.$$

Let

$$F(y)=c_0+c_2y+c_4y^2+\cdots .$$

Then

$$q(t)=F(t^2).$$

Substituting back $t=x-\frac a2$, we obtain

$$p(x) = q\!\left(x-\frac a2\right) = F\!\left(\left(x-\frac a2\right)^2\right).$$

Hence $p(x)$ is a polynomial in

$$\left(x-\frac a2\right)^2.$$

This completes the proof.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the change of variables. Starting from

$$q(t)=p\!\left(\frac a2+t\right),$$

we compute

$$q(-t)=p\!\left(\frac a2-t\right).$$

The identity

$$\frac a2-t=a-\left(\frac a2+t\right)$$

shows that the argument of $p$ on the right is exactly the reflection of $\frac a2+t$ about $a/2$. This is the precise place where the hypothesis is used.

The second delicate step is proving that odd coefficients vanish. If

$$q(t)=\sum_{k=0}^{n} c_k t^k,$$

then

$$q(-t)=\sum_{k=0}^{n} (-1)^k c_k t^k.$$

Since two polynomials are equal if and only if their corresponding coefficients are equal, for odd $k$ we obtain

$$c_k=(-1)^k c_k=-c_k.$$

Thus $2c_k=0$, giving $c_k=0$. No appeal to intuition about symmetry is needed.

The final step is the passage from an even polynomial to a polynomial in $t^2$. After all odd coefficients have been shown to vanish,

$$q(t)=c_0+c_2t^2+c_4t^4+\cdots+c_{2m}t^{2m}.$$

Setting $y=t^2$ gives

$$q(t)=c_0+c_2y+c_4y^2+\cdots+c_{2m}y^m,$$

which is genuinely a polynomial in $y$.

## Alternative Approaches

Another proof uses the division algorithm. Let

$$t=x-\frac a2$$

and define

$$q(t)=p\!\left(\frac a2+t\right).$$

Divide $q(t)$ by $t^2-y$, treating $y$ as a parameter. The remainder has the form

$$A(y)+tB(y).$$

Substituting $t$ and $-t$ gives the same value of $q$ because $q(t)=q(-t)$. The odd part $tB(y)$ must therefore vanish identically, so $B(y)=0$. Hence $q(t)=A(t^2)$, proving the claim.

The main proof is preferable because it relies only on the elementary fact that equal polynomials have equal coefficients and directly exhibits the structure of an even polynomial.
