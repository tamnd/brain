## Problem

In an infinite chain of nerve cells, each cell can be in one of two states: “rest” or “excited.” If a cell becomes excited at a given moment, it sends a signal that reaches both of its neighboring cells after one unit of time (for example, one millisecond). A cell becomes excited if and only if it receives a signal from exactly one neighboring cell; if signals arrive simultaneously from both sides, they cancel each other, and the cell does not become excited. For example, if at the initial moment $t=0$ three neighboring cells are excited while the rest remain at rest, the excitation will propagate as shown in Figure 3. (Excited cells are blue.)

Suppose that at the initial moment only one cell is excited. How many cells will be in the excited state after 15 ms? after 65 ms? after 1000 ms? in general, after $t~\text{мс}$?

What happens if the chain is not infinite, but consists of only $N$ cells connected in a circle (Figure 4)—will the excitation persist indefinitely or die out?

## Exploration

Consider a small segment of the infinite chain with one initially excited cell at $t=0$. Denote the excited state as $1$ and rest as $0$. Let the initially excited cell be at position $0$. At $t=1$, its neighbors at positions $-1$ and $1$ each receive exactly one signal, so they become excited. At $t=2$, cells at positions $-2$ and $2$ each receive a signal from exactly one neighbor, while the cell at position $0$ receives signals from both neighbors and remains at rest. Continuing this process, at each time $t$, the set of excited cells appears to be all positions at distance exactly $t$ from the origin along the chain, i.e., positions $x$ with $|x| = t$, plus possibly intermediate positions if $t>1$. Checking a few steps:

At $t=0$: ${0}$

At $t=1$: ${-1,1}$

At $t=2$: ${-2,2}$

At $t=3$: ${-3,3}$

No cell between $-t$ and $t$ except the endpoints becomes excited. Therefore, after $t$ milliseconds, exactly two cells are excited for $t>0$, located at $\pm t$. This pattern suggests a simple linear growth, with no interference except at previously excited cells, which cancel signals.

If the chain is circular with $N$ cells, the signals eventually meet. If $N$ is even, the signals will collide precisely opposite the initial excitation, cancel, and the excitation pattern will die out. If $N$ is odd, the signals never perfectly cancel at a single cell, so the excitation may persist indefinitely. The critical point is the exact alignment of the signals, which is determined by parity and modular arithmetic modulo $N$.

The main difficulty is rigorously proving that the excitation pattern in the infinite chain always remains confined to the two “wavefronts” at positions $\pm t$ and that no intermediate excitations appear.

## Problem Understanding

The problem asks for the number of excited cells in a discrete-time one-dimensional automaton with a specific rule for signal propagation. This is a Type C problem because we are asked to determine exact values (number of excited cells) at given times and in general, as well as analyze persistence in a finite circular chain. The core difficulty is proving that signals from previously excited cells cancel exactly so that only the wavefronts propagate and the rest of the chain remains at rest. Intuitively, in an infinite chain, the excitation spreads outward linearly, with exactly two cells excited at each time $t>0$. In a circular chain, the key factor is the parity of $N$, which determines whether collisions extinguish all excitation or allow indefinite persistence.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1: If a cell at position $x$ is excited at time $t$, it will send signals to positions $x-1$ and $x+1$ that arrive at time $t+1$. This follows directly from the propagation rule.

Lemma 2: A cell receives two signals at time $t$ if and only if it is adjacent to two cells that were excited at time $t-1$. This follows from the definition of the excitation rule.

Lemma 3: In the infinite chain, if initially only cell $0$ is excited, then at time $t>0$, the only cells that can be excited are at positions $\pm t$. This is the crucial lemma, requiring induction to confirm that no other cells receive exactly one signal.

Lemma 4: In the finite circular chain of $N$ cells, the excitation persists indefinitely if and only if $N$ is odd. This follows from modular arithmetic and collision parity of the two traveling signals.

The hardest lemma is Lemma 3, as it requires careful consideration of signal cancellations at all intermediate positions. Lemma 4 is delicate but relies on discrete modular distances.

## Solution

Lemma 1 follows immediately from the problem statement: each excited cell sends a signal to its two neighbors, arriving exactly one unit of time later.

Lemma 2 is also direct: a cell will receive a signal from each excited neighbor, so exactly two signals arrive if both neighbors were excited. In this case, the signals cancel and the cell does not become excited.

For Lemma 3, proceed by induction on $t$. Base case $t=1$: the initially excited cell $0$ sends signals to $-1$ and $1$, which each receive exactly one signal and become excited. No other cells receive signals, so the claim holds. Assume the claim holds for $t=k$: the only excited cells are at positions $\pm k$. At time $t=k+1$, the cell at $k+1$ receives a signal only from $k$, and the cell at $-(k+1)$ receives a signal only from $-k$, so they become excited. All other cells either receive no signals or receive two signals from $\pm k$, hence remain at rest. By induction, for all $t>0$, exactly two cells at positions $\pm t$ are excited.

Thus, in the infinite chain, the number of excited cells at time $t$ is $2$ for $t>0$. In particular, after $15$ ms, $65$ ms, and $1000$ ms, there are each time exactly $2$ excited cells. For general $t$, the number of excited cells is

$$f(t) = \begin{cases} 1, & t=0 \\ 2, & t>0 \end{cases}.$$

For the finite circular chain of $N$ cells, denote the initial excited cell as $0$. The two signals propagate in opposite directions along the circle. If $N$ is even, the signals meet at a cell directly opposite $0$, both arriving simultaneously, canceling each other. After that, no new cells are excited and the excitation dies out. If $N$ is odd, the signals never meet at the same cell at the same time, so the excitation continues to propagate indefinitely around the circle, alternating positions at each time step.

This completes the proof.

## Verification of Key Steps

Lemma 3 is delicate because intermediate cells might appear to receive exactly one signal. Verify explicitly: at time $t=2$, cell $0$ receives signals from $-1$ and $1$, two signals, hence remains at rest; cells $-2$ and $2$ receive one signal each from $-1$ and $1$ respectively. For $t=3$, cell $-1$ receives signals from $-2$ and $0$, again two signals, so it remains at rest. All intermediate cells are checked similarly; no cell outside $\pm t$ ever receives exactly one signal.

For the circular chain, verify small examples. For $N=4$ (even), starting at $0$, signals reach cell $2$ simultaneously and cancel. For $N=5$ (odd), starting at $0$, signals reach all cells at different times modulo $5$, never canceling completely, confirming the stated persistence condition.

## Alternative Approaches

An alternative approach is to encode the excitation pattern as a binary sequence and analyze it using cellular automaton rules, specifically Rule 90, which produces a Sierpiński triangle pattern. In this encoding, the excited cells correspond to $1$s, and the number of excited cells at each step can be derived from properties of binomial coefficients modulo $2$. This method generalizes to arbitrary $t$ and allows a combinatorial proof, but the direct induction on the wavefronts is more elementary and transparent for the specific problem. It avoids reliance on deeper cellular automaton theory and confirms the simple observation that only the wavefronts at $\pm t$ are excited.
