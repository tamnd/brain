## Problem

In an infinite chain of nerve cells, each cell can be in one of two states: “rest” and “excitation.” If a cell becomes excited at a given moment, it sends a signal that reaches both neighboring cells after one unit of time (say, after one millisecond). A cell becomes excited if and only if it receives a signal from one of its neighboring cells; if signals arrive simultaneously from both sides, they cancel each other out, and the cell does not become excited. For example, if at the initial moment of time $t=0$ three neighboring cells are excited and all the others are left at rest, then the excitation propagates as shown in Figure 3. (The excited cells are blue.)

Suppose that at the initial moment only one cell is excited. How many cells will be in the excited state after 15 ms? after 65 ms? after 1000 ms? in general, after $t~\text{мс}$?

## Exploration

Let the cells be indexed by the integers, and let cell $0$ be excited at time $0$. Denote by $S_t$ the set of excited cells at time $t$.

The rule is linear modulo $2$. A cell becomes excited at time $t+1$ exactly when an odd number of its two neighbors are excited at time $t$. Since there are only two neighbors, this means that the state of cell $n$ at time $t+1$ is the sum modulo $2$ of the states of cells $n-1$ and $n+1$ at time $t$.

Write $a_n(t)\in{0,1}$ for the state of cell $n$ at time $t$. Then

$$a_n(t+1)=a_{n-1}(t)+a_{n+1}(t)\pmod 2.$$

Starting from

$$S_0=\{0\},$$

the first few generations are

$$S_1=\{-1,1\},$$

$$S_2=\{-2,2\},$$

because the two signals arriving at $0$ cancel.

Next,

$$S_3=\{-3,-1,1,3\}.$$

Then

$$S_4=\{-4,4\}.$$

The numbers of excited cells are

$$1,2,2,4,2,\ldots$$

This resembles Pascal's triangle modulo $2$. To test that idea, represent a configuration by a Laurent polynomial

$$F_t(x)=\sum_n a_n(t)x^n.$$

The evolution rule becomes

$$F_{t+1}(x)=(x+x^{-1})F_t(x).$$

Hence

$$F_t(x)=(x+x^{-1})^t.$$

Since coefficients are taken modulo $2$, the excited cells correspond exactly to the nonzero coefficients of $(x+x^{-1})^t$ over $\mathbf F_2$.

Now use the binary structure. If

$$t=\sum \varepsilon_k 2^k,$$

then in characteristic $2$

$$(x+x^{-1})^{2^k}=x^{2^k}+x^{-2^k},$$

and therefore

$$(x+x^{-1})^t = \prod_{\varepsilon_k=1} \left(x^{2^k}+x^{-2^k}\right).$$

Each factor contributes one of two choices. Distinct choices give distinct exponents because powers of $2$ are linearly independent in binary representation. Thus the number of nonzero monomials equals

$$2^{s(t)},$$

where $s(t)$ is the number of $1$'s in the binary expansion of $t$.

Checking small cases:

$$t=3=11_2,\qquad 2^{s(t)}=4,$$

which matches.

$$t=4=100_2,\qquad 2^{s(t)}=2,$$

which also matches.

The crucial point is proving that different selections of signs in the product produce different exponents.

For the circular chain, let the cells be numbered modulo $N$. The state space has only $2^N$ configurations. The evolution is deterministic, so every trajectory eventually becomes periodic.

The all-rest state is absorbing. The question is whether the excitation always dies out.

Let $T$ be the linear operator

$$(Ta)_n=a_{n-1}+a_{n+1}.$$

If $N$ is odd, then from

$$(Ta)_n=0$$

we obtain

$$a_{n+1}=a_{n-1},$$

so all cells with indices of the same parity are equal. Since $N$ is odd, repeated shifts by $2$ traverse all residues modulo $N$, hence all coordinates are equal. The kernel consists of the two constant configurations. Over $\mathbf F_2$, the constant configuration $(1,\ldots,1)$ is not reachable from a single excited cell because the parity of the total number of excited cells is preserved when $N$ is odd. Thus the evolution is invertible on the reachable states, and a nonzero state can never reach the zero state. Consequently the motion is periodic and never dies out.

For even $N$, the kernel is larger. Taking $N=2$ already gives extinction after one step. Taking $N=4$ gives a periodic orbit. Thus both behaviors occur. The finite circular chain does not have a universal fate depending only on finiteness; it depends on $N$.

## Problem Understanding

We are given a one-dimensional cellular automaton. A cell becomes excited at the next moment exactly when exactly one of its two neighbors is excited at the current moment. Simultaneous signals from both sides cancel.

This is a Type A problem. We must determine exactly how many cells are excited after an arbitrary time $t$ when the infinite chain starts with a single excited cell, and then analyze the behavior for a circular chain of $N$ cells.

The core difficulty is to obtain a closed formula for the number of excited cells at time $t$. The evolution is governed by repeated cancellations modulo $2$, and the answer is encoded by the binary expansion of $t$.

The answer for the infinite chain is that the number of excited cells at time $t$ equals

$$2^{s(t)},$$

where $s(t)$ is the number of $1$'s in the binary expansion of $t$.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma states that if $a_n(t)$ denotes the state of cell $n$ at time $t$, then $a_n(t+1)=a_{n-1}(t)+a_{n+1}(t)\pmod2$.

The second lemma states that the generating polynomial $F_t(x)=\sum_n a_n(t)x^n$ satisfies $F_t(x)=(x+x^{-1})^t$.

The third lemma states that if $t=\sum\varepsilon_k2^k$, then over $\mathbf F_2$,

$$(x+x^{-1})^t = \prod_{\varepsilon_k=1}(x^{2^k}+x^{-2^k}).$$

The fourth lemma states that every choice of one term from each factor produces a distinct exponent, so the number of nonzero monomials equals $2^{s(t)}$.

The final step substitutes particular values of $t$ and then analyzes the circular chain through the linear operator $T$.

The most delicate lemma is the fourth one, because distinctness of exponents must be proved rigorously.

## Solution

Let $a_n(t)\in{0,1}$ denote the state of cell $n$ at time $t$, where $1$ means excited and $0$ means at rest.

A cell becomes excited at time $t+1$ precisely when exactly one of its two neighbors is excited at time $t$. Thus

$$a_n(t+1)=a_{n-1}(t)+a_{n+1}(t)\pmod2.$$

Introduce the Laurent polynomial

$$F_t(x)=\sum_{n\in\mathbf Z}a_n(t)x^n.$$

Then

$$\begin{aligned} F_{t+1}(x) &=\sum_n\bigl(a_{n-1}(t)+a_{n+1}(t)\bigr)x^n \\ &=x\sum_m a_m(t)x^m+x^{-1}\sum_m a_m(t)x^m \\ &=(x+x^{-1})F_t(x). \end{aligned}$$

Initially only cell $0$ is excited, so

$$F_0(x)=1.$$

Consequently,

$$F_t(x)=(x+x^{-1})^t.$$

Let

$$t=\sum_{k=0}^r\varepsilon_k2^k, \qquad \varepsilon_k\in\{0,1\}.$$

Since we are working modulo $2$,

$$(a+b)^{2^k}=a^{2^k}+b^{2^k}.$$

Hence

$$(x+x^{-1})^{2^k} = x^{2^k}+x^{-2^k}.$$

Therefore

$$F_t(x) = \prod_{\varepsilon_k=1} \left(x^{2^k}+x^{-2^k}\right).$$

Each monomial in this product is obtained by choosing either $x^{2^k}$ or $x^{-2^k}$ from every factor. The resulting exponent has the form

$$\sum_{\varepsilon_k=1}\sigma_k2^k, \qquad \sigma_k\in\{1,-1\}.$$

Suppose two different choices of signs produced the same exponent. Subtracting the two expressions gives

$$\sum_{\varepsilon_k=1}\tau_k2^k=0,$$

where each $\tau_k$ belongs to ${0,\pm2}$ and not all $\tau_k$ are zero.

Let $m$ be the largest index with $\tau_m\neq0$. Then

$$|\tau_m|2^m > \sum_{k<m}|\tau_k|2^k,$$

because

$$2^{m+1} > 2\sum_{k=0}^{m-1}2^k = 2^{m+1}-2.$$

Hence cancellation is impossible. Thus all exponents are distinct.

There are exactly two choices in every factor, and the number of factors equals

$$s(t)=\sum_k\varepsilon_k,$$

the number of $1$'s in the binary expansion of $t$. Therefore the number of nonzero monomials in $F_t(x)$ equals

$$2^{s(t)}.$$

Each nonzero monomial corresponds to one excited cell. Consequently the number of excited cells after time $t$ is

$$2^{s(t)}.$$

For the requested values,

$$15=1111_2, \qquad s(15)=4,$$

so the number of excited cells is

$$2^4=16.$$

Next,

$$65=1000001_2, \qquad s(65)=2,$$

so the number is

$$2^2=4.$$

Finally,

$$1000=1111101000_2, \qquad s(1000)=6,$$

so the number is

$$2^6=64.$$

Now consider a circular chain of $N$ cells. The states form a vector space of dimension $N$ over $\mathbf F_2$, and the evolution is the linear map

$$(Ta)_n=a_{n-1}+a_{n+1}.$$

Since there are only finitely many states, every trajectory eventually becomes periodic.

If $N$ is odd and $Ta=0$, then

$$a_{n+1}=a_{n-1}$$

for every $n$. Repeatedly adding $2$ modulo $N$ reaches every residue class because $N$ is odd. Hence all coordinates are equal, so the kernel of $T$ consists only of the two constant configurations.

Starting from one excited cell, the total parity

$$\sum_n a_n$$

remains odd when $N$ is odd, because

$$\sum_n(Ta)_n = 2\sum_n a_n \equiv \sum_n a_n \pmod2.$$

The zero configuration has even parity and is unreachable. Since $T$ is injective on the odd-parity states, the trajectory can never reach the zero configuration. Thus for odd $N$ the excitation never dies out and eventually becomes periodic.

For even $N$, extinction may occur. For example, when $N=2$, one excited cell produces two simultaneous signals to the other cell, they cancel, and the system reaches the zero state after one step.

For even $N$, periodic motion may also occur. When $N=4$, a single excited cell alternates with the configuration consisting of its two neighbors, producing a cycle of period $2$.

Hence for a circular chain there is no universal extinction law. The evolution is always eventually periodic; for odd $N$ the excitation never dies out, while for even $N$ either extinction or a nontrivial periodic cycle may occur, depending on $N$.

Thus, for the infinite chain,

$$\boxed{\text{number of excited cells at time }t=2^{s(t)}},$$

where $s(t)$ is the number of $1$'s in the binary expansion of $t$.

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the factorization

$$(x+x^{-1})^t = \prod_{\varepsilon_k=1}(x^{2^k}+x^{-2^k}).$$

For $t=5=101_2$ this gives

$$(x+x^{-1})^5 = (x+x^{-1})(x^4+x^{-4}),$$

which expands to four monomials. Direct computation from the automaton yields four excited cells at time $5$, confirming the factorization.

The second delicate step is the proof that distinct sign choices give distinct exponents. Consider the largest power of two appearing in the difference of two exponent sums. Its contribution has magnitude at least $2^{m+1}$, while the combined magnitude of all smaller powers is at most

$$2(2^m-1)=2^{m+1}-2.$$

Hence the largest term cannot be cancelled. Omitting this estimate would leave open the possibility that different choices produce the same exponent.

The third delicate step is the odd-$N$ argument. From

$$a_{n+1}=a_{n-1}$$

one obtains

$$a_n=a_{n+2}=a_{n+4}=\cdots.$$

Because $\gcd(2,N)=1$ when $N$ is odd, every residue class modulo $N$ appears in this sequence. If this fact were overlooked, the kernel could be incorrectly described.

## Alternative Approaches

Instead of generating functions, one may identify the automaton directly with Pascal's triangle modulo $2$. The state of cell $n$ at time $t$ equals the binomial coefficient

$$\binom{t}{(t+n)/2}$$

modulo $2$ whenever $t+n$ is even. Lucas's theorem then shows that the odd binomial coefficients in row $t$ are exactly $2^{s(t)}$ in number. This yields the same formula.

The generating-function method is preferable because the identity

$$(x+x^{-1})^{2^k}=x^{2^k}+x^{-2^k}$$

immediately exposes the binary structure responsible for the answer and gives the count of excited cells with very little computation.
