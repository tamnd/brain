## Problem

In an infinite chain of nerve cells, each cell can be in one of two states: "rest" or "excited." If a cell becomes excited at a given moment, it sends a signal that reaches both of its neighboring cells after a unit of time (for example, after one millisecond). A cell becomes excited if and only if it receives a signal from exactly one neighboring cell; if signals arrive simultaneously from both sides, they cancel each other out, and the cell does not become excited. For example, if at the initial moment $t=0$ three adjacent cells are excited while the others remain at rest, the excitation will propagate as shown in Figure 3. (Excited cells are blue.)

Suppose that at the initial moment only one cell is excited. How many cells will be in the excited state after 15 ms? after 65 ms? after 1000 ms? in general, after $t~\text{мс}$?

What happens if the chain is not infinite but contains only $N$ cells connected in a circle (Figure 4)? Will the excitation persist indefinitely, or will it die out?

## Exploration

Let the cells be indexed by the integers, and let $a_t(n)\in{0,1}$ denote whether cell $n$ is excited at time $t$.

A cell is excited at time $t+1$ precisely when exactly one of its two neighbors was excited at time $t$. Since the states are binary, "exactly one" is the exclusive OR operation. Hence

$$a_{t+1}(n)=a_t(n-1)+a_t(n+1)\pmod 2.$$

Initially only cell $0$ is excited:

$$a_0(0)=1,\qquad a_0(n)=0\ (n\ne0).$$

Computing the first few rows:

$$t=0:\quad 1,$$

$$t=1:\quad 1\qquad 1,$$

$$t=2:\quad 1\qquad 0\qquad 1,$$

$$t=3:\quad 1\ 1\ 1\ 1,$$

$$t=4:\quad 1\ 0\ 0\ 0\ 1.$$

This is the familiar Sierpiński pattern generated by Pascal's triangle modulo $2$.

Let

$$P_t(x)=\sum_n a_t(n)x^n$$

over the field $\mathbf F_2$. Then

$$P_{t+1}(x)=(x+x^{-1})P_t(x),$$

so

$$P_t(x)=(x+x^{-1})^t.$$

The coefficient of $x^{2k-t}$ equals

$$\binom tk \pmod 2.$$

Thus the excited cells at time $t$ correspond exactly to the odd binomial coefficients in row $t$ of Pascal's triangle.

The number of odd coefficients in row $t$ is known to be $2^{s(t)}$, where $s(t)$ is the number of $1$'s in the binary expansion of $t$. This can be proved directly from Lucas' theorem. If

$$t=\sum \varepsilon_i2^i, \qquad k=\sum \kappa_i2^i,$$

then

$$\binom tk\equiv \prod_i \binom{\varepsilon_i}{\kappa_i} \pmod2.$$

The coefficient is odd exactly when every binary digit $\kappa_i$ satisfies $\kappa_i\le\varepsilon_i$. Each digit $\varepsilon_i=1$ gives two choices for $\kappa_i$, and each digit $\varepsilon_i=0$ gives one choice. Hence there are $2^{s(t)}$ odd coefficients.

For the circular chain with $N$ cells, the state space has only $2^N$ configurations. Since the evolution rule is deterministic and linear over $\mathbf F_2$, every trajectory is eventually periodic. The question is whether excitation can disappear completely. The zero configuration is a fixed point.

Testing small values:

$$N=3: \quad 100\to011\to011.$$

Excitation persists.

$$N=4: \quad 1000\to0101\to0000.$$

It dies.

$$N=5: \quad 10000\to01001\to10110\to10000.$$

A period $3$ cycle appears.

The decisive observation is obtained from the polynomial model modulo $x^N-1$:

$$P_t(x)\equiv (x+x^{-1})^tP_0(x).$$

If $N=2^m$, then in characteristic $2$

$$(x+x^{-1})^{2^m} = x^{2^m}+x^{-2^m} \equiv x^{N}+x^{-N} \equiv 1+1 \equiv0 \pmod{x^N-1}.$$

Therefore every state becomes zero after at most $N$ steps.

If $N$ is not a power of $2$, write $N=2^mr$ with $r>1$ odd. Then

$$(x+x^{-1})^{2^m} = x^{2^m}+x^{-2^m}.$$

Since $r>1$, the monomial exponents $\pm2^m$ are distinct modulo $N$, so this polynomial is nonzero modulo $x^N-1$. Multiplication by it is invertible because

$$\gcd(x^{2^m}+x^{-2^m},\,x^N-1)=1$$

in $\mathbf F_2[x]/(x^N-1)$ when $r$ is odd. Hence the evolution map is invertible, so a nonzero state can never reach the zero state. Excitation persists forever.

The delicate point is proving the criterion "dies out iff $N$ is a power of $2$".

## Problem Understanding

We have a one-dimensional cellular automaton. A cell is excited at the next moment exactly when an odd number of its neighbors, namely one, are excited now. Since there are only two neighbors, the rule is XOR.

The first task is to determine the number of excited cells after time $t$ when initially only one cell is excited. This is a Type C problem, since a definite quantity must be found.

The second task asks for the long-term behavior on a circular chain of $N$ cells. We must determine exactly when excitation eventually disappears and when it persists.

The expected answer for the infinite chain is that the number of excited cells at time $t$ equals

$$2^{s(t)},$$

where $s(t)$ is the number of ones in the binary expansion of $t$.

For the circular chain, excitation dies out exactly when $N$ is a power of $2$
