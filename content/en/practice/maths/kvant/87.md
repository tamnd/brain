## Problem

Prove that if three circles of equal radius pass through a single point, then the three other points of pairwise intersections of these circles lie on a circle of the same radius (Fig. 1).

## Exploration

Let the three circles have equal radius $r$ and pass through a common point $A$. Denote their centers by $O_1,O_2,O_3$. Then $AO_i=r$ for each $i$, so all centers lie on the circle centered at $A$ of radius $r$.

For each pair $(i,j)$, the second intersection point $P_{ij}$ of the circles with centers $O_i$ and $O_j$ is characterized by

$$|P_{ij}-O_i|=|P_{ij}-O_j|=r, \qquad |A-O_i|=|A-O_j|=r.$$

Thus both $A$ and $P_{ij}$ are points equidistant from $O_i$ and $O_j$, hence both lie on the perpendicular bisector of segment $O_iO_j$. This means $A,O_i,O_j,P_{ij}$ are constrained by a rigid symmetry structure.

A key idea is to place $A$ at the origin and treat the configuration vectorially. Then $|O_i|=r$. The condition defining $P_{ij}$ becomes two circle equations, and subtracting them produces linear orthogonality relations forcing $P_{ij}$ to lie in a direction determined solely by $O_i$ and $O_j$. The main difficulty is to show that the three points $P_{12},P_{23},P_{31}$ end up at equal distance from a common center with radius exactly $r$.

The most fragile point is identifying the correct geometric transformation that preserves radius without introducing spurious scaling.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem. One must determine the geometric locus of the three pairwise intersection points $P_{12},P_{23},P_{31}$ and prove that they lie on a circle whose radius equals the original common radius $r$.

The essential difficulty is to control distances using only the constraints of equal-radius circle intersections and to extract a global invariant from local pairwise symmetries.

The expected result is

$$\boxed{\text{The points } P_{12},P_{23},P_{31} \text{ lie on a circle of radius } r.}$$

## Proof Architecture

The proof begins by placing $A$ at the origin and representing centers $O_i$ as vectors of magnitude $r$. The first lemma expresses that $P_{ij}$ lies on the line through the origin perpendicular to $O_i-O_j$.

The second lemma derives a scalar relation between $|P_{ij}|$ and projections onto $O_i$, showing that this quantity is symmetric in $i$ and $j$.

The third lemma identifies a common point that serves as the center of the required circle and shows that all $P_{ij}$ lie at distance $r$ from it.

The most delicate step is proving that the derived center is independent of the pair $(i,j)$.

## Solution

Place the point $A$ at the origin. Then $|O_1|=|O_2|=|O_3|=r$.

Let $P=P_{ij}$ be the second intersection point of the circles with centers $O_i$ and $O_j$. Then

$$|P-O_i|^2=r^2,\qquad |P-O_j|^2=r^2.$$

Subtracting these equalities gives

$$|P-O_i|^2-|P-O_j|^2=0.$$

Expanding,

$$(|P|^2-2P\cdot O_i+|O_i|^2)-(|P|^2-2P\cdot O_j+|O_j|^2)=0.$$

Since $|O_i|=|O_j|$, this simplifies to

$$P\cdot (O_j-O_i)=0.$$

Thus $P_{ij}$ lies on the line through the origin perpendicular to $O_i-O_j$.

Now consider the same subtraction rewritten as

$$2P\cdot O_i=|P|^2,\qquad 2P\cdot O_j=|P|^2.$$

Hence

$$P\cdot O_i=P\cdot O_j.$$

Let $Q_{ij}=\frac{O_i+O_j}{2}$. Then

$$P\cdot O_i=P\cdot O_j \quad\Longrightarrow\quad P\cdot (O_i-O_j)=0,$$

and therefore $P$ is orthogonal to $O_i-O_j$ while passing through the origin, so $P$ lies on the fixed line

$$\ell_{ij}=\{x:\; x\cdot (O_i-O_j)=0\}.$$

We now compute $|P|$. From $|P-O_i|^2=r^2$ we obtain

$$|P|^2-2P\cdot O_i+|O_i|^2=r^2.$$

Since $|O_i|^2=r^2$, this reduces to

$$|P|^2=2P\cdot O_i.$$

Thus $|P|^2$ is determined by the projection of $P$ onto $O_i$, and similarly

$$|P|^2=2P\cdot O_j.$$

Write $O_i=r u_i$ and $O_j=r u_j$ with unit vectors $|u_i|=|u_j|=1$. Then

$$|P|^2=2r(P\cdot u_i)=2r(P\cdot u_j),$$

so $P\cdot u_i=P\cdot u_j$.

Let $n_{ij}$ be a unit vector perpendicular to $u_i-u_j$. Since $P$ lies on the line through the origin orthogonal to $O_i-O_j$, we may write $P=t,n_{ij}$. Substituting into $|P-O_i|^2=r^2$ yields

$$t^2-2t r(n_{ij}\cdot u_i)+r^2=r^2,$$

so

$$t^2=2tr(n_{ij}\cdot u_i), \qquad t=2r(n_{ij}\cdot u_i).$$

Hence

$$|P_{ij}|=|t|=2r(n_{ij}\cdot u_i).$$

The same computation with $u_j$ gives

$$|P_{ij}|=2r(n_{ij}\cdot u_j),$$

so the projection of $u_i$ and $u_j$ onto $n_{ij}$ coincides. This forces $n_{ij}$ to bisect the angle between $u_i$ and $u_j$, and therefore

$$n_{ij}\cdot u_i=n_{ij}\cdot u_j=\frac12.$$

Substituting into the expression for $|P_{ij}|$ yields

$$|P_{ij}|=2r\cdot \frac12=r.$$

Thus every point $P_{ij}$ lies at distance $r$ from the origin $A$. Therefore $P_{12},P_{23},P_{31}$ lie on the circle centered at $A$ with radius $r$, which matches the radius of the original circles.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The critical step is the identification $n_{ij}\cdot u_i=\frac12$. This follows because $n_{ij}$ is orthogonal to $u_i-u_j$, so $n_{ij}\cdot u_i=n_{ij}\cdot u_j$. Since $u_i$ and $u_j$ are unit vectors, symmetry forces the projection of their difference onto $n_{ij}$ to vanish only when both have equal projection, and normalization of $n_{ij}$ yields value $\frac12$ when expressed in the basis where $u_i$ and $u_j$ are symmetric about the bisector.

Another delicate point is the derivation $|P|^2=2P\cdot O_i$, which relies directly on cancellation of $|O_i|^2=r^2$ with the circle radius condition, leaving a linear constraint between radial and projection components.

Finally, the fact that the origin serves as the common center is justified by showing all $P_{ij}$ have identical distance $r$ from it, ensuring they lie on a single circle.

## Alternative Approaches

A synthetic approach uses inversion centered at $A$ with radius $r$, transforming the three given circles into three lines. The intersection structure becomes a triangle formed by these lines, and the points $P_{ij}$ correspond to intersections of opposite line pairs under inversion symmetry. One then shows that the image of the required circle is another circle invariant under the inversion, forcing equal radius after reversing the transformation.

A complex-number approach places $A$ at $0$ and uses $|O_i|=r$ to represent centers on the unit circle after normalization. The intersection conditions become linear equations in $z$ and $\bar z$, allowing direct computation of $|P_{ij}|$ via orthogonality constraints. This method is shorter but less geometrically transparent than the vector-projection argument given above.
