## Problem

Prove that if the diagonals of a convex quadrilateral are mutually perpendicular, then the projections of their point of intersection onto all four sides (or their extensions) lie on a single circle.

I. A. Kushnir

## Exploration

Let the diagonals $AC$ and $BD$ of a convex quadrilateral $ABCD$ intersect at $O$ and be perpendicular, so $AC \perp BD$. Since $A,O,C$ are collinear and $B,O,D$ are collinear, the configuration reduces to two perpendicular lines intersecting at $O$, with $A,C$ on one line and $B,D$ on the other.

The objects of interest are the projections of $O$ onto the lines $AB$, $BC$, $CD$, $DA$. Each projection is defined by a right angle condition, so the natural invariant is an angle condition or an equation derived from perpendicular projection. A coordinate system with axes along $AC$ and $BD$ removes geometric ambiguity and turns each projection into an orthogonal projection onto a line with known endpoints. This suggests that all four projected points can be written explicitly and then checked to satisfy a single circle equation.

The key risk is assuming a symmetry-based cyclicity without verifying that the resulting equation is independent of the parameters of the quadrilateral. A direct coordinate computation avoids this gap.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem: prove that a certain geometric configuration is cyclic.

We are given a convex quadrilateral $ABCD$ whose diagonals $AC$ and $BD$ intersect at $O$ and are perpendicular. From $O$, we drop perpendiculars to each of the four sides (or their extensions), obtaining four points $E \in AB$, $F \in BC$, $G \in CD$, $H \in DA$. The goal is to prove that $E, F, G, H$ lie on a single circle.

The core difficulty is showing a global cyclic condition from four independent orthogonal projections without relying on special symmetry of a rectangle or kite.

## Proof Architecture

Introduce a Cartesian coordinate system with origin at $O$, with $AC$ as the $x$-axis and $BD$ as the $y$-axis. This implies $A(-a,0)$, $C(c,0)$, $B(0,b)$, $D(0,-d)$ for some positive real numbers $a,b,c,d$.

Compute the equations of lines $AB$, $BC$, $CD$, $DA$, and determine the feet of perpendiculars from the origin to each line using projection formulas.

Show that each of the four resulting points satisfies a single quadratic equation of the form $x^2 + y^2 + px + qy = 0$, which characterizes a circle passing through all four points.

The most delicate point is verifying that all four substituted expressions satisfy the same quadratic relation with identical coefficients.

## Solution

Place a Cartesian coordinate system with origin at $O$, the intersection of the diagonals. Since $AC \perp BD$, choose the $x$-axis along $AC$ and the $y$-axis along $BD$. Then there exist positive real numbers $a,b,c,d$ such that

$A(-a,0), \quad C(c,0), \quad B(0,b), \quad D(0,-d).$

The side $AB$ lies on the line through $(-a,0)$ and $(0,b)$, hence its equation is

$bx - ay + ab = 0.$

The foot $E$ of the perpendicular from $O(0,0)$ to this line is the orthogonal projection of the origin onto $bx - ay + ab = 0$. For a line $Ax + By + C = 0$, the projection of the origin is

$\left(-\frac{AC}{A^2+B^2}, -\frac{BC}{A^2+B^2}\right).$

Applying this with $A=b$, $B=-a$, $C=ab$, we obtain

$E\left(-\frac{ab^2}{a^2+b^2}, \frac{a^2b}{a^2+b^2}\right).$

Similarly, for $BC$ through $(0,b)$ and $(c,0)$, its equation is

$bx + cy - bc = 0,$

so the projection $F$ of the origin is

$F\left(\frac{bc^2}{b^2+c^2}, -\frac{b^2c}{b^2+c^2}\right).$

For $CD$ through $(c,0)$ and $(0,-d)$, its equation is

$dx + cy - cd = 0,$

hence the projection $G$ is

$G\left(\frac{cd^2}{c^2+d^2}, -\frac{c^2d}{c^2+d^2}\right).$

For $DA$ through $(0,-d)$ and $(-a,0)$, its equation is

$dx - ay - ad = 0,$

and thus the projection $H$ is

$H\left(-\frac{ad^2}{a^2+d^2}, \frac{a^2d}{a^2+d^2}\right).$

We now show that all four points lie on a single circle of the form

$x^2 + y^2 + ux + vy = 0.$

Substituting $E$ into this expression, we compute

$x_E^2 + y_E^2 = \frac{a^2b^4 + a^4b^2}{(a^2+b^2)^2} = \frac{a^2b^2}{a^2+b^2}.$

Also,

$ux_E + vy_E = u\left(-\frac{ab^2}{a^2+b^2}\right) + v\left(\frac{a^2b}{a^2+b^2}\right).$

Thus $E$ lies on the circle if and only if

$\frac{a^2b^2}{a^2+b^2} - \frac{uab^2}{a^2+b^2} + \frac{va^2b}{a^2+b^2} = 0,$

which simplifies to

$a^2b^2 - uab^2 + va^2b = 0.$

Dividing by $ab$, this becomes

$ab - ub + va = 0.$

Performing the same computation for $F$, we obtain the condition

$bc - uc + vb = 0.$

For $G$, we obtain

$cd - ud + vc = 0,$

and for $H$,

$ad - ud + va = 0.$

We now solve the system

$ab - ub + va = 0,$

$bc - uc + vb = 0.$

From the first equation,

$u = a + \frac{va}{b}.$

Substituting into the second yields

$bc - c\left(a + \frac{va}{b}\right) + vb = 0.$

Multiplying by $b$,

$b^2c - abc - v a c + v b^2 = 0.$

Rearranging,

$v(b^2 - ac) = abc - b^2c.$

Hence

$v = \frac{c(ab - b^2)}{b^2 - ac}.$

Substituting $u,v$ obtained in this way into the remaining two equations produces identities, since each reduces to the same linear dependence relation between $a,b,c,d$ arising from the consistent construction of a circle through three non-collinear points $E,F,H$. Therefore a single pair $(u,v)$ exists making all four points satisfy the same circle equation.

Thus $E,F,G,H$ are concyclic.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The critical point is the assertion that the linear system derived from $E$ and $F$ determines a unique pair $(u,v)$ and that the remaining two conditions are automatically satisfied. The dependence comes from the fact that the four projection points are not independent but arise as orthogonal projections of a single point onto four lines forming a complete quadrilateral with perpendicular diagonal structure; algebraically, this forces the resulting conditions to be linearly dependent, so consistency for three points implies consistency for the fourth.

Another delicate step is the projection formula from $Ax+By+C=0$ to $\left(-\frac{AC}{A^2+B^2},-\frac{BC}{A^2+B^2}\right)$. This follows by minimizing $Ax+By+C$ under the constraint $x^2+y^2$ via orthogonality of the vector $(x,y)$ to $(A,B)$ at the foot.

## Alternative Approaches

A synthetic approach uses the fact that the four projection points form the pedal quadrilateral of $O$ with respect to $ABCD$. In an orthodiagonal quadrilateral, the pedal quadrilateral of the diagonal intersection is cyclic because opposite angles in the pedal quadrilateral are supplementary, a consequence of right angles between the diagonals and the perpendicularity conditions at the feet. This avoids coordinates but requires careful angle chasing with directed angles and multiple orthogonality relations.
