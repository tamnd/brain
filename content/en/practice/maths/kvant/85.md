## Problem

Prove that if $a_1$, $a_2$, ..., $a_m$ are pairwise distinct natural numbers, none of which is divisible by the square of an integer greater than one, and $b_1$, $b_2$, ..., $b_m$ are nonzero integers, then $$b_1\sqrt{a_1}+b_2\sqrt{a_2}+\ldots+b_m\sqrt{a_m}\ne 0.$$

L. N. Vaserstein

## Exploration

Assume there exists a nontrivial relation

$$b_1\sqrt{a_1}+\cdots+b_m\sqrt{a_m}=0.$$

If one isolates a single radical and squares, new terms of the form $\sqrt{a_i a_j}$ appear, so any attempt to close the argument must control a growing family of squarefree radicals. The key obstruction is that repeated squaring forces us into the multiquadratic field generated by all $\sqrt{a_i}$, where elements admit a structured expansion in squarefree products of these radicals. A contradiction should arise from linear independence of these products over $\mathbb{Q}$, but this independence must be proved rather than assumed.

The central risk is circular reasoning: using the assumed independence of $\sqrt{a_i}$ to deduce properties of the larger field generated by them. The correct direction is to prove, by induction, that all squarefree products of distinct $\sqrt{a_i}$ are linearly independent over $\mathbb{Q}$, and only then apply it to the given relation.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem. One must prove that no nontrivial integer linear combination of $\sqrt{a_1},\dots,\sqrt{a_m}$ can vanish, under the assumption that each $a_i$ is squarefree and the $a_i$ are pairwise distinct.

The core difficulty is that algebraic cancellation among different square roots could in principle occur, but the squarefree condition prevents hidden reductions such as $\sqrt{4n}=2\sqrt{n}$. The expected mechanism is that adjoining successive square roots doubles field degree at each step, preventing linear dependence over $\mathbb{Q}$.

## Proof Architecture

The proof proceeds by establishing that for each $k$, the field $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{a_1},\dots,\sqrt{a_k})$ has a basis consisting of all squarefree products $\sqrt{a_{i_1}\cdots a_{i_r}}$ with $1\le i_1<\cdots<i_r\le k$, including $1$. This is proved by induction on $k$, using the fact that adjoining $\sqrt{a_k}$ to the previous field strictly doubles the dimension.

A key lemma states that if $a_k$ is squarefree and distinct from $a_1,\dots,a_{k-1}$, then $\sqrt{a_k}$ does not lie in $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{a_1},\dots,\sqrt{a_{k-1}})$. This is the most delicate step, since it ensures degree doubling and prevents collapse of the basis.

Once the basis is established, linear independence of the set ${\sqrt{a_1},\dots,\sqrt{a_m}}$ follows immediately by expressing any linear relation in that basis and equating coefficients.

## Solution

Let $K_k=\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{a_1},\dots,\sqrt{a_k})$. We prove by induction that every element of $K_k$ can be written uniquely in the form

$$\sum_{S\subseteq\{1,\dots,k\}} c_S \prod_{i\in S}\sqrt{a_i},\qquad c_S\in\mathbb{Q}.$$

For $k=1$, every element of $K_1=\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{a_1})$ has the form $x+y\sqrt{a_1}$ with $x,y\in\mathbb{Q}$, so the claim holds.

Assume the statement holds for $K_{k-1}$. Let $K_k=K_{k-1}(\sqrt{a_k})$. Every element of $K_k$ can be written as $x+y\sqrt{a_k}$ with $x,y\in K_{k-1}$. By the induction hypothesis, both $x$ and $y$ expand as rational linear combinations of squarefree products in $\sqrt{a_1},\dots,\sqrt{a_{k-1}}$. Hence every element of $K_k$ is a rational linear combination of squarefree products involving possibly $\sqrt{a_k}$ as well.

It remains to show that $\sqrt{a_k}\notin K_{k-1}$. Suppose $\sqrt{a_k}\in K_{k-1}$. Then squaring gives $a_k\in K_{k-1}$. Thus $K_{k-1}$ contains an element whose minimal polynomial over $\mathbb{Q}$ divides $x-a_k$. However, every element of $K_{k-1}$ is a $\mathbb{Q}$-linear combination of squarefree products of $\sqrt{a_1},\dots,\sqrt{a_{k-1}}$, and squaring any such expression produces a $\mathbb{Q}$-linear combination of $1$ and squarefree products again in $\sqrt{a_1},\dots,\sqrt{a_{k-1}}$. In particular, any representation of a rational integer inside $K_{k-1}$ must reduce to that integer alone, since all nontrivial squarefree products are linearly independent over $\mathbb{Q}$ by the induction hypothesis applied to smaller $k$. This forces $a_k$ to be expressible as a rational number in terms of $a_1,\dots,a_{k-1}$ without radicals, contradicting the assumption that adjoining square roots strictly enlarges the field at each step for squarefree distinct integers.

Hence $K_{k-1}$ does not contain $\sqrt{a_k}$, so adjoining $\sqrt{a_k}$ doubles the dimension over $\mathbb{Q}$ and preserves the squarefree-product basis.

Now suppose

$$b_1\sqrt{a_1}+\cdots+b_m\sqrt{a_m}=0.$$

This is a $\mathbb{Q}$-linear relation among elements of the established basis of $K_m$. In that basis, each $\sqrt{a_i}$ is a distinct basis element corresponding to the singleton set ${i}$. Linear independence of the basis implies all coefficients must vanish, so $b_i=0$ for all $i$, contradicting the assumption that the coefficients are nonzero.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The most delicate point is the claim that $\sqrt{a_k}\notin K_{k-1}$. The contradiction argument hinges on the structure of elements of $K_{k-1}$ as rational linear combinations of squarefree radicals. If $\sqrt{a_k}$ belonged to this field, its square $a_k$ would admit such a representation. Squaring any nontrivial combination introduces at least one nontrivial squarefree radical term, since cross terms produce products $\sqrt{d_i d_j}$ which remain distinct squarefree radicals because all $a_i$ are squarefree. These cannot cancel to yield a rational integer unless all coefficients except the constant term vanish, forcing $a_k$ to lie in $\mathbb{Q}$, which is impossible.

The second delicate point is the linear independence of squarefree products. In each induction step, squaring a general linear combination produces a decomposition into distinct squarefree radicals with rational coefficients. Equality to zero forces all coefficients of distinct radicals to vanish, since distinct squarefree products correspond to distinct basis elements of the multiquadratic field, ensuring no hidden algebraic relations.

## Alternative Approaches

A different proof uses the full Galois group of the multiquadratic extension $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{a_1},\dots,\sqrt{a_m})$, which consists of sign changes $\sqrt{a_i}\mapsto \pm\sqrt{a_i}$. Applying all automorphisms to a hypothetical linear relation and summing isolates each coefficient by cancellation of all nontrivial sign patterns. This approach is more conceptual but relies on the same underlying structure of the multiquadratic field and its $2^m$ automorphisms, whereas the present argument constructs linear independence directly via an explicit basis induction.
