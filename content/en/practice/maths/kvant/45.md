## Problem

Prove that from any two hundred integers one can choose one hundred numbers whose sum is divisible by 100.

Try to generalize this problem: prove that from any $2n—1$ numbers one can choose $n$ whose sum is divisible by $n$, where $n\ge 2$.

All-Union Mathematical Olympiad (1970, Grade 9)

## Exploration

Let $a_1,\dots,a_{2n-1}$ be integers. The goal is to select $n$ of them whose sum is divisible by $n$, equivalently congruent to $0$ modulo $n$.

Reducing all numbers modulo $n$ does not change the problem, so we work in $\mathbb{Z}_n$. The task becomes: from $2n-1$ elements of $\mathbb{Z}_n$, choose $n$ whose sum is $0$ in $\mathbb{Z}_n$.

Small cases suggest a structural inevitability. For $n=2$, any three integers contain two of the same parity, whose sum is even. For $n=3$, among five residues in ${0,1,2}$ one can always find three summing to $0$ modulo $3$.

A direct pigeonhole argument on single residues is insufficient, since no residue needs to appear $n$ times. The correct structure must come from relations between sums of subsets rather than individual elements.

A useful idea is to study all $n$-subsets and their sums modulo $n$. Since there are many such subsets but only $n$ possible residues, two subsets must share the same sum modulo $n$. The crucial step is to exploit two different $n$-subsets with equal sums to extract a structured cancellation producing a new subset of size $n$ with sum $0$.

The key difficulty is ensuring that a symmetric difference argument produces exactly $n$ elements while preserving control over the sum modulo $n$.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem. One must prove that from any $2n-1$ integers one can select $n$ whose sum is divisible by $n$.

The core difficulty is that no simple counting argument on residues guarantees a large homogeneous set, so the proof must use additive structure of subsets rather than individual elements.

The final answer is that such a selection is always possible, so the desired subset always exists.

## Proof Architecture

The proof uses the following structure.

First, reduce all integers modulo $n$, since divisibility by $n$ depends only on residues.

Next, consider all subsets of size $n$ and assign to each subset its sum in $\mathbb{Z}_n$.

Then use a double counting argument on pairs consisting of a subset and an element of it to produce two distinct $n$-subsets with equal sums modulo $n$ and with intersection of size $n-1$.

From such a pair, construct a new $n$-subset by exchanging elements so that the sum becomes $0$ modulo $n$.

The most delicate point is proving the existence of two $n$-subsets with equal sum and intersection exactly $n-1$, since weaker intersection information is insufficient for the exchange step.

## Solution

All numbers are replaced by their residues modulo $n$, since the sum of a set is divisible by $n$ if and only if its residue sum in $\mathbb{Z}_n$ is $0$.

Let $A_1,\dots,A_{\binom{2n-1}{n}}$ be all subsets of ${1,\dots,2n-1}$ of size $n$, and define $s(A)$ as the sum of elements of $A$ in $\mathbb{Z}_n$.

Since $s(A)$ takes values in $\mathbb{Z}_n$, there exist at least $\left\lceil \binom{2n-1}{n}/n \right\rceil$ subsets having the same sum. Fix a collection $\mathcal{F}$ of such subsets all having the same sum $r$ in $\mathbb{Z}_n$, chosen maximal with respect to inclusion.

Among all pairs $A,B \in \mathcal{F}$, choose a pair maximizing $|A \cap B|$. Let $|A \cap B| = k$. Since $|A|=|B|=n$, the symmetric difference has size $2(n-k)$.

Assume $k \le n-2$. Then both $A\setminus B$ and $B\setminus A$ contain at least two elements. Choose $x \in A\setminus B$ and $y \in B\setminus A$. Define

$$A' = (A \setminus \{x\}) \cup \{y\}, \quad B' = (B \setminus \{y\}) \cup \{x\}.$$

Both $A'$ and $B'$ still have size $n$. Moreover,

$$s(A') = s(A) - x + y, \quad s(B') = s(B) - y + x.$$

Since $s(A)=s(B)=r$, if $x \not\equiv y \pmod n$, then $s(A') \ne r$ or $s(B') \ne r$, contradicting maximality of $\mathcal{F}$. Therefore every such exchange must preserve membership in $\mathcal{F}$, which forces a closure under all such swaps inside the symmetric difference.

This closure implies that all elements of $A\setminus B$ are congruent modulo $n$ to all elements of $B\setminus A$, hence every element in $A\cup B$ lies in a single residue class. Then all elements of $A$ are congruent, so $s(A)\equiv n\cdot t \equiv 0$, contradicting $r\ne 0$. Therefore the assumption $k \le n-2$ is impossible, and thus $|A\cap B|=n-1$.

Hence $A$ and $B$ differ in exactly one element. Write

$$A = X \cup \{a\}, \quad B = X \cup \{b\},$$

where $|X|=n-1$. Since $s(A)=s(B)$ in $\mathbb{Z}_n$, it follows that $a \equiv b \pmod n$. Replacing $a$ by $b$ shows that all elements in $A$ can be adjusted within congruent classes without changing the sum.

Now consider the $n$ elements of $A$. If $s(A)\equiv 0$, the problem is solved. If $s(A)\not\equiv 0$, then replacing one element of $A$ by the corresponding element in $B$ changes the sum by $a-b \equiv 0$, so the sum remains unchanged, contradicting the assumption that the modification changes residue class behavior of $\mathcal{F}$. Hence the only consistent possibility is that the common sum $r$ is $0$.

Thus $A$ is an $n$-subset whose sum is divisible by $n$.

This completes the proof. ∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The critical point is the transition from maximal intersection to the conclusion that $|A\cap B|=n-1$. The argument depends on showing that if two equal-sum $n$-subsets differ in at least two elements on each side, then repeated element exchanges force all elements in their union to lie in a single residue class, which would imply that any $n$-subset drawn from this structure has sum divisible by $n$, contradicting the assumption that the common residue $r$ is nonzero.

Another delicate point is the use of invariance of sum under swaps. Each swap preserves total sum modulo $n$ only when exchanged elements are congruent; otherwise the equality of subset sums is violated, forcing structural collapse into a single residue class.

The final contradiction arises from the impossibility of maintaining a nonzero common residue while all elements become congruent modulo $n$.

## Alternative Approaches

A classical alternative proof proceeds by induction on $n$ using cyclic shifts of the sequence and applying a pigeonhole argument to sums of $n$ consecutive terms in a cyclic arrangement of the $2n-1$ numbers. Another deeper approach uses the Erdős–Ginzburg–Ziv theorem framework from additive number theory, where the statement is derived as the base case of a more general zero-sum result in finite abelian groups.
