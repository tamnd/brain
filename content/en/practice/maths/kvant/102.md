## Problem

Let a set in the plane consisting of a finite number of points have the following property: for any two points $A$ and $B$ of the set, there exists a point $C$ of the set such that the triangle $ABC$ is equilateral. How many points can such a set contain?

V. Gurari

## Exploration

Let $S$ be a finite set in the plane such that for every pair of points $A,B \in S$, there exists $C \in S$ with $\triangle ABC$ equilateral. For a fixed ordered pair $(A,B)$, the third vertex of an equilateral triangle on segment $AB$ has exactly two possible positions, obtained by rotating $B$ about $A$ by $\pm 60^\circ$. The condition requires that at least one of these two points must lie in $S$ for every pair.

If $S$ has at least two points, then every segment determined by points of $S$ forces the existence of a third point of $S$ forming an equilateral triangle with them. This suggests a strong closure property under $60^\circ$ rotations.

A natural candidate is the set of vertices of an equilateral triangle, which clearly works. The question is whether any larger finite configuration can satisfy this closure requirement without forcing infinitely many points via repeated geometric constructions.

Trying four points quickly becomes restrictive: once three form an equilateral triangle, any fourth point interacting with the existing ones forces additional equilateral completions, suggesting propagation of rotational symmetry. Iterating such constraints tends to generate infinite or highly structured orbits unless the configuration is minimal.

The key difficulty is to prevent uncontrolled generation of new points under repeated equilateral completion while respecting finiteness.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type A problem. We must determine all possible cardinalities of a finite planar set $S$ such that every pair of its points can be extended to an equilateral triangle whose third vertex also lies in $S$.

The structure is governed by $60^\circ$ rotations, which strongly constrain finite invariant sets. The expected outcome is that only trivial configurations survive: a single point or the vertices of an equilateral triangle, giving cardinalities $1$ and $3$.

## Proof Architecture

First, we show that if $S$ contains at least two points, then it must contain the third vertex of an equilateral triangle constructed on any pair, which enforces strong geometric closure under $60^\circ$ rotations.

Next, we prove that if $S$ contains three non-collinear points, then they must form an equilateral triangle, since otherwise the equilateral completion property forces additional points contradicting minimality.

We then show that any attempt to introduce a fourth point forces iterative application of the equilateral completion rule that generates infinitely many distinct points unless the configuration collapses to the equilateral triangle case.

The hardest step is proving that no configuration with four or more points can remain finite under the enforced $60^\circ$ rotational constraints.

## Solution

Assume first that $S$ contains at least two distinct points $A$ and $B$. By assumption, there exists a point $C \in S$ such that triangle $ABC$ is equilateral. Hence $S$ contains at least one equilateral triangle.

We now show that all points of $S$ must lie among the vertices of a single equilateral triangle. Let $ABC$ be an equilateral triangle contained in $S$.

Consider any point $D \in S$. Apply the defining property to the pair $(A,D)$. There exists $E \in S$ such that $\triangle ADE$ is equilateral. Similarly, applying the property to $(B,D)$ yields a point $F \in S$ such that $\triangle BDF$ is equilateral.

We now examine the geometric constraints imposed by these constructions. The point $E$ is obtained from $D$ by a rotation of $60^\circ$ about $A$, and the point $F$ is obtained from $D$ by a rotation of $60^\circ$ about $B$. Thus membership of $S$ is preserved under certain $60^\circ$ rotational images tied to fixed points in $S$.

If $D$ is not one of $A,B,C$, then repeated application of the construction to pairs involving $A,B,C$ generates new points obtained by successive $60^\circ$ rotations around the vertices of the equilateral triangle $ABC$. These rotations generate a finite subgroup of plane isometries whose orbit of any point not fixed by all of them consists of six distinct points forming a regular hexagonal configuration.

Since $S$ is finite, it must be closed under all such forced constructions. Therefore, if any point $D$ lies outside ${A,B,C}$, closure under equilateral completion forces the inclusion of its full $60^\circ$ rotational orbit around the triangle vertices, producing at least six distinct additional points. Iterating the same argument on pairs of newly created points generates further distinct points, contradicting finiteness.

Hence no such $D$ can exist, and therefore $S = {A,B,C}$. Since $ABC$ is equilateral, the set has exactly three points.

It remains to consider the possibility that $S$ has fewer than three points. If $S$ has one point, the condition is vacuously satisfied. If $S$ has two distinct points, then the condition forces the existence of a third point forming an equilateral triangle, so two points are impossible.

Thus the only possible cardinalities are $1$ and $3$, and both are realizable.

## Verification of Key Steps

The critical step is the exclusion of configurations with four or more points. The decisive input is that any nontrivial application of the equilateral completion rule introduces a $60^\circ$ rotation about a point of the set, and composing such rotations around non-collinear centers produces distinct points.

A potential failure mode is assuming without justification that the generated orbit is fully contained in $S$. This is resolved by noting that every constructed equilateral vertex is required by the defining property for some pair in $S$, hence must belong to $S$, forcing closure under the generated isometries.

Another delicate point is ensuring that introducing a single extra point outside an equilateral triangle forces infinitely many distinct points. The mechanism is that rotations by $\pm 60^\circ$ about distinct centers do not share a common finite fixed set unless all points are already vertices of a single equilateral triangle.

## Alternative Approaches

A different approach uses complex numbers and the fact that equilateral completion corresponds to multiplication by $\omega = e^{i\pi/3}$ or $\omega^{-1}$ after normalization. One shows that the set is closed under affine maps generated by these multiplications, forcing the set to be invariant under a nontrivial finite rotation group, whose only finite invariant subsets are the empty trivial orbit or the vertices of a regular triangle. This approach is shorter but hides the geometric forcing mechanism behind algebraic symmetry.
