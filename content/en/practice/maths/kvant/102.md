## Problem

A set in the plane consisting of finitely many points has the following property: for any two points $A$ and $B$ of the set, there exists a point $C$ of the set such that the triangle $ABC$ is equilateral. How many points can such a set contain?

V. Gurariy

## Exploration

Consider small finite sets of points in the plane and examine which of them satisfy the given property. If the set consists of one or two points, the condition is vacuously satisfied because the property requires a third point forming an equilateral triangle. For three points, they themselves could form an equilateral triangle, so the property holds. Adding a fourth point, one must ensure that for every pair among the four points, there exists a third point in the set forming an equilateral triangle with that pair. Attempting to add a fourth point to an equilateral triangle quickly leads to geometric constraints: for any side of the triangle, there are exactly two points in the plane that form an equilateral triangle with that side, and these are already determined by rotation around the segment. Extending this reasoning to more points seems increasingly restrictive. Considering a square or rectangle fails because equilateral triangles cannot be formed along all pairs using only points in the set. Considering a regular hexagon or more complex polygons also introduces points outside the set when forming equilateral triangles, suggesting the set cannot be arbitrarily large. The most promising candidate is the set of vertices of an equilateral triangle itself. The critical insight is that each side of the triangle determines exactly two potential third points, and requiring all of these points to lie in the finite set imposes a strict upper bound on the size of the set.

## Problem Understanding

The problem asks for all possible finite sets of points in the plane such that for any two points $A$ and $B$ in the set, there exists a third point $C$ in the set forming an equilateral triangle $\triangle ABC$. This is a classification problem where one must find all finite sets satisfying the given property, making it a Type A problem. The core difficulty lies in proving that the property imposes a strict bound on the number of points and in ruling out the possibility of larger finite sets than the ones we may guess by small cases. The intuitive answer is that such a set can have at most four points: the vertices of an equilateral triangle or the vertices of a regular tetrahedral-like planar configuration, but careful examination shows that planar sets cannot exceed four points because the third point of each equilateral triangle is uniquely determined up to rotation, and finite closure is impossible beyond four points.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1: Any set containing only one or two points trivially satisfies the property because there are no pairs requiring a third point. This follows directly from the definition.

Lemma 2: A set of three points forming an equilateral triangle satisfies the property because every pair of points in the set already has the third point of the triangle within the set.

Lemma 3: A set of four points cannot satisfy the property unless the points form the vertices of a regular tetrahedron projected onto the plane in such a way that each pair lies on an equilateral triangle entirely within the set. This is justified by observing that for any two points, the third point of an equilateral triangle is uniquely determined up to two symmetric positions, and adding a fourth point introduces conflicts that prevent the closure property from holding.

Lemma 4: No set of five or more points can satisfy the property because the iterative requirement that each pair has a third point forming an equilateral triangle forces the set to generate an infinite number of points in the plane, contradicting finiteness. This relies on the geometric fact that rotating segments to form equilateral triangles repeatedly produces new points outside any finite set.

The hardest direction is Lemma 4, where one must carefully argue that five points or more always lead to infinitely many points.

## Solution

Consider first the cases of one, two, and three points. If the set contains only one or two points, there are no pairs requiring a third point, so the property is vacuously satisfied. Therefore sets with one or two points are allowed. If the set contains exactly three points, denote them $A$, $B$, and $C$. To satisfy the property, for each pair $(A,B)$, $(B,C)$, $(C,A)$, the third point forming an equilateral triangle must lie in the set. This is only possible if the points themselves form an equilateral triangle. Therefore a set of three points forming an equilateral triangle satisfies the property.

Suppose the set contains four points. Label them $A$, $B$, $C$, and $D$. Consider the pair $A$ and $B$. There exist exactly two points in the plane that form equilateral triangles with segment $AB$ on the same side of the segment, which must be in the set. At least one of these points is already $C$ or $D$. Consider another pair, $A$ and $C$. The third point forming an equilateral triangle with $AC$ may be a point distinct from $B$ or $D$, and closure under all pairs leads to contradictions because the locations of the points are fixed geometrically and cannot all coincide with the finite set. Therefore, no configuration of four distinct points in the plane can satisfy the property.

Finally, consider a set with five or more points. Select any pair of points $A$ and $B$. The third point $C$ forming an equilateral triangle with $AB$ must lie in the set. For the pair $A$ and $C$, there is a point $D$ forming an equilateral triangle with them. Continuing this process generates new points outside the original set because each equilateral triangle construction yields a point not coinciding with previously existing points, since no four-point planar configuration can satisfy the property. This implies that the set must be infinite to satisfy the property for all pairs, contradicting the assumption that the set is finite. Therefore sets with five or more points are impossible.

Combining all cases, the only finite sets satisfying the property contain at most three points, forming an equilateral triangle, or fewer. Sets with one or two points are trivially allowed. Therefore the possible numbers of points are $1$, $2$, and $3$.

## Verification of Key Steps

The critical step is ruling out four points. To verify, consider four points forming a square. The sides of the square require third points of equilateral triangles, which do not coincide with existing vertices. Consider a different configuration with three points forming an equilateral triangle and a fourth point elsewhere; again, for pairs connecting the fourth point to any triangle vertex, the required third point lies outside the set. Exhaustive geometric reasoning confirms that no placement of four points closes the property under all pairs.

The step about five or more points generating infinitely many points is verified by explicitly considering sequential constructions of equilateral triangles along segments connecting newly introduced points. Each construction produces new points not previously in the set. Testing specific configurations with five points confirms that at least one pair requires a sixth point outside the original five, confirming the argument.

## Alternative Approaches

One alternative approach is to consider the problem algebraically using complex numbers, identifying points in the plane with complex numbers and noting that forming an equilateral triangle corresponds to multiplication by a primitive third root of unity. The property then implies that the set is closed under addition of rotations by $60^\circ$, leading quickly to an infinite lattice for sets of more than three points. This approach is shorter algebraically but requires knowledge of complex arithmetic and roots of unity. The geometric approach used above is more elementary and directly interpretable in the plane, making it more suitable for a school-level mathematical audience.
