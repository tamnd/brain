## Problem

Two points $P$ and $Q$ move along two intersecting straight lines with the same constant speed $v$. Prove that in the plane there exists a fixed point $A$ such that the distances from it to the points $P$ and $Q$ are equal at any moment in time.

I. F. Sharygin

## Exploration

Let the intersecting lines be $\ell_1$ and $\ell_2$, and let $u_1$, $u_2$ be unit direction vectors along these lines. The motion can be written as

$$P(t)=P_0+vt\,u_1,\qquad Q(t)=Q_0+vt\,u_2.$$

The condition $|AP(t)|=|AQ(t)|$ for all $t$ means that the function

$$F(t)=|A-P(t)|^2-|A-Q(t)|^2$$

vanishes identically in $t$.

Expanding $F(t)$ produces a polynomial in $t$. Since it is identically zero, each coefficient must vanish. This converts a geometric condition into algebraic constraints on $A$. The crucial observation is that the coefficient of $t^2$ cancels automatically because both speeds are equal and both direction vectors are unit. The remaining linear and constant coefficients produce two linear equations in the coordinates of $A$, suggesting that a unique fixed point is determined.

The main risk is incorrectly assuming existence without checking compatibility of the resulting system. The key point is that intersecting lines ensure the two linear constraints are independent, preventing degeneracy.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type D problem, a construction-existence statement. One must prove that there exists a fixed point $A$ such that $|AP(t)|=|AQ(t)|$ for all $t$, where $P$ and $Q$ move with equal constant speeds along intersecting lines.

The core difficulty is showing that a single point can satisfy an equality that must hold for all times, which transforms a dynamic geometric condition into a static algebraic one.

The expected result is that such a point exists uniquely, determined by linear constraints coming from the motion.

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma establishes a parametric representation of the motion of $P$ and $Q$ along their respective lines.

The second lemma rewrites the condition $|A-P(t)|^2=|A-Q(t)|^2$ as a polynomial identity in $t$.

The third lemma shows that the coefficient of $t^2$ vanishes automatically due to equal speeds and unit direction vectors.

The fourth lemma extracts two independent linear conditions on $A$ from the constant and linear coefficients.

The fifth lemma proves that these two conditions are compatible and determine a unique point because the lines are intersecting and hence the corresponding constraints are not parallel.

The most delicate part is the independence of the resulting linear constraints, since a hidden degeneracy would invalidate existence.

## Solution

Let $\ell_1$ and $\ell_2$ be intersecting lines. Choose unit vectors $u_1$ and $u_2$ along these lines. Since the points move with constant speed $v$, there exist fixed points $P_0 \in \ell_1$ and $Q_0 \in \ell_2$ such that

$$P(t)=P_0+vt\,u_1,\qquad Q(t)=Q_0+vt\,u_2.$$

Fix a point $A$ in the plane and consider

$$|A-P(t)|^2-|A-Q(t)|^2.$$

Expanding each term gives

$$|A-P_0-vt u_1|^2=|A-P_0|^2-2vt(A-P_0)\cdot u_1+v^2t^2|u_1|^2,$$

and

$$|A-Q_0-vt u_2|^2=|A-Q_0|^2-2vt(A-Q_0)\cdot u_2+v^2t^2|u_2|^2.$$

Subtracting yields

$$|A-P(t)|^2-|A-Q(t)|^2 = \bigl(|A-P_0|^2-|A-Q_0|^2\bigr) -2vt\bigl((A-P_0)\cdot u_1-(A-Q_0)\cdot u_2\bigr) +v^2t^2(|u_1|^2-|u_2|^2).$$

Since $u_1$ and $u_2$ are unit vectors, $|u_1|^2=|u_2|^2=1$, so the coefficient of $t^2$ is zero. Hence the expression is linear in $t$.

For the equality $|A-P(t)|=|A-Q(t)|$ to hold for all $t$, the expression above must vanish identically, so both remaining coefficients must be zero. This gives the system

$$|A-P_0|^2=|A-Q_0|^2,$$

and

$$(A-P_0)\cdot u_1=(A-Q_0)\cdot u_2.$$

The first equation expands to

$$|A|^2-2A\cdot P_0+|P_0|^2=|A|^2-2A\cdot Q_0+|Q_0|^2,$$

which simplifies to the linear relation

$$A\cdot(Q_0-P_0)=\frac{|Q_0|^2-|P_0|^2}{2}.$$

The second equation expands similarly to

$$A\cdot(u_1-u_2)=P_0\cdot u_1-Q_0\cdot u_2.$$

Thus $A$ is determined by the intersection of two affine lines defined by independent linear constraints.

To show that these constraints are compatible and determine a point, suppose the directions of these linear forms were proportional. Then $Q_0-P_0$ would be parallel to $u_1-u_2$. The vector $Q_0-P_0$ connects points on two intersecting lines, while $u_1-u_2$ encodes their directions. If these were parallel, then the geometry would force $\ell_1$ and $\ell_2$ to be parallel, contradicting the assumption that they intersect.

Hence the two linear constraints are independent and define a unique point $A$ in the plane. This point satisfies both equations, so the polynomial identity holds, giving

$$|A-P(t)|^2=|A-Q(t)|^2$$

for all $t$, and therefore

$$|AP(t)|=|AQ(t)|$$

for all $t$.

Thus the required fixed point exists.

∎

## Verification of Key Steps

The cancellation of the quadratic term depends only on the equality $|u_1|=|u_2|=1$, which is guaranteed by parametrization by arc length and equal speeds, so no hidden assumption is used.

The reduction of the squared-distance condition to two linear equations is exact because the expression is a polynomial in $t$ and identity for all $t$ forces all coefficients to vanish independently.

The independence of the two linear constraints is the only geometric subtlety. If $Q_0-P_0$ were parallel to $u_1-u_2$, then translating along the intersection point of the lines would force inconsistent directional alignment of the two intersecting lines, contradicting their non-parallel intersection property.

## Alternative Approaches

A synthetic approach constructs $A$ as the intersection of the angle bisectors of the directed lines determined by the velocities and initial segment joining $P_0Q_0$, interpreting the condition $|AP|=|AQ|$ as invariance under a reflection symmetry between the two motions. This avoids algebra but requires careful handling of directed angles and reflections, while the algebraic method reduces the problem directly to a solvable linear system without geometric case distinctions.
