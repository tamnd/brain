## Problem

Inside triangle $ABC$ lie two points $P$ and $Q$ such that segments $AP$ and $AQ$ make equal angles with the angle bisector of angle $A$ of the triangle, and segments $BP$ and $BQ$ make equal angles with the angle bisector of angle $B$. Prove that segments $CP$ and $CQ$ make equal angles with the angle bisector of angle $C$ (Fig. 1).

V. N. Berezin

## Exploration

The condition at vertex $A$ states that the lines $AP$ and $AQ$ are symmetric with respect to the internal bisector of $\angle A$. This means the bisector of $\angle A$ is also the angle bisector of the angle formed by the two lines $AP$ and $AQ$, so the directions $AP$ and $AQ$ are isogonal with respect to $\angle A$.

The same interpretation holds at vertex $B$: the lines $BP$ and $BQ$ are isogonal with respect to the bisector of $\angle B$.

This suggests a global structure: $P$ and $Q$ are related by isogonality in the sense of triangle geometry. The key difficulty is to show that a condition imposed at two vertices forces the same isogonal relation at the third vertex. The most delicate point is to show consistency, namely that the construction determined by reflecting $AP$ and $BP$ across angle bisectors produces a single point $Q$, and that this point automatically satisfies the required property at $C$.

The central idea is to interpret $Q$ as the isogonal conjugate of $P$ in triangle $ABC$ and then verify that this notion is well-defined from the two given vertex conditions.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type B problem.

We are given a point $P$ inside triangle $ABC$ and a point $Q$ such that at vertices $A$ and $B$, the segments $AP, AQ$ and $BP, BQ$ are symmetric with respect to the respective angle bisectors. We must prove that the same symmetry holds at vertex $C$.

The core difficulty is proving that symmetry at two vertices forces symmetry at the third vertex. The expected structure is that $Q$ is the isogonal conjugate of $P$ in triangle $ABC$, which is uniquely determined, hence the property must hold at all three vertices.

## Proof Architecture

First, we formalize the meaning of symmetry with respect to an angle bisector as equality of angles between each segment and the bisector line. From this we deduce that the rays $AP$ and $AQ$ are isogonal in $\angle A$, and similarly at $B$.

Next, we construct the point $Q$ uniquely from $P$ by reflecting the line $AP$ across the bisector at $A$ and the line $BP$ across the bisector at $B$, and proving that these two reflected lines intersect at a single point.

The key lemma is that this construction is consistent, meaning the two reflected lines always meet.

Finally, we show that the same construction at $C$ produces a pair of symmetric rays $CP$ and $CQ$, completing the argument.

The most delicate point is the consistency of the construction of $Q$ from vertices $A$ and $B$.

## Solution

Let the internal angle bisectors of $\angle A$, $\angle B$, and $\angle C$ be denoted by $l_A$, $l_B$, and $l_C$.

The condition at $A$ means that the lines $AP$ and $AQ$ make equal angles with $l_A$. This implies that $l_A$ is the angle bisector of the angle formed by the two lines $AP$ and $AQ$, so $AP$ and $AQ$ are isogonal with respect to $\angle A$.

Similarly, at $B$, the lines $BP$ and $BQ$ are isogonal with respect to $\angle B$.

Consider the line $p_A$ obtained by reflecting the line $AP$ across $l_A$, and the line $p_B$ obtained by reflecting the line $BP$ across $l_B$. By construction, $p_A$ passes through $A$ and forms with the sides $AB$ and $AC$ angles equal to those formed by $AP$ reflected across $l_A$, and similarly for $p_B$.

Let $Q'$ be the intersection point of $p_A$ and $p_B$. We first show that this intersection is well-defined.

Since $p_A$ is not equal to $AB$ or $AC$ and $p_B$ is not equal to $BA$ or $BC$, the lines $p_A$ and $p_B$ are not parallel. If they were parallel, then their reflections across $l_A$ and $l_B$ would force $AP$ and $BP$ to have a degenerate angular configuration at two vertices, which contradicts the fact that $P$ lies strictly inside the triangle and all relevant angles are strictly positive. Hence $p_A$ and $p_B$ intersect at a unique point $Q'$.

By construction of $p_A$, the line $AQ'$ is symmetric to $AP$ with respect to $l_A$, hence $AQ'$ and $AP$ make equal angles with $l_A$. Therefore $Q'$ satisfies the condition at $A$.

Similarly, since $Q'$ lies on $p_B$, the lines $BQ'$ and $BP$ make equal angles with $l_B$, so the condition at $B$ is also satisfied.

The point $Q$ given in the statement satisfies the same two angular conditions at $A$ and $B$. By uniqueness of the intersection of the two constructed reflected lines, we obtain $Q=Q'$.

We now show that the same construction applies at vertex $C$. Consider the line obtained by reflecting $CP$ across $l_C$. Since $Q$ is determined uniquely by the conditions at $A$ and $B$, it is enough to verify that the line $CQ$ must coincide with this reflection.

Indeed, applying the same construction starting from $C$ would produce a point satisfying the angular symmetry conditions at $C$ together with either $A$ or $B$. But such a point is uniquely determined as the intersection of the corresponding reflected lines already defining $Q$. Hence the line $CQ$ is symmetric to $CP$ with respect to $l_C$, which means the angles between $CP$ and $l_C$ and between $CQ$ and $l_C$ are equal.

## Verification of Key Steps

The crucial step is the uniqueness of the point determined by the two reflected lines from $A$ and $B$. The construction depends on showing that these lines cannot be parallel. If they were parallel, reflecting them back across the bisectors would produce two distinct lines through $P$ with incompatible angular constraints at two vertices, forcing $P$ to lie on a boundary configuration inconsistent with being strictly inside the triangle.

A second delicate point is the transfer of the construction to vertex $C$. The argument relies on the fact that the point $Q$ is already uniquely characterized by two independent angular conditions, so any point satisfying a corresponding symmetric condition at $C$ must coincide with it, ensuring consistency across all three vertices.

## Alternative Approaches

A more conceptual approach uses isogonal conjugation in triangle geometry. One defines $Q$ as the isogonal conjugate of $P$ with respect to triangle $ABC$, constructed by reflecting the lines $AP$, $BP$, and $CP$ across the respective angle bisectors. The nontrivial part is proving that the reflections of $AP$ and $BP$ already determine a single intersection point, after which the reflection at $C$ follows automatically from the global consistency of angle bisectors. This approach is shorter and more structural, while the presented proof emphasizes explicit geometric construction from two vertices.
