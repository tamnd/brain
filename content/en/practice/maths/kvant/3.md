## Problem

In Figure 1, the plane is tiled by squares of five colors. The centers of squares of the same color are located at the vertices of a square lattice. For what numbers of colors is a similar tiling of the plane possible?

In Figure 2, the plane is tiled by hexagons of seven colors in such a way that the centers of hexagons of the same color form the vertices of a lattice of congruent equilateral triangles. For what numbers of colors is a similar construction possible?

Note. In the first problem, the number of colors may be equal to one (all squares the same color) and to two (as on a chessboard). In the second problem, you will easily find solutions with one color and with three colors. However, send in partial solutions as well if they seem interesting to you. Consider, for example, whether a solution with thirteen colors exists in the second problem.

## Exploration

The centers of the unit squares form the integer lattice $\mathbb Z^2$. If squares of the same color have centers forming a square lattice, then for each color the set of centers of that color is a sublattice of $\mathbb Z^2$ generated by two perpendicular vectors of equal length.

Since every lattice point receives exactly one color, the color classes partition $\mathbb Z^2$. Let $L$ be the lattice corresponding to one color. Translating $L$ by suitable lattice vectors gives all other color classes. Hence the number of colors equals the index $[\mathbb Z^2:L]$.

A square sublattice of $\mathbb Z^2$ is generated by $(a,b)$ and $(-b,a)$. Its index is

$$\left|\det \begin{pmatrix} a&-b\\ b&a \end{pmatrix} \right| =a^2+b^2.$$

Thus every construction with squares yields a number of colors that is a sum of two squares.

Conversely, if $n=a^2+b^2$, the lattice generated by $(a,b)$ and $(-b,a)$ has index $n$, and its $n$ cosets color the lattice points. Each color class is again a square lattice. Hence the possible numbers of colors in the square problem are exactly the sums of two squares.

For the hexagonal tiling, the centers form the triangular lattice

$$T=\{m+n\omega:m,n\in\mathbb Z\}, \qquad \omega=e^{i\pi/3}.$$

A triangular sublattice similar to $T$ is obtained by multiplication by an Eisenstein integer $\alpha=a+b\omega$. The index equals the norm

$$N(\alpha)=\alpha\bar\alpha=a^2+ab+b^2.$$

Again, a coloring corresponds to a partition of $T$ into cosets of such a sublattice, so the number of colors is the index. Therefore the admissible numbers are precisely the integers of the form $a^2+ab+b^2$.

The delicate point is proving that every coloring must arise from cosets of a single sublattice. Since every color class is a lattice congruent to the others and together they partition the ambient lattice, one color class is a finite-index sublattice and the others are necessarily its cosets.

## Problem Understanding

We are given two regular tilings.

For the square tiling, the centers of all squares form the lattice $\mathbb Z^2$. A coloring is required such that the centers of squares of any fixed color form a square lattice. We must determine all possible numbers of colors.

For the hexagonal tiling, the centers form a triangular lattice. The centers of hexagons of each color must form a lattice of congruent equilateral triangles. We must determine all possible numbers of colors.

This is a Type A problem. We must classify all admissible numbers of colors and prove both existence and necessity.

The core difficulty is translating the geometric condition into a statement about finite-index sublattices of the square lattice and the triangular lattice.

The answers are:

For squares, exactly the integers representable as

$$a^2+b^2.$$

For hexagons, exactly the integers representable as

$$a^2+ab+b^2.$$

The reason is that these expressions are precisely the indices of similar sublattices in the square and triangular lattices.

## Proof Architecture

Lemma 1. In either problem, the centers of one color form a finite-index sublattice of the ambient lattice, and every other color class is a coset of this sublattice. The color classes partition the lattice points.

Lemma 2. A square sublattice of $\mathbb Z^2$ is generated by vectors $(a,b)$ and $(-b,a)$, and its index equals $a^2+b^2$. The generators are perpendicular and have equal length.

Lemma 3. For every pair of integers $a,b$, the lattice generated by $(a,b)$ and $(-b,a)$ is a square sublattice of index $a^2+b^2$. Its cosets yield a valid coloring with $a^2+b^2$ colors.

Lemma 4. A triangular sublattice similar to the standard triangular lattice is obtained by multiplying by an Eisenstein integer $a+b\omega$, and its index equals $a^2+ab+b^2$. This follows from the norm formula.

Lemma 5. For every pair of integers $a,b$, the corresponding triangular sublattice has index $a^2+ab+b^2$, and its cosets yield a valid coloring.

The hardest direction is necessity, namely proving that every admissible coloring corresponds to a finite-index similar sublattice and hence that the number of colors equals the index of that sublattice.

## Solution

Consider first the square tiling.

The centers of all squares form the lattice $\mathbb Z^2$. Let $L$ be the set of centers of squares of one fixed color.

By hypothesis, $L$ is itself a square lattice. Since only finitely many colors are used and every point of $\mathbb Z^2$ has a color, $L$ has finite index in $\mathbb Z^2$. Every other color class is a translate of $L$, because all color classes are congruent lattices and together partition $\mathbb Z^2$. Hence the number of colors equals

$$[\mathbb Z^2:L].$$

Let $u=(a,b)$ be a primitive vector of $L$ of minimal positive length. Since $L$ is a square lattice, a basis of $L$ consists of $u$ and a vector obtained from $u$ by a quarter-turn. Thus a basis may be written as

$$(a,b),\qquad (-b,a).$$

Therefore

$$[\mathbb Z^2:L] = \left| \det \begin{pmatrix} a&-b\\ b&a \end{pmatrix} \right| = a^2+b^2.$$

Hence every admissible number of colors is a sum of two squares.

Conversely, suppose

$$n=a^2+b^2.$$

Let $L$ be the lattice generated by

$$(a,b),\qquad (-b,a).$$

These generators are perpendicular and have the same length, so $L$ is a square lattice. Its index in $\mathbb Z^2$ equals

$$a^2+b^2=n.$$

Color all lattice points belonging to the same coset of $L$ with the same color. Since there are exactly $n$ cosets, this gives a coloring with $n$ colors, and the points of each color form a square lattice.

Thus the possible numbers of colors in the square problem are exactly the integers of the form

$$a^2+b^2.$$

Now consider the hexagonal tiling.

The centers of the hexagons form the triangular lattice

$$T=\{m+n\omega:m,n\in\mathbb Z\}, \qquad \omega=e^{i\pi/3}.$$

Let $L$ be the set of centers of one color. By the same argument as above, $L$ is a finite-index sublattice of $T$, and the number of colors equals the index

$$[T:L].$$

Since $L$ is similar to the triangular lattice, there exists an Eisenstein integer

$$\alpha=a+b\omega$$

such that

$$L=\alpha T.$$

The index of $\alpha T$ in $T$ equals the norm of $\alpha$:

$$N(\alpha) = \alpha\bar\alpha = (a+b\omega)(a+b\bar\omega).$$

Using

$$\omega+\bar\omega=1, \qquad \omega\bar\omega=1,$$

we obtain

$$N(\alpha) = a^2+ab+b^2.$$

Hence every admissible number of colors has the form

$$a^2+ab+b^2.$$

Conversely, for any integers $a,b$, let

$$L=(a+b\omega)T.$$

Its index in $T$ is

$$a^2+ab+b^2.$$

Color points of $T$ according to the coset of $L$ to which they belong. The number of colors is then

$$a^2+ab+b^2,$$

and each color class is a triangular lattice congruent to $L$.

Therefore the possible numbers of colors in the hexagonal problem are exactly the integers of the form

$$a^2+ab+b^2.$$

The complete answers are

$$\boxed{\text{Square tiling: }n=a^2+b^2}$$

and

$$\boxed{\text{Hexagonal tiling: }n=a^2+ab+b^2}$$

for integers $a,b$.

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate point is the index computation for the square lattice. Taking generators $(a,b)$ and $(-b,a)$, the determinant is

$$a\cdot a-(-b)b=a^2+b^2.$$

Since the index of a sublattice generated by two integer vectors equals the absolute value of the determinant, the number of cosets is exactly $a^2+b^2$. Any sign error in the determinant would produce the incorrect expression $a^2-b^2$.

The second delicate point is the norm computation in the triangular lattice. Writing

$$\alpha=a+b\omega,$$

one obtains

$$\alpha\bar\alpha = a^2+ab(\omega+\bar\omega)+b^2\omega\bar\omega.$$

The identities $\omega+\bar\omega=1$ and $\omega\bar\omega=1$ give

$$\alpha\bar\alpha=a^2+ab+b^2.$$

Replacing $\omega$ by a primitive cube root of unity would instead yield $a^2-ab+b^2$, so the chosen model of the triangular lattice must be used consistently.

The third delicate point is the passage from a sublattice to a coloring. If a sublattice has index $n$, then the ambient lattice splits into exactly $n$ cosets. Distinct cosets are disjoint and their union is the whole lattice. Coloring each coset with one color produces exactly $n$ colors. No further compatibility condition is required.

## Alternative Approaches

For the square lattice one may work entirely in the ring of Gaussian integers $\mathbb Z[i]$. A square sublattice is an ideal $(a+bi)$, and the index equals the Gaussian norm

$$(a+bi)(a-bi)=a^2+b^2.$$

The classification of colorings becomes the classification of finite-index ideals of $\mathbb Z[i]$.

Similarly, the hexagonal problem may be formulated in the ring of Eisenstein integers $\mathbb Z[\omega]$. Triangular sublattices correspond to principal ideals generated by $a+b\omega$, and the number of colors equals the Eisenstein norm $a^2+ab+b^2$. This algebraic formulation is shorter, but the lattice argument given above keeps the geometric content visible and requires less background.
