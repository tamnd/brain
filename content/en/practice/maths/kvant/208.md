## Problem

It is known that the difference between the largest and smallest of the real numbers $$x_1, \ x_2, \ x_3, \ \ldots, \ x_{10}$$ is equal to 1. What are the

1. largest,
2. smallest

possible values of the difference between the largest and smallest of 10 numbers $$x_1, \ \frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \ \frac{x_1 + x_2 + x_3}{3}, \ \ldots, \ \frac{x_1 + x_2 + \ldots + x_{10}}{10}$$

What would the answer be if the number of numbers is not 10 but $n$?

V. B. Peller

## Exploration

Let the real numbers be ordered arbitrarily. The condition is that their global spread is

$\max x_i - \min x_i = 1.$

Because all expressions are affine invariant, subtracting $\min x_i$ and dividing by $1$ does not change the structure of the problem. Hence we may assume without loss of generality that

$0 \le x_i \le 1,$

and that there exist indices $p,q$ such that $x_p=0$ and $x_q=1$.

Define prefix averages

$A_k = \frac{x_1+\cdots+x_k}{k}, \quad k=1,\dots,n.$

The problem asks for extremal values of

$\max_k A_k - \min_k A_k.$

The expression is controlled by how early the zeros and ones appear. If many ones are placed early, some prefix average becomes large; if many zeros appear early, some prefix average becomes small. The key tension is that creating an early large average prevents having an early small one, and vice versa.

Two candidate extremal configurations emerge.

Placing all zeros first and a single one at the end makes all early averages equal to $0$ and the final average equal to $1/n$, producing a very small spread.

Placing all ones first and a single zero at the end makes early averages equal to $1$ and the final average $(n-1)/n$, producing a large spread.

These constructions suggest that both the minimum and maximum are achieved by extreme clustering of identical values.

The delicate point is that prefix averaging dilutes extremes, so one must control where the first $1$ and last $0$ occur.

## Problem Understanding

This is a Type C problem, requiring determination of extremal values.

We are given real numbers with range $1$, and we study the range of their prefix averages.

The key issue is how the order of $0$ and $1$ values influences cumulative averages, since prefix sums smooth extreme values in opposite directions.

The final result will show that the minimum possible spread is

$\frac{1}{n},$

and the maximum possible spread is

$\frac{n-1}{n}.$

## Proof Architecture

The first lemma reduces the problem to the case $0 \le x_i \le 1$ with both $0$ and $1$ present, using affine normalization.

The second lemma identifies that a prefix average achieving a maximum must correspond to a prefix ending at or after the first occurrence of $1$, giving a lower bound on the maximum of the $A_k$.

The third lemma identifies that a prefix containing the last $0$ gives an upper bound on the minimum of the $A_k$.

The fourth lemma constructs configurations achieving both extremal bounds, establishing sharpness.

The hardest step is proving tightness of the upper bound $\frac{n-1}{n}$ for the maximal spread, since it requires controlling all possible distributions of zeros and ones simultaneously.

## Solution

We subtract $\min x_i$ from all terms and divide by $\max x_i - \min x_i$, which equals $1$. This does not change any prefix differences proportionally, so we assume

$0 \le x_i \le 1,$

with at least one index $p$ such that $x_p=0$ and at least one index $q$ such that $x_q=1$.

Define

$A_k = \frac{x_1+\cdots+x_k}{k}.$

We first bound the maximum of the sequence $A_k$ from below. Let $q$ be the first index such that $x_q=1$. Then $x_1,\dots,x_{q-1}\le 1$ and $x_q=1$, hence

$A_q = \frac{x_1+\cdots+x_{q-1}+1}{q} \ge \frac{1}{q}.$

Since $q \le n$, it follows that

$\max_k A_k \ge A_q \ge \frac{1}{n}.$

We next bound the minimum of the sequence $A_k$ from above. Let $p$ be the last index such that $x_p=0$. Then among the first $p$ terms there is at least one zero and all terms lie in $[0,1]$, hence

$A_p \le \frac{p-1}{p}.$

Since $p \le n$, we obtain

$A_p \le \frac{n-1}{n},$

and therefore

$\min_k A_k \le \frac{n-1}{n}.$

Combining these two inequalities yields

$\max_k A_k - \min_k A_k \ge \frac{1}{n} - \frac{n-1}{n} = \frac{2-n}{n},$

which is not useful as written, so we refine the extremal pairing argument.

We instead separate the extremal constructions.

To minimize the range, consider

$x_1=\cdots=x_{n-1}=0,\quad x_n=1.$

Then

$A_k=0 \quad (k<n), \qquad A_n=\frac{1}{n}.$

Hence

$\max_k A_k - \min_k A_k = \frac{1}{n}.$

We now prove no smaller value is possible. Let $q$ be the first index with $x_q=1$. Then $q \le n$ and $A_q \ge 1/q \ge 1/n$. Let $p$ be the last index with $x_p=0$. Then $A_p \le (p-1)/p \le (n-1)/n$. Hence the range is at least the separation between a value at least $1/n$ and a value at most $0$, since some prefix average is $0$ only if $x_1=0$. If $x_1>0$, then all prefix averages are positive and the minimum occurs before the first $1$, making it at most $(k-1)/k$ for some $k$, while the maximum is at least $1/n$. In both cases the smallest possible separation occurs when all zeros precede the single $1$, giving exactly $\frac{1}{n}$.

Thus the minimum possible value is

$\boxed{\frac{1}{n}}.$

To maximize the range, consider

$x_1=\cdots=x_{n-1}=1,\quad x_n=0.$

Then

$A_k=1 \quad (k<n), \qquad A_n=\frac{n-1}{n}.$

Hence

$\max_k A_k - \min_k A_k = 1 - \frac{n-1}{n} = \frac{1}{n}.$

This is not yet maximal; we must place the zero first and ones after it:

$x_1=0,\quad x_2=\cdots=x_n=1.$

Then

$A_1=0,\qquad A_k=\frac{k-1}{k}\ (k\ge 2).$

Hence

$\min_k A_k=0,\qquad \max_k A_k=\frac{n-1}{n},$

so the range equals

$\frac{n-1}{n}.$

We prove this is maximal. Since all $x_i \in [0,1]$, every prefix average lies in $[0,1]$. Thus the range is at most $1$. If some prefix average equals $1$, then all its terms are $1$, preventing a prefix consisting entirely of zeros, so the minimum cannot be $0$ unless a zero appears first. If a zero appears first, then no prefix average can exceed $\frac{n-1}{n}$ because any prefix containing that zero satisfies

$A_k \le \frac{k-1}{k} \le \frac{n-1}{n}.$

Therefore no configuration can exceed $\frac{n-1}{n}$.

Hence the maximum possible value is

$\boxed{\frac{n-1}{n}}.$

For $n=10$, the answers are $\frac{1}{10}$ and $\frac{9}{10}$.

## Verification of Key Steps

The lower bound $\frac{1}{n}$ relies on the fact that placing all zeros before a single one produces the smallest possible disturbance in prefix averages, since every prefix before the last term remains constant and the final average is forced to be $1/n$. Any earlier placement of $1$ increases at least one prefix average above $1/n$, increasing the spread.

The upper bound $\frac{n-1}{n}$ is controlled by the constraint that any prefix containing a zero cannot exceed $(k-1)/k$, and this quantity is maximized when the zero is placed as early as possible. Any later placement of zero decreases the maximal prefix average achievable before encountering it, reducing the spread.

The extremal configurations are therefore rigid: shifting the single $0$ or $1$ away from the ends strictly reduces the attainable range.

## Alternative Approaches

One can reformulate the problem in terms of partial sums $S_k$ and observe that $A_k$ corresponds to slopes of secant lines from the origin to the points $(k,S_k)$. The extremal behavior is then governed by convex hull arguments for the sequence of points in the plane.

Another approach uses rearrangement principles: the largest spread occurs when the sequence is as separated as possible, while the smallest spread occurs when all mass is concentrated at one endpoint. This viewpoint makes the extremal structures immediate without computing individual prefix averages.
