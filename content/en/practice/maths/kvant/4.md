## Problem

Given a segment $AB$. Find the locus of points $C$ in the plane such that, in triangle $ABC$, the median drawn from vertex $A$ is equal to the altitude drawn from vertex $B$.

Moscow Mathematical Olympiad (XXXII)

## Exploration

Let $M$ be the midpoint of $BC$. The condition says that the median from $A$ equals the altitude from $B$.

Denote by $h_B$ the altitude from $B$ to line $AC$. Then

$$h_B=\frac{2[ABC]}{AC}.$$

Since $AB$ is fixed, it is natural to place coordinates. Let

$$A=(0,0), \qquad B=(d,0),$$

where $d=AB>0$, and let

$$C=(x,y).$$

The median from $A$ goes to the midpoint

$$M=\left(\frac{x+d}{2},\frac y2\right),$$

hence

$$AM^2=\frac{(x+d)^2+y^2}{4}.$$

The altitude from $B$ has length

$$h_B=\frac{|dy|}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}},$$

because the area equals $\frac12 d|y|$.

The condition $AM=h_B$ gives

$$\frac{(x+d)^2+y^2}{4} = \frac{d^2y^2}{x^2+y^2}.$$

Multiplying by $4(x^2+y^2)$ yields

$$\bigl((x+d)^2+y^2\bigr)(x^2+y^2)=4d^2y^2.$$

Introducing polar coordinates with pole at $A$,

$$x=r\cos\theta,\qquad y=r\sin\theta,$$

gives

$$r^2(r^2+2dr\cos\theta+d^2)=4d^2r^2\sin^2\theta.$$

For $r\ne0$,

$$r^2+2dr\cos\theta+d^2 = 4d^2\sin^2\theta.$$

This is quadratic in $r$:

$$r^2+2d(\cos\theta)r+d^2(4\cos^2\theta-3)=0.$$

The discriminant is

$$4d^2\bigl(3\sin^2\theta\bigr),$$

so

$$r=d\bigl(-\cos\theta\pm\sqrt3\,|\sin\theta|\bigr).$$

This looks like a pair of circles passing through $A$. Converting back to Cartesian coordinates should reveal them.

The most delicate point is factorizing the quartic equation correctly and showing that no points are lost when dividing by $r^2$.

## Problem Understanding

A segment $AB$ is fixed. We seek all points $C$ in the plane such that in triangle $ABC$ the median drawn from $A$ has the same length as the altitude drawn from $B$.

This is a Type A problem. We must determine the complete locus and prove both that every point on the proposed locus satisfies the condition and that every point satisfying the condition belongs to the locus.

The core difficulty is transforming the metric condition into a geometric equation whose locus can be identified.

The answer will turn out to be the union of two circles passing through $A$ and $B$. The condition compares a median and an altitude, quantities naturally expressed through the midpoint of $BC$ and the area of the triangle, and the resulting algebra factors into two circle equations.

## Proof Architecture

First, place the coordinate system so that $A=(0,0)$ and $B=(d,0)$, where $d=AB$.

Second, compute the squared length of the median from $A$ and the squared length of the altitude from $B$.

Third, translate the equality of these lengths into a quartic equation in the coordinates of $C$.

Fourth, factor this equation into the product of two circle equations; this identifies the locus as the union of two circles.

Fifth, prove that every point on either circle satisfies the original condition by reversing the algebra.

The hardest direction is the factorization step, because an incorrect factorization would produce an incorrect locus.

## Solution

Let

$$A=(0,0),\qquad B=(d,0),$$

where $d=AB>0$, and let

$$C=(x,y).$$

Let $M$ be the midpoint of $BC$. Then

$$M=\left(\frac{x+d}{2},\frac y2\right),$$

so the median from $A$ has length

$$AM=\frac12\sqrt{(x+d)^2+y^2}.$$

The area of triangle $ABC$ equals

$$[ABC]=\frac12 d|y|.$$

Hence the altitude from $B$ to line $AC$ has length

$$h_B=\frac{2[ABC]}{AC} =\frac{d|y|}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}.$$

The condition of the problem is

$$AM=h_B.$$

Squaring and substituting the formulas above gives

$$\frac{(x+d)^2+y^2}{4} = \frac{d^2y^2}{x^2+y^2}.$$

Multiplying by $4(x^2+y^2)$, we obtain

$$\bigl((x+d)^2+y^2\bigr)(x^2+y^2)=4d^2y^2.$$

Expanding,

$$(x^2+y^2)^2+2dx(x^2+y^2)+d^2(x^2+y^2)-4d^2y^2=0.$$

Since

$$d^2(x^2+y^2)-4d^2y^2=d^2(x^2-3y^2),$$

the equation becomes

$$(x^2+y^2)^2+2dx(x^2+y^2)+d^2(x^2-3y^2)=0.$$

This factors as

$$\bigl(x^2+y^2+d(x+\sqrt3\,y)\bigr) \bigl(x^2+y^2+d(x-\sqrt3\,y)\bigr)=0.$$

Indeed, multiplying the two factors yields

$$(x^2+y^2+dx)^2-3d^2y^2,$$

which is exactly the preceding expression.

Therefore every admissible point $C$ satisfies at least one of the equations

$$x^2+y^2+d(x+\sqrt3\,y)=0,$$

or

$$x^2+y^2+d(x-\sqrt3\,y)=0.$$

Completing squares in the first equation,

$$\left(x+\frac d2\right)^2+ \left(y+\frac{\sqrt3\,d}{2}\right)^2=d^2.$$

This is a circle of radius $d$ with center

$$\left(-\frac d2,-\frac{\sqrt3\,d}{2}\right).$$

Similarly, the second equation becomes

$$\left(x+\frac d2\right)^2+ \left(y-\frac{\sqrt3\,d}{2}\right)^2=d^2,$$

a circle of radius $d$ with center

$$\left(-\frac d2,\frac{\sqrt3\,d}{2}\right).$$

Both circles pass through $A=(0,0)$ and $B=(d,0)$.

Conversely, if $C$ lies on either of these circles, then one of the two circle equations holds. Multiplying the two factors yields

$$\bigl((x+d)^2+y^2\bigr)(x^2+y^2)=4d^2y^2,$$

which is equivalent to

$$\frac{(x+d)^2+y^2}{4} = \frac{d^2y^2}{x^2+y^2}.$$

Hence

$$AM^2=h_B^2.$$

Since lengths are nonnegative, $AM=h_B$. Thus every point of either circle satisfies the required condition.

The locus is exactly the union of these two circles.

$$\boxed{\text{The locus of }C\text{ is the union of the two circles of radius }AB \text{ through }A\text{ and }B,\text{ whose centers form equilateral triangles with }AB.}$$

## Verification of Key Steps

The first delicate step is the formula for the altitude from $B$. Using

$$[ABC]=\frac12\cdot AC\cdot h_B$$

and also

$$[ABC]=\frac12 d|y|,$$

gives

$$h_B=\frac{d|y|}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}.$$

Any omission of the denominator $\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$ would change the degree of the locus equation and produce an incorrect curve.

The second delicate step is the factorization. Starting from

$$(x^2+y^2)^2+2dx(x^2+y^2)+d^2(x^2-3y^2),$$

set

$$U=x^2+y^2+dx.$$

Then the expression becomes

$$U^2-3d^2y^2 =(U+\sqrt3\,dy)(U-\sqrt3\,dy),$$

which yields exactly

$$\bigl(x^2+y^2+d(x+\sqrt3\,y)\bigr) \bigl(x^2+y^2+d(x-\sqrt3\,y)\bigr).$$

The third delicate point is the converse direction. No division by $x^2+y^2$ was used after the factorization. In particular, the point $C=A$ lies on both circles and satisfies the algebraic equation. If degenerate triangles are excluded, one simply removes the point $A$ from the locus; the nondegenerate part remains the same union of circles.

## Alternative Approaches

A synthetic approach starts from the polar form

$$r=d\bigl(-\cos\theta\pm\sqrt3\sin\theta\bigr).$$

Using

$$r=2R\cos(\theta-\varphi)$$

as the polar equation of a circle through the pole, one immediately identifies two circles of radius $d$ whose centers are at angular positions $\pm120^\circ$ from the positive $x$-axis. This yields the same locus with less algebra, but requires familiarity with polar equations of circles.

Another geometric route expresses the median condition through the midpoint $M$ of $BC$ and rewrites the altitude using the area of the triangle. After converting the condition into a relation among distances from $A$ and $B$, one obtains an equation equivalent to the two circle equations above. The coordinate method is preferable because every transformation is explicit and the factorization reveals the locus directly.
