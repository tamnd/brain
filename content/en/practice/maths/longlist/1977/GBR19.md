# IMO 1977 LL GBR19

**Origin:** GBR

## Problem

Given any integer m > 1 prove that there exist inﬁnitely
many positive integers n such that the last m digits of 5n are a sequence
am, am−1, . . . , a1 = 5 (0 \leqaj < 10) in which each digit except the last is
of opposite parity to its successor (i.e., if ai is even, then ai−1 is odd, and
if ai is odd, then ai−1 is even).

## Solution

We shall prove the statement by induction on m. For m = 2 it is trivial,
since each power of 5 greater than 5 ends in 25. Suppose that the statement
is true for some m \geq2, and that the last m digits of 5n alternate in parity.
It can be shown by induction that the maximum power of 2 that divides
52m−2 −1 is 2m, and consequently the diﬀerence 5n+2m−2 −5n is divisible
by 10m but not by 2\cdot10m. It follows that the last m digits of the numbers
5n+2m−2 and 5n coincide, but the digits at the position m+1 have opposite
parity. Hence the last m+1 digits of one of these two powers of 5 alternate
in parity. The inductive proof is completed.
