# IMO 1967 LL MON34

**Origin:** MON

## Problem

The faces of a convex polyhedron are six squares and eight
equilateral triangles, and each edge is a common side for one triangle and
one square. All dihedral angles obtained from the triangle and square
with a common edge are equal. Prove that it is possible to circumscribe
a sphere around this polyhedron and compute the ratio of the squares
of the volumes of the polyhedron and of the ball whose boundary is the
circumscribed sphere.

## Solution

Each vertex of the polyhedron is a vertex of exactly two squares and
triangles (more than two is not possible; otherwise, the sum of angles at
a vertex exceeds 360◦). By using the condition that the trihedral angles
are equal it is easy to see that such a polyhedron is uniquely determined
by its side length.

The polyhedron obtained from a
cube by “cutting” its vertices, as

conditions.
Now it is easy to calculate that
the ratio of the squares of vol-
umes of that polyhedron and of the
ball whose boundary is the circum-
scribed sphere is equal to 25/(8\pi2).
