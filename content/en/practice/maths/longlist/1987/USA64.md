# IMO 1987 LL USA64

**Origin:** USA

## Problem

Let r > 1 be a real number, and let n be the largest integer
smaller than r. Consider an arbitrary real number x with 0 \leqx \leq
n
r−1.
By a base-r expansion of x we mean a representation of x in the form
x = a1
r + a2
r2 + a3
r3 + \cdot \cdot \cdot ,
where the ai are integers with 0 \leqai < r.
You may assume without proof that every number x with 0 \leqx \leq
n
r−1
has at least one base-r expansion.
Prove that if r is not an integer, then there exists a number p, 0 \leqp \leq
n
r−1,
which has inﬁnitely many distinct base-r expansions.
