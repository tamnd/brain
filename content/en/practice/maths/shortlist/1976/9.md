# IMO 1976 SL 9

**Origin:** FIN

## Problem

Let P1(x) = x2 −2, Pj(x) = P1(Pj−1(x)), j = 2, 3, . . . .
Show that for arbitrary n the roots of the equation Pn(x) = x are real
and diﬀerent from one another.

## Solution

The equation Pn(x) = x is of degree 2n, and has at most 2n distinct
roots. If x > 2, then by simple induction Pn(x) > x for all n. Similarly,
if x < −1, then P1(x) > 2, which implies Pn(x) > 2 for all n. It follows
that all real roots of the equation Pn(x) = x lie in the interval [−2, 2], and
thus have the form x = 2 cost.
Now we observe that P1(2 cos t) = 4 cos2 t −2 = 2 cos 2t, and in general
Pn(2 cost) = 2 cos 2nt. Our equation becomes
cos 2nt = cos t,
which indeed has 2n diﬀerent solutions t = 2\pim
2n−1 (m = 0, 1, . . . , 2n−1 −1)
and t = 2\pim
2n+1 (m = 1, 2, . . ., 2n−1).
