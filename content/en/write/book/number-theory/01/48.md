## Powers Modulo a Prime

Modular exponentiation often reveals striking regularities when the modulus is prime.

For example, modulo $5$,

$$
2^1\equiv2,
$$

$$
2^2\equiv4,
$$

$$
2^3\equiv8\equiv3,
$$

$$
2^4\equiv16\equiv1.
$$

The fourth power returns to $1$. Similar patterns occur for many bases modulo primes.

Fermat's little theorem explains this phenomenon.

## Statement of the Theorem

Let $p$ be prime. If

$$
p\nmid a,
$$

then

$$
a^{p-1}\equiv1\pmod p.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
a^p\equiv a\pmod p
$$

for every integer $a$.

The second form includes the case where

$$
p\mid a,
$$

because then both sides are congruent to $0$ modulo $p$.

## First Examples

Take

$$
p=7,
\qquad
a=3.
$$

Then

$$
3^6=729.
$$

Reducing modulo $7$,

$$
729\equiv1\pmod7.
$$

Thus

$$
3^{7-1}\equiv1\pmod7.
$$

As another example,

$$
2^{10}=1024\equiv1\pmod{11}.
$$

So

$$
2^{11}\equiv2\pmod{11}.
$$

## Proof Using Residue Classes

Let

$$
1,2,\ldots,p-1
$$

be the nonzero residue classes modulo $p$.

Since $p\nmid a$, multiplication by $a$ permutes these residue classes modulo $p$.

Indeed, suppose

$$
ai\equiv aj\pmod p.
$$

Since

$$
\gcd(a,p)=1,
$$

we may cancel $a$ to obtain

$$
i\equiv j\pmod p.
$$

Thus the numbers

$$
a,2a,3a,\ldots,(p-1)a
$$

are congruent modulo $p$ to the numbers

$$
1,2,\ldots,p-1
$$

in some order.

Therefore

$$
a\cdot2a\cdot3a\cdots(p-1)a
\equiv
1\cdot2\cdot3\cdots(p-1)
\pmod p.
$$

The left side equals

$$
a^{p-1}(p-1)!.
$$

Hence

$$
a^{p-1}(p-1)!
\equiv
(p-1)!
\pmod p.
$$

Since none of the integers

$$
1,2,\ldots,p-1
$$

is divisible by $p$, we have

$$
\gcd((p-1)!,p)=1.
$$

Thus cancellation is valid modulo $p$, giving

$$
a^{p-1}\equiv1\pmod p.
$$

## Polynomial Form

The theorem can also be written as

$$
a^p-a\equiv0\pmod p.
$$

Thus every integer $a$ is a root modulo $p$ of the polynomial

$$
x^p-x.
$$

This observation becomes important in finite field theory.

Over the field

$$
\mathbb{F}_p,
$$

the polynomial

$$
x^p-x
$$

has all elements of the field as roots.

## Example of Fast Computation

Fermat's theorem greatly simplifies modular exponentiation.

Compute

$$
7^{100}\pmod{13}.
$$

Since $13$ is prime and

$$
\gcd(7,13)=1,
$$

we have

$$
7^{12}\equiv1\pmod{13}.
$$

Now

$$
100=12\cdot8+4.
$$

Therefore

$$
7^{100} =
(7^{12})^8 7^4
\equiv
1^8\cdot7^4
\pmod{13}.
$$

Since

$$
7^2=49\equiv10\pmod{13},
$$

we get

$$
7^4\equiv10^2=100\equiv9\pmod{13}.
$$

Thus

$$
7^{100}\equiv9\pmod{13}.
$$

The theorem reduces a huge exponent to a small one.

## Relation to Euler's Theorem

Fermat's little theorem is a special case of Euler's theorem.

Euler's theorem states that if

$$
\gcd(a,n)=1,
$$

then

$$
a^{\varphi(n)}\equiv1\pmod n.
$$

When $n=p$ is prime,

$$
\varphi(p)=p-1,
$$

so Euler's theorem becomes

$$
a^{p-1}\equiv1\pmod p.
$$

Thus Fermat's theorem is the prime-modulus case of a more general principle about invertible residue classes.

## Converse Failure

The converse of Fermat's little theorem is false.

If

$$
a^{n-1}\equiv1\pmod n,
$$

it does not necessarily follow that $n$ is prime.

For example,

$$
2^{340}\equiv1\pmod{341},
$$

but

$$
341=11\cdot31
$$

is composite.

Composite numbers satisfying Fermat-type congruences are called pseudoprimes.

This shows that Fermat's theorem gives a necessary condition for primality, but not a sufficient one.

## Fermat Primality Test

Despite the converse failure, Fermat's theorem provides a useful probabilistic primality test.

Given an integer $n>1$, choose a base $a$ with

$$
1<a<n.
$$

Compute

$$
a^{n-1}\pmod n.
$$

If the result is not $1$, then $n$ is definitely composite.

If the result is $1$, then $n$ may be prime or may be a pseudoprime.

Repeated testing with different bases often detects compositeness quickly.

This idea is foundational in computational number theory and cryptography.

## Role in Number Theory

Fermat's little theorem is one of the central results of modular arithmetic. It connects primes, powers, inverses, and finite multiplicative structures.

It is used in modular exponentiation, primality testing, cryptography, finite fields, and algebraic number theory.

The theorem reflects a deeper principle: modulo a prime, nonzero residue classes form a finite multiplicative group of order

$$
p-1.
$$

This group structure governs the behavior of powers modulo primes.
