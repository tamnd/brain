## 1. Prime Distribution

### Twin Prime Conjecture

Are there infinitely many primes $p$ such that

$$
p+2
$$

is also prime?

Examples:

$$
(3,5),\ (5,7),\ (11,13),\ (17,19).
$$

Modern sieve methods have shown that infinitely many primes differ by at most a fixed constant, but the exact gap $2$ remains unresolved.

## Goldbach Conjecture

Every even integer greater than $2$ can be expressed as the sum of two primes.

Examples:

$$
10=3+7,
$$

$$
100=47+53.
$$

This has been verified computationally for extremely large ranges, but no complete proof is known.

## Prime Gaps

How large or small can gaps between consecutive primes become?

The prime number theorem suggests average gap size near $x$ is approximately

$$
\log x.
$$

However, actual gaps fluctuate irregularly.

Major questions include:

- infinitely many bounded gaps,
- arbitrarily large normalized gaps,
- precise maximal growth rates.

## Distribution in Arithmetic Progressions

Dirichlet’s theorem proves infinitely many primes in each progression

$$
a,\ a+q,\ a+2q,\ldots
$$

with

$$
\gcd(a,q)=1.
$$

Open problems concern:

- error terms,
- distribution in short intervals,
- generalized Riemann hypothesis implications.

# 2. The Riemann Hypothesis

## Classical Statement

The Riemann Hypothesis asserts that every nontrivial zero of

$$
\zeta(s)
$$

has real part

$$
\frac12.
$$

$$
\zeta(s)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^s}
$$

This problem controls the fine structure of prime distribution.

## Consequences

If true, the Riemann Hypothesis would imply strong bounds for:

- prime counting errors,
- divisor sums,
- Möbius cancellation,
- arithmetic fluctuations.

Many known results are conditional on RH.

## Generalized Riemann Hypothesis

The generalized form concerns zeros of Dirichlet $L$-functions and automorphic $L$-functions.

It influences:

- class numbers,
- least quadratic nonresidues,
- cryptographic estimates,
- distribution of primes in progressions.

# 3. Diophantine Equations

## Rational Points on Curves

Given a polynomial equation

$$
f(x,y)=0,
$$

determine all rational solutions.

Some curves have:

- finitely many rational points,
- infinitely many rational points,
- no rational points.

Understanding this behavior is a central theme of arithmetic geometry.

## Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer Conjecture

This conjecture connects rational points on elliptic curves with analytic properties of an associated $L$-function.

The rank of the elliptic curve should equal the order of vanishing of the $L$-function at

$$
s=1.
$$

This is one of the Clay Millennium Problems.

## Effective Methods

Many theorems guarantee finiteness of solutions without giving explicit methods to find them.

Important challenges include:

- effective height bounds,
- explicit point computation,
- algorithmic rational point classification.

# 4. Algebraic Number Theory

## Class Number Problems

The class number measures failure of unique factorization.

Open questions include:

- behavior of class numbers in families,
- distribution of class groups,
- Cohen-Lenstra heuristics.

## Explicit Class Field Theory

Class field theory describes abelian extensions abstractly, but explicit generators remain difficult in many cases.

Finding concrete constructions is a major computational and theoretical problem.

## Nonabelian Extensions

Class field theory handles abelian Galois groups well. Nonabelian extensions remain much less understood.

The Langlands program may be viewed as a vast attempt to generalize reciprocity into the nonabelian setting.

---

# 5. Modular Forms and Automorphic Forms

## Fourier Coefficients

The coefficients of modular forms often encode arithmetic information.

Open questions concern:

- growth,
- sign changes,
- congruences,
- statistical behavior.

## Automorphic $L$-Functions

Understanding analytic continuation, functional equations, and zero distributions of general automorphic $L$-functions remains central.

## Functoriality

Langlands functoriality predicts deep correspondences among automorphic representations.

Many special cases are known, but the general theory remains incomplete.

---

# 6. Arithmetic Geometry

## Rational Points on Higher-Dimensional Varieties

Understanding rational points on surfaces and higher-dimensional varieties is vastly more difficult than for curves.

Key themes include:

- local-global principles,
- obstructions to rational points,
- arithmetic of K3 surfaces,
- arithmetic dynamics.

## Motives

Motives aim to unify cohomology theories into a universal arithmetic framework.

They remain partly conjectural but strongly influence modern arithmetic research.

## Étale Cohomology and Galois Actions

Understanding how Galois groups act on geometric invariants remains a major research direction.

This connects algebraic geometry, topology, and arithmetic.

# 7. Computational Number Theory

## Integer Factorization

No efficient classical algorithm for factoring arbitrary large integers is known.

The hardness of factorization supports RSA cryptography.

Quantum algorithms, particularly Shor’s algorithm, threaten this assumption.

## Discrete Logarithms

The discrete logarithm problem is central in finite-field and elliptic curve cryptography.

Its complexity varies dramatically across different groups.

## Post-Quantum Cryptography

New cryptographic systems aim to resist quantum attacks.

Major directions include:

- lattice cryptography,
- code-based cryptography,
- multivariate systems,
- isogeny methods.

---

# 8. Additive and Combinatorial Number Theory

## Sum-Product Phenomena

Finite sets often cannot simultaneously have small sumsets and product sets.

Understanding this balance is a major topic in arithmetic combinatorics.

## Arithmetic Progressions in Dense Sets

The Green-Tao theorem proved that primes contain arbitrarily long arithmetic progressions.

Open directions include:

- quantitative improvements,
- polynomial patterns,
- multidimensional structures.

## Randomness and Structure

Modern additive combinatorics studies decomposition into:

- structured components,
- pseudorandom components.

This philosophy influences analytic number theory, ergodic theory, and theoretical computer science.

---

# 9. Probabilistic Number Theory

## Random Matrix Models

Statistics of zeta zeros appear closely related to eigenvalues of random matrices.

Understanding this connection remains one of the deepest mysteries in analytic number theory.

## Distribution of Arithmetic Functions

Questions include:

- normal order,
- variance,
- limiting distributions,
- probabilistic models for primes.

## Möbius Randomness

The Möbius function appears to behave randomly in many settings.

The Sarnak conjecture predicts strong orthogonality between Möbius values and low-complexity dynamical systems.

---

# 10. The Langlands Program

## Reciprocity

The Langlands program seeks correspondences between:

- Galois representations,
- automorphic representations.

This generalizes classical reciprocity laws.

## Geometric Langlands

A geometric analogue replaces number fields with function fields and algebraic curves.

This connects number theory with:

- algebraic geometry,
- representation theory,
- mathematical physics.

## Global Vision

The Langlands program is not a single conjecture but a broad framework linking many branches of mathematics.

It currently shapes much of modern arithmetic research.

---

# 11. Experimental Mathematics

## Large Databases

Modern arithmetic increasingly relies on massive datasets:

- elliptic curves,
- modular forms,
- zeta zeros,
- integer sequences.

These datasets reveal patterns and guide conjectures.

## Computer-Assisted Proofs

Computers now participate directly in proofs through:

- exhaustive search,
- symbolic verification,
- formal proof systems.

## AI and Automated Reasoning

Emerging work studies whether machine learning and automated theorem proving can assist arithmetic research.

This area remains early but rapidly developing.

# 12. Philosophical Questions

## Randomness in the Primes

Prime numbers exhibit both structure and apparent randomness.

A central philosophical question is whether this randomness reflects hidden deterministic structure or genuine statistical behavior.

## Finite Versus Infinite Computation

Computers can verify huge ranges of cases, but finite verification does not replace proof.

Understanding the relationship between computation and proof remains philosophically important.

## Unity of Mathematics

Modern number theory increasingly connects:

- algebra,
- geometry,
- topology,
- analysis,
- computation,
- probability.

One of the deepest questions is whether these connections arise from a single underlying arithmetic structure not yet fully understood.
