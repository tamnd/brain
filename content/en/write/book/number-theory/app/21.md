## 1. Analytic Number Theory

Analytic number theory studies arithmetic using methods from analysis.

Central tools include:

- complex analysis,
- Fourier analysis,
- harmonic analysis,
- asymptotic methods,
- probability,
- spectral theory.

Major topics include:

| Topic | Main Questions |
|---|---|
| Prime distribution | how primes are spaced |
| Zeta functions | zeros and analytic continuation |
| Sieve methods | counting primes and almost primes |
| Exponential sums | cancellation in arithmetic sequences |
| Additive problems | sums of primes and powers |

A typical feature of analytic number theory is the conversion of arithmetic questions into statements about functions, integrals, or spectral data.

## 2. Algebraic Number Theory

Algebraic number theory generalizes arithmetic from integers to algebraic number fields.

Core structures include:

- algebraic integers,
- ideals,
- units,
- class groups,
- valuations,
- local fields.

Important themes include:

| Theme | Description |
|---|---|
| Factorization | how arithmetic decomposes |
| Ramification | behavior of primes in extensions |
| Reciprocity laws | hidden arithmetic symmetries |
| Class groups | failure of unique factorization |
| Local-global principles | reconstruction from local data |

Modern algebraic number theory strongly interacts with geometry and representation theory.

## 3. Arithmetic Geometry

Arithmetic geometry studies geometric objects defined by arithmetic equations.

Typical objects include:

- algebraic curves,
- elliptic curves,
- abelian varieties,
- schemes,
- arithmetic surfaces.

Key ideas:

| Concept | Role |
|---|---|
| Rational points | arithmetic solutions |
| Cohomology | hidden geometric structure |
| Moduli spaces | classification of objects |
| Galois actions | arithmetic symmetry |
| Motives | universal arithmetic structure |

Arithmetic geometry became central after the development of schemes and cohomological methods.

## 4. Modular Forms and Automorphic Forms

Modular forms combine symmetry, analysis, and arithmetic.

A modular form satisfies transformation laws under actions of matrix groups.

Important structures include:

- Hecke operators,
- Fourier coefficients,
- automorphic representations,
- adelic methods.

Applications include:

| Area | Connection |
|---|---|
| Elliptic curves | modularity |
| Prime distribution | $L$-functions |
| Representation theory | automorphic representations |
| Langlands program | reciprocity correspondences |

Modern number theory increasingly views arithmetic objects through automorphic structures.

## 5. The Langlands Program

The Langlands program seeks a vast correspondence between:

- Galois representations,
- automorphic representations.

It generalizes class field theory from abelian to nonabelian settings.

The program connects:

- harmonic analysis,
- representation theory,
- algebraic geometry,
- arithmetic.

The Langlands viewpoint has reshaped much of modern research.

## 6. Additive Combinatorics

Additive combinatorics studies arithmetic structure inside sets.

Typical questions include:

- arithmetic progressions,
- sumsets,
- pseudorandomness,
- additive structure.

Central tools include:

- Fourier analysis on finite groups,
- ergodic methods,
- combinatorial decomposition,
- graph methods.

The subject helped prove major results such as long arithmetic progressions in the primes.

## 7. Probabilistic Number Theory

Probabilistic number theory studies statistical behavior of arithmetic objects.

Examples:

- distribution of prime factors,
- random multiplicative functions,
- normal orders,
- zeta zero statistics.

This subject often models arithmetic phenomena probabilistically even though integers themselves are deterministic.

Connections with random matrix theory became especially important after numerical studies of zeta zeros.

## 8. Computational Number Theory

Computational number theory studies explicit algorithms and arithmetic computation.

Core topics include:

| Area | Examples |
|---|---|
| Fast arithmetic | multiplication and modular exponentiation |
| Factorization | Pollard rho, number field sieve |
| Primality testing | Miller-Rabin, AKS |
| Elliptic curves | point arithmetic |
| Lattice reduction | LLL algorithm |

Modern computation influences both theoretical research and practical applications.

## 9. Cryptographic Number Theory

Public-key cryptography depends heavily on arithmetic hardness assumptions.

Examples:

| Problem | Cryptographic Use |
|---|---|
| Integer factorization | RSA |
| Discrete logarithm | Diffie-Hellman |
| Elliptic curve discrete log | elliptic curve cryptography |
| Short vector problems | lattice cryptography |

Post-quantum cryptography studies systems resistant to quantum attacks.

## 10. Arithmetic Dynamics

Arithmetic dynamics studies repeated iteration of arithmetic maps.

Examples include:


$$
x_{n+1}=f(x_n).
$$


Questions include:

- periodic points,
- orbit growth,
- arithmetic entropy,
- dynamical zeta functions.

The subject combines:

- number theory,
- dynamical systems,
- algebraic geometry.

## 11. Transcendence Theory

Transcendence theory studies numbers not satisfying algebraic equations over $\mathbb{Q}$.

Famous transcendental numbers include:


$$
e,\qquad \pi.
$$


Key topics:

- irrationality proofs,
- transcendence proofs,
- Diophantine approximation,
- linear forms in logarithms.

This subject studies the boundary between algebraic structure and analytic complexity.

## 12. $p$-Adic Analysis

$p$-adic analysis replaces ordinary absolute value with the $p$-adic metric.

Key objects include:

- $p$-adic numbers,
- $p$-adic analytic functions,
- $p$-adic modular forms,
- rigid analytic spaces.

This theory provides local arithmetic information invisible over the real numbers.

## 13. Topological and Geometric Methods

Modern number theory frequently uses topology and geometry.

Examples include:

| Method | Arithmetic Role |
|---|---|
| Cohomology | arithmetic invariants |
| Homotopy theory | higher structures |
| Algebraic topology | arithmetic geometry |
| Spectral sequences | cohomological computation |

Arithmetic increasingly interacts with deep geometric structure.

## 14. Representation Theory

Representation theory studies groups through linear actions on vector spaces.

Arithmetic applications include:

- automorphic forms,
- Galois representations,
- harmonic analysis,
- trace formulas.

Representation theory provides one of the main languages of modern arithmetic.

## 15. Formal Verification and Automated Reasoning

Computer verification of proofs is becoming increasingly important.

Research directions include:

- proof assistants,
- formal arithmetic libraries,
- automated theorem proving,
- verified cryptography.

This area connects mathematical rigor with computer science.

## 16. Experimental Mathematics

Modern arithmetic research often begins experimentally.

Large-scale computation allows researchers to:

- test conjectures,
- visualize arithmetic patterns,
- analyze huge datasets,
- discover unexpected regularities.

Databases and symbolic computation now play a central role in mathematical exploration.

## 17. Interdisciplinary Connections

Number theory interacts with many fields.

| Discipline | Connection |
|---|---|
| Physics | quantum chaos, string theory |
| Computer science | cryptography, complexity |
| Probability | random matrices, stochastic models |
| Geometry | arithmetic varieties |
| Topology | cohomological invariants |
| Logic | decidability and formal systems |

Modern arithmetic is one of the most interconnected areas of mathematics.

## 18. Long-Term Perspective

The subject evolved through several major transitions:

| Era | Main Character |
|---|---|

Future developments will likely deepen connections among arithmetic, geometry, computation, and physics.

## 20. Final Perspective

Number theory began with integers and divisibility. It now studies:

- arithmetic symmetries,
- geometric structures,
- analytic behavior,
- representation-theoretic correspondences,
- computational complexity,
- probabilistic phenomena.

The modern subject combines exactness with abstraction. It moves between concrete computation and highly structural theories. Prime numbers remain central, but the surrounding language now includes schemes, cohomology, automorphic representations, motives, and $L$-functions.

Despite enormous progress, many of the most basic questions remain unresolved. This mixture of accessibility and depth is one reason number theory continues to occupy a central place in mathematics.
