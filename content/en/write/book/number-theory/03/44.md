## From Fields to Larger Fields

A field is a set in which addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division by nonzero elements are possible. The rational numbers


$$
\mathbb Q
$$


form the basic field of arithmetic. Many number-theoretic problems, however, force us to enlarge this field.

For example, the equation


$$
x^2-2=0
$$


has no solution in $\mathbb Q$, but it has solutions in the larger field


$$
\mathbb Q(\sqrt2).
$$


A field extension is a formal way to describe this kind of enlargement.

## Definition

Let $K$ and $L$ be fields. We say that $L$ is a field extension of $K$ if


$$
K\subseteq L
$$


and the operations of $K$ agree with the operations inherited from $L$.

This is written as


$$
L/K.
$$


The field $K$ is called the base field, and $L$ is called the extension field.

For example,


$$
\mathbb C/\mathbb R
$$


is a field extension, as is


$$
\mathbb Q(\sqrt2)/\mathbb Q.
$$


## Adjoining Elements

If $K$ is a field and $\alpha$ is an element of some larger field, then


$$
K(\alpha)
$$


denotes the smallest field containing both $K$ and $\alpha$.

For instance,


$$
\mathbb Q(\sqrt2)
=
\{a+b\sqrt2 : a,b\in\mathbb Q\}.
$$


This field contains all rational numbers and also contains $\sqrt2$. It is closed under addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division by nonzero elements.

More generally, one may adjoin several elements:


$$
K(\alpha_1,\ldots,\alpha_n).
$$


## Extensions as Vector Spaces

A crucial observation is that if $L/K$ is a field extension, then $L$ is naturally a vector space over $K$.

The scalars come from $K$, and the vectors are elements of $L$. Scalar multiplication is just field multiplication.

For example,


$$
\mathbb Q(\sqrt2)
$$


is a two-dimensional vector space over $\mathbb Q$, with basis


$$
1,\sqrt2.
$$


Every element has a unique expression


$$
a+b\sqrt2,
\qquad a,b\in\mathbb Q.
$$


## Degree of an Extension

The degree of a field extension $L/K$ is the dimension of $L$ as a vector space over $K$. It is denoted


$$
[L:K].
$$


Thus


$$
[\mathbb Q(\sqrt2):\mathbb Q]=2.
$$


If this dimension is finite, then $L/K$ is called a finite extension.

The degree measures the algebraic size of the extension.

## Algebraic Elements

Let $L/K$ be a field extension. An element


$$
\alpha\in L
$$


is algebraic over $K$ if it satisfies a nonzero polynomial equation with coefficients in $K$:


$$
f(\alpha)=0,
\qquad f(x)\in K[x],
\qquad f(x)\neq0.
$$


For example, $\sqrt2$ is algebraic over $\mathbb Q$ because


$$
(\sqrt2)^2-2=0.
$$


The number $\pi$, by contrast, is transcendental over $\mathbb Q$, meaning it satisfies no nonzero polynomial equation with rational coefficients.

## Minimal Polynomial

If $\alpha$ is algebraic over $K$, there is a unique monic irreducible polynomial


$$
m_\alpha(x)\in K[x]
$$


such that


$$
m_\alpha(\alpha)=0.
$$


This polynomial is called the minimal polynomial of $\alpha$ over $K$.

For example, the minimal polynomial of $\sqrt2$ over $\mathbb Q$ is


$$
x^2-2.
$$


The degree of the minimal polynomial determines the extension degree:


$$
[K(\alpha):K]=\deg m_\alpha.
$$


## Example: A Cubic Extension

Let


$$
\alpha=\sqrt[3]{2}.
$$


Then $\alpha$ satisfies


$$
x^3-2=0.
$$


The polynomial


$$
x^3-2
$$


is irreducible over $\mathbb Q$, so it is the minimal polynomial of $\alpha$. Hence


$$
[\mathbb Q(\sqrt[3]{2}):\mathbb Q]=3.
$$


Every element of this field can be written uniquely as


$$
a+b\alpha+c\alpha^2,
\qquad a,b,c\in\mathbb Q.
$$


## Tower Law

Field extensions can be stacked.

If


$$
K\subseteq L\subseteq M,
$$


then


$$
[M:K]=[M:L][L:K],
$$


provided the degrees are finite.

This is called the tower law.

For example,


$$
\mathbb Q\subseteq \mathbb Q(\sqrt2)\subseteq \mathbb Q(\sqrt2,\sqrt3).
$$


If


$$
[\mathbb Q(\sqrt2):\mathbb Q]=2
$$


and


$$
[\mathbb Q(\sqrt2,\sqrt3):\mathbb Q(\sqrt2)]=2,
$$


then


$$
[\mathbb Q(\sqrt2,\sqrt3):\mathbb Q]=4.
$$


## Why Field Extensions Matter

Field extensions allow number theory to move beyond ordinary rational arithmetic.

They provide the setting for:


$$
\sqrt d,\quad \zeta_n,\quad \sqrt[3]{m},
\quad\text{and roots of polynomial equations.}
$$


Algebraic number theory studies finite extensions of $\mathbb Q$, called number fields. Their arithmetic generalizes the arithmetic of integers and rational numbers.

In later sections, field extensions will lead naturally to splitting fields, Galois groups, ramification, cyclotomic fields, and class field theory.
