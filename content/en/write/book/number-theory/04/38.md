## Geometry from Polynomial Equations

Arithmetic geometry studies solutions of polynomial equations using both algebraic and geometric methods.

A polynomial equation such as


$$
x^2+y^2=1
$$


defines a geometric object: a circle over the real numbers. But the same equation may also be studied over other fields, such as


$$
\mathbb{Q},
\qquad
\mathbb{C},
\qquad
\mathbb{F}_p,
\qquad
\mathbb{Q}_p.
$$


The central idea is that polynomial equations define spaces, and the arithmetic of their solutions depends on the field over which they are studied.

These spaces are called algebraic varieties.

## Affine Space

Let $K$ be a field.

Affine $n$-space over $K$ is


$$
\mathbb{A}^n_K.
$$


Its points over $K$ are $n$-tuples


$$
(a_1,\ldots,a_n),
\qquad
a_i\in K.
$$


Thus


$$
\mathbb{A}^1_K=K,
$$


and


$$
\mathbb{A}^2_K=K^2.
$$


Affine space is the natural setting for ordinary polynomial equations.

## Affine Varieties

Let


$$
f_1,\ldots,f_m\in K[x_1,\ldots,x_n].
$$


The common zero set


$$
V(f_1,\ldots,f_m)
=
\{P\in \mathbb{A}^n_K : f_i(P)=0 \text{ for all } i\}
$$


is called an affine algebraic set.

When this set satisfies suitable irreducibility conditions, it is called an affine variety.

For example,


$$
V(y^2-x^3-x)
\subseteq
\mathbb{A}^2_K
$$


defines an affine plane curve.

The geometry of this curve depends strongly on the field $K$.

## Coordinate Rings

An affine variety is not studied only through its points. It is also studied through its polynomial functions.

If


$$
X=V(I)
\subseteq
\mathbb{A}^n_K
$$


is defined by an ideal


$$
I\subseteq K[x_1,\ldots,x_n],
$$


then its coordinate ring is


$$
K[X]
=
K[x_1,\ldots,x_n]/I.
$$


This ring records all polynomial functions on $X$.

Thus geometry and algebra are linked:


$$
\text{variety}
\quad\longleftrightarrow\quad
\text{coordinate ring}.
$$


This correspondence is one of the foundational ideas of algebraic geometry.

## Rational Points

If $X$ is a variety over $K$, its $K$-rational points are denoted


$$
X(K).
$$


These are the solutions whose coordinates lie in $K$.

For example, if


$$
X: x^2+y^2=1,
$$


then $X(\mathbb{R})$ is the real circle, while $X(\mathbb{Q})$ is the set of rational points on the circle.

Arithmetic geometry is deeply concerned with understanding sets such as


$$
X(\mathbb{Q}),
\qquad
X(\mathbb{F}_p),
\qquad
X(\mathbb{Q}_p).
$$


The same geometric equation may have very different arithmetic behavior over different fields.

## Projective Space

Affine space is insufficient for many geometric purposes because it has points “missing at infinity.”

Projective geometry remedies this.

Projective $n$-space over $K$, denoted


$$
\mathbb{P}^n_K,
$$


is the set of lines through the origin in


$$
K^{n+1}.
$$


A point is written using homogeneous coordinates:


$$
[x_0:x_1:\cdots:x_n],
$$


where not all $x_i$ are zero, and


$$
[x_0:\cdots:x_n]
=
[\lambda x_0:\cdots:\lambda x_n]
$$


for every nonzero


$$
\lambda\in K^\times.
$$


Thus projective points are coordinates up to common scaling.

## Projective Varieties

A polynomial


$$
F(x_0,\ldots,x_n)
$$


is homogeneous of degree $d$ if every term has total degree $d$.

Only homogeneous polynomials define well-defined equations in projective space.

A projective variety is the common zero set of homogeneous polynomials:


$$
V(F_1,\ldots,F_m)
\subseteq
\mathbb{P}^n_K.
$$


For example,


$$
X^2+Y^2=Z^2
$$


defines a projective conic in


$$
\mathbb{P}^2_K.
$$


Setting $Z=1$ recovers the affine equation


$$
x^2+y^2=1.
$$


The points with


$$
Z=0
$$


are the points at infinity.

## Why Projective Geometry Matters

Projective varieties behave better than affine varieties.

For example, a line and a conic in the projective plane intersect in exactly two points, counted with multiplicity, over an algebraically closed field.

This clean intersection behavior fails in affine geometry because intersections may escape to infinity.

Projective varieties also have compactness-like properties in algebraic geometry.

Many major theorems, including Bézout’s theorem and much of intersection theory, are naturally projective.

## Curves, Surfaces, and Higher Dimensions

Varieties are classified by dimension.

A variety of dimension $0$ is a finite set of points.

A variety of dimension $1$ is a curve.

A variety of dimension $2$ is a surface.

Higher-dimensional varieties arise from systems of polynomial equations with more variables.

Number theory often focuses on curves and higher-dimensional varieties over non-algebraically closed fields.

Examples include:


$$
y^2=x^3+ax+b
$$


for elliptic curves, and more general equations defining abelian varieties, modular curves, and Shimura varieties.

## Smoothness and Singularities

A variety may have singular points.

For example,


$$
y^2=x^3
$$


has a cusp at the origin.

Smooth varieties behave like manifolds in algebraic geometry. Singular varieties have points where the local structure degenerates.

Smoothness is usually tested using derivatives.

If a variety is defined by


$$
f_1,\ldots,f_m,
$$


then the Jacobian matrix


$$
\left(\frac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_j}\right)
$$


detects singularities.

Smoothness is essential in arithmetic geometry because singularities often distort counting, cohomology, and local behavior.

## Varieties over Finite Fields

If $X$ is defined over a finite field


$$
\mathbb{F}_q,
$$


one can count its points:


$$
|X(\mathbb{F}_q)|.
$$


More generally, one studies


$$
|X(\mathbb{F}_{q^n})|
$$


for all positive integers $n$.

These point counts are encoded in the zeta function of the variety.

The Weil conjectures describe the deep structure of these zeta functions and connect them with cohomology.

Thus varieties over finite fields link geometry with analytic number theory.

## Varieties over Number Fields

If $X$ is defined over


$$
\mathbb{Q}
$$


or a number field $K$, one studies rational points:


$$
X(K).
$$


This leads to some of the deepest questions in number theory.

For example:

- Which equations have rational solutions?
- Are there finitely many rational points?
- Can local solvability determine global solvability?
- How are rational points related to height functions and $L$-functions?

These questions form the heart of Diophantine geometry.

## Affine and Projective Viewpoints

Affine and projective geometry are complementary.

Affine varieties are often easier to compute with because they use ordinary coordinates.

Projective varieties are often better behaved because they include points at infinity.

In practice, one frequently begins with an affine equation and then takes its projective closure.

For example,


$$
y^2=x^3+ax+b
$$


becomes the projective equation


$$
Y^2Z=X^3+aXZ^2+bZ^3.
$$


The added point at infinity becomes the identity element in the group law of an elliptic curve.

## Importance in Modern Number Theory

Affine and projective varieties provide the basic language of arithmetic geometry.

They allow number theorists to treat polynomial equations as geometric spaces.

This shift makes it possible to use:

- dimension;
- smoothness;
- cohomology;
- intersection theory;
- moduli spaces;
- Galois actions.

Modern number theory studies solutions not merely as numbers, but as points on geometric objects.

The passage from equations to varieties is the first step in that transformation.
