## Implicit Differentiation

Most automatic differentiation systems differentiate computations by explicitly tracing operations in a computational graph. The derivative is obtained by applying the chain rule through every intermediate step.

Implicit differentiation takes a different approach.

Instead of differentiating through the entire computation process, it differentiates the mathematical condition that defines the solution.

This can dramatically reduce:

- memory usage,
- computational cost,
- graph depth,
- and numerical instability.

Implicit differentiation is central to:

- optimization layers,
- equilibrium models,
- root-finding systems,
- differential equations,
- constrained optimization,
- physics simulation,
- probabilistic inference,
- and many large-scale scientific applications.

### Explicit vs Implicit Definitions

An explicitly defined variable has the form:

$$
y = f(x).
$$

Differentiation proceeds directly:

$$
\frac{dy}{dx} = f'(x).
$$

An implicitly defined variable satisfies a constraint:

$$
F(x,y)=0.
$$

The variable $y$ is not given directly as a formula. Instead, it is defined as the solution of an equation.

The derivative is obtained by differentiating the constraint itself.

### Basic Implicit Differentiation

Suppose:

$$
F(x,y)=0.
$$

Differentiate both sides:

$$
\frac{d}{dx}F(x,y)=0.
$$

Apply the chain rule:

$$
\frac{\partial F}{\partial x}
+
\frac{\partial F}{\partial y}
\frac{dy}{dx} =
0.
$$

Solve for the derivative:

$$
\frac{dy}{dx} = -
\left(
\frac{\partial F}{\partial y}
\right)^{-1}
\frac{\partial F}{\partial x}.
$$

This is the core implicit differentiation formula.

### Geometric Interpretation

The equation:

$$
F(x,y)=0
$$

defines a manifold or constraint surface.

Implicit differentiation computes how the solution moves along this surface as $x$ changes.

Instead of differentiating a sequence of computational steps, we differentiate the geometry of the constraint itself.

### Vector Form

Now suppose:

$$
F(x,y)=0,
$$

with:

$$
x \in \mathbb{R}^n,
\quad
y \in \mathbb{R}^m,
\quad
F:\mathbb{R}^{n+m}\to\mathbb{R}^m.
$$

Differentiation gives:

$$
\frac{\partial F}{\partial x}
+
\frac{\partial F}{\partial y}
\frac{dy}{dx} =
0.
$$

Rearranging:

$$
\frac{dy}{dx} = -
\left(
\frac{\partial F}{\partial y}
\right)^{-1}
\frac{\partial F}{\partial x}.
$$

This matrix equation appears constantly in optimization and scientific computing.

### Implicit Function Theorem

Implicit differentiation relies on the implicit function theorem.

Suppose:

$$
F(x,y)=0,
$$

and:

$$
\det\left(
\frac{\partial F}{\partial y}
\right)\neq 0.
$$

Then locally there exists a differentiable function:

$$
y = g(x).
$$

The derivative is:

$$
Dg(x) = -
\left(
\frac{\partial F}{\partial y}
\right)^{-1}
\frac{\partial F}{\partial x}.
$$

The invertibility condition is crucial.

If the Jacobian becomes singular, derivatives may not exist or may become unstable.

### Why Implicit Differentiation Matters

Suppose $y$ is obtained by running an iterative solver:

$$
y_{k+1}=G(y_k,x).
$$

A naive AD system differentiates through every iteration.

This creates problems:

| Problem | Consequence |
|---|---|
| Long computation graph | Large memory use |
| Many iterations | Expensive backward pass |
| Deep recurrence | Numerical instability |
| Solver internals exposed | Complex graphs |

Implicit differentiation bypasses the iteration history entirely.

Instead, differentiate only the fixed-point condition.

### Fixed Point Differentiation

Suppose:

$$
y = G(y,x).
$$

Define:

$$
F(x,y)=G(y,x)-y.
$$

Then:

$$
F(x,y)=0.
$$

Differentiate:

$$
\frac{\partial G}{\partial x}
+
\frac{\partial G}{\partial y}
\frac{dy}{dx} -
\frac{dy}{dx} =
0.
$$

Rearranging:

$$
\left(
I-\frac{\partial G}{\partial y}
\right)
\frac{dy}{dx} =
\frac{\partial G}{\partial x}.
$$

Thus:

$$
\frac{dy}{dx} =
\left(
I-\frac{\partial G}{\partial y}
\right)^{-1}
\frac{\partial G}{\partial x}.
$$

This avoids storing the entire iteration trajectory.

### Example: Newton's Method

Suppose $y$ solves:

$$
f(y,x)=0.
$$

Newton iteration computes:

$$
y_{k+1} =
y_k -
\left(
\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}
\right)^{-1}
f(y_k,x).
$$

Differentiating through every Newton iteration is expensive.

Implicit differentiation instead differentiates the final condition directly:

$$
f(y,x)=0.
$$

This is usually far cheaper and more stable.

### Optimization Problems

Optimization layers are a major application.

Suppose:

$$
y^*(x) =
\arg\min_y f(x,y).
$$

At the optimum:

$$
\nabla_y f(x,y^*) = 0.
$$

Define:

$$
F(x,y)=\nabla_y f(x,y).
$$

Then implicit differentiation gives:

$$
\frac{dy^*}{dx} = -
\left(
\nabla_{yy}^2 f
\right)^{-1}
\nabla_{xy}^2 f.
$$

This is fundamental in bilevel optimization and differentiable optimization layers.

### Differentiating Through Argmin

The operation:

$$
y = \arg\min_z f(x,z)
$$

appears nondifferentiable at first glance.

But under smoothness and regularity assumptions, the optimality conditions provide a differentiable implicit system.

This enables gradient-based optimization through optimization procedures themselves.

### KKT Systems

Constrained optimization introduces Karush-Kuhn-Tucker conditions.

Suppose:

$$
\min_y f(x,y)
$$

subject to:

$$
g(x,y)=0.
$$

The KKT conditions define an implicit system:

$$
\nabla_y \mathcal{L}(x,y,\lambda)=0,
$$

$$
g(x,y)=0.
$$

Differentiating this system yields derivatives of optimal solutions with respect to parameters.

### Equilibrium Models

Deep equilibrium models define hidden states implicitly:

$$
h = f(h,x).
$$

Instead of stacking many explicit layers, the model directly solves for the equilibrium.

Backward propagation uses implicit differentiation:

$$
\frac{dh}{dx} =
\left(
I-\frac{\partial f}{\partial h}
\right)^{-1}
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}.
$$

This enables effectively infinite-depth architectures without storing all intermediate states.

### Memory Advantages

Implicit differentiation often reduces memory dramatically.

Explicit reverse mode through $T$ iterations requires:

$$
O(T)
$$

activation storage.

Implicit differentiation often requires only:

- the converged solution,
- local Jacobian information,
- linear solves.

This can reduce memory from linear in iteration count to nearly constant.

### Linear System Solves

Implicit differentiation typically requires solving systems like:

$$
A v = b.
$$

The inverse matrix is rarely formed explicitly.

Instead, iterative methods solve the system:

- conjugate gradients,
- GMRES,
- MINRES,
- Krylov methods.

This is essential for large-scale systems.

### Jacobian-Vector Products

The matrix:

$$
\frac{\partial F}{\partial y}
$$

may be enormous.

Implicit methods often use Jacobian-vector products:

$$
Jv.
$$

These can be computed efficiently using AD itself.

Thus AD becomes a subroutine inside implicit differentiation.

### Reverse-Mode Implicit Differentiation

Suppose loss:

$$
L(y(x)).
$$

We want:

$$
\frac{dL}{dx}.
$$

Rather than computing:

$$
\frac{dy}{dx},
$$

explicitly, reverse mode solves the adjoint system:

$$
\left(
\frac{\partial F}{\partial y}
\right)^T
\lambda =
\left(
\frac{dL}{dy}
\right)^T.
$$

Then:

$$
\frac{dL}{dx} = -
\lambda^T
\frac{\partial F}{\partial x}.
$$

This avoids forming full Jacobians.

### Stability Considerations

Implicit differentiation is not automatically stable.

Problems include:

| Problem | Cause |
|---|---|
| Singular Jacobians | Ill-posed systems |
| Poor conditioning | Large sensitivity |
| Inexact solves | Approximate gradients |
| Non-convergence | Invalid assumptions |
| Chaotic fixed points | Extreme sensitivity |

The matrix:

$$
\frac{\partial F}{\partial y}
$$

must usually be well-conditioned enough to solve reliably.

### Approximate Implicit Gradients

Large systems often use approximate solves.

Instead of exact:

$$
A^{-1}b,
$$

compute approximate iterative solutions.

This introduces gradient error.

In practice, approximate implicit gradients may still work well for optimization.

### Implicit Differentiation vs Unrolled Differentiation

Suppose an optimizer runs for $T$ iterations.

Two approaches exist:

| Method | Backward strategy |
|---|---|
| Unrolled differentiation | Differentiate every iteration |
| Implicit differentiation | Differentiate optimality condition |

Unrolling provides exact gradients of the finite iteration process.

Implicit differentiation gives gradients of the converged solution.

These are not always identical.

### Differentiable Physics

Physical systems often solve equations implicitly:

- fluid pressure solves,
- rigid-body constraints,
- PDE systems,
- equilibrium mechanics.

Differentiating through every solver iteration is expensive.

Implicit differentiation instead differentiates the governing equations directly.

### PDE-Constrained Optimization

Suppose state variable $u$ satisfies PDE:

$$
F(u,\theta)=0.
$$

Optimization depends on parameter $\theta$.

Adjoint methods solve:

$$
\left(
\frac{\partial F}{\partial u}
\right)^T
\lambda =
\left(
\frac{\partial L}{\partial u}
\right)^T.
$$

This is essentially reverse-mode implicit differentiation for PDE systems.

### Implicit Layers in Deep Learning

Modern architectures increasingly use implicit structures:

| Architecture | Implicit component |
|---|---|
| Deep equilibrium networks | Fixed points |
| Neural ODEs | Continuous dynamics |
| Optimization layers | Argmin solutions |
| Energy-based models | Equilibrium inference |
| Diffusion inversion | Solver-defined states |

These systems would be impractical without implicit differentiation.

### Computational Complexity

Suppose explicit unrolling requires:

$$
O(T)
$$

memory and computation.

Implicit differentiation often replaces this with:

- one forward solve,
- one backward linear solve.

Complexity depends more on solving the implicit linear system than on iteration depth.

### Automatic Differentiation Perspective

Implicit differentiation does not replace automatic differentiation.

Instead, it changes what is differentiated.

Rather than differentiating solver execution traces, AD computes derivatives of:

- residual equations,
- optimality conditions,
- fixed-point systems,
- equilibrium constraints.

AD is still used internally for Jacobian and adjoint computations.

### Core Idea

Implicit differentiation computes derivatives of solutions defined by equations or optimization conditions rather than differentiating through every computational step used to obtain those solutions. This often reduces memory usage, improves scalability, and enables differentiation through optimization, equilibrium, and simulation systems that would otherwise be impractical to differentiate explicitly.
