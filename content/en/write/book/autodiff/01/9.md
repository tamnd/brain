This chapter establishes the mathematical language used throughout the book. Automatic differentiation is simple at its core: it applies the chain rule to a computation. But to understand AD precisely, we need a few stable concepts: functions, vectors, linear maps, Jacobians, tangent vectors, adjoints, and computational graphs.

The goal is not to rebuild calculus from the ground up. The goal is to name the objects that AD manipulates.

## Functions and Mappings

A function maps inputs to outputs. In elementary calculus, we often write

$$
f : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}
$$

to mean that $f$ takes one real number and returns one real number.

For example,

$$
f(x) = x^2 + 1
$$

maps each input $x$ to an output $x^2 + 1$.

Automatic differentiation is usually concerned with more general functions:

$$
f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m.
$$

Here the input is a vector with $n$ components, and the output is a vector with $m$ components.

If

$$
x =
\begin{bmatrix}
x_1 \\
x_2 \\
\vdots \\
x_n
\end{bmatrix},
$$

then

$$
f(x) =
\begin{bmatrix}
f_1(x) \\
f_2(x) \\
\vdots \\
f_m(x)
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Each output component $f_i$ may depend on some or all input components.

## Scalar, Vector, and Matrix-Valued Functions

The shape of the function determines the shape of its derivative.

| Function type | Form | Derivative object |
|---|---|---|
| Scalar to scalar | $f : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ | Scalar derivative |
| Vector to scalar | $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ | Gradient |
| Vector to vector | $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ | Jacobian |
| Vector to matrix | $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^{p \times q}$ | Higher-rank derivative |
| Tensor to tensor | $f : \mathbb{R}^{d_1 \times \cdots \times d_k} \to \mathbb{R}^{e_1 \times \cdots \times e_l}$ | Linear map between tensor spaces |

Automatic differentiation systems often hide these distinctions behind arrays or tensors, but the mathematical object is always a local linear map.

For a scalar-output function,

$$
f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R},
$$

the derivative is commonly represented as the gradient

$$
\nabla f(x) =
\begin{bmatrix}
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1} \\
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2} \\
\vdots \\
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

For a vector-output function,

$$
f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m,
$$

the derivative is represented as the Jacobian matrix

$$
J_f(x) =
\begin{bmatrix}
\frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_n} \\
\vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
\frac{\partial f_m}{\partial x_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial f_m}{\partial x_n}
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

## Inputs as Points, Perturbations as Vectors

A key distinction in AD is the distinction between a point and a perturbation.

The point is the value where the function is evaluated:

$$
x \in \mathbb{R}^n.
$$

A perturbation is a small change to that point:

$$
\Delta x \in \mathbb{R}^n.
$$

The derivative describes how perturbations in the input produce perturbations in the output.

For a differentiable function,

$$
f(x + \Delta x)
\approx
f(x) + J_f(x)\Delta x.
$$

The Jacobian is therefore the local linear map from input perturbations to output perturbations.

This is the central mathematical model behind forward mode AD.

Forward mode computes the effect of an input perturbation as it moves through the program:

$$
\Delta x
\mapsto
\Delta y.
$$

In differential notation, this is written as

$$
\dot{y} = J_f(x)\dot{x}.
$$

The dot variable $\dot{x}$ is a tangent. It says how the input changes along a chosen direction.

## Outputs as Measurements, Adjoints as Covectors

Reverse mode uses the dual view.

Instead of asking how an input perturbation affects the output, reverse mode asks how a scalar measurement of the output changes with respect to earlier variables.

For

$$
f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m,
$$

a covector

$$
\bar{y} \in (\mathbb{R}^m)^*
$$

assigns a scalar sensitivity to output perturbations.

Reverse mode propagates this sensitivity backward:

$$
\bar{x} = J_f(x)^T \bar{y}.
$$

In machine learning, when the final output is a scalar loss

$$
L : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R},
$$

we seed reverse mode with

$$
\bar{L}=1.
$$

The backward pass then computes

$$
\bar{x} =
\nabla L(x).
$$

This is why reverse mode is efficient for training large models: one scalar loss defines one reverse sweep that gives sensitivities for many parameters.

## Functions in Programs

Mathematical notation hides the fact that functions in AD are usually programs.

A program may compute a function through many intermediate variables:

```text
function f(x):
    a = x * x
    b = a + 1
    c = sin(b)
    return c
```

Mathematically,

$$
f(x)=\sin(x^2+1).
$$

Operationally, the program defines a sequence of maps:

$$
x \mapsto a,
\qquad
a \mapsto b,
\qquad
b \mapsto c.
$$

Automatic differentiation follows this sequence. It does not need the compact formula $\sin(x^2+1)$. It only needs the derivative rule for each primitive operation:

$$
a = x^2,
\qquad
b = a+1,
\qquad
c = \sin b.
$$

The global derivative comes from composing local derivatives.

## Function Composition

If

$$
h(x)=g(f(x)),
$$

then $h$ is the composition of $f$ and $g$.

If

$$
f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m
$$

and

$$
g : \mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}^p,
$$

then

$$
h : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^p.
$$

The derivative of the composition is

$$
J_h(x) =
J_g(f(x))J_f(x).
$$

This is the matrix form of the chain rule.

Automatic differentiation is a systematic way of applying this rule to programs. A program with many intermediate operations is a long composition:

$$
f = f_k \circ f_{k-1} \circ \cdots \circ f_1.
$$

Its derivative is

$$
J_f =
J_{f_k}
J_{f_{k-1}}
\cdots
J_{f_1}.
$$

Forward mode evaluates this product from right to left as it propagates tangents forward through the computation.

Reverse mode applies the transposed product from left to right during the backward pass:

$$
J_f^T =
J_{f_1}^T
\cdots
J_{f_{k-1}}^T
J_{f_k}^T.
$$

## Domains and Partial Functions

Many functions used in numerical programs are only defined on part of their apparent input space.

For example,

$$
\log x
$$

is defined over positive real numbers. The function

$$
\sqrt{x}
$$

is real-valued only for nonnegative $x$. Division

$$
\frac{x}{y}
$$

is undefined when $y=0$.

Programs also introduce partiality through array bounds, failed factorizations, overflow, underflow, and invalid numerical states.

An AD system differentiates the computation where it is valid. If the primal computation is undefined, the derivative usually has no meaningful value. If the primal computation returns `NaN`, the derivative often propagates `NaN` as well.

This matters because AD does not repair invalid programs. It differentiates the computation that exists.

## Smoothness and Differentiability

Automatic differentiation assumes derivative rules for primitive operations. But not every function is differentiable everywhere.

For example,

$$
f(x)=|x|
$$

is not differentiable at

$$
x=0.
$$

The ReLU function,

$$
\operatorname{ReLU}(x)=\max(0,x),
$$

has the same issue at zero.

In practice, AD systems often define a conventional derivative at non-smooth points. For ReLU, many systems use:

$$
\operatorname{ReLU}'(0)=0.
$$

This is a convention, not a classical derivative.

For most numerical workloads, these conventions are acceptable because the non-smooth points occupy small regions of the input space. But they must be remembered when interpreting gradients.

## Local Linearity

The derivative is best understood as local linear behavior.

A nonlinear function may be complicated globally, but near a point $x$, it can be approximated by a linear map:

$$
f(x+\Delta x)
\approx
f(x)
+
Df(x)[\Delta x].
$$

Here

$$
Df(x)
$$

denotes the derivative as a linear operator, and

$$
Df(x)[\Delta x]
$$

denotes the action of that operator on the perturbation $\Delta x$.

When coordinates are chosen, this linear operator is represented by the Jacobian matrix:

$$
Df(x)[\Delta x] = J_f(x)\Delta x.
$$

This operator view is often cleaner than thinking only in terms of matrices. AD systems frequently compute the action of the derivative without ever forming the matrix itself.

## Why This Foundation Matters

Automatic differentiation is built on one recurring idea:

A program computes a function, and the derivative of that function is a local linear map.

Forward mode pushes perturbations through that map.

Reverse mode pulls sensitivities back through the transpose map.

Everything else in AD, including tapes, computational graphs, adjoints, custom gradients, checkpointing, sparse Jacobians, and differentiable programming languages, is an implementation of this idea under real programming constraints.
