## Multivariate Calculus

Automatic differentiation is mostly used for functions with many inputs and many outputs. The one-variable derivative is still useful, but it is too small for most real programs. A model parameter vector may contain millions of numbers. A simulation state may contain pressure, velocity, temperature, and density at every point in a mesh. A tensor computation may map one high-dimensional array into another.

Multivariate calculus gives the language for these cases.

## Partial Derivatives

Let

$$
f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}
$$

be a scalar-valued function of several variables:

$$
f(x) = f(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n).
$$

The partial derivative with respect to $x_i$ measures how $f$ changes when $x_i$ changes and all other variables are held fixed:

$$
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(x).
$$

For example, if

$$
f(x_1,x_2)=x_1^2x_2+\sin x_2,
$$

then

$$
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}=2x_1x_2,
$$

and

$$
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}=x_1^2+\cos x_2.
$$

A partial derivative is a coordinate-level measurement. It tells us about change along one axis of the input space.

## The Gradient

For a scalar-valued function

$$
f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R},
$$

the gradient collects all first partial derivatives into one vector:

$$
\nabla f(x) =
\begin{bmatrix}
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}(x) \\
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}(x) \\
\vdots \\
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}(x)
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

The gradient has the same dimension as the input. It points in the direction of steepest local increase under the Euclidean inner product.

If $u$ is a unit vector, the directional derivative in direction $u$ is

$$
D_u f(x) =
\nabla f(x)^T u.
$$

This dot product is largest when $u$ points in the same direction as $\nabla f(x)$. It is most negative when $u$ points in the opposite direction.

This explains the basic gradient descent update:

$$
x_{k+1}=x_k-\eta\nabla f(x_k).
$$

The negative gradient is the direction of steepest local decrease.

## Directional Derivatives

A directional derivative measures change along a chosen direction.

Let

$$
u \in \mathbb{R}^n.
$$

Define a one-dimensional slice through $x$:

$$
\phi(t)=f(x+tu).
$$

The directional derivative is

$$
D_u f(x)=\phi'(0).
$$

Using the chain rule,

$$
D_u f(x)=\nabla f(x)^T u.
$$

This concept is central to forward mode AD. Forward mode does not need to compute every partial derivative. It can compute the derivative of the output along one chosen input direction.

For a vector-valued function,

$$
f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m,
$$

the same idea gives

$$
D_u f(x)=J_f(x)u.
$$

This is a Jacobian-vector product.

## The Jacobian

For

$$
f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m,
$$

write the output as

$$
f(x) =
\begin{bmatrix}
f_1(x) \\
f_2(x) \\
\vdots \\
f_m(x)
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

The Jacobian matrix is

$$
J_f(x) =
\begin{bmatrix}
\frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_1}(x) & \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_2}(x) & \cdots & \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_n}(x) \\
\frac{\partial f_2}{\partial x_1}(x) & \frac{\partial f_2}{\partial x_2}(x) & \cdots & \frac{\partial f_2}{\partial x_n}(x) \\
\vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
\frac{\partial f_m}{\partial x_1}(x) & \frac{\partial f_m}{\partial x_2}(x) & \cdots & \frac{\partial f_m}{\partial x_n}(x)
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

The Jacobian is the coordinate representation of the derivative as a linear map.

For a small perturbation $\Delta x$,

$$
f(x+\Delta x)
\approx
f(x)+J_f(x)\Delta x.
$$

This approximation is the foundation for linearization, sensitivity analysis, Newton-type methods, Kalman filtering, and many control algorithms.

## Total Differential

The total differential of a scalar-valued function is

$$
df =
\sum_{i=1}^{n}
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}
dx_i.
$$

This notation says that a small change in $f$ is the sum of the contributions from each input coordinate.

In vector form,

$$
df =
\nabla f(x)^T dx.
$$

For vector-valued functions,

$$
dy = J_f(x)dx.
$$

This notation matches the way AD systems propagate derivatives. Forward mode propagates $dx$-like quantities. Reverse mode propagates objects that act on $dy$-like quantities.

## Chain Rule in Several Variables

Let

$$
g : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m
$$

and

$$
f : \mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}^p.
$$

Define

$$
h(x)=f(g(x)).
$$

Then

$$
J_h(x) =
J_f(g(x))J_g(x).
$$

This is the multivariate chain rule.

It has the same structure as the scalar chain rule, but the derivatives are matrices and composition becomes matrix multiplication.

This rule is the core of automatic differentiation. A program is a composition of primitive operations. Each operation has a local Jacobian. AD combines those local Jacobians in the correct order.

## Example: Two-Step Function

Consider

$$
g(x_1,x_2) =
\begin{bmatrix}
x_1x_2 \\
\sin x_1
\end{bmatrix},
$$

and

$$
f(u_1,u_2)=u_1+u_2^2.
$$

The composition is

$$
h(x_1,x_2)=f(g(x_1,x_2)).
$$

First compute the Jacobian of $g$:

$$
J_g(x) =
\begin{bmatrix}
x_2 & x_1 \\
\cos x_1 & 0
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Then compute the Jacobian of $f$:

$$
J_f(u) =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 2u_2
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

By the chain rule,

$$
J_h(x) =
J_f(g(x))J_g(x).
$$

Substituting

$$
u_2=\sin x_1,
$$

we get

$$
J_f(g(x)) =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 2\sin x_1
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Therefore,

$$
J_h(x) =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 2\sin x_1
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
x_2 & x_1 \\
\cos x_1 & 0
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

So

$$
J_h(x) =
\begin{bmatrix}
x_2 + 2\sin x_1\cos x_1
&
x_1
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

This is the gradient row of the scalar-output function $h$.

## Coordinate-Free View

The Jacobian matrix depends on coordinates. The derivative itself is more general: it is a linear map.

At a point $x$, the derivative of $f$ is often written as

$$
Df(x).
$$

It maps input perturbations to output perturbations:

$$
Df(x) : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m.
$$

With coordinates,

$$
Df(x)[u]=J_f(x)u.
$$

This operator view is useful because AD systems often avoid forming the Jacobian explicitly. They compute its action on vectors or covectors.

Forward mode computes:

$$
Df(x)[u].
$$

Reverse mode computes:

$$
Df(x)^T[v].
$$

This distinction is one of the central design choices in AD.

## Hessians

For a scalar-valued function

$$
f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R},
$$

the Hessian matrix collects second partial derivatives:

$$
H_f(x) =
\begin{bmatrix}
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1 \partial x_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1 \partial x_n} \\
\vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_n \partial x_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_n \partial x_n}
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

The Hessian describes curvature. It appears in second-order optimization, uncertainty estimates, Laplace approximations, and stability analysis.

Newton's method uses the update:

$$
x_{k+1} =
x_k -
H_f(x_k)^{-1}\nabla f(x_k).
$$

For large $n$, forming the Hessian explicitly is usually too expensive. Many methods instead use Hessian-vector products:

$$
H_f(x)v.
$$

Automatic differentiation can compute these products through combinations of forward and reverse mode.

## Mixed Partials and Symmetry

If $f$ is sufficiently smooth, then mixed partial derivatives agree:

$$
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_i \partial x_j} =
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_j \partial x_i}.
$$

Under these conditions, the Hessian is symmetric.

This symmetry can reduce computation and storage. But numerical programs may contain non-smooth operations, branches, or floating point artifacts. In those cases, symmetry may fail at certain points or may only hold approximately.

AD systems compute derivatives of the executed computation, so mathematical smoothness assumptions should be checked when using higher-order derivatives.

## Vector Fields

A vector field assigns a vector to each point:

$$
F : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n.
$$

Vector fields appear in differential equations:

$$
\frac{dx}{dt}=F(x,t).
$$

The Jacobian

$$
J_F(x)
$$

describes how the vector field changes with state. It is used for stability analysis, implicit solvers, sensitivity equations, and continuous-time adjoint methods.

AD is valuable here because vector fields may be implemented as complex programs rather than closed-form expressions.

## Multivariate Calculus in AD Terms

The objects of multivariate calculus correspond directly to AD operations.

| Calculus object | AD interpretation |
|---|---|
| Partial derivative | Sensitivity to one input coordinate |
| Gradient | Reverse-mode derivative of scalar output |
| Directional derivative | Forward-mode derivative along one direction |
| Jacobian | Local linear map of vector function |
| Jacobian-vector product | Forward-mode result |
| Vector-Jacobian product | Reverse-mode result |
| Hessian | Derivative of gradient |
| Hessian-vector product | Higher-order AD product |

This table is the practical bridge between calculus notation and AD implementation.

## Summary

Multivariate calculus provides the derivative objects used by automatic differentiation.

The gradient describes scalar-output functions.

The Jacobian describes vector-output functions.

The Hessian describes second-order curvature.

Forward mode computes how perturbations move through the derivative map.

Reverse mode computes how sensitivities move backward through the transposed derivative map.

Most real AD systems are built around these products rather than explicit derivative matrices.
