## Second Derivatives

First-order derivatives measure local linear change. Second derivatives measure how that linear behavior itself changes.

In automatic differentiation, second derivatives introduce fundamentally new structure:

- curvature
- interaction between variables
- local quadratic approximation
- sensitivity of gradients

Second-order information is central in:

- optimization
- scientific computing
- uncertainty estimation
- geometry
- physics
- numerical analysis

Computing second derivatives efficiently and accurately is one of the major extensions of automatic differentiation beyond basic gradient propagation.

### From First-Order to Second-Order Behavior

For a smooth scalar function:

$$
f : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R},
$$

the first derivative:

$$
f'(x)
$$

describes slope.

The second derivative:

$$
f''(x)
$$

describes curvature.

If:

$$
f''(x)>0,
$$

the function bends upward.

If:

$$
f''(x)<0,
$$

the function bends downward.

If:

$$
f''(x)=0,
$$

the function is locally linear to second order.

### Local Quadratic Approximation

The first-order Taylor approximation is:

$$
f(x+h)
\approx
f(x)+f'(x)h.
$$

Second-order approximation adds curvature:

$$
f(x+h)
\approx
f(x)
+
f'(x)h
+
\frac12 f''(x)h^2.
$$

This local quadratic model is significantly more accurate near the expansion point.

In multivariate systems:

$$
f(x+h)
\approx
f(x)
+
\nabla f(x)^T h
+
\frac12 h^T H(x) h,
$$

where:

- $\nabla f(x)$ is the gradient
- $H(x)$ is the Hessian matrix

### Hessian Matrix

For:

$$
f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R},
$$

the Hessian is:

$$
H_{ij}(x) =
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_i \partial x_j}.
$$

The Hessian captures all second-order interactions between variables.

Structure:

$$
H(x)=
\begin{bmatrix}
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1^2}
&
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1\partial x_2}
&
\cdots
\\
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_2\partial x_1}
&
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_2^2}
&
\cdots
\\
\vdots & \vdots & \ddots
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Under sufficient smoothness:

$$
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_i\partial x_j} =
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_j\partial x_i}.
$$

Thus the Hessian is symmetric.

### Geometric Interpretation

The gradient defines the best local linear approximation.

The Hessian defines local curvature.

Geometrically:

| Derivative | Meaning |
|---|---|
| first derivative | tangent |
| second derivative | curvature |

For surfaces, the Hessian determines:

- local bending
- saddle structure
- convexity
- principal curvature directions

Second-order structure describes how tangent planes rotate across space.

### Example

Let:

$$
f(x,y)=x^2+xy+y^2.
$$

Gradient:

$$
\nabla f=
\begin{bmatrix}
2x+y \\
x+2y
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Hessian:

$$
H=
\begin{bmatrix}
2 & 1 \\
1 & 2
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

The Hessian is constant because the function is quadratic.

The off-diagonal terms:

$$
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x\partial y}=1
$$

measure interaction between variables.

### Second Derivatives in Optimization

First-order optimization uses gradients:

$$
x_{k+1} =
x_k-\eta\nabla f(x_k).
$$

Second-order optimization uses curvature:

$$
x_{k+1} =
x_k-H^{-1}\nabla f.
$$

This is Newton's method.

Curvature information can dramatically improve convergence.

Advantages:

| First-order | Second-order |
|---|---|
| cheap | more expensive |
| linear local model | quadratic local model |
| slower convergence | faster convergence near optimum |

Second-order methods become especially important for stiff or ill-conditioned systems.

### Directional Second Derivatives

The second derivative along direction $v$ is:

$$
v^T H v.
$$

This measures curvature in direction $v$.

Operationally:

- positive value: upward curvature
- negative value: downward curvature
- zero: locally flat direction

Many large-scale systems compute directional curvature rather than full Hessians.

### Hessian-Vector Products

A particularly important operation is:

$$
Hv.
$$

This computes how curvature acts on a direction vector.

Advantages:

- avoids constructing dense Hessians
- scales to large systems
- efficient in optimization algorithms

Automatic differentiation can compute Hessian-vector products much more efficiently than explicit Hessians.

### Forward-over-Reverse

One common technique:

1. reverse mode computes gradient
2. forward mode differentiates the gradient

Result:

$$
Hv.
$$

This is called forward-over-reverse differentiation.

Complexity is often close to gradient computation itself.

### Reverse-over-Forward

Alternative orderings exist:

| Composition | Result |
|---|---|
| forward-over-forward | second directional derivative |
| reverse-over-forward | Hessian rows |
| forward-over-reverse | Hessian-vector products |
| reverse-over-reverse | full second-order reverse mode |

Different structures optimize different computational patterns.

### Second Derivatives and Dual Numbers

Ordinary dual numbers compute:

$$
f(x+\varepsilon) =
f(x)+f'(x)\varepsilon.
$$

To obtain second derivatives, one may use:

- truncated polynomial algebras
- nested dual numbers
- hyper-dual numbers

For example:

$$
\varepsilon^3=0
$$

gives:

$$
f(x+\varepsilon) =
f(x)
+
f'(x)\varepsilon
+
\frac12 f''(x)\varepsilon^2.
$$

The coefficient of:

$$
\varepsilon^2
$$

stores second-order information.

### Hyper-Dual Numbers

Hyper-dual numbers isolate second derivatives exactly.

Using:

$$
x+\varepsilon_1+\varepsilon_2,
$$

the coefficient of:

$$
\varepsilon_1\varepsilon_2
$$

is:

$$
f''(x).
$$

This avoids finite-difference instability and symbolic expansion complexity.

### Computational Complexity

For:

$$
f:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R},
$$

the Hessian contains:

$$
n^2
$$

entries.

Because the Hessian is symmetric:

$$
\frac{n(n+1)}{2}
$$

independent values remain.

Complexity grows rapidly.

Examples:

| Variables | Hessian Entries |
|---|---|
| 10 | 100 |
| 1,000 | 1,000,000 |
| 1,000,000 | $10^{12}$ |

Dense Hessian construction quickly becomes infeasible.

### Sparse Hessians

Many systems have sparse second-order structure.

Examples:

- PDE discretizations
- graphical models
- physical simulations
- optimization constraints

Automatic differentiation systems exploit sparsity using:

- graph coloring
- compressed propagation
- structured storage
- symbolic sparsity analysis

Sparse second-order methods are critical for large-scale optimization.

### Curvature and Stability

Second derivatives strongly influence optimization dynamics.

Large curvature:

- causes instability
- amplifies gradient sensitivity
- creates stiff dynamics

Small curvature:

- produces flat regions
- slows optimization

Eigenvalues of the Hessian describe curvature directions.

### Hessian Eigenvalues

For symmetric Hessian:

$$
H=Q\Lambda Q^T.
$$

Eigenvalues determine local geometry:

| Eigenvalue | Meaning |
|---|---|
| positive | convex direction |
| negative | concave direction |
| zero | flat direction |

Mixed signs imply saddle points.

Deep neural networks often contain extremely high-dimensional saddle geometry.

### Laplacian

The trace of the Hessian gives the Laplacian:

$$
\Delta f =
\sum_i
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_i^2}.
$$

The Laplacian appears in:

- diffusion equations
- physics
- geometry
- probabilistic systems

Automatic differentiation can compute Laplacians compositionally.

### Second Derivatives in Physics

Second derivatives appear throughout physics.

Examples:

| Quantity | Meaning |
|---|---|
| acceleration | second derivative of position |
| curvature tensor | second geometric variation |
| Hessian of potential | local force structure |
| wave operators | second differential operators |

AD systems used in physics simulation frequently rely on second-order propagation.

### Second Derivatives and Probabilistic Models

Probabilistic inference often requires Hessians.

Examples:

- Fisher information
- Laplace approximation
- covariance estimation
- uncertainty propagation

Curvature determines local probability geometry.

Second-order AD enables scalable probabilistic optimization.

### Numerical Stability

Second derivatives are numerically more sensitive than first derivatives.

Issues include:

- floating-point cancellation
- exploding higher-order terms
- instability in reverse-over-reverse systems
- memory growth

Stable higher-order AD is an active research area.

### Tape Complexity

Reverse-mode AD stores intermediate values on tapes.

Second-order reverse mode may require:

- nested tapes
- higher-order graph retention
- large memory overhead

Checkpointing and recomputation become increasingly important.

### Differentiating Through Solvers

Second derivatives become particularly complex when differentiating through:

- optimization algorithms
- implicit systems
- PDE solvers
- iterative methods

The resulting Hessians may be:

- dense
- implicit
- structured
- operator-defined

Advanced AD systems increasingly compute Hessian actions rather than explicit matrices.

### Differential Geometry Perspective

The Hessian measures how tangent vectors change across space.

First derivative:

$$
Df_x
$$

is linear approximation.

Second derivative measures variation of:

$$
Df_x.
$$

Thus curvature is derivative-of-derivative structure.

This connects second-order AD directly to Riemannian geometry.

### Summary

Second derivatives extend local linear approximation into local quadratic approximation.

Core structures include:

| Object | Meaning |
|---|---|
| gradient | first-order sensitivity |
| Hessian | second-order curvature |
| Hessian-vector product | directional curvature |
| Laplacian | trace of curvature |

Automatic differentiation computes second derivatives through structured propagation of higher-order infinitesimal information.

Techniques include:

- nested dual numbers
- truncated polynomial algebras
- hyper-dual numbers
- mixed forward/reverse composition

Second-order automatic differentiation forms the computational foundation for curvature-aware optimization, scientific simulation, and higher-order differentiable systems.
