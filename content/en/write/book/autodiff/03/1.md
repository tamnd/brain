## Elementary Operations

Automatic differentiation reduces a program to elementary operations. An elementary operation is a primitive computation whose derivative rule is known locally.

Examples include:

```text
v = a + b
v = a * b
v = sin(a)
v = exp(a)
v = matmul(a, b)
v = relu(a)
```

The AD system does not need to derive these rules from scratch each time. It stores derivative rules for each primitive operation and applies them during forward or reverse propagation.

## Scalar Elementary Operations

For scalar values, the usual rules are small and direct.

| Operation | Primal rule | Forward tangent rule | Reverse adjoint rule |
|---|---|---|---|
| Addition | $v=a+b$ | $\dot v=\dot a+\dot b$ | $\bar a{+}=\bar v,\ \bar b{+}=\bar v$ |
| Subtraction | $v=a-b$ | $\dot v=\dot a-\dot b$ | $\bar a{+}=\bar v,\ \bar b{-}=\bar v$ |
| Multiplication | $v=ab$ | $\dot v=b\dot a+a\dot b$ | $\bar a{+}=\bar v b,\ \bar b{+}=\bar v a$ |
| Division | $v=a/b$ | $\dot v=(b\dot a-a\dot b)/b^2$ | $\bar a{+}=\bar v/b,\ \bar b{-}=\bar v a/b^2$ |
| Negation | $v=-a$ | $\dot v=-\dot a$ | $\bar a{-}=\bar v$ |

The reverse rules use `+=` because a variable may influence the output through several paths. Each path contributes one adjoint term.

## Unary Functions

Unary functions take one input and produce one output.

| Operation | Primal rule | Forward tangent rule | Reverse adjoint rule |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sine | $v=\sin(a)$ | $\dot v=\cos(a)\dot a$ | $\bar a{+}=\bar v\cos(a)$ |
| Cosine | $v=\cos(a)$ | $\dot v=-\sin(a)\dot a$ | $\bar a{-}=\bar v\sin(a)$ |
| Exponential | $v=\exp(a)$ | $\dot v=\exp(a)\dot a$ | $\bar a{+}=\bar v\exp(a)$ |
| Logarithm | $v=\log(a)$ | $\dot v=\dot a/a$ | $\bar a{+}=\bar v/a$ |
| Square root | $v=\sqrt a$ | $\dot v=\dot a/(2\sqrt a)$ | $\bar a{+}=\bar v/(2\sqrt a)$ |
| Hyperbolic tangent | $v=\tanh(a)$ | $\dot v=(1-v^2)\dot a$ | $\bar a{+}=\bar v(1-v^2)$ |

Some rules can be written using the input, the output, or both. For $\tanh$, using the output $v$ is often cheaper and more stable than recomputing $\tanh(a)$.

## Local Rules as Linear Maps

Each elementary operation has a local linearization.

For:


$$
v = g(a,b),
$$


a small perturbation gives:


$$
dv = \frac{\partial g}{\partial a}da + \frac{\partial g}{\partial b}db.
$$


Forward mode evaluates this differential directly.

Reverse mode applies the transpose of this local linear map. If:


$$
dv = p\,da + q\,db,
$$


then:


$$
\bar a += p\bar v,
\qquad
\bar b += q\bar v.
$$


Forward and reverse rules are therefore two views of the same local derivative.

## Constants

Constants have zero tangent and receive no adjoint.

```text
v = 3.0
```

Forward mode:


$$
\dot v = 0.
$$


Reverse mode:

```text
no input adjoint update
```

A constant contributes values to downstream computations, but it has no differentiable input.

## Copy and Identity

Copy operations appear trivial, but they matter in graph representations.

```text
v = a
```

Forward mode:


$$
\dot v = \dot a.
$$


Reverse mode:


$$
\bar a += \bar v.
$$


Identity operations are often removed by optimization, but preserving them can simplify graph construction and debugging.

## Comparisons

Comparisons produce discrete outputs.

```text
v = a > b
```

The result is Boolean. Ordinary derivatives do not propagate through the comparison in a useful way.

Most AD systems treat comparisons as non-differentiable control inputs. The comparison may select a branch, mask, or index, but gradients do not flow into the Boolean decision itself.

For example:

```text
y = where(x > 0, x, 0)
```

The comparison creates a mask. The derivative flows through the selected numeric values, not through the truth value.

## Piecewise Elementary Operations

Some primitives are piecewise smooth.

The ReLU operation is:


$$
v = \max(0,a).
$$


Its derivative is:


$$
\frac{dv}{da}
=
\begin{cases}
1, & a>0,\\
0, & a<0.
\end{cases}
$$


At $a=0$, the classical derivative is undefined. AD systems usually choose a convention, commonly $0$.

Forward rule:


$$
\dot v =
1_{a>0}\dot a.
$$


Reverse rule:


$$
\bar a += 1_{a>0}\bar v.
$$


This is a useful derivative rule for optimization, but it includes a convention at the kink.

## Absolute Value

The absolute value operation is another piecewise primitive.


$$
v = |a|.
$$


For $a \ne 0$:


$$
\frac{dv}{da} = \operatorname{sign}(a).
$$


Forward rule:


$$
\dot v = \operatorname{sign}(a)\dot a.
$$


Reverse rule:


$$
\bar a += \operatorname{sign}(a)\bar v.
$$


At $a=0$, the derivative is undefined. Some systems choose $0$ as a subgradient-like convention.

## Min and Max

For:


$$
v = \max(a,b),
$$


the derivative follows the selected argument.

If $a>b$:


$$
\dot v = \dot a,
\qquad
\bar a += \bar v.
$$


If $b>a$:


$$
\dot v = \dot b,
\qquad
\bar b += \bar v.
$$


At ties, the derivative is not uniquely defined. Systems choose conventions that may differ.

This matters in optimization because different tie-breaking rules can produce different gradients.

## Power

The power operation has several cases.

For constant exponent:


$$
v = a^c.
$$


Forward rule:


$$
\dot v = c a^{c-1}\dot a.
$$


Reverse rule:


$$
\bar a += \bar v c a^{c-1}.
$$


For variable base and exponent:


$$
v = a^b.
$$


Assuming $a>0$:


$$
dv = a^b\left(b\frac{da}{a} + \log(a)db\right).
$$


Forward rule:


$$
\dot v =
v\left(b\frac{\dot a}{a}+\log(a)\dot b\right).
$$


Reverse rules:


$$
\bar a += \bar v\,b a^{b-1},
\qquad
\bar b += \bar v\,v\log(a).
$$


The domain restriction matters. Real-valued AD cannot ignore it.

## Vector Operations

A vector primitive maps vectors to vectors or scalars.

Example:


$$
v = a + b
$$


for vectors $a,b\in\mathbb{R}^n$.

Forward rule:


$$
\dot v = \dot a + \dot b.
$$


Reverse rule:


$$
\bar a += \bar v,
\qquad
\bar b += \bar v.
$$


Elementwise operations usually apply scalar derivative rules element by element.

## Reduction Operations

A reduction maps many values to fewer values.

Example:


$$
v = \sum_i a_i.
$$


Forward rule:


$$
\dot v = \sum_i \dot a_i.
$$


Reverse rule:


$$
\bar a_i += \bar v
\quad \text{for all } i.
$$


For mean:


$$
v = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i a_i.
$$


Reverse rule:


$$
\bar a_i += \frac{1}{n}\bar v.
$$


Reductions are common sources of broadcasting in reverse mode.

## Broadcasting

Broadcasting expands a smaller value over a larger shape.

Example:

```text
v[i] = a[i] + b
```

where $b$ is scalar.

Forward rule:


$$
\dot v_i = \dot a_i + \dot b.
$$


Reverse rules:


$$
\bar a_i += \bar v_i,
$$



$$
\bar b += \sum_i \bar v_i.
$$


The broadcasted input receives the sum over all positions where it was reused.

This is one of the most common implementation details in tensor AD.

## Matrix Multiplication

Matrix multiplication is a central tensor primitive.

Let:


$$
Y = AB.
$$


The differential is:


$$
dY = dA\,B + A\,dB.
$$


Forward rules:


$$
\dot Y = \dot A B + A\dot B.
$$


Reverse rules:


$$
\bar A += \bar Y B^T,
$$



$$
\bar B += A^T \bar Y.
$$


These rules are local, but their implementation must respect shapes, batch dimensions, layouts, and device placement.

## Dot Product

For:


$$
v = a^T b,
$$


the differential is:


$$
dv = b^T da + a^T db.
$$


Forward rule:


$$
\dot v = \dot a^T b + a^T \dot b.
$$


Reverse rules:


$$
\bar a += \bar v b,
$$



$$
\bar b += \bar v a.
$$


The dot product is the vector analogue of scalar multiplication.

## Norm

For the Euclidean norm:


$$
v = \lVert a\rVert_2 = \sqrt{a^T a}.
$$


For $a \ne 0$:


$$
dv = \frac{a^T da}{\lVert a\rVert_2}.
$$


Forward rule:


$$
\dot v = \frac{a^T\dot a}{\lVert a\rVert_2}.
$$


Reverse rule:


$$
\bar a += \bar v\frac{a}{\lVert a\rVert_2}.
$$


At $a=0$, the derivative is undefined.

## Reshape and View Operations

A reshape changes the interpretation of memory layout but not the numerical values.

```text
v = reshape(a, shape)
```

Forward rule:


$$
\dot v = reshape(\dot a, shape).
$$


Reverse rule:


$$
\bar a += reshape(\bar v, original\_shape).
$$


A view operation may share storage with the input. This introduces aliasing concerns, but the derivative rule is usually a corresponding inverse view or accumulation into the original shape.

## Slice and Gather

A slice selects part of an array.

```text
v = a[i:j]
```

Forward rule:


$$
\dot v = \dot a[i:j].
$$


Reverse rule:

```text
bar_a[i:j] += bar_v
```

A gather selects indexed elements:

```text
v[k] = a[index[k]]
```

Reverse mode scatters adjoints back:

```text
bar_a[index[k]] += bar_v[k]
```

If the same index appears more than once, adjoints accumulate.

## Scatter

Scatter writes values into indexed positions.

```text
v[index[k]] = a[k]
```

The derivative depends on write semantics.

If scatter overwrites, repeated indices create ambiguity because later writes replace earlier writes.

If scatter adds, the operation is linear and the reverse rule gathers from the indexed positions.

AD systems must define scatter semantics precisely.

## Concatenation and Split

Concatenation joins arrays.

```text
v = concat(a, b)
```

Forward rule:

```text
dot_v = concat(dot_a, dot_b)
```

Reverse rules:

```text
bar_a += first_slice(bar_v)
bar_b += second_slice(bar_v)
```

Split is the inverse pattern: forward splits values, reverse concatenates adjoints.

## Softmax

Softmax maps a vector to a probability vector:


$$
v_i = \frac{\exp(a_i)}{\sum_j \exp(a_j)}.
$$


The Jacobian is:


$$
\frac{\partial v_i}{\partial a_j}
=
v_i(\delta_{ij}-v_j).
$$


Forward mode can compute:


$$
\dot v_i =
v_i\left(\dot a_i-\sum_j v_j\dot a_j\right).
$$


Reverse mode computes:


$$
\bar a_i =
v_i\left(\bar v_i-\sum_j \bar v_j v_j\right).
$$


Efficient implementations avoid materializing the full Jacobian.

## Log-Sum-Exp

Log-sum-exp is:


$$
v = \log\sum_i \exp(a_i).
$$


Its derivative is softmax:


$$
\frac{\partial v}{\partial a_i}
=
\frac{\exp(a_i)}{\sum_j \exp(a_j)}.
$$


Reverse rule:


$$
\bar a_i += \bar v\,\operatorname{softmax}(a)_i.
$$


A stable implementation subtracts the maximum before exponentiation. The derivative rule remains the same, but the primal computation avoids overflow.

## Loss Functions as Primitives

Some systems treat common losses as fused primitives.

Example:

```text
loss = cross_entropy(logits, target)
```

This may combine log-softmax and indexing into one numerically stable operation.

The derivative rule is often simpler and more stable than differentiating a naive expression.

For one-hot target $t$ and softmax output $p$:


$$
\frac{\partial loss}{\partial logits}
=
p - t.
$$


Fusing the primitive reduces memory use and improves numerical behavior.

## Domains and Undefined Derivatives

Elementary operations have domains.

| Operation | Domain issue |
|---|---|
| `log(x)` | $x>0$ |
| `sqrt(x)` | $x\ge 0$, derivative singular at $0$ |
| `divide(a,b)` | $b\ne0$ |
| `acos(x)` | $-1<x<1$ for finite derivative |
| `norm(x)` | derivative undefined at zero |

AD does not remove these mathematical constraints. It applies local rules to the executed computation. If a rule evaluates outside its domain, the result may be `NaN`, `Inf`, or implementation-defined.

## Custom Elementary Operations

A system can be extended with custom primitives.

A primitive needs at least:

| Component | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Primal implementation | compute output |
| Forward rule | compute tangent output |
| Reverse rule | propagate adjoints |
| Shape rule | infer output shape |
| Type rule | infer output dtype |
| Effect annotation | describe mutation or side effects |

For a scalar primitive:

```go
type Primitive struct {
    Name string
    Eval func(args []Value) Value
    JVP  func(args []Value, tangents []Value) Value
    VJP  func(args []Value, outAdj Value) []Value
}
```

This is a simplified interface, but it shows the contract.

## Primitive Granularity

Choosing primitives is a design decision.

Fine-grained primitives:

```text
mul
add
exp
sum
divide
```

are simple and general.

Coarse-grained primitives:

```text
softmax_cross_entropy
layer_norm
attention
```

can be faster and more stable.

The tradeoff is:

| Primitive style | Advantages | Costs |
|---|---|---|
| Fine-grained | simple, flexible, compositional | more graph nodes, less fusion |
| Coarse-grained | faster, stable, less memory | more custom rules, larger API |

Good AD systems usually support both.

## Core Idea

Elementary operations are the atoms of automatic differentiation. Each primitive has a primal rule and local derivative rules. Forward mode applies tangent rules. Reverse mode applies adjoint rules.

The quality of an AD system depends heavily on its primitive set. Correct local rules give correct global derivatives. Stable, fused, and well-designed primitives give practical performance.
