# Chapter 109. Linear Regression

Linear regression studies how a scalar quantity depends on one or more input variables. It is one of the simplest and most important applications of linear algebra. The unknown parameters form a vector, the data form a matrix, and the best-fit model is found by solving a least-squares problem.

The central linear-algebra problem is this:

$$
X\beta \approx y.
$$

Here \(X\) is the design matrix, \(\beta\) is the coefficient vector, and \(y\) is the observed output vector. Linear regression chooses \(\beta\) so that \(X\beta\) is as close as possible to \(y\), usually in squared Euclidean distance. This is the ordinary least-squares formulation.

## 109.1 Data and Model

Suppose we observe \(m\) data points.

For each observation \(i\), we have input variables

$$
x_{i1},x_{i2},\ldots,x_{in}
$$

and an output value

$$
y_i.
$$

A linear regression model assumes that \(y_i\) is approximated by a linear combination of the inputs:

$$
y_i \approx
\beta_0+\beta_1x_{i1}+\cdots+\beta_nx_{in}.
$$

The scalar \(\beta_0\) is the intercept. The scalars \(\beta_1,\ldots,\beta_n\) are coefficients.

The model is linear in the coefficients. The variables themselves may be transformed before entering the model. For example, \(x^2\), \(\log x\), or interaction terms may appear as columns of the design matrix, as long as the unknown coefficients enter linearly.

## 109.2 Simple Linear Regression

In simple linear regression, there is one input variable.

The model has the form

$$
y_i \approx \beta_0+\beta_1x_i.
$$

The coefficient \(\beta_0\) shifts the line vertically. The coefficient \(\beta_1\) is the slope.

The fitted line is

$$
\hat y = \beta_0+\beta_1x.
$$

The goal is to choose \(\beta_0\) and \(\beta_1\) so that the fitted line is close to the observed data points.

## 109.3 Multiple Linear Regression

In multiple linear regression, there are several input variables.

The model is

$$
y_i \approx
\beta_0+\beta_1x_{i1}+\beta_2x_{i2}+\cdots+\beta_nx_{in}.
$$

Each coefficient measures the contribution of one feature while the others are held in the model.

For \(m\) observations, the system can be written as

$$
X\beta \approx y,
$$

where

$$
X=
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & x_{11} & x_{12} & \cdots & x_{1n}\\
1 & x_{21} & x_{22} & \cdots & x_{2n}\\
\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots\\
1 & x_{m1} & x_{m2} & \cdots & x_{mn}
\end{bmatrix},
$$

$$
\beta=
\begin{bmatrix}
\beta_0\\
\beta_1\\
\vdots\\
\beta_n
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
y=
\begin{bmatrix}
y_1\\
y_2\\
\vdots\\
y_m
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

The first column of \(X\) contains ones so that the model includes an intercept.

## 109.4 Residuals

The fitted values are

$$
\hat y=X\beta.
$$

The residual vector is

$$
r=y-X\beta.
$$

Its \(i\)-th component is

$$
r_i=y_i-\hat y_i.
$$

A residual is the error left after fitting the model to one observation.

Linear regression does not usually make all residuals zero. In an overdetermined system, there are more equations than unknowns, so an exact solution may not exist.

Instead, regression chooses the coefficient vector that makes the residual vector as small as possible.

## 109.5 Least Squares

Ordinary least squares chooses \(\beta\) to minimize the sum of squared residuals:

$$
S(\beta) =
\|y-X\beta\|_2^2.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
S(\beta) =
\sum_{i=1}^m
(y_i-\hat y_i)^2.
$$

The square has three important effects. It makes positive and negative residuals contribute positively. It penalizes large residuals more strongly than small residuals. It gives an objective function with convenient linear-algebraic structure.

Thus the least-squares problem is

$$
\min_{\beta}
\|y-X\beta\|_2^2.
$$

This is the standard linear least-squares problem used in ordinary linear regression.

## 109.6 Normal Equations

To find the minimizer, expand the objective:

$$
S(\beta) =
(y-X\beta)^T(y-X\beta).
$$

This gives

$$
S(\beta) =
y^Ty-2\beta^TX^Ty+\beta^TX^TX\beta.
$$

Taking the gradient with respect to \(\beta\) gives

$$
\nabla S(\beta) =
-2X^Ty+2X^TX\beta.
$$

At the minimum,

$$
\nabla S(\beta)=0.
$$

Therefore,

$$
X^TX\beta=X^Ty.
$$

These are the normal equations. Least-squares solutions of \(X\beta \approx y\) solve the normal equations.

## 109.7 Closed-Form Solution

If \(X^TX\) is invertible, then the normal equations have the unique solution

$$
\hat\beta =
(X^TX)^{-1}X^Ty.
$$

The vector \(\hat\beta\) is the ordinary least-squares estimator.

Invertibility of \(X^TX\) is equivalent to the columns of \(X\) being linearly independent.

If the columns of \(X\) are linearly dependent, then there may be infinitely many coefficient vectors giving the same fitted values. In that case, the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse gives a canonical minimum-norm solution.

## 109.8 Geometric Interpretation

The column space of \(X\) is

$$
\operatorname{col}(X) =
\{X\beta:\beta\in F^n\}.
$$

The fitted vector

$$
\hat y=X\hat\beta
$$

lies in this column space.

Least squares chooses the point in \(\operatorname{col}(X)\) closest to \(y\).

Thus \(\hat y\) is the orthogonal projection of \(y\) onto the column space of \(X\). The residual vector

$$
r=y-\hat y
$$

is orthogonal to every column of \(X\). This condition is written as

$$
X^Tr=0.
$$

Substituting \(r=y-X\hat\beta\) gives

$$
X^T(y-X\hat\beta)=0,
$$

which is the normal equation again.

Thus the normal equations express orthogonality of the residual to the model space.

## 109.9 Projection Matrix

When \(X^TX\) is invertible, the fitted vector is

$$
\hat y =
X\hat\beta =
X(X^TX)^{-1}X^Ty.
$$

The matrix

$$
P =
X(X^TX)^{-1}X^T
$$

is the projection matrix onto the column space of \(X\).

It satisfies

$$
P^T=P
$$

and

$$
P^2=P.
$$

Symmetry means the projection is orthogonal. Idempotence means that projecting twice is the same as projecting once.

The residual vector is

$$
r=(I-P)y.
$$

Thus regression decomposes the observed vector into two orthogonal components:

$$
y=\hat y+r.
$$

## 109.10 Rank and Identifiability

The rank of \(X\) controls whether the coefficients are identifiable.

If \(X\) has full column rank, then

$$
X^TX
$$

is invertible, and the least-squares coefficient vector is unique.

If \(X\) does not have full column rank, then some columns are linear combinations of others. The model contains redundant information.

In that case, different coefficient vectors may produce the same predictions:

$$
X\beta_1=X\beta_2.
$$

The fitted vector may still be unique, but the coefficient vector may not be.

This distinction is important. Prediction may be well-defined even when individual coefficients are not.

## 109.11 Simple Regression Formula

For simple linear regression,

$$
\hat y_i=\beta_0+\beta_1x_i.
$$

The least-squares slope is

$$
\hat\beta_1 =
\frac{\sum_{i=1}^m (x_i-\bar x)(y_i-\bar y)}
{\sum_{i=1}^m (x_i-\bar x)^2}.
$$

The intercept is

$$
\hat\beta_0 =
\bar y-\hat\beta_1\bar x.
$$

Here

$$
\bar x=\frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m x_i,
\qquad
\bar y=\frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m y_i.
$$

This formula is the two-parameter version of the matrix equation

$$
\hat\beta=(X^TX)^{-1}X^Ty.
$$

## 109.12 Example

Suppose the data are

| \(x\) | \(y\) |
|---:|---:|
| 1 | 6 |
| 2 | 5 |
| 3 | 7 |
| 4 | 10 |

Fit the model

$$
y\approx \beta_0+\beta_1x.
$$

The design matrix and response vector are

$$
X=
\begin{bmatrix}
1&1\\
1&2\\
1&3\\
1&4
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
y=
\begin{bmatrix}
6\\
5\\
7\\
10
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Compute

$$
X^TX=
\begin{bmatrix}
4&10\\
10&30
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
X^Ty=
\begin{bmatrix}
28\\
77
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

The normal equations are

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
4&10\\
10&30
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
\beta_0\\
\beta_1
\end{bmatrix} =
\begin{bmatrix}
28\\
77
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Solving gives

$$
\hat\beta_0=3.5,
\qquad
\hat\beta_1=1.4.
$$

Therefore the fitted line is

$$
\hat y=3.5+1.4x.
$$

This is a standard least-squares line-fitting computation.

## 109.13 Residuals in the Example

The fitted values are:

| \(x\) | \(y\) | \(\hat y=3.5+1.4x\) | \(r=y-\hat y\) |
|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 1 | 6 | 4.9 | 1.1 |
| 2 | 5 | 6.3 | -1.3 |
| 3 | 7 | 7.7 | -0.7 |
| 4 | 10 | 9.1 | 0.9 |

The residual sum of squares is

$$
1.1^2+(-1.3)^2+(-0.7)^2+0.9^2=4.2.
$$

The residuals do not vanish. The fitted line is not required to pass through every data point. It is required to minimize the sum of squared residuals.

## 109.14 Statistical Model

In statistics, linear regression is often written as

$$
y=X\beta+\varepsilon.
$$

Here \(\varepsilon\) is an error vector.

The model separates the observed response into a systematic linear part and an unexplained error part.

Common assumptions include:

| Assumption | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Zero mean errors | \(E[\varepsilon]=0\) |
| Constant variance | Errors have common variance |
| Uncorrelated errors | Different errors are uncorrelated |
| Full rank design | Coefficients are identifiable |

Under suitable assumptions, ordinary least squares has strong optimality properties. The Gauss-Markov theorem states that, under standard linear-model conditions, the least-squares estimator has minimum variance among linear unbiased estimators.

## 109.15 Interpretation of Coefficients

In the model

$$
y\approx \beta_0+\beta_1x_1+\cdots+\beta_nx_n,
$$

the coefficient \(\beta_j\) measures the change in the fitted value associated with a one-unit change in \(x_j\), while the other variables are held fixed.

This interpretation is algebraic and model-based. It depends on the chosen features and on the assumption that the linear model is appropriate.

If two features are strongly correlated, individual coefficients may become unstable even when predictions remain accurate.

## 109.16 Multicollinearity

Multicollinearity occurs when columns of \(X\) are nearly linearly dependent.

Then \(X^TX\) is nearly singular.

The least-squares solution may become numerically unstable. Small changes in the data can produce large changes in the coefficient vector.

This is a conditioning problem.

It does not necessarily mean the model predicts poorly. It means the individual coefficients are difficult to estimate reliably.

Regularization methods such as ridge regression address this by modifying the optimization problem.

## 109.17 Numerical Methods

Although the formula

$$
\hat\beta=(X^TX)^{-1}X^Ty
$$

is compact, one usually avoids explicitly forming the inverse in numerical computation.

Common methods include:

| Method | Use |
|---|---|
| Normal equations | Simple, but may worsen conditioning |
| QR factorization | Stable standard method |
| Singular value decomposition | Best for rank-deficient or ill-conditioned problems |
| Iterative methods | Useful for large sparse systems |

The normal equations involve \(X^TX\), whose condition number may be much worse than that of \(X\). QR and SVD methods are usually more stable for serious numerical work.

## 109.18 Weighted Least Squares

Sometimes different observations have different reliability.

Weighted least squares minimizes

$$
\sum_{i=1}^m w_i(y_i-\hat y_i)^2,
$$

where

$$
w_i>0.
$$

In matrix form, this is

$$
\min_\beta
(y-X\beta)^TW(y-X\beta),
$$

where \(W\) is a diagonal matrix of weights.

The normal equations become

$$
X^TWX\beta=X^TWy.
$$

Weighted least squares gives more influence to observations with larger weights.

## 109.19 Ridge Regression

Ridge regression adds a penalty on coefficient size:

$$
\min_\beta
\|y-X\beta\|_2^2+\lambda\|\beta\|_2^2.
$$

Here

$$
\lambda>0
$$

controls the strength of regularization.

The solution satisfies

$$
(X^TX+\lambda I)\beta=X^Ty.
$$

Ridge regression improves stability when \(X^TX\) is ill-conditioned or singular. It trades small bias for reduced variance and better numerical behavior.

## 109.20 Linear Regression as Projection

The deepest linear-algebra view is projection.

Regression does not merely fit a line. It projects the observed vector \(y\) onto the subspace of all possible fitted vectors.

That subspace is

$$
\operatorname{col}(X).
$$

The fitted vector is

$$
\hat y=P_{\operatorname{col}(X)}y.
$$

The residual is the perpendicular error:

$$
r=y-\hat y.
$$

Thus ordinary least squares is an orthogonal projection problem in \(\mathbb R^m\).

This is why inner products, orthogonality, rank, column spaces, and matrix factorizations are central to regression.

## 109.21 Summary

Linear regression is a least-squares application of linear algebra.

| Concept | Linear-algebra meaning |
|---|---|
| Observations | Rows of \(X\) |
| Features | Columns of \(X\) |
| Coefficients | Vector \(\beta\) |
| Predictions | Vector \(X\beta\) |
| Residuals | Vector \(y-X\beta\) |
| Least squares | Minimize \(\|y-X\beta\|_2^2\) |
| Normal equations | \(X^TX\beta=X^Ty\) |
| Fitted values | Projection of \(y\) onto \(\operatorname{col}(X)\) |

Linear regression is therefore not separate from linear algebra. It is one of its most direct applications: approximate an observed vector by the closest vector in a linear subspace.
